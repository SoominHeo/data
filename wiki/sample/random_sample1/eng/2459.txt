<p>The <b>Crisis of the Third Century</b>, also known as <b>Military Anarchy</b> or the <b>Imperial Crisis</b> (AD 235–284), was a period in which the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> nearly collapsed under the combined pressures of <a href="/wiki/Invasion" title="Invasion">invasion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_war" title="Civil war">civil war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Cyprian" title="Plague of Cyprian">plague</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economic_collapse" title="Economic collapse">economic depression</a>. The Crisis began with the <a href="/wiki/Assassination" title="Assassination">assassination</a> of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Severus_Alexander" title="Severus Alexander">Severus Alexander</a> by his own troops in 235, initiating a 50-year period in which there were at least 26 claimants to the title of Emperor, mostly prominent <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">generals</a>, who assumed imperial power over all or part of the Empire. The same number of men became accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman Senate</a> as emperor during this period and so became legitimate emperors.</p>
<p>By 268, the empire had split into three competing states: the <a href="/wiki/Gallic_Empire" title="Gallic Empire">Gallic Empire</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a> and (briefly) <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Palmyrene_Empire" title="Palmyrene Empire">Palmyrene Empire</a>, including the eastern provinces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syria_(Roman_province)" title="Syria (Roman province)">Syria Palaestina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt_(Roman_province)" title="Egypt (Roman province)">Aegyptus</a>; and the Italian-centered and independent Roman Empire, proper, between them. Later, <a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a> (270–275) reunited the empire; the Crisis ended with the ascension and reforms of <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> in 284.</p>
<p>The Crisis resulted in such profound changes in the empire's institutions, society, economic life and, eventually, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religion_in_Ancient_Rome" title="Religion in Ancient Rome">religion</a>, that it is increasingly seen by most historians as defining the transition between the <a href="/wiki/Periodization" title="Periodization">historical periods</a> of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">late antiquity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Alemanni#Conflicts_with_the_Roman_Empire" title="Alemanni">Alemanni § Conflicts with the Roman Empire</a></div>
<p>The situation of the Roman Empire became dire in 235 AD, when emperor Alexander Severus was murdered by his own troops. Many <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legions</a> had been defeated during a campaign against <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a> raiding across the borders, while the emperor was focused primarily on the dangers from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanid_Empire" title="Sassanid Empire">Sassanid Persian Empire</a>. Leading his troops personally, Alexander Severus resorted to diplomacy and paying tribute in an attempt to pacify the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_chieftain" title="Germanic chieftain">Germanic chieftains</a> quickly. According to <a href="/wiki/Herodian" title="Herodian">Herodian</a> this cost him the respect of his troops, who may have felt they should be punishing the tribes who were intruding on Rome's territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the years following the emperor's death, generals of the Roman army fought each other for control of the Empire and neglected their duties of defending the empire from invasion. Provincials became victims of frequent raids along the length of the Rhine and Danube rivers by such foreign tribes as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpians" title="Carpians">Carpians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>, and attacks from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanids" title="Sassanids">Sassanids</a> in the east. Climate changes and a rise in sea levels ruined the agriculture of what is now the Low Countries forcing tribes to migrate; popular figures at the time blamed human activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Additionally, in 251, the <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Cyprian" title="Plague of Cyprian">Plague of Cyprian</a> (possibly <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>) broke out, causing large-scale death, possibly weakened the ability of the Empire to defend itself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the loss of <a href="/wiki/Valerian_(emperor)" title="Valerian (emperor)">Valerian</a> in 260, the Roman Empire was beset by usurpers, who broke it up into three competing states. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> broke off to form the <a href="/wiki/Gallic_Empire" title="Gallic Empire">Gallic Empire</a>. After the death of Odaenathus in 267, the eastern provinces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syria_(Roman_province)" title="Syria (Roman province)">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegyptus_(Roman_province)" title="Aegyptus (Roman province)">Aegyptus</a> became independent as the <a href="/wiki/Palmyrene_Empire" title="Palmyrene Empire">Palmyrene Empire</a>, leaving the remaining Italian-centered Roman Empire-proper in the middle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An invasion by a vast host of Goths was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Naissus" title="Battle of Naissus">Battle of Naissus</a> in 268 or 269. This victory was significant as the turning point of the crisis, when a series of tough, energetic soldier-emperors took power. Victories by the emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_II" title="Claudius II">Claudius II Gothicus</a> over the next two years drove back the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> and recovered Hispania from the Gallic Empire. When Claudius died in 270 of the plague, <a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a>, who had commanded the cavalry at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naissus" title="Naissus">Naissus</a>, succeeded him as the emperor and continued the restoration of the Empire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Aurelian reigned (270–275) through the worst of the crisis, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Palmyrene_Empire" title="Palmyrene Empire">Palmyrenes</a>, the Persians, and then the remainder of the Gallic Empire. By late 274, the Roman Empire was reunited into a single entity, and the frontier troops were back in place. More than a century would pass before Rome again lost military ascendancy over its external enemies. However, dozens of formerly thriving cities, especially in the Western Empire, had been ruined, their populations dispersed and, with the breakdown of the economic system, could not be rebuilt. Major cities and towns, even Rome itself, had not needed fortifications for many centuries; many then surrounded themselves with thick walls.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Finally, although Aurelian had played a significant role in restoring the Empire's borders from external threat, more fundamental problems remained. In particular, the right of succession had never been clearly defined in the Roman Empire, leading to continuous civil wars as competing factions in the military, Senate, and other parties put forward their favoured candidate for emperor. Another issue was the sheer size of the Empire, which made it difficult for a single autocratic ruler to effectively manage multiple threats at the same time. These continuing problems would be radically addressed by <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>, allowing the Empire to continue to survive in the West for over a century and in the East for over a millennium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynasties">Dynasties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crisis_of_the_Third_Century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dynasties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several emperors who rose to power through acclamation of their troops attempted to create stability by appointing their descendants as <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)" title="Caesar (title)">Caesar</a></i>, resulting in several brief dynasties. These generally failed to maintain any form of coherence beyond one generation, although there were exceptions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact">Economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crisis_of_the_Third_Century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diocletian_bust.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1085" data-file-width="870" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Diocletian_bust.png/200px-Diocletian_bust.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Diocletian_bust.png/300px-Diocletian_bust.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Diocletian_bust.png/400px-Diocletian_bust.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diocletian_bust.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emperor <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>. With his rise to power in 284, the Crisis of the Third Century ended and gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">Tetrarchy</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Internally, the empire faced <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a> caused by years of coinage devaluation. This had started earlier under the <a href="/wiki/Severan_dynasty" title="Severan dynasty">Severan emperors</a> who enlarged the army by one quarter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and doubled the legionaries' base pay. As each of the short-lived emperors took power, he needed ways to raise money quickly to pay the military's "accession bonus" and the easiest way to do so was by inflating the coinage severely, a process made possible by debasing the coinage with bronze and copper.</p>
<p>This resulted in runaway rises in prices, and by the time Diocletian came to power, the old coinage of the Roman Empire had nearly collapsed. Some taxes were collected in kind and values were often notional in <a href="/wiki/Bullion" title="Bullion">bullion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> coinage. <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value" title="Real versus nominal value">Real values</a> continued to be figured in gold coinage, but the silver coin, the denarius, used for 300 years, was gone (1 pound of gold = 40 gold <a href="/wiki/Aureus" title="Aureus">aurei</a> = 1000 <a href="/wiki/Denarius" title="Denarius">denarii</a> = 4000 <a href="/wiki/Sestertius" title="Sestertius">sestertii</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This currency had almost no value by the end of the third century, and trade was carried out without retail coinage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakdown_of_internal_trade_network">Breakdown of internal trade network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crisis_of_the_Third_Century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breakdown of internal trade network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most profound and lasting effects of the Crisis of the Third Century was the disruption of Rome's extensive internal trade network. Ever since the <a href="/wiki/Pax_Romana" title="Pax Romana">Pax Romana</a>, starting with <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, the empire's economy had depended in large part on trade between Mediterranean ports and across the extensive road systems to the Empire's interior. Merchants could travel from one end of the empire to the other in relative safety within a few weeks, moving agricultural goods produced in the provinces to the cities, and manufactured goods produced by the great cities of the East to the more rural provinces.</p>
<p>Large estates produced cash crops for export, and used the resulting revenues to import food and urban manufactured goods. This resulted in a great deal of economic interdependence among the empire’s inhabitants. The historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moss&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Moss (page does not exist)">Henry Moss</a> describes the situation as it stood before the crisis:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Along these roads passed an ever-increasing traffic, not only of troops and officials, but of traders, merchandise and even tourists. An interchange of goods between the various provinces rapidly developed, which soon reached a scale unprecedented in previous history and not repeated until a few centuries ago. Metals mined in the uplands of Western Europe, hides, fleeces, and livestock from the pastoral districts of Britain, Spain, and the shores of the Black Sea, wine and oil from Provence and Aquitaine, timber, pitch and wax from South Russia and northern Anatolia, <a href="/wiki/Dried_fruit" title="Dried fruit">dried fruits</a> from Syria, marble from the Aegean coasts, and – most important of all – grain from the wheat-growing districts of North Africa, Egypt, and the Danube Valley for the needs of the great cities; all these commodities, under the influence of a highly organized system of transport and marketing, moved freely from one corner of the Empire to the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>With the onset of the Crisis of the Third Century, however, this vast internal trade network broke down. The widespread civil unrest made it no longer safe for merchants to travel as they once had, and the financial crisis that struck made exchange very difficult with the debased currency. This produced profound changes that, in many ways, foreshadowed the very decentralized economic character of the coming <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.</p>
<p>Large landowners, no longer able to successfully export their crops over long distances, began producing food for subsistence and local barter. Rather than import manufactured goods from the empire's great urban areas, they began to manufacture many goods locally, often on their own estates, thus beginning the self-sufficient "house economy" that would become commonplace in later centuries, reaching its final form in the Middle Ages' <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorialism</a>. The common free people of the Roman cities, meanwhile, began to move out into the countryside in search of food and better protection.</p>
<p>Made desperate by economic necessity, many of these former city dwellers, as well as many small farmers, were forced to give up hard-earned basic civil rights in order to receive protection from large land-holders. In doing so, they became a half-free class of Roman citizen known as <i><a href="/wiki/Colonus_(person)" title="Colonus (person)">coloni</a></i>. They were tied to the land, and in later Imperial law their status was made hereditary. This provided an early model for <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a>, the origins of medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feudal_society" title="Feudal society">feudal society</a> and of the medieval peasantry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_localism">Increased localism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crisis_of_the_Third_Century&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Increased localism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even the Roman cities themselves began to change in character. The large, open cities of classical antiquity slowly gave way to the smaller, walled cities that became common in the Middle Ages. These changes were not restricted to the third century, but took place slowly over a long period, and were punctuated with many temporary reversals. However, in spite of extensive reforms by later emperors, the Roman trade network was never able to fully recover to what it had been during the Pax Romana (27 BC – AD 180) of the first century AD. This economic decline was far more noticeable and important in the western part of the empire, which was also invaded several times during the century. Hence, the balance of power clearly shifted eastward during this period, as evidenced by the choice of Diocletian to rule from <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, putting his second in command <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">Maximian</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. This would have considerable impact on the later development of the empire with a richer, more stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">eastern empire</a> surviving the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">end of Roman rule in the west</a>.</p>
<p>While Imperial revenues fell, Imperial expenses rose sharply. More soldiers, greater proportions of cavalry, and the ruinous expense of walling in cities all added to the toll. Goods and services previously paid for by the government were now demanded in addition to monetary taxes. The steady exodus of both rich and poor from the cities and now-unprofitable professions forced <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> to use compulsion; most trades were made hereditary, and workers could not legally leave their jobs or travel elsewhere to seek better-paying ones.</p>
<p>The decline in commerce between the Imperial provinces put them on a path towards increased self-sufficiency. Large landowners, who had become more self-sufficient, became less mindful of Rome’s central authority, particularly in the Western Empire, and were downright hostile towards its tax collectors. The measure of wealth at this time began to have less to do with wielding urban civil authority and more to do with controlling large agricultural estates in rural regions, since this guaranteed access to the only economic resource of real value — agricultural land and the crops it produced. The common people of the Empire lost economic and political status to the land-holding nobility, and the commercial middle classes waned along with their trade-derived livelihoods. The Crisis of the Third Century thus marked the beginning of a long gradual process that would transform the ancient world of Classical antiquity into the medieval one of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>.</p>
