<p><i><b>The Prince</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it"><i>Il Principe</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[il ˈprintʃipe]</a></span>) is a 16th-century political <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">treatise</a>, by the Italian diplomat and <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political theorist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a>. From correspondence a version appears to have been distributed in 1513, using a Latin title, <i>De Principatibus</i> (<i>About Principalities</i>). However, the printed version was not published until 1532, five years after Machiavelli's death. This was done with the permission of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_VII" title="Clement VII">Clement VII</a>, but "long before then, in fact since the first appearance of <i>The Prince</i> in manuscript, controversy had swirled about his writings".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although it was written as if it were a traditional work in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mirrors_for_princes" title="Mirrors for princes">mirrors for princes</a></i> style, it is generally agreed that it was especially innovative. This is only partly because it was written in the vernacular Italian rather than Latin, a practice which had become increasingly popular since the publication of Dante's <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i> and other works of Renaissance literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITALIAN_VERNACULAR_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITALIAN_VERNACULAR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Prince</i> is sometimes claimed to be one of the first works of <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, especially modern <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>, in which the effective truth is taken to be more important than any abstract ideal. It was also in direct conflict with the dominant Catholic and <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholastic</a> doctrines of the time concerning politics and ethics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-counter_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-counter-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although it is relatively short, the treatise is the most remembered of Machiavelli's works and the one most responsible for bringing the word "<a href="/wiki/Machiavellianism" title="Machiavellianism">Machiavellian</a>" into usage as a pejorative. It even contributed to the modern negative connotations of the words "politics" and "politician" in western countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In terms of subject matter it overlaps with the much longer <i><a href="/wiki/Discourses_on_Livy" title="Discourses on Livy">Discourses on Livy</a></i>, which was written a few years later. In its use of near-contemporary Italians as examples of people who perpetrated criminal deeds for politics, another lesser-known work by Machiavelli which <i>The Prince</i> has been compared to is the <i><a href="/wiki/Life_of_Castruccio_Castracani" title="Life of Castruccio Castracani">Life of Castruccio Castracani</a></i>.</p>
<p>The descriptions within <i>The Prince</i> have the general theme of accepting that the aims of princes—such as glory and survival—can justify the use of immoral means to achieve those ends:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He who neglects what is done for what ought to be done, sooner effects his ruin than his preservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work has a recognizable structure, for the most part indicated by the author himself, which can be summarized as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_subject_matter:_New_Princedoms_.28Chapters_1_.26_2.29">The subject matter: New Princedoms (Chapters 1 &amp; 2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The subject matter: New Princedoms (Chapters 1 &amp; 2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Prince</i> starts by describing the subject matter it will handle. In the first sentence Machiavelli uses the word "<a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>" (Italian <i>stato</i> which could also mean "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/status" title="wikt:status">status</a>") in order to neutrally cover "all forms of organization of supreme political power, whether republican or princely". The way in which the word state came to acquire this modern type of meaning during the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> has been the subject of many academic discussions, with this sentence and similar ones in the works of Machiavelli being considered particularly important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Machiavelli said that <i>The Prince</i> would be about princedoms, mentioning that he has written about republics elsewhere (possibly referring to the <i><a href="/wiki/Discourses_on_Livy" title="Discourses on Livy">Discourses on Livy</a></i> although this is debated), but in fact he mixes discussion of republics into this in many places, effectively treating republics as a type of princedom also, and one with many strengths. More importantly, and less traditionally, he distinguishes new princedoms from hereditary established princedoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ch1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ch1-12">[12]</a></sup> He deals with hereditary princedoms quickly in Chapter 2, saying that they are much easier to rule. For such a prince, "unless extraordinary vices cause him to be hated, it is reasonable to expect that his subjects will be naturally well disposed towards him".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ch2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ch2-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="#CITEREFGilbert1938">Gilbert (1938</a>:19–23), comparing this to traditional presentations of advice for princes, wrote that the novelty in chapters 1 and 2 is the "deliberate purpose of dealing with a new ruler who will need to establish himself in defiance of custom". Normally, these types of works were addressed only to hereditary princes. He thinks Machiavelli may have been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> as well as his own experience, but finds no clear predecessor for this.</p>
<p>This categorization of regime types is also "un-Aristotelian"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and apparently simpler than the traditional one found for example in <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristotle_(Politics)" title="Aristotle (Politics)">Politics</a></i>, which divides <a href="/wiki/Regime" title="Regime">regimes</a> into those ruled by a single monarch, an <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a>, or by the people, in a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He also ignores the classical distinctions between the good and corrupt forms, for example between monarchy and tyranny.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a>, on the other hand, made exactly the same distinction between types of rulers in the beginning of his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Education_of_Cyrus" title="Education of Cyrus">Education of Cyrus</a></i> where he says that, concerning the knowledge of how to rule human beings, <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>, his exemplary prince, was very different "from all other kings, both those who have inherited their thrones from their fathers and those who have gained their crowns by their own efforts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Machiavelli divides the subject of new states into two types, "mixed" cases and purely new states.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Mixed.22_princedoms_.28chapters_3.E2.80.935.29">"Mixed" princedoms (chapters 3–5)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "Mixed" princedoms (chapters 3–5)'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New princedoms are either totally new, or they are “mixed” meaning that they are new parts of an older state, already belonging to that prince.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ch3_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ch3-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_conquests_added_to_older_states_.28chapter_3.29">New conquests added to older states (chapter 3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: New conquests added to older states (chapter 3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Machiavelli generalizes that there were several virtuous Roman ways to hold a newly acquired province, using a republic as an example of how new princes can act:</p>
<ul>
<li>to install one's princedom in the new acquisition, or to install colonies of one's people there, which is better.</li>
<li>to indulge the lesser powers of the area without increasing their power.</li>
<li>to put down the powerful people.</li>
<li>not to allow a foreign power to gain reputation.</li>
</ul>
<p>More generally, Machiavelli emphasizes that one should have regard not only for present problems but also for the future ones. One should not “enjoy the benefit of time” but rather the benefit of one's virtue and prudence, because time can bring evil as well as good.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conquered_kingdoms_.28chapter_4.29">Conquered kingdoms (chapter 4)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conquered kingdoms (chapter 4)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

A 16th-century Italian impression of the family of <a href="/wiki/Darius_III" title="Darius III">Darius III</a>, emperor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>, before their conqueror, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>. Machiavelli explained that in his time the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> was again ruled by an empire, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, with similar characteristics to that of Darius – seen from the viewpoint of a potential conqueror.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In some cases the old king of the conquered kingdom depended on his lords. 16th century France, or in other words France as it was at the time of writing of <i>The Prince</i>, is given by Machiavelli as an example of such a kingdom. These are easy to enter but difficult to hold.</p>
<p>When the kingdom revolves around the king, then it is difficult to enter but easy to hold. The solution is to eliminate the old bloodline of the prince. Machiavelli used the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian empire</a> of <a href="/wiki/Darius_III" title="Darius III">Darius III</a>, conquered by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, to illustrate this point and then noted that the Medici, if they think about it, will find this historical example similar to the "kingdom of the Turk" (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>) in their time – making this a potentially easier conquest to hold than France would be.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conquered_Free_States.2C_with_their_own_laws_and_orders_.28Chapter_5.29">Conquered Free States, with their own laws and orders (Chapter 5)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conquered Free States, with their own laws and orders (Chapter 5)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="#CITEREFGilbert1938">Gilbert (1938</a>:34) notes that this chapter is quite atypical of any previous books for princes. Gilbert supposed the need to discuss conquering free republics is linked to Machiavelli's project to unite Italy, which contained some free republics. As he also notes, the chapter in any case makes it clear that holding such a state is highly difficult for a prince. Machiavelli gives three options:-</p>
<ul>
<li>Ruin them, as Rome destroyed <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, and also as Machiavelli says the Romans eventually had to do in Greece, even though they had wanted to avoid it.</li>
<li>Go to live there (or install colonies, if you are a prince of a republic).</li>
<li>Let them keep their own orders but install a puppet regime.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Totally_New_States_.28Chapters_6.E2.80.939.29">Totally New States (Chapters 6–9)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Totally New States (Chapters 6–9)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conquests_by_virtue_.28Chapter_6.29">Conquests by virtue (Chapter 6)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conquests by virtue (Chapter 6)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moses_dore.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="531" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Moses_dore.jpg/220px-Moses_dore.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Moses_dore.jpg/330px-Moses_dore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Moses_dore.jpg/440px-Moses_dore.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moses_dore.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Machiavelli described <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> as a conquering prince, who founded new modes and orders by force of arms, which he used willingly to kill many of his own people. The Bible describes the reasons behind his success differently.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Princes who rise to power through their own skill and resources (their "virtue") rather than luck tend to have a hard time rising to the top, but once they reach the top they are very secure in their position. This is because they effectively crush their opponents and earn great respect from everyone else. Because they are strong and more self-sufficient, they have to make fewer compromises with their allies.</p>
<p>Machiavelli writes that reforming an existing order is one of the most dangerous and difficult things a prince can do. Part of the reason is that people are naturally resistant to change and reform. Those who benefited from the old order will resist change very fiercely. By contrast, those who can benefit from the new order will be less fierce in their support, because the new order is unfamiliar and they are not certain it will live up to its promises. Moreover, it is impossible for the prince to satisfy everybody's expectations. Inevitably, he will disappoint some of his followers. Therefore, a prince must have the means to force his supporters to keep supporting him even when they start having second thoughts, otherwise he will lose his power. Only armed prophets, like Moses, succeed in bringing lasting change. Machiavelli claims that Moses killed uncountable numbers of his own people in order to enforce his will.</p>
<p>Machiavelli was not the first thinker to notice this pattern. Allan Gilbert wrote: "In wishing new laws and yet seeing danger in them Machiavelli was not himself an innovator,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> because this idea was traditional and could be found in <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s writings. But Machiavelli went much further than any other author in his emphasis on this aim, and Gilbert associates Machiavelli's emphasis upon such drastic aims with the level of corruption to be found in Italy.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conquest_by_fortune.2C_meaning_by_someone_else.E2.80.99s_virtue_.28Chapter_7.29">Conquest by fortune, meaning by someone else’s virtue (Chapter 7)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conquest by fortune, meaning by someone else’s virtue (Chapter 7)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to Machiavelli, when a prince comes to power through luck or the blessings of powerful figures within the regime, he typically has an easy time gaining power but a hard time keeping it thereafter, because his power is dependent on his benefactors' goodwill. He does not command the loyalty of the armies and officials that maintain his authority, and these can be withdrawn from him at a whim. Having risen the easy way, it is not even certain such a prince has the skill and strength to stand on his own feet.</p>
<p>This is not necessarily true in every case. Machiavelli cites <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Borgia" title="Cesare Borgia">Cesare Borgia</a> as an example of a lucky prince who escaped this pattern. Through cunning political maneuvers, he managed to secure his power base. Cesare was made commander of the papal armies by his father, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>, but was also heavily dependent on mercenary armies loyal to the Orsini brothers and the support of the French king. Borgia won over the allegiance of the Orsini's followers with better pay and prestigious government posts. When some of his mercenary captains started to plot against him, he had them imprisoned and executed. When it looked as though the king of France would abandon him, Borgia sought new alliances.</p>
<p>Finally, Machiavelli makes a point that bringing new benefits to a conquered people will not be enough to cancel the memory of old injuries, an idea Allan Gilbert said can be found in <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca the Younger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conquests_by_.E2.80.9Ccriminal_virtue.E2.80.9D_.28Chapter_8.29">Conquests by “criminal virtue” (Chapter 8)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conquests by “criminal virtue” (Chapter 8)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Conquests by "criminal virtue" are ones in which the new prince secures his power through cruel, immoral deeds, such as the execution of political rivals. Machiavelli advises that a prince should carefully calculate all the wicked deeds he needs to do to secure his power, and then execute them all in one stroke, such that he need not commit any more wickedness for the rest of his reign. In this way, his subjects will slowly forget his cruel deeds and his reputation can recover. Princes who fail to do this, who hesitate in their ruthlessness, find that their problems mushroom over time and they are forced to commit wicked deeds throughout their reign. Thus they continuously mar their reputations and alienate their people.</p>
<p>Machiavelli's case study is <a href="/wiki/Agathocles_of_Syracuse" title="Agathocles of Syracuse">Agathocles of Syracuse</a>. After Agathocles became Praetor of Syracuse, he called a meeting of the city's elite. At his signal, his soldiers killed all the senators and the wealthiest citizens, completely destroying the old oligarchy. He declared himself ruler with no opposition. So secure was his power that he could afford to absent himself to go off on military campaigns in Africa.</p>
<p>However, Machiavelli then strongly rebukes Agothocles, stating, "Yet one cannot call it virtue to kill one's citizens, betray one's friends, to be without faith, without mercy, without religion; these modes can enable one to acquire empire, but not glory. [...] Nonetheless, his savage cruelty and inhumanity, together with his infinite crimes, do not permit him to be celebrated among the most excellent men. Thus, one cannot attribute to fortune or virtue what he achieved without either."</p>
<p><a href="#CITEREFGilbert1938">Gilbert (1938</a>:51–55) remarks that this chapter is even less traditional than those it follows, not only in its treatment of criminal behavior, but also in the advice to take power from people at a stroke, noting that precisely the opposite had been advised by Aristotle in his <i><a href="/wiki/Politics_(Aristotle)" title="Politics (Aristotle)">Politics</a></i> (5.11.1315a13). On the other hand, Gilbert shows that another piece of advice in this chapter, to give benefits when it will not appear forced, was traditional.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming_a_prince_by_the_selection_of_one.27s_fellow_citizens_.28Chapter_9.29">Becoming a prince by the selection of one's fellow citizens (Chapter 9)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Becoming a prince by the selection of one's fellow citizens (Chapter 9)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These "civic principalities" do not require real virtue, only “fortunate astuteness”. Machiavelli breaks this case into two basic types, depending upon which section of the populace supports the new prince.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Supported_by_the_great_.28those_who_wish_to_command_the_people.29">Supported by the great (those who wish to command the people)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Supported by the great (those who wish to command the people)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>This, according to Machiavelli, is an unstable situation, which must be avoided after the initial coming to power. The great should be made and unmade every day at your convenience. There are two types of great people that might be encountered:</p>
<ol>
<li>Those who are bound to the prince. Concerning these it is important to distinguish between two types of obligated great people, those who are rapacious and those who are not. It is the latter who can and should be honoured.</li>
<li>Those who are not bound to the new prince. Once again these need to be divided into two types: those with a weak spirit (a prince can make use of them if they are of good counsel) and those who shun being bound because of their own ambition (these should be watched and feared as enemies).</li>
</ol>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Supported_by_the_people_.28those_who_wish_not_to_be_commanded_by_the_great.29">Supported by the people (those who wish not to be commanded by the great)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Supported by the people (those who wish not to be commanded by the great)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>How to win over people depends on circumstances. Machiavelli advises:</p>
<ul>
<li>Do not get frightened in adversity.</li>
<li>One should avoid ruling via magistrates, if one wishes to be able to “ascend” to absolute rule quickly and safely.</li>
<li>One should make sure that the people need the prince, especially if a time of need should come.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="How_to_judge_the_strength_of_principalities_.28Chapter_10.29">How to judge the strength of principalities (Chapter 10)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: How to judge the strength of principalities (Chapter 10)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The way to judge the strength of a princedom is to see whether it can defend itself, or whether it needs to depend on allies. This does not just mean that the cities should be prepared and the people trained; a prince who is hated is also exposed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_principates_.28Chapter_11.29">Ecclesiastical principates (Chapter 11)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical principates (Chapter 11)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="360" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg/220px-Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg/330px-Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_X" title="Leo X">Leo X</a>: a pope, but also a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">Medici family</a>. Machiavelli suggested they should treat the church as a princedom, as the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Borgia" title="House of Borgia">Borgia family</a> had, in order to conquer Italy, and found new modes and orders.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This type of "princedom" refers for example explicitly to the Catholic church, which is of course not traditionally thought of as a princedom. According to Machiavelli, these are relatively easy to maintain, once founded. They do not need to defend themselves militarily, nor to govern their subjects.</p>
<p>Machiavelli discusses the recent history of the Church as if it were a princedom that was in competition to conquer Italy against other princes. He points to factionalism as a historical weak point in the Church, and points to the recent example of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Borgia" title="House of Borgia">Borgia</a> family as a better strategy which almost worked. He then explicitly proposes that the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">Medici</a> are now in a position to try the same thing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_and_military_.28Chapter_12.E2.80.9314.29">Defense and military (Chapter 12–14)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Defense and military (Chapter 12–14)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Having discussed the various types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principalities" title="Principalities">principalities</a>, Machiavelli turns to the ways a state can attack other territories or defend itself. The two most essential foundations for any state, whether old or new, are sound laws and strong military forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ch12_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ch12-20">[20]</a></sup> A self-sufficient prince is one who can meet any enemy on the battlefield. He should be "armed" with his own arms. However, a prince that relies solely on fortifications or on the help of others and stands on the defensive is not self-sufficient. If he cannot raise a formidable army, but must rely on defense, he must fortify his city. A well-fortified city is unlikely to be attacked, and if it is, most armies cannot endure an extended siege. However, during a siege a virtuous prince will keep the morale of his subjects high while removing all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissenters" title="Dissenters">dissenters</a>. Thus, as long as the city is properly defended and has enough supplies, a wise prince can withstand any siege.</p>
<p>Machiavelli stands strongly against the use of <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenaries</a>, and in this he was innovative, and he also had personal experience in Florence. He believes they are useless to a ruler because they are undisciplined, cowardly, and without any loyalty, being motivated only by money. Machiavelli attributes the Italian city states’ weakness to their reliance on mercenary armies.</p>
<p>Machiavelli also warns against using auxiliary forces, troops borrowed from an ally, because if they win, the employer is under their favor and if they lose, he is ruined. Auxiliary forces are more dangerous than mercenary forces because they are united and controlled by capable leaders who may turn against the employer.</p>
<p>The main concern for a prince should be war, or the preparation thereof, not books. Through war a hereditary prince maintains his power or a private citizen rises to power. Machiavelli advises that a prince must frequently hunt in order to keep his body fit and learn the landscape surrounding his kingdom. Through this, he can best learn how to protect his territory and advance upon others. For intellectual strength, he is advised to study great military men so he may imitate their successes and avoid their mistakes. A prince who is diligent in times of peace will be ready in times of adversity. Machiavelli writes, “thus, when fortune turns against him he will be prepared to resist it.”</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Qualities_of_a_Prince_.28Chapters_14.E2.80.9319.29">The Qualities of a Prince (Chapters 14–19)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The Qualities of a Prince (Chapters 14–19)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each of the following chapters presents a discussion about a particular virtue or vice that a prince might have, and is therefore structured in a way which appears like traditional advice for a prince. However, the advice is far from traditional.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Prince.27s_Duty_Concerning_Military_Matters_.28Chapter_14.29">A Prince's Duty Concerning Military Matters (Chapter 14)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: A Prince's Duty Concerning Military Matters (Chapter 14)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Machiavelli believes that a prince's main focus should be on perfecting the art of war. He believes that by taking this profession a ruler will be able to protect his kingdom. He claims that "being disarmed makes you despised." He believes that the only way to ensure loyalty from one's soldiers is to understand military matters. The two activities Machiavelli recommends practicing to prepare for war are physical and mental. Physically, he believes rulers should learn the landscape of their territories. Mentally, he encouraged the study of past military events. He also warns against idleness.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reputation_of_a_prince_.28Chapter_15.29">Reputation of a prince (Chapter 15)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Reputation of a prince (Chapter 15)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Because, says Machiavelli, he wants to write something useful to those who understand, he thought it more fitting "to go directly to the effectual truth ("<i>verità effettuale</i>") of the thing than to the imagination of it". This section is one where Machiavelli’s pragmatic ideal can be seen most clearly. The prince should, ideally, be virtuous, but he should be willing and able to abandon those virtues if it becomes necessary. Concerning the behavior of a prince toward his subjects, Machiavelli announces that he will depart from what other writers say, and writes:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Men have imagined republics and principalities that never really existed at all. Yet the way men live is so far removed from the way they ought to live that anyone who abandons what is for what should be pursues his downfall rather than his preservation; for a man who strives after goodness in all his acts is sure to come to ruin, since there are so many men who are not good.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Since there are many possible qualities that a prince can be said to possess, he must not be overly concerned about having all the good ones. Also, a prince may be perceived to be merciful, faithful, humane, frank, and religious, but most important is only to <i>seem</i> to have these qualities. A <i>prince</i> cannot truly have these qualities because at times it is <i>necessary</i> to act against them. In fact, he must sometimes deliberately choose evil. Although a bad reputation should be avoided, it is sometimes necessary to have one.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Generosity_vs._parsimony_.28Chapter_16.29">Generosity vs. parsimony (Chapter 16)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Generosity vs. parsimony (Chapter 16)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>If a prince is overly generous to his subjects, Machiavelli asserts he will not be appreciated, and will only cause greed for more. Additionally, being overly generous is not economical, because eventually all resources will be exhausted. This results in higher taxes, and will bring grief upon the prince. Then, if he decides to discontinue or limit his generosity, he will be labeled as a miser. Thus, Machiavelli summarizes that guarding against the people’s hatred is more important than building up a reputation for generosity. A wise prince should be willing to be more reputed a miser than be hated for trying to be too generous.</p>
<p>On the other hand: "of what is not yours or your subjects' one can be a bigger giver, as were <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Caesar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a>, because spending what is someone else's does not take reputation from you but adds it to you; only spending your own hurts you".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cruelty_vs._Mercy_.28Chapter_17.29">Cruelty vs. Mercy (Chapter 17)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cruelty vs. Mercy (Chapter 17)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Young_Folks%27_History_of_Rome_illus174.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="284" data-file-width="383" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Young_Folks%27_History_of_Rome_illus174.png/300px-Young_Folks%27_History_of_Rome_illus174.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/95/Young_Folks%27_History_of_Rome_illus174.png 1.5x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Young_Folks%27_History_of_Rome_illus174.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> meeting <a href="/wiki/Scipio_Africanus" title="Scipio Africanus">Scipio Africanus</a>. Machiavelli describes Hannibal as having the "<a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtue</a>" of "inhuman cruelty". But he lost to someone, <a href="/wiki/Scipio_Africanus" title="Scipio Africanus">Scipio Africanus</a>, who showed the weakness of "excessive mercy" and who could therefore only have held power in a republic.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addressing the question of whether it is better to be loved or feared, Machiavelli writes, “The answer is that one would like to be both the one and the other; but because it is difficult to combine them, it is far safer to be feared than loved if you cannot be both.” As Machiavelli asserts, commitments made in peace are not always kept in adversity; however, commitments made in fear are kept out of fear. Yet, a prince must ensure that he is not feared to the point of hatred, which is very possible.</p>
<p>This chapter is possibly the most well-known of the work, and it is important because of the reasoning behind Machiavelli’s famous idea that it is better to be feared than loved<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> – his justification is purely pragmatic; as he notes, “Men worry less about doing an injury to one who makes himself loved than to one who makes himself feared.” Fear is simply a means to an end, and that end is security for the prince. The fear instilled should never be excessive, for that could be dangerous to the prince. Above all, Machiavelli argues, a prince should not interfere with the property of their subjects, their women, or the life of somebody without proper justification.</p>
<p>Regarding the troops of the prince, fear is absolutely necessary to keep a large garrison united and a prince should not mind the thought of cruelty in that regard. For a prince who leads his own army, it is imperative for him to observe cruelty because that is the only way he can command his soldiers' absolute respect. Machiavelli compares two great military leaders: <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scipio_Africanus" title="Scipio Africanus">Scipio Africanus</a>. Although Hannibal's army consisted of men of various races, they were never rebellious because they feared their leader. Machiavelli says this required "inhuman cruelty" which he refers to as a virtue. Scipio's men, on the other hand, were known for their mutiny and dissension, due to Scipio's "excessive mercy" – which was however a source of glory because he lived in a republic.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_what_way_princes_should_keep_their_word_.28Chapter_18.29">In what way princes should keep their word (Chapter 18)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In what way princes should keep their word (Chapter 18)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Machiavelli notes that a prince is praised for keeping his word. However, he also notes that a prince is also praised for the illusion of being reliable in keeping his word. A prince, therefore, should only keep his word when it suits his purposes, but do his utmost to maintain the illusion that he does keep his word and that he is reliable in that regard. Therefore, a prince should not break his word unnecessarily.</p>
<p>As Machiavelli notes, “He should appear to be compassionate, faithful to his word, guileless, and devout. And indeed he should be so. But his disposition should be such that, if he needs to be the opposite, he knows how.” As noted in chapter 15, the prince must appear to be virtuous, and should be virtuous, but he should be able to be otherwise when the time calls for it; that includes being able to lie, though however much he lies he should always keep the appearance of being truthful.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Avoiding_contempt_and_hatred_.28Chapter_19.29">Avoiding contempt and hatred (Chapter 19)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Avoiding contempt and hatred (Chapter 19)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Machiavelli observes that most men are content as long as they are not deprived of their property and women. A prince should command respect through his conduct, because a prince that is highly respected by his people is unlikely to face internal struggles. Additionally, a prince who does not raise the contempt of the nobles and keeps the people satisfied, Machiavelli assures, should have no fear of conspirators. Machiavelli advises monarchs to have both internal and external fears. Internal fears exist inside his kingdom and focus on his subjects, Machiavelli warns to be suspicious of everyone when hostile attitudes emerge. External fears are of foreign powers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Prudence_of_the_Prince_.28Chapters_20.E2.80.9325.29">The Prudence of the Prince (Chapters 20–25)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: The Prudence of the Prince (Chapters 20–25)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Whether_ruling_conquests_with_fortresses_works_.28Chapter_20.29">Whether ruling conquests with fortresses works (Chapter 20)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Whether ruling conquests with fortresses works (Chapter 20)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Machiavelli mentions that placing fortresses in conquered territories, although it sometimes works, often fails. Using fortresses can be a good plan, but Machiavelli says he shall "blame anyone who, trusting in fortresses, thinks little of being hated by the people".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaining_honors_.28Chapter_21.29">Gaining honors (Chapter 21)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Gaining honors (Chapter 21)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A prince truly earns honor by completing great feats. <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">King Ferdinand of Spain</a> is cited by Machiavelli as an example of a monarch who gained esteem by showing his ability through great feats and who, in the name of religion, conquered many territories and kept his subjects occupied so that they had no chance to rebel. Regarding two warring states, Machiavelli asserts it is always wiser to choose a side, rather than to be neutral. Machiavelli then provides the following reasons why:</p>
<ul>
<li>If your allies win, you benefit whether or not you have more power than they have.</li>
<li>If you are more powerful, then your allies are under your command; if your allies are stronger, they will always feel a certain obligation to you for your help.</li>
<li>If your side loses, you still have an ally in the loser.</li>
</ul>
<p>Machiavelli also notes that it is wise for a prince not to ally with a stronger force unless compelled to do so. In conclusion, the most important virtue is having the wisdom to discern what ventures will come with the most reward and then pursuing them courageously.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nobles_and_staff_.28Chapter_22.29">Nobles and staff (Chapter 22)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Nobles and staff (Chapter 22)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The selection of good servants is reflected directly upon the prince’s intelligence, so if they are loyal, the prince is considered wise; however, when they are otherwise, the prince is open to adverse criticism. Machiavelli asserts that there are three types of intelligence:</p>
<ul>
<li>The kind that understands things for itself—which is excellent to have.</li>
<li>The kind that understands what others can understand—which is good to have.</li>
<li>The kind that does not understand for itself, nor through others—which is useless to have.</li>
</ul>
<p>If the prince does not have the first type of intelligence, he should at the very least have the second type. For, as Machiavelli states, “A prince needs to have the discernment to recognize the good or bad in what another says or does even though he has no acumen himself".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Avoiding_flatterers_.28Chapter_23.29">Avoiding flatterers (Chapter 23)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Avoiding flatterers (Chapter 23)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This chapter displays a low opinion of flatterers; Machiavelli notes that “Men are so happily absorbed in their own affairs and indulge in such self-deception that it is difficult for them not to fall victim to this plague; and some efforts to protect oneself from flatterers involve the risk of becoming despised.” Flatterers were seen as a great danger to a prince, because their flattery could cause him to avoid wise counsel in favor of rash action, but avoiding all advice, flattery or otherwise, was equally bad; a middle road had to be taken. A prudent prince should have a select group of wise counselors to advise him truthfully on matters all the time. All their opinions should be taken into account. Ultimately, the decision should be made by the counselors and carried out absolutely. If a prince is given to changing his mind, his reputation will suffer. A prince must have the wisdom to recognize good advice from bad. Machiavelli gives a negative example in <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Maximilian I</a>; Maximilian, who was secretive, never consulted others, but once he ordered his plans and met dissent, he immediately changed them.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prudence_and_chance">Prudence and chance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Prudence and chance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Why_the_princes_of_Italy_lost_their_states_.28Chapter_24.29">Why the princes of Italy lost their states (Chapter 24)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Why the princes of Italy lost their states (Chapter 24)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After first mentioning that a new prince can quickly become as respected as a hereditary one, Machiavelli says princes in Italy who had longstanding power and lost it cannot blame bad luck, but should blame their own indolence. One "should never fall in the belief that you can find someone to pick you up". They all showed a defect of arms (already discussed) and either had a hostile populace or did not know to secure themselves with the great.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortune_.28Chapter_25.29">Fortune (Chapter 25)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Fortune (Chapter 25)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As pointed out by <a href="#CITEREFGilbert1938">Gilbert (1938)</a>:206 it was traditional in the genre of Mirrors of Princes to mention fortune, but "Fortune pervades The Prince as she does no other similar work". Machiavelli argues that fortune is only the judge of half of our actions and that we have control over the other half with "sweat", prudence and virtue. Even more unusual, rather than simply suggesting caution as a prudent way to try to avoid the worst of bad luck, Machiavelli holds that the greatest princes in history tend to be ones who take more risks, and rise to power through their own labour, virtue, prudence, and particularly by their ability to adapt to changing circumstances.</p>
<p>Machiavelli even encourages risk taking as a reaction to risk. In a well-known metaphor, Machiavelli writes that "it is better to be impetuous than cautious, because fortune is a woman; and it is necessary, if one wants to hold her down, to beat her and strike her down."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ch25_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ch25-22">[22]</a></sup> Gilbert (p. 217) points out that Machiavelli's friend the historian and diplomat <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Guicciardini" title="Francesco Guicciardini">Francesco Guicciardini</a> expressed similar ideas about fortune.</p>
<p>Machiavelli compares fortune to a torrential river that cannot be easily controlled during flooding season. In periods of calm, however, people can erect dams and levees in order to minimize its impact. Fortune, Machiavelli argues, seems to strike at the places where no resistance is offered, as had recently been the case in Italy. As <a href="#CITEREFde_Alvarez1999">de Alvarez (1999</a>:125–130) points out that what Machiavelli actually says is that Italians in his time leave things not just to fortune, but to "fortune and God". Machiavelli is indicating in this passage, as in some others in his works, that Christianity itself was making Italians helpless and lazy concerning their own politics, as if they would leave dangerous rivers uncontrolled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhortation_to_Seize_Italy_and_to_Free_Her_from_the_Barbarians_.28Chapter_26.29">Exhortation to Seize Italy and to Free Her from the Barbarians (Chapter 26)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Exhortation to Seize Italy and to Free Her from the Barbarians (Chapter 26)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_X" title="Leo X">Leo X</a> was pope at the time the book was written and a member of the de Medici family. This chapter directly appeals to the Medici to use what has been summarized in order to conquer Italy using Italian armies, following the advice in the book. <a href="#CITEREFGilbert1938">Gilbert (1938</a>:222–230) showed that including such exhortation was not unusual in the genre of books full of advice for princes. But it is unusual that the Medici family's position of Papal power is openly named as something that should be used as a personal power base, as a tool of secular politics. Indeed, one example is the Borgia family's "recent" and controversial attempts to use church power in secular politics, often brutally executed. This continues a controversial theme throughout the book.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="536" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg/220px-Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg/330px-Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg/440px-Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anon-Cesare-Borgia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Cesare_Borgia" title="Cesare Borgia">Cesare Borgia</a>, Duke of Valentinois. According to Machiavelli, a risk taker and example of "criminal virtue." Failed in the end because of one mistake: he was naive to trust a new Pope.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As shown by his letter of dedication, Machiavelli's work eventually came to be dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_II_de%27_Medici,_Duke_of_Urbino" title="Lorenzo II de' Medici, Duke of Urbino">Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici</a>, grandson of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_the_Magnificent" title="Lorenzo the Magnificent">Lorenzo the Magnificent</a>", and a member of the ruling Florentine Medici family, whose uncle Giovanni became Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_X" title="Leo X">Leo X</a> in 1513. It is known from his personal correspondence that it was written during 1513, the year after the Medici took control of Florence, and a few months after Machiavelli's arrest, torture, and banishment by the in-coming Medici regime. It was discussed for a long time with <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Vettori" title="Francesco Vettori">Francesco Vettori</a> - a friend of Machiavelli - whom he wanted to pass it and commend it to the Medici. The book had originally been intended for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giuliano_di_Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Giuliano di Lorenzo de' Medici">Giuliano di Lorenzo de' Medici</a>, young Lorenzo's uncle, who however died in 1516.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It is not certain that the work was ever read by any of the Medici before it was printed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Machiavelli describes the contents as being an un-embellished summary of his knowledge about the nature of princes and "the actions of great men", based not only on reading but also, unusually, on real experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Let_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Let-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The types of political behavior which are discussed with apparent approval by Machiavelli in <i>The Prince</i> were regarded as shocking by contemporaries, and its immorality is still a subject of serious discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Although the work advises princes how to tyrannize, Machiavelli is generally thought to have preferred some form of free republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Some commentators justify his acceptance of immoral and criminal actions by leaders by arguing that he lived during a time of continuous political conflict and instability in Italy, and that his influence has increased the "pleasures, equality and freedom" of many people, loosening the grip of medieval Catholicism's "classical <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleology</a>", which "disregarded not only the needs of individuals and the wants of the common man, but stifled innovation, enterprise, and enquiry into cause and effect relationships that now allow us to control <a href="/wiki/Nature_(philosophy)" title="Nature (philosophy)">nature</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, <a href="#CITEREFStrauss1958">Strauss (1958</a>:11) notes that "even if we were forced to grant that Machiavelli was essentially a patriot or a scientist, we would not be forced to deny that he was a teacher of evil".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Furthermore, Machiavelli "was too thoughtful not to know what he was doing and too generous not to admit it to his reasonable friends".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Machiavelli emphasized the need for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Realism_in_international_relations_theory" title="Realism in international relations theory">realism</a>, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a>. Along with this, he stresses the difference between human-beings and animals since "there are two ways of contending, one in accordance with the laws, the other by force; the first of which is proper to men, the second to beast".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In <i>The Prince</i> he does not explain what he thinks the best ethical or political goals are, except the control of one's own fortune, as opposed to waiting to see what chance brings. Machiavelli took it for granted that would-be leaders naturally aim at glory or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honor" title="Honor">honor</a>. He associated these goals with a need for "<a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtue</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Prudence" title="Prudence">prudence</a>" in a leader, and saw such virtues as essential to good politics and indeed the common good. That great men should develop and use their virtue and prudence was a traditional theme of advice to Christian princes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> And that more virtue meant less reliance on chance was a classically influenced "<a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> commonplace" in Machiavelli's time, as <a href="#CITEREFFischer2000">Fischer (2000</a>:75) says, even if it was somewhat controversial. However, Machiavelli went far beyond other authors in his time, who in his opinion left things to fortune, and therefore to bad rulers, because of their Christian beliefs. He used the words "virtue" and "prudence" to refer to glory-seeking and spirited excellence of character, in strong contrast to the traditional Christian uses of those terms, but more keeping with the original pre-Christian Greek and Roman concepts from which they derived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He encouraged ambition and risk taking. So in another break with tradition, he treated not only stability, but also radical <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a>, as possible aims of a prince in a political community. Managing major reforms can show off a Prince's virtue and give him glory. He clearly felt Italy needed major reform in his time, and this opinion of his time is widely shared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Machiavelli's descriptions encourage leaders to attempt to control their fortune gloriously, to the extreme extent that some situations may call for a fresh "founding" (or re-founding) of the "modes and orders" that define a community, despite the danger and necessary evil and lawlessness of such a project. Founding a wholly new state, or even a new religion, using injustice and immorality has even been called the chief theme of <i>The Prince</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Machiavelli justifies this position by explaining how if "a prince did not win love he may escape hate" by personifying injustice and immorality;therefore, he will never loosen his grip since "fear is held by the apprehension of punishment" and never diminishes as time goes by. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> For a political theorist to do this in public was one of Machiavelli's clearest breaks not just with medieval scholasticism, but with the classical tradition of <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>, especially the favorite philosopher of Catholicism at the time, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. This is one of Machiavelli's most lasting influences upon <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, Machiavelli was heavily influenced by classical pre-Christian <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>. According to <a href="#CITEREFStrauss1958">Strauss (1958</a>:291) Machiavelli refers to <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a> more than Plato, Aristotle, and <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> put together. Xenophon wrote one of the classic mirrors of princes, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Education_of_Cyrus" title="Education of Cyrus">Education of Cyrus</a></i>. <a href="#CITEREFGilbert1938">Gilbert (1938</a>:236) wrote: "The Cyrus of Xenophon was a hero to many a literary man of the sixteenth century, but for Machiavelli he lived". Xenophon also, as Strauss pointed out, wrote a dialogue, <i><a href="/wiki/Hiero_(Xenophon)" title="Hiero (Xenophon)">Hiero</a></i> which showed a wise man dealing sympathetically with a tyrant, coming close to what Machiavelli would do in questioning the ideal of "the imagined prince". Xenophon however, like Plato and Aristotle, was a follower of <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>, and his works show approval of a "<a href="/wiki/Teleological_argument" title="Teleological argument">teleological argument</a>", while Machiavelli rejected such arguments. On this matter, <a href="#CITEREFStrauss1958">Strauss (1958</a>:222–223) gives evidence that Machiavelli may have seen himself as having learned something from <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atomism" title="Atomism">classical materialism</a>, which was however not associated with political realism, or even any interest in politics.</p>
<p>On the topic of <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a> Machiavelli, in his introduction, stated that “I have not embellished or crammed this book with rounded periods or big, impressive words, or with any blandishment or superfluous decoration of the kind which many are in the habit of using to describe or adorn what they have produced”. This has been interpreted as showing a distancing from traditional rhetoric styles, but there are echoes of classical rhetoric in several areas. In Chapter 18, for example, he uses a metaphor of a lion and a fox, examples of cunning and force; according to <a href="#CITEREFZerba2004">Zerba (2004</a>:217), “the Roman author from whom Machiavelli in all likelihood drew the simile of the lion and the fox” was Cicero. The <i><a href="/wiki/Rhetorica_ad_Herennium" title="Rhetorica ad Herennium">Rhetorica ad Herennium</a></i>, a work which was believed during Machiavelli’s time to have been written by Cicero, was used widely to teach rhetoric, and it is likely that Machiavelli was familiar with it. Unlike Cicero's more widely accepted works however, according to <a href="#CITEREFCox1997">Cox (1997</a>:1122), “Ad Herennium ... offers a model of an ethical system that not only condones the practice of force and deception but appears to regard them as habitual and indeed germane to political activity”. This makes it an ideal text for Machiavelli to have used.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To quote <a href="#CITEREFBireley1990">Bireley (1990</a>:14):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...there were in circulation approximately fifteen editions of the <i>Prince</i> and nineteen of the <i>Discourses</i> and French translations of each before they were placed on <a href="/wiki/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum" title="Index Librorum Prohibitorum">the Index</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_IV" title="Paul IV">Paul IV</a> in 1559, a measure which nearly stopped publication in Catholic areas except in France. Three principal writers took the field against Machiavelli between the publication of his works and their condemnation in 1559 and again by the Tridentine Index in 1564. These were the English cardinal <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Pole" title="Reginald Pole">Reginald Pole</a> and the Portuguese bishop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeronymo_Osorio" title="Jeronymo Osorio">Jeronymo Osorio</a>, both of whom lived for many years in Italy, and the Italian humanist and later bishop, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrogio_Caterino_Politi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ambrogio Caterino Politi (page does not exist)">Ambrogio Caterino Politi</a>.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Machiavelli's ideas on how to accrue honor and power as a leader had a profound impact on political leaders throughout the modern west, helped by the new technology of the printing press. Pole reported that it was spoken of highly by his enemy <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cromwell" title="Thomas Cromwell">Thomas Cromwell</a> in England, and had influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a> in his turn towards <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, and in his tactics, for example during the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage_of_Grace" title="Pilgrimage of Grace">Pilgrimage of Grace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BireleyP15_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-BireleyP15-38">[38]</a></sup> A copy was also possessed by the Catholic king and emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In France, after an initially mixed reaction, Machiavelli came to be associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catherine_de_Medici" title="Catherine de Medici">Catherine de Medici</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Bartholomew%27s_Day_Massacre" title="St Bartholomew's Day Massacre">St Bartholomew's Day Massacre</a>. As <a href="#CITEREFBireley1990">Bireley (1990</a>:17) reports, in the 16th century, Catholic writers "associated Machiavelli with the Protestants, whereas Protestant authors saw him as Italian and Catholic". In fact, he was apparently influencing both Catholic and Protestant kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most important early works dedicated to criticism of Machiavelli, especially <i>The Prince</i>, was that of the <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innocent_Gentillet" title="Innocent Gentillet">Innocent Gentillet</a>, <i>Discourse against Machiavelli</i>, commonly also referred to as <i>Anti Machiavel</i>, published in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> in 1576. He accused Machiavelli of being an atheist and accused politicians of his time by saying that they treated his works as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koran" title="Koran">Koran</a> of the courtiers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Another theme of Gentillet was more in the spirit of Machiavelli himself: he questioned the effectiveness of immoral strategies (just as Machiavelli had himself done, despite also explaining how they could sometimes work). This became the theme of much future political discourse in Europe during the 17th century. This includes the Catholic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_Reformation" title="Counter Reformation">Counter Reformation</a> writers summarised by Bireley: <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Botero" title="Giovanni Botero">Giovanni Botero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justus_Lipsius" title="Justus Lipsius">Justus Lipsius</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carlo_Scribani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carlo Scribani (page does not exist)">Carlo Scribani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Contzen" title="Adam Contzen">Adam Contzen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Ribadeneira" title="Pedro de Ribadeneira">Pedro de Ribadeneira</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diego_Saavedra_Fajardo" title="Diego Saavedra Fajardo">Diego Saavedra Fajardo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> These authors criticized Machiavelli, but also followed him in many ways. They accepted the need for a prince to be concerned with reputation, and even a need for cunning and deceit, but compared to Machiavelli, and like later modernist writers, they emphasized <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic progress</a> much more than the riskier ventures of war. These authors tended to cite <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> as their source for realist political advice, rather than Machiavelli, and this pretense came to be known as "<a href="/wiki/Tacitean_studies" title="Tacitean studies">Tacitism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialist</a> philosophy developed in the 16th, 17th and 18th century, starting in the generations after Machiavelli. The importance of Machiavelli's realism was noted by many important figures in this endeavor, for example <a href="/wiki/Jean_Bodin" title="Jean Bodin">Jean Bodin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Harrington_(author)" title="James Harrington (author)">Harrington</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Spinoza</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Rousseau</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">Hume</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon" title="Edward Gibbon">Edward Gibbon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>. Although he was not always mentioned by name as an inspiration, due to his controversy, he is also thought to have been an influence for other major philosophers, such as <a href="/wiki/Michel_de_Montaigne" title="Michel de Montaigne">Montaigne</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">Descartes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Hobbes</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">Locke</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Montesquieu" title="Montesquieu">Montesquieu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In literature:</p>
<ul>
<li>Machiavelli is featured as a character in the <a href="/wiki/Prologue" title="Prologue">prologue</a> of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jew_of_Malta" title="The Jew of Malta">The Jew of Malta</a></i>.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare%27s" title="William Shakespeare's">William Shakespeare's</a> tragedy, <i><a href="/wiki/Othello" title="Othello">Othello</a></i>, the antagonist <a href="/wiki/Iago" title="Iago">Iago</a> has been noted by some literary critics as being archetypal in adhering to Machiavelli's ideals by advancing himself through machination and duplicity with the consequence of causing the demise of both <a href="/wiki/Othello_(character)" title="Othello (character)">Othello</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desdemona" title="Desdemona">Desdemona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Amongst later political leaders:</p>
<ul>
<li>The republicanism in seventeenth century England which led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_civil_war" title="English civil war">English civil war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> and subsequent development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Constitution" title="English Constitution">English Constitution</a> was strongly influenced by Machiavelli's political thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>Most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_fathers" title="Founding fathers">founding fathers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> are known or often proposed to have been strongly influenced by Machiavelli's political works, including <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-thompson-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>Under the guidance of <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a> of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> criticised Machiavelli's conclusions in his "<a href="/wiki/Anti-Machiavel" title="Anti-Machiavel">Anti-Machiavel</a>", published in 1740.</li>
<li>At different stages in his life, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon I of France</a> wrote extensive comments to <i>The Prince</i>. After his defeat at <a href="/wiki/Waterloo,_Belgium" title="Waterloo, Belgium">Waterloo</a>, these comments were found in the emperor's coach and taken by Prussian military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> dictator <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> wrote a discourse on <i>The Prince</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> read <i>The Prince</i> and annotated his own copy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>20th century Italian-American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobster" title="Mobster">mobsters</a> were influenced by <i>The Prince</i>. <a href="/wiki/John_Gotti" title="John Gotti">John Gotti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roy_DeMeo" title="Roy DeMeo">Roy DeMeo</a> would regularly quote <i>The Prince</i> and consider it to be the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_mafia" title="American mafia">Mafia</a> Bible".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation_of_The_Prince_as_political_satire_or_as_deceit">Interpretation of <i>The Prince</i> as political satire or as deceit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Prince&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Interpretation of The Prince as political satire or as deceit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As discussed by <a href="#CITEREFJohnston1958">Johnston (1958)</a> many authors have historically argued that "the book is, first and foremost, a satire, so that many of the things we find in it which are morally absurd, specious, and contradictory, are there quite deliberately in order to ridicule ... the very notion of tyrannical rule". Hence, Johnston says, "the satire has a firm moral purpose – to expose tyranny and promote republican government."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>This position was the standard one in Europe during the 18th century, amongst the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophes" title="Philosophes">philosophes</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diderot" title="Diderot">Diderot</a> thought it was a satire. And in his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Contract_(Rousseau)" title="Social Contract (Rousseau)"><i>The Social Contract</i></a>, the French philosopher <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Machiavelli was a proper man and a good citizen; but, being attached to the court of the Medici, he could not help veiling his love of liberty in the midst of his country's oppression. The choice of his detestable hero, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar_Borgia" title="Caesar Borgia">Caesar Borgia</a>, clearly enough shows his hidden aim; and the contradiction between the teaching of the <i>Prince</i> and that of the <i>Discourses on Livy</i> and the <i>History of Florence</i> shows that this profound political thinker has so far been studied only by superficial or corrupt readers. The Court of Rome sternly prohibited his book. I can well believe it; for it is that Court it most clearly portrays.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="external text" href="http://www.constitution.org/jjr/socon_03.htm#23" rel="nofollow"><i>Social Contract</i>, Book 3, note to Chapter 6</a>.</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Whether or not the word "satire" is the best choice, there is more general agreement that despite seeming to be written for someone wanting to be a monarch, and not the leader of a republic, <i>The Prince</i> can be read as deliberately emphasizing the benefits of free republics as opposed to monarchies.</p>
<p>Differences of opinion amongst commentators revolve around whether this sub-text was intended to be understood, let alone understood as deliberately satirical or comic.</p>
<p>One such commentator, Mary Dietz, writes that Machiavelli's agenda was not to be satirical, as Rousseau had argued, but instead was "offering carefully crafted advice (such as arming the people) designed to undo the ruler if taken seriously and followed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> By this account, the aim was to reestablish the republic in Florence. She focuses on three categories in which Machiavelli gives paradoxical advice:</p>
<ul>
<li>He discourages liberality and favors deceit to guarantee support from the people. Yet Machiavelli is keenly aware of the fact that an earlier pro-republican coup had been thwarted by the people's inaction that itself stemmed from the prince's liberality.</li>
<li>He supports arming the people despite the fact that he knows the Florentines are decidedly pro-democratic and would oppose the prince.</li>
<li>He encourages the prince to live in the city he conquers. This opposes the Medici's habitual policy of living outside the city. It also makes it easier for rebels or a civilian militia to attack and overthrow the prince.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to Dietz the trap never succeeded because Lorenzo did not read the work and did not trust Machiavelli, a consistently staunch republican.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Italian Marxist philosopher <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Antonio Gramsci</a> argued that Machiavelli's audience for this work was not the classes who already rule (or have "hegemony") over the common people, but the common people themselves, trying to establish a new hegemony, and making Machiavelli the first "Italian Jacobin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hans Baron is one of the few major commentators who argues that Machiavelli must have changed his mind dramatically in favour of free republics, after having written <i>The Prince</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaron1961_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaron1961-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
