<p>The term <b>military medicine</b> has a number of potential connotations. It may mean:</p>
<ul>
<li>A medical specialty, specifically a branch of <a href="/wiki/Occupational_medicine" title="Occupational medicine">occupational medicine</a> attending to the medical risks and needs (both preventive and interventional) of soldiers, sailors and other service members. This disparate arena has historically involved the prevention and treatment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Tropical_disease" title="Tropical disease">tropical diseases</a>), and, in the 20th Century, the ergonomics and health effects of operating military-specific machines and equipment such as submarines, tanks, helicopters and airplanes. <a href="/wiki/Diving_medicine" title="Diving medicine">Undersea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviation_medicine" title="Aviation medicine">aviation medicine</a> can be understood as subspecialties of military medicine, or in any case originated as such. Few countries certify or recognize "military medicine" as a formal speciality or subspeciality in its own right.</li>
<li>The planning and practice of the surgical management of mass battlefield casualties and the logistical and administrative considerations of establishing and operating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_support_hospitals" title="Combat support hospitals">combat support hospitals</a>. This involves military medical hierarchies, especially the organization of structured medical command and administrative systems that interact with and support deployed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_unit" title="Combat unit">combat units</a>. (See <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_medicine" title="Battlefield medicine">Battlefield medicine</a>.)</li>
<li>The administration and practice of health care for military service members and their dependents in non-deployed (peacetime) settings. This may (as in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>) consist of a medical system paralleling all the medical specialties and sub-specialties that exist in the civilian sector. (See also <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration" title="Veterans Health Administration">Veterans Health Administration</a> which serves U.S. veterans.)</li>
<li>Medical <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> specifically bearing upon problems of military medical interest. Historically, this encompasses all of the medical advances emerging from medical research efforts directed at addressing the problems encountered by deployed military forces (e.g., vaccines or drugs for soldiers, <a href="/wiki/Medical_evacuation" title="Medical evacuation">medical evacuation</a> systems, drinking water <a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">chlorination</a>, etc.) many of which ultimately prove important beyond the purely military considerations that inspired them.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Military medical personnel engage in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanitarianism_(international_humanitarian_law)" title="Humanitarianism (international humanitarian law)">humanitarian</a> work and are "<a href="/wiki/Protected_persons" title="Protected persons">protected persons</a>" under <a href="/wiki/International_humanitarian_law" title="International humanitarian law">international humanitarian law</a> in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/First_Geneva_Convention" title="First Geneva Convention">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Geneva_Convention" title="Second Geneva Convention">Second Geneva Conventions</a> and their Additional Protocols, which established legally binding rules guaranteeing neutrality and protection for wounded soldiers, field or ship's medical personnel, and specific humanitarian institutions in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_conflict" title="Armed conflict">armed conflict</a>. International humanitarian law makes no distinction between medical personnel who are members of the <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" title="Armed forces">armed forces</a> (and who hold military ranks) and those who are civilian volunteers. All medical personnel are considered <a href="/wiki/Non-combatant" title="Non-combatant">non-combatants</a> under international humanitarian law because of their humanitarian duties, and they may not be attacked and not be taken as <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoners of war</a>; hospitals and other medical facilities and transports identified as such, whether they are military or civilian, may not be attacked either. The <a href="/wiki/Emblems_of_the_International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="Emblems of the International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">red cross, the red crescent and the red crystal</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Protective_sign" title="Protective sign">protective signs</a> recognised under international humanitarian law, and are used by military medical personnel and facilities for this purpose. Attacking military medical personnel, patients in their care, or medical facilities or transports legitimately marked as such is a <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crime</a>. Likewise, misusing these protective signs to mask military operations is the war crime of <a href="/wiki/Perfidy" title="Perfidy">perfidy</a>. Military medical personnel may be armed, usually with <a href="/wiki/Service_pistol" title="Service pistol">service pistols</a>, for the purpose of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_defense" title="Self defense">self defense</a> or the defense of patients.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_significance">Historical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The significance of military medicine for combat strength goes far beyond treatment of battlefield injuries; in every major war fought until the late 19th century disease claimed more soldier casualties than did enemy action. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> (1860–65), for example, about twice as many soldiers died of disease as were killed or mortally wounded in combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> (1870–71) is considered to have been the first conflict in which combat injury exceeded disease, at least in the German coalition army which lost 3.47% of its average headcount to combat and only 1.82% to disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In new world countries, such as Australia, New Zealand, the United States and Canada, military physicians and surgeons contributed significantly to the development of civilian health care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historypmc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Improvements in military medicine have increased the survival rates in successive wars, due to improvements in <a href="/wiki/Medical_evacuation" title="Medical evacuation">medical evacuation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_medicine" title="Battlefield medicine">battlefield medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Major_trauma" title="Major trauma">trauma care</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historypmc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Similar improvements have been seen in the trauma practices during the Iraq war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Some military trauma care practices are disseminated by citizen soldiers who return to civilian practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historypmc_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> One such practice is where major trauma patients are transferred to an operating theater as soon as possible, to stop internal bleeding, increasing the survival rate. Within the United States, the survival rate for gunshot wounds has increased, leading to apparent declines in the gun death rate in states that have stable rates of gunshot hospitalizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JenaSun2014_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-JenaSun2014-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
