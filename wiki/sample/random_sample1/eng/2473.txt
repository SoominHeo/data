<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gung Ye</h1>

<p><b>Gung Ye</b> (c. 869 - 24 July 918, ruled 901–918) was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Korea" title="List of monarchs of Korea">king</a> of the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Taebong" title="Taebong">Hugoguryeo</a> (901–918) (see <a href="/wiki/Later_Three_Kingdoms" title="Later Three Kingdoms">Later Three Kingdoms</a>) on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a>. Although he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> royal family, he became a victim of the power struggle among the royal family members during the late 9th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_aks_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_aks-2">[2]</a></sup> He became a rebel leader against the unpopular Silla government, which almost abandoned the affairs of their subjects for the struggle for power among royal family members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">Birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gung_Ye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exact date of Gung Ye's birth is unknown, but records assume that he was a son of <a href="/wiki/Gyeongmun_of_Silla" title="Gyeongmun of Silla">King Gyeongmun</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_doosan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_doosan-4">[4]</a></sup> his mother is said to be a beloved lady of the court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_aks_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_aks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to legend, Gung Ye was born on the traditional holiday of <a href="/wiki/Dano_(Korean_festival)" title="Dano (Korean festival)">Dano</a>. The royal <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">seer</a> prophesied that a baby born on Dano would bring disaster to the nation, and the court officials and royal family members urged to the king to get rid of the infant. So the king ordered his servants to kill him. However, when the troops rushed to the residence of Gung Ye's mother, she threw her baby from the second floor, with her maid hiding in nearby bushes to catch the baby. Although her plot tricked the soldiers, the maid had accidentally poked the left eye of the baby, causing Gung Ye to lose one eye. She hid Gung Ye and raised him secretly; when she died, Gung Ye became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> monk at Sedalsa (세달사), a Buddhist temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_aks_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_aks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolt">Revolt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gung_Ye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Revolt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Rulers_of_Korea" title="List of Rulers of Korea">monarch of Silla</a> was <a href="/wiki/Jinseong_of_Silla" title="Jinseong of Silla">Queen Jinseong</a> (Gung Ye's half sister), who was the third and the last female head of state in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_history" title="Korean history">Korean history</a> (the other two being: <a href="/wiki/Queen_Seondeok_of_Silla" title="Queen Seondeok of Silla">Queen Seondeok of Silla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jindeok_of_Silla" title="Jindeok of Silla">Jindeok of Silla</a>). Queen Jinseong was a powerless ruler and the government was largely corrupted by interventions of royal family members and rampant bribery among members of the royal court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The corrupt government continuously exploited the peasants and farmers, and after a year of famine, massively raised taxes in 889 which led to many revolts and rebellions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_aks_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_aks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Local aristocrats, called <i>hojok</i> (hangul:호족, hanja:豪族) emerged as de facto rulers of many provinces, with the attention of government concentrated on suppression of rebellion and their own power struggles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Among the rebel leaders and local aristocrats, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gi_Hwon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gi Hwon (page does not exist)">Gi Hwon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yang_Gil" title="Yang Gil">Yang Gil</a> gained the most power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gung Ye first joined the troops of Gi Hwon in 891 but left shortly after as Gi Hwon did not fully trust him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Gung Ye joined Yang Gil's rebellion force in 892,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and became leading general of the rebel forces by defeating the local Silla army and other rebel groups. Most local aristocrats of <a href="/wiki/Gangneung" title="Gangneung">Myeongju</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hwanghaedo" title="Hwanghaedo">Paeseo</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Taejo_of_Goryeo" title="Taejo of Goryeo">Wang Gun</a>, submitted to his force, making him even more powerful than his master Yang Gil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_aks_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_aks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup> Silla, after nearly a millennium as a centralized kingdom was quickly declining, and Gung Ye instigated his own rebellion in present-day <a href="/wiki/Kaesong" title="Kaesong">Kaesŏng</a> in 898. After turning against his master, Yang Gil, He eventually defeated and subjugated him and other local lords in central Korea to proclaim himself king of <a href="/wiki/Taebong" title="Taebong">Hugoguryeo</a> in 901.With his rival <a href="/wiki/Gyeon_Hwon" title="Gyeon Hwon">Gyeon Hwon</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubaekje" title="Hubaekje">Hubaekje</a> taking control of the peninsula's southwest, he opened up the Later Three Kingdoms period of Korean History, a reference to the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_period" title="Three Kingdoms period">Three Kingdoms period</a> of Korea, several centuries earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_doosan_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_doosan-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downfall">Downfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gung_Ye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Downfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gung Ye changed the country's name to Majin in 904, and moved the capital to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheorwon" title="Cheorwon">Cheorwon</a> in the following year. Since Cheorwon was a fortress located in a mountainous area, he moved people from the populous city of <a href="/wiki/Cheongju" title="Cheongju">Cheongju</a> and expanded his rule into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chungcheong" title="Chungcheong">Chungcheong</a> region, taking control of almost two-thirds of the land once controlled by Silla. In the same year Gung Ye took over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyeongyang" title="Pyeongyang">Pyeongyang</a> and called for total destruction of the state of Silla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_doosan_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_doosan-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Searching for a better way to unite his citizens, he wanted a more potent unifying theology. Therefore, around 905, he turned to religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He decided what was needed to unite people under his power was religious faith, and using his previous occupation as a Buddhist monk, he referred to himself as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maitreya_Buddha" title="Maitreya Buddha">Maitreya Buddha</a>, who came to the world to guide and save the suffering people from all hardship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ka_gung_ye_112_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ka_gung_ye_112-12">[12]</a></sup> He changed the name of his kingdom to <a href="/wiki/Taebong" title="Taebong">Taebong</a> in 911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_doosan_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_doosan-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his later days, it is recorded that Gung Ye started to suffer from <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">paranoia</a>. He accused many arbitrarily of treason and sentenced anyone who opposed him to death, including Kang, one of his wives, and his two sons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup> As a result, in 918 four of his own top generals – Hong Yu (hangul:홍유, hanja:洪儒), Bae Hyeongyeong (hangul:배현경, hanja:裵玄慶), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shin_Sunggyeom" title="Shin Sunggyeom">Shin Sunggyeom</a> (hangul:신숭겸, hanja:申崇謙) and Bok Jigyeom (hangul:복지겸, hanja:卜智謙) – overthrew Taebong and enthroned <a href="/wiki/Taejo_of_Goryeo" title="Taejo of Goryeo">Wang Geon</a>, one of Gung Ye's followers and the previous chief minister, of his nation, as king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gung_ye_aks_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-gung_ye_aks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ka_gung_ye_112_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ka_gung_ye_112-12">[12]</a></sup> Gung Ye is said to have escaped the palace, but was killed shortly thereafter either by a soldier or by peasants who mistook him for a thief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon thereafter, the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> dynasty was proclaimed, and Wang Geon went on to defeat the rivaling Silla and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubaekje" title="Hubaekje">Hubaekje</a> to reunite the three kingdoms in 936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_theory_of_origin">Alternative theory of origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gung_Ye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternative theory of origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some historians present a theory that states that Gung Ye was, in fact, a direct descendant of <a href="/wiki/Anseung" title="Anseung">Go Anseung</a>, who had been the ruler of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goguryeo-Guk" title="Goguryeo-Guk">Goguryeo-Guk</a>, which had been a failed <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> revival state. Records of Silla reported that Go Anseung was given the surname of the <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> Royal Family, "Kim." Therefore, Gung Ye's commonly known origin as a prince of Silla was right in a way, but Gung Ye being a son of a king of Silla may have not been true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gung_Ye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Even though Gung Ye was not able to keep his rule and achieve the reunification of the Korean peninsula under his rule, many scholars today are attempting to review the true character of Gung Ye. Historical records regarding Gung Ye are negative, since many historians during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goryeo_Dynasty" title="Goryeo Dynasty">Goryeo Dynasty</a> tried to justify the coup by Wang Geon that dethroned Gung Ye, in order to give legitimacy to the dynasty. However, even after the founding of Goryeo, many people rejected the rule of Wang Geon and rebelled against the newly formed dynasty; some even voluntarily defected to Gyeon Hwon's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubaekje" title="Hubaekje">Hubaekje</a>. It can be assumed that many people, even after the coup that crowned Wang Geon, favored the rule of Gung Ye and that he was not a total despot as described in history. Some scholars explain Gung Ye's self-proclamation as Buddha as an attempt to strengthen his power, since he, as a royal family member of Silla, had no influence over powerful local landlords and merchants, so he tried to use the power of religion in order to keep his rule, which did not prove to be effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gung_Ye_ekc_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-Gung_Ye_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
