<p><b>Kuo Tai-yuan</b>, (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">郭泰源</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Guō Tàiyuán</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Kuo1 Tai4 Yüan2</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">かく たいげん</span> (Kaku Taigen); born March 20, 1962), is a retired Taiwanese <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Professional_Baseball" title="Nippon Professional Baseball">NPB</a> baseball <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> and currently a baseball <a href="/wiki/Coach_(baseball)" title="Coach (baseball)">coach</a>. With 117 wins accumulated during his 13 seasons in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seibu_Lions" title="Seibu Lions">Seibu Lions</a>, Kuo set the record of being the international player who achieves most wins in the NPB history, and is widely regarded as the greatest Taiwanese pitcher before <a href="/wiki/Chien-Ming_Wang" title="Chien-Ming Wang">Chien-Ming Wang</a> (Both of them plus <a href="/wiki/Hong-Chih_Kuo" title="Hong-Chih Kuo">Hong-Chih Kuo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chin-Lung_Hu" title="Chin-Lung Hu">Chin-Lung Hu</a> are all Tainan City natives).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuo_Tai-yuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur">Amateur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuo_Tai-yuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amateur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kuo was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tainan_City" title="Tainan City">Tainan City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. Originally a <a href="/wiki/Shortstop" title="Shortstop">shortstop</a>, he started to practice pitching in high school and immediately became well known for his high quality fastball and <a href="/wiki/Slider" title="Slider">slider</a>. In the 1983 <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Taipei_national_baseball_team" title="Chinese Taipei national baseball team">Chinese Taipei</a> versus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korea_national_baseball_team" title="Korea national baseball team">South Korea</a> match in <a href="/wiki/Asian_Baseball_Championship" title="Asian Baseball Championship">Asian Baseball Championship</a>, Kuo did not allow a run to score during 17 consecutive innings, while his fastballs clocked up to 154 km/h (96 mph). He finally won the game and helped Chinese Taipei qualify for the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1984 Olympics preliminary round against the United States, Kuo pitched up to 158 km/h (98 mph) and allowed only 2 runs (one <a href="/wiki/Earned_run" title="Earned run">earned run</a>) during his complete game effort, rightfully earning the nickname "The Oriental Express". However, Chinese Taipei eventually lost to team USA, which was studded with <a href="/wiki/Will_Clark" title="Will Clark">Will Clark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> and the like. Kuo later pitched in the semifinal round against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_national_baseball_team" title="Japanese national baseball team">Japan</a> match, allowing one run in 4.2 innings. Kuo's excellent performance drew special attention from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seibu_Lions" title="Seibu Lions">Seibu Lions</a>, and he signed with the club after the 1984 Summer Olympics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seibu_Lions">Seibu Lions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuo_Tai-yuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seibu Lions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a rookie, Kuo immediately gained a position in Seibu Lion's starting rotation and <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hit</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nippon_Ham_Fighters" title="Nippon Ham Fighters">Nippon Ham Fighters</a> on June 4, 1985, less than 2 months after his debut, and is the first international player to achieve so in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_League" title="Pacific League">Pacific League</a> history. Kuo's other notable achievements during his NPB career included:</p>
<ul>
<li>Achieved a streak of 10 consecutive wins during the 1989 season</li>
<li>Achieved a streak of 9 consecutive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complete_games" title="Complete games">complete games</a>, and was elected as the <a href="/wiki/Most_valuable_player" title="Most valuable player">most valuable player</a> of the Pacific League in the 1991 season</li>
<li>Achieved a streak of 3 consecutive <a href="/wiki/Shutout" title="Shutout">shutouts</a> in the 1992 season</li>
<li>Highest winning percentage in the 1988 and 1994 season</li>
</ul>

<p>Kuo finally retired by the end of 1997 season after he suffered long time wound which cost him a vacant season. Shortly after Kuo's retirement a poll in the Seibu Lions' official website unanimously voted Kuo as the most popular international player in the Seibu Lions history.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_Baseball_in_Taiwan">Professional Baseball in Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuo_Tai-yuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Professional Baseball in Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After retiring from Seibu Lions, the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Major_League" title="Taiwan Major League">Taiwan Major League</a>(TML) immediately hired Kuo as "senior technical consultant" until the league's collapse in early 2003. Meantime he introduced his former Seibu teammates <a href="/wiki/Takehiro_Ishii" title="Takehiro Ishii">Takehiro Ishii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hisanobu_Watanabe" title="Hisanobu Watanabe">Hisanobu Watanabe</a> to join TML, originally as players and later as coaches. He also acted as go-between to help then young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hsu_Ming-Chieh" title="Hsu Ming-Chieh">Hsu Ming-Chieh</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chang_Chih-chia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chang Chih-chia (page does not exist)">Chang Chih-chia</a> join Seibu Lions, following his steps.</p>
<p>After a disappointing 2003 in which he was temporarily jobless and suffered marital crisis(as revealed in the "Personal Life" section later), in 2004 he started to head coach the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macoto_Cobras" title="Macoto Cobras">Macoto Cobras</a> until the end of 2005 season. During his 2 seasons with the Cobras Kuo accumulated a 93 wins, 97 loses, and 17 ties performance. In the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Series" title="Taiwan Series">Taiwan Series</a> the Cobras were swept by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinon_Bulls" title="Sinon Bulls">Sinon Bulls</a> 0-4 and Kuo resigned right after the fiasco, addressing that "I would like to spend more time with my family". He later introduced Macoto Cobras players <a href="/wiki/Lin_En-yu" title="Lin En-yu">Lin En-yu</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lin_Ying-Chieh" title="Lin Ying-Chieh">Lin Ying-Chieh</a> to join <a href="/wiki/Tohoku_Rakuten_Golden_Eagles" title="Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles">Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_Taipei_national_baseball_team">Chinese Taipei national baseball team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuo_Tai-yuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chinese Taipei national baseball team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 15, 2007, Kuo was appointed as the head coach of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Taipei_national_baseball_team" title="Chinese Taipei national baseball team">Chinese Taipei national baseball team</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Taipei_Baseball_Association" title="Chinese Taipei Baseball Association">Chinese Taipei Baseball Association</a>. His tasks included the <a href="/wiki/2007_Baseball_World_Cup" title="2007 Baseball World Cup">2007 Baseball World Cup</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007_Asian_Baseball_Championships" title="2007 Asian Baseball Championships">2007 Asian Baseball Championships</a>. However the Chinese Taipei national baseball team performed poorly in both tournaments and he was exempted from the post on December 15, 2007.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuo_Tai-yuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kuo married Taiwanese model and actress Chang Chiung-tzu (張瓊姿, also credited as Zhang Qiuzi by Chinese media) in December 1993. After their marriage, Chang temporarily gave up her acting career in Taiwan to live with Kuo in Japan until his retirement late in 1997. Together, they had 2 daughters born in 1997 and 1999. However their relationship was badly damaged when <cite><a href="/wiki/Apple_Daily_(Taiwan)" title="Apple Daily (Taiwan)">Apple Daily (Taiwan)</a></cite> revealed Chang's extramarital affair with local fitness coach Pan Ruo-di (潘若迪) in May 2003. For several years this couple had been in separation and were repeatedly reported to have agreed to divorce, until their mutual announcement that they had mended their relationship and would not divorce early in 2007.</p>
