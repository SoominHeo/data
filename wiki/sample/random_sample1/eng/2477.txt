<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Zonal constructed language</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Zonal constructed languages</b> are <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">constructed languages</a> made to facilitate communication between speakers of a certain group of closely related languages. They form a subgroup of the <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">international auxiliary languages</a>, but unlike languages like <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volap%C3%BCk" title="Volapük">Volapük</a> they are not intended to serve as languages for the whole world, but merely for a limited linguistic or geographic area. There is some overlap with the term "Euroclone", however, the latter usually refers to languages intended for global use but based (almost) exclusively on European material. Another related concept is known as a <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">koiné language</a>, a dialect which naturally emerges as a means of communication among speakers of divergent dialects of a language.</p>
<p>Most zonal constructed languages were created during the period of <a href="/wiki/Romantic_nationalism" title="Romantic nationalism">National revival</a> at the end of the 19th century, some were created later. Nowadays, most older zonal constructed languages are known only to specialists. Modern examples are <a href="/wiki/Interslavic_language" title="Interslavic language">Interslavic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folkspraak" title="Folkspraak">Folkspraak</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-Slavic_languages">Pan-Slavic languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zonal_constructed_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pan-Slavic languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pan-Slavic_language" title="Pan-Slavic language">Pan-Slavic language</a></div>
<p>Most numerous within this category by far are <a href="/wiki/Pan-Slavic_language" title="Pan-Slavic language">Pan-Slavic languages</a>. The oldest known example is <a href="/wiki/Interslavic_language#Early_projects" title="Interslavic language">Ruski jezik</a> (1665) by the Croatian priest <a href="/wiki/Juraj_Kri%C5%BEani%C4%87" title="Juraj Križanić">Juraj Križanić</a>, who is often regarded as the first recorded <a href="/wiki/Pan-Slavism" title="Pan-Slavism">Pan-Slavist</a>. Other notable examples of early Pan-Slavic language projects are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universalis_Lingua_Slavica" title="Universalis Lingua Slavica">Universalis Lingua Slavica</a> by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1n_Herke%C4%BE" title="Ján Herkeľ">Ján Herkeľ</a> (1826), <i>Uzajemni Pravopis Slavjanski</i> by the Slovene <a href="/wiki/Matija_Majar" title="Matija Majar">Matija Majar</a> (1865), <i>Neuslawisch</i> by the Czech Ignac Hošek (1907) and <a href="/wiki/Pan-Slavic_language#Slava-Esperanto" title="Pan-Slavic language">Slavina</a> by the Czech Josef Konečný (1912). Until the beginning of the 20th century, all projects were characterized by a heavily <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language#Classification" title="International auxiliary language">naturalistic</a> grammar, based directly or indirectly on <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a>. Their authors were motivated by the belief that all Slavic languages were dialects of one single Slavic language rather than separate languages. They deplored the fact that these dialects had diverged beyond mutual comprehensability, and the language they envisioned was intended to reverse this process. Their long-term objective was that it would replace the individual Slavic languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Naturalistic projects have been created later as well. Notable examples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Me%C5%BEduslavjanski_jezik" title="Mežduslavjanski jezik">Mežduslavjanski jezik</a>, an unpublished project from the years 1954–1958 by a team of Czech interlinguists, including Ladislav Podmele; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovianski" title="Slovianski">Slovianski</a>, a collaborative project started in 2006; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novosloviensky_jezyk" title="Novosloviensky jezyk">Novosloviensky</a>, based on Old Church Slavonic and published in 2010 by the Czech Vojtěch Merunka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2011, Slovianski and Novosloviensky merged into one common project under the name <a href="/wiki/Interslavic_language" title="Interslavic language">Interslavic</a> (<i>Medžuslovjanski</i>), also incorporating material from older naturalistic projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most naturalistic projects are so similar that they can easily be considered versions of the same language. During the 20th century, however, a few <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language#Classification" title="International auxiliary language">schematic</a> projects have emerged as well, for example <i>Slovanština</i> (Edmund Kolkop, 1912), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neposlava" title="Neposlava">Neposlava</a> (Vsevolod Cheshikhin, 1915), <i>Slavski jezik</i> (Bohumil Holý, 1920) and <a href="/wiki/Slovio" title="Slovio">Slovio</a> (Mark Hučko, 1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These projects aim at radical simplification of the grammar, often combining Slavic vocabulary with <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a> grammar.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-Germanic_languages">Pan-Germanic languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zonal_constructed_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pan-Germanic languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pan-Germanic_language" title="Pan-Germanic language">Pan-Germanic language</a></div>
<p>Languages for Pan-Germanic use have been created as well. Known examples are <a href="/wiki/Tutonish" title="Tutonish">Tutonish</a>, a Pan-Germanic project by Elias Molee (1902), which was intended to be an auxiliary language at first but to eventually supplant all other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">Germanic languages</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euronord" title="Euronord">Euronord</a> for Northern Europe by A.J. Pilgrim (1965) and <a href="/wiki/Folkspraak" title="Folkspraak">Folkspraak</a>, a heterogeneous project consisting of various dialects, started in 1995.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-Romance_languages">Pan-Romance languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zonal_constructed_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pan-Romance languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many international auxiliary languages intended for global use consist exclusively or predominantly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a> material, like <a href="/wiki/Latino_sine_flexione" title="Latino sine flexione">Latino sine flexione</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">Interlingua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lingua_Franca_Nova" title="Lingua Franca Nova">Lingua Franca Nova</a>, which makes it hard to distinguish them from Pan-Romance languages. Some languages, however, have been presented explicitly as languages for Pan-Romance use, for example Interlatino, Latino Moderne, <a href="/wiki/Romanid" title="Romanid">Romanid</a> and Romanova.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_zonal_constructed_languages">Other zonal constructed languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zonal_constructed_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other zonal constructed languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from these European examples, there have also been attempts on other continents:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Afrihili" title="Afrihili">Afrihili</a> (1973) by K. A. Kumi Attobrah, which was created to be used as a <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> in all of Africa</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guosa (page does not exist)">Guosa</a> (1965) by Alexander Ekhoguosa Igbinéwéká, mostly based on <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a> and intended as a common language for <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Efatese_language" title="Efatese language">Efatese</a> (19th century), an artificially mixed language based on the languages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Efate_Island" title="Efate Island">Efate Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a></li>
</ul>
