<p>An <b>ulcer</b> is a sore on the skin or a mucous membrane, accompanied by the disintegration of tissue. Ulcers can result in complete loss of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> and often portions of the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_fat" title="Subcutaneous fat">subcutaneous fat</a>. Ulcers are most common on the skin of the lower extremities and in the gastrointestinal tract. An ulcer that appears on the skin is often visible as an inflamed tissue with an area of reddened skin. A skin ulcer is often visible in the event of exposure to heat or cold, irritation, or a problem with blood circulation. They can also be caused due to a lack of mobility, which causes prolonged pressure on the tissues. This stress in the blood circulation is transformed to a skin ulcer, commonly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decubitus_ulcer" title="Decubitus ulcer">bedsores or decubitus ulcers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kumar_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar-1">[1]</a></sup> Ulcers often become <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infected</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pus" title="Pus">pus</a> forms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms">Symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulcer_(dermatology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Skin ulcers appear as open craters, often round, with layers of skin that have eroded. The skin around the ulcer may be red, swollen, and tender. Patients may feel pain on the skin around the ulcer, and fluid may ooze from the ulcer. In some cases, ulcers can <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleed</a> and, rarely, patients experience <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>. Ulcers sometimes seem not to heal; healing, if it does occur, tends to be slow. Ulcers that heal within 12 weeks are usually classified as <a href="/wiki/Acute_(medicine)" title="Acute (medicine)">acute</a>, and longer-lasting ones as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medicine)" title="Chronic (medicine)">chronic</a>.</p>
<p>Ulcers develop in stages. In stage 1 the skin is red with soft underlying <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a>. In the second stage the redness of the skin becomes more pronounced, swelling appears, and there may be some <a href="/wiki/Blister" title="Blister">blisters</a> and loss of outer skin layers. During the next stage, the skin may become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Necrotic" title="Necrotic">necrotic</a> down through the deep layers of skin, and the <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> beneath the skin may become exposed and visible. In stage 4, deeper necrosis usually occurs, the fat underneath the skin is completely exposed, and the <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> may also become exposed. In the last two stages the sore may cause a deeper loss of fat and necrosis of the muscle; in severe cases it can extend down to <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> level, destruction of the bone may begin, and there may be <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Joint" title="Joint">joints</a>.</p>
<p>Chronic ulcers may be <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">painful</a>. Most patients complain of constant pain at night and during the day. Chronic ulcer symptoms usually include increasing pain, friable granulation tissue, foul odour, and wound breakdown instead of healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Symptoms tend to worsen once the wound has become infected.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Venous_ulcer" title="Venous ulcer">Venous skin ulcers</a> that may appear on the lower leg, above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calf_(anatomy)" title="Calf (anatomy)">calf</a> or on the lower <a href="/wiki/Ankle" title="Ankle">ankle</a> usually cause achy and swollen legs. If these ulcers become infected they may develop an unpleasant odour, increased tenderness and redness. Before the ulcer establishes definitively, there may be a dark red or purple skin over the affected area as well as a thickening, drying, and itchy skin.</p>
<p>Although skin ulcers do not seem of great concern at a first glance, they are worrying conditions especially in people suffering from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>, as they are at risk of developing diabetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuropathy" title="Neuropathy">neuropathy</a>.</p>
<p>Ulcers may also appear on the <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">cheeks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">soft palate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>, and on the inside of the lower <a href="/wiki/Lip" title="Lip">lip</a>. These ulcers usually last from 7 to 14 days and can be painful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discharges">Discharges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulcer_(dermatology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discharges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Different types of discharges from ulcer are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-srb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Serous, usually seen in healing ulcer</li>
<li>Purulent, seen in infected ulcer. Yellow creamy discharge is observed in staphylococcal infection; bloody opalescent discharge in streptococcal infection, while greenish discharge is seen in pseudomonas ulcer</li>
<li>Bloody (sanguineous), usually seen in <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Malignant_ulcer_(disambiguation)" title="Malignant ulcer (disambiguation)">malignant ulcers</a> and in healing ulcers with healthy <a href="/wiki/Granulation_tissue" title="Granulation tissue">granulation tissue</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seropurulent" title="Seropurulent">Seropurulent</a></li>
<li>Serosanguinous</li>
<li>Serous with sulphur granules, seen in <a href="/wiki/Actinomycosis" title="Actinomycosis">actinomycosis</a></li>
<li>Yellowish, as seen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuberculous_ulcer" title="Tuberculous ulcer">tuberculous ulcer</a></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulcer_(dermatology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Skin ulcers may take a very long time to heal. Treatment is typically to avoid the ulcer getting infected, remove any excess discharge, maintain a moist wound environment, control the <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a>, and ease pain caused by <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">nerve</a> and tissue damage.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topical_antibiotic" title="Topical antibiotic">Topical antibiotics</a> are normally used to prevent the ulcer getting infected, and the wound or ulcer is usually kept clear of dead tissue through surgical <a href="/wiki/Debridement" title="Debridement">debridement</a>.</p>
<p>Commonly, as a part of the treatment, patients are advised to change their lifestyle if possible and to change their <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a>. Improving the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulation</a> is important in treating skin ulcers, and patients are consequently usually recommended to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">exercise</a>, stop <a href="/wiki/Smoking" title="Smoking">smoking</a>, and lose weight.</p>
<p>In recent years, advances have been made in accelerating healing of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_wound" title="Chronic wound">chronic wounds</a> and ulcers. Chronic wounds produce fewer growth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormones" title="Hormones">hormones</a> than necessary for healing tissue, and healing may be accelerated by replacing or stimulating growth factors while controlling the formation of other substances that work against them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leg ulcers can be prevented by using <a href="/wiki/Compression_stockings" title="Compression stockings">compression stockings</a> to prevent blood pooling and back flow. It is likely that a person who has had a skin ulcer will have it again; use of compression stockings every day for at least 5 years after the skin ulcer has healed may help to prevent recurrence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulcer_(dermatology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wounds from which ulcers arise can be caused by a wide variety of factors, but the main cause is impaired blood circulation. Especially, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_wound" title="Chronic wound">chronic wounds</a> and ulcers are caused by poor circulation, either through cardiovascular issues or external pressure from a <a href="/wiki/Bed" title="Bed">bed</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair" title="Wheelchair">wheelchair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A very common and dangerous type of skin ulcers are caused by what are called pressure-sensitive sores, more commonly called bed sores and which are frequent in people who are bedridden or who use wheelchairs for long periods. Other causes producing skin ulcers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_infections" title="Bacterial infections">bacterial</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viral_infection" title="Viral infection">viral infections</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungal_infection_in_animals" title="Fungal infection in animals">fungal infections</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancers</a>. Blood disorders and chronic wounds can result in skin ulcers as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Venous leg ulcers due to impaired circulation or a blood flow disorder are more common in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elderly" title="Elderly">elderly</a>.</p>
