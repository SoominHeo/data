<p><i><b>Onimusha</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">鬼武者</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, literally "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oni_(folklore)" title="Oni (folklore)">Oni</a> Warrior")</span> is a series of video games by developer <a href="/wiki/Capcom" title="Capcom">Capcom</a>. The series makes use of the historic figures that shaped <a href="/wiki/History_of_Japan" title="History of Japan">Japan's history</a>, retelling their stories with <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> elements. Most of the games are of the <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">action-adventure game</a> genre, a combination of <a href="/wiki/Third-person_shooter" title="Third-person shooter">third person</a> combat and puzzle solving, where the protagonist wields the power of the Oni, enabling them to fight the Genma, the main enemy of the series. As of 2012, <i>Onimusha</i> is Capcom's sixth biggest <a href="/wiki/Media_franchise" title="Media franchise">franchise</a>, behind the <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil" title="Resident Evil">Resident Evil</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Fighter" title="Street Fighter">Street Fighter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mega_Man" title="Mega Man">Mega Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Monster_Hunter" title="Monster Hunter">Monster Hunter</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Devil_May_Cry" title="Devil May Cry">Devil May Cry</a></i> series.</p>

<p>The series originates in <a href="/wiki/Yoshiki_Okamoto" title="Yoshiki Okamoto">Yoshiki Okamoto</a>'s 1997 idea to create <i>Sengoku Biohazard</i>, a ninja version of Capcom's own 1996 <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_(1996_video_game)" title="Resident Evil (1996 video game)">Resident Evil</a></i> (known as <i>Biohazard</i> in Japan), set in the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a> and featuring a "ninja house" filled with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booby_traps" title="Booby traps">booby traps</a>, similar to the mansion from <i>Resident Evil</i>, where battles would be fought using swords and <a href="/wiki/Shuriken" title="Shuriken">shuriken</a>: "The house will contain hidden doors behind walls, ceilings that fall down to you, scrolls and ninja magic, and many other ninja techniques."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The project was originally intended for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a>'s <a href="/wiki/64DD" title="64DD">64DD</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ign-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Onimusha:_Warlords" title="Onimusha: Warlords">Onimusha: Warlords</a></i> was originally being developed for the original <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_(console)" title="PlayStation (console)">PlayStation</a>, but the project was eventually moved to the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>. The half-finished original PlayStation version of <i>Onimusha</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> was then scrapped and never released.</p>
<p>The central character of the series, Samanosuke Akechi, is modeled after <a href="/wiki/Takeshi_Kaneshiro" title="Takeshi Kaneshiro">Takeshi Kaneshiro</a>, who also voiced the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Real people were used as models for other characters in the series, including the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y%C5%ABsaku_Matsuda" title="Yūsaku Matsuda">Yūsaku Matsuda</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Onimusha_2:_Samurai%27s_Destiny" title="Onimusha 2: Samurai's Destiny">Onimusha 2: Samurai's Destiny</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Reno" title="Jean Reno">Jean Reno</a> (alongside Takeshi Kaneshiro) in <i><a href="/wiki/Onimusha_3:_Demon_Siege" title="Onimusha 3: Demon Siege">Onimusha 3: Demon Siege</a></i>. Character movements throughout the series were created using <a href="/wiki/Motion_capture" title="Motion capture">motion capture</a>.</p>
<p>The series was initially planned to be only a trilogy, but a fourth installment, <i><a href="/wiki/Onimusha:_Dawn_of_Dreams" title="Onimusha: Dawn of Dreams">Onimusha: Dawn of Dreams</a></i> was released in 2006. In 2012, Capcom announced a browser-based game <i>Onimusha Soul</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-browsergame_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-browsergame-5">[5]</a></sup> which was also scheduled to be released for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> in Japan in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Onimusha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the protagonist changes in every <i>Onimusha</i> title, he is always a skilled swordsman who embarks on a set mission which involves slaying demons and fearsome enemies during the waning years of the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a> of feudal Japan. In each game, the protagonist has the ability to absorb Genma <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">souls</a> from defeated enemies, which help to restore health, infuse power in weapons and armor, and provide power for the elemental attacks of special weapons.</p>
<p>The player controls their character using the <a href="/wiki/D-pad" title="D-pad">D-pad</a> (although later games such as <i>Onimusha 3: Demon Siege</i> and <i>Onimusha: Dawn of Dreams</i> introduced <a href="/wiki/Analog_stick" title="Analog stick">analog stick</a> control) and travels in a fairly linear method, able to rotate slowly with the input of an opposing direction. Characters tend to move slowly and can only slightly increase their speed with the <a href="/wiki/Sprint_(running)" title="Sprint (running)">dash</a> maneuver by tapping twice in any direction. Actions common to many action-oriented games, such as jumping, grabbing, and climbing over obstacles, cannot be performed in <i>Onimusha</i> games.</p>
<p><i>Onimusha</i> is very action-oriented with an emphasis on combat, and employing some horror elements. The player has an arsenal of weaponry, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">katana</a> to elemental-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadsword" title="Broadsword">broadswords</a>. The player possesses a limited supply of spiritual energy which can be used for magical attacks. These magical attacks, which vary depending upon which weapon is equipped and other offensive attributes, can be improved throughout the game by accumulation of souls from defeated enemies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Onimusha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based primarily throughout the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a>, <i>Onimusha: Warlords</i> starts with the feudal lord Nobunaga Oda being killed during a battle. One of the prominent fighters, Hidemitsu Samanosuke Akechi, receives a letter from his cousin Princess Yuki who is concerned about servants from her castle disappearing. Samanosuke joins with the kunoichi Kaede to rescue Yuki and discover demonic creatures known as Genma are the culprits. In order to defeat the Genma, the Oni clan grant Samanosuke powers from their kind. Across his fights Samanosuke discovers the Genma have resurrected Nobunaga and that they are serving him. The Genma plan to sacrifice Yuki and an orphan named Yumemaru to grant Nobunaga more powers. Samanosuke manages to save Yuki and Yumemaru and kills the Genma Lord, Fortinbras. Samanosuke's group then takes different paths.</p>
<p>The sequel, <i>Onimusha 2: Samurai's Destiny</i>, has Nobunaga replacing Fortinbras as the new Genma Lord and using his new forces in order to unify Japan. One of his targets is the Yagyu village whose clans are in charge of eliminating the Genma. The clan's only survivor, Jubei Yagyu, goes on a quest to avenge his clan. Jubei discovers that he inherited Oni powers from his mother and uses them alongside the Oni's five orbs to battle Nobunaga's soldiers. Across his journey, Jubei meets several allies who also seek the defeat of the new Genma Lord. Jubei manages to infiltrate Gifu castle and confronts Nobunaga alone. Although Jubei kills Nobunaga, his soul swears to return. The spin-off <i>Onimusha: Blade Warriors</i> has the cast from <i>Warlords</i> and <i>Samurai's Destiny</i> in a new battle against Nobunaga's forces.</p>
<p>The third and final chapter from the trilogy, <i>Onimusha 3: Demon Siege</i>, has Samanosuke's clan attacking the Oda forces again. Before confronting Nobunaga, Samanosuke is transported to Modern Paris as a result of an experiment made by Genma scientist Guildernstern in order to let the Genma conquer more lands. In the meantime, service agent Jacques Blanc is a victim of Guildernstern and is transported to Japan in the Sengoku period. There, Jacques is granted oni powers by an oni who tells him to join forces with this timeline's Samanosuke and defeat Nobunaga if he wishes to return home. While Jacques aids Samanosuke in the past, in the future Samanosuke is helped by Jacques' family to investigate the Genma. In the end the two oni warriors are successful in stopping the invasion and return to their respective times. In the past Samanosuke slays Nobunaga and absorbs his soul to avoid another resurrection.</p>
<p>The fourth game, <i>Onimusha: Dawn of Dreams</i>, has Nobunaga's servant Hideyoshi Toyotomi unifying Japan in league with the Genma. His illegitimate son, Hideyasu "Soki" Yuki, goes on a quest to defeat Hideyoshi and stop the Genma. He is aided by several other warriors including an elder Samanosuke who recognizes him as the Black Oni, the God of Darkness. After mastering his oni powers, Soki joins with his friends to defeat Hideyoshi's army. In the aftermath it is revealed that Hideyoshi was a puppet of the Genma Triumvirate who wish to resurrect the Genma Lord Fortinbras, the God of Light. Although the Genma Triumvirate and Hideyoshi are defeated, Fortinbras resurrects. Soki manages to kill Fortinbras and restore country's peace but at the cost of his life.</p>

<p>The <i>Onimusha</i> series has received overall positive reviews with most of the main games on the PlayStation 2, receiving average scores of more than 80%. As a comparison, most spin-offs from the series have not been as successful. The titles have influenced other games from the company including <i><a href="/wiki/Devil_May_Cry_(video_game)" title="Devil May Cry (video game)">Devil May Cry</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shadow_of_Rome" title="Shadow of Rome">Shadow of Rome</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_4" title="Resident Evil 4">Resident Evil 4</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egm_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-egm-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The samurai game <i><a href="/wiki/Genji:_Dawn_of_the_Samurai" title="Genji: Dawn of the Samurai">Genji: Dawn of the Samurai</a></i> was cited by Inafune as an "<i>Onimusha</i> clone" although its designer Yoshiki Okamoto denied such statement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series has often been compared with the <i>Resident Evil</i> series and has been praised for its focus in action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespotReview1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespotReview1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamerevreview_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gamerevreview-18">[18]</a></sup> However, the first two games were criticized for forcing the player to use the Directional Pad rather than the left analogue to make the playable character to move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNreview_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGNreview-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespotReview1_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamespotReview1-17">[17]</a></sup> This issue was solved with the third release which generated a good response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Another subject of criticism is the length of each game with opinions sometimes differing due to the replay value they offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PALGNreview_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-PALGNreview-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamerevreview_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gamerevreview-18">[18]</a></sup> Yoshinori Ono acknowledged the third game's short length and thus made <i>Dawn of Dreams</i> become the series' longest title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurogamerinter_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurogamerinter-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original game was a major hit on the PS2, becoming the console's first game to sell over a million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> While <i>Onimusha 2</i> was also a best selling titles Capcom noticed how it did poorly in European regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1upinter_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-1upinter-25">[25]</a></sup> The third and fourth titles received less favorable sales with Keiji Inafune addressing people's concerns about how the former game did not feel like a samurai story while GamesRadar noted the latter was overshadowed by next generation consoles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVGinterdawn_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CVGinterdawn-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As December 31, 2012, the series has sold at least 7.9 million copies to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thegamergene.com_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-thegamergene.com-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptation_project">Film adaptation project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Onimusha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film adaptation project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 2003, <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>, Davis Films and Gaga Productions announced its joint venture to adapt the <i>Onimusha</i> game series into a $50 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live-action" title="Live-action">live-action</a> <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">feature film</a>. According to Paramount and Davis Films' Samuel Hadida, "It's samurai fighting against demons – it's very close to this simple pitch. There's also a love story woven in. It's a big adventure movie with lots of special effects." He also proposed the possibility of a film franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, director <a href="/wiki/Christophe_Gans" title="Christophe Gans">Christophe Gans</a> said that he had <i>Onimusha</i> lined up to film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdrama_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvdrama-30">[30]</a></sup> The film, budgeted at over $70 million, was to begin production in China in February 2008 for a December 2009 release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It was reported that Takeshi Kaneshiro would be in the movie, reprising his role as Samanosuke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Hadida had to delay the filming of <i>Onimusha</i>, which has resulted in the film's Japanese cast working on other film projects during the delay, and being unavailable to start filming <i>Onimusha</i>. These factors meant that Gans would direct an adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Leo_Perutz" title="Leo Perutz">Leo Perutz</a>'s novel <i>The Swedish Cavalier</i> first. <a href="/wiki/Satomi_Ishihara" title="Satomi Ishihara">Satomi Ishihara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsuyoshi_Ihara" title="Tsuyoshi Ihara">Tsuyoshi Ihara</a> remained attached to the project.</p>
