<p>The <b>hammerhead sharks</b> are a group of <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> in the family <b>Sphyrnidae</b>, so named for the unusual and distinctive structure of their heads, which are flattened and laterally extended into a "hammer" shape called a cephalofoil. Most hammerhead species are placed in the genus <i><b>Sphyrna</b></i> while the <a href="/wiki/Winghead_shark" title="Winghead shark">winghead shark</a> is placed in its own genus, <i><b>Eusphyra</b></i>. Many not necessarily mutually exclusive functions have been proposed for the cephalofoil, including sensory reception, manoeuvering, and prey manipulation. Hammerheads are found worldwide in warmer waters along coastlines and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" title="Continental shelves">continental shelves</a>. Unlike most sharks, hammerheads usually swim in schools during the day, becoming solitary hunters at night. Some of these schools can be found near <a href="/wiki/Malpelo_Island" title="Malpelo Island">Malpelo Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Island" title="Cocos Island">Cocos Island</a> off <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, and near <a href="/wiki/Molokai" title="Molokai">Molokai</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>. Large schools are also seen in the waters off southern and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Africa" title="Eastern Africa">eastern Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The known <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> range from 0.9 to 6 m (3.0 to 19.7 ft) in length and weigh from 3 to 580 kg (6.6 to 1,278.7 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-record_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-record-3">[3]</a></sup> They are usually light gray and have a greenish tint. Their bellies are white which allows them to blend into the ocean when viewed from the bottom and sneak up on their prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Their heads have lateral projections which give them a hammer-like shape.</p>
<p>Hammerheads have disproportionately small mouths and seem to do a lot of bottom-hunting. They are also known to form <a href="/wiki/Shoaling_and_schooling" title="Shoaling and schooling">schools</a> during the day, sometimes in groups of over 100. In the evening, like other sharks, they become solitary hunters. <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic</a></i> explains that hammerheads can be found in warm tropical waters, but during the summer Hammerheads participate in a mass migration to search for cooler waters. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since sharks do not have mineralized bones and rarely <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossilize</a>, it is their teeth alone that are commonly found as fossils. The hammerheads seem closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Carcharhinus" title="Carcharhinus">carcharhinid sharks</a> that evolved during the mid-<a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> Period. According to DNA studies, the ancestor of the hammerheads probably lived in the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> epoch about 20 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Using <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a> of the hammerhead sharks showed the <a href="/wiki/Winghead_shark" title="Winghead shark">winghead shark</a> as its most <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> member. As the winghead shark has proportionately the largest "hammer" of the hammerhead sharks, this suggests that the first ancestral hammerhead sharks also had large hammers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mutation_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mutation-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cephalofoil">Cephalofoil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cephalofoil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theory has been advanced that the hammer-like shape of the head may have evolved (at least in part) to enhance the animal's vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The positioning of the eyes, mounted on the sides of the shark's distinctive hammer head, gives the shark good 360-degree vision in the vertical plane, meaning they can see above and below them at all times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The shape of the head was previously thought to help the shark find food, aiding in close-quarters maneuverability and allowing sharp turning movement without losing stability. However, it has been found that the unusual structure of its <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a> was instrumental in making the turns correctly, more often than the shape of its head, though it would also shift and provide lift. From what is known about the <a href="/wiki/Winghead_shark" title="Winghead shark">winghead shark</a>, it would appear that the shape of the hammer-head has to do with an evolved sensory function. Like all sharks, hammerheads have <a href="/wiki/Electroreception" title="Electroreception">electroreceptory</a> sensory pores called <a href="/wiki/Ampullae_of_Lorenzini" title="Ampullae of Lorenzini">ampullae of Lorenzini</a>. By distributing the receptors over a wider area, like a larger radio antenna, hammerheads can sweep for prey more effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammer_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammer-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reproduction occurs only once a year for hammerhead sharks, and usually occurs with the male shark biting the female shark violently until she agrees to mate with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aquaticcommunity_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aquaticcommunity-12">[12]</a></sup> The hammerhead sharks exhibit a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viviparous" title="Viviparous">viviparous</a> mode of reproduction with females giving birth to live young. Like other sharks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> is internal with the male transferring <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> to the female through one of two <a href="/wiki/Intromittent_organ" title="Intromittent organ">intromittent organs</a> called <a href="/wiki/Clasper" title="Clasper">claspers</a>. The developing <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryos</a> are at first sustained by a <a href="/wiki/Yolk_sac" title="Yolk sac">yolk sac</a>. When the supply of <a href="/wiki/Yolk" title="Yolk">yolk</a> is exhausted, the depleted yolk sac transforms into a structure analogous to a <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">placenta</a> (called a "yolk sac placenta" or "pseudoplacenta"), through which the mother delivers sustenance until birth. Once the baby sharks are born, they are not taken care of by the parents in any way. There is usually a litter of 12 to 15 pups; except for the Great Hammerhead which births litters of 20 to 40 pups. These baby sharks huddle together and swim toward warmer water until they are old enough and large enough to survive on their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aquaticcommunity_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-aquaticcommunity-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Bonnethead" title="Bonnethead">bonnethead</a> shark was found to be capable of <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a> via <a href="/wiki/Parthenogenesis" title="Parthenogenesis">automictic parthenogenesis</a>, in which a female's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">ovum</a> fuses with a <a href="/wiki/Polar_body" title="Polar body">polar body</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a> without the need for a male. This was the first shark known to do this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hammerhead sharks are known to eat a large range of prey including <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustaceans" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a>, and other sharks. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stingrays" title="Stingrays">Stingrays</a> are a particular favorite. These sharks are often found swimming along the bottom of the ocean, stalking their prey. Their unique head is used as a weapon when hunting down prey. The hammerhead shark uses its head to pin down stingrays and eats the ray when the ray is weak and in shock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aquaticcommunity_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-aquaticcommunity-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_hammerhead" title="Great hammerhead">great hammerhead</a>, tending to be larger and more aggressive than most hammerheads, occasionally engages in <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism_(zoology)" title="Cannibalism (zoology)">cannibalism</a>, eating other hammerhead sharks, including its own young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1256" data-file-width="2432" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg/220px-Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg/330px-Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg/440px-Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hammerhead_Atlantis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A hammerhead shark at <a href="/wiki/Atlantis_Paradise_Island" title="Atlantis Paradise Island">Atlantis Paradise Island</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Of the nine known species of hammerhead, only three are known to be particularly dangerous to humans: the <a href="/wiki/Scalloped_hammerhead" title="Scalloped hammerhead">scalloped</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_hammerhead" title="Great hammerhead">great</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smooth_hammerhead" title="Smooth hammerhead">smooth hammerheads</a>. As of 2013, 33 attacks have been recorded, with no fatalities.</p>
<p>The great and the scalloped hammerheads are listed on the World Conservation Union's (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>) 2008 Red List as <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a>, whereas the smalleye hammerhead is listed as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>. The status given to these sharks is as a result of overfishing and demand for their fins, an expensive delicacy. Among others, scientists expressed their concern about the plight of the scalloped hammerhead at the American Association for the Advancement of Science annual meeting in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. The young swim mostly in shallow waters along shores all over the world to avoid predators.</p>
<p>Shark fins are prized as a delicacy in certain countries in Asia (such as China), and overfishing is putting many hammerhead sharks at risk of extinction. Fishermen who harvest the animals typically cut off the fins and toss the remainder of the fish, which is often still alive, back into the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This practice, known as <a href="/wiki/Shark_finning" title="Shark finning">finning</a>, is lethal to the shark.</p>
<p>In Native Hawaiian culture, sharks are considered to be gods of the sea, protectors of humans, and cleaners of excessive ocean life. Some of these sharks are believed to be family members who died and have been reincarnated into shark form. However, some sharks are considered man-eaters, also known as <i>niuhi</i>. These sharks include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_white_sharks" title="Great white sharks">great white sharks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiger_sharks" title="Tiger sharks">tiger sharks</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull_sharks" title="Bull sharks">bull sharks</a>. The hammerhead shark, also known as <i>mano kihikihi</i>, is not considered a man-eater or <i>niuhi</i>; it is considered to be one of the most respected sharks of the ocean, an <i>aumakua</i>. Many Hawaiian families believe that they have an <i>aumakua</i> watching over them and protecting them from the <i>niuhi</i>. The hammerhead shark is thought to be the birth animal of some children. Hawaiian children who are born with the hammerhead shark as an animal sign are believed to be warriors and are meant to sail the oceans. It is extremely rare for hammerhead sharks to pass through the waters of <a href="/wiki/Maui" title="Maui">Maui</a>, but many Maui natives believe that hammerhead sharks swimming by is a sign that the gods are watching over the families, and the oceans are clean and balanced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hammerheads_in_captivity">Hammerheads in captivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hammerheads in captivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Great hammerheads have been kept in captivity at several facilities, including <a href="/wiki/Atlantis_Paradise_Island" title="Atlantis Paradise Island">Atlantis Paradise Island Resort</a> (Bahamas), <a href="/wiki/Adventure_Aquarium" title="Adventure Aquarium">Adventure Aquarium</a> (New Jersey), <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Aquarium" title="Georgia Aquarium">Georgia Aquarium</a> (Atlanta), <a href="/wiki/Mote_Marine_Laboratory" title="Mote Marine Laboratory">Mote Marine Laboratory</a> (Florida), and the shark reef exhibit at <a href="/wiki/Mandalay_Bay" title="Mandalay Bay">Mandalay Bay</a> (Las Vegas).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Aquarist Dean Trinh has successfully raised scalloped hammerhead pups, which he has claimed were collected by hand from an environment in which they might otherwise face the prospect of starvation. Other aquarists have noted the importance of providing adequate room for hammerheads to swim constantly and for the provision of mechanical filtration systems to maintain suitable water quality for shark survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2015, the proprietors of the nightclub Atlantis in Adelaide, South Australia, announced plans to keep two hammerhead sharks in a 3-metre (10 ft) diameter cylindrical tank on their premises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The proposal prompted animal-rights activists to start a petition calling for the abandonment of the idea. The petition attracted 35,000 signatures in two weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hammerhead_shark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2013, three endangered commercially valuable sharks, the hammerheads, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_whitetip" title="Oceanic whitetip">oceanic whitetip</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porbeagle" title="Porbeagle">porbeagle</a> were added to Appendix II of <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>, bringing shark fishing and commerce of these species under licensing and regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_31-0"><a href="#cite_note--31">[31]</a></sup></p>
