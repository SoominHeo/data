<p>The <b>Chinese pangolin</b> (<i>Manis pentadactyla</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Pangolin" title="Pangolin">pangolin</a> found in northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>, through most of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (including the islands of <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-3">[3]</a></sup> The Chinese pangolin is one of eight species of pangolins. Although these species are difficult to observe due to their elusive and solitary habits, all eight are believed to be on the decline. Asian pangolin species, especially the Chinese pangolin and the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_pangolin" title="Sunda pangolin">Sunda pangolin</a>, are the most endangered of all the pangolin species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROJECT_PANGOLIN_2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PROJECT_PANGOLIN_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a> reports that the number of Chinese pangolins has declined greatly over the past 15 years. Despite being listed as critically endangered by the IUCN and being protected by <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>, poaching continues to be the main cause of their decline in numbers. Deforestation has also contributed to their depletion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Chinese pangolin has the appearance of a scaly anteater. Its head and body measure about 40â58Â cm and its tail measures about 25â38Â cm (9.8â15.0Â in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A mature Chinese pangolin weighs from 2 to 7 kilograms (4.4 to 15.4Â lb). It has 18 rows of overlapping scales accompanied by hair, a rare combination in mammals. It has a small, narrow mouth and a little, pointed head. Also its claws grow in as it grows older. The female gives birth to a single offspring at a time.</p>
<p>A newborn pangolin weighs about 93Â g (3.3Â oz), its length is about 45Â cm (18Â in). The Chinese pangolin reproduces in April and May when the weather warms. The young also have scales; however, they remain very soft for at least two days, then harden. Although the young pangolin can walk on its first day, the mother carries it on her back or tail. If the mother feels threatened, she immediately folds her baby onto her belly with the help of her tail. Male pangolins have been observed allowing the female and baby to share his burrow.</p>

<p>Chinese pangolins are rather secretive, nocturnal creatures. They move very slowly and are known for their nonaggressive behavior. Their hard scales work as a protective cover from predators, and when they feel threatened, they curl themselves into balls. For further defense, they can climb trees, although this is uncommon.</p>
<p>They mainly eat insects, particularly termites and ants. They dig into ant nests and termite mounds with their large fore claws and extract their prey with their long, sticky tongues.</p>
<p>In Vietnam and Hong Kong, Chinese pangolins are considered a delicacy. They are hunted on a wide scale for human consumption. Factors such as habitat destruction and hunting constantly challenge their survival. Chinese pangolins are now on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species, however, since the forests they inhabit are difficult to patrol, and preventing people from hunting these animals is difficult.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22The_Critter.22">"The Critter"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "The Critter"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Chinese pangolin is probably "The Critter", one of the pets of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raven_FACs" title="Raven FACs">Raven FACs</a> at their secret base in <a href="/wiki/Long_Tieng" title="Long Tieng">Long Tieng</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barrel_Roll" title="Operation Barrel Roll">covert war in Laos</a>. It was described as a foot-long "prehistoric" beast, covered in armor plating with a long tail and a pointed nose, a "cross between a <a href="/wiki/Sloth" title="Sloth">sloth</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Armadillo" title="Armadillo">armadillo</a>", by the US pilots.</p>
<p>After its accidental death, the Critter's body was preserved in a one-gallon jar filled with alcohol. A picture taken of the preserved animal was sent to the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> in Washington, DC, as well as to the natural history department of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Sorbonne" title="La Sorbonne">La Sorbonne</a> in Paris, but no positive reply was forthcoming. For a long time, nobody knew what kind of animal it was until one of the pilots stationed in Laos happened to see the animal on a Laotian postage stamp, part of a stamp series on indigenous animals from Laos, under the name "Panis Auritas".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_needs_and_eating_habits">Dietary needs and eating habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dietary needs and eating habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study done by the <i>Chinese Journal of Applied and Environmental Biology</i> identified the Chinese pangolin as a "susceptible species due to its food specialization and stenophagy (only eating several species of ants and termites)â.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en.cnki.com.cn_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-en.cnki.com.cn-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to the pangolin's very specific diet, it can become arduous to provide the appropriate food for them while they are being observed and maintained. Pangolins are typically held in zoos due to their abilities to feed and preserve the rare animals. However, since the 1970s, "pangolins are now almost unknown to visitors and are exhibited infrequently in zoos", and have "historically been difï¬cult to maintain, with most captive animals dying within a short period after capture". When in their natural habitat, this species lives "on a diet of ants, termites, and various other invertebrates including bee larvae, flies, worms, earthworms, and cricketsâ. After carefully creating new, more sustainable recipes in zoos, some of the ingredients used have included "egg, meat (ground beef, horse, canned feline diet), evaporated milk products, milk powder, fish protein, orchid leaves, commercial chows, psyllium seed, carrots, yeast, multivitamins, and insects (mixtures of silkworm larvae, earth, ants, termites, meal worms, or crickets)". A number of zoos that have kept pangolin's under observation have found that the animals died most commonly after a few years, without breeding successfully. Researchers claim this outcome is correlated to the "poor acceptance of captive diets and digestive problems."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Chinese Pangolin is considered to be high risk in terms of extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en.cnki.com.cn_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-en.cnki.com.cn-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poaching_and_trafficking">Poaching and trafficking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Poaching and trafficking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species" title="Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species">Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species</a> in 2002 prohibited selling pangolins across national borders. Although China has already passed laws to protect the pangolin, it might not be enough to save the species. The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora reports that pangolins are the most trafficked and poached mammal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Chinese pangolin is hunted for its meat, claws, and scales. Pangolin meat, which is considered a delicacy in parts of China and Vietnam, has been reported to sell for as high as US$200/kg. Pangolin scales and blood are in demand in Asia for their supposed medicinal qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROJECT_PANGOLIN_2014_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-PROJECT_PANGOLIN_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> Some Chinese believe pangolin scales, made of <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a>, can be used to treat a wide variety of ailments, from cancer to an upset stomach or asthma. Other pangolin body parts are also used in traditional Chinese medicine. According to one survey composed in 2013, certain Nepalese natives believe pangolin scales are also good-luck charms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Each pangolin has about 500Â g (18Â oz) of scales which can be sold for roughly US$350 on the black market.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Though pangolins have been protected by legislation since the 1970s and 1980s, people still choose to hunt these endangered animals. After random inspections on May 28, 2014, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kwai_Chung_Container_Port" title="Kwai Chung Container Port">Kwai Chung</a> cargo port in Hong Kong, officials detained scales from nearly 8,000 pangolins. Just two weeks later, Hong Kong officials seized a second shipment that contained scales from about 5,000 pangolins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clifton.2C_Merritt_2014_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clifton.2C_Merritt_2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The journal <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frontiers_in_Ecology_%26_the_Environment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frontiers in Ecology &amp; the Environment (page does not exist)">Frontiers in Ecology &amp; the Environment</a></i> estimated that the remains of about 10,000 pangolins are intercepted each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philippine_Park_2014_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philippine_Park_2014-15">[15]</a></sup> Zhao-Min Zhou and Macdonald from <i>Frontiers in Ecology &amp; the Environment</i> claim from their records that 220 living pangolins and the remains of 4,909 dead pangolins were seized in 43 law enforcement actions since 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clifton.2C_Merritt_2014_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clifton.2C_Merritt_2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Forestry_(Indonesia)" title="Ministry of Forestry (Indonesia)">Indonesian Forestry Ministry</a> director of investigations and forest observation, Raffles Panjaitan, told the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakarta_Post" title="Jakarta Post">Jakarta Post</a></i> that in October 2011, his agency had 587 cases of pangolin trafficking since 2006. The estimated value is US$4.3 million worth of pangolins on the illegal market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clifton.2C_Merritt_2014_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Clifton.2C_Merritt_2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2013, a Philippine coast guard inspected a boat where they found 22,000Â lb (10,000Â kg) of pangolin meat. They also discovered 400 boxes containing thousands of frozen skinned pangolins and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaly_anteater" title="Scaly anteater">scaly anteaters</a> from Indonesia. The Regional Trial Court in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Princesa" title="Puerto Princesa">Puerto Princesa</a> city in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palawan_province" title="Palawan province">Palawan</a> province sentenced the boat captain to 12 years in prison and each crew member received from six to 10 years. Each member of the crew was also fined $100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philippine_Park_2014_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Philippine_Park_2014-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> has classified the Chinese pangolin under Appendix II, which means it is not directly threatened with extinction, but will be if measures are not taken to prevent the exploitation of this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucnredlist.org_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucnredlist.org-16">[16]</a></sup> Many of the countries where the Chinese pangolin resides have already passed legislation to protect them. Below is a list of the different countries' legislation in order from oldest to newest laws:</p>
<ul>
<li>1972- <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> fully protected the Chinese Pangolin by classifying it as a Schedule I species under the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Protection_Act,_1972" title="Wildlife Protection Act, 1972">Wildlife Protection Act</a>.</li>
<li>1973- <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> made it a Schedule I protected animal under the National Parks and Wildlife Protection Act.</li>
<li>1976- <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> implemented the Wild Animals Protection Ordinance to protect this species and others that are endangered.</li>
<li>1989- <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> classified the species as a State Category II protected animal with the Protection of Wildlife Act.</li>
<li>1990- <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> added the entire <i><a href="/wiki/Manis" title="Manis">Manis</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> to be protected under the Wildlife Conservation Law, first passed in 1989.</li>
<li>1992- China increased protection of the species using the Regulations on the Implementation of Protection of Terrestrial Wild Animals legislation. In the same year, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> classified the <i>Manis</i> genus as protected under the Wild Animals Reservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535.</li>
<li>1994- <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> enacted the Protection of Wildlife and Wild Plants and Conservation of Natural Areas law, which fully protected the species.</li>
<li>2000- China established more defined terms for the punishment of crimes specifically involving pangolins.</li>
<li>2006- China enacted the Regulations on Management of Import and Export of Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora to meet CITES guidelines. Additionally, Hong Kong increased its protection of the Chinese pangolin with the Protection of Endangered Species of Animals and Plants law. In 2006, Vietnam classified it as a fully protected species in group IIB of Decree 32 in the Management of Endangered, Precious, and Rare Species of Wild Plants and Animals.</li>
<li>2007- China intensified its regulations for the use of pangolins in traditional medicines by terminating pangolin hunting licenses, and requiring current stockpiles of pangolin scales to be registered and subject to trade only through designated buyers, like hospitals. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> declared the species as near extinction, but of high importance in the prohibition category of the Wildlife and Aquatic Law.</li>
<li>2012- <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> granted protection of the Chinese pangolin through the Wildlife Conservation and Security Act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucnredlist.org_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucnredlist.org-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>China has passed much more legislation for pangolin protection than other countries, because the species' population has drastically declined in China over the last few decades. This is the direct result of extreme <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a> for pangolin scales and meat. Clearly, though, legislation is not enough and alternatives to laws need to be considered. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> SSC Pangolin Specialist Group made a proposal in July 2014 to increase awareness, funding, and research for pangolin <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_conservation" title="Wildlife conservation">conservation</a>. Some of the plan's highlights include making protocols to monitor pangolin populations, establishing a consumption index of pangolin products, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_analysis" title="DNA analysis">DNA analysis</a> to determine variation between and within species, and identifying species strongholds to determine best allocation of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Challender.2C_DWS_2014_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Challender.2C_DWS_2014-17">[17]</a></sup> Furthermore, the conservation plan aims to increase patrol-based monitoring around stronghold populations, increase awareness and education about the severity of the problem, and, most importantly, implement a <a href="/wiki/Demand_reduction" title="Demand reduction">demand reduction</a> strategy for pangolin meat and scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Challender.2C_DWS_2014_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Challender.2C_DWS_2014-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another alternative to legislation includes offering positive incentives, like monetary payments or control over land's resources, to local communities for their involvement in conservation efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, the incentives would have to be more beneficial to the community than poaching. Other researchers have proposed the importance of finding biological substitutes for endangered species used in traditional medicines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA_barcoding" title="DNA barcoding">DNA barcoding</a> and analysis could be used to determine what common species are genetically similar enough and produce similar effects as the Chinese pangolin scales. To crack down on poaching, the barcoding technique could also be used for accurate detection of species products being imported and exported.</p>
