<p><i><b>Spirits’ Homecoming</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: 귀향) is a 2016 South Korean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_drama" title="Period drama">period drama</a> film written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Cho_Jung-rae" title="Cho Jung-rae">Cho Jung-rae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-han_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-han-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadline_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-deadline-2">[2]</a></sup> It was released in South Korea on February 24, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-han_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-han-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Production of the movie was halted several times because of financial issues. But 75,200 people donated for the movie to be finished. The date of screening was delayed due to lack of screens to show the movie. However, people brought tickets in advance and issued petitions for the movie to be screened in more cinemas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The first screening was March 1, the Anniversary of the Samil Independence Movement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spirits%27_Homecoming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movie is set during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Korea" title="Japanese occupation of Korea">Japanese occupation of Korea</a> in 1943. The movie tells the story of 14-year old Jung-min and 16-year old Young-hee. Jung-min's family is poor due to frequent warfare but lives happily and peacefully. Because Young-hee's parents died during the war, Young-hee has to take care of her younger brothers. One day, Japanese soldiers forcefully take the two girls from their homes giving them just minutes to say goodbye and pack their clothes, then ship them to Manchuria in wagons for livestock and use them as '<a href="/wiki/Comfort_women" title="Comfort women">comfort women</a>', forcing them to perform sexual service to the servicemen of the Imperial Army.</p>
<p>The second plot line shows an elderly woman who has been a 'comfort woman' in her youth. She sews talismans for sale and does not want to remember those days, but as some former military sexual slaves start to speak up, she decides to declare her status too. She comes to a government office, but the officials mistreat her: they vocally express their disgust towards the victims.</p>
<p>From now on Young-hee, Jung-min and others must only use Japanese, speaking any other languages is punishable. In the brothel girls are given only a tiny room with a bed, with an endless flow of soldiers coming for 10 minutes each. The servicemen are mistreating them, beating them up, and many have <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Sadist" title="Sadist">sadistic</a> tendencies. 'Comfort women' are trying to cope with their situation, but some loose their sanity while others try to stay sane. One of the soldiers treats Jung-min well and gives her a map. Jung-min, Young-hee and four others plan to escape because of the constant sexual assault and beatings, but one of them gets lost in the dark. The Japanese catch her and severely beat up. Jung-min and Young-hee witness that from a hideout and decide to return so that other girls don't get killed.</p>
<p>The former 'comfort woman' makes friends with her old friend's young disciple (the friend is a <a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism" title="Korean shamanism">shaman</a> herself). The disciple is a medium and is able to channel the spirits of the dead to their loved ones. The old woman asks the girl to call her 'grandmother' and they visit her home town together, although 'grandmother' is upset as everything she remembers has changed.</p>
<p>Japanese soldiers torture the 'comfort women' during the night; in the morning they order the escaped girl, the one that got insane, and several injured ones in a lorry promising medical treatment in a bigger camp. In reality the servicemen kill them and burn their corpses. Later the Chinese army starts a counter-attack, and the Japanese decide to kill all the girls at the exact place of previous execution. Jung-min gives Young-hee her personal talisman and asks her to obey the orders, but the Chinese interrupt the execution. Meanwhile the shaman's disciple channels the spirit of Jung-min and elderly Young-hee experiences the events of her adolescence again.</p>
<p>Young-hee and Jung-min happily escape the massacre holding hands. As they walk in tall grass, a wounded Japanese soldier tries to kill them with his sword, but the Chinese shoot him. They take the girls with them, yet the dying Japanese soldier shoots Jung-min, while Young-hee returns home and lives a long life.</p>
<p>The disciple starts a homecoming ritual for the spirits of 'comfort women' killed by the Japanese. The spirit of Jung-min returns to her parents' house and the whole family has a simple feast.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spirits%27_Homecoming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was <a href="/wiki/List_of_2016_box_office_number-one_films_in_South_Korea" title="List of 2016 box office number-one films in South Korea">number-one</a> on its opening weekend in South Korea, with <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$6.4 million</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadline_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-deadline-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motive_of_the_movie">Motive of the movie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spirits%27_Homecoming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Motive of the movie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The director, Cho Jung-rae got the inspiration for the movie from a painting by Kang Il-chul.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> When a young girl, she was abused by the Japanese soldiers as a <a href="/wiki/Comfort_women" title="Comfort women">'Comfort Woman'</a>. The drawing shows a situation she experienced herself: Japanese soldiers pull the 'Comfort Women' who were suffering from diseases or weaknesses to the incinerator and shoot them. The soldiers then set the dead bodies on fire to destroy any proof of what they have done to the girls. Kang Il-chul drew the picture during a psychological treatment session. Through his movie, director Cho Jung-rae wants to reach the 'Comfort Women' who cannot go back to their hometowns and wants to console the damaged memories of the old 'Comfort Women'.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spirits%27_Homecoming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hug-_together_Campaign">Hug- together Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spirits%27_Homecoming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hug- together Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jo entertainment, the production company of the movie, is advertising the 'Hug-Together Campaign'. This campaign is held to cure the hurt and mental pain of former 'Comfort Women' by giving a warm and big hug. It is also pertinent to the modern people who suffer in their lives, work hard and have a wounded heart that needs a warm embrace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
