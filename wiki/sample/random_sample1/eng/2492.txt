<p><b>Sillimanite</b> is an alumino-silicate <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> Al<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>5</sub>. Sillimanite is named after the American chemist <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Silliman" title="Benjamin Silliman">Benjamin Silliman</a> (1779–1864). It was first described in 1824 for an occurrence in <a href="/wiki/Chester,_Connecticut" title="Chester, Connecticut">Chester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Connecticut" title="Middlesex County, Connecticut">Middlesex County, Connecticut</a>, US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mindat_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mindat-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sillimanite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sillimanite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1452" data-file-width="1040" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Sillimanite.jpg/170px-Sillimanite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Sillimanite.jpg/255px-Sillimanite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Sillimanite.jpg/340px-Sillimanite.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>Sillimanite is one of three <a href="/wiki/Aluminosilicate" title="Aluminosilicate">aluminosilicate</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>, the other two being <a href="/wiki/Andalusite" title="Andalusite">andalusite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyanite" title="Kyanite">kyanite</a>. A common variety of sillimanite is known as <i>fibrolite</i>, so named because the mineral appears like a bunch of fibres twisted together when viewed in <a href="/wiki/Thin_section" title="Thin section">thin section</a> or even by the naked eye. Both the fibrous and traditional forms of sillimanite are common in <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphosed</a> <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>. It is an <a href="/wiki/Index_mineral" title="Index mineral">index mineral</a> indicating high temperature but variable pressure. Example rocks include <a href="/wiki/Gneiss" title="Gneiss">gneiss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Granulite" title="Granulite">granulite</a>. It occurs with andalusite, kyanite, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_feldspar" title="Potassium feldspar">potassium feldspar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almandine" title="Almandine">almandine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cordierite" title="Cordierite">cordierite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotite" title="Biotite">biotite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a>, gneiss, <a href="/wiki/Hornfels" title="Hornfels">hornfels</a> and also rarely in <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sillimanite has been found in <a href="/wiki/Brandywine_Springs" title="Brandywine Springs">Brandywine Springs</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Castle_County,_Delaware" title="New Castle County, Delaware">New Castle County, Delaware</a>, US. It was named by the State Legislature in 1977 as the state mineral of Delaware by suggestion of the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Mineralogical_Society" title="Delaware Mineralogical Society">Delaware Mineralogical Society</a>, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Natural sillimanite rocks cut into the required shape and size are used mainly in <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> industries. Sillimanite is the best raw material for the manufacture of high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> refractories or 55-60% alumina bricks. But its use on large scale is not possible due to its fine grading and high cost.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Dumortierite" title="Dumortierite">Dumortierite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mullite" title="Mullite">mullite</a> are similar mineral species found in <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klein-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
