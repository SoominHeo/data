<p><b>Gnaeus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pompeius" title="Pompeius">Pompeius</a></b> (ca. 75 BC – April 12, 45 BC), also known as <b>Pompey the Younger</b> (sometimes spelled Cneius, Gneius), was a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> politician and general from the late <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Republic</a> (1st century BC).</p>
<p>Gnaeus Pompeius was the elder son of <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey the Great</a> (Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus) by his third wife, <a href="/wiki/Mucia_Tertia" title="Mucia Tertia">Mucia Tertia</a>. Both he and his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Sextus_Pompey" title="Sextus Pompey">Sextus Pompey</a> grew up in the shadow of their father, one of Rome's best generals and not originally a conservative politician who drifted to the more traditional faction when <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> became a threat. When Caesar crossed the <a href="/wiki/Rubicon" title="Rubicon">Rubicon</a> in 49 BC, thus starting a civil war, Gnaeus followed his father in their escape to the East, as did most of the conservative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_senate" title="Roman senate">senators</a>. Pompey's army lost the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pharsalus" title="Battle of Pharsalus">Battle of Pharsalus</a> in 48 BC, and Pompey himself had to run for his life, only to be murdered in Egypt on September 29th of the same year.</p>
<p>After the murder, Gnaeus and his brother Sextus joined the resistance against Caesar in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Africa_Province" title="Africa Province">Africa Province</a>. Together with <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Caecilius_Metellus_Pius_Scipio_Nasica" title="Quintus Caecilius Metellus Pius Scipio Nasica">Metellus Scipio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cato_the_younger" title="Cato the younger">Cato the Younger</a> and other senators, they prepared to oppose Caesar and his army to the end. Caesar defeated Metellus Scipio and Cato, who subsequently committed suicide, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thapsus" title="Battle of Thapsus">Battle of Thapsus</a> in 46 BC. Gnaeus escaped, this time to the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, where he joined Sextus. Together with <a href="/wiki/Titus_Labienus" title="Titus Labienus">Titus Labienus</a>, former general in Caesar's army, the Pompey brothers crossed over to <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, comprising modern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>), where they raised yet another army.</p>
<p>Caesar soon followed and, on March 17, 45 BC, the armies met in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Munda" title="Battle of Munda">Battle of Munda</a>. Both armies were large and led by able generals. The battle was closely fought, but eventually a cavalry charge by Caesar turned events to his side. In the battle and the panicked escape that followed, Titus Labienus and an estimated 30,000 men of the Pompeian side died. Gnaeus and Sextus managed to escape another time but supporters were difficult to find. It was by now clear Caesar had won the civil war. Within a few weeks, Gnaeus Pompeius was caught and executed by <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Caesennius_Lento" title="Lucius Caesennius Lento">Lucius Caesennius Lento</a> for treason. Sextus Pompeius was able to keep one step ahead of his enemies, and survived his brother for another decade.</p>
<p>Gnaeus Pompeius married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudia_Pulchra_Minor" title="Claudia Pulchra Minor">Claudia Pulchra</a>, who survived him; they had no children.</p>
