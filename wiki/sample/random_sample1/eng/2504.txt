<p><b>Gradec</b> (<small>Croatian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[grǎdet͡s]</a></span>) or <b>Grič</b> (<small>Croatian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[grîːtʃ]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>Gréc</i></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Mons Graecensis prope Zagrabiam</i></span>) is a part of <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, and together with <a href="/wiki/Kaptol,_Zagreb" title="Kaptol, Zagreb">Kaptol</a> it is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> nucleus of the city. It is situated on the hill of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gornji_Grad_-_Medve%C5%A1%C4%8Dak" title="Gornji Grad - Medveščak">Gornji Grad</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gradec,_Zagreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gradec was given a royal charter by King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_IV" title="Béla IV">Béla IV</a> in 1242. The royal charter, also called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Bull" title="Golden Bull">Golden Bull</a>, was a very important document by which Gradec was declared and proclaimed "a free royal city on Gradec, the hill of Zagreb". This act made Gradec a feudal holding responsible directly to the king. The citizens were given rights of different kinds; among other things they were entitled to elect their own city magistrate (<a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>: <span lang="hr" xml:lang="hr"><i>gradski sudac</i></span>) fulfilling the role of <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a>. They were also entitled to manage their own affairs.</p>
<p>The citizens engaged in building defensive walls and towers around their settlement, fearing a new <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_Europe" title="Mongol invasion of Europe">Mongol invasion</a>. They completed the defensive system at a time between 1242 and 1261. It could be rightly assumed that by building its fortification walls in the middle of the 13th century, Gradec acquired its outward appearance that can be clearly seen in today's Upper Town (Gornji Grad). The defensive walls enclosed the settlement in the shape of a triangle, its top located near the tower called Popov toranj and its base at the south wall (today's Strossmayer Promenade) and Lotrščak tower, which could be explained by the shape of the hill. In some places, rectangular and semicircular towers fortified the defensive walls.</p>
<p>There were four main gates leading to the town: the west gate in the Mesnička Street, the new north gate, later known as the Opatička Street gate, Dverce in the south and the Kamenita vrata (English: Stone gate) in the east. Kamenita vrata is the only gate still preserved to date.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gornji_Grad_.28Upper_Town.29_today">Gornji Grad (Upper Town) today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gradec,_Zagreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gornji Grad (Upper Town) today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gornji Grad is a local government unit, encompassing a population of 3,432.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Undoubtedly, the focal point of Gornji Grad is the square around <a href="/wiki/St._Mark%27s_Church,_Zagreb" title="St. Mark's Church, Zagreb">St. Mark's Church</a> that had been called <a href="/wiki/St._Mark%27s_Square,_Zagreb" title="St. Mark's Square, Zagreb">St. Mark's Square</a> for years. St. Mark's Church is the <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> of Old Zagreb. When guilds developed in Gradec in the 15th, and later in the 17th century, being the societies of craftsmen, their members including masters, journeymen and apprentices would gather regularly in St. Mark's Church.</p>
<p>On the opposite side of the Square at the corner of Basaričekova Street lies the St. Mark's parish office. The house has been standing there since the 16th century, although it underwent reconstruction in the 18th century and had an extension added in the 19th century. At the west end of St. Mark's Square, the mansion called <i><a href="/wiki/Banski_dvori" title="Banski dvori">Banski dvori</a></i>, the former residence of the Civil Governor of Croatia (<a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>: <span lang="hr" xml:lang="hr"><i><a href="/wiki/Ban_(title)" title="Ban (title)">Ban</a></i></span>), was built at the beginning of the 19th century and yet, it can be classed among the Zagreb antiquities. <i>Banski dvori</i>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> mansion beside it, is the seat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Croatia" title="Government of Croatia">Government of the Republic of Croatia</a>. Since 1734, the <a href="/wiki/Croatian_Parliament" title="Croatian Parliament">Croatian Parliament</a> has taken up the east side of St. Mark's Square.</p>
<p>Gornji Grad was recently closed to car traffic except for residents, making it a primarily pedestrian zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gradec_zatvoren_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gradec_zatvoren-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
