<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Grassmannian</h1>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, the <b>Grassmannian</b> <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>V</i>)</span> is a space which parameterizes all <a href="/wiki/Linear_subspace" title="Linear subspace">linear subspaces</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vector space</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> of given <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>. For example, the Grassmannian <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(1, <i>V</i>)</span> is the space of lines through the origin in <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>, so it is the same as the <a href="/wiki/Projective_space" title="Projective space">projective space</a> of one dimension lower than <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>.</p>
<p>When <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> is a real or complex vector space, Grassmannians are <a href="/wiki/Compact_space" title="Compact space">compact</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_manifold" title="Smooth manifold">smooth manifolds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In general they have the structure of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_algebraic_variety" title="Smooth algebraic variety">smooth algebraic variety</a>.</p>
<p>The earliest work on a non-trivial Grassmannian is due to <a href="/wiki/Julius_Pl%C3%BCcker" title="Julius Plücker">Julius Plücker</a>, who studied the set of lines in projective 3-space and parameterized them by what are now called <a href="/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_coordinates" title="Plücker coordinates">Plücker coordinates</a>. Grassmannians are named after <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Grassmann" title="Hermann Grassmann">Hermann Grassmann</a>, who introduced the concept in general.</p>
<p>Notations vary between authors, with <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>V</i>, <i>r</i>)</span> being equivalent to <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>V</i>)</span>, and with some authors using <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span> or <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>n</i>, <i>r</i>)</span> to denote the Grassmannian of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional subspaces of an unspecified <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">n</span>-dimensional vector space.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By giving a collection of subspaces of some vector space a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topological" title="Topological">topological</a> structure, it is possible to talk about a continuous choice of subspace or open and closed collections of subspaces; by giving them the structure of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_manifold" title="Differential manifold">differential manifold</a> one can talk about smooth choices of subspace.</p>
<p>A natural example comes from <a href="/wiki/Tangent_bundle" title="Tangent bundle">tangent bundles</a> of smooth manifolds embedded in <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>. Suppose we have a manifold <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span> of dimension <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span> embedded in <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup></span>. At each point <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span> in <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span>, the tangent space to <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span> can be considered as a subspace of the tangent space of <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup></span>, which is just <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup></span>. The map assigning to <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span> its tangent space defines a map from <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span> to <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span>. (In order to do this, we have to translate the geometrical tangent space to <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span> so that it passes through the origin rather than <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span>, and hence defines a <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional vector subspace. This idea is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Gauss_map" title="Gauss map">Gauss map</a> for surfaces in a 3-dimensional space.)</p>
<p>This idea can with some effort be extended to all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_bundles" title="Vector bundles">vector bundles</a> over a manifold <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span>, so that every vector bundle generates a continuous map from <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span> to a suitably generalised Grassmannian—although various embedding theorems must be proved to show this. We then find that the properties of our vector bundles are related to the properties of the corresponding maps viewed as continuous maps. In particular we find that vector bundles inducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homotopic" title="Homotopic">homotopic</a> maps to the Grassmannian are isomorphic. But the definition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homotopic" title="Homotopic">homotopic</a> relies on a notion of continuity, and hence a topology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_dimensions">Low dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Low dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For <span class="texhtml"><i>r</i> = 1</span>, the Grassmannian <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(1, <i>n</i>)</span> is the space of lines through the origin in <i>n</i>-space, so it is the same as the <a href="/wiki/Projective_space" title="Projective space">projective space</a> of <i>n</i>−1 dimensions.</p>
<p>For <span class="texhtml"><i>r</i> = 2</span>, the Grassmannian is the space of all planes through the origin. In Euclidean 3-space, a plane containing the origin is completely characterized by the one and only line through the origin <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to that plane (and vice versa); hence <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(2, 3) ≅ <b>Gr</b>(1, 3) ≅ <b>P</b><sup>2</sup></span>, the <a href="/wiki/Projective_plane" title="Projective plane">projective plane</a>.</p>
<p>The simplest Grassmannian that is not a projective space is <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(2, 4)</span>, which may be parameterized via <a href="/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_coordinates" title="Plücker coordinates">Plücker coordinates</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Grassmannian_as_a_set">The Grassmannian as a set</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Grassmannian as a set">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Let <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> be a finite-dimensional vector space over a <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">k</span>. The Grassmannian <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>V</i>)</span> is the set of all <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional linear subspaces of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>. If <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> has dimension <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">n</span>, then the Grassmannian is also denoted <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span>.</p>
<p>Vector subspaces of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> are equivalent to linear subspaces of the projective space <span class="texhtml"><b>P</b>(<i>V</i>)</span>, so it is equivalent to think of the Grassmannian as the set of all linear subspaces of <span class="texhtml"><b>P</b>(<i>V</i>)</span>. When the Grassmannian is thought of this way, it is often written as <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i> − 1, <b>P</b>(<i>V</i>))</span> or <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i> − 1, <i>n</i> − 1)</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Grassmannian_as_a_homogeneous_space">The Grassmannian as a homogeneous space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Grassmannian as a homogeneous space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The quickest way of giving the Grassmannian a geometric structure is to express it as a <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_space" title="Homogeneous space">homogeneous space</a>. First, recall that the <a href="/wiki/General_linear_group" title="General linear group">general linear group</a> <span class="texhtml">GL(<i>V</i>)</span> <a href="/wiki/Group_action" title="Group action">acts</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transitive_action" title="Transitive action">transitively</a> on the <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional subspaces of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>. Therefore, if <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">H</span> is the <a href="/wiki/Group_action#Orbits_and_stabilizers" title="Group action">stabilizer</a> of any of the subspaces under this action, we have</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>V</i>) = GL(<i>V</i>)/<i>H</i></span>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>If the underlying field is <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b></span> or <span class="texhtml"><b>C</b></span> and <span class="texhtml">GL(<i>V</i>)</span> is considered as a <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">Lie group</a>, then this construction makes the Grassmannian into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_manifold" title="Smooth manifold">smooth manifold</a>. It also becomes possible to use other groups to make this construction. To do this, fix an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_product" title="Inner product">inner product</a> on <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>. Over <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b></span>, one replaces <span class="texhtml">GL(<i>V</i>)</span> by the <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_group" title="Orthogonal group">orthogonal group</a> <span class="texhtml">O(<i>V</i>)</span>, and by restricting to orthonormal frames, one gets the identity</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>) = O(<i>n</i>)/(O(<i>r</i>) × O(<i>n</i> – <i>r</i>))</span>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>In particular, the dimension of the Grassmannian is <span class="texhtml"><i>r</i>(<i>n</i> – <i>r</i>)</span>.</p>
<p>Over <span class="texhtml"><b>C</b></span>, one replaces <span class="texhtml">GL(<i>V</i>)</span> by the <a href="/wiki/Unitary_group" title="Unitary group">unitary group</a> <span class="texhtml">U(<i>V</i>)</span>. This shows that the Grassmannian is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_manifold" title="Compact manifold">compact</a>. These constructions also make the Grassmannian into a <a href="/wiki/Metric_space" title="Metric space">metric space</a>: For a subspace <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">W</span> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>, let <span class="texhtml"><i>P<sub>W</sub></i></span> be the projection of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> onto <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">W</span>. Then</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Grassmannian_as_a_real_affine_algebraic_variety">The Grassmannian as a real affine algebraic variety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Grassmannian as a real affine algebraic variety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Let <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup>)</span> denote the Grassmannian of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional subspaces of <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup></span>. Let <span class="texhtml">M(<i>n</i>, <b>R</b>)</span> denote the space of real <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i> × <i>n</i></span> matrices. Consider the set of matrices <span class="texhtml"><i>A</i>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>) ⊂ M(<i>n</i>, <b>R</b>)</span> defined by <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i> ∈ <i>A</i>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span> if and only if the three conditions are satisfied:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">X</span> is a projection operator: <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i><sup>2</sup> = <i>X</i></span>.</li>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">X</span> is symmetric: <span class="texhtml"><i>X<sup>t</sup></i> = <i>X</i></span>.</li>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">X</span> has trace <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>: <span class="texhtml">tr(<i>X</i>) = <i>r</i></span>.</li>
</ul>
<p><span class="texhtml"><i>A</i>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span> and <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup>)</span> are homeomorphic, with a correspondence established by sending <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i> ∈ <i>A</i>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span> to the column space of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">X</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duality">Duality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Duality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Every <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional subspace <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">W</span> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> determines an <span class="texhtml">(<i>n</i> – <i>r</i>)</span>-dimensional quotient space <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i>/<i>W</i></span> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span>. This gives the natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_exact_sequence" title="Short exact sequence">short exact sequence</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">0 → <i>W</i> → <i>V</i> → <i>V</i>/<i>W</i> → 0</span>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Taking the <a href="/wiki/Dual_space" title="Dual space">dual</a> to each of these three spaces and linear transformations yields an inclusion of <span class="texhtml">(<i>V/W</i>)<sup>∗</sup></span> in <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sup>∗</sup></span> with quotient <span class="texhtml"><i>W</i><sup>∗</sup></span>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">0 → (<i>V</i>/<i>W</i>)<sup>∗</sup> → <i>V</i><sup>∗</sup> → <i>W</i><sup>∗</sup> → 0</span>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Using the natural isomorphism of a finite-dimensional vector space with its double dual shows that taking the dual again recovers the original short exact sequence. Consequently there is a one-to-one correspondence between <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional subspaces of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> and <span class="texhtml">(<i>n</i> – <i>r</i>)</span>-dimensional subspaces of <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sup>∗</sup></span>. In terms of the Grassmannian, this is a canonical isomorphism</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>V</i>) ≅ <b>Gr</b>(<i>n</i> − <i>r</i>, <i>V</i><sup>∗</sup>)</span>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Choosing an isomorphism of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> with <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sup>∗</sup></span> therefore determines a (non-canonical) isomorphism of <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>V</i>)</span> and <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>n</i> − <i>r</i>, <i>V</i>)</span>. An isomorphism of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> with <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sup>∗</sup></span> is equivalent to a choice of an <a href="/wiki/Inner_product_space" title="Inner product space">inner product</a>, and with respect to the chosen inner product, this isomorphism of Grassmannians sends an <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-dimensional subspace into its <span class="texhtml">(<i>n</i> – <i>r</i>)</span>-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_complement" title="Orthogonal complement">orthogonal complement</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schubert_cells">Schubert cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Schubert cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The detailed study of the Grassmannians uses a decomposition into <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subsets</a> called <i>Schubert cells</i>, which were first applied in <a href="/wiki/Enumerative_geometry" title="Enumerative geometry">enumerative geometry</a>. The Schubert cells for <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span> are defined in terms of an auxiliary <a href="/wiki/Flag_(linear_algebra)" title="Flag (linear algebra)">flag</a>: take subspaces <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>V</i><sub>2</sub>, ..., <i>V<sub>r</sub></i></span>, with <span class="texhtml"><i>V<sub>i</sub></i> ⊂ <i>V</i><sub><i>i</i> + 1</sub></span>. Then we consider the corresponding subset of <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span>, consisting of the <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">W</span> having intersection with <span class="texhtml"><i>V<sub>i</sub></i></span> of dimension at least <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span>, for <span class="texhtml"><i>i</i> = 1, ..., <i>r</i></span>. The manipulation of Schubert cells is <a href="/wiki/Schubert_calculus" title="Schubert calculus">Schubert calculus</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cohomology_ring_of_the_complex_Grassmannian">Cohomology ring of the complex Grassmannian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cohomology ring of the complex Grassmannian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Every point in the complex Grassmannian manifold <span class="texhtml"><b>Gr</b>(<i>r</i>, <i>n</i>)</span> defines an <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>-plane in <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">n</span>-space. Fibering these planes over the Grassmannian one arrives at the <a href="/wiki/Vector_bundle" title="Vector bundle">vector bundle</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">E</span> which generalizes the <a href="/wiki/Tautological_bundle" title="Tautological bundle">tautological bundle</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Projective_space" title="Projective space">projective space</a>. Similarly the <span class="texhtml">(<i>n</i> − <i>r</i>)</span>-dimensional orthogonal complements of these planes yield an orthogonal vector bundle <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">F</span>. The integral <a href="/wiki/Cohomology" title="Cohomology">cohomology</a> of the Grassmannians is generated, as a <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">ring</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Chern_class" title="Chern class">Chern classes</a> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">E</span>. In particular, all of the integral cohomology is at even degree as in the case of a projective space.</p>
<p>These generators are subject to a set of relations, which defines the ring. The defining relations are easy to express for a larger set of generators, which consists of the Chern classes of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">E</span> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">F</span>. Then the relations merely state that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_sum_of_vector_bundles" title="Direct sum of vector bundles">direct sum</a> of the bundles <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">E</span> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">F</span> is trivial. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functoriality" title="Functoriality">Functoriality</a> of the total Chern classes allows one to write this relation as</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grassmann manifolds have found application in <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">computer vision</a> tasks of video-based face recognition and shape recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They are also used in the data-visualization technique known as the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Tour_(data_visualisation)" title="Grand Tour (data visualisation)">grand tour</a>.</p>
<p>Grassmannians allow the <a href="/wiki/Scattering_amplitude" title="Scattering amplitude">scattering amplitudes</a> of subatomic particles to be calculated via a positive Grassmannian construct called the <a href="/wiki/Amplituhedron" title="Amplituhedron">amplituhedron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassmannian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>For an example of use of Grassmannians in <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Gauss_map" title="Gauss map">Gauss map</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Projective_geometry" title="Projective geometry">projective geometry</a>, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_co-ordinates" title="Plücker co-ordinates">Plücker co-ordinates</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_manifold" title="Flag manifold">Flag manifolds</a> are generalizations of Grassmannians and <a href="/wiki/Stiefel_manifold" title="Stiefel manifold">Stiefel manifolds</a> are closely related.</li>
<li>Given a distinguished class of subspaces, one can define Grassmannians of these subspaces, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_Grassmannian" title="Lagrangian Grassmannian">Lagrangian Grassmannian</a>.</li>
<li>Grassmannians provide <a href="/wiki/Classifying_space" title="Classifying space">classifying spaces</a> in <a href="/wiki/K-theory" title="K-theory">K-theory</a>, notably the <a href="/wiki/Classifying_space_for_U(n)" title="Classifying space for U(n)">classifying space for U(n)</a>. In the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Homotopy_theory_of_schemes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Homotopy theory of schemes (page does not exist)">homotopy theory of schemes</a>, the Grassmannian plays a similar rôle for <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_K-theory" title="Algebraic K-theory">algebraic K-theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Affine_Grassmannian" title="Affine Grassmannian">Affine Grassmannian</a>.</li>

</ul>
