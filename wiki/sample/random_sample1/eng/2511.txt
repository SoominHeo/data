<p><b>Graz</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈɡʁaːt͡s]</a></span>) is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Austria#List_of_cities_and_towns_by_population_size" title="List of cities and towns in Austria">second-largest city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> after <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. On 1 January 2017, it had a population of 320,587 (of which 286,686 had principal residence status).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grazpraes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-grazpraes-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2014, the population of the Graz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larger_Urban_Zone" title="Larger Urban Zone">Larger Urban Zone</a> who had principal residence status stood at 605,143.</p>
<p>Graz has a long tradition as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_town" title="University town">university town</a>": its six universities have more than 44,000 students. Its "Old Town" is one of the best-preserved city centres in Central Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Politically and culturally, Graz was for centuries more important for <a href="/wiki/Slovenes" title="Slovenes">Slovenes</a> than <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, and still remains influential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granda_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granda-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, Graz was added to the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> list of <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Cultural Heritage Sites</a>, and the site was extended in 2010 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schloss_Eggenberg_(Graz)" title="Schloss Eggenberg (Graz)">Schloss Eggenberg</a>. Graz was sole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_Capital_of_Europe" title="Cultural Capital of Europe">Cultural Capital of Europe</a> for 2003 and got the title of a City of Culinary Delights in 2008.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the city, Graz, formerly spelled <b>Gratz</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> stems most likely from the Slavic <i>gradec</i>, "small castle". Some archaeological finds point to the erection of a small castle by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_Slavs" title="Alpine Slavs">Alpine Slavic</a> people, which in time became a heavily defended fortification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In literary <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>, <i>gradec</i> still means "small castle", forming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypocoristic" title="Hypocoristic">hypocoristic</a> derivative of Proto-West-South Slavic *gradьcъ, whichs descends via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_metathesis" title="Liquid metathesis">liquid metathesis</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Slavic" title="Common Slavic">Common Slavic</a> *gardьcъ and via the Slavic third <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">palatalisation</a> from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Slavic" title="Proto-Slavic">Proto-Slavic</a> *gardiku (cf. the Ancient Greek toponym <span lang="grc"><a href="/wiki/Gardiki,_Trikala" title="Gardiki, Trikala">Γαρδίκι</a></span>), originally denoting "small town, settlement". The name thus follows the common South Slavic pattern for naming settlements as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grad_(Slavic_settlement)" title="Grad (Slavic settlement)">grad</a></i>. The German name 'Graz' first appears in records in 1128.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graz-sh_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-graz-sh-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Graz is situated on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mur_River" title="Mur River">Mur River</a> in the southeast of Austria. It is about 200 km (120 mi) southwest of Vienna. The nearest larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_center" title="Urban center">urban center</a> is <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a> in Slovenia which is about 50 km (31 mi) away. Graz is the capital and largest city in Styria, a green and heavily forested area.</p>

<p>The oldest settlement on the ground of the modern city of Graz dates back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_Age" title="Copper Age">Copper Age</a>. However, no historical continuity exists of a settlement before the Middle Ages.</p>
<p>During the 12th century, dukes under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babenberg" title="Babenberg">Babenberg</a> rule made the town into an important commercial center. Later, Graz came under the rule of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a>, and in 1281, gained special privileges from <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_I_of_Germany" title="Rudolf I of Germany">King Rudolph I</a>.</p>
<p>In the 14th century, Graz became the city of residence of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Austria" title="Inner Austria">Inner Austrian</a> line of the Habsburgs. The royalty lived in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazer_Schlo%C3%9Fberg" title="Grazer Schloßberg">Schloßberg</a> castle and from there ruled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Styria_(duchy)" title="Styria (duchy)">Styria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(duchy)" title="Carinthia (duchy)">Carinthia</a>, most of today's Slovenia, and parts of Italy (<a href="/wiki/Carniola" title="Carniola">Carniola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gorizia_and_Gradisca" title="Gorizia and Gradisca">Gorizia and Gradisca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>).</p>
<p>In the 16th century, the city's design and planning were primarily controlled by Italian Renaissance architects and artists. One of the most famous buildings built in this style is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Landhaus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Landhaus (page does not exist)">Landhaus</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Domenico_dell%27Allio" title="Domenico dell'Allio">Domenico dell'Allio</a>, and used by the local rulers as a governmental headquarters.</p>

<p>Karl-Franzens-Universität, also called the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Graz" title="University of Graz">University of Graz</a>, is the city's oldest university, founded in 1585 by Archduke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Austria" title="Charles II of Austria">Karl II</a>. For most of its existence, it was controlled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic church</a>, and was closed in 1782 by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a> in an attempt to gain state control over educational institutions. Joseph II transformed it into a lyceum where civil servants and medical personnel were trained. In 1827 it was re-instituted as a university by Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_I_of_Austria" title="Franz I of Austria">Franz I</a>, thus gaining the name 'Karl-Franzens Universität,' meaning 'Charles-Francis University.' Over 30,000 students currently study at this university.</p>
<p>The astronomer <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> lived in Graz for a short period. There, he worked as a math teacher and was a professor of mathematics at the University of Graz, but still found time to study astronomy. He left Graz to go to <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutherans</a> were banned from the city.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann" title="Ludwig Boltzmann">Ludwig Boltzmann</a> was Professor for Mathematical Physics from 1869 to 1890. During that time, <a href="/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" title="Nikola Tesla">Nikola Tesla</a> studied electrical engineering at the <a href="/wiki/Graz_University_of_Technology" title="Graz University of Technology">Polytechnic</a> in 1875. Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Otto_Loewi" title="Otto Loewi">Otto Loewi</a> taught at the University of Graz from 1909 until 1938. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivo_Andric" title="Ivo Andric">Ivo Andric</a>, the 1961 Nobel Prize for Literature Laureate obtained his doctorate at the University of Graz. <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Erwin Schrödinger</a> was briefly chancellor of the University of Graz in 1936.</p>
<p>Graz lies in Styria, or <i>Steiermark</i> in German. <i>Mark</i> is an old German word indicating a large area of land used as a defensive border, in which the peasantry is taught how to organize and fight in the case of an invasion. With a strategic location at the head of the open and fertile Mur valley, Graz was often assaulted (unsuccessfully), e.g. by the Hungarians under <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Corvinus" title="Matthias Corvinus">Matthias Corvinus</a> in 1481, and by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> in 1529 and 1532. Apart from the Riegersburg, the Schloßberg was the only fortification in the region that never fell to the Ottoman Turks. Graz is home to the region's provincial armory, which is the world's largest historical collection of Baroque weaponry. It has been preserved since 1551, and displays over 30,000 items.</p>
<p>From the earlier part of the 15th century, Graz was the residence of the younger branch of the Habsburgs, which succeeded to the imperial throne in 1619 in the person of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Ferdinand_II" title="Emperor Ferdinand II">Emperor Ferdinand II</a>, who moved the capital to Vienna. New fortifications were built on the Schloßberg at the end of the 16th century. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a>'s army occupied Graz in 1797. In 1809, the city withstood another assault by the French army. During this attack, the commanding officer in the fortress was ordered to defend it with about 900 men against Napoleon's army of about 3,000. He successfully defended the Schloßberg against eight attacks, but they were forced to give up after the Grande Armée occupied Vienna and the Emperor ordered to surrender. Following the defeat of Austria by Napoleonic forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wagram" title="Battle of Wagram">Battle of Wagram</a> in 1809, the fortifications were demolished using explosives, as stipulated in the Peace of <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%B6nbrunn_Palace" title="Schönbrunn Palace">Schönbrunn</a> of the same year. The belltower and the civic clock tower, often used as the symbol of Graz, were spared after the people of Graz paid a ransom for their preservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graz-sh_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-graz-sh-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Archduke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Austria" title="Charles II of Austria">Karl II</a> of Inner Austria had 20,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> books burned in the square of what is now a mental hospital, and succeeded in returning Styria to the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archduke_Franz_Ferdinand" title="Archduke Franz Ferdinand">Archduke Franz Ferdinand</a> was born in Graz, in what is now the Stadtmuseum (city museum).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its position southeast of the Alps, Graz is shielded from the prevailing westerly winds that bring weather fronts in from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> to northwestern and central Europe. The weather in Graz is thus influenced by the Mediterranean, and it has more hours of sunshine per year than Vienna or Salzburg and also less wind or rain. Graz lies in a basin that is only open to the south, causing the climate to be warmer than would be expected at that latitude. Plants are found in Graz that normally grow much further south. However, this milder, less windy climate is detrimental to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> in Graz as it makes the city prone to <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a> in winter. The exhaust fumes of the around 120,000 cars driven into Graz every weekday by people living in the surrounding areas, together with the car journeys made by the inhabitants of Graz itself, are the most significant source of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>.</p>

<li>average temperatures: <a href="/wiki/Graz_Airport" title="Graz Airport">Graz Airport</a> 8.7 °C (48 °F) / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl-Franzens_University" title="Karl-Franzens University">Karl-Franzens University</a> 9.4 °C (49 °F)</li>
<li>average rainfall: 818 mm (32 in) with on average 92 days of rain (Karl Franzens University)</li>
<li>average hours of sunshine: 1,989 (Karl Franzens University)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slovenes_and_Graz">Slovenes and Graz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Slovenes and Graz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Politically, culturally, scientifically and religiously, Graz was an important centre for all the <a href="/wiki/Slovenes" title="Slovenes">Slovenes</a>, especially from the establishment of the University of Graz in 1586 until the establishment of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ljubljana" title="University of Ljubljana">University of Ljubljana</a> in 1919. In 1574, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Compendium_Catechismi_in_Slauonica_lingua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Compendium Catechismi in Slauonica lingua (page does not exist)">first Slovene Catholic book</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://sl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compendium_Catechismi_Catholici_in_Slavonica_lingua_per_Questiones_in_gratiam_Catholicae_iuventutis_propositum" title="sl:Compendium Catechismi Catholici in Slavonica lingua per Questiones in gratiam Catholicae iuventutis propositum">sl</a>)</span> was published in Graz, and in 1592, <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Megiser" title="Hieronymus Megiser">Hieronymus Megiser</a> published in Graz the book <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionarium_quatuor_linguarum" title="Dictionarium quatuor linguarum">Dictionarium quatuor linguarum</a></i>, the first multilingual dictionary of Slovene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Styria_(Slovenia)" title="Styria (Slovenia)">Styrian</a> Slovenes did not consider Graz a German city, but their own, a place to study while living at their relatives' homes and to fulfill one's career ambitions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The student associations in Graz were a crucible of the Slovene identity and the Slovene students in Graz were more nationally aware than some others. This led to fierce anti-Slovene efforts of German nationalists in Graz before and during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granda_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Granda-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many Slovenian Styrians study there. The Slovenes are among the professors at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_for_Jazz,_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute for Jazz, Graz (page does not exist)">Institute for Jazz</a> in Graz. Numerous Slovenes have found employment there, while being formally unemployed in Slovenia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granda_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Granda-3">[3]</a></sup> For the Slovene culture, Graz remains permanently important due to its university and the <a href="/wiki/Universalmuseum_Joanneum" title="Universalmuseum Joanneum">Universalmuseum Joanneum</a> archives containing numerous documents from the Slovenian Styria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granda_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Granda-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A symposium on the relation of Graz and the Slovenes was held in Graz in 2010, at the occasion of the 200th anniversary of the establishment of the first and oldest chair of Slovene. It was established at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Graz_Lyzeum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Graz Lyzeum (page does not exist)">Lyzeum of Graz</a> in July 1811 on the initiative of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Janez_Nepomuk_Primic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Janez Nepomuk Primic (page does not exist)">Janez Nepomuk Primic</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://sl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janez_Nepomuk_Primic" title="sl:Janez Nepomuk Primic">sl</a>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A collection of lectures on the topic was published. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po%C5%A1ta_Slovenije" title="Pošta Slovenije">Slovenian Post</a> commemorated the anniversary with a stamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>For Graz's stint as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_Capital_of_Europe" title="Cultural Capital of Europe">Cultural Capital of Europe</a> some strikingly modern new public buildings were erected in the city. The most famous is the <a href="/wiki/Kunsthaus_Graz" title="Kunsthaus Graz">Kunsthaus</a> (house of modern art) designed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cook_(architect)" title="Peter Cook (architect)">Peter Cook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colin_Fournier" title="Colin Fournier">Colin Fournier</a>, a museum constructed right next to the river Mur, and the "<a href="/wiki/Murinsel" title="Murinsel">Murinsel</a>" (island in the Mur), an island made of steel, situated in the river. It was designed by the American architect <a href="/wiki/Vito_Acconci" title="Vito Acconci">Vito Acconci</a> and contains a café, an open-air theatre and a playground.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_town">Old town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Old town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Innere_Stadt_(Graz)" title="Innere Stadt (Graz)">Innere Stadt (Graz)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg/220px-Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg/330px-Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg/440px-Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grazer_Schlossberg_Kriegssteig.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazer_Schlo%C3%9Fberg" title="Grazer Schloßberg">Grazer Schloßberg</a> (Castle mountain) with clock tower</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The old town was added to the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage List</a> in 1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graz-sh_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-graz-sh-5">[5]</a></sup> due to the harmonious co-existence of typical buildings from different epochs and in different architectural styles. Situated in a cultural borderland between Central Europe, Italy and the Balkan States, Graz absorbed various influences from the neighbouring regions and thus received its exceptional townscape. Today the old town consists of over 1000 buildings, their age ranging from Gothic to Contemporary.</p>
<p>The most important sights in the old town are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Rathaus</b> (Town Hall).</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazer_Schlo%C3%9Fberg" title="Grazer Schloßberg">Schloßberg</a></b>, hill dominating the old town (475 m (1,558.40 ft) high), site of demolished fortress, with views over Graz.</li>
<li><b>Uhrturm</b> clocktower, symbol of Graz, on the top of Schloßberg.</li>
<li><b>Neue Galerie</b>. Museum of art.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schlo%C3%9Fbergbahn_(Graz)" title="Schloßbergbahn (Graz)">Schloßbergbahn</a></b>, a funicular railway up the Schloßberg.</li>
<li>The <b>Landhaus</b>, the building where the federal state parliament of Styria resides, a palace in Lombardic style. It is one of the most important examples of Renaissance architecture in Austria and was built by the Italian architect <a href="/wiki/Domenico_dell%27Allio" title="Domenico dell'Allio">Domenico dell'Allio</a> between 1557 and 1565.</li>
<li>The <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landeszeughaus" title="Landeszeughaus">Landeszeughaus</a></b>, armoury, the largest of its kind in the world.</li>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/Graz_Opera" title="Graz Opera">Opernhaus</a></b>, the principal venue for opera, ballet, and operetta performances. It is the 2nd largest opera house in Austria.</li>
<li>The <b>Schauspielhaus</b>, the principal theatre for productions of plays.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Graz_Cathedral" title="Graz Cathedral">Dom</a></b> (cathedral), a rare monument of Gothic architecture. Once, there were many frescos on the outer walls; today, only a few remain, like the <i>Landplagenbild</i> ("picture of plagues") painted in 1485, presumably by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_von_Villach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas von Villach (page does not exist)">Thomas von Villach</a>. The three plagues it depicts are locusts, pestilence and the invasion of the Turks, all of them striking the town in 1480. It features the oldest painted view of Graz.</li>
<li><b>Mausoleum</b> of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand II</a> next to the cathedral, the most important building of <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a> in Graz. It includes both the grave where Ferdinand II and his wife are buried, and a church dedicated to St Catherine of Alexandria.</li>
<li><b>Burg</b> (castle complex), with Gothic double staircase, built between 1438 and 1453 by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick III, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick III</a> because the old castle on the Schloßberg was too small and uncomfortable. The <i>Burg</i> remained the residence of the Inner Austrian Court until 1619. Today, it serves as residence of the government of Styria.</li>
<li><b>Gemaltes Haus</b> ("painted house"), in Herrengasse 3. It is completely covered with frescos (painted in 1742 by Johann Mayer).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kunsthaus_Graz" title="Kunsthaus Graz">Kunsthaus</a></b> (museum of modern art).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Murinsel" title="Murinsel">Murinsel</a></b>, an artificial island in the Mur.</li>
<li><b>Buildings</b>, <b>courtyards</b> (e. g. Early Renaissance courtyard of the <i>Former House of Teutonic Knights</i> in Sporgasse 22) and <b>roofscape</b> of the old town.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_the_Old_Town">Outside the Old Town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Outside the Old Town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg/220px-Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg/330px-Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg/440px-Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graz_Schloss_Eggenberg_front_facade.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schloss_Eggenberg_(Graz)" title="Schloss Eggenberg (Graz)">Schloss Eggenberg</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schloss_Eggenberg_(Graz)" title="Schloss Eggenberg (Graz)">Schloss Eggenberg</a></b> a Baroque palace on the western edge of Graz with State rooms and museum. In 2010 it was added to the existing World Heritage site of the historic centre of Graz.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mariatrost_Basilica" title="Mariatrost Basilica">Basilika Mariatrost</a></b> a late Baroque church, on the eastern edge of Graz.</li>
<li>The <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herz-Jesu-Kirche_(Graz)" title="Herz-Jesu-Kirche (Graz)">Herz Jesu Kirche</a></b> is the largest church in Graz with the third highest spire in Austria, built in Gothic Revival style.</li>
<li><b>Calvary Hill</b> in the Gösting area of Graz with a 17th-century calvary and church.</li>
<li>The <b>LKH-Universitätsklinikum</b>, is the biggest hospital of Graz and one of the biggest hospitals in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. It is the largest Jugendstil building complex in Austria and was built between 1904 and 1912. It is run by the state and one of the most renowned hospitals in Austria and Central Europe.</li>
<li>Best viewpoints for vistas of the city are <b><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6sting_Castle" title="Gösting Castle">Ruine Gösting</a></b>, hilltop castle ruins on northwestern edge of city, and <b>Plabutsch/Fürstenstand</b>, behind Schloss Eggenberg with a hilltop restaurant and viewing tower.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During 2003 Graz held the title of "<a href="/wiki/European_Capital_of_Culture" title="European Capital of Culture">European Capital of Culture</a>" and was one of the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> "Cities of Design" in 2011.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Old Town and the adjacent districts are characterized by the historic residential buildings and churches found there. In the outer districts buildings are predominantly of the architectural styles from the second half of the 20th century.</p>
<p>In 1965 the <i>Grazer Schule</i> (School of Graz) was founded. Several buildings around the universities are of this style, for example the green houses by Volker Giencke and the <i>RESOWI</i> center by Günther Domenig.</p>
<p>Before Graz became the European Capital of Culture in 2003, several new projects were realized, such as the <i>Stadthalle</i>, the <i>Kindermuseum</i> (museum for children), the <i>Helmut-List-Halle</i>, the <i>Kunsthaus</i> and the <i>Murinsel</i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/SK_Sturm_Graz" title="SK Sturm Graz">SK Sturm Graz</a> is the main football club of the city, with three Austrian championships and five runner-up seasons. The <a href="/wiki/Grazer_AK" title="Grazer AK">Grazer AK</a> also won an Austrian championship, but went into administration in 2007 and was excluded from the professional league system.</p>
<p>In ice hockey, the <a href="/wiki/ATSE_Graz" title="ATSE Graz">ATSE Graz</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Hockey_League" title="Austrian Hockey League">Austrian Hockey League</a> champion in 1975 and 1978. The <a href="/wiki/EC_Graz" title="EC Graz">EC Graz</a> was runner-up in 1991-92, 1992-93 and 1993-94. The <a href="/wiki/Graz_99ers" title="Graz 99ers">Graz 99ers</a> plays in first division since 2000.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/UBSC_Raiffeisen_Graz" title="UBSC Raiffeisen Graz">UBSC Raiffeisen Graz</a> plays in the <a href="/wiki/%C3%96sterreichische_Basketball_Bundesliga" title="Österreichische Basketball Bundesliga">Austrian Basketball League</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tramway_graz23.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1924" data-file-width="2960" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Tramway_graz23.jpg/220px-Tramway_graz23.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Tramway_graz23.jpg/330px-Tramway_graz23.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Tramway_graz23.jpg/440px-Tramway_graz23.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Tram at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jakominiplatz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jakominiplatz (page does not exist)">Jakominiplatz</a></div>

<p>An extensive <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> network makes Graz an easy city to navigate without a car. The city has a comprehensive bus network, complementing the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Graz" title="Trams in Graz">Graz tram network</a> consisting of eight lines. Four lines pass through the new underground tramstop at the main railway station (Hauptbahnhof) and on to the old town before branching out. Furthermore, there are seven night-time bus routes, although these run only at weekends and on evenings preceding public holidays.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Schlossbergbahn_(Graz)" title="Schlossbergbahn (Graz)">Schlossbergbahn</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funicular_railway" title="Funicular railway">funicular railway</a>, and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Schlossberg_lift&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Schlossberg lift (page does not exist)">Schlossberg lift</a>, a vertical lift, link the city centre to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazer_Schlo%C3%9Fberg" title="Grazer Schloßberg">Schloßberg</a>.</p>
<p>From the main railway station (<a href="/wiki/Graz_Hauptbahnhof" title="Graz Hauptbahnhof">Graz Hauptbahnhof</a>), regional trains link to most of Styria. Direct trains also run to most major cities nearby including <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a> in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a> in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brno" title="Brno">Brno</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Trains for Vienna leave every hour. In recent years many railway stations within the city limits and in the suburbs have been rebuilt or modernised and are now part of the "S-Bahn Graz", a commuter train service connecting the city with its suburban area and towns nearby.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graz_Airport" title="Graz Airport">Graz Airport</a> is about 10 km (6 mi) south of the city centre and has a railway station ("S-Bahn") within walking distance (east of the airport). Graz Airport has flights to various destinations including Frankfurt, Munich, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, Zürich, Istanbul and Vienna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Ryanair formerly flew to London (Stansted) from Graz until 2012.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Graz there are seven hospitals, several private hospitals and sanatoriums, as well as 44 pharmacies.</p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LKH-Universit%C3%A4tsklinikum_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LKH-Universitätsklinikum Graz (page does not exist)">LKH-Universitätsklinikum Graz</a> is one of the hospitals that can provide maximum care, with 1556 beds and 7190 employees. It covers the east of the city. In the west of the city there is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LKH_Graz-West&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LKH Graz-West (page does not exist)">LKH Graz-West</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eggenberg_(Graz)" title="Eggenberg (Graz)">Eggenberg</a> with 280 beds and about 500 employees, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Landesnervenklinik_Sigmund_Freud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Landesnervenklinik Sigmund Freud (page does not exist)">Landesnervenklinik Sigmund Freud</a> (LSF) in <a href="/wiki/Stra%C3%9Fgang" title="Straßgang">Straßgang</a> with 880 beds and 1,100 employees, as well as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Allgemeine_Unfallversicherungsanstalt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Allgemeine Unfallversicherungsanstalt (page does not exist)">Unfallkrankenhaus der AUVA</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eggenberg_(Graz)" title="Eggenberg (Graz)">Eggenberg</a> with 180 beds and a total of 444 employees.</p>
<p>Furthermore, there is the geriatric hospital Albert-Schweitzer-Klinik in the west of the city with 304 beds, the Krankenhaus der Barmherzigen Brüder I in <a href="/wiki/Lend_(Graz)" title="Lend (Graz)">Lend</a> with 225 beds, the Krankenhaus der Barmherzigen Brüder II in <a href="/wiki/Eggenberg_(Graz)" title="Eggenberg (Graz)">Eggenberg</a> with 260 beds and the Krankenhaus der Elisabethinen in <a href="/wiki/Gries_(Graz)" title="Gries (Graz)">Gries</a> with 182 beds.</p>
<p>There are several private clinics as well: the Privatklinik Kastanienhof, the Privatklinik Leech, the Privatklinik der Kreuzschwestern, the Sanatorium St. Leonhard, the Sanatorium Hansa and the Privatklinik Graz-Ragnitz.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_services_in_Austria" title="Emergency medical services in Austria">EMS</a> in Graz is provided solely by the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Red_Cross" title="Austrian Red Cross">Austrian Red Cross</a>. Perpetually two emergency doctor's cars (<i>NEF – Notarzteinsatzfahrzeug</i>), two NAWs (<i>Notarztwagen</i> – ambulances staffed with a doctor in addition to regular personnel) and about 30 RTWs (<i>Rettungswagen</i> – regular ambulances) are on standby. Furthermore, several non-emergency ambulances (<i>KTW – Krankentransportwagen</i>) and a <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit#Mobile_intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">Mobile Intensive Care Unit (MICU)</a> are operated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> in order to organise transportation of non-emergency patients to and between hospitals. In addition to the Red Cross the Arbeiter-Samariter-Bund Österreichs (Labor-Samaritan-Alliance), the Malteser Hospitaldienst Austria (the Austrian organisation of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Malta_Ambulance_Corps" title="Order of Malta Ambulance Corps">Order of Malta Ambulance Corps</a>) and the Grünes Kreuz (<i>Green Cross</i>) operate various non-emergency ambulances (<i>KTW</i>) for non-emergency patient transportation. In addition to the land-ambulances there's also the C12 air ambulance helicopter stationed at Graz airport, which is also staffed with an emergency doctor in addition to regular personnel.</p>
