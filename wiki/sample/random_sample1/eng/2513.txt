<p><i><b>Gran Turismo 4</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">グランツーリスモ4</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Guran Tsūrisumo Fō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, commonly abbreviated <i><b>GT4</b></i>)</span> is a 2004 <a href="/wiki/Racing_video_game" title="Racing video game">racing video game</a>, and the fourth installment in the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(series)" title="Gran Turismo (series)"><i>Gran Turismo</i> series</a>. It is also the final game to support the <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>. It was developed by <a href="/wiki/Polyphony_Digital" title="Polyphony Digital">Polyphony Digital</a> and published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony Computer Entertainment</a> and was released on December 27, 2004 in Japan and Hong Kong (NTSC-J), February 22, 2005 in North America (NTSC-U/C), and March 9, 2005 in Europe (PAL), and has since been re-issued under Sony's 'Greatest Hits' line. <i>Gran Turismo 4</i> is one of only four titles for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> that is capable of <a href="/wiki/1080i" title="1080i">1080i</a> output, for another release being <a href="/wiki/Tourist_Trophy_(video_game)" title="Tourist Trophy (video game)">Tourist Trophy</a> which was also created by Polyphony Digital, and based heavily on the physics engine of <i>GT4</i>.</p>
<p>Originally slated for a 2003 release, <i>GT4</i> was delayed for over a year and a half by Polyphony Digital, and had its online mode removed (later added in <i>Gran Turismo</i> online test version). <a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(video_game)" title="Gran Turismo (video game)">Gran Turismo</a> has held the title of being the number one seller and highest production Polyphony Digital has ever seen. The game features over 700 cars from 80 manufacturers, from as early as the 1886 Daimler Motor Carriage, and as far into the future as concepts for 2022. The game also features 51 tracks, many of which are new or modified versions of old <i>Gran Turismo</i> favorites, with some notable real-world additions.</p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>GT4</i> was met with critical acclaim and was a commercial success, becoming one of the highest selling games of 2005. The Chinese, Japanese, and South Korean versions of the game were bundled with a 212-page driving guide and lessons on the physics of racing. A limited edition, <i>Gran Turismo 4 Online test version</i>, was released in Japan in summer 2006. A <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PSP</a> enhanced port entitled <i>Gran Turismo Mobile</i> was originally planned for development, but was later renamed to <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(PSP)" title="Gran Turismo (PSP)">Gran Turismo</a></i>, which was released October 1, 2009. The follow-up game, <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_5" title="Gran Turismo 5">Gran Turismo 5</a></i>, was released in 2010 exclusively for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Players now accumulate points by winning races in the normal first-person driving mode, called <i>A-Spec</i> mode. Each race event can yield up to a maximum of 200 A-Spec points. Generally, a win using a car with less of an advantage over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_artificial_intelligence" title="Game artificial intelligence">AI</a> opponents is worth more points. Points can only be won once, so to win further points from a previously-won event, it must be re-won using a car with less of an advantage over the AI. There are also the 34 <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_4#Driving_missions" title="Gran Turismo 4">Missions</a></i> which can yield 250 points each. Despite this, A-Spec points are experience points, not money.</p>

<p>The new <i>B-Spec</i> mode puts players in the place of a racing <a href="/wiki/Pit_stop" title="Pit stop">crew chief</a>: telling the driver how aggressively to drive, when to pass, and mandating <a href="/wiki/Pit_stop" title="Pit stop">pit stops</a> (by monitoring tire wear and fuel level). The speed of the time in the race can be increased up to 3x, allowing for Endurance races to be completed in less time than would take in A-Spec mode. The 3x feature, however, must be turned on after every pit stop because it resets to normal time. The game manual says that the player may speed up B-Spec mode by up to 5x, but this is believed to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typo" title="Typo">typo</a>. B-Spec points are given out for each race completed in B-Spec mode. This increases the skill level of the AI driver in the categories of vehicle skill, course skill, and battle skill. Players can thereby use B-Spec mode in harder races as the game progresses, but this mode cannot be used on wet, dirt and snow courses.</p>
<p>Another new addition to the game is the <i>Driving Missions</i>, which are similar in experience to the license tests, but award successful completion with 250 A-Spec points and 1000 or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Credits_(science_fiction)" title="Credits (science fiction)">credits</a>. Each mission takes place with a given car on a given track or section of track, and a given set of opponents. There are 4 sets of missions: <i>The Pass</i>, in which the driver must overtake an opponent within a certain distance; <i>3 Lap Battle</i>, in which the driver must pass 5 opponents over the course of 3 laps; <i>Slipstream Battle</i>, in which the driver must overtake identical opponents by way of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drafting_(racing)" title="Drafting (racing)">drafting</a>; and <i>1 Lap Magic</i>, in which the driver starts with a significant time penalty against much slower opponents and must overtake them all in the space of a single lap. Completing each set of missions earns the player a prize car. There are a total of 5 prize cars available to be won, they are the <a href="/wiki/DeLorean_DMC-12" title="DeLorean DMC-12">DeLorean DMC-12</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay_Leno_Tank_Car" title="Jay Leno Tank Car">Jay Leno Tank Car</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pagani_Zonda" title="Pagani Zonda">Pagani Zonda</a> Race Car, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_7" title="Toyota 7">Toyota 7</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_R89C" title="Nissan R89C">Nissan R89C</a>.</p>
<p>A new <i>Photo Mode</i> is included in the game, which allows the player to control a virtual camera, taking pictures of their cars on the track or at specific locations, including the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a>. This game is able to produce a selection of screenshots with variable compression rate (Normal/Fine/SuperFine) and size (up to 1280x960 72dpi), and the user can choose to save or print to a supported USB device.</p>
<p>Compared with <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_3:_A-Spec" title="Gran Turismo 3: A-Spec">Gran Turismo 3: A-Spec</a></i>, graphics are greatly improved (despite running on the same PlayStation 2 hardware). The physics are also greatly improved, with the major upgrade that cars now experience body movement, such as pitching (forwards and backwards rolling) under braking. Barriers have considerably more friction to slow down the cars in GT4 (in an attempt to stop the use of "wall riding"), but there is minimal friction between cars, so the advantage obtained by running into the side of another car (instead of braking) is still present. The driving missions do give a 5-second penalty for hitting the walls or opponents' cars in this way, where the car's speed is restricted until the counter reaches zero.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware_compatibility">Hardware compatibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hardware compatibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>GT4</i> supports <a href="/wiki/Dolby_Digital" title="Dolby Digital">Dolby Digital</a> surround, <a href="/wiki/480p" title="480p">480p</a>/<a href="/wiki/1080i" title="1080i">1080i</a> (<a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a> only) and <a href="/wiki/Widescreen" title="Widescreen">widescreen</a> modes, but 1080i is only supported in single player races. Despite the lack of online gameplay, <i>GT4</i> does support use of the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> Network Adapter, which can be used to communicate with additional PS2s to create a multi-screen setup. In addition, the Network Adapter can be used to play games on a local <a href="/wiki/Subnetwork" title="Subnetwork">subnet</a> for up to six players, though player customized cars cannot be used in a LAN game. Support for the <a href="/wiki/Logitech" title="Logitech">Logitech</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driving_Force_Pro" title="Driving Force Pro">Driving Force Pro</a> and GT Force steering wheels is continued from <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_3:_A-Spec" title="Gran Turismo 3: A-Spec">Gran Turismo 3: A-Spec</a></i>. Other "PC" steering wheels previously (and unofficially) supported in <i>GT3</i> were explicitly disabled for <i>GT4</i>. New support is given for <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USB</a> storage and print devices used in Photo Mode.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicles">Vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>GT4</i> continues in its predecessors' footsteps by offering an extremely large list of cars; the PAL version, for example, features 721 cars from 80 manufacturers. There are differences in the car lists between the different <i>GT4</i> regional versions, and some cars have different names, e.g. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Domestic_Market" title="Japanese Domestic Market">JDM</a> <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Vitz" title="Toyota Vitz">Toyota Vitz</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Yaris" title="Toyota Yaris">Toyota Yaris</a> in places such as Europe and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the second generation <a href="/wiki/Mazda_Demio" title="Mazda Demio">Mazda Demio</a> is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mazda_2" title="Mazda 2">Mazda 2</a> in the same places (PAL version only), and a well spotted example is the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Speedster" title="Opel Speedster">Opel Speedster</a> which is more commonly known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vauxhall_VX220" title="Vauxhall VX220">Vauxhall VX220</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Z-car" title="Nissan Z-car">Nissan Z-car</a> is known as the Nissan Fairlady Z in the NTSC-J version. One notable omission of the game is that it does not feature some well-known car manufacturer names such as Porsche, Ferrari, and Lamborghini. However, RUF, a company that produces Porsche-like cars, but working in its own rights, is in GT4 replacing Porsche. Some of the cars are multiple variations on a single base model; there are 20 different <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Impreza" title="Subaru Impreza">Subaru Imprezas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Legacy" title="Subaru Legacy">Legacies</a>, 25 <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Lancer" title="Mitsubishi Lancer">Mitsubishi Lancer</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Lancer_Evolution" title="Mitsubishi Lancer Evolution">Lancer Evolutions</a>, and 48 <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Skyline" title="Nissan Skyline">Nissan Skylines</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_GT-R" title="Nissan GT-R">Nissan GT-R</a> Concept. One vehicle, another Skyline, is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pace_car" title="Pace car">pace car</a> from the "Guide Lap" licence tests and is also a prize car. There is also the GT Edition, which was the pace car without the pace car lights, and even more power (541 horsepower, the standard version packs 276). Each vehicle model has over 5000 polygons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo2-2">[2]</a></sup> Car prices range from about 2500 credits for basic 1980s Japanese used cars up to 4.5 million (450,000,000 in the Japanese version) credits for the top end (mostly <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans_Prototype" title="Le Mans Prototype">Le Mans</a>) race cars. Some special prize-only cars (such as the <a href="/wiki/Pagani_Zonda" title="Pagani Zonda">Pagani Zonda</a> LM Race Car '01) are not visible in the vehicle showrooms, and a few do not have corresponding dealerships, and thus are unmodifiable, for example, the Formula Gran Turismo (Formula 1 car). Also, some concept cars featured in the game ended up becoming mass production cars, such as the <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Swift" title="Suzuki Swift">Suzuki Swift</a>.</p>
<p><i>GT4</i> is responsible for a few vehicle firsts in the <i>Gran Turismo</i> series. It is the first to feature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pickup_trucks" title="Pickup trucks">pickup trucks</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Tacoma" title="Toyota Tacoma">Toyota Tacoma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dodge_Ram" title="Dodge Ram">Dodge Ram</a>. It is the first game in the series to feature the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delorean_DMC-12" title="Delorean DMC-12">DeLorean</a>, using the stage II spec engine (developed in 2004, hence the 2004 designation). It is also the first in the series to feature a diesel-powered car, the <a href="/wiki/BMW_1_Series" title="BMW 1 Series">BMW 120d</a>. A special edition of <i>GT4</i> featuring the 120d (and the rest of the <a href="/wiki/BMW_1_Series" title="BMW 1 Series">1 Series</a> line), and three tracks were provided to <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> customers who purchased their 1 Series automobile before the release of <i>GT4</i>. While <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_2" title="Gran Turismo 2">Gran Turismo 2</a></i> did have a one-off F1 engine version of the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Espace" title="Renault Espace">Renault Espace</a>, <i>GT4</i> was the first of the series to feature a production <a href="/wiki/Minivan" title="Minivan">minivan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Odyssey_(international)" title="Honda Odyssey (international)">Honda Odyssey (JDM version)</a>. A first-generation <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Pajero" title="Mitsubishi Pajero">Mitsubishi Pajero</a> <a href="/wiki/Dakar_Rally" title="Dakar Rally">Paris-Dakar rally</a> car, a winner of the 1985 rally, makes an appearance as the first SUV in racing trim; the first SUV to appear in the GT series was the <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Forester" title="Subaru Forester">Subaru Forester</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_2" title="Gran Turismo 2">Gran Turismo 2</a></i> (although in fact the Forester can be racing modified, and a concept SUV, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toyota_RSC" title="Toyota RSC">Toyota RSC</a>, both in the concept version and a fantasy, Dakar-inspired rally car, existed first in <a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_Concept" title="Gran Turismo Concept">Gran Turismo Concept</a>). It was also the first in the series to feature <a href="/wiki/D1_Grand_Prix" title="D1 Grand Prix">D1 Grand Prix</a> tuned cars such as <a href="/wiki/Ken_Nomura" title="Ken Nomura">Ken Nomura</a>'s Blitz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skyline_GT-R" title="Skyline GT-R">ER34 D1GP</a>.</p>

<p>The game includes some prize cars of historical interest, such as vehicles from as far back as 1886 at the dawn of the automobile. A special car called the <a href="/wiki/Auto_Union_racing_car" title="Auto Union racing car">Auto Union V16 Type C Streamline</a>, built in 1937, can only be used in Power and Speed (which tests the performance of vehicles) or a test drive in <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrburgring" title="Nürburgring">Nürburgring</a>, but interestingly has around 542 HP. Even some modern cars with complex body shapes cannot be raced against opponents, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caterham_Seven" title="Caterham Seven">Caterham Seven</a> Fireblade. In Arcade Mode, these cars can be raced against a single opponent; this is the case for any convertible with the top down.</p>
<p>There is also a special car which is specially tuned and returns from the previous game, called the '<a href="/wiki/Shuichi_Shigeno" title="Shuichi Shigeno">Shuichi Shigeno</a>' version of <a href="/wiki/Toyota_AE86" title="Toyota AE86">Toyota AE86</a>, which is taken from <i><a href="/wiki/Initial_D" title="Initial D">Initial D</a></i>, and is licensed by <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>, although Toyota has never used the name of the author of <i>Initial D</i>. Another <i>Initial D</i> car, the <a href="/wiki/Sileighty" title="Sileighty">Sileighty</a>, makes another returning appearance. Comedian <a href="/wiki/Jay_Leno" title="Jay Leno">Jay Leno</a>, an avid car collector, is listed in the game as a manufacturer; one of his custom cars, the <a href="/wiki/Blastolene_Special" title="Blastolene Special">Blastolene Special</a> or "Tank Car", is included in the game as a prize car, available after beating missions 11-20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-channel4_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-channel4-3">[3]</a></sup> The 2022 <a href="/wiki/Nike_ONE" title="Nike ONE">Nike ONE</a> has <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a> on both sides of the car. They read "www.phil-frank.com", the website of the artist commissioned to design this car for <i>GT4</i>, and "PFD", the initials of the artist's company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamespot_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gamespot-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phil_Frank_Design_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phil_Frank_Design-5">[5]</a></sup> There is also Morse code visible on the inside of all four tires that read "PFD" when mirrored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morse_code_on_Nike_One_2022_-_GTPlanet_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morse_code_on_Nike_One_2022_-_GTPlanet-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xenns_GT4_Research_Topic_-_GTPlanet_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Xenns_GT4_Research_Topic_-_GTPlanet-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>GT4</i> retains all the familiar tuning parameters from the previous games in the series, but also allows weight to be added to the car. This can be positioned to affect handling or used as a form of handicapping. Another new vehicle tuning addition is <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> injection. Also, GT Auto can now install a rear wing on some cars, making it possible to adjust the car's rear downforce, which was previously only possible on racing cars or, on the first two games in the series, cars with the racing modification performed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracks">Tracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The game features 51 tracks, which are divided into 4 groups: World Circuits, Original Circuits, City Courses, and Dirt &amp; Snow. Many of the tracks are new or modified versions of old Gran Turismo favorites. Notable real-world track inclusions are the longest circuit <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrburgring" title="Nürburgring">Nürburgring</a> Nordschleife, <a href="/wiki/Suzuka_Circuit" title="Suzuka Circuit">Suzuka Circuit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twin_Ring_Motegi" title="Twin Ring Motegi">Twin Ring Motegi</a> (with three Road Course configurations, as well as the Super Speedway) and <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_la_Sarthe" title="Circuit de la Sarthe">Circuit de la Sarthe</a> (Le Mans). There are also tracks modelled after world-famous attractions such as New York City's <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_George-V" title="Hôtel George-V">Hôtel George-V</a> Paris, and the <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas Strip</a>. The Hong Kong course is located in the <a href="/wiki/Tsim_Sha_Tsui" title="Tsim Sha Tsui">Tsim Sha Tsui</a> district, which, in its clockwise configuration, starts at <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Road,_Hong_Kong" title="Salisbury Road, Hong Kong">Salisbury Road</a>, passes through the city's waterfront and then <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Road" title="Nathan Road">Nathan Road</a>.</p>
<p>The Città d'Aria course follows actual roads in <a href="/wiki/Assisi" title="Assisi">Assisi</a>, Italy. The race starts/finishes in the piazza in front of the temple Minerva. Just before the start/finish line on this course, written on the tarmac is an inscription in broken Italian: "Dio lo benedice — fate il suo guidare il più sicuro e divertirsi", which, when translated, means "God blesses him; make his driving the most safely and to have fun." On the Seattle circuit, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdome" title="Kingdome">Kingdome</a>, the previous home of the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a>, is visible next to their current stadium, <a href="/wiki/Safeco_Field" title="Safeco Field">Safeco Field</a>. Kingdome was demolished in an implosion on March 26, 2000. Because the Seattle circuit was created for GT2 in 1999, before the Kingdome's demolition, it has been left unchanged and the Mariners' now completed home, <a href="/wiki/Safeco_Field" title="Safeco Field">Safeco Field</a>, remains under construction, not to be completed until 2002. (GT4 reuses the Seattle map the GT3),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A section of the Opera Paris course passing through <a href="/wiki/Place_de_la_Concorde" title="Place de la Concorde">Place de la Concorde</a> was traversed by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lelouch" title="Claude Lelouch">Claude Lelouch</a> in the short film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%27etait_un_rendez-vous" title="C'etait un rendez-vous">C'etait un rendez-vous</a></i>.</p>
<p>There are no changes in weather or time conditions during races. Even during the 24-hour endurance races, there is no transition from day to night or between different types of weather. All of the tracks are run in dry weather, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Tsukuba_Circuit" title="Tsukuba Circuit">Tsukuba</a> Wet Race.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_Issues">Technical Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technical Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

(July 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> speculate on the fact that the laser has "worn out". So it either needs to be properly cleaned, or replaced. <p><i><b>Gran Turismo 4 Prologue</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">グランツーリスモ4 プロローグ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Guran Tsūrisumo Fō Purorōgu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Racing_video_game" title="Racing video game">racing</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(series)" title="Gran Turismo (series)"><i>Gran Turismo</i> series</a> for <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> which is published by <a href="/wiki/Polyphony_Digital" title="Polyphony Digital">Polyphony Digital</a>. It was released on December 4, 2003 in Japan and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and May 26, 2004 in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtsales_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-gtsales-1">[1]</a></sup> For undisclosed reasons, the game was not released in the North American market. This short version title preceded the release of the full-length version <i>Gran Turismo 4</i> in 2004 and was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_5_Prologue" title="Gran Turismo 5 Prologue">Gran Turismo 5 Prologue</a></i> in 2007.</p>
<p>In Japan a limited "Signature Edition" featuring the signature of the series producer <a href="/wiki/Kazunori_Yamauchi" title="Kazunori Yamauchi">Kazunori Yamauchi</a> on the front cover package preceded the release of the regular edition. The regular edition was also bundled with a white ceramic PlayStation 2 console in a Christmas limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock-keeping_unit" title="Stock-keeping unit">SKU</a> called "PlayStation Racing Pack" released in Japan on October 13, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Europe, the game was bundled with a promotional "Making Of" DVD-Video. While intended to be a limited content, the DVD-Video was also included in the Platinum re-release. The DVD was later included in the limited <i>Gran Turismo 4</i> "Special DVD Set" released in Japan on June 28, 2004. The regular edition was discontinued and the game was re-issued under Sony's 'Greatest Hits' line in Japan ("PlayStation 2 the Best") on August 5, 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and Europe ("Platinum"). As <i>Gran Turismo 4</i> was intended to be released in time for the worldwide 2002/2003 Christmas release but was delayed, in consolation, Polyphony brought out <i>Gran Turismo 4 Prologue</i> as a sneak preview (including a documentary bonus disc in Europe) to the full experience of <i>Gran Turismo 4</i>.</p>
<p>Though it was necessary to limit the number of cars and courses included, this product still included some of the main features of the <i>Gran Turismo</i> franchise such as races, time attack and license tests. The sneak preview also expressed the latest technology Polyphony Digital had accumulated over the past two years. Featuring 50 of <i>GT4'</i>s then-planned 500 cars as well as five courses, this expanded demo was designed as a stop-gap until the complete version was released.</p>
<p>The game includes a Driving School (License Tests), Free Run and Time Attack as well as early version of some circuits, like the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> track which was modified in the full version. A new <i>Gran Turismo</i> official steering wheel, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driving_Force_Pro" title="Driving Force Pro">Driving Force Pro</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GT_Force_Pro" title="GT Force Pro">GT Force Pro</a> in Japan and supporting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Force_feedback" title="Force feedback">force feedback</a>, was released by <a href="/wiki/Logitech" title="Logitech">Logitech</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logicool" title="Logicool">Logicool</a> in Japan) to coincide with the <i>Gran Turismo 4 Prologue</i> launch date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The European version came with an extra car not included in the Japanese version; the <a href="/wiki/BMW_M5" title="BMW M5">BMW Concept M5</a>.</p>
<p><i>Gran Turismo 4 Prologue</i> has sold 1.4 million copies since its release.</p>

<p><i><b>Gran Turismo 4 Online test version</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">グランツーリスモ 4 オンライン実験バージョン</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Guran Tsūrisumo 4 Onlain Jīken Bāshyon</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a free 2006 <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> game by <a href="/wiki/Polyphony_Digital" title="Polyphony Digital">Polyphony Digital</a>. This is a limited edition of the 2004/2005 racing game <i>Gran Turismo 4</i> featuring extra online services that were removed from the standard game due to some issues and a lack of time as the game had been delayed several times. Selecting the extra "Online" mode leads to the "Online Home" that features additional game modes, "Quick Race", "Tuned Car Race", "Private Race" (that requires a password) and "Time Attack". "News" inform about the availability for the online courses or special events, with limited date and time. Public online services ended on September 1, 2006.</p>
<p>This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_beta" title="Public beta">public beta</a> for <i>Gran Turismo 4 Online</i> (<i>GT4OL</i>) was freely shipped to 4,700 selected "GranTurismo.com" members from Japan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gran-turismo.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gran-turismo.com-12">[12]</a></sup> and 300 from <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> to collaborate as "test players".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Online services lasted three months from June 1 to September 1, 2006 and included 6-player "Competition" (対戦, <i>taisen</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Trial" title="Time Trial">Time Trial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">chat</a> (mail message and microphone communication), and an Internet ranking chart was available in the game's website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gran-turismo.com_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-gran-turismo.com-12">[12]</a></sup> (A version of the game intended for the United States market has since been discovered)</p>
<p>This test was not intended to precede a <i>Gran Turismo 4 Online</i> full scale release but instead to be used as a way to develop and test online features and structures for the upcoming <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_5" title="Gran Turismo 5">Gran Turismo 5</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> system. Besides the 5,000 test players, seven special guests were invited to test <i>Gran Turismo 4 Online</i>. First, a special event named "Top Racer Battle" (トップレーサーBATTLE) was staged in the Polyphony Digital headquarters on August 17. Six drivers from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JGTC" title="JGTC">JGTC</a> Japanese championship entered the game's online multiplayer "Competition" mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Motoyama won the test round, a 5-lap <a href="/wiki/Fuji_Speedway" title="Fuji Speedway">Fuji Speedway 2005</a> race with the drivers' respective JGTC GT500 cars. The actual Top Racer Battle though, a 10-lap <a href="/wiki/Tsukuba_Circuit" title="Tsukuba Circuit">Tsukuba Circuit</a> one make race on board a <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MX-5" title="Mazda MX-5">Mazda Roadster 1600 NR-A '04</a>, was won by Ryo Michigami. Later from August 25 to September 5, 2006, <a href="/wiki/Kazunori_Yamauchi" title="Kazunori Yamauchi">Kazunori Yamauchi</a> the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(series)" title="Gran Turismo (series)"><i>Gran Turismo</i> series</a> producer (re)invited two Japanese and two European professional racing drivers sponsored by <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">PlayStation</a> to enter GT4OL's Time Attack mode Internet ranking chart and, either to challenge him or another guest in a versus race, either to compete with five test drivers in a 8~10 rounds multiplayer online competition called "trophy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gran-turismo.com_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-gran-turismo.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first guest was <a href="/wiki/Super_GT" title="Super GT">Super GT GT500</a> <a href="/wiki/Nissan_350Z" title="Nissan 350Z">Nissan Motul Pitwork Z</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Satoshi_Motoyama" title="Satoshi Motoyama">Satoshi Motoyama</a> (Top Racer Battle challenger), he entered the dedicated "Motoyama Trophy", a 2-lap 10 races online multiplayer competition, and won three rounds. Second guest specially came from France, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Loeb" title="Sébastien Loeb">Sébastien Loeb</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="2005 24 Hours of Le Mans">LeMans 2005</a> <a href="/wiki/Courage_C60" title="Courage C60">PlayStation Pescarolo C60 Judd LMP Race Car '04</a> driver and <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">WRC</a> champion, he set a new Time Attack Internet record on the <a href="/wiki/24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="24 Hours of Le Mans">24 Hours of Le Mans</a>' <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_la_Sarthe" title="Circuit de la Sarthe">Circuit de la Sarthe I</a> ("Loeb Special Attack") and defeated Kazunori Yamauchi in their Pescarolo Online Time Attack duel on the same circuit (Internet rank 10th vs 11th). The last guests were both Top Racer Battle challengers, one is <a href="/wiki/Ryo_Michigami" title="Ryo Michigami">Ryo Michigami</a>, Super GT GT500 <a href="/wiki/Honda_NSX" title="Honda NSX">Honda Takata Dome NSX</a> driver, the other is Michael Krumm, GT500 Nissan Motul Pitwork Z driver. They competed with each other in a versus battle then entered a special trophy similar to Motoyama's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gran-turismo.com_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-gran-turismo.com-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gran_Turismo_.28PSP.29"><i>Gran Turismo</i> (PSP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gran Turismo (PSP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(PSP)" title="Gran Turismo (PSP)">Gran Turismo (PSP)</a></div>
<p><i><b>Gran Turismo</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PlayStation Portable</a> game developed by <a href="/wiki/Polyphony_Digital" title="Polyphony Digital">Polyphony Digital</a>. The game was announced at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E3_(Electronic_Entertainment_Expo)" title="E3 (Electronic Entertainment Expo)">E3</a> <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> press conference on May 11, 2004, the same conference where Sony announced the PlayStation Portable. It has since been delayed repeatedly and its completion has been pushed back. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E3_2009" title="E3 2009">E3 2009</a>, Gran Turismo PSP was finally confirmed for an October 1, 2009 date, and was launch title for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PSP_Go" title="PSP Go">PSP Go</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The game was intended to be an exact port of the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> game, <i>Gran Turismo 4</i>, although it could include cars from the PlayStation 3 game, Gran Turismo 5 Prologue, and cars to be featured in Gran Turismo 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Gran Turismo 4</i> was met with generally positive reaction from game critics. It received an aggregated score of 89.53% on <a href="/wiki/GameRankings" title="GameRankings">GameRankings</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamerankings_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamerankings-18">[18]</a></sup> and 89/100 on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacritic_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Clarkson" title="Jeremy Clarkson">Jeremy Clarkson</a>, host of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_Gear_(current_format)" title="Top Gear (current format)">Top Gear</a></i> television program, performed a head-to-head test of real life versus <i>GT4</i> on <a href="/wiki/Top_Gear_(series_7)#ep65" title="Top Gear (series 7)">an episode of the program</a>. He ran <a href="/wiki/Mazda_Raceway_Laguna_Seca" title="Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca">Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca</a> in real life and used an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acura_NSX" title="Acura NSX">Acura NSX</a> for a lap time of 1:57. But in the game he used a <a href="/wiki/Honda_NSX#Second_generation_NSX-R_.28JDM.29" title="Honda NSX">Honda NSX-R</a> (which is lighter) with a lap time of 1:41:148.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Clarkson also had to be shown by a race driving instructor where the line was between the game and reality. He pointed out that adjusting one's braking mid-turn in a real car could cause loss of control, and also mentioned that in the game, he is compelled to take bigger risks than he would in real life, and that in the game, the car did not suffer from <a href="/wiki/Brake_fade" title="Brake fade">brake fade</a>. Despite the apparent discrepancies, in a column for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i>, Clarkson gave the game a score of three stars out of five and had this to say about it:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">I called Sony and asked it to send me a game chip already loaded with the 700 computer cars. And I am in a position to test out its claims because, unlike most people, I really have driven almost all of them in real life. There are mistakes. The BMW M3 CSL, for instance, brakes much better on the road than it does on the screen. And there's no way a Peugeot 106 could outdrag a Fiat Punto off the line. But other than this, I’m struggling: they’ve even managed to accurately reflect the differences between a Mercedes SL 600 and the Mercedes SL 55, which is hard enough to do in real life. There's more, too. If you take a banked curve in the Bentley Le Mans car flat out, you’ll be fine. If you back off, even a little bit, you lose the aerodynamic grip and end up spinning. That's how it is. This game would only be more real if a big spike shot out of the screen and skewered your head every time you crashed. In fact that's the only real drawback: that you can hit the barriers hard without ever damaging you or your car. Maybe they’re saving that for GT5. Perhaps it’ll be called Death or Glory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkson_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarkson-34">[34]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Karl Brauer of <a href="/wiki/Edmunds.com" title="Edmunds.com">edmunds.com</a> performed a similar test, also at Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca, in which he and two others — professional race driver <a href="/wiki/A._J._Allmendinger" title="A. J. Allmendinger">A. J. Allmendinger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gaming editor Justin Kaehler — set times in <i>GT4</i> and real life in a variety of cars. Brauer's best time in a <a href="/wiki/Ford_GT" title="Ford GT">Ford GT</a> in the game was 1:38, and his best time on the real track was 1:52. In the four vehicles the trio tested, none was able to duplicate his game times on the real track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edmundspg1_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-edmundspg1-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaehler_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaehler-36">[36]</a></sup> Brauer suggested the main differences between the game and reality:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Which brings up the single biggest difference between reality and virtual reality — consequences. A mistake on Gran Turismo 4 costs me nothing more than a bad lap time. A mistake with a real exotic car on a real racetrack is... a bit more costly. The other major difference between virtual racing and the real thing is feedback from the car — or an almost total lack thereof. Yes, the force feedback steering wheel does its best to let you know when you're veering off the track, or sliding the rear end, but none of this comes close to the kind of information you get while driving a real vehicle. And in a car like the Ford GT, that's vital information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edmundsintro_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-edmundsintro-37">[37]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Many reviewers criticized the game for its continued lack of rendered damage. Instead of actual damage, the cars (depending on the speed and angle in which the collision occurred) simply bounce or spin off of the car, wall, or obstacle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN3_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN3-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkson_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-clarkson-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globeandmail_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-globeandmail-38">[38]</a></sup> Reviewers complained of the continued ability to take unrealistic short cuts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globeandmail_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-globeandmail-38">[38]</a></sup> such as the ones on <a href="/wiki/Fuji_Speedway" title="Fuji Speedway">Fuji Speedway</a> 90's, Driving Park Beginner Course and <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_la_Sarthe" title="Circuit de la Sarthe">Circuit de la Sarthe</a> I, where the driver can cut right across the chicane, allowing a player to win by cheating. One reviewer also complained that the game's vehicles do not have enough grip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot3_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot3-26">[26]</a></sup> The game has also been criticized for lack of online play which had been promised during early development, but was announced as being removed at the time of release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot3_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot3-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN3_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-IGN3-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamezindustry_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gamezindustry-39">[39]</a></sup> Many reviewers expressed disappointment in the game's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AI" title="AI">AI</a> system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot3_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot3-26">[26]</a></sup> noting that "virtual racers will follow their (driving) line with little concern for where the human driver is at any one time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN3_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-IGN3-29">[29]</a></sup> This is more evident during rally races and missions in which a 5-second speed penalty is given for hitting the other cars or the barriers, regardless of who initiated the contact. Some critics found B-Spec mode to offer little to the overall experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot3_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot3-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globeandmail_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-globeandmail-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Non video-game publications made some positive reception too. Sean Cunningham of <i><a href="/wiki/Maxim_(magazine)" title="Maxim (magazine)">Maxim</a></i> gave the game a perfect ten and asked, "Is <i>GT4</i> more fetish than game? Probably. But if this is a fetish, then put a leather hood on us and whip us till we bleed!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxim_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-maxim-32">[32]</a></sup> Jason Hill of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sydney_Morning_Herald" title="The Sydney Morning Herald">The Sydney Morning Herald</a></i> gave it all five stars and stated that the game's biggest strength "is the realistic handling. You feel every nuance of the car's movements, weight shifts and suspension, particularly with a Driving Force Pro steering wheel. This is a peerless driving simulation that will test even professional drivers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Charles Herold of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> gave it a positive review and stated that while the game "aims for realism, it occasionally falls short. Competitors drive like mindless automatons, seemingly unaware of your existence. Cars can take endless amounts of damage, allowing you to control your turns by bouncing off other cars like a pinball. If a track doesn't have walls, like one set in the Grand Canyon, then an invisible barrier prevents you from going off track."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> However, Jim Schaefer of <i><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Free_Press" title="Detroit Free Press">Detroit Free Press</a></i> gave it three stars out of four and stated that "GT Mode does a great job of organizing your choices on a large home map. You can leave your garage to race, or jump over to ogle new and used rides at domestic and foreign dealerships, take on special challenges and buy parts upgrades."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-detroit_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-detroit-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sales">Sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Turismo_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By March 2016, <i>Gran Turismo 4</i> had shipped 1.27 million copies in Japan, 3.47 million in North America, 6.83 million in Europe, and 180,000 in Asia for a total of 11.76 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtsales_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-gtsales-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the second highest-selling game in the <i>Gran Turismo</i> franchise ahead of <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_(video_game)" title="Gran Turismo (video game)">Gran Turismo</a></i> but behind <i><a href="/wiki/Gran_Turismo_3:_A-Spec" title="Gran Turismo 3: A-Spec">Gran Turismo 3: A-Spec</a></i> respectively.</p>
