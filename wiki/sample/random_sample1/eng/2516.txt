<p><b>Grandidier's trident bat</b> (<i>Paratriaenops auritus</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bat</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Hipposideridae" title="Hipposideridae">Hipposideridae</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. It was formerly assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Triaenops" title="Triaenops">Triaenops</a></i>, but is now placed in the separate genus <i><a href="/wiki/Paratriaenops" title="Paratriaenops">Paratriaenops</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandidier%27s_trident_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Grandidier" title="Guillaume Grandidier">Guillaume Grandidier</a> first described the species in 1912, as <i>Triaenops aurita</i>, on the basis of a single poorly preserved specimen collected at Diégo-Suarez (now <a href="/wiki/Antsiranana" title="Antsiranana">Antsiranana</a>) in northernmost Madagascar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In his 1939 list of African mammals, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glover_Morris_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glover Morris Allen (page does not exist)">Glover Morris Allen</a> placed the species as a <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonym</a> of <i>Triaenops furcula</i> (now <i><a href="/wiki/Paratriaenops_furculus" title="Paratriaenops furculus">Paratriaenops furculus</a></i>) of western Madagascar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A82_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-A82-3">[3]</a></sup> and in his 1948 review of the genus <i>Triaenops</i>, Jean Dorst concurred, as did <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Edwards_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Edwards Hill (page does not exist)">John Edwards Hill</a>, who reviewed the genus in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG964_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RG964-4">[4]</a></sup> In their 1995 study of Madagascar bats, however, R.L. Peterson and colleagues reinstated it as a species. They also changed the <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> to <i>auritus</i>, presumably for <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Zoological_Nomenclature#Gender_agreement" title="International Code of Zoological Nomenclature">gender agreement</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Name_of_a_biological_genus" title="Name of a biological genus">generic name</a> <i>Triaenops</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S378_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-S378-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Julie Ranivo and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steven_Goodman" title="Steven Goodman">Steven Goodman</a> revised Madagascar <i>Triaenops</i> in 2006 and clarified the distinction between <i>T. auritus</i> of northern Madagascar and the closely related <i>T. furculus</i> of western Madagascar, which have nonoverlapping ranges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG964_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RG964-4">[4]</a></sup> Their paper reported the first new material of the species since its original description.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG965_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-RG965-6">[6]</a></sup> They described a third species of this group in 2008—<i>Triaenops pauliani</i> (now <i><a href="/wiki/Paratriaenops_pauliani" title="Paratriaenops pauliani">Paratriaenops pauliani</a></i>) from <a href="/wiki/Aldabra" title="Aldabra">Aldabra</a> in the nearby Seychelles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2007 and 2008, Amy Russell and colleagues investigated the relationships among <i>Triaenops</i> species using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> data; they confirmed the relationship between <i>T. auritus</i> and <i>T. furculus</i> and their status as distinct species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In view of the significant differences between the <i>T. furculus</i> group and other species of <i>Triaenops</i>, Petr Benda and Peter Villa removed the former in 2009 to a separate genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Paratriaenops" title="Paratriaenops">Paratriaenops</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BV34_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BV34-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Triaenops</i> still includes the Malagasy species <i><a href="/wiki/Triaenops_menamena" title="Triaenops menamena">Triaenops menamena</a></i> (formerly <i>Triaenops rufus</i>), which is widespread in western Madagascar and overlaps with <i>P. auritus</i> and <i>P. furculus</i> in range,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Triaenops_goodmani" title="Triaenops goodmani">Triaenops goodmani</a></i>, an extinct species described in 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S46_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-S46-11">[11]</a></sup> that may also be related to <i>Paratriaenops</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandidier%27s_trident_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Paratriaenops auritus</i> is a large-eared, short-tailed bat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G72_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-G72-13">[13]</a></sup> The fur is reddish gold and is darker above than below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR688_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR688-14">[14]</a></sup> Both males and females are significantly larger than individuals of <i>P. furculus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On its face are the three lancets at the back of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Noseleaf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Noseleaf (page does not exist)">noseleaf</a> that are characteristic of <i>Triaenops</i> and <i>Paratriaenops</i>. As in <i>P. furculus</i>, the three lancets are straight and about equal in length; in <i>Triaenops</i>, the middle lancet is longer and the outer two are curved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandidier%27s_trident_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>P. auritus</i> has a small range in far northern Madagascar, perhaps south to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrafiamena_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrafiamena Mountains (page does not exist)">Andrafiamena Mountains</a>. It is relatively abundant in its range and occurs in dry forests. It roosts in caves, and the largest colony contains an estimated 2000 bats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandidier%27s_trident_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> lists <i>P. auritus</i> (as <i>Triaenops auritus</i>) as "<a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a>" because of its small, fragmented, and declining <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a>. Agricultural activities in particular are causing <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">fragmentation</a>, and disturbance of its cave roosts may pose another threat. However, it occurs in three <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">protected areas</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9serve_Sp%C3%A9ciale_d%27Ankarana" title="Réserve Spéciale d'Ankarana">Réserve Spéciale d'Ankarana</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=R%C3%A9serve_Sp%C3%A9ciale_d%27Analamerana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Réserve Spéciale d'Analamerana (page does not exist)">Réserve Spéciale d'Analamerana</a>, and a forest at <a href="/wiki/Daraina" title="Daraina">Daraina</a>. The IUCN recommends that conservation efforts be focused on monitoring and protecting the known cave roosts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandidier%27s_trident_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
