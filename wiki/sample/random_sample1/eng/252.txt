<p>The <b>1972 Winter Olympics</b>, officially known as the <b>XI Olympic Winter Games</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">第十一回オリンピック冬季競技大会</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Dai Jūichi-kai Orinpikku Tōkikyōgi Taikai</i>)</span> (French: Les <i>XIes Jeux olympiques d'hiver</i>), were a winter <a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">multi-sport event</a> which was held from February 3 to February 13, 1972, in <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaidō">Hokkaidō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It was the first Winter Olympics to be held outside Europe and North America, and only the third game (summer or winter) held outside those regions over all, after Melbourne (<a href="/wiki/1956_Summer_Olympics" title="1956 Summer Olympics">1956 Summer Olympics</a>) and Tokyo (<a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">1964 Summer Olympics</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Host_city_selection">Host city selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1972_Winter_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Host city selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bids_for_the_1972_Winter_Olympics" title="Bids for the 1972 Winter Olympics">Bids for the 1972 Winter Olympics</a></div>
<p>Sapporo first won the rights to host the <a href="/wiki/1940_Winter_Olympics" title="1940 Winter Olympics">1940 Winter Olympics</a>, but Japan resigned as the Games' host after its <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">1937 invasion of China</a>. The 1940 Games were later cancelled. All the cities awarded Games that were cancelled due to war have since hosted the Games (<a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cortina_d%27Ampezzo" title="Cortina d'Ampezzo">Cortina d'Ampezzo</a>).</p>
<p>Sapporo competed with Banff, Lahti, and Salt Lake City. The Games were awarded at the 64th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IOC_Session" title="IOC Session">IOC Session</a> in Rome, Italy, on April 26, 1966.</p>
<p>In preparation, the Japanese constructed new largescale facilities at Sapporo and conducted a trial run a full year in advance of the Games. An international sport week was held in February, 1971, to assess the city's preparations as well as "to test its civic mettle and hospitality", and this effort was acclaimed by Olympic observers as "a complete success".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Findling285_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Findling285-1">[1]</a></sup> The development of new infrastructure proved to be a huge boon for the Sapporo economy: by the time of the Games, the national government had invested some US$500 million in upgrades, including a new subway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Findling285_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Findling285-1">[1]</a></sup> The Games' organizers themselves turned a healthy profit in part because they arranged a record $8.47 million for broadcast rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Washington-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Highlights">Highlights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1972_Winter_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Highlights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Prior to these games, Japan had never won a gold medal in the Winter Olympics. The host country's fans in Sapporo were booed when three Japanese athletes, led by <a href="/wiki/Yukio_Kasaya" title="Yukio Kasaya">Yukio Kasaya</a>, swept the <a href="/wiki/Ski_jumping" title="Ski jumping">ski jumping</a> 70 m (current K-90 normal hill) event for gold (Kasaya), silver (<a href="/wiki/Akitsugu_Konno" title="Akitsugu Konno">Akitsugu Konno</a>), and bronze (<a href="/wiki/Seiji_Aochi" title="Seiji Aochi">Seiji Aochi</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Galina_Kulakova" title="Galina Kulakova">Galina Kulakova</a> of the USSR won all three cross-country skiing events for women.</li>
<li>Dutch skater <a href="/wiki/Ard_Schenk" title="Ard Schenk">Ard Schenk</a> won three gold medals in speed skating.</li>
<li>In Women's Alpine Skiing, American Barbara Ann Cochran, one of three siblings on the U.S. Ski Team, surprised the world and became the first U.S. woman since Andrea Mead Lawrence to win a Gold Medal in Skiing when she took first place in the Slalom with an excellent first run backed up by an even better second run.</li>
<li>In Alpine skiing, virtual unknown Swiss <a href="/wiki/Marie-Theres_Nadig" title="Marie-Theres Nadig">Marie-Thérès Nadig</a> won both the downhill and the giant slalom events.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnar_Solberg" title="Magnar Solberg">Magnar Solberg</a> from Norway was the first repeat winner in the individual 20 km biathlon event, having first won in <a href="/wiki/1968_Winter_Olympics" title="1968 Winter Olympics">Grenoble</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Spain scored its first Winter gold medal courtesy of <a href="/wiki/Slalom_skiing" title="Slalom skiing">slalom</a> skier <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Ochoa" title="Francisco Fernández Ochoa">Francisco Fernández Ochoa</a>.</li>
<li>American speedskaters <a href="/wiki/Anne_Henning" title="Anne Henning">Anne Henning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dianne_Holum" title="Dianne Holum">Dianne Holum</a> made the United States' best showing in the Winter Games, winning two gold, a silver, and a bronze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Washington-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Three days before the Games, controversy over amateur status arose when IOC president <a href="/wiki/Avery_Brundage" title="Avery Brundage">Avery Brundage</a> threatened to disqualify 40 alpine skiers who received endorsement and other deals. Austrian skier <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schranz" title="Karl Schranz">Karl Schranz</a>, who received over $50,000 per year from ski manufacturers, was banned as an example. Meanwhile, Canada refused to send an ice hockey team, maintaining that professional ice hockey players from Communist nations were allowed to compete with no restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>On a historical note, these Games are the last where a skier won the gold medal using all-wooden skis. Since this time, top-level cross-country skiers use skis made mostly of fibreglass synthetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>In female <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating_at_the_1972_Winter_Olympics" title="Figure skating at the 1972 Winter Olympics">Figure skating</a> event, American skater <a href="/wiki/Janet_Lynn" title="Janet Lynn">Janet Lynn</a> won not only a bronze medal, but also tremendous popularity among Japanese audiences because of her artistic free program, as to make appearance on the cover of "Olympic Winter Games, Sapporo 1972" photo books published in Japan, and even on Japanese TV commercials later.</li>
<li>Luge had its only tie in the history of the Winter Olympics in the men's doubles event.</li>
</ul>
