<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Grandmaster (chess)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The title <b>Grandmaster</b> is awarded to <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> players by the world chess organization <a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">FIDE</a>. Apart from <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship" title="World Chess Championship">World Champion</a>, Grandmaster is the highest title a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_player" title="Chess player">chess player</a> can attain.</p>
<p>Once achieved, the title is held for life. In chess literature it is usually abbreviated to <b>GM</b>. (Other titles are also often abbreviated: FM for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIDE_Master" title="FIDE Master">FIDE Master</a>, IM for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Master" title="International Master">International Master</a>, and CM for Candidate Master.) The abbreviation IGM for International Grandmaster is also sometimes used, particularly in older literature.</p>
<p>Grandmaster, International Master, and FIDE Master are open to both men and women. A number of women have earned the GM title, with the first two having been <a href="/wiki/Nona_Gaprindashvili" title="Nona Gaprindashvili">Nona Gaprindashvili</a> in 1978 and <a href="/wiki/Susan_Polgar" title="Susan Polgar">Susan Polgar</a> in 1991. Since about 2000, most of the top 10 women have held the GM title.</p>
<p>A separate gender-segregated title, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woman_Grandmaster" title="Woman Grandmaster">Woman Grandmaster</a> (WGM), is also available. It is awarded to women who attain a level of skill between that of a FIDE Master and an International Master.</p>
<p>FIDE awards separate Grandmaster titles to composers and solvers of <a href="/wiki/Chess_problem" title="Chess problem">chess problems</a> (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_grandmasters_for_chess_composition" title="List of grandmasters for chess composition">List of grandmasters for chess composition</a>). The <a href="/wiki/International_Correspondence_Chess_Federation" title="International Correspondence Chess Federation">International Correspondence Chess Federation</a> (ICCF) awards the title of <a href="/wiki/International_Correspondence_Chess_Grandmaster" title="International Correspondence Chess Grandmaster">International Correspondence Chess Grandmaster</a> (ICCGM).</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known use of the term <i>grandmaster</i> in connection with chess was in an 1838 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_Life_in_London" title="Bell's Life in London">Bell's Life</a></i>, in which a correspondent referred to <a href="/wiki/William_Lewis_(chess_player)" title="William Lewis (chess player)">William Lewis</a> as "our past grandmaster".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOCOC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TOCOC-1">[1]</a></sup> Lewis himself later referred to <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Andr%C3%A9_Danican_Philidor" title="François-André Danican Philidor">Philidor</a> as a grandmaster, and the term was also applied to a few other players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOCOC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TOCOC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_tournament_use">Early tournament use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early tournament use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tarrasch_72.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="497" data-file-width="422" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Tarrasch_72.jpg/220px-Tarrasch_72.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Tarrasch_72.jpg/330px-Tarrasch_72.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/88/Tarrasch_72.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tarrasch_72.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Siegbert_Tarrasch" title="Siegbert Tarrasch">Siegbert Tarrasch</a> (1862–1934)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ostend_1907_chess_tournament" title="Ostend 1907 chess tournament">Ostend tournament of 1907</a> the term <i>grandmaster</i> (<i>Großmeister</i> in German) was used. The tournament was divided into two sections: the Championship Tournament and the Masters' Tournament. The Championship section was for players who had previously won an international tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fqmpqc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fqmpqc-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Siegbert_Tarrasch" title="Siegbert Tarrasch">Siegbert Tarrasch</a> won the Championship section, over <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schlechter" title="Carl Schlechter">Carl Schlechter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dawid_Janowski" title="Dawid Janowski">Dawid Janowski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Marshall_(chess_player)" title="Frank Marshall (chess player)">Frank Marshall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amos_Burn" title="Amos Burn">Amos Burn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Chigorin" title="Mikhail Chigorin">Mikhail Chigorin</a>. These players were described as grandmasters for the purposes of the tournament.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_chess_tournament" title="San Sebastián chess tournament">San Sebastián 1912 tournament</a> won by <a href="/wiki/Akiba_Rubinstein" title="Akiba Rubinstein">Akiba Rubinstein</a> was a designated grandmaster event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOCOC_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TOCOC-1">[1]</a></sup> Rubinstein won with 12½ points out of 19. Tied for second with 12 points were <a href="/wiki/Aron_Nimzowitsch" title="Aron Nimzowitsch">Aron Nimzowitsch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Spielmann" title="Rudolf Spielmann">Rudolf Spielmann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>By some accounts, in the <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg_1914_chess_tournament" title="St. Petersburg 1914 chess tournament">St. Petersburg 1914 chess tournament</a>, the title "Grandmaster" was formally conferred by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Tsar_Nicholas_II" title="Russian Tsar Nicholas II">Russian Tsar Nicholas II</a>, who had partially funded the tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fqmpqc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-fqmpqc-2">[2]</a></sup> The Tsar reportedly awarded the title to the five finalists: <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Lasker" title="Emanuel Lasker">Emanuel Lasker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ra%C3%BAl_Capablanca" title="José Raúl Capablanca">José Raúl Capablanca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Alekhine" title="Alexander Alekhine">Alexander Alekhine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siegbert_Tarrasch" title="Siegbert Tarrasch">Siegbert Tarrasch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Marshall_(chess_player)" title="Frank Marshall (chess player)">Frank Marshall</a>. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Winter_(chess_historian)" title="Edward Winter (chess historian)">Chess historian Edward Winter</a> has questioned this, stating that the earliest known sources that support this story are an article by Robert Lewis Taylor in the June 15, 1940, issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> and Marshall's autobiography <i>My 50 Years of Chess</i> (1942).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KCAK_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-KCAK-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chess_Omnibus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chess_Omnibus-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-standard_and_Soviet_usage_before_1950">Non-standard and Soviet usage before 1950</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-standard and Soviet usage before 1950">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before 1950, the term <i>grandmaster</i> was sometimes informally applied to other world class players. The <i><a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">Fédération Internationale des Échecs</a></i> (FIDE, or World Chess Federation) was formed in Paris in 1924, but at that time did not formulate criteria on who should earn the title.</p>
<p>In 1927, the Soviet Union's Chess Federation established the title of <i>Grandmaster of the Soviet Union</i>, for their own players, since at that time Soviets were not competing outside their own country. This title was abolished in 1931, after having been awarded to <a href="/wiki/Boris_Verlinsky" title="Boris Verlinsky">Boris Verlinsky</a>, who won the 1929 <a href="/wiki/USSR_Chess_Championship" title="USSR Chess Championship">Soviet Championship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soviet_Championships_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soviet_Championships-7">[7]</a></sup> The title was brought back in 1935, and awarded to <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Botvinnik" title="Mikhail Botvinnik">Mikhail Botvinnik</a>, who thus became the first "official" Grandmaster of the USSR. Verlinsky did not get his title back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soviet_Championships_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Soviet_Championships-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_status_.281950_onwards.29">Official status (1950 onwards)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Official status (1950 onwards)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>When FIDE reorganized after World War II it adopted regulations concerning the award of international titles. Titles were awarded by a resolution of the FIDE General Assembly and the Qualification Committee. FIDE first awarded the Grandmaster title in 1950 to 27 players. These players were:</p>
<ul>
<li>The top players of the day: world champion <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Botvinnik" title="Mikhail Botvinnik">Mikhail Botvinnik</a>, and those who had qualified for (or been seeded into) the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Candidates_Tournament" title="Candidates Tournament">Candidates Tournament</a> in 1950: <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Boleslavsky" title="Isaac Boleslavsky">Isaac Boleslavsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Igor_Bondarevsky" title="Igor Bondarevsky">Igor Bondarevsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Bronstein" title="David Bronstein">David Bronstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Euwe" title="Max Euwe">Max Euwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Fine" title="Reuben Fine">Reuben Fine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salo_Flohr" title="Salo Flohr">Salo Flohr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Keres" title="Paul Keres">Paul Keres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Kotov" title="Alexander Kotov">Alexander Kotov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andor_Lilienthal" title="Andor Lilienthal">Andor Lilienthal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Najdorf" title="Miguel Najdorf">Miguel Najdorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Reshevsky" title="Samuel Reshevsky">Samuel Reshevsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vasily_Smyslov" title="Vasily Smyslov">Vasily Smyslov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gideon_St%C3%A5hlberg" title="Gideon Ståhlberg">Gideon Ståhlberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Szab%C3%B3_(chess_player)" title="László Szabó (chess player)">László Szabó</a>.</li>
<li>Players still living who, though past their best in 1950, were recognised as having been world class when at their peak: <a href="/wiki/Ossip_Bernstein" title="Ossip Bernstein">Ossip Bernstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old%C5%99ich_Duras" title="Oldřich Duras">Oldřich Duras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Gr%C3%BCnfeld" title="Ernst Grünfeld">Ernst Grünfeld</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borislav_Kosti%C4%87" title="Borislav Kostić">Borislav Kostić</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Levenfish" title="Grigory Levenfish">Grigory Levenfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_Mar%C3%B3czy" title="Géza Maróczy">Géza Maróczy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Mieses" title="Jacques Mieses">Jacques Mieses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viacheslav_Ragozin" title="Viacheslav Ragozin">Viacheslav Ragozin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akiba_Rubinstein" title="Akiba Rubinstein">Akiba Rubinstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_S%C3%A4misch" title="Friedrich Sämisch">Friedrich Sämisch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savielly_Tartakower" title="Savielly Tartakower">Savielly Tartakower</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Milan_Vidmar" title="Milan Vidmar">Milan Vidmar</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since FIDE did not award the Grandmaster title posthumously, world-class players who died prior to 1950, including World Champions <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Steinitz" title="Wilhelm Steinitz">Steinitz</a>, Lasker, Capablanca, and Alekhine, never received the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Title awards under the original regulations were subject to political concerns. <a href="/wiki/Efim_Bogoljubov" title="Efim Bogoljubov">Efim Bogoljubov</a>, who had emigrated from the Soviet Union to Germany, was not entered in the first class of Grandmasters, even though he had played two matches for the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship" title="World Chess Championship">World Championship</a> with Alekhine. He received the title in 1951, by a vote of thirteen to eight with five abstentions. <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> supported his application, but all other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> countries opposed it. In 1953, FIDE abolished the old regulations, although a provision was maintained that allowed older masters who had been overlooked to be awarded titles. The new regulations awarded the title of International Grandmaster of the FIDE to players meeting any of the following criteria:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness1956_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness1956-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The world champion.</li>
<li>Masters who have the absolute right to play in the World Championship <a href="/wiki/Candidates_Tournament" title="Candidates Tournament">Candidates Tournament</a>, or any player who replaces an absent contestant and earns at least a 50 percent score.</li>
<li>The winner of an international tournament meeting specified standards, and any player placing second in two such tournaments within a span of four years. The tournament must be at least eleven rounds with seven or more players, 80 percent or more being International Grandmasters or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Master" title="International Master">International Masters</a>. Additionally, 30 percent of the players must be Grandmasters who have the absolute right to play in the next World Championship Candidates Tournament, or who have played in such a tournament in the previous ten years.</li>
<li>A player who demonstrates ability manifestly equal to that of (3) above in an international tournament or match. Such titles must be approved by the Qualification Committee with the support of at least five members.</li>
</ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1957_regulations">1957 regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1957 regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After FIDE issued the 1953 title regulations, it was recognized that they were somewhat haphazard, and work began to revise the regulations. The FIDE Congress in Vienna in 1957 adopted new regulations, called the FAV system, in recognition of the work done by <a href="/wiki/International_Arbiter" title="International Arbiter">International Judge</a> Giovanni Ferrantes (Italy), Alexander (probably <a href="/wiki/Conel_Hugh_O%27Donel_Alexander" title="Conel Hugh O'Donel Alexander">Conel Hugh O'Donel Alexander</a>), and Giancarlo Dal Verme (Italy). Under the 1957 regulations, the title of International Grandmaster of the FIDE was automatically awarded to:</p>
<ol>
<li>The world champion.</li>
<li>Any player qualifying from the <a href="/wiki/Interzonal" title="Interzonal">Interzonal</a> tournament to play in the Candidates Tournament, even if he did not play in the Candidates for any reason.</li>
<li>Any player who would qualify from the Interzonal to play in the Candidates but who was excluded because of a limitation on the number of participants from his Federation.</li>
<li>Any player who actually plays in a Candidates Tournament and scores at least 33⅓ percent.</li>
</ol>
<p>The regulations also allowed titles to be awarded by a FIDE Congress on recommendation by the Qualification Committee. Recommendations were based on performance in qualifying tournaments, with the required score depending on the percentage of Grandmasters and International Masters in the tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness1967_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness1967-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1965_regulations">1965 regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1965 regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Concerns were raised that the 1957 regulations were too lax. At the FIDE Congress in 1961, GM <a href="/wiki/Milan_Vidmar" title="Milan Vidmar">Milan Vidmar</a> said that the regulations "made it possible to award international titles to players without sufficient merit". At the 1964 Congress in <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>, a subcommittee was formed to propose changes to the regulations. The subcommittee recommended that the automatic award of titles be abolished, criticized the methods used for awarding titles based on qualifying performances, and called for a change in the makeup of the Qualification Committee. Several delegates supported the subcommittee recommendations, including GM <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Najdorf" title="Miguel Najdorf">Miguel Najdorf</a> who felt that existing regulations were leading to an inflation of international titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness1967_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness1967-10">[10]</a></sup> At the 1965 Congress in <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a> FIDE raised the standards required for international titles. The International Grandmaster title regulations were:</p>
<ul>
<li>1. Any World Champion is automatically awarded the GM title</li>
<li>2a. Anyone who scores at least 40 percent in a quarter-final match in the Candidates Tournament</li>
<li>2b. Scores at least the number of points in a tournament corresponding to the total of a 55 percent score against Grandmasters plus 75 percent against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Masters" title="International Masters">International Masters</a> (IM) plus 85 percent against other players (a GM "<a href="/wiki/Norm_(chess)" title="Norm (chess)">norm</a>").</li>
</ul>
<p>To fulfill requirement 2b, the candidate must score one GM norm in a category 1a tournament or two norms within a three-year period in two Category 1b tournaments, or one Category 2a tournament and one Category 1b tournament.</p>
<p>The categories of tournaments are:</p>
<ul>
<li>1a—at least sixteen players, at least 50 percent are GMs, and 70 percent at least IMs</li>
<li>1b—at least twelve players, at least 33⅓ percent GMs and 70 percent IMs</li>
<li>2a—at least fifteen players, at least 50 percent IMs</li>
<li>2b—ten to fourteen players, at least 50 percent IMs.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since FIDE titles are for life, a GM or IM does not count for the purposes of this requirement if he had not had a GM or IM result in the five years prior to the tournament.</p>
<p>In addition, no more than 50 percent plus one of the players can be from the same country for tournaments of 10 to 12 players, or no more than 50 percent plus two for larger tournaments.</p>
<p>Seventy-four GM titles were awarded in 1951 through 1968. During that period, ten GM titles were awarded in 1965, but only one in 1966 and in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1970_regulations">1970 regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1970 regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The modern system for awarding <a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">FIDE</a> titles evolved from the "Dorazil" proposals, presented to the <a href="/wiki/19th_Chess_Olympiad" title="19th Chess Olympiad">1970 Siegen Chess Olympiad</a> FIDE Congress. The proposals were put together by Dr Wilfried Dorazil (then FIDE Vice-President) and fellow Committee members Grandmaster <a href="/wiki/Svetozar_Gligori%C4%87" title="Svetozar Gligorić">Svetozar Gligorić</a> and Professor <a href="/wiki/Arpad_Elo" title="Arpad Elo">Arpad Elo</a>. The recommendations of the Committee report were adopted in full.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegen_Chess_Olympiad_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siegen_Chess_Olympiad-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In essence, the proposals built on the work done by Professor Elo in devising his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elo_rating" title="Elo rating">Elo rating</a> system. The establishment of an updated list of players and their Elo rating enabled significantly strong international chess tournaments to be allocated a "Category", based on the average rating of the contestants. For instance, it was decided that 'Category 1' status would apply to tournaments with an average Elo rating of participants falling within the range 2251–2275; similarly Category 2 would apply to the range 2276–2300 etc. The higher the tournament Category, the stronger the tournament.</p>
<p>Another vital component involved the setting of meritorious "scores" for each Category of tournament. A player must meet or surpass the relevant score to demonstrate that they had performed at Grandmaster (GM) or International Master (IM) level. Scores were expressed as percentages of a perfect maximum score and decreased as the tournament Category increased, thereby reflecting the strength of a player's opposition and the relative difficulty of the task.</p>
<p>Tournament organisers could then apply the percentages to their own tournament format and declare in advance the actual score that participants must achieve to attain a GM or IM result (nowadays referred to as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandmaster_norm" title="Grandmaster norm">norm</a>).</p>

<p>To qualify for the Grandmaster title, a player needed to achieve three such GM results within a rolling period of three years. Exceptionally, if a player's contributory games totalled thirty or more, then the title could be awarded on the basis of two such results. There were also circumstances where the system could be adapted to fit team events and other competitions.</p>
<p>The full proposals included many other rules and regulations, covering such topics as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Eligible tournament formats.</li>
<li>Eligible participants.</li>
<li>Unrated participants.</li>
<li>Registration of tournaments with FIDE.</li>
<li>Calculations, including the handling of fractions.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorary_grandmasters">Honorary grandmasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Honorary grandmasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_regulations">Current regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Current regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The requirements for becoming a Grandmaster are somewhat complex. A player must have attained an <a href="/wiki/Elo_rating_system" title="Elo rating system">Elo rating</a> of at least 2500 (although they need not maintain this level to obtain or keep the title). In addition, at least two favorable results (called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandmaster_norm" title="Grandmaster norm">norms</a></i>) from a total of at least 27 games in tournaments involving other Grandmasters, including some from countries other than the applicant's, are usually required before FIDE will confer the title on a player. There are other milestones a player can achieve to get the title, such as winning the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_World_Chess_Championship" title="Women's World Chess Championship">Women's World Championship</a>, the <a href="/wiki/World_Junior_Chess_Championship" title="World Junior Chess Championship">World Junior Championship</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/World_Senior_Chess_Championship" title="World Senior Chess Championship">World Senior Championship</a>. Current regulations can be found in the FIDE Handbook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_inflation">Title inflation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grandmaster_(chess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Title inflation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1957, there were 50 GMs (USSR: 19, Yugoslavia: 7, USA: 5, Argentina: 4, West Germany: 2, France: 2, Sweden: 1, Czechoslovakia: 1, Hungary: 1, Austria: 1, Belgium: 1, Denmark: 1, Netherlands: 1, Poland: 1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1972, there were 88 GMs with 33 representing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a>. The current FIDE ratings list includes over 1000 Grandmasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rating_fide_download_page_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-rating_fide_download_page-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Short" title="Nigel Short">Nigel Short</a> was rated the third best player in the world in 1989 with a rating of 2650; in the 21st century such a rating would only be good enough for a player to reach the top 100 or so, with the third best player in the world usually rated around 2800. Other minor factors come into play: there are more tournaments worldwide and cheaper air travel makes them more accessible to globe-trotting chess professionals, who include many players from the former Soviet Union and Eastern Europe whose movements are no longer restricted as they were before the 1990s. Additionally, players can make norms in tournaments that would have been previously considered too short for norms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Short_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Short-16">[16]</a></sup> making norms easier to get and allowing for more norm tournaments to be held.</p>
<p>December 2008 saw a record number of GMs (1,192) and IMs (2,916), causing some <a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">FIDE</a> officials to suggest that FIDE should consider an "elite grandmaster" title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACP_Report_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACP_Report-17">[17]</a></sup> The unofficial title "Super Grandmaster" is sometimes used by players to refer to those with a 2700+ rating to distinguish the most serious world champion contenders. The proportion of titled players among rated players is actually becoming smaller due to the rise in the number of all chess players worldwide who have FIDE ratings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACP_Report_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ACP_Report-17">[17]</a></sup> In response, one member of the FIDE Titles &amp; Ratings Committee observed that it is now more common for weaker players to get FIDE ratings, so the comparison of Grandmasters as a proportion of all rated players is not really helpful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
