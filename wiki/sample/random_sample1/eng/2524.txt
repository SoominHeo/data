<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Grand Banks of Newfoundland</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Grand Banks of Newfoundland</b> are a group of underwater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plateaus" title="Plateaus">plateaus</a> south-east of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> on the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>. These areas are relatively shallow, ranging from 50 to 300 feet (15 to 91 m) in depth. The cold <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Current" title="Labrador Current">Labrador Current</a> mixes with the warm waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> here.</p>
<p>The mixing of these waters and the shape of the ocean bottom lifts nutrients to the surface. These conditions helped to create one of the richest <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> grounds in the world. <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a> species include <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swordfish" title="Swordfish">swordfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haddock" title="Haddock">haddock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capelin" title="Capelin">capelin</a>; <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> include <a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">scallop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a>. The area also supports large colonies of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Northern_gannet" title="Northern gannet">northern gannets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwaters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaduck" title="Seaduck">sea ducks</a> and various sea <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(mammal)" title="Seal (mammal)">seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>.</p>
<p>In addition to the effects on nutrients, the mixing of the cold and warm currents often causes <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> in the area. It is also noted for its proximity to the sinking of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a> and thus the launching point of <i>Titanic</i> shipwreck expeditions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Grand Banks were extensively glaciated during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" title="Last glacial maximum">last glacial maximum</a>. By approximately 13,000 years ago, the majority of the ice had melted, leaving the Grand Banks exposed as several islands extending for hundreds of kilometers. It is believed that rising sea levels submerged these around 8,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GrandBanks23.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="300" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/GrandBanks23.jpg" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GrandBanks23.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Historic map including the Grand Banks.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While there is no <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> evidence for a European presence near the Grand Banks between the short-lived <a href="/wiki/History_of_Greenland#Norse_settlement" title="History of Greenland">Greenland Norse</a> settlement at <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a> in CE 1000 and <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_crossing" title="Transatlantic crossing">transatlantic crossing</a> in 1497, there is some evidence that voyagers from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Region</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cod:_A_Biography_of_the_Fish_that_Changed_the_World_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cod:_A_Biography_of_the_Fish_that_Changed_the_World-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> (especially those from <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> preceded Cabot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the 15th century some texts refer to a land called <i><a href="/wiki/Bacalao" title="Bacalao">Bacalao</a></i>, the land of the codfish, which is possibly Newfoundland. Within a few years of Cabot's voyage the existence of fishing grounds on the Grand Banks became generally known in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Ships from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> were first to fish there, followed by those from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> while ships from <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> were scarce in the early years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This soon changed especially after <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Drake" title="Bernard Drake">Bernard Drake</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_expedition_(1585)" title="Newfoundland expedition (1585)">devastating raid in 1585</a> which virtually wiped out the Spanish and Portuguese fishing industry in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> These fish stocks thus became important for the early economies of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canada" title="Eastern Canada">eastern Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.</p>
<p>On 18 November 1929, a major <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> (known as the <a href="/wiki/1929_Grand_Banks_earthquake" title="1929 Grand Banks earthquake">1929 Grand Banks earthquake</a>) on the southwestern part of the Grand Banks bordering the <a href="/wiki/Laurentian_Channel" title="Laurentian Channel">Laurentian Channel</a> caused an underwater landslide which resulted in extensive damage to <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">transatlantic cables</a> and generated a rare Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> that struck the south coast of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Cape_Breton_Island" title="Cape Breton Island">Cape Breton Island</a>, claiming 28 lives in the <a href="/wiki/Burin_Peninsula" title="Burin Peninsula">Burin Peninsula</a>.</p>
<p>Technological advances in fishing such as large <a href="/wiki/Factory_ship" title="Factory ship">factory ships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a>, as well as geopolitical disputes over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territorial_sea" title="Territorial sea">territorial sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> (EEZ) boundaries, have led to <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> and a serious decline in the <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a> of the Grand Banks from around 1990.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>'s EEZ currently occupies the majority of the Grand Banks except for the lucrative "nose" (eastern extremity, near the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Cap" title="Flemish Cap">Flemish Cap</a>) and "tail" (southern extremity) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_bank" title="Fishing bank">fishing bank</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1783)" title="Treaty of Paris (1783)">1783 Treaty of Paris</a> gave the United States shared rights to fish in these waters, but that section of the Treaty is no longer in force. The French territory <a href="/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint Pierre and Miquelon">Saint Pierre and Miquelon</a> Exclusive Economic Zone occupies a pin-shaped section at the west edge of the Grand Banks, with the 22 km radius head of the pin surrounding the islands and the needle heading south for 348 km.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Canada is currently performing the hydrographic and geological surveys necessary for claiming the entire continental shelf off eastern Canada, under the auspices of the latest <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a>. Once this aspect of UNCLOS is ratified, Canada will presumably control these remaining parts of Grand Banks which are outside of its EEZ jurisdiction.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">Petroleum</a> reserves have also been discovered and a number of oil fields are under development in this region, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Hibernia_oil_field" title="Hibernia oil field">Hibernia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_oil_field" title="Terra Nova oil field">Terra Nova</a>, and <a href="/wiki/White_Rose_oil_field" title="White Rose oil field">White Rose</a> projects; the harsh environment on the Grand Banks also led to the <i><a href="/wiki/Ocean_Ranger" title="Ocean Ranger">Ocean Ranger</a></i> disaster.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Semi-fictional depictions of fishermen working on the Grand Banks can be found in <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Captains_Courageous" title="Captains Courageous">Captains Courageous</a></i> (1897) and in Sebastian Junger's non-fiction book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(book)" title="The Perfect Storm (book)">The Perfect Storm</a></i> (1997). The Grand Banks are also portrayed in the 1990 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunt_for_Red_October_(film)" title="The Hunt for Red October (film)">The Hunt for Red October</a></i>.</p>
