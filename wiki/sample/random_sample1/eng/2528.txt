<p>The <b>Grumman F6F Hellcat</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">carrier</a><a href="/wiki/Carrier-based_aircraft" title="Carrier-based aircraft">-based</a> <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter aircraft</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Designed to replace the earlier <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F4F_Wildcat" title="Grumman F4F Wildcat">F4F Wildcat</a> and to counter the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_A6M_Zero" title="Mitsubishi A6M Zero">Mitsubishi A6M Zero</a>, it was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s dominant fighter in the second half of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a>. The Hellcat competed with the faster <a href="/wiki/Vought_F4U_Corsair" title="Vought F4U Corsair">Vought F4U Corsair</a> for that role and prevailed, as the Corsair had significant issues with carrier landings. The Corsair instead was primarily deployed to great effect in land-based use by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>.</p>
<p>Powered by a 2,000 hp <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_R-2800" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800">Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800</a>, the same powerplant used for both the Corsair and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Force" title="United States Army Air Force">United States Army Air Force</a> (USAAF) <a href="/wiki/Republic_P-47_Thunderbolt" title="Republic P-47 Thunderbolt">Republic P-47 Thunderbolt</a> fighters, the F6F was an entirely new design, but it still resembled the Wildcat in many ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Some military observers tagged the Hellcat as the "Wildcat's big brother".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sul4_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sul4-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The F6F was best known for its role as a rugged, well-designed carrier fighter which was able, after its combat debut in September 1943, to outperform the A6M Zero and help secure air superiority over the Pacific Theater. 12,275 were built in just over two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hellcats were credited with destroying a total of 5,223 enemy aircraft while in service with the U.S. Navy, U.S. Marine Corps and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[Note 2]</a></sup> This was more than any other Allied naval aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> Postwar, the Hellcat was phased out of front line service but remained in service as late as 1954 as a night fighter.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="XF6F">XF6F</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: XF6F">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="726" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg/220px-Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg/330px-Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg/440px-Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grumman_XF6F-1_Hellcat_1942.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The unpainted XF6F-1 prior to its first flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Grumman had been working on a successor to the F4F Wildcat since 1938 and the contract for the prototype <b>XF6F-1</b> was signed on 30 June 1941. The aircraft was originally designed to use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright_R-2600" title="Wright R-2600">Wright R-2600 Twin Cyclone</a> two-row, 14-cylinder radial engine of 1,700 hp (1,268 kW) driving a three-bladed Curtiss Electric propeller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> Instead of the Wildcat's narrow-track, hand-cranked main <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a> retracting into the fuselage that it had inherited, little changed in design from the 1931-debuted <a href="/wiki/Grumman_FF" title="Grumman FF">Grumman FF</a>-1 fighter biplane, the Hellcat had wide-set, hydraulically actuated landing gear struts which rotated through 90° while retracting backwards into the wings, much like that of the earlier Chance Vought F4U Corsair, but with full wheel doors fitted to the struts that covered the entire strut and the upper half of the main wheel when retracted, and twisted with the main gear struts during retraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._503._13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._503.-13">[11]</a></sup> The wing was mounted lower on the fuselage and was able to be hydraulically or manually folded, with each panel outboard of the undercarriage bay folding backwards from pivoting on a specially oriented, Grumman-patented <i>Sto-Wing</i> diagonal axis pivoting system much like the earlier F4F, with a folded stowage position parallel to the fuselage with the leading edges pointing diagonally down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout early 1942 <a href="/wiki/Leroy_Grumman" title="Leroy Grumman">Leroy Grumman</a>, along with his chief designers <a href="/wiki/Jake_Swirbul" title="Jake Swirbul">Jake Swirbul</a> and Bill Schwendler, worked closely with the U.S. Navy's <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Aeronautics" title="Bureau of Aeronautics">Bureau of Aeronautics</a> (BuAer) and experienced F4F pilots,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup> to develop the new fighter in such a way that it could counter the Zero's strengths and help gain air command in the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific Theater of Operations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> On 22 April 1942, Lieutenant Commander Butch O'Hare toured the Grumman Aircraft company and spoke with Grumman engineers, analyzing the performance of the F4F Wildcat against the Mitsubishi A6M Zero in aerial combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ewing_Thach_Weave_p._86_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ewing_Thach_Weave_p._86-17">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[Note 3]</a></sup> BuAer's LT CDR A. M. Jackson <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[Note 4]</a></sup> directed Grumman's designers to mount the cockpit higher in the fuselage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[18]</a></sup> In addition, the forward fuselage sloped down slightly to the engine cowling, affording the Hellcat's pilot good visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kin966_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kin966-23">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on combat accounts of encounters between the F4F Wildcat and A6M Zero, on 26 April 1942, BuAer directed Grumman to install the more powerful 18-cylinder <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_R-2800_Double_Wasp" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800 Double Wasp">Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800 Double Wasp</a> radial engine — already powering Chance Vought's Corsair design since its own beginnings in 1940 — in the second XF6F-1 prototype.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[20]</a></sup> Grumman complied by redesigning and strengthening the F6F airframe to incorporate the 2,000 hp (1,500 kW) R-2800-10, driving a three-bladed <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Standard" title="Hamilton Standard">Hamilton Standard</a> propeller. With this combination Grumman estimated the <b>XF6F-3</b>s performance would increase by 25% over that of the XF6F-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sul4_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sul4-6">[5]</a></sup> The Cyclone-powered <b>XF6F-1</b> (02981) first flew on 26 June 1942, followed by the first Double Wasp-equipped aircraft, the <b>XF6F-3</b> (02982), which first flew on 30 July 1942. The first production F6F-3, powered by an R-2800-10, flew on 3 October 1942, with the type reaching operational readiness with <a href="/wiki/VF-9" title="VF-9">VF-9</a> on <a href="/wiki/USS_Essex_(CV-9)" title="USS Essex (CV-9)">USS <i>Essex</i></a> in February 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinzey_p._6_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kinzey_p._6-25">[21]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[Note 5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_development">Further development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The F6F series were designed to take damage and get the pilot safely back to base. A bullet-resistant windshield and a total of 212 lb (96 kg) of cockpit armor was fitted, along with armor around the oil tank and oil cooler. A 250 gal (946 l) <a href="/wiki/Self-sealing_fuel_tank" title="Self-sealing fuel tank">self-sealing fuel tank</a> was fitted in the fuselage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinzey_p._6_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kinzey_p._6-25">[21]</a></sup> Standard armament on the F6F-3 consisted of six .50 in (12.7 mm) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Browning_machine_gun" title="M2 Browning machine gun">M2/AN Browning</a> air-cooled <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine guns</a> with 400 rounds per gun. A center-section <a href="/wiki/Hardpoint" title="Hardpoint">hardpoint</a> under the fuselage could carry a single 150 gal (568 l) disposable <a href="/wiki/Drop_tank" title="Drop tank">drop tank</a>, while later aircraft had single bomb racks installed under each wing, inboard of the undercarriage bays; with these and the center-section hard point late model F6F-3s could carry a total bomb-load in excess of 2,000 lb (900 kg). Six 5 in (127 mm) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVAR" title="HVAR">HVARs</a> (High Velocity Aircraft Rocket) could be carried; three under each wing on "zero-length" launchers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two night fighter sub-variants of the F6F-3 were developed: the 18 <b>F6F-3E's</b> were converted from standard-3s and featured the AN/APS-4 radar in a pod mounted on a rack beneath the right wing, with a small radar-scope fitted in the middle of the main instrument panel and radar operating controls installed on the port side of the cockpit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[24]</a></sup> The later <b>F6F-3N</b>, first flown in July 1943, was fitted with the AN/APS-6 radar in the fuselage, with the antenna dish in a bulbous fairing mounted on the leading-edge of the outer right wing; approximately 200 F6F-3Ns were built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[25]</a></sup> Hellcat night fighters claimed their first victories in November 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[26]</a></sup> A total of 4,402 F6F-3s were built through until April 1944, when production was changed to the F6F-5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kin966_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kin966-23">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>The F6F-5 featured several improvements including a more powerful R-2800-10W engine employing a water-injection system and housed in a slightly more streamlined engine cowling, spring-loaded control <a href="/wiki/Trim_tab" title="Trim tab">tabs</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Aileron" title="Aileron">ailerons</a>, and an improved, clear view windscreen, with a flat armored-glass front panel replacing the F6F-3's curved plexiglass panel and internal armor glass screen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._503._13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._503.-13">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kin966_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kin966-23">[19]</a></sup> In addition, the rear fuselage and tail units were strengthened, and, apart from some early production aircraft, the majority of the F6F-5's built were painted in an overall gloss sea blue finish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[27]</a></sup> After the first few F6F-5s were built, the small windows behind the main canopy were deleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[28]</a></sup> The <b>F6F-5N</b> night fighter variant was fitted with an AN/APS-6 radar in a fairing on the outer-starboard wing. A small number of standard F6F-5s were also fitted with camera equipment for reconnaissance duties as the <b>F6F-5P</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[29]</a></sup> While all F6F-5s were capable of carrying an armament mix of one 20 mm (.79 in) <a href="/wiki/Hispano-Suiza_HS.404" title="Hispano-Suiza HS.404">M2 cannon</a> in each of the inboard gun bays (220 rounds per gun), along with two pairs of .50 in (12.7 mm) machine guns (each with 400 rounds per gun), this configuration was only used on later F6F-5N night fighters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinzey_p._27_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kinzey_p._27-35">[30]</a></sup> The F6F-5 was the most common F6F variant, with 7,870 being built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kin966_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kin966-23">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other prototypes in the F6F series included the <b>XF6F-4</b> (02981, a conversion of the XF6F-1 powered by an R-2800-27 and armed with four 20mm M2 cannon) which first flew on 3 October 1942 as the prototype for the projected <b>F6F-4</b>. This version never entered production and 02981 was converted to an F6F-3 production aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[31]</a></sup> Another experimental prototype was the <b>XF6F-2</b> (66244), an F6F-3 converted to use a Wright R-2600-15, fitted with a Birman manufactured mixed-flow <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a>, which was later replaced by a Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800-21, also fitted with a Birman turbocharger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[32]</a></sup> The turbochargers proved to be unreliable on both engines, while performance improvements were marginal. As with the XF6F-4, 66244 was soon converted back to a standard F6F-3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[33]</a></sup> Two <b>XF6F-6s</b> (70188 and 70913) were converted from F6F-5s and used the 18-cylinder 2,100 hp (1,567 kW) Pratt and Whitney R-2800-18W two-stage supercharged radial engine with water injection and driving a Hamilton-Standard four-bladed propeller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[34]</a></sup> The XF6F-6s were the fastest version of the Hellcat series with a top speed of 417 mph (671 km/h), but the war ended before this variant could be mass-produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._503._13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._503.-13">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The last Hellcat rolled out in November 1945, the total production being 12,275, of which 11,000 had been built in just two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[36]</a></sup> This high production rate was credited to the sound original design, which required little modification once production was under way.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Navy_and_Marines">U.S. Navy and Marines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: U.S. Navy and Marines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The U.S. Navy much preferred the more docile flight qualities of the F6F compared with the <a href="/wiki/Vought_F4U_Corsair" title="Vought F4U Corsair">Vought F4U Corsair</a>, despite the superior speed of the Corsair. This preference was especially noted during carrier landings, a critical success requirement for the Navy, in which the Corsair was fundamentally flawed in comparison. The Corsair was thus released by the Navy to the Marine Corps who without the need to worry about carrier landings, used the Corsair to immense effect in land-based sorties. The Hellcat remained the standard USN carrier-borne fighter until the F4U series was finally cleared for U.S. carrier operations in late-1944 (the carrier landing issues had by now been tackled largely thanks to use of Corsair by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[37]</a></sup> In addition to its good flight qualities, the Hellcat was easy to maintain and had an airframe tough enough to withstand the rigors of routine carrier operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[38]</a></sup> Like the Wildcat, the Hellcat was designed for ease of manufacture and ability to withstand significant damage.</p>

<p>The Hellcat first saw action against the Japanese on 1 September 1943 when fighters off the <a href="/wiki/USS_Independence_(CVL-22)" title="USS Independence (CVL-22)">USS <i>Independence</i></a> shot down a <a href="/wiki/Kawanishi_H8K" title="Kawanishi H8K">Kawanishi H8K</a> "Emily" <a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dean_p._559._44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dean_p._559.-44">[39]</a></sup> Soon after, on 23 and 24 November, Hellcats engaged Japanese aircraft over <a href="/wiki/Tarawa" title="Tarawa">Tarawa</a>, shooting down a claimed 30 Mitsubishi Zeros for the loss of one F6F.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dean_p._559._44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dean_p._559.-44">[39]</a></sup> Over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabaul,_New_Britain" title="Rabaul, New Britain">Rabaul, New Britain</a>, on 11 November 1943, Hellcats and F4U Corsairs were engaged in day-long fights with many Japanese aircraft including A6M Zeros, claiming nearly 50 aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dean_p._559._44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dean_p._559.-44">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>When trials were flown against a captured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_A6M#A6M5_Type_0_Model_52" title="Mitsubishi A6M">A6M5</a> model Zero, they showed that the Hellcat was faster at all altitudes. The F6F out-climbed the Zero marginally above 14,000 ft and rolled faster at speeds above 235 mph. The Japanese fighter could out-turn its American opponent with ease at low speed and enjoyed a slightly better rate of climb below 14,000 ft. The trials report concluded:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Do not dogfight with a Zero 52. Do not try to follow a loop or half-roll with a pull-through. When attacking, use your superior power and high speed performance to engage at the most favourable moment. To evade a Zero 52 on your tail, roll and dive away into a high speed turn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spick_p._118._45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spick_p._118.-45">[40]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Hellcats were the major U.S. Navy fighter type involved in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Philippine_Sea" title="Battle of the Philippine Sea">Battle of the Philippine Sea</a>, where so many Japanese aircraft were shot down that Navy aircrews nicknamed the battle <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Philippine_Sea#Japanese_raids:_The_Great_Marianas_Turkey_Shoot" title="Battle of the Philippine Sea">"the Great Marianas Turkey Shoot"</a>. The F6F accounted for 75 percent of all aerial victories recorded by the U.S. Navy in the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a>-equipped Hellcat night fighter squadrons appeared in early 1944.</p>
<p>A formidable opponent for the Hellcat was the <a href="/wiki/Kawanishi_N1K" title="Kawanishi N1K">Kawanishi N1K</a>, but it was produced too late and in insufficient numbers to affect the outcome of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sortie.2C_kill_and_loss_figures">Sortie, kill and loss figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sortie, kill and loss figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>U.S. Navy and Marine F6F pilots flew 66,530 combat sorties and claimed 5,163 kills (56% of all U.S. Navy/Marine air victories of the war) at a recorded cost of 270 Hellcats in aerial combat (an overall kill-to-loss ratio of 19:1 based on claimed but not confirmed kills).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barber2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2-48">[43]</a></sup> The aircraft performed well against the best Japanese opponents with a claimed 13:1 kill ratio against the A6M Zero, 9.5:1 against the <a href="/wiki/Nakajima_Ki-84" title="Nakajima Ki-84">Nakajima Ki-84</a>, and 3.7:1 against the <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_J2M" title="Mitsubishi J2M">Mitsubishi J2M</a> during the last year of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barber28_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barber28-49">[44]</a></sup> The F6F became the prime ace-maker aircraft in the American inventory, with 305 Hellcat aces. The U.S. successes were not only attributed to superior aircraft, but also from 1942 onwards, they faced increasingly inexperienced Japanese aviators as well as having the advantage of increasing numerical superiority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[Note 6]</a></sup> In the ground attack role, Hellcats dropped 6,503 tons (5,899 tonnes) of bombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barber2_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2-48">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Navy's all-time leading ace, <a href="/wiki/Captain_(naval)" title="Captain (naval)">Captain</a> <a href="/wiki/David_McCampbell" title="David McCampbell">David McCampbell</a> USN (Ret), scored all his 34 victories in the Hellcat. He once described the F6F as "... an outstanding fighter plane. It performed well, was easy to fly and was a stable gun platform. But what I really remember most was that it was rugged and easy to maintain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the course of World War II, 2,462 F6F Hellcats were lost to all causes; 270 in aerial combat, 553 lost to anti-aircraft ground and shipboard fire, and 341 were lost to operational causes. Of the total figure 1,298 were destroyed in training and ferry operations, normally outside of the combat zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>The British <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> (FAA) received 1,263 F6Fs under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lend-Lease_Act" title="Lend-Lease Act">Lend-Lease Act</a>; initially it was known as the <b>Grumman <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">Gannet</a> Mark I</b>. The name Hellcat replaced it in early 1943 for the sake of simplicity, the Royal Navy at that time adopting the use of the existing American naval names for all the U.S.-made aircraft supplied to it, with the F6F-3 being designated <b>Hellcat F Mk.I</b>, the F6F-5, the <b>Hellcat F Mk.II</b> and the F6F-5N, the <b>Hellcat NF Mk.II</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[Note 7]</a></sup> They saw action off Norway, in the Mediterranean and in the Far East. A number were fitted with photographic reconnaissance equipment similar to the F6F-5P, receiving the designation <b>Hellcat FR Mk.II</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a> being a naval war, the FAA Hellcats primarily faced land based aircraft in the European and Mediterranean Theaters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[50]</a></sup> and as a consequence experienced far fewer opportunities for air-to-air combat than their USN/Marines counterparts; they claimed a total of 52 enemy aircraft kills during 18 aerial combats from May 1944 to July 1945. <a href="/wiki/1844_Naval_Air_Squadron" title="1844 Naval Air Squadron">1844 Naval Air Squadron</a>, on board <a href="/wiki/HMS_Indomitable_(92)" title="HMS Indomitable (92)">HMS <i>Indomitable</i></a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Pacific_Fleet" title="British Pacific Fleet">British Pacific Fleet</a> was the highest scoring unit, with 32.5 kills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>FAA Hellcats, as with other Lend-Lease aircraft, were rapidly replaced by British aircraft after the end of the war, with only two of the 12 squadrons equipped with the Hellcat at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VJ-Day" title="VJ-Day">VJ-Day</a> still retaining Hellcats by the end of 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thetford_naval_p217_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-thetford_naval_p217-59">[52]</a></sup> These two squadrons were disbanded in 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thetford_naval_p217_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-thetford_naval_p217-59">[52]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar">Postwar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Postwar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Postwar, the Hellcat was succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F8F_Bearcat" title="Grumman F8F Bearcat">F8F Bearcat</a>, which was smaller, more powerful (powered by uprated Double Wasp radials) and more maneuverable, but entered service too late to see combat in World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[53]</a></sup> The Hellcat was used for second-line USN duties, including training. In late 1952, Guided Missile Unit 90 used F6F-5K drones, each carrying a 2000 lb bomb, to attack bridges in Korea; flying from <a href="/wiki/USS_Boxer_(CV-21)" title="USS Boxer (CV-21)">USS <i>Boxer</i></a>, radio controlled from an escorting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD_Skyraider" title="AD Skyraider">AD Skyraider</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[54]</a></sup> The French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ronavale" title="Aéronavale">Aéronavale</a> was equipped with F6F-5 Hellcats and used them in <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>. The Uruguayan Navy also used them until the early 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donald_american_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-donald_american-62">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The F6F-5 subtype also gained fame as the first aircraft used by the U.S. Navy's <a href="/wiki/Blue_Angels" title="Blue Angels">Blue Angels</a> official flight demonstration team at its formation in 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="XF6F_prototypes">XF6F prototypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: XF6F prototypes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>XF6F-1</dt>
<dd>First prototype, powered by a two-stage 1,600 hp (1,500 kW) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright_R-2600" title="Wright R-2600">Wright R-2600</a>-10 Cyclone 14 radial piston engine.</dd>
<dt>XF6F-2</dt>
<dd>The first XF6F-1 prototype revised and fitted with a turbocharged Wright R-2600-16 Cyclone radial piston engine. R-2600 replaced by turbo-charged R-2800-21.</dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="226" data-file-width="451" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg/220px-Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg/330px-Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg/440px-Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grumman_XF6F-2_Hellcat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
XF6F-2 showing the later R-2800-21 installation with Birman turbo-charger.</div>
</div>
</div>
<dl>
<dt>XF6F-3</dt>
<dd>Second prototype fitted with a two-stage supercharged 2,000 hp (1,500 kW) Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800-10 Double Wasp radial piston engine.</dd>
<dt>XF6F-4</dt>
<dd>One F6F-3 fitted with a two-speed turbocharged 2,100 hp (1,567 kW) Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800-27 Double Wasp radial piston engine.</dd>
<dt>XF6F-6</dt>
<dd>Two F6F-5s that were fitted with the 2,100 hp (1,566 kW) Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800-18W radial piston engine, and four-bladed propellers.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Series_production">Series production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Series production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>F6F-3 (British designations Gannet Mk. I then Hellcat Mk. I)</dt>
<dd>Single-seat fighter, fighter-bomber aircraft, powered by a 2,000 hp (1,500 kW) Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800-10 Double Wasp radial piston engine.</dd>
<dt>F6F-3E</dt>
<dd>Night fighter version, equipped with an AN/APS-4 radar in a fairing on the starboard outer wing.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>F6F-3N</dt>
<dd>Another night fighter version, equipped with a newer AN/APS-6 radar in a fairing on the starboard outer wing.</dd>
<dt>F6F-5 Hellcat (British Hellcat Mk. II)</dt>
<dd>Improved version, with a redesigned engine cowling, a new windscreen structure with an integral bulletproof windscreen, new ailerons and strengthened tail surfaces; powered by a 2,200 hp (1,640 kW) Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800-10W (-W denotes Water Injection) radial piston engine.</dd>
<dt>F6F-5K Hellcat</dt>
<dd>A number of F6F-5s and F6F-5Ns were converted into radio-controlled target drones.</dd>
</dl>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:F6F-3N_NAS_Jax_1943.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
F6F-5N night fighter with AN/APS-6 radar and 2 20mm M2 cannon.</div>
</div>
</div>
<dl>
<dt>F6F-5N Hellcat (British Hellcat N.F. Mk II)</dt>
<dd>Night fighter version, fitted with an AN/APS-6 radar. Some were armed with two 20 mm (0.79 in) <a href="/wiki/Hispano-Suiza_HS.404" title="Hispano-Suiza HS.404">AN/M2</a> cannon in the inner wing bays and four 0.50 in (12.7 mm) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Browning_machine_gun" title="M2 Browning machine gun">M2 Browning machine guns</a> in the outer.</dd>
<dt>F6F-5P Hellcat</dt>
<dd>Small numbers of F6F-5s were converted into photo-reconnaissance aircraft, with the camera equipment being fitted in the rear fuselage.</dd>
<dt>Hellcat FR Mk II</dt>
<dd>This designation was given to British Hellcats fitted with camera equipment.</dd>
<dt>FV-1</dt>
<dd>Proposed designation for Hellcats to be built by <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Vickers" title="Canadian Vickers">Canadian Vickers</a>; cancelled before any built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norton-64">[57]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i> <i>WWII Aircraft Performance</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[88]</a></sup><i>Jane’s Fighting Aircraft of World War II</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jane_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-jane-96">[89]</a></sup><i>Standard Aircraft Characteristics</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAC_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAC-97">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> 1</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 33 ft 7 in (10.24 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 42 ft 10 in (13.06 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 13 ft 1 in (3.99 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 334 ft² (31 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">Airfoil</a>:</b> <a href="/wiki/NACA_airfoil" title="NACA airfoil">NACA 23015.6 mod</a> root; NACA 23009 tip</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 9,238 lb (4,190 kg)</li>
<li><b>Loaded weight:</b> 12,598 lb (5,714 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 15,415 lb (6,990 kg)</li>
<li>*<b>Fuel capacity:</b> 250 gal (946 L) internal; up to 3 × 150 gal (568 L) external drop tanks</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Zero-lift_drag_coefficient" title="Zero-lift drag coefficient">Zero-lift drag coefficient</a>:</b> 0.0211</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Zero-lift_drag_coefficient" title="Zero-lift drag coefficient">Drag area:</a></b> 7.05 ft² (0.65 m²)</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(wing)" title="Aspect ratio (wing)">Aspect ratio</a>:</b> 5.5</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 1 × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_R-2800" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800">Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800</a>-10W "Double Wasp" two-row <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engine</a> with a two-speed two-stage <a href="/wiki/Supercharger" title="Supercharger">supercharger</a>, 2,200 hp (1,491 kW<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[91]</a></sup>)</li>
<li><b>Propellers:</b> 3-blade Hamilton Standard
<ul>
<li><b>Propeller diameter:</b> 13 ft 1 in (4.0 m)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 330 kn (391 mph, 629 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Stall_(fluid_mechanics)#Stall_speed" title="Stall (fluid mechanics)">Stall speed</a>:</b> 73 kn (84 mph, 135 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_radius" title="Combat radius">Combat radius</a>:</b> 820 nmi (945 mi, 1,520 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Ferry range</a>:</b> 1,330 nmi (1,530 mi, 2,460 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 37,300 ft (11,370 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 3,500 ft/min (17.8 m/s)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 37.7 lb/ft² (184 kg/m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">Power/mass</a>:</b> 0.16 hp/lb (260 W/kg)<br/></li>
<li><b>Time-to-altitude:</b> 7.7 min to 20,000 ft (6,100 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lift-to-drag_ratio" title="Lift-to-drag ratio">Lift-to-drag ratio</a>:</b> 12.2</li>
<li><b>Takeoff roll:</b> 799 ft (244 m)</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Guns:</b><br/>
<ul>
<li>6× 0.50 in (12.7 mm) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Browning_machine_gun" title="M2 Browning machine gun">M2 Browning machine guns</a>, with 400 rounds per gun, (All F6F-3, and most F6F-5) <i>or</i></li>
<li>2 × 0.79 in (20 mm) AN/M2 cannon, with 225 rounds per gun <i>and</i> 4 × 0.50 in (12.7 mm) Browning machine guns with 400 rounds per gun</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Rockets:</b><br/>
<ul>
<li>6 × 5 in (127 mm) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_velocity_aircraft_rocket" title="High velocity aircraft rocket">HVARs</a> <i>or</i></li>
<li>2 × 11¾ in (298 mm) <a href="/wiki/Tiny_Tim_(rocket)" title="Tiny Tim (rocket)">Tiny Tim</a> unguided rockets</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Bombs:</b> up to 4,000 lb (1,814 kg) full load, including:
<ul>
<li><b>Bombs or Torpedoes:</b> (Fuselage mounted on centreline rack)
<ul>
<li>1 × 2,000 lb (907 kg) bomb <i>or</i></li>
<li>1 × Mk.13-3 torpedo;</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Underwing bombs:</b> (F6F-5 had two additional weapons racks either side of fuselage on wing centre-section)
<ul>
<li>2 × 1,000 lb (450 kg) <i>or</i></li>
<li>4 × 500 lb (227 kg)</li>
<li>8 × 250 lb (110 kg)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F6F_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Vraciu" title="Alexander Vraciu">Alexander Vraciu</a>, who had 19 victories on Hellcats flying with VF-6 (9) and VF-16 (10) during World War II.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_McCampbell" title="David McCampbell">David McCampbell</a>, the top U.S. Navy ace of World War II with all of his 34 victories in the Hellcat, commander of Air Group 15 during World War II and recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eugene_A._Valencia,_Jr." title="Eugene A. Valencia, Jr.">Eugene A. Valencia, Jr.</a>, top scoring ace of VF-9 during World War II with 23 victories while flying Hellcats.</li>
</ul>
