<p>The <b>Invasion of Grenada</b> was a 1983 <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>–led <a href="/wiki/Invasion" title="Invasion">invasion</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_nation" title="Island nation">island nation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>, with a population of about 91,000 located 160 kilometres (99 mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, that resulted in a U.S. victory within a matter of weeks. Codenamed <b>Operation Urgent Fury</b>, it was triggered by a hard-line Stalinist coup that resulted in the execution of the previous left-wing revolutionary leader. The invasion resulted in the appointment of an interim government, followed by <a href="/wiki/Grenadian_general_election,_1984" title="Grenadian general election, 1984">democratic elections in 1984</a>. The country has remained a democratic nation since then.</p>
<p>Grenada gained independence from the United Kingdom in 1974. The leftist <a href="/wiki/New_Jewel_Movement" title="New Jewel Movement">New Jewel Movement</a> seized power in a coup in 1979 under <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop" title="Maurice Bishop">Maurice Bishop</a>, suspending the constitution and detaining a number of political prisoners. In 1983, an internal power struggle began over Bishop's relatively moderate foreign policy approach, and on October 19, hard-line Stalinists captured and executed Bishop, his partner <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jacqueline_Creft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jacqueline Creft (page does not exist)">Jacqueline Creft</a>, along with three cabinet ministers and two union leaders. Subsequently, following appeals by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organization_of_Eastern_Caribbean_States" title="Organization of Eastern Caribbean States">Organization of Eastern Caribbean States</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor-General" title="Governor-General">Governor-General</a> of Grenada, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scoon" title="Paul Scoon">Paul Scoon</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_Administration" title="Reagan Administration">Reagan Administration</a> in the U.S. quickly decided to launch a military intervention. From the U.S. perspective, the decision to intervene was in part motivated by concerns over the 600 U.S. medical students on the island and fearing a repeat of the <a href="/wiki/Iran_hostage_crisis" title="Iran hostage crisis">Iran hostage crisis</a>.</p>
<p>The U.S. invasion began six days after Bishop's death, on the morning of 25 October 1983. The <a href="/wiki/Rapid_deployment_force" title="Rapid deployment force">U.S. Army's Rapid Deployment Force</a> (1st, 2nd Ranger Battalions and <a href="/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division" title="82nd Airborne Division">82nd Airborne Division</a> Paratroopers), U.S. Marines, U.S. Army Delta Force, and U.S. Navy SEALs and other combined forces constituted the 7,600 troops from the United States, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, and members of the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Security_System" title="Regional Security System">Regional Security System</a> (RSS)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> defeated Grenadian resistance after a low-altitude airborne assault by the 75th Rangers on Point Salines Airport on the southern end of the island, and a Marine helicopter and amphibious landing occurred on the northern end at Pearl's Airfield shortly afterward. The <a href="/wiki/Military_dictatorship" title="Military dictatorship">military government</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Austin" title="Hudson Austin">Hudson Austin</a> was deposed and replaced by a government appointed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scoon" title="Paul Scoon">Governor-General Paul Scoon</a> until elections were held in 1984.</p>
<p>The invasion was criticized by several countries including <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> privately disapproved of the mission and the lack of notice she received, but publicly supported the intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a>, on 2 November 1983 with a vote of 108 to 9, condemned it as "a flagrant violation of international law".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_GA_Resolution_38_7_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_GA_Resolution_38_7-8">[8]</a></sup> Conversely, it enjoyed broad public support in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson-9">[9]</a></sup> and, over time, a positive evaluation from the Grenadian population, who appreciated the low civilian casualties and the return to democratic elections in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_2012_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP_2012-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="Spurious claim that receives little attention or explanation in the article. No source is provided and no evidence is given. (January 2017)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The U.S. awarded more than 5,000 medals for merit and valor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The date of the invasion is now a national holiday in Grenada, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Day#Grenada" title="Thanksgiving Day">Thanksgiving Day</a>. The commemoration was due to several political prisoners that were freed after the invasion, and subsequently elected into office. A <a href="/wiki/History_of_Grenada#Truth_and_reconciliation_commission" title="History of Grenada">Truth and reconciliation commission</a> was launched in 2000, re-examining some of the controversies of the era; in particular, they made an unsuccessful attempt to find Bishop's body, which was disposed of under Hudson Austin's orders and never found.</p>
<p>For the U.S., the invasion also highlighted problematic issues with communication and coordination between the different branches of the United States military when operating together as a joint force, contributing to investigations and sweeping changes in the form of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldwater-Nichols_Act" title="Goldwater-Nichols Act">Goldwater-Nichols Act</a> and other reorganizations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gairy" title="Eric Gairy">Eric Gairy</a> had led <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> to independence from the United Kingdom in 1974. His term in office coincided with civil strife in Grenada. The political environment was highly charged and although Gairy—head of the <a href="/wiki/Grenada_United_Labour_Party" title="Grenada United Labour Party">Grenada United Labour Party</a>—claimed victory in the general election of 1976, the opposition did not accept the result as legitimate. The civil strife took the form of street violence between Gairy's <a href="/wiki/Private_army" title="Private army">private army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mongoose_Gang" title="Mongoose Gang">Mongoose Gang</a>, and gangs organized by the <a href="/wiki/New_Jewel_Movement" title="New Jewel Movement">New Jewel Movement</a> (NJM). In the late 1970s the NJM began planning to overthrow the government. Party members began to receive military training outside of Grenada. On 13 March 1979, while Gairy was out of the country, the NJM—led by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop" title="Maurice Bishop">Maurice Bishop</a>—launched an armed revolution and overthrew the government, establishing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Government" title="People's Revolutionary Government">People's Revolutionary Government</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport">Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101,_Berlin,_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada,_Bootsfahrt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="800" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101%2C_Berlin%2C_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada%2C_Bootsfahrt.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101%2C_Berlin%2C_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada%2C_Bootsfahrt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101%2C_Berlin%2C_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada%2C_Bootsfahrt.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101%2C_Berlin%2C_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada%2C_Bootsfahrt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101%2C_Berlin%2C_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada%2C_Bootsfahrt.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1982-0610-101%2C_Berlin%2C_Besuch_Regierungsdelegation_Grenada%2C_Bootsfahrt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Bishop government began constructing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Salines_International_Airport" title="Point Salines International Airport">Point Salines International Airport</a> with the help of Britain, Cuba, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, Algeria, and other nations. The airport had been first proposed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_government" title="British government">British government</a> in 1954, when Grenada was still a British colony. It had been designed by <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadians</a>, underwritten by the British government, and partly built by a London firm. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._government" title="U.S. government">U.S. government</a> accused Grenada of constructing facilities to aid a Soviet-Cuban military buildup in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> based upon the 9,000-foot length runway, which could accommodate the largest Soviet aircraft like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An-12" title="An-12">An-12</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An-22" title="An-22">An-22</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An-124" title="An-124">An-124</a>, which would enhance the Soviet and Cuban transportation of weapons to Central American insurgents and expand Soviet regional influence. Bishop's government claimed that the airport was built to accommodate commercial aircraft carrying tourists, pointing out that such jets could not land at Pearl's Airstrip on the island's north end (5,200 feet) and could not be expanded because its runway abutted a mountain at one end and the ocean at the other.</p>

<p>In 1983, then-member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> <a href="/wiki/Ron_Dellums" title="Ron Dellums">Ron Dellums</a> (D, California), traveled to Grenada on a fact-finding mission, having been invited by the country's prime minister. Dellums described his findings before Congress:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>... based on my personal observations, discussion and analysis of the new international airport under construction in Grenada, it is my conclusion that this project is specifically now and has always been for the purpose of economic development and is not for military use. ... It is my thought that it is absurd, patronizing, and totally unwarranted for the United States government to charge that this airport poses a military threat to the United States' national security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commentary_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-commentary-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In March 1983, President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> began issuing warnings about the threat posed to the United States and the Caribbean by the "Soviet-Cuban militarization" of the Caribbean as evidenced by the excessively long airplane runway being built, as well as intelligence sources indicating increased Soviet interest in the island. He said that the 9,000-foot (2,700 m) runway and the numerous fuel storage tanks were unnecessary for commercial flights, and that evidence pointed that the airport was to become a Cuban-Soviet forward military airbase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 May 2009 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Salines_International_Airport" title="Point Salines International Airport">Point Salines International Airport</a> was officially renamed the <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop_International_Airport" title="Maurice Bishop International Airport">Maurice Bishop International Airport</a>, in honour of the slain pre-coup leader <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop" title="Maurice Bishop">Maurice Bishop</a> by the Government of Grenada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airport_rename1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-airport_rename1-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airport_rename2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-airport_rename2-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="October_1983">October 1983</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: October 1983">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 16 October 1983, a party faction led by Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Coard" title="Bernard Coard">Bernard Coard</a> seized power. Bishop was placed under house arrest. Mass protests against the action led to Bishop's escaping detention and reasserting his authority as the head of the government. Bishop was eventually captured and murdered, along his pregnant partner, and several government officials and union leaders loyal to him. The army under <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Austin" title="Hudson Austin">Hudson Austin</a> then stepped in and formed a military council to rule the country. The <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Grenada" title="Governor-General of Grenada">governor-general</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scoon" title="Paul Scoon">Paul Scoon</a>, was placed under house arrest. The army announced a four-day total curfew where anyone seen on the streets would be subject to <a href="/wiki/Summary_execution" title="Summary execution">summary execution</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1950" data-file-width="3020" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg/220px-Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg/330px-Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg/440px-Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eastern_Caribbean_Defense_Force.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Members of the Eastern Caribbean Defense Force</div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organization_of_Eastern_Caribbean_States" title="Organization of Eastern Caribbean States">Organization of Eastern Caribbean States</a> (OECS), as well as the nations of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, appealed to the United States for assistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-dtic-3">[3]</a></sup> It was later announced that <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Grenada" title="Governor-General of Grenada">Grenada's governor-general</a>, Paul Scoon, had actually requested the invasion through secret diplomatic channels and for his safety it had not been made public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z9f6WdltuE_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-z9f6WdltuE-18">[18]</a></sup> Scoon was well within his rights to take this action under the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_power" title="Reserve power">reserve powers</a> vested in the Crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTObit_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTObit-19">[19]</a></sup> On Saturday 22 October 1983, the Deputy High Commissioner in Bridgetown, Barbados visited Grenada and reported that Sir Paul Scoon was well and "did not request military intervention, either directly or indirectly".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 25 October, Grenada was invaded by the combined forces of the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Security_System" title="Regional Security System">Regional Security System</a> (RSS) based in Barbados, in an operation codenamed <i>Operation Urgent Fury</i>. The U.S. stated this was done at the request of the prime ministers of Barbados and <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Adams_(politician)" title="Tom Adams (politician)">Tom Adams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eugenia_Charles" title="Eugenia Charles">Dame Eugenia Charles</a>, respectively. Nonetheless, the invasion was highly criticized by the governments in Canada, Trinidad and Tobago, and the United Kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> condemned it as "a flagrant violation of international law"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_Resolution_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_Resolution-21">[21]</a></sup> by a vote of 108 in favour to 9, with 27 abstentions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_Resolution_Vote_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_Resolution_Vote-22">[22]</a></sup> The United Nations Security Council considered a similar resolution, which failed to pass when vetoed by the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_day_of_the_invasion">First day of the invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First day of the invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The invasion commenced at 05:00 on 25 October 1983. American forces refuelled and departed from the <a href="/wiki/Grantley_Adams_International_Airport" title="Grantley Adams International Airport">Grantley Adams International Airport</a> on the nearby <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> before daybreak en route to Grenada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It was the first major operation conducted by the U.S. military since the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_Admiral" title="Vice Admiral">Vice Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Metcalf,_III" title="Joseph Metcalf, III">Joseph Metcalf, III</a>, Commander <a href="/wiki/United_States_Second_Fleet" title="United States Second Fleet">Second Fleet</a>, was the overall commander of U.S. forces, designated Joint Task Force 120, which included elements of each military service and multiple special operations units. Fighting continued for several days and the total number of U.S. troops reached some 7,000 along with 300 troops from the OAS. The invading forces encountered about 1,500 Grenadian soldiers and about 700 armed <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> nationals manning defensive positions. Grenada's security forces possessed no tanks, only 8 <a href="/wiki/BTR-60" title="BTR-60">BTR-60PB</a> armored personnel carriers and 2 <a href="/wiki/BRDM-2" title="BRDM-2">BRDM-2</a> scout cars they had received from the Soviet Union in February 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PH2004_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-PH2004-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-25">[25]</a></sup> Their arsenal also included twelve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZU-23" title="ZU-23">ZU-23</a> anti-aircraft guns, <a href="/wiki/DShK" title="DShK">DShK</a> heavy machine guns, and a limited number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/82-PM-37" title="82-PM-37">M37</a> 82mm mortars and <a href="/wiki/RPG-7" title="RPG-7">RPG-7</a> launchers.</p>
<p>The main objectives on the first day of the invasion were the capture of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Salines_International_Airport" title="Point Salines International Airport">Point Salines International Airport</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75th_Ranger_Regiment" title="75th Ranger Regiment">75th Ranger Regiment</a>, to permit the <a href="/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division" title="82nd Airborne Division">82nd Airborne Division</a> to land reinforcements on the island; the capture of <a href="/wiki/Pearls_Airport" title="Pearls Airport">Pearls Airport</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8th_Marine_Regiment" title="8th Marine Regiment">8th Marine Regiment</a>; and the rescue of the U.S. students at the True Blue Campus of <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_University" title="St. George's University">St. George's University</a>. In addition, a number of special operations missions were undertaken to obtain intelligence and secure key individuals and equipment. In general, many of these missions were plagued by inadequate intelligence, planning, and accurate maps of any kind (the American forces mostly relied upon tourist maps).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuban_forces_in_Grenada">Cuban forces in Grenada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cuban forces in Grenada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nature of the Cuban military presence in Grenada was more complex than initially suggested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castro_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castro-26">[26]</a></sup> As in <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, and other nations with large contingents of Cuban troops, the line between civilians and military personnel was blurred. For example, <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> often described Cuban construction crews deployed overseas as "workers and soldiers at the same time"; the duality of their role being consistent with Havana's 'citizen soldier' tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castro_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Castro-26">[26]</a></sup> At the time of the invasion, there were an estimated 784 Cuban nationals on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demographic_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Demographic-27">[27]</a></sup> At least 636 were formally listed as construction workers, another 64 as military personnel, and 18 as dependents. The remainder claimed to be either medical staff or teachers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demographic_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Demographic-27">[27]</a></sup> Colonel Pedro Tortoló Comas, the highest ranking Cuban military official in Grenada in 1983, later stated that he'd issued weapons and ammunition to many of the construction workers for self-defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demographic_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Demographic-27">[27]</a></sup> According to journalist <a href="/wiki/Bob_Woodward" title="Bob Woodward">Bob Woodward</a> in his book <i>Veil</i>, captured "military advisors" from socialist countries were actually accredited diplomats and included their dependents. None, Woordward claimed, took any actual part in the fighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Other historians have asserted that most of the supposed civil technicians on Grenada were Cuban special forces and combat engineers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the invasion began, Cuba's lack of adequate naval transport facilities and its ongoing military commitments in Africa made it difficult to reinforce Grenada on such short notice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demographic_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Demographic-27">[27]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Cuban nationals were expressly forbidden to surrender to U.S. forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demographic_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-Demographic-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navy_SEAL_reconnaissance_missions">Navy SEAL reconnaissance missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Navy SEAL reconnaissance missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>U.S. Special Operations Forces were deployed to Grenada beginning on October 23, before the invasion on October 25. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy_Seals" title="U.S. Navy Seals">U.S. Navy Seals</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SEAL_Team_6" title="SEAL Team 6">SEAL Team 6</a> were airdropped at sea with inflatable boats to perform a reconnaissance mission on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Point_Salines&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Point Salines (page does not exist)">Point Salines</a>, but stormy weather resulted in four of the SEALs drowning upon landing. The motor on the boat used by the survivors flooded while evading a patrol boat, causing the mission to be aborted. A SEAL mission on the 24th also was made unsuccessful due to harsh weather, resulting in little intelligence being gathered in advance of the impending U.S. intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_SEAL_Museum_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Navy_SEAL_Museum-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air-Landing_at_Point_Salines">Air-Landing at Point Salines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Air-Landing at Point Salines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="539" data-file-width="816" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg/350px-Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg/525px-Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg/700px-Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Op_Urgent_Fury_Invasion_Map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Initial troop invasion areas</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At midnight on October 24, the A and B companies of the 1st Battalion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75th_Ranger_Regiment" title="75th Ranger Regiment">75th Ranger Regiment</a> embarked on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130" title="C-130">C-130s</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Army_Airfield" title="Hunter Army Airfield">Hunter Army Airfield</a> to perform an air assault landing on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Salines_International_Airport" title="Point Salines International Airport">Point Salines International Airport</a>. Initially intending to land at the airport and then disembark, the Rangers had to switch abruptly to a parachute landing when it was learned mid-flight that the runway was obstructed. The air drop began at 5:30 AM on the 25th in the face of moderate resistance from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZU-23" title="ZU-23">ZU-23</a> anti-aircraft guns and several BTR-60 APCs, the latter of which were knocked out by <a href="/wiki/M67_recoilless_rifle" title="M67 recoilless rifle">90mm recoilless rifles</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC-130" title="AC-130">AC-130</a> gunships also provided support for the landing. Cuban construction vehicles were commandeered to help clear the airfield, and one was even used to provide mobile cover for the Rangers as they moved to seize the heights surrounding the airfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 10 AM, the air strip had been cleared of obstructions and transport planes were able to land directly and unload additional reinforcements, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M151" title="M151">M151 Jeeps</a> and elements of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Peace_Force" title="Caribbean Peace Force">Caribbean Peace Force</a>, which were assigned to guard the perimeter and detainees. Starting at 2 PM, units from the <a href="/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division" title="82nd Airborne Division">82nd Airborne Division</a>, under MG Edward Trobaugh, began landing at Point Salines, including battalions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/325th_Infantry_Regiment" title="325th Infantry Regiment">325th Infantry Regiment</a>. At 3:30 PM, a counterattack by 3 BTR-60s of the Grenadian Army Motorized Company was repelled with fire from recoilless rifles and an AC-130.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CombatReform_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-CombatReform-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Rangers proceeded to fan out and secure the surrounding area, including negotiating the surrender of over a hundred Cubans in an aviation hangar. However, a Jeep-mounted Ranger patrol became lost searching for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_University" title="Saint George's University">True Blue Campus</a> and was ambushed, suffering 4 KIA. The Rangers eventually secured True Blue campus and its students, where they were shocked to discover only 140 students, and were told that more were located at another campus in Anse. In all, the Rangers lost 5 men on the first day, but succeeded in securing the Point Salines and the surrounding area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capture_of_Pearls_Airport">Capture of Pearls Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Capture of Pearls Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Close to midnight on October 24, a platoon of Navy SEALs, from SEAL Team 4, under Lieutenant Mike Walsh approached the beach near <a href="/wiki/Pearls_Airport" title="Pearls Airport">Pearls Airport</a>, evading patrol boats and overcoming stormy weather, they determined that the beach was undefended but unsuitable for an amphibious landing. The 2nd Battalion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8th_Marine_Regiment" title="8th Marine Regiment">8th Marine Regiment</a> then landed south of Pearls Airport using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CH-46_Sea_Knight" title="CH-46 Sea Knight">CH-46 Sea Knight</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CH-53_Sea_Stallion" title="CH-53 Sea Stallion">CH-53 Sea Stallion</a> helicopters at 5:30 AM on the 25th. The Marines proceeded to capture Pearl Airport, encountering only light resistance, including a <a href="/wiki/DShK" title="DShK">DShK</a> machine gun which was destroyed by a Marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AH-1_Cobra" title="AH-1 Cobra">AH-1 Cobra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kreisher_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kreisher-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raid_on_Radio_Free_Grenada">Raid on Radio Free Grenada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Raid on Radio Free Grenada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the early morning of the 25th, another team from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SEAL_Team_6" title="SEAL Team 6">SEAL Team 6</a> was inserted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UH-60_Blackhawk" title="UH-60 Blackhawk">UH-60 Blackhawk</a> helicopters to capture Radio Free Grenada, in order to use it for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PsyOps" title="PsyOps">PsyOps</a> purposes. Although the station was captured unopposed, a counter-attack with armored cars forced the lightly-armed SEALs to retreat in the jungle, destroying the radio transmitter as they left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_SEAL_Museum_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Navy_SEAL_Museum-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raids_on_Fort_Rupert_and_Richmond_Hill_Prison">Raids on Fort Rupert and Richmond Hill Prison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Raids on Fort Rupert and Richmond Hill Prison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the October 25, raids were undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Delta_Force" title="Delta Force">Delta Force</a> and C Company of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75th_Ranger_Regiment" title="75th Ranger Regiment">75th Ranger Regiment</a>, embarked upon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UH-60_Blackhawk" title="UH-60 Blackhawk">MH-60</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MH-6_Little_Bird" title="MH-6 Little Bird">MH-6 Little Bird</a> helicopters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/160th_Special_Operations_Aviation_Regiment" title="160th Special Operations Aviation Regiment">Task Force 160</a> to capture Fort Ruppert, where the leadership of the Revolutionary Council was believed to reside, and Richmond Hill Prison, where many political prisoners were being held. The raid on Richmond Hill Prison lacked vital intelligence, including the fact that multiple anti-aircraft guns were located around and above the prison, and that the prison was located on a steep hill without room for a helicopter to land. Withering anti-craft fire wounded passengers and crew, and forced one MH-60 helicopter to crash land, causing another helicopter to land next to it to protect the survivors. One pilot was killed, and the Delta force operators had to be relieved by a separate force of Rangers. The raid on Fort Rupert, however, was successful in capturing several leaders of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Government" title="People's Revolutionary Government">People's Revolutionary Government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CombatReform_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-CombatReform-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_to_rescue_Governor_General_Scoon">Mission to rescue Governor General Scoon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mission to rescue Governor General Scoon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The last major special operation was a mission to rescue and evacuate <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scoon" title="Paul Scoon">Governor General Paul Scoon</a> from his mansion in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_George,_Grenada" title="Saint George, Grenada">Saint George, Grenada</a>. The mission departed late at 5:30 AM on October 25 from Barbados, resulting in the Grenadian forces being already aware of the U.S. invasion when it landed and secured Governor Scoon. Although the SEAL team's entry into the mansion went unopposed, a large local counterattack led by <a href="/wiki/BTR-60" title="BTR-60">BTR-60</a> armored personnel carriers trapped the SEALs and the governor inside. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC-130" title="AC-130">AC-130</a> gunships, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair" title="A-7 Corsair">A-7 Corsair</a> strike planes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AH-1_Cobra" title="AH-1 Cobra">AH-1 Cobra</a> attack helicopters were called in to support the besieged SEALs, but the SEALs remained trapped for the next 24 hours.</p>

<p>At 7 PM on October 25, 250 U.S. Marines from G Company of the <a href="/wiki/22nd_Marine_Expeditionary_Unit" title="22nd Marine Expeditionary Unit">22nd Marine Assault Unit</a> equipped with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibious_Assault_Vehicles" title="Amphibious Assault Vehicles">Amphibious Assault Vehicles</a> and four <a href="/wiki/M60_Patton" title="M60 Patton">M60 Patton</a> tanks landed at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Mal_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grand Mal Bay (page does not exist)">Grand Mal Bay</a>, and relieved the Navy SEALs the following morning on October 26, allowing Governor Scoon, his wife and nine aides to be safely evacuated at 10 AM that day. The Marine tank crews continued advancing in the face of sporadic resistance, knocking out a BRDM-2 armored car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-25">[25]</a></sup> G Company subsequently defeated and overwhelmed the Grenadian defenders at Fort Frederick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kreisher_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kreisher-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airstrikes">Airstrikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Airstrikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Airstrikes were undertaken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair" title="A-7 Corsair">A-7 Corsairs</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine" title="U.S. Marine">U.S. Marine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AH-1_Cobra" title="AH-1 Cobra">AH-1 Cobra</a> attack helicopters against Fort Rupert and Fort Frederick. An A-7 raid on Fort Frederick targeting anti-aircraft guns hit a nearby mental hospital, killing 18 civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-dtic-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62</sup> Two Marine AH-1T Cobras and a UH-60 Blackhawk were shot down in a raid against Fort Frederick, resulting in five KIA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kreisher_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kreisher-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_day_of_the_invasion">Second day of the invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Second day of the invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the second day, U.S. Commander on the ground, General Trobaugh of the 82nd Airborne Division, had two goals: securing the perimeter around Salines Airport and rescuing the U.S. students they had learned were at the campus in Anse. Because of the lack of undamaged helicopters after the losses on the first day, the Army had to delay pursuing the second objective until it made contact with Marine forces.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attack_on_the_Cuban_compound">Attack on the Cuban compound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Attack on the Cuban compound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in the morning of the 26th, a patrol from the 2nd Battalion of the 325th Infantry Regiment was ambushed by Cuban forces near the village of Calliste, suffering six wounded and two killed in the ensuing firefight including the commander of Company B. Following that, U.S. Navy air strikes and an artillery bombardment by <a href="/wiki/M102_howitzer" title="M102 howitzer">105mm howitzers</a> targeting the main Cuban encampment eventually lead to their surrender at 8:30. US forces pushed on into the village of Frequente, where they discovered a Cuban weapon cache supposedly sufficient to equip "six battalions." There, a reconnaissance platoon mounted of gun-jeeps was ambushed by Cuban forces, but return fire from the jeeps, and mortars from a nearby infantry unit inflicted four casualties on the ambushers at no U.S. loss. Cuban resistance largely ended after these engagements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".27Rescue.27_at_Grand_Anse">'Rescue' at Grand Anse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 'Rescue' at Grand Anse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the afternoon of the 26, US Rangers of the 2nd Battalion of the Ranger Regiment mounted U.S. Marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CH-46_Sea_Knight" title="CH-46 Sea Knight">CH-46 Sea Knight</a> helicopters to launch an air assault on the Anse campus. The campus guards offered light resistance before fleeing, wounding one Ranger, but one of the helicopters crashed on the approach after its blade hit a palm tree. The 233 U.S. students present were successfully evacuated into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CH-53_Sea_Stallion" title="CH-53 Sea Stallion">CH-53 Sea Stallion</a> helicopters, but informed the U.S. commanders that there was a <i>third</i> campus with U.S. students at Prickly Bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kreisher_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kreisher-33">[33]</a></sup> A squad of 11 Rangers was accidentally left behind, ultimately egressing on a rubber raft which was picked up by the <a href="/wiki/USS_Caron" title="USS Caron">USS Caron</a> at 11 PM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CombatReform_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-CombatReform-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_day_of_the_invasion_and_after">Third day of the invasion and after</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Third day of the invasion and after">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By October 27, organized resistance was rapidly diminishing, but the American forces did not yet realize this. The Marine 22nd MAU and 8th Regiment continued advancing along the coast and capturing additional towns, meeting little resistance, although one patrol did encounter a single BTR-60 during the night and dispatched it with their <a href="/wiki/M72_LAW" title="M72 LAW">M72 LAW</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/325th_Infantry_Regiment" title="325th Infantry Regiment">325th Infantry Regiment</a> advanced toward Saint George, capturing Grand Anse (where they discovered 20 U.S. students they had missed the first day), the town of Ruth Howard, and the capital of Saint George, meeting only scattered resistance. An A-7 airstrike called by an Air-Naval Gunfire Liaison team accidentally hit the command post of the 2nd Brigade, wounding 17 troops, one of whom died of wounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

Bombardment of Point Calivigny</div>

<p>The Army had reports that PRA forces were amassing at the Calivigny barracks, only five kilometers miles away from the Point Salines airfield. They therefore organized an air assault by the 2nd Battalion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75th_Ranger_Regiment" title="75th Ranger Regiment">75th Ranger Regiment</a> which would be preceded by a heavy preparatory bombardment by field howitzers (which mostly missed, their shells falling into ocean), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair" title="A-7 Corsair">A-7 Corsairs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC-130" title="AC-130">AC-130s</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/USS_Caron" title="USS Caron">USS Caron</a>. However, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UH-60_Blackhawk" title="UH-60 Blackhawk">Blackhawk</a> helicopters began dropping off troops near the barracks, they approached at too high a speed, and one of them crash-landed and the two behind it collided into it, killing three and wounding four. Ironically, the barracks were deserted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CombatReform_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-CombatReform-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the following days, resistance ended entirely and the Army and Marines spread across the island, arresting PRA officials, seizing caches of weapons, and seeing to the repatriation of Cuban engineers.</p>
<p>On November 1, two companies from the 22nd Marine Amphibious Unit made a combined sea and helicopter landing on the island of <a href="/wiki/Carriacou" title="Carriacou">Carriacou</a> 17 miles (27 km) to the northeast of Grenada. The nineteen Grenadan soldiers defending the island surrendered without a fight. This was the last military action of the campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outcome">Outcome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Outcome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Official U.S. sources state that some of their opponents were well prepared and well positioned and put up stubborn resistance, to the extent that the U.S. called in two <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">battalions</a> of reinforcements on the evening of 26 October. The total naval and air superiority of the coalition forces—including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helicopter_gunship" title="Helicopter gunship">helicopter gunships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naval_gunfire_support" title="Naval gunfire support">naval gunfire support</a> as well as members of reserve <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_SEALs" title="United States Navy SEALs">Navy SEALs</a>, had overwhelmed the defenders.</p>
<p>Nearly 8,000 soldiers, sailors, airmen, and Marines had participated in Operation Urgent Fury along with 353 Caribbean allies of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Peace_Force" title="Caribbean Peace Force">Caribbean Peace Forces</a>. U.S. forces sustained 19 killed and 116 wounded; Cuban forces sustained 25 killed, 59 wounded, and 638 combatants captured. Grenadian forces suffered 45 dead and 358 wounded; at least 24 civilians were also killed, 18 of whom died in the accidental bombing of a Grenadian mental hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-dtic-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62</sup> The U.S. also destroyed a significant amount of Grenada's military hardware, including six APCs and an armored car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-25">[25]</a></sup> A second armored car was impounded and later shipped back to <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Base_Quantico" title="Marine Corps Base Quantico">Marine Corps Base Quantico</a> for inspection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BRDM_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-BRDM-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_in_the_United_States">Reaction in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Reaction in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="545" data-file-width="355" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg/220px-Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg/330px-Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3c/Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Citizens_of_Grenada-US_leaflet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Leaflet distributed during the invasion by 9th PSYOP Bn</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A month after the invasion, <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i></a> magazine described it as having "broad popular support." A <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">congressional</a> study group concluded that the invasion had been justified, as most members felt that U.S. students at the university near a contested runway could have been taken hostage as <a href="/wiki/Iran_hostage_crisis" title="Iran hostage crisis">U.S. diplomats in Iran</a> had been four years previously. The group's report caused <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">House Speaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Tip_O%27Neill" title="Tip O'Neill">Tip O'Neill</a> to change his position on the issue from opposition to support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1990" data-file-width="2920" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg/220px-M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg/330px-M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg/440px-M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M102_howitzers_during_Operation_Urgent_Fury.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
M102 howitzers of 1st Bn 320th FA, 82D Abn Div firing during battle</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>However, some members of the study group dissented from its findings. Congressman <a href="/wiki/Louis_Stokes" title="Louis Stokes">Louis Stokes</a>, D-Ohio, stated: "Not a single American child nor single American national was in any way placed in danger or placed in a hostage situation prior to the invasion." The <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Black_Caucus" title="Congressional Black Caucus">Congressional Black Caucus</a> denounced the invasion and seven <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> congressmen, led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ted_Weiss" title="Ted Weiss">Ted Weiss</a>, introduced an unsuccessful resolution to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impeach" title="Impeach">impeach</a> Ronald Reagan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the evening of 25 October 1983 by telephone, on the newscast <i><a href="/wiki/Nightline" title="Nightline">Nightline</a></i>, anchor <a href="/wiki/Ted_Koppel" title="Ted Koppel">Ted Koppel</a> spoke to medical students on Grenada who stated that they were safe and did not feel their lives were in danger. The next evening, again by telephone, medical students told Koppel how grateful they were for the invasion and the Army Rangers, which probably saved their lives. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">State Department</a> officials had assured the medical students that they would be able to complete their medical school education in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_reaction">International reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: International reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grenada_Invasion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="901" data-file-width="639" height="494" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Grenada_Invasion.jpg/350px-Grenada_Invasion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Grenada_Invasion.jpg/525px-Grenada_Invasion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e3/Grenada_Invasion.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grenada_Invasion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of invasion plan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 2 November 1983 by a vote of 108 in favour to 9 voting against (<a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">Antigua and Barbuda</a>, Barbados, <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, El Salvador, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, Jamaica, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, and the United States), with 27 abstentions, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> adopted General Assembly Resolution 38/7, which "deeply deplores the armed intervention in Grenada, which constitutes a flagrant violation of international law and of the independence, sovereignty and territorial integrity of that State."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_GA_Resolution_38_7_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-UN_GA_Resolution_38_7-8">[8]</a></sup> It went on to deplore “the death of innocent civilians” the “killing of the prime Minister and other prominent Grenadians” and called for an “immediate cessation of the armed intervention” and demanded “that free elections be organized”.</p>
<p>This was the first military <a href="/wiki/Rollback" title="Rollback">rollback</a> of a Communist nation. The Soviet Union said that Grenada had for a long time been the object of United States threats, that the invasion violated international law, and that no small nation not to the liking of the United States would find itself safe if the aggression against Grenada were not rebuffed. The governments of some countries stated that the United States intervention was a return to the era of barbarism. The governments of other countries said the United States by its invasion had violated several treaties and conventions to which it was a party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>A similar resolution was discussed in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> and although receiving widespread support it was ultimately <a href="/wiki/Permanent_members_of_the_United_Nations_Security_Council" title="Permanent members of the United Nations Security Council">vetoed</a> by the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zunes_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zunes-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spartacus_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spartacus-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-41">[41]</a></sup> President of the United States <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, when asked if he was concerned by the lopsided 108–9 vote in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_General_Assembly" title="UN General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a> said "it didn't upset my breakfast at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grenada is part of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> and, following the invasion, it requested help from other Commonwealth members. The intervention was opposed by the United Kingdom, Trinidad and Tobago, and Canada, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-dtic-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:50</sup> British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, a close ally of Reagan on other matters, personally opposed the U.S. invasion. Reagan told her it might happen; she did not know for sure it was coming until three hours before. At 12:30 am on the morning of the invasion, Thatcher sent a message to Reagan:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This action will be seen as intervention by a Western country in the internal affairs of a small independent nation, however unattractive its regime. I ask you to consider this in the context of our wider East/West relations and of the fact that we will be having in the next few days to present to our Parliament and people the siting of <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">Cruise missiles</a> in this country. I must ask you to think most carefully about these points. I cannot conceal that I am deeply disturbed by your latest communication. You asked for my advice. I have set it out and hope that even at this late stage you will take it into account before events are irrevocable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> (The full text remains classified.)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Reagan told Thatcher before anyone else that the invasion would begin in a few hours, but ignored her complaints. None of the worries Thatcher had mentioned came true, and she publicly supported the American action. Reagan phoned to apologized for the miscommunication, and the long-term friendly relationship endured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Grenada#Grenada_since_1983" title="Grenada">Grenada since 1983</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1970" data-file-width="3070" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg/220px-Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg/330px-Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg/440px-Urgent_fury_civilians_waiting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Following the U.S. victory, the American and Caribbean governments quickly reaffirmed Scoon as Queen Elizabeth II's sole legitimate representative in Grenada--and hence, the only lawful authority on the island. In accordance with Commonwealth constitutional practice, Scoon assumed power as interim head of government, and formed an advisory council which named <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Brathwaite" title="Nicholas Brathwaite">Nicholas Brathwaite</a> as chairman pending new elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z9f6WdltuE_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-z9f6WdltuE-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTObit_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTObit-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Grenadian_general_election,_1984" title="Grenadian general election, 1984">Democratic elections held in December 1984</a> were won by the <a href="/wiki/Grenada_National_Party" title="Grenada National Party">Grenada National Party</a> and a government was formed led by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Blaize" title="Herbert Blaize">Herbert Blaize</a>.</p>

<p>U.S. forces remained in Grenada after combat operations finished in December as part of Operation Island Breeze. Elements remaining, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_Police_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Military Police Corps (United States Army)">military police</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._special_forces" title="U.S. special forces">special forces</a>, and a specialized <a href="/wiki/Military_Intelligence_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Military Intelligence Corps (United States Army)">intelligence detachment</a>, performed security missions and assisted members of the Caribbean Peacekeeping Force and the Royal Grenadian Police Force.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Salines_International_Airport" title="Point Salines International Airport">Point Salines International Airport</a> was renamed in honor of Prime Minister Maurice Bishop on 29 May 2009, the 65th anniversary of his birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airport_rename1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-airport_rename1-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airport_rename2_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-airport_rename2-17">[17]</a></sup> Hundreds of Grenadians turned out to commemorate the historical event. <a href="/wiki/Tillman_Thomas" title="Tillman Thomas">Tillman Thomas</a>, Prime Minister of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">Saint Vincent and the Grenadines</a> and close friend of Bishop, gave the keynote speech and referred to the renaming as an act of the Grenadian people coming home to themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grenadian_Connection_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grenadian_Connection-47">[47]</a></sup> He also hoped that it would help bring closure to a chapter of denial in Grenada's history.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The invasion showed problems with the U.S. government's "information apparatus," which <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_magazine" title="Time magazine">Time</a></i> described as still being in "some disarray" three weeks after the invasion. For example, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">U.S. State Department</a> falsely claimed that a <a href="/wiki/Mass_grave" title="Mass grave">mass grave</a> had been discovered that held 100 bodies of islanders who had been killed by Communist forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson-9">[9]</a></sup> Major General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Schwarzkopf" title="Norman Schwarzkopf">Norman Schwarzkopf</a>, deputy commander of the invasion force, said that 160 Grenadian soldiers and 71 Cubans had been killed during the invasion; <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a> had given a much lower count of 59 Cuban and Grenadian deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson-9">[9]</a></sup> Ronald H. Cole's <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Grenada#The_Invasion" title="Invasion of Grenada">report</a> for the Joint Chiefs of Staff showed an even lower count.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-dtic-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also of concern were the problems that the invasion showed with the military. There was a lack of intelligence about Grenada, which exacerbated the difficulties faced by the quickly assembled invasion force. For example, it was not known that the students were actually at two different campuses and there was a thirty-hour delay in reaching students at the second campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson-9">[9]</a></sup> Maps provided to soldiers on the ground were tourist maps on which <a href="/wiki/Military_Grid_Reference_System" title="Military Grid Reference System">military grid reference lines</a> were drawn by hand to report locations of units and request artillery and aircraft fire support. They also did not show <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> and were not marked with crucial positions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> ships providing naval gunfire and U.S. Marine, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> and Navy fighter/bomber support aircraft providing close air support mistakenly fired upon and killed U.S. ground forces due to differences in charts and location coordinates, data, and methods of calling for fire support. Communications between services were also noted as not being compatible and hindered the coordination of operations. The landing strip was drawn-in by hand on the map given to some members of the invasion force.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A heavily fictionalized account of the invasion from a U.S. military perspective is shown in the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Clint_Eastwood" title="Clint Eastwood">Clint Eastwood</a> motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Heartbreak_Ridge" title="Heartbreak Ridge">Heartbreak Ridge</a></i>, in which Marines replaced the actual roles of U.S. Army units. Due to the movie's portrayal of several incompetent officers and NCOs, the Army opted out its military support of the movie.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goldwater-Nichols_Act">Goldwater-Nichols Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Goldwater-Nichols Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldwater-Nichols_Act" title="Goldwater-Nichols Act">Goldwater-Nichols Act</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="787" data-file-width="528" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg/220px-Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg/330px-Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg/440px-Grenada_barracks_before_and_after_attack_1983.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Analysis by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> showed a need for improved communications and coordination between the branches of the U.S. forces. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congressional</a> investigations of many of the reported problems resulted in the most important legislative change affecting the U.S. military organization, doctrine, career progression, and operating procedures since the end of World War II – the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldwater-Nichols_Act" title="Goldwater-Nichols Act">Goldwater-Nichols Department of Defense Reorganization Act of 1986</a> (Pub. L.99–433).</p>
<p>The Goldwater-Nichols Act reworked the command structure of the United States military, thereby making the most sweeping changes to the United States Department of Defense since the department was established in the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Act_of_1947" title="National Security Act of 1947">National Security Act of 1947</a>. It increased the powers of the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff and created the concept of a truly unified joint U.S. forces (i.e., Army, Air Force, Marines, and Navy forces organized under one command). One of the first reorganizations resulting from both the Department of Defense analysis and the legislation was the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Special_Operations_Command" title="U.S. Special Operations Command">U.S. Special Operations Command</a> in 1987.</p>

<p>October 25 is a national holiday in Grenada, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Day#Grenada" title="Thanksgiving Day">Thanksgiving Day</a>, to commemorate the invasion.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_University" title="St. George's University">St. George's University</a> built a monument on its <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_University" title="St. George's University">True Blue Campus</a> to memorialize the U.S. servicemen killed during the invasion, and marks the day with an annual memorial ceremony.</p>
<p>In 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Grenada" title="Government of Grenada">Government of Grenada</a> announced a move to build a monument to honour the Cubans killed during the invasion. At the time of the announcement the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Cuba" title="Government of Cuba">Cuban</a> and Grenadian governments are still seeking to locate a suitable site for the monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_Admiral" title="Vice Admiral">Vice Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Metcalf,_III" title="Joseph Metcalf, III">Joseph Metcalf, III</a>, COMSECONDFLT, became Commander, Joint Task Force 120 (CJTF 120), and commanded units from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army" title="US Army">Army</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">Navy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a> from the Flag Spaces aboard the MARG flagship USS Guam. CJTF 120 was supported by Rear Admiral Richard E. Berry (COMCRUDESGRU Eight) (Commander Task Group 20), embarked on the aircraft carrier USS Independence. Commanding Officer, USS Guam (Task Force 124) was assigned the mission of seizing the Pearls Airport and the port of Grenville, and of neutralizing any opposing forces in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spector_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spector-49">[49]</a></sup> Simultaneously, several SOF elements and Army Rangers (Task Force 123) would secure points at the southern end of the island, including the nearly completed jet airfield under construction near Point Salines. Elements of the 82d Airborne Division (Task Force 121) were designated as follow-on forces and were tasked to follow and assume the security at Point Salines, once seized by Task Force 123. –Task Group 20.5, a carrier battle group built around <a href="/wiki/USS_Independence_(CV-62)" title="USS Independence (CV-62)">USS <i>Independence</i> (CV-62)</a>, and Air Force elements would support the ground forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spector_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spector-49">[49]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/136th_Airlift_Wing" title="136th Airlift Wing">136th Tactical Airlift Wing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas_Air_National_Guard" title="Texas Air National Guard">Texas Air National Guard</a> – provided <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a> combat airlift support, cargo and supplies</li>
<li>Various <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a> tactical fighter wings and squadrons – provided <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair_II" title="A-7 Corsair II">A-7D Corsair II</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground-attack_aircraft" title="Ground-attack aircraft">ground-attack aircraft</a> for <a href="/wiki/Close_air_support" title="Close air support">close air support</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/23rd_Wing" title="23rd Wing">23rd Tactical Fighter Wing</a> – provided close air support for allied forces with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-10_Warthog" title="A-10 Warthog">A-10 Warthogs</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=26th_Air_Defense_Squadron_NORAD&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="26th Air Defense Squadron NORAD (page does not exist)">26th Air Defense Squadron NORAD</a> – provided air support for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15 Eagles</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/33rd_Fighter_Wing" title="33rd Fighter Wing">33rd Tactical Fighter Wing</a> – provided air superiority cover for allied forces with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15 Eagles</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/437th_Airlift_Wing" title="437th Airlift Wing">437th Military Airlift Wing</a> – provided airlift support with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-141_Starlifter" title="C-141 Starlifter">C-141 Starlifters</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/16th_Special_Operations_Wing" title="16th Special Operations Wing">16th Special Operations Wing</a> – flew <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_AC-130" title="Lockheed AC-130">AC-130H Spectre</a> gunships</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/317th_Airlift_Group" title="317th Airlift Group">317th Military Airlift Wing</a> – provided airlift support with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pope_AFB" title="Pope AFB">Pope AFB</a> / <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bragg" title="Fort Bragg">Fort Bragg</a>, NC complex to Grenada</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/63d_Military_Airlift_Wing" title="63d Military Airlift Wing">63d Military Airlift Wing</a> – Provided airlift support with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-141_Starlifter" title="C-141 Starlifter">C-141 Starlifteraircraft</a> in the air landing of Airborne troops, 63rd Security Police Squadron provided airfield security support – (Norton AFB CA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/443d_Airlift_Wing" title="443d Airlift Wing">443rd Military Airlift Wing</a>, 443rd Security Police Squadron (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altus_AFB" title="Altus AFB">Altus AFB</a>, OK) – provided a 44-man Airbase Ground Defense flight (Oct–Nov 1983)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19th_Air_Refueling_Wing" title="19th Air Refueling Wing">19th Air Refueling Wing</a> – provided aerial refueling support for all other aircraft</li>
<li>507th Tactical Air Control Wing (elements of the 21st TASS at Shaw AFB, SC and Detachment 1, Fort Bragg, NC) – provided Tactical Air Control Parties (<a href="/wiki/Tactical_Air_Control_Party" title="Tactical Air Control Party">TACPs</a>) in support of the <a href="/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division" title="82nd Airborne Division">82nd Airborne Division</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/552nd_Air_Control_Wing" title="552nd Air Control Wing">552nd Air Control Wing</a>, providing air control support with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-3_Sentry" title="E-3 Sentry">E-3 Sentry</a> <a href="/wiki/Airborne_early_warning_and_control" title="Airborne early warning and control">AWACS</a> aircraft</li>
<li>62nd Security Police Group (Provisional) Multi Squadron Law Enforcement &amp; Security Forces – Prisoner detaining and transport attached to 82nd Airborne</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/60th_Air_Mobility_Wing" title="60th Air Mobility Wing">60th Military Airlift Wing</a>'s 60th Security Police Squadron (Travis AFB, CA) provided airfield security in Grenada as well as Barbados.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Navy">U.S. Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Invasion_of_Grenada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: U.S. Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
