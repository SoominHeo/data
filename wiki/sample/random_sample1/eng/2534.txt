<p><b>Joseph Graham</b> "<b>Gray</b>" <b>Davis, Jr.</b> (born December 26, 1942) is an American politician who served as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_California" title="List of Governors of California">37th</a> <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_California" title="Governor of California">Governor of California</a> from 1999 to 2003. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>, only months into his second term, in <a href="/wiki/California_gubernatorial_recall_election" title="California gubernatorial recall election">2003 Davis was recalled</a> and removed from office, the second state governor successfully recalled in U.S. history. Prior to serving as governor, Davis was chief of staff to Governor <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Jerry Brown</a> (1975–81), a <a href="/wiki/California_State_Assembly" title="California State Assembly">California State Assemblyman</a> (1983–87), <a href="/wiki/California_State_Controller" title="California State Controller">California State Controller</a> (1987–95) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Lieutenant_Governors_of_California" title="List of Lieutenant Governors of California">44th</a> <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_California" title="Lieutenant Governor of California">Lieutenant Governor of California</a> (1995–99). Davis holds a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> in <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> from <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">J.D.</a> from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Law_School" title="Columbia Law School">Columbia Law School</a>. He was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Star_Medal" title="Bronze Star Medal">Bronze Star</a> for his service as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(OF-2)" title="Captain (OF-2)">Captain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.</p>
<p>During his time as governor, Davis made education his top priority and California spent eight billion dollars more than was required under <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_98_(1988)" title="California Proposition 98 (1988)">Proposition 98</a> during his first term. Under Davis, California standardized test scores increased for five straight years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup> Davis signed the nation's first state law requiring automakers to limit auto emissions. Davis supported laws to ban <a href="/wiki/Assault_weapon" title="Assault weapon">assault weapons</a> and he is also credited with improving relations between California and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Davis began his tenure as governor with strong approval ratings, but those ratings declined as voters blamed Davis for the <a href="/wiki/California_electricity_crisis" title="California electricity crisis">California electricity crisis</a> and the California budget crisis that followed the bursting of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>. Voters were also alienated by Davis's record-breaking fundraising efforts and <a href="/wiki/Negative_campaigning" title="Negative campaigning">negative campaigning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 7, 2003, Davis was recalled in the second of three such elections in U.S. history, which include the removal of <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Frazier" title="Lynn Frazier">Lynn Frazier</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota_gubernatorial_recall_election" title="North Dakota gubernatorial recall election">1921</a> and the unsuccessful recall of <a href="/wiki/Scott_Walker_(politician)" title="Scott Walker (politician)">Scott Walker</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_gubernatorial_recall_election" title="Wisconsin gubernatorial recall election">2012</a>. He was succeeded in office on November 17, 2003 by actor <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a>, who won the recall replacement race. Davis spent 1,778 days as governor and signed 5,132 bills out of 6,244, vetoing 1,112 bills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup> Since being recalled, Davis has worked as a lecturer at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA_School_of_Public_Affairs" title="UCLA School of Public Affairs">UCLA School of Public Affairs</a>, as an attorney at Loeb &amp; Loeb and sat on the Board of Directors of the animation company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DiC_Entertainment" title="DiC Entertainment">DiC Entertainment</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_political_career">Early life and political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Davis was born in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronx" title="Bronx">Bronx</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the son of Doris (Meyer) Morell and Joseph G. Davis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Davis moved to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> with his family as a child in 1954. He was the first of the family's five children: three boys and two girls. He was raised a Roman Catholic. Davis and his family were one of the millions of Americans to migrate to the southwest and California as part of the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_belt" title="Sun belt">sun belt</a> migration.</p>
<p>His diverse educational experiences at public, private and Catholic schools allowed him an opportunity to compare all three systems as a lawmaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup> Davis graduated from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Hollywood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="North Hollywood, Los Angeles, California">North Hollywood</a> <a href="/wiki/Military_academy" title="Military academy">military academy</a>, the Harvard School for Boys (now part of <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Westlake_School" title="Harvard-Westlake School">Harvard-Westlake School</a>). Davis' family was upper middle class and was led by his demanding mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup> Davis was nicknamed Gray by his mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> His father, Joseph Graham Davis, Sr., an advertising manager at Time Inc. and an alcoholic, was the son of businessman <a href="/wiki/William_Rhodes_Davis" title="William Rhodes Davis">William Rhodes Davis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>His strong academic accomplishments earned him acceptance to <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup> He played on the Stanford golf team with a two handicap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bright_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bright-10">[10]</a></sup> After Davis entered <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, his father left the family, forcing Davis to join the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Officers%27_Training_Corps" title="Reserve Officers' Training Corps">ROTC</a> to stay in school. The deal included a promise to enter the regular Army after completing his education. He earned a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> in <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> at Stanford, where he was admitted to the <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Psi" title="Zeta Psi">Zeta Psi</a> fraternity, graduating in 1964 with distinction. He then returned to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to attend <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Law_School" title="Columbia Law School">Columbia Law School</a> where he won the Moot Court award. During law school Davis had a romantic encounter with actress <a href="/wiki/Cybill_Shepherd" title="Cybill Shepherd">Cybill Shepherd</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cybill_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cybill-11">[11]</a></sup> He graduated from Columbia in 1967 and then clerked at the law firm of Beekman &amp; Bogue in New York City.</p>
<p>After completing the program in 1967 he entered active duty in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, serving in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> during its height until 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shining_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shining-2">[2]</a></sup> Davis saw time on the battlefield during his time in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup> Davis returned home as a captain with a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Star_Medal" title="Bronze Star Medal">Bronze Star Medal</a> for meritorious service. Friends who knew him at the time said Davis—like many war veterans—came back a changed man, interested in politics and more intense, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Bee" title="Sacramento Bee">Sacramento Bee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shining_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shining-2">[2]</a></sup> He returned from Vietnam more "serious and directed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup> Davis was surprised to discover that many of those serving in Vietnam were <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">Latinos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African Americans</a> and southern whites with very few from schools like Stanford and Columbia; Davis believed that the burden of the war should be felt equally and he resolved early on to go about changing America so that would change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interview_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Interview-12">[12]</a></sup> Davis is a life member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">American Legion</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Veterans_of_Foreign_Wars" title="Veterans of Foreign Wars">Veterans of Foreign Wars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bio_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bio-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis volunteered for the campaign of <a href="/wiki/John_V._Tunney" title="John V. Tunney">John V. Tunney</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interview_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Interview-12">[12]</a></sup> He started a statewide neighborhood crime watch program while serving as chairman of the California Council on Criminal Justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shining_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shining-2">[2]</a></sup> His initial political experience included working to help <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bradley_(American_politician)" title="Tom Bradley (American politician)">Tom Bradley</a> win election as Los Angeles' first black mayor in 1973. The historical significance of Bradley's victory further inspired Davis to pursue a career in politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interview_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Interview-12">[12]</a></sup> Davis ran for <a href="/wiki/California_State_Treasurer" title="California State Treasurer">State Treasurer</a> in 1974 but lost when the more popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesse_Unruh" title="Jesse Unruh">Jesse Unruh</a> filed to run on the deadline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interview_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Interview-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis returned to California and entered politics, serving as Executive Secretary and Chief of Staff to Governor <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Edmund G. "Jerry" Brown Jr.</a> from 1975 to 1981. Davis was not as liberal as Brown and some said he offset Brown's style by projecting a more intense, controlled personality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shining_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Shining-2">[2]</a></sup> While Brown was campaigning for <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> in 1980, Davis ran California in Brown's absence though Davis would later claim that "we always did what he thought Brown would have done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>He met his wife-to-be, Sharon Ryer, while on an airplane tending to official business in 1978. Ryer, a flight attendant for <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Southwest_Airlines" title="Pacific Southwest Airlines">Pacific Southwest Airlines</a>, was annoyed when Davis held up the departure of the flight from Sacramento to Los Angeles. Davis apologized and asked her out and they later married in 1983, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_Supreme_Court" title="California Supreme Court">California Supreme Court</a> Justice <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bird" title="Rose Bird">Rose Bird</a> officiating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was elected to the office of <a href="/wiki/California_State_Assembly" title="California State Assembly">Assemblyman</a> from the 43rd district, representing parts of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County</a> including <a href="/wiki/West_Los_Angeles" title="West Los Angeles">West Los Angeles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills" title="Beverly Hills">Beverly Hills</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> from 1983–87. Davis championed a popular campaign to help find missing children by placing their pictures on milk cartons and grocery bags.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shining_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Shining-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_to_Governorship">Prior to Governorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prior to Governorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_Controller">State Controller</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: State Controller">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1986, Davis ran against six other contenders in his race for <a href="/wiki/California_State_Controller" title="California State Controller">State Controller</a>; several of those candidates, including Democrat <a href="/wiki/John_Garamendi" title="John Garamendi">John Garamendi</a> and Republican <a href="/wiki/Bill_Campbell_(California_politician)" title="Bill Campbell (California politician)">Bill Campbell</a>, were arguably better known at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup> Davis served as State Controller for eight years until 1995. As California's chief fiscal officer, he saved taxpayers more than half a billion dollars by cracking down on Medi-Cal fraud, rooting out government waste and inefficiency and exposing the misuse of public funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smart-18">[18]</a></sup> He was the first controller to withhold paychecks from all state elected officials, including himself, until the governor and the Legislature passed an overdue budget. He also found and returned more than $1.8 billion in unclaimed property to California citizens, including forgotten bank accounts, insurance settlements and stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smart-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992_campaign_for_Senate">1992 campaign for Senate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1992 campaign for Senate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Davis ran against <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> mayor <a href="/wiki/Dianne_Feinstein" title="Dianne Feinstein">Dianne Feinstein</a> for the Democratic nomination for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> in the 1992 special election to fill the Senate seat vacated by <a href="/wiki/Pete_Wilson" title="Pete Wilson">Pete Wilson</a> who was elected <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_California" title="Governor of California">Governor of California</a> in 1990. The race is often cited as an example of Davis' history of negative campaign tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGOV_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-LGOV-19">[19]</a></sup> The Davis campaign featured an ad that compared Feinstein to the incarcerated hotelier <a href="/wiki/Leona_Helmsley" title="Leona Helmsley">Leona Helmsley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup> Some experts consider that ad to be the most negative in state history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiFi_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiFi-20">[20]</a></sup> The ad backfired with Davis losing to Feinstein by a significant margin for the nomination although this loss did not stop Davis from using negative campaign ads in the future, including in his race for lieutenant governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGOV_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-LGOV-19">[19]</a></sup> Davis blamed his campaign managers for the defeat and vowed not to let major decisions in future campaigns be decided by his campaign staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2003, when Feinstein urged voters to vote no during the recall election, she was constantly reminded through questions, video and the media about the 1992 primary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiFi_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-DiFi-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lieutenant_governor">Lieutenant governor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lieutenant governor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Many Democrats came to believe that Davis' political career was over after his defeat in his run for the Senate, but Davis created a new campaign team. He won a landslide victory in his race for <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_California" title="Lieutenant Governor of California">Lieutenant Governor</a> in 1994, receiving more votes than any other Democratic candidate in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smart-18">[18]</a></sup> Davis ran as a moderate candidate against Republican Cathie Wright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Davis used ads to depict Wright as a Republican who was too conservative for California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGOV_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-LGOV-19">[19]</a></sup> Davis had a large advantage in campaign funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGOV_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-LGOV-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>As lieutenant governor until 1999, Gray Davis focused on efforts on the California economy and worked to encourage new industries to locate and expand in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smart-18">[18]</a></sup> He also worked to keep college education affordable for California's middle-class families and oversaw the largest student-fee reduction in California history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smart-18">[18]</a></sup> As the state's second-highest officeholder, he served as President of the State Senate, Chair of the Commission for Economic Development, Chair of the State Lands Commission, Regent of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> and Trustee of the <a href="/wiki/California_State_University" title="California State University">California State University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Smart-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1998_campaign_for_Governor">1998 campaign for Governor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1998 campaign for Governor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/California_gubernatorial_election,_1998" title="California gubernatorial election, 1998">California gubernatorial election, 1998</a></div>
<p>In the June primary election, Davis surprised political observers by handily defeating two better funded Democratic opponents: multimillionaire airline executive <a href="/wiki/Al_Checchi" title="Al Checchi">Al Checchi</a> and Congresswoman <a href="/wiki/Jane_Harman" title="Jane Harman">Jane Harman</a>, wife of multimillionaire <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Harman" title="Sidney Harman">Sidney Harman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primary_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Primary-22">[22]</a></sup> Davis' campaign slogan during the primary was "Experience Money Can't Buy." Early primary polls showed Davis in third for the Democratic nomination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primary_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Primary-22">[22]</a></sup> Davis surprised many political insiders with his landslide come-from-behind victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circus_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Circus-23">[23]</a></sup> Davis even finished ahead of the unopposed Republican nominee in California's first <a href="/wiki/Blanket_primary" title="Blanket primary">blanket</a> gubernatorial primary.</p>
<p>Davis won the 1998 general election for governor with 57.9% of the vote, defeating Republican <a href="/wiki/Dan_Lungren" title="Dan Lungren">Dan Lungren</a> who had 38.4%. Davis aimed to portray himself as a moderate centrist Democrat and to label Lungren a Republican too conservative for California and out of touch with its views on issues like guns and abortion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Key_states_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Key_states-24">[24]</a></sup> After his victory, Davis declared that he would work to end the "divisive politics" of his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Pete_Wilson" title="Pete Wilson">Pete Wilson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Key_states_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Key_states-24">[24]</a></sup> In his campaign, Davis emphasized the need to improve California's public schools, which voters had cited as their top concern in this election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Key_states_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Key_states-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_term">First term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_start_and_education">Popular start and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Popular start and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, Davis was elected the Golden State's first Democratic governor in 16 years. <i>The San Jose Mercury News</i> called him "perhaps the best-trained governor-in-waiting California has ever produced."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shining_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Shining-2">[2]</a></sup> Davis was strongly viewed as a possible Democratic candidate for <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In March 1999, Davis enjoyed a 58% approval rating and just 12% disapproval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> His numbers peaked in February 2000 with 62% approval and 20% disapproval, coinciding with the peak of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com boom</a> in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bright_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-bright-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Davis held his strong poll numbers into January 2001.</p>
<p>Davis' first official act as governor was to call a special session of the state legislature to address his plan for all California children to be able to read by age 9.</p>

<p>Davis used California's growing budget surplus to increase education spending. He signed legislation that provided for a new statewide accountability program and for the Academic Performance Index and supported the high school exit exam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup> He signed legislation that authorized the largest expansion of the Cal Grant program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup> Under the Davis administration, California began recognizing students for outstanding academic achievement in math and sciences on the new <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Exams" title="Golden State Exams">Golden State Exams</a>. Davis' Governors Scholarship program provided $1,000 scholarships to those students who scored in the top 1% in two subject areas on the state's annual statewide standardized test.</p>

<p>Davis signed into law legislation that began the Eligibility in the Local Context (ELC) program that guaranteed admission to a <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> institution to students that finished in the top 4% of their high school class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-First-28">[28]</a></sup> Public schools received $8 billion over the minimum required by <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_98_(1988)" title="California Proposition 98 (1988)">Proposition 98</a> during Davis' first term. Davis increased spending on recruiting more and better-qualified teachers. He campaigned to lower the approval threshold for local school bonds from two-thirds to 55 percent in a statewide proposition that passed. Davis earmarked $3 billion over four years for new textbooks and, between 1999 and 2004, increased state per-pupil spending from $5,756 to $6,922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, Gov. Gray Davis signed Senate Bill 19, which establishes nutritional standards for food at elementary schools and bans the sale of carbonated beverages in elementary and middle schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Child_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Child-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another early act of Davis's was the reversal of his predecessor Republican Governor Pete Wilson's alteration of California's eight-hour overtime pay rule for wage earners.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_partnerships">Domestic partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Domestic partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Davis recognized the domestic partnerships registry in 1999 and, in 2001, gave same-sex partners a few of the rights enjoyed by opposite-sex spouses such as making health care decisions for an incapacitated partner, acting as a conservator and inheriting property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup> He also signed a bill to prevent disqualification from a jury based on sexual orientation. He also signed a bill allowing employees to use family leave to care for a domestic partner, though he didn't make good on a campaign promise to convene a task force on civil unions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guns_and_public_safety">Guns and public safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Guns and public safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He signed laws in 1999 banning assault weapons by characteristic rather than brand name, as well as limiting handgun purchases to one a month, requiring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigger_lock" title="Trigger lock">trigger locks</a> with all sales of new firearms and reducing the sale of cheap handguns. Davis' ban included a ban on .50 caliber firearms and so-called "Saturday Night Specials."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-First-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2001, Davis signed a bill requiring gun buyers to pass a safety test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A supporter of the death penalty and tougher sentencing laws, Davis blocked nearly all parole recommendations by the parole board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_Mexico">Relations with Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Relations with Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:125px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voa_pool_05sep01_mexico_vincente_fox.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="123" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bb/Voa_pool_05sep01_mexico_vincente_fox.jpg" width="123"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Davis sought to improve relations with Mexican President <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Fox" title="Vicente Fox">Vicente Fox</a>.</div>

</div>
<p>Early in 1999, Davis sought to improve relations with <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Davis believed that California under <a href="/wiki/Pete_Wilson" title="Pete Wilson">Pete Wilson</a> had left millions of dollars of potential trade revenues "on the table."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mexico_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mexico-31">[31]</a></sup> Davis said he wanted California to have relations with Mexico that were more like <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> under then-Governor <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mexico_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mexico-31">[31]</a></sup> Controversy over the California-Mexico border and <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_187" title="California Proposition 187">California Proposition 187</a> had strained the relationship between the two parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Davis met with Mexican President <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Zedillo" title="Ernesto Zedillo">Ernesto Zedillo</a> to improve relations with California's southern neighbor and major trading partner within Davis' first 30 days in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup> Davis later met with President <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Fox" title="Vicente Fox">Vicente Fox</a> and participated in his inauguration. The Governor met with Mexican presidents eight times. Under the Davis administration, California and <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a> signed a "Memorandum of Understanding" expanding cooperation in several policy areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup> Under Davis, Mexico became California's leading export market for the first time in history and California's trade with Mexico surpasses all of Mexico's trade with Latin America, Europe and Asia combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup> Because of the growth in the California economy, Davis opened and expanded trade offices around the world, including in Mexico. But most of these offices were eliminated in the 2003 California budget due to difficult fiscal times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health.2C_environment.2C_business_and_transportation">Health, environment, business and transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Health, environment, business and transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Davis significantly expanded the number of low-income children with state-subsidized health coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup> He signed laws to allow patients to get a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_opinion_(medicine)" title="Second opinion (medicine)">second opinion</a> if their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMO" title="HMO">HMO</a> denies treatment and, in limited cases, the right to sue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-First-28">[28]</a></sup> Davis signed legislation that provided HMO patients a bill of rights, including help-line to resolve disputes and independent medical review of claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup> Under Davis, staff-to-patient ratios in nursing homes improved. However, Davis reneged on a campaign promise to expand low-cost healthcare to parents of needy children due to budget constraints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis allowed non-disabled low-income people with <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> to be treated under <a href="/wiki/Medi-Cal" title="Medi-Cal">Medi-Cal</a>. He signed a law allowing people participating in needle exchange programs to be immune from criminal prosecution. He also increased state spending on AIDS prevention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under Governor Davis, California's anti-<a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> campaign became one of the largest and most effective in the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/R._J._Reynolds" title="R. J. Reynolds">R. J. Reynolds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorillard" title="Lorillard">Lorillard</a> Tobacco sued over California's antismoking campaign but their lawsuit was dismissed in July 2003. Davis also authorized a new hard-hitting anti-smoking ad that graphically depicts the damage caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondhand_smoke" title="Secondhand smoke">secondhand smoke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2002, Governor Davis signed bills to ensure age verification was obtained for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cigarettes" title="Cigarettes">cigarettes</a> and other tobacco products sold over the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> or through the mail, ensured that all state taxes are being fully paid on tobacco purchases and increased the penalty for possessing or purchasing untaxed cigarettes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-la_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-la-34">[34]</a></sup> He also signed legislation to expand smoke-free zones around public buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-la_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-la-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis approved legislation creating a telemarketing do-not-call list in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-First-28">[28]</a></sup> Under Davis, benefits for injured and unemployed workers increased. The minimum wage increased by $1 to $6.75.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup> Davis backed higher research and development tax credits. He pushed for elimination of the minimum franchise tax paid by new businesses during the first two years of operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-6"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Davis's record is generally considered pro-environmental due to increases in spending for land acquisition, maintenance of the state's park system, signing legislation that attempts to cut greenhouse gas emissions by having automakers produce more efficient vehicles, cutting fees to state parks and opposing <a href="/wiki/Offshore_drilling" title="Offshore drilling">offshore drilling</a>, he was criticized for not backing tougher restrictions on timber companies as some environmentalists desired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-7"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup> Under the Davis administration, California purchased 10,000 acres (40 km<sup>2</sup>) for urban parks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis signed the first state law in the US in July 2002 to require automakers to limit auto emissions. The law required the California Air Resources Board to obtain the "maximum feasible" cuts in greenhouse gases emitted by all non-commercial vehicles in 2009 and beyond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Automakers claimed the law would lead smaller and more expensive cars sold in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 25, 1999, Davis issued an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">executive order</a> calling for the removal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTBE" title="MTBE">MTBE</a> (a toxic gasoline additive) from gasoline sold in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2001, in order for gas prices to remain reasonable in California while removing MTBE, Davis asked President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> to order the EPA to grant California a waiver on the federal minimum oxygen requirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Without a waiver, California would have to import a much larger amount of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> per year and gas prices were projected to increase drastically. Bush did not grant the waiver and in 2002, Davis issued an executive order reversing his earlier executive order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis's actions when it came to regulating business suggested that Davis was a more moderate governor. He worked to kill a comprehensive bill opposed by banks and insurance companies to protect consumers' personal financial information. "What you saw in the campaign was what you got," said UC Berkeley professor Bruce Cain. "He's tried to negotiate a course between the different interest groups and keep Democrats on a more centrist, business-oriented track".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-8"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis approved $5.3 billion over five years for more than 150 transit and highway projects. One of those projects was construction on the new eastern section of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco%E2%80%93Oakland_Bay_Bridge" title="San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge">Bay Bridge</a>. During 1999 and 2000, California spent millions on onetime projects like buying new rail cars and track improvements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-9"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declining_popularity">Declining popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Declining popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2001, in the middle of the <a href="/wiki/California_electricity_crisis" title="California electricity crisis">California electricity crisis</a>, his numbers declined to 42% approval and 49% disapproval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> By December 2001, Davis' approval ratings spiked up to 51%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> His numbers declined back to the May 2001 level and remained about the same over the next year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In April 2003, Davis had a 24% approval rating and 65% disapproval rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Voters cited disapproval of the state's record $34.6 billion budget shortfall, growing unemployment and dubious campaign contributor connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis had tried to maintain a middle-of-the-road approach, but ultimately alienated many of the state's liberals who viewed him as too conservative and many conservatives who viewed him as too liberal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Many were upset that in trying to balance the budget, Davis cut spending for schools while increasing spending for prisons. Some critics attributed the proposal to the <a href="/wiki/California_Correctional_Peace_Officers_Association" title="California Correctional Peace Officers Association">California Correctional Peace Officers Associations</a> donations to Davis' re-election campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-End2-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Negative sentiment was expressed on <a href="/wiki/Talk_radio" title="Talk radio">talk radio</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/John_and_Ken" title="John and Ken">John and Ken</a> Show</i> in Los Angeles called Davis <i><a href="/wiki/Gumby" title="Gumby">Gumby</a></i> in response to his changing positions on issues, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mr._KABC" title="Mr. KABC">Mr. KABC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Al_Rantel" title="Al Rantel">Al Rantel</a> (also in Los Angeles) coined the term <i>Governor Lowbeam</i> as a reference to his alleged mishandling of the electricity crisis and his term as Chief of Staff for Jerry Brown, who was often mocked as <i>Governor Moonbeam</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ken-48">[48]</a></sup> Many talk radio programs played a role in collecting signatures to force a recall election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ken-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="California_electricity_crisis">California electricity crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: California electricity crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/California_electricity_crisis" title="California electricity crisis">California electricity crisis</a></div>
<p>Soon after taking office, Davis was able to fast-track the first power plant construction in twelve years in April 1999, although the plant did not come on line before the electricity crisis.</p>
<p>According to the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">Federal Energy Regulatory Commission</a>'s investigation and report, numerous energy trading companies, many based in Texas, such as <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron Corporation</a>, illegally restricted their supply to the point where the spikes in power usage would cause blackouts. <a href="/wiki/Rolling_blackout" title="Rolling blackout">Rolling blackouts</a> affecting 97,000 customers hit the San Francisco Bay area on June 14, 2000 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Gas_%26_Electric_Company" title="San Diego Gas &amp; Electric Company">San Diego Gas &amp; Electric Company</a> filed a complaint alleging <a href="/wiki/Market_manipulation" title="Market manipulation">market manipulation</a> by some energy producers in August 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Highest_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Highest-49">[49]</a></sup> On December 7, 2000, suffering from low supply and idled power plants, the <a href="/wiki/California_Independent_System_Operator" title="California Independent System Operator">California Independent System Operator</a> (CAISO), which manages the California power grid, declared the first statewide Stage 3 power alert, meaning power reserves were below 3 percent. Rolling blackouts were avoided when the state halted two large state and federal water pumps to conserve electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Highest_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Highest-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs,_California.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs%2C_California.jpg/220px-Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs%2C_California.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs%2C_California.jpg/330px-Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs%2C_California.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs%2C_California.jpg/440px-Windmill_Field_outside_Palm_Springs%2C_California.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On January 17, 2001, Davis declared a state of emergency in response to the electricity crisis. Speculators, led by <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron Corporation</a>, were collectively making large profits while the state teetered on the edge for weeks and finally suffered rolling blackouts on January 17 and 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Highest_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-Highest-49">[49]</a></sup> Davis stepped in to buy power at highly unfavorable terms on the open market, since the California power companies were technically <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankrupt</a> and had no buying power. California agreed to pay $43 billion for power over the next 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Highest_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-Highest-49">[49]</a></sup> Newspaper publishers sued Davis to force him to make public the details of the energy deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chain_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chain-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the electricity crisis, the Davis administration implemented a power conservation program that included television ads and financial incentives to reduce energy consumption. These efforts, the fear of rolling blackouts and the increased cost of electricity resulted in a 14.1% reduction in electricity usage from June 2000 to June 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Highest_49-4"><a href="#cite_note-Highest-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gray Davis critics often charge that he did not respond properly to the crisis, while his defenders attribute the crisis solely to the <a href="/wiki/Accounting_scandals" title="Accounting scandals">corporate accounting scandals</a> and say that Davis did all he could. Some critics on the left, such as <a href="/wiki/Arianna_Huffington" title="Arianna Huffington">Arianna Huffington</a>, alleged that Davis was lulled to inaction by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campaign_contribution" title="Campaign contribution">campaign contributions</a> from energy producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Some of Davis' energy advisers were formerly employed by the same energy speculators who made millions from the crisis. In addition, the Democratic-controlled legislature would sometimes push Davis to act decisively by taking over power plants which were known to have been gamed and place them back under control of the utilities. Some conservatives argued that Davis signed overpriced energy contracts, employed incompetent negotiators and refused to allow prices to rise for residences statewide much like they did in San Diego, which they argue could have given Davis more leverage against the energy traders and encouraged more conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The electricity crisis is considered one of the major factors that led to Davis' recall.</p>

<p>In a speech at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> on August 19, 2003, Davis apologized for being slow to act during the energy crisis, but then forcefully attacked the <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>-based energy suppliers: "I inherited the energy deregulation scheme which put us all at the mercy of the big energy producers. We got no help from the Federal government. In fact, when I was fighting <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a> and the other energy companies, these same companies were sitting down with <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> Cheney to draft a national energy strategy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the Enron verdicts were rendered years later, convicting Enron and other companies of market manipulation, Davis responded with the following quote:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">

<p>On November 13, 2003, shortly before leaving office, Davis officially brought the energy crisis to an end by issuing a proclamation ending the state of emergency he declared on January 17, 2001. The state of emergency allowed the state to buy electricity for the financially strapped utility companies. The emergency authority allowed Davis to order the <a href="/wiki/California_Energy_Commission" title="California Energy Commission">California Energy Commission</a> to streamline the application process for new power plants. During that time, California issued licenses to 38 new power plants, amounting to 14,365 megawatts of electricity production when completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accomp._3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Accomp.-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> published an article that credited Davis' signing of the long term projects for preventing future blackouts and providing California a cheap supply of energy with the increasing costs of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2003, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission's long-awaited report on the so-called "energy crisis" was released. That report mostly vindicated Davis, laying the blame for the energy disruption and raiding of California's treasury on some 25 energy trading companies, most of which were based in Texas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_crisis">Budget crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Budget crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the economic boom years of the Davis administration, the California budget expanded to cover Davis' new programs. California's low national K-12 education rankings and Davis' campaign pledge to help education, along with the large majority that elected Davis to his first term and his early popularity, suggest that a majority of Californians supported increases in education spending during the early part of his first term when California was in budget surplus. Polls also showed that increased spending in education was supported by the California voters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Under the Davis administration, taxes were cut by over $5.1 billion that included a $3.5 billion cut in sales tax and a reduction in the vehicle licensing fees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-First-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-sales-56">[56]</a></sup> The cut in sales taxes was mandated due to a 1991 law that required sales taxes to be reduced a quarter percent when budget reserves exceed 4 percent of the state general fund for two straight fiscal years which they did in 1999 and 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-sales-56">[56]</a></sup> Davis also vetoed $5.1 billion in appropriations during that span.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-sales-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<p>While California's economy was expanding, California was producing record budget surpluses under Davis even after his tax cuts and new spending. According to the California Department of Finance, California had a 10% surplus at the end of 1999 and California was projected to have a 4% surplus at the end fiscal year 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-sales-56">[56]</a></sup> These surplus monies were left in the treasury. Davis claimed to be cautious with state finances.</p>

<p>Then, the dot-com boom that had been fueling California's record tax revenues burst unexpectedly because of the large number of high tech firms in California and California's dependence on state income taxes. When the dot-com boom turned to bust, state revenues fell while ongoing spending commitments created deficits. Restoring the licensing fees to pre tax cut levels to close the budget gap and stabilize the state's credit rating became unpopular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bright_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-bright-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The beginning shortfall for the 2002-2003 state budget was $23.6 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Davis announced that the 2003-2004 budget shortfall would be $34.6 billion while the Legislative Analyst projected a $21.1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_reelection">2002 reelection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2002 reelection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/California_gubernatorial_election,_2002" title="California gubernatorial election, 2002">California gubernatorial election, 2002</a></div>
<p>Davis began fundraising for his 2002 reelection campaign early in his governorship. Davis raised $13.2 million in 1999 and $14.2 million in 2000, both unprecedented sums at the time so early in an elected term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gladstone_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gladstone-60">[60]</a></sup> Davis' 1999 and 2000 contributions included contributions from Pacific Gas &amp; Electric and Edison International.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gladstone_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gladstone-60">[60]</a></sup> Davis also received large contributions from labor groups, environmental groups and individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gladstone_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gladstone-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gray_Davis_Files.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="244" data-file-width="325" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/Gray_Davis_Files.JPG/200px-Gray_Davis_Files.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/Gray_Davis_Files.JPG/300px-Gray_Davis_Files.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/8c/Gray_Davis_Files.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gray_Davis_Files.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Arnoldwatch.org, whose home page is partially depicted here, was one group that was critical of Davis' fundraising efforts.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Davis's fundraising efforts attracted much attention. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_Berkeley" title="University of California Berkeley">University of California Berkeley</a>'s Institute of Government Studies claimed that Davis' fundraising skills were "second to none in the political arena" while Senator <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> called Davis' 2001 goal of $26 million "disgraceful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-igs.berkeley.edu_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-igs.berkeley.edu-61">[61]</a></sup> One article in the San Francisco Chronicle claimed that Davis was raising $34,000 a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-igs.berkeley.edu_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-igs.berkeley.edu-61">[61]</a></sup> Although Davis' fundraising pace was criticized by his many detractors, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a> would later collect contributions at a quicker rate during the early years of his governorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Now Arnoldwatch.org, a project of the Foundation for Taxpayer and Consumer Rights, which is a nonpartisan organization that is critical of both Democrats and Republicans, called Davis a "pay to play" politician and a "sellout".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2002 election campaign, Davis took the unusual step of taking out campaign ads during the Republican <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primaries</a> against Los Angeles mayor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Riordan" title="Richard Riordan">Richard Riordan</a>. Davis claimed that Riordan had attacked his record and that his campaign was defending his record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Polls showed that, as a moderate, Riordan would be a more formidable challenger in the general election than a conservative candidate. Polls even showed that Riordan would defeat Davis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Davis attacked Riordian with negative ads in the primary. The ads questioned Riordan's pro-choice stance by questioning Riordan's support of pro-life politicians and judges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The ads pointed out Riordan's position of wanting a moratorium on the death penalty as being to the left of Gray Davis, who strongly supported it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis's negative ads against Riordan and a variety of other equally important factors explained on the 2002 election page, led to Riordan's defeat in the Republican primary by the more staunchly conservative candidate <a href="/wiki/Bill_Simon_(politician)" title="Bill Simon (politician)">Bill Simon</a>. In the first 10 weeks of 2002, Davis spent $10 million on ads: $3 million on positive ads boasting of his record, $7 million on negative ads against Riordan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis was re-elected in the November 2002 general election following a long and bitter campaign against Simon, marked by accusations of ethical lapses on both sides and widespread voter apathy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Simon was also hurt by a financial fraud scandal that tarnished Simon's reputation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Davis' campaign touted California's improving test scores, environmental protection, health insurance coverage for children and lower prescription drug costs for seniors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Davis' campaign featured several negative ads that highlighted Simon's financial fraud scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heat_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heat-74">[74]</a></sup> The 2002 gubernatorial race was the most expensive in California state history with over $100 million spent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Davis' campaign was better financed; Davis had over $26 million in campaign reserves more than Simon in August 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heat_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heat-74">[74]</a></sup> Davis gained re-election with 47.4% of the vote to Simon's 42.4%. However, the Simon-Davis race led in the lowest turnout percentage in modern gubernatorial history, allowing a lower than normal amount of signatures required for a recall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Davis won the election, but the majority of voters disliked Davis and did not approve of his job performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_challenges">Other challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While polls attributed Davis' declining popularity to the energy and budget problems, some newspaper articles and commentators have identified other issues that limited his effectiveness and political appeal. Davis, a moderate, had some disagreements with the more liberal Democratic-controlled Legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-10"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup> Democrat <a href="/wiki/John_L._Burton" title="John L. Burton">John L. Burton</a>, the leader of the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Senate" title="California State Senate">California State Senate</a>, was Davis' chief antagonist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dark_29-11"><a href="#cite_note-Dark-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2003, Republican leader <a href="/wiki/Jim_Brulte" title="Jim Brulte">Jim Brulte</a> told The Los Angeles Times that Davis lacked the basics of political collegiality to pull him through hard times. "I never felt I got to know him ... I always felt a little sorry for him".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Child_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Child-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis' moderate record made it difficult for him to appeal to the core constituency of the Democratic Party. During the recall, Davis failed to gain the full support he needed from his more liberal Democratic base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup> He had the reputation of being beholden to supporters yet unable to satisfy them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Child_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Child-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis' leadership and compromise-building skills have also been questioned. Many of the challenges that California faced during his years required a strong force of personality to forge compromise but Davis lacked such skill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Child_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Child-30">[30]</a></sup> He was also hurt by redistricting in 2000 that made most districts safe for the incumbent party, limiting some legislators' need and willingness to compromise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Child_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Child-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Davis was inaugurated, he said, regarding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_Proposition_187_(1994)" title="California Proposition 187 (1994)">Proposition 187</a>, "I'm a governor, not a judge" and vowed to uphold all of the laws of the state, even the ones with which he personally disagreed. Davis defended the law initially,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> but when it was declared unconstitutional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninth_Circuit_Court_of_Appeals" title="Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals">Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals</a> he did not appeal the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis' personality was often described as aloof and his political style cautious and calculated instead of charismatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup> His personality forced him to depend more on political skills, such as fundraising, to win elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup> Davis' tendency to micromanage his administration made it difficult for people to present opposing views and even drove some out of service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chain_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chain-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Davis left office in 2003, the <i>San Francisco Chronicle</i> published an editorial discussing his legacy. The newspaper claimed that he lacked vision, allowed the legislature and its policies to define his tenure and had a "robotic governing style" that focused on fundraising instead of personal relationships. The Chronicle commented that Davis was often on the right side of the issues but that being on the right side of the issues alienated the electorate. Davis lacked charisma and seemed to be more passionate about winning campaigns than governing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-7"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup> Davis never showed emotion to the voters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> He spent much of his campaign time talking about his accomplishments instead of providing voters with a vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-End-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Davis' second term, which lasted only ten months, was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Recall_election" title="Recall election">recall election</a>. Davis signed into law several controversial measures during the closing weeks of the recall campaign, including one granting drivers' licenses to undocumented immigrants. Davis also signed legislation requiring employers to pay for medical insurance for workers and legislation granting domestic partners many of the same rights as married people. He vetoed legislation that would have given undocumented immigrants free tuition for community college. Many of Davis' opponents were furious over the signings of these measures so late in his administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Floor_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Floor-82">[82]</a></sup> Some political observers saw these efforts as an attempt to reinforce support from Hispanics, labor union members and liberal Democrats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Floor_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-Floor-82">[82]</a></sup> Ultimately, Davis did not have as much support from Hispanics and union members in the recall election as he did in his 2002 re-election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Style_17-8"><a href="#cite_note-Style-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Davis was governor during the southern California fires of 2003, more commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Fire_(2003)" title="Cedar Fire (2003)">Cedar Fire</a>. Davis declared a state of emergency in Los Angeles County, San Bernardino County, San Diego County and Ventura County in October 2003 and deployed the National Guard to help with disaster relief. By mid-November, the greater South Los Angeles area had been declared a disaster area. This enabled federal funding to help repair flooding and weather-related damage, including the destruction of thousands of acres of vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> The Cedar Fire was the last major event during Davis' tenure as governor. Both Davis and governor-elect Schwarzenegger worked to help with disaster relief. Schwarzenegger went to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and met Vice President <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a> to lobby the federal government for more disaster relief funds.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recall">Recall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Recall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_gubernatorial_recall_election,_2003" title="California gubernatorial recall election, 2003">California gubernatorial recall election, 2003</a></div>
<p>In July 2003, a sufficient number of citizen signatures were collected for a <a href="/wiki/Recall_election" title="Recall election">recall election</a>. The initial drive for the recall was fueled by funds from the personal fortune of <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">U.S. Rep.</a> <a href="/wiki/Darrell_Issa" title="Darrell Issa">Darrell Issa</a>, a Republican who originally hoped to replace Davis himself. The 2003 California recall special election was the goal of the "Dump Davis" campaign and constituted the first gubernatorial recall in Californian history and only the second in U.S. history.</p>
<p>Early in the recall election, Davis called the recall election an “insult” to the eight million voters who had voted in the 2002 gubernatorial election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> The Davis campaign tried to run against the recall Yes/No vote instead of against the candidates that were trying to replace him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circus_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Circus-23">[23]</a></sup> Davis tried to depict the recall as a $66 million waste of money that could allow a candidate with a very small percentage of the vote to become Governor—potentially someone who was very liberal or conservative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circus_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Circus-23">[23]</a></sup> There are no primaries in a recall election. Davis tried to run “outside the recall circus” and to make himself appear gubernatorial and hard at work for California, and who had made improvements to education and healthcare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circus_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Circus-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Early August polls showed that over 50% supported the recall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circus_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Circus-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2003, Davis conceded that he had lost touch with the voters and he was trying to correct that with numerous townhall meetings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Concedes_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Concedes-86">[86]</a></sup> Poll numbers in September showed a 3% drop in the number of California voters who were planning to vote yes on the recall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mid_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mid-87">[87]</a></sup> According to some analysts and campaign aides, Davis' town hall meetings and conversations with voters were softening his image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mid_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mid-87">[87]</a></sup> Many political insiders remarked that Davis had made several comebacks and that he should not be counted out of the race despite poll numbers that showed over 50% planning to vote yes on the recall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survival_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-survival-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circus_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-Circus-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mid_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mid-87">[87]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the recall, Davis blamed some of the state's problems on his predecessor Pete Wilson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defends_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Defends-88">[88]</a></sup> Davis claimed that he would have rather raised taxes on the upper tax brackets instead of restoring vehicle registration fees and college student tuition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defends_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Defends-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis called the recall a right-wing effort to rewrite history after losing the fall election last year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defends_88-2"><a href="#cite_note-Defends-88">[88]</a></sup> In a major 19 minute campaign address that was broadcast statewide, Davis said the recall was a "right-wing power grab" by Republicans and he blamed Republicans in the legislature and in Washington for many of the state's problems while at the same time he tried to take some of the responsibility for the state's problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>

<p>On October 7, 2003, Davis was recalled with 55.4% of the votes in favor of the recall and Republican <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a> was elected to replace him as governor. The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">Bay Area</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County" title="Los Angeles County">Los Angeles County</a> were the only regions in California to vote no on the recall. Davis joined <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Frazier" title="Lynn Frazier">Lynn Frazier</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, who was ousted in 1921, as the only governors in American history to be recalled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recalled_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recalled-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the night of the recall, Davis conceded defeat and thanked California for having elected him in 5 statewide elections. Davis mentioned what he defined as the accomplishments of his administration such as improvements in education, environmental protection, and health insurance for children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Davis said he would help Schwarzenegger in the transition and he later urged his staff to do the same.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_politics">Life after politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Life after politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After leaving public office, Davis appeared on several shows, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_with_Jay_Leno" title="The Tonight Show with Jay Leno">The Tonight Show with Jay Leno</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Show_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Show with David Letterman">Late Show with David Letterman</a></i>, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo</a> on the <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Yes,_Dear" title="Yes, Dear">Yes, Dear</a></i>. In December 2004, he announced that he was joining the law firm of Loeb &amp; Loeb.</p>
<p>Davis spends 80% of his workdays practicing corporate law as "<a href="/wiki/Of_counsel" title="Of counsel">of counsel</a>" to Loeb &amp; Loeb in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Century_City" title="Century City">Century City</a>, a firm where all attorneys wear casual attire, even Davis. <i>American Lawyer</i> magazine called the firm one of the "best places" in the country for legal staff to work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bright_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-bright-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis has done several media interviews about his legacy. He appeared prominently in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Enron:_The_Smartest_Guys_in_the_Room" title="Enron: The Smartest Guys in the Room">Enron: The Smartest Guys in the Room</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enron_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enron-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>The debate about his legacy and role in the energy woes that proved to be his downfall remains. In a CNN interview on August 5, 2005, Davis expressed that he feels complete vindication because of the revelation that Enron manipulated the California energy market and because of Schwarzenegger's then-low approval ratings. He also indicated that he had no interest in running for governor again, although he had been urged to run by some Democrats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enron_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-Enron-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was a guest lecturer at UCLA's School of Public Policy in 2006 alongside former Republican State Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Brulte" title="Jim Brulte">Jim Brulte</a>. He wrote an introduction for a journalist's book on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amber_Alert" title="Amber Alert">Amber Alert</a> system for missing children, a cause he championed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bright_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-bright-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 23, 2007, Davis was appointed to the Board of Directors of animation company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DiC_Entertainment" title="DiC Entertainment">DiC Entertainment</a>, as a non-executive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 21, 2009, Davis was the keynote speaker at the Columbia Law School graduation ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
