<p><i><b>Gracie</b></i> is a 2007 American historical sports drama film directed by <a href="/wiki/Davis_Guggenheim" title="Davis Guggenheim">Davis Guggenheim</a>. It stars <a href="/wiki/Carly_Schroeder" title="Carly Schroeder">Carly Schroeder</a> as Gracie Bowen, <a href="/wiki/Dermot_Mulroney" title="Dermot Mulroney">Dermot Mulroney</a> as Bryan Bowen, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Shue" title="Elisabeth Shue">Elisabeth Shue</a> as Lindsay Bowen, <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Lee_Soffer" title="Jesse Lee Soffer">Jesse Lee Soffer</a> as Johnny Bowen, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Shue" title="Andrew Shue">Andrew Shue</a> as Coach Owen Clark.</p>
<p><i>Gracie</i> takes place in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, United States in 1978, before 1972's <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a> had taken much effect in women's college sports <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">[1]</a></sup> and when organized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women%27s_soccer" title="Women's soccer">women's soccer</a> was still very rare in the United States. <i>Gracie</i>, the film's central <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a>, overcomes the loss of her brother by convincing her family and school to allow her to play <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">varsity soccer</a> on an all-boys team. The story is very loosely based on the childhood experiences of <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Shue" title="Elisabeth Shue">Elisabeth Shue</a>.</p>
<p>The novelization <i>Gracie</i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Weyn" title="Suzanne Weyn">Suzanne Weyn</a>, was released in June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is 1978 and 15-year-old <a href="/wiki/Tomboy" title="Tomboy">tomboy</a> Gracie Bowen, who lives in <a href="/wiki/South_Orange,_New_Jersey" title="South Orange, New Jersey">South Orange, New Jersey</a>, is crazy about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer">soccer</a>, as are her three brothers and their former soccer star father. Although Gracie wants to join her brothers and neighbor Kyle in the nightly practices her father runs, she is discouraged by everyone except her older brother, Johnny.</p>
<p>Johnny, Gracie and Kyle attend <a href="/wiki/Columbia_High_School_(New_Jersey)" title="Columbia High School (New Jersey)">Columbia High School</a>, where Johnny is the captain and star player for the varsity soccer team. After missing a shot at the end of a game, the despondent Johnny drives off with a friend's car and dies in a traffic accident.</p>
<p>Struggling with grief, Gracie decides that she wants to replace her brother on the team. Her father does not believe that girls should play soccer, telling her she is neither tough nor talented enough. Her mother is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse" title="Nurse">nurse</a> who lacks the competitive drive of the rest of her family and fears for Gracie's safety. Her mother later tells Gracie that she would have liked to become a <a href="/wiki/Surgeon" title="Surgeon">surgeon</a>, but that option had not been available to her as a woman.</p>
<p>Rejected and depressed, Gracie begins to rebel; she stops doing her schoolwork, is caught cheating on an exam, and experiments with wild and self-destructive behavior. She is finally caught by her father almost having sex with a guy she met near the docks after telling her friend "I want to do something that I've never done before." This serves as a wake-up call for her parents, particularly her father. He quits his job to work with her on her soccer training.</p>
<p>When the school board rejects her request to play boys' soccer, Gracie files an appeal. Citing the newly passed <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a>, Gracie argues that since a girls' soccer team does not exist, she should be allowed to play on the boys' varsity soccer team. The school board allows her to tryout for the team. After a very rough tryout, she makes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junior_varsity" title="Junior varsity">junior varsity</a> team and has to decide if she is willing to settle for playing at that level. She decides to make the most of playing on junior varsity.</p>
<p>One of the coaches asks her to come up to the main team for their championship game. After saying no at first she finally goes. Gracie watches from the bench as the game goes to <a href="/wiki/Sudden_death_(sport)" title="Sudden death (sport)">sudden death overtime</a>. New captain Kyle gets hurt and Gracie goes in for him, scoring the winning goal with a move that her dad taught her.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The premise of the film rests upon the conflicting expectations of two different <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> generations. As Gracie's parents were most likely both products of what is now referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Silent_Generation" title="Silent Generation">Silent Generation</a> (children born during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>), they would have been raised to adhere to traditional <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beliefnet.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-beliefnet.com-3">[3]</a></sup> This would include discouraging a girl from participating in "boys sports" such as soccer as opposed to "girls sports" such as <a href="/wiki/Cheerleading" title="Cheerleading">cheerleading</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Gracie, on the other hand, came of age during the 1970s, when <a href="/wiki/Second-wave_feminism" title="Second-wave feminism">new and controversial ideas about gender</a> were being introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eleanor Ringel Gillespie, critic for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta_Journal-Constitution" title="Atlanta Journal-Constitution">Atlanta Journal-Constitution</a>,</i> noted an additional generation gap between those in the film and those in the audience, many of whom grew up with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women's national soccer team">United States women's national soccer team</a> (which played its first game in 1985) as a reality:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">

<p>Other critics have also noted the multiple generation gaps. Kevin Cahillane observes in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> that, "this being 1978 (before <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a> turned a generation of girls from onlookers into athletes), her desire goes against the wishes of the coach, the principal, the other players and her heartbroken parents."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">[1]</a></sup> In another article for the <i>New York Times,</i> Jeannette Catsoulis further argues that, "[Gracie] is accustomed to having her abilities overlooked [...] When tragedy strikes, and Grace channels her grief into a resolution to play on the all-male varsity team, even her best friend is horrified. As everyone knows, only lesbians play soccer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film closes with the following remarks: "Thanks to <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a> and brave girls like Gracie, there are over 5 million girls who play soccer in America. Since 1991 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women's national soccer team">U.S. Women's National team</a> has won Soccer's World Championships two times." (FIFA Women's World Cup Champions 1991, 1999)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_casting">Development and casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development and casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LisaShueJI1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="463" data-file-width="463" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/LisaShueJI1.jpg/225px-LisaShueJI1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/LisaShueJI1.jpg/338px-LisaShueJI1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/LisaShueJI1.jpg/450px-LisaShueJI1.jpg 2x" width="225"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LisaShueJI1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Shue" title="Elisabeth Shue">Elisabeth Shue</a> in August 2007.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The film is loosely based on life events concerning the Shue family. Former <i><a href="/wiki/Melrose_Place" title="Melrose Place">Melrose Place</a></i> star <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Shue" title="Andrew Shue">Andrew Shue</a> (who developed, produced, and had a supporting role in the film) initially conceived of it as a story about his late brother William, the oldest Shue sibling, who was the captain of the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_High_School_(New_Jersey)" title="Columbia High School (New Jersey)">Columbia High School</a> soccer team that won the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_State_Interscholastic_Athletic_Association" title="New Jersey State Interscholastic Athletic Association">New Jersey state championship</a> in 1978; he died in an accident in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">[1]</a></sup> As Andrew developed the idea with his brother-in-law (<a href="/wiki/Davis_Guggenheim" title="Davis Guggenheim">Davis Guggenheim</a>, the director of <i><a href="/wiki/An_Inconvenient_Truth" title="An Inconvenient Truth">An Inconvenient Truth</a></i>) the storyline began to shift towards Guggenheim's wife, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Shue" title="Elisabeth Shue">Elisabeth Shue</a>, Andrew's older sister. She became the model for Gracie and William the model for Johnny. Andrew Shue noted in an interview that,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...It was going to be about my older brother, originally. It was really Davis, my sister's husband, who pointed out to me that my sister was the real underdog in our family. I didn't know the emotional turmoil of a girl in an all-boys family trying to get some credit for the fact that she had this courage to play with boys. She has gone from being a girl who was scared, to being this brave girl who could stand up and play with boys, and [went] into acting before any of us and inspired all of us in many ways. So I wanted to celebrate her and her journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beliefnet.com_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-beliefnet.com-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This refers to Elisabeth Shue's decision at the age of nine (in 1972) to play soccer on an all-boys team, making her the first girl in the South Orange and Maplewood areas of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters.com-8">[8]</a></sup> Shue stated in a 2007 interview that she did this because, "There was no other choice back then. There was no girls team to play on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters.com-8">[8]</a></sup> Shue continued to play on the team until 1976, when she turned 13. Of her decision to leave the team, Shue stated, "the movie is really what would have happened if I hadn’t quit [playing soccer]. I quit because of what people would think of me. The pressure from the boys. The awkward development of my body. I really, really regret it. I wish I’d been brave enough."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Andrew, Elizabeth, and their brother John (who was also a producer on the film) along with Guggenheim then engaged in an extensive search to find the right actress to portray fifteen-year-old Gracie. The search was promoted via the website, <i>findinggracie.com</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The role eventually went to athlete - actress <a href="/wiki/Carly_Schroeder" title="Carly Schroeder">Carly Schroeder</a>. Frederic and Mary Ann Brussat argue that Schroeder "puts in a stellar performance as the teenager who becomes a warrior when most of her peers are cheering from the sidelines."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Filming partly took place at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_High_School_(New_Jersey)" title="Columbia High School (New Jersey)">Columbia High School</a>, both the setting of the events of the film as well as the actual high school attended by the Shue family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Filming also took place in various locations in <a href="/wiki/South_Plainfield,_New_Jersey" title="South Plainfield, New Jersey">South Plainfield, New Jersey</a>.</p>
<p>Andrew Shue chose to honor his former Dartmouth and Columbia High School teammate Chris Colasanti by naming the stories coach after him. Colasanti, who was captain of Columbia's 1985 state championship team was killed in the 9/11/2001 attack on the World Trade Center. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviews">Reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/At_the_Movies_with_Ebert_%26_Roeper" title="At the Movies with Ebert &amp; Roeper">At the Movies with Ebert &amp; Roeper</a> gave <i>Gracie</i> "Two <a href="/wiki/Thumbs_signal" title="Thumbs signal">Thumbs up</a>," stating, "You've seen it before, but you'll rarely see it better."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Gracie</i> is a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> Critics' Pick.</i> Times critic Jeannette Catsoulis described <i>Gracie</i> as "a familiar underdog story told with unusual sensitivity [...] “Gracie” connects the adversity-drama dots — the beat-down, the bounce-back, the last-minute support from an unexpected quarter — with a subtle awareness of the shock waves of bereavement. Balancing the emotional complexity is Chris Manley’s refreshingly unaffected cinematography; the drama of a free kick, like that of a good movie, is best viewed through a steady lens."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Bill Zwecker of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun_Times" title="Chicago Sun Times">Chicago Sun Times</a></i>, stated that "It's a sweet and uplifting film, and though quite predictable, gives us a family drama that showcases simple truths about overcoming seemingly impossible odds and leaves you with a warm and very satisfying feeling deep down. It's a solid, hopeful and inspiring story that reminds us of what we might call "old-fashioned" values about perseverance and making your dreams come true. Old- fashioned? Not at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Owen Gleiberman of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> argued that, "in 1978, a high school girl playing competitive soccer wasn't just novel — it was barely heard of. This amiable rouser, based on the experiences of Elisabeth Shue and her family tries to convey how gutsy and role-smashing it is for [...] Gracie to cleat her way onto an all-boys soccer team. So why is Gracie as processed as an <a href="/wiki/After_school_special" title="After school special">after-school special</a>? You miss the knockabout edge of <i><a href="/wiki/Bend_It_Like_Beckham" title="Bend It Like Beckham">Bend It Like Beckham</a></i> — though the ending, in its <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Pavlov" title="Ivan Pavlov">Pavlovian</a> sports-flick way, pumps you up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Scott Tobias of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> suggested that, "though <i>Gracie</i> fashions Shue's story into ready-made inspirational formula, it's nonetheless vivid in its particulars, from the looks and sounds of late-'70s New Jersey to the portrait of a soccer-driven family reformed by loss."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Lael Loewenstein of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> stated that <i>Gracie</i> is "an earnest, well-acted, poignant drama that nevertheless runs afoul of <a href="/wiki/List_of_sports_clich%C3%A9s#Sports_film_clich.C3.A9s" title="List of sports clichés">sports movie clichés</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film received a 60% rating from <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> (56 <i>fresh</i> and 37 <i>rotten</i> reviews).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gracie_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On a budget of $9 million, the limited release film made about $4 million at the box office and over $3 million in rentals.</p>
