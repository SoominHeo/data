<p>The <b>Great Ocean Road</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_Heritage_List" title="Australian National Heritage List">Australian National Heritage listed</a> 243 kilometres (151 mi) stretch of road along the south-eastern coast of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victorian</a> cities of <a href="/wiki/Torquay,_Victoria" title="Torquay, Victoria">Torquay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allansford,_Victoria" title="Allansford, Victoria">Allansford</a>. Built by returned soldiers between 1919 and 1932 and dedicated to soldiers killed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the road is the world's largest <a href="/wiki/War_memorial" title="War memorial">war memorial</a>. Winding through varying terrain along the coast and providing access to several prominent landmarks, including the <a href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Apostles_(Victoria)" title="The Twelve Apostles (Victoria)">Twelve Apostles</a> limestone stack formations, the road is an important tourist attraction in the region.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Ocean Road starts at Torquay and travels 244 kilometres westward to finish at Allansford near <a href="/wiki/Warrnambool" title="Warrnambool">Warrnambool</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75anniversary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-75anniversary-1">[1]</a></sup> the largest city along the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The road is two lane (one in each direction),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and is covered by a speed limit changing between 50 kilometres per hour and 100 kilometres per hour.</p>

<p>The road is considered a tourist attraction in the area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in which much of the road hugs coastline affectionately known as the Surf Coast between Torquay and Cape Otway and the <i><a href="/wiki/Shipwreck_Coast" title="Shipwreck Coast">Shipwreck Coast</a></i> further west of Cape Otway, providing visibility of <a href="/wiki/Bass_Strait" title="Bass Strait">Bass Strait</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>. The road traverses rainforests, as well as beaches and cliffs composed of limestone and sandstone, which is susceptible to erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indi_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-indi-6">[6]</a></sup> The road travels via <a href="/wiki/Anglesea,_Victoria" title="Anglesea, Victoria">Anglesea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorne,_Victoria" title="Lorne, Victoria">Lorne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Bay" title="Apollo Bay">Apollo Bay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Port_Campbell" title="Port Campbell">Port Campbell</a>, the latter being notable for its natural limestone and sandstone rock formations including <a href="/wiki/Loch_Ard_Gorge" title="Loch Ard Gorge">Loch Ard Gorge</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Grotto,_Victoria" title="The Grotto, Victoria">The Grotto</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Arch" title="London Arch">London Arch</a> (formerly London Bridge) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Apostles,_Victoria" title="The Twelve Apostles, Victoria">The Twelve Apostles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At the stretch of the Great Ocean Road nearer to Geelong, the road meanders along the <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coast</a>, with tall, almost-vertical <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a> on the other side of it. <a href="/wiki/Traffic_sign" title="Traffic sign">Road signs</a> put up along the road warn motorists of possible <a href="/wiki/Rockfall" title="Rockfall">rockfalls</a>, which have occurred before.</p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a>, close to Torquay, experiences great benefit from Australian and international visitors to the road; with Geelong Otway Tourism affirming it as an invaluable asset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geelong_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-geelong-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Automobile_Club_of_Victoria" title="Royal Automobile Club of Victoria">Royal Automobile Club of Victoria</a> (RACV) listed the road as the state's top tourism experience in its <i>Victoria 101 survey</i>, based on spots recommended by members and the public on what they would recommend to visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, according to a survey performed by the <a href="/wiki/Surf_Coast_Shire" title="Surf Coast Shire">Surf Coast Shire</a>, residents have expressed concern that there are not enough safe crossings along the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Ocean_Road_Marathon">Great Ocean Road Marathon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Great Ocean Road Marathon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 45 kilometre section of the Great Ocean Road, between Lorne and Apollo Bay, is the location of the annual "Great Ocean Road Marathon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> First run in 2005, the current marathon record of two hours, 27 minutes and 42 seconds was set in 2011 by James Kipkelwon of Kenya who also won the event in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadel_Evans_Great_Ocean_Road_Race">Cadel Evans Great Ocean Road Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cadel Evans Great Ocean Road Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2014 former <a href="/wiki/UCI_Road_World_Championships_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_road_race" title="UCI Road World Championships – Men's road race">World Road Cycling Champion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Cadel_Evans" title="Cadel Evans">Cadel Evans</a> announced that the Great Ocean Road would play host to the <a href="/wiki/Cadel_Evans_Great_Ocean_Road_Race" title="Cadel Evans Great Ocean Road Race">Cadel Evans Great Ocean Road Race</a> in early 2015, including elite races for men and women and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclosportive" title="Cyclosportive">mass participation</a> People's Ride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Island_Archway,_Great_Ocean_Rd,_Victoria,_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2478" data-file-width="7000" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Island_Archway%2C_Great_Ocean_Rd%2C_Victoria%2C_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg/350px-Island_Archway%2C_Great_Ocean_Rd%2C_Victoria%2C_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Island_Archway%2C_Great_Ocean_Rd%2C_Victoria%2C_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg/525px-Island_Archway%2C_Great_Ocean_Rd%2C_Victoria%2C_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Island_Archway%2C_Great_Ocean_Rd%2C_Victoria%2C_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg/700px-Island_Archway%2C_Great_Ocean_Rd%2C_Victoria%2C_Australia_-_Nov_08.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>

<p>The Great Ocean Road was first planned towards the end of the first world war, when chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Country_Roads_Board" title="Country Roads Board">Country Roads Board</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Calder_(engineer)" title="William Calder (engineer)">William Calder</a>, asked the State War Council for funds to be provided for returned soldiers to work on roads in sparsely populated areas in the Western District.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By the time of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the rugged south-west coast of Victoria was accessible only by sea or rough bush track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50years_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-50years-16">[16]</a></sup> Besides being dedicated as a memorial, it was also envisaged that the road would connect isolated settlements on the coast, and become a vital transport link for the timber industry and tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moment_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-moment-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surveying for the road, tentatively titled the <i>South Coast Road</i>, started in 1918 – with the road suggested to travel from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barwon_Heads" title="Barwon Heads">Barwon Heads</a>, follow the coast west around <a href="/wiki/Cape_Otway" title="Cape Otway">Cape Otway</a>, and end near Warrnambool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscomhistory_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscomhistory-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1918, the Great Ocean Road Trust was formed as a private company, under the helm of president <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hitchcock" title="Howard Hitchcock">Howard Hitchcock</a>. The company managed to secure £81,000 in capital from private subscription and borrowing, with Hitchcock himself contributing £3000. Money would be repaid by charging drivers a toll until the debt was cleared, and the road would then be gifted to the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hitchcock_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-hitchcock-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_effort">Construction effort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Construction effort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_ocean_road.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="800" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Great_ocean_road.jpg/220px-Great_ocean_road.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Great_ocean_road.jpg/330px-Great_ocean_road.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Great_ocean_road.jpg/440px-Great_ocean_road.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_ocean_road.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Great Ocean Road</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Construction on the road began on 19 September 1919, built by approximately 3,000 <a href="/wiki/Digger_(soldier)" title="Digger (soldier)">returned servicemen</a> as a war memorial for fellow servicemen who had been killed in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. An advance survey team progressed through dense wilderness at approximately 3 kilometres a month. Construction was done by hand; using explosives, pick and shovel, wheel barrows, and some small machinery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indi_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-indi-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moment_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-moment-17">[17]</a></sup> and was at times perilous, with several workers killed on the job; the final sections along steep coastal mountains being the most difficult to work on. Anecdotal evidence from <a href="/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Australian Broadcasting Corporation">ABC</a> archives in 1982 suggested workers would rest <a href="/wiki/Detonator" title="Detonator">detonators</a> on their knees during travel, as it was the softest ride for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moment_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-moment-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The soldiers were paid 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shillings" title="Shillings">shillings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sixpence_(Australian)" title="Sixpence (Australian)">sixpence</a> for eight hours per day, also working a half-day on Saturdays. They used tents for accommodation throughout, and made use of a communal dining marquee and kitchen; food costing up to 10 shillings a week. Despite the difficulty involved in constructing the road, the workers had access to a piano, gramophone, games, newspapers and magazines at the camps. Additionally, in 1924, the <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamboat</a> <i>Casino</i> became stranded near Cape Patton after hitting a reef, forcing it to jettison 500 barrels of beer and 120 cases of spirits. The workers obtained the cargo, resulting in an unscheduled two-week-long drinking break.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gowest_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-gowest-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Completion_and_early_usage">Completion and early usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Completion and early usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hitchcock_GOR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="406" data-file-width="672" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Hitchcock_GOR.jpg/220px-Hitchcock_GOR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Hitchcock_GOR.jpg/330px-Hitchcock_GOR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Hitchcock_GOR.jpg/440px-Hitchcock_GOR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hitchcock_GOR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hitchcock" title="Howard Hitchcock">Hitchcock</a> Memorial at Mount Defiance</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 18 March 1922 the section from Eastern View to <a href="/wiki/Lorne,_Victoria" title="Lorne, Victoria">Lorne</a> was officially opened with celebrations. However it was then closed from 10 May 1922 for further work; opening again on 21 December along with tolls to recoup construction costs. The charge, payable at Eastern View, was two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shillings" title="Shillings">shillings</a> for motor cars, and 10 shillings for wagons with more than two horses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75anniversary_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-75anniversary-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscomhistory_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-newscomhistory-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gowest_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-gowest-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1932, the section from Lorne to <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Bay" title="Apollo Bay">Apollo Bay</a> was finished, bringing the road to completion. The road was officially opened with Victoria's Lieutenant-Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_William_Irvine" title="Sir William Irvine">Sir William Irvine</a> holding a ceremony near Lorne's <a href="/wiki/Lorne,_Victoria" title="Lorne, Victoria">Grand Pacific Hotel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscomhistory_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-newscomhistory-18">[18]</a></sup> and the road subsequently being acknowledged as the world's largest war memorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geelong_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-geelong-9">[9]</a></sup> At the time, newspaper <a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a> commented – "In the face of almost insurmountable odds, the Great Ocean Road has materialised from a dream or 'wild-cat scheme', as many dubbed it, into concrete reality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75anniversary_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-75anniversary-1">[1]</a></sup> Hitchcock had however died of heart disease on 22 August 1932, before the road was completed, though his car was driven behind the governor's in the procession along the road during the opening ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hitchcock_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-hitchcock-19">[19]</a></sup> A memorial was constructed in Hitchcock's name on the road at Mount Defiance, near Lorne, and he is still affectionately considered the <i>Father of the Road</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>In its original state, the road was considered a formidable drive; fitting only a single vehicle comfortably at a time. Areas with sheer cliffs would be most hazardous, with only few places for drivers to pull over to allow others to proceed in the opposite direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50years_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-50years-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> On 2 October 1936, the road was handed to the State Government; with the deed for the road presented to the Victorian Premier at a ceremony at the Cathedral Rock toll gate. It was at this time that the tolls were also removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75anniversary_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-75anniversary-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscomhistory_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-newscomhistory-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gowest_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-gowest-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1962, the road was deemed by the Tourist Development Authority to be <i>one of the world's great scenic roads</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It also had sections widened between the Lorne Hotel and the Pacific Hotel to improve traffic, while aiming to preserve its character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Despite improvements, the road was still considered a challenging drive; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Police" title="Victorian Police">Victorian Police</a> motor school even using it for training around 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over its life, the Great Ocean Road has been susceptible to natural elements; in 1960 the section at Princetown was partially washed away by water during storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It experienced landslides on 11 August 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and in 1971; both closing sections of the road near Lorne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Because of the terrain surrounding the road, it was also closed due to bush-fires in 1962<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and 1964; particularly in areas with nearby camp-sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In January 2011 a section of the overhanging cliffs collapsed due to heavy rain.</p>
<p>In 2011, the road was added to the <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_Heritage_List" title="Australian National Heritage List">Australian National Heritage List</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANHL_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANHL-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Ocean_Walk">Great Ocean Walk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Ocean_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Great Ocean Walk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Ocean_Walk" title="Great Ocean Walk">Great Ocean Walk</a> opened, connecting 104 km of walking trails that follow the coastline near the Great Ocean Road, stretching from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Bay" title="Apollo Bay">Apollo Bay</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Apostles,_Victoria" title="The Twelve Apostles, Victoria">12 Apostles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
