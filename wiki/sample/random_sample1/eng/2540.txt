<p>The <b>grapefruit</b> (<i>Citrus</i> × <i>paradisi</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> tree known for its sour to semi-sweet <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>. Grapefruit is a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> originating in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> as an accidental cross between two introduced species, <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">sweet orange</a> (<i>C. sinensis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelo</a> or shaddock (<i>C. maxima</i>), both of which were introduced from Asia in the seventeenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrington_.26_Fraser_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carrington_.26_Fraser-1">[1]</a></sup> When found, it was named the “<a href="/wiki/Forbidden_fruit" title="Forbidden fruit">forbidden fruit</a>”;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbidden_Fruit_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbidden_Fruit-2">[2]</a></sup> and it has also been misidentified with the pomelo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The grapefruit's name alludes to clusters of the fruit on the tree, which often appear similar to <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc.gov_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg/170px-Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg/255px-Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg/340px-Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grapefruit.ebola.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grapefruit growing in the grape-like clusters from which their name derives.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The evergreen grapefruit trees usually grow to around 5–6 meters (16–20 ft) tall, although they can reach 13–15 m (43–49 ft). The leaves are glossy dark green, long (up to 15 centimeters (5.9 in)) and thin. It produces 5 cm (2 in) white four-petaled <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a>. The fruit is yellow-orange skinned and generally an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">oblate spheroid</a> in shape; it ranges in diameter from 10–15 cm (3.9–5.9 in). The flesh is segmented and <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a>, varying in color depending on the <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>, which include white, pink and red pulps of varying sweetness (generally, the redder varieties are sweeter). The 1929 US Ruby Red (of the Redblush variety) has the first grapefruit <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-txsweet_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-txsweet-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Citrus_taxonomy#Oranges" title="Citrus taxonomy">Citrus taxonomy § Oranges</a></div>
<p>One ancestor of the grapefruit was the Jamaican <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweet_orange" title="Sweet orange">sweet orange</a> (<i>Citrus sinensis</i>), itself an ancient hybrid of Asian origin; the other was the Indonesian <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelo</a> (<i>C. maxima</i>). One story of the fruit's origins is that a certain "Captain Shaddock"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> brought pomelo seeds to Jamaica and bred the first fruit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-culinaire_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-culinaire-7">[7]</a></sup> However, it probably originated as a naturally occurring hybrid between the two plants some time after they had been introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrington_.26_Fraser_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carrington_.26_Fraser-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<div class="poem">
<p>                         FORBIDDEN-FRUIT-TREE<br/>
The Trunk, Leaves, and Flowers of this Tree, very much resemble<br/>
those of the Orange-tree.<br/>
The Fruit, when ripe, is something longer and larger than the largest<br/>
Orange; and exceeds, in the Delicacy of its Taste, the Fruit of every<br/>
Tree in this or any of our neighbouring Islands.<br/>
It hath somewhat of the Taste of a Shaddock; but far exceeds that, as<br/>
well as the best Orange, in its delicious Taste and Flavour.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Description from Hughes' 1750 <i>Natural History of Barbados</i>.</cite></div>
<p>The hybrid fruit, then called "the forbidden fruit", was first documented in 1750 by a <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welshman</a>, Rev. <a href="/wiki/Griffith_Hughes" title="Griffith Hughes">Griffith Hughes</a>, who described specimens from Barbados in <i>The Natural History of Barbados</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q_A:Grapefruit_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Q_A:Grapefruit-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Currently, the grapefruit is said to be one of the "Seven Wonders of Barbados".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The grapefruit was brought to Florida by Count <a href="/wiki/Odet_Philippe" title="Odet Philippe">Odet Philippe</a> in 1823 in what is now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_Harbor" title="Safety Harbor">Safety Harbor</a>. Further crosses have produced the <a href="/wiki/Tangelo" title="Tangelo">tangelo</a> (1905), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneola_tangelo" title="Minneola tangelo">Minneola tangelo</a> (1931), and the <a href="/wiki/Oroblanco" title="Oroblanco">oroblanco</a> (1984).</p>
<p>The grapefruit was known as the <i>shaddock</i> or <i>shattuck</i> until the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Its current name alludes to clusters of the fruit on the tree, which often appear similar to <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc.gov_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-loc.gov-4">[4]</a></sup> Botanically, it was not distinguished from the pomelo until the 1830s, when it was given the name <i>Citrus paradisi.</i> Its true origins were not determined until the 1940s. This led to the official name being altered to <i>Citrus × paradisi,</i> the "×" identifying its hybrid origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_Citrus_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Texas_Citrus-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_of_Florida_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-University_of_Florida-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KC_Atwood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="321" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/KC_Atwood.jpg/170px-KC_Atwood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/KC_Atwood.jpg/255px-KC_Atwood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f3/KC_Atwood.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KC_Atwood.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kimball Chase Atwood</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An early pioneer in the American citrus industry was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kimball_Chase_Atwood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kimball Chase Atwood (page does not exist)">Kimball Chase Atwood</a>, a wealthy entrepreneur who founded the Atwood Grapefruit Co. in the late 19th century. The Atwood Grove became the largest grapefruit grove in the world, with a yearly output of 80,000 boxes of fruit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It was there that pink grapefruit was first discovered in 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruby_Red">Ruby Red</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ruby Red">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1929 Ruby Red patent was associated with real commercial success, which came after the discovery of a red grapefruit growing on a pink variety. The Red grapefruit, starting with the Ruby Red, has even become a symbolic fruit of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, where white “inferior” grapefruit were eliminated and only red grapefruit were grown for decades.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Using radiation to trigger mutations, new varieties were developed to retain the red tones which typically faded to pink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Rio Red variety is the current (2007) Texas grapefruit with registered trademarks Rio Star and Ruby-Sweet, also sometimes promoted as "Reddest" and "Texas Choice". The Rio Red is a <a href="/wiki/Mutation_breeding" title="Mutation breeding">mutation bred</a> variety which was developed by treatment of bud sticks with thermal neutrons. Its improved attributes of mutant variety are fruit and juice color, deeper red, and wide adaptation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_Ruby">Star Ruby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Star Ruby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Star Ruby is the darkest of the red varieties. Developed from an <a href="/wiki/Irradiation" title="Irradiation">irradiated</a> Hudson grapefruit, it has found limited commercial success because it is more difficult to grow than other varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colors_and_flavors">Colors and flavors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Colors and flavors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Grapefruit mercaptan</div>

<p>Grapefruit comes in many varieties. One way to differentiate between varieties is by the flesh color of fruit they produce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHF_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHF-21">[21]</a></sup> The most popular varieties cultivated today are <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>, <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a> hues, referring to the internal pulp color of the fruit. The family of flavors range from highly acidic and somewhat sour to sweet and tart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHF_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHF-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit_mercaptan" title="Grapefruit mercaptan">Grapefruit mercaptan</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>-containing <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpene</a>, is one of the substances which has a strong influence on the taste and odor of grapefruit, compared with other citrus fruits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrea_Buettner_and_Peter_Schieberle_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andrea_Buettner_and_Peter_Schieberle-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drug_interactions">Drug interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Drug interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit%E2%80%93drug_interactions" title="Grapefruit–drug interactions">Grapefruit–drug interactions</a></div>
<p>Grapefruit and <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit_juice" title="Grapefruit juice">grapefruit juice</a> have been found to <a href="/wiki/Drug_interaction" title="Drug interaction">interact</a> with numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drugs" title="Drugs">drugs</a> in many cases resulting in adverse effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>This happens in two ways. One is that grapefruit can block an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> which <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolizes</a> medication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_May_2016_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_May_2016-24">[24]</a></sup> If the drug is not metabolized, then the level of the drug in the blood can become too high leading to an <a href="/wiki/Adverse_effect" title="Adverse effect">adverse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_May_2016_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_May_2016-24">[24]</a></sup> The other effect is that grapefruit can block the absorption of drugs in the intestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_May_2016_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_May_2016-24">[24]</a></sup> If the drug is not absorbed, then not enough of it is in the blood to have a therapeutic effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_May_2016_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_May_2016-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>One whole grapefruit, or a glass of 200 mL (6.8 US fl oz) of grapefruit juice can cause drug overdose toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaileyDresser2012_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BaileyDresser2012-25">[25]</a></sup> Drugs which are incompatible with grapefruit are typically labeled on the container or <a href="/wiki/Package_insert" title="Package insert">package insert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_May_2016_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_May_2016-24">[24]</a></sup> People taking drugs can ask their health care provider or pharmacist questions about grapefruit / drug interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_May_2016_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_May_2016-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>Grapefruit is a rich source (&gt;20% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Value" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a>, DV in a 100 gram serving) of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHF_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHF-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fellers_PJ_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fellers_PJ-26">[26]</a></sup> contains the <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pectin_Benefits_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pectin_Benefits-27">[27]</a></sup> and the pink and red hues contain the beneficial antioxidant <a href="/wiki/Lycopene" title="Lycopene">lycopene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHF_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHF-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_HS_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee_HS-28">[28]</a></sup> Studies have shown grapefruit helps lower <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHF_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-WHF-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platt_R_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Platt_R-29">[29]</a></sup> and there is evidence that the seeds have <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Extract_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Extract-30">[30]</a></sup> Grapefruit forms a core part of the "<a href="/wiki/Grapefruit_diet" title="Grapefruit diet">grapefruit diet</a>", the theory being that the fruit's low <a href="/wiki/Glycemic_index" title="Glycemic index">glycemic index</a> is able to help the body's <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> burn <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grapefruit_Diet_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grapefruit_Diet-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit_seed_extract" title="Grapefruit seed extract">grapefruit seed extract</a> (GSE) is promoted as a plant-based preservative by some natural personal care manufacturers, studies have shown that the apparent antimicrobial activity associated with GSE preparations is merely due to <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a> with synthetic <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservatives</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Paraben" title="Paraben">parabens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Study_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Study-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pharmazie_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pharmazie-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contamination_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Contamination-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adulteration_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adulteration-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preservatives_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Preservatives-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a popular myth that grapefruits contain high amounts of <a href="/wiki/Spermidine" title="Spermidine">spermidine</a>, a simple <a href="/wiki/Polyamine" title="Polyamine">polyamine</a> that may be related to aging.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The myth probably relies on the confusion between spermidine and <a href="/wiki/Putrescine" title="Putrescine">putrescine</a>. While citrus fruits show high amounts of putrescine, they contain very little spermidine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grapefruit_sweets">Grapefruit sweets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grapefruit sweets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Atenas" title="Atenas">Atenas</a>, grapefruit are often cooked to remove their sourness, rendering them as <a href="/wiki/Confectionery" title="Confectionery">sweets</a>; they are also stuffed with <i><a href="/wiki/Dulce_de_leche" title="Dulce de leche">dulce de leche</a></i>, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">dessert</a> called <i>toronja rellena</i> (stuffed grapefruit). In <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, grapefruit is used primarily for its juice (<i>jus de Chadèque</i>), but is also used to make jam (<i>confiture de Chadèque</i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grapefruit has also been investigated in cancer medicine <a href="/wiki/Pharmacodynamics" title="Pharmacodynamics">pharmacodynamics</a>. Its inhibiting effect on the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of some drugs may allow smaller doses to be used, which can help to reduce costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grapefruit_relatives">Grapefruit relatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Grapefruit relatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Citrus_taxonomy" title="Citrus taxonomy">Citrus taxonomy</a></div>
<p>Grapefruit is a pummelo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backcross" title="Backcross">backcross</a>, a hybrid of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pummelo" title="Pummelo">pummelo</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweet_orange" title="Sweet orange">sweet orange</a>, with sweet orange itself being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pummelo" title="Pummelo">pummelo</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandarin_(fruit)" title="Mandarin (fruit)">mandarin</a> hybrid.</p>
<p>The grapefruit is itself a parent to many hybrids:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Tangelo" title="Tangelo">Tangelo</a> is any hybrid of a <a href="/wiki/Tangerine" title="Tangerine">tangerine</a> and either a <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelo</a> or a grapefruit.
<ul>
<li>'<a href="/wiki/Tangelo#Minneola" title="Tangelo">Minneola</a>': <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Duncan_grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Duncan grapefruit (page does not exist)">Duncan grapefruit</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dancy_(fruit)" title="Dancy (fruit)">Dancy tangerine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-morton-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>'Orlando' (formerly Take'): <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bowen_grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bowen grapefruit (page does not exist)">Bowen grapefruit</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dancy_(fruit)" title="Dancy (fruit)">Dancy tangerine</a>(pollen parent)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-morton-39">[39]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairchild_(fruit)" title="Fairchild (fruit)">Fairchild</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Clementine" title="Clementine">Clementine</a> × Orlando hybrid</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>'Seminole': <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bowen_grapefruit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bowen grapefruit (page does not exist)">Bowen grapefruit</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dancy_(fruit)" title="Dancy (fruit)">Dancy tangerine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-morton-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>'Thornton': <a href="/wiki/Tangerine" title="Tangerine">tangerine</a> × grapefruit, unspecified<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-morton-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>'<a href="/wiki/Ugli_fruit" title="Ugli fruit">Ugli</a>': <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarine</a> × grapefruit, probable (wild seedling)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_39-4"><a href="#cite_note-morton-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>'Nova' is a second-generation hybrid: <a href="/wiki/Clementine" title="Clementine">Clementine</a> × Orlando tangelo cross<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_39-5"><a href="#cite_note-morton-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Oroblanco" title="Oroblanco">Oroblanco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melogold" title="Melogold">Melogold</a> grapefruits are hybrids between pummelo (<i>Citrus maxima</i>) and the grapefruit.</li>
</ul>
<p>The grapefruit's cousins include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweet_orange" title="Sweet orange">sweet orange</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pummelo" title="Pummelo">pummelo</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandarin_(fruit)" title="Mandarin (fruit)">mandarin</a> hybrid</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bitter_orange" title="Bitter orange">Bitter orange</a>: a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pummelo" title="Pummelo">pummelo</a> × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandarin_(fruit)" title="Mandarin (fruit)">mandarin</a> hybrid</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mandelo" title="Mandelo">Mandelos</a>: pummelo × <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarine</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_maxima" title="Citrus maxima">Citrus maxima</a></i>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hyuganatsu" title="Hyuganatsu">Hyuganatsu</a> may also be a pumelo hybrid</li>
</ul>

This fruit is believed to have originated in Barbados as a natural cross between sweet orange (<i>C. sinesis</i>) and Shaddock (<i>C. grandis</i>), both of which were introduced from Asia in the seventeenth century. The grapefruit first appeared as an illustration entitled 'The Forbidden Fruit Tree' in the Rev. Griffith Hughes' <i>The Natural History of Barbados</i> (1750). This accords with the scientific name which literally is 'citrus of paradise'. The fruit was obviously fairly common around that time since George Washington in his Barbados Journal (1750-1751) mentions 'the Forbidden Fruit' as one of the local fruit available at a dinner party he attended.
