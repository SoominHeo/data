<p><b>Gregory Stuart</b> "<b>Greg</b>" <b>Lake</b> (10 November 1947 â 7 December 2016) was an English bassist, guitarist, singer, songwriter, and producer who gained prominence as a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> bands <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">King Crimson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer" title="Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer">Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer</a> (ELP).</p>
<p>Born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, Lake began to play the guitar at the age of 12 and wrote his first song, "<a href="/wiki/Lucky_Man_(Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer_song)" title="Lucky Man (Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer song)">Lucky Man</a>", at the same age. He became a full time musician at 17, playing in several rock bands until fellow Dorset guitarist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fripp" title="Robert Fripp">Robert Fripp</a> invited him to join King Crimson as their singer and bassist. They found commercial success with their influential debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Court_of_the_Crimson_King" title="In the Court of the Crimson King">In the Court of the Crimson King</a></i> (1969). Lake left the band in 1970 and achieved significant success in the 1970s and beyond as the singer, guitarist, bassist, and producer of ELP. As a member of ELP, Lake wrote and recorded several popular songs including "Lucky Man" and "<a href="/wiki/From_the_Beginning_(song)" title="From the Beginning (song)">From the Beginning</a>". Both songs entered the UK and US singles charts.</p>
<p>Lake launched a solo career, beginning with his 1975 single "<a href="/wiki/I_Believe_in_Father_Christmas" title="I Believe in Father Christmas">I Believe in Father Christmas</a>" which reached number two in the UK. He went on to release solo albums and singles thereafter, collaborating with several artists in the process. Lake performed with various groups in the 1980s, and occasional ELP reunions in the 1990s, and toured regularly as a solo artist into the 21st century. He died on 7 December 2016 in London, after suffering from cancer, at the age of 69.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greg Lake was born on 10 November 1947 in the <a href="/wiki/Parkstone" title="Parkstone">Parkstone</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> to Harry, an engineer, and Pearl, a housewife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytobit_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytobit-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes2016_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes2016-4">[4]</a></sup> He grew up in the residential suburb of <a href="/wiki/Oakdale,_Dorset" title="Oakdale, Dorset">Oakdale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkstone2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-parkstone2013-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200651_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200651-6">[6]</a></sup> Speaking about his childhood, Lake said he was "born in an asbestos <a href="/wiki/Prefabs_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prefabs in the United Kingdom">prefab</a> housing unit" into a "very poor" family, and remembered several cold winters at home,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200651.E2.80.9352_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200651.E2.80.9352-7">[7]</a></sup> but credits his parents for sending him money and food during his time as a struggling musician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200652_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200652-8">[8]</a></sup> He later described his upbringing as a happy one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkstone2013_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-parkstone2013-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake discovered <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> in 1957 when he bought <a href="/wiki/Little_Richard" title="Little Richard">Little Richard</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Lucille_(Little_Richard_song)" title="Lucille (Little Richard song)">Lucille</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAwde2008501_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAwde2008501-9">[9]</a></sup> At the age of 12, he first learned to play the guitar and wrote his first song, "<a href="/wiki/Lucky_Man_(Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer_song)" title="Lucky Man (Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer song)">Lucky Man</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revisited_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-revisited-11">[11]</a></sup> which he did not write down and committed it to memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmme2013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-dmme2013-12">[12]</a></sup> He named his mother, a pianist, as his initial musical influence and she bought Lake a second hand guitar to learn on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200652_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200652-8">[8]</a></sup> Lake then took guitar lessons from Don Strike, who had a shop in Westbourne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAwde2008501_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAwde2008501-9">[9]</a></sup> Strike taught him "these awful <a href="/wiki/Bert_Weedon" title="Bert Weedon">Bert Weedon</a> things", reading <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a> exercises with violin pieces by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Paganini" title="NiccolÃ² Paganini">NiccolÃ² Paganini</a>, and playing 1930s pop tunes, the latter of which became an influence on Lake at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200652_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200652-8">[8]</a></sup> After roughly one year with Strike, Lake ended his tuition as he wished to learn songs by <a href="/wiki/The_Shadows" title="The Shadows">The Shadows</a>, a favourite band of his, but Strike "wouldn't have any of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200653_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200653-14">[14]</a></sup> Lake's second guitar was a pink <a href="/wiki/Fender_Stratocaster" title="Fender Stratocaster">Fender Stratocaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1981_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1981-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake attended Oakdale Junior School followed by <a href="/wiki/Poole_High_School" title="Poole High School">Henry Harbin Secondary Modern School</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkstone2013_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-parkstone2013-5">[5]</a></sup> and left the latter in 1963 or 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200654_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200654-16">[16]</a></sup> He then took up work loading and unloading cargo at the <a href="/wiki/Poole_Harbour" title="Poole Harbour">Poole docks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200654_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200654-16">[16]</a></sup> and as a <a href="/wiki/Drafter" title="Drafter">draughtsman</a> for a short period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup> Lake then decided to become a full time musician at the age of 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200654_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200654-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkstone2013_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-parkstone2013-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_bands">Early bands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early bands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lake joined his first band, Unit Four, playing cover songs as their singer and guitarist, through 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200654_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200654-16">[16]</a></sup> Following their split, Lake and Unit Four bassist Dave Genes formed another covers group, the Time Checks, until 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200655_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200655-17">[17]</a></sup> He then became a member of The Shame, where he is featured on their single, "Don't Go Away Little Girl", written by <a href="/wiki/Janis_Ian" title="Janis Ian">Janis Ian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup> During his stay in <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a> for a gig, Lake contracted <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> and continued to perform on stage. His band mates refused to drive back home that night, leaving Lake to sleep in the van where he "woke up blue ... When we got home I was nearly dead ... That was probably the worst I went through".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200655_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200655-17">[17]</a></sup> Following a brief stint in the Shy Limbs, by 1968 Lake was involved with <a href="/wiki/The_Gods_(band)" title="The Gods (band)">The Gods</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Hatfield,_Hertfordshire" title="Hatfield, Hertfordshire">Hatfield</a>, which he described as "a very poor training college",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200657_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200657-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup> but the group secured a residency at the <a href="/wiki/Marquee_Club" title="Marquee Club">Marquee Club</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200658_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200658-19">[19]</a></sup> Lake left the group in 1968 over creative differences as the band were to enter the recording studio. Their keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Ken_Hensley" title="Ken Hensley">Ken Hensley</a> later said that Lake "was far too talented to be kept in the background".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200657_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200657-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200658_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200658-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="King_Crimson">King Crimson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: King Crimson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1960s, Lake formed a friendship with future <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">King Crimson</a> co-founder and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fripp" title="Robert Fripp">Robert Fripp</a>, who was also from Dorset, had received lessons from Strike,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200653_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200653-14">[14]</a></sup> and saw Lake perform in Unit Four in Poole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan200654_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan200654-16">[16]</a></sup> Fripp was asked to be a roadie for a gig at <a href="/wiki/Ventnor" title="Ventnor">Ventnor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>, but no audience turned up. Consequently, Lake and Fripp decided to just play tunes from their guitar lessons that Strike had taught them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAwde2008502_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAwde2008502-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fripp formed King Crimson since his previous group, <a href="/wiki/Giles,_Giles_and_Fripp" title="Giles, Giles and Fripp">Giles, Giles and Fripp</a> was not commercially successful, and their record company suggested getting a proper lead singer. He chose Lake for this role, but asked him to play bass instead of guitar to avoid having to get a bass player in the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAwde2008504_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAwde2008504-22">[22]</a></sup> This marked Lake's first time playing the instrument as he had primarily been a guitarist for the previous eleven years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup> Though <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sinfield" title="Peter Sinfield">Peter Sinfield</a> was the band's lyricist, Lake had some involvement in the lyrics for their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Court_of_the_Crimson_King" title="In the Court of the Crimson King">In the Court of the Crimson King</a></i>. After their contracted producer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Clarke_(producer)" title="Tony Clarke (producer)">Tony Clarke</a> walked away from the project, Lake produced the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERomano201482_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERomano201482-23">[23]</a></sup> Released in October 1969, the album was an immediate commercial and critical success, as Lake recalled: "There was this huge wave of response. The audiences were really into us because we were an underground thing â the critics loved us because we offered something fresh".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epitaphsleeve_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-epitaphsleeve-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>King Crimson supported <i>In the Court of the Crimson King</i> with a tour of the UK and the US, with some of the shows featuring rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Nice" title="The Nice">the Nice</a> as the opening act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epitaphsleeve_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-epitaphsleeve-24">[24]</a></sup> During the US leg, Lake struck up a friendship with Nice keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Keith_Emerson" title="Keith Emerson">Keith Emerson</a>; the two shared similar musical interests and talked about the benefits of forming a new group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme1971-part1_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-nme1971-part1-13">[13]</a></sup> When King Crimson returned to the UK in early 1970, Lake agreed to sing on the band's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Wake_of_Poseidon" title="In the Wake of Poseidon">In the Wake of Poseidon</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC08122016_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC08122016-25">[25]</a></sup> and appear on the music television show <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i> with them, performing the song "Cat Food".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emerson.2C_Lake_.26_Palmer">Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In April 1970, Lake left King Crimson and reunited with Emerson, along with drummer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Palmer" title="Carl Palmer">Carl Palmer</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Crazy_World_of_Arthur_Brown" title="The Crazy World of Arthur Brown">The Crazy World of Arthur Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Rooster" title="Atomic Rooster">Atomic Rooster</a> to form the progressive rock <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer" title="Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer">Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epitaphsleeve_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-epitaphsleeve-24">[24]</a></sup> Lake began with a Fender bass before he switched to a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Ripper" title="Gibson Ripper">Gibson Ripper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1976_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1976-27">[27]</a></sup> As well as bass, Lake contributed acoustic and electric guitar work to Emerson Lake &amp; Palmer, and his voice had a wider and more diverse range than anything The Nice had recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan199739_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan199739-28">[28]</a></sup> Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer became one of the most successful groups in the 1970s. Lake became known for performing on a <a href="/wiki/Persian_carpet" title="Persian carpet">Persian carpet</a> on stage, which originally sold for Â£1,500 and, by 1976, had increased its value to Â£7,000. In addition, Lake would change his guitar strings after each show on tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1976_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1976-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer conflicted between Emerson's interest in complex, classically-influenced music and Lake's more straightforward rock tastes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1976_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1976-27">[27]</a></sup> Lake complained that Emerson chose to play in keys that were not a good fit for his voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAwde2008509_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAwde2008509-29">[29]</a></sup> During the making of the band's second album <i><a href="/wiki/Tarkus" title="Tarkus">Tarkus</a></i>, Lake initially rejected the title track, but was persuaded to record it following a band meeting with management, which ended in the addition of an original Lake tune, "Battlefield", into the suite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacan1997117_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacan1997117-30">[30]</a></sup> Lake's track "<a href="/wiki/From_the_Beginning_(song)" title="From the Beginning (song)">From the Beginning</a>", released on <i><a href="/wiki/Trilogy_(Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer_album)" title="Trilogy (Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer album)">Trilogy</a></i> in 1972, had no particular source of inspiration; "I just felt an inspiration to do it, and it flowed through me in a natural way. My hands fell upon these very unusual chords ... It was kind of a gift".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone2013_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone2013-31">[31]</a></sup> It was released as a single, and reached number 39 in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer took a break in activity. Lake used this time to focus on his family life, travel, and write and release music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melodymaker1977_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-melodymaker1977-33">[33]</a></sup> By then the band were <a href="/wiki/Tax_exile" title="Tax exile">tax exiles</a> and relocated to Switzerland, France, Canada and the Bahamas as they were restricted to two months stay in England a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melodymaker1977_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-melodymaker1977-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-express2016_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-express2016-34">[34]</a></sup> In March 1977 the band released <i><a href="/wiki/Works_Volume_1" title="Works Volume 1">Works Volume 1</a></i>, a double album featuring one side dedicated to each member. Lake wrote five acoustic songs with lyrical assistance from Sinfield, with a conscious effort not to record "just ballads" and attempt a wider variety of musical styles. He then incorporated orchestral overdubs to the songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melodymaker1977_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-melodymaker1977-33">[33]</a></sup> One of them, "C'est la Vie", was released as a single. Lake called the album the "beginning of the end" of the band, as he no longer produced their future albums, neither of which were a "really innovative record".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone2013_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone2013-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band split in 1979 following the unsuccessful album <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Beach" title="Love Beach">Love Beach</a></i>, an album the group were contractually obliged to record. The group reformed for a number of years in the mid-1990s before permanently disbanding barring a one-off gig in 2010 at London's <a href="/wiki/High_Voltage_Festival" title="High Voltage Festival">High Voltage Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roughguide_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-roughguide-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career_and_other_projects">Solo career and other projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solo career and other projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1975, while still a member of ELP, Lake achieved solo chart success when his single, "<a href="/wiki/I_Believe_in_Father_Christmas" title="I Believe in Father Christmas">I Believe in Father Christmas</a>", reached number two on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>. It has become a <a href="/wiki/Yule" title="Yule">Yuletide</a> perennial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In the UK, the single sold over 13,000 copies in two days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1976_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1976-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several months following the break-up of ELP in 1979, Lake began to write new songs and had "put down a tremendous amount of material" for his first solo album. He travelled to Los Angeles and worked with a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Session_musicians" title="Session musicians">session musicians</a> to develop his songs further, but he found a lack of personality in the music, though not at the fault of the performers. Lake realised he wished to play as part of a group, and began to assemble members of the Greg Lake Band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1981_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1981-15">[15]</a></sup> The result, <i><a href="/wiki/Greg_Lake_(album)" title="Greg Lake (album)">Greg Lake</a></i>, was released in September 1981 on <a href="/wiki/Chrysalis_Records" title="Chrysalis Records">Chrysalis Records</a>, which reached number 62 in the UK and the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_charts_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_charts-40">[40]</a></sup> Lake supported the album with a tour, of which their debut gig took place in August 1981 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_Festival" title="Reading Festival">Reading Festival</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gary_Moore" title="Gary Moore">Gary Moore</a> on guitars, <a href="/wiki/Ted_McKenna" title="Ted McKenna">Ted McKenna</a> on drums, <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Eyre" title="Tommy Eyre">Tommy Eyre</a> on keyboards, and Triss Margetts on bass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1981_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1981-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake's second solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Manoeuvres" title="Manoeuvres">Manoeuvres</a></i>, was released in July 1983. Later that year, he briefly joined the 1980s supergroup <a href="/wiki/Asia_(band)" title="Asia (band)">Asia</a>, replacing fellow King Crimson alumnus <a href="/wiki/John_Wetton" title="John Wetton">John Wetton</a>, and then co-formed <a href="/wiki/Emerson,_Lake_%26_Powell" title="Emerson, Lake &amp; Powell">Emerson, Lake &amp; Powell</a> with drummer <a href="/wiki/Cozy_Powell" title="Cozy Powell">Cozy Powell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roughguide_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-roughguide-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2001, Lake toured as a member of the seventh incarnation of <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr_%26_His_All-Starr_Band" title="Ringo Starr &amp; His All-Starr Band">Ringo Starr &amp; His All-Starr Band</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes2016_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimes2016-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2003, Lake played the bass on <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Real_Good_Looking_Boy" title="Real Good Looking Boy">Real Good Looking Boy</a>". The group's usual bassist, <a href="/wiki/Pino_Palladino" title="Pino Palladino">Pino Palladino</a>, was set to do it but he was touring during the time of recording, so Lake was asked instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone2013_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone2013-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2005, Lake toured Germany and the UK with his assembled group, the Greg Lake Band, which included <a href="/wiki/David_Arch" title="David Arch">David Arch</a> on keyboards, <a href="/wiki/Florian_Opahle" title="Florian Opahle">Florian Opahle</a> on guitar, Trevor Barry on bass, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brett_Morgan" title="Brett Morgan">Brett Morgan</a> on drums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2006, Lake played as a member of the supergroup <a href="/wiki/The_RD_Crusaders" title="The RD Crusaders">The RD Crusaders</a> in aid for charity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ok2005_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-ok2005-42">[42]</a></sup> Lake performed "<a href="/wiki/Karn_Evil_9" title="Karn Evil 9">Karn Evil 9</a>" with the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Siberian_Orchestra" title="Trans-Siberian Orchestra">Trans-Siberian Orchestra</a> at several shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He was a special guest on their album <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Castle" title="Night Castle">Night Castle</a></i> (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Lake and Emerson completed an acoustic world tour, performing ELP songs. The tour got off at a bad start following a backstage altercation between the two, but "we completed the tour and it was very happy. We actually ended up enjoying ourselves".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone2013_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone2013-31">[31]</a></sup> That July, Lake joined Emerson and Palmer for a one-off gig from Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer at the <a href="/wiki/High_Voltage_Festival" title="High Voltage Festival">High Voltage Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Park,_London" title="Victoria Park, London">Victoria Park, London</a>, to commemorate the band's fortieth anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grauniad_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-grauniad-45">[45]</a></sup> The concert was released on CD, DVD, and Blu-ray as <a href="/wiki/High_Voltage_(Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer_album)" title="High Voltage (Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer album)"><i>High Voltage</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It was the final performance by the group. Lake wished to continue touring, but claimed his band mates "didn't want to", thus ending such plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone2013_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone2013-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake continued to tour solo in the 2010s. His Songs of a Lifetime Tour began in 2012 which featured songs of his career and those by his favourite artists, including <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Kidd_%26_the_Pirates" title="Johnny Kidd &amp; the Pirates">Johnny Kidd &amp; the Pirates</a>. The tour ended in November of that year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-express2016_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-express2016-34">[34]</a></sup> and produced the live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_of_a_Lifetime" title="Songs of a Lifetime">Songs of a Lifetime</a></i> (2013).</p>
<p>On 9 January 2016, he was awarded an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary degree</a> in music and lyrics composition by Conservatorio Nicolini in <a href="/wiki/Piacenza" title="Piacenza">Piacenza</a>, Italy, the first degree awarded by the conservatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake spent several years writing his autobiography <i>Lucky Man</i>, originally planned to be published in 2012, and now due for release in September 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late 1974, Lake moved from a flat in Cornwall Gardens in <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a>, London, to a home near <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Berkshire" title="Windsor, Berkshire">Windsor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recordingworld1976_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-recordingworld1976-27">[27]</a></sup> Lake later lived in the <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Thames" title="Kingston upon Thames">Kingston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_London" title="Richmond, London">Richmond</a> areas of London with his wife Regina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-express2016_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-express2016-34">[34]</a></sup> The couple had one daughter, Natasha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ok2005_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-ok2005-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lake died in London on 7 December 2016, at the age of 69, after suffering from pancreatic cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytobit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytobit-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His manager announced the news on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, describing Lake's battle with the illness as "long and stubborn".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC08122016_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC08122016-25">[25]</a></sup> Numerous fellow musicians paid tribute, including <a href="/wiki/Rick_Wakeman" title="Rick Wakeman">Rick Wakeman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Hackett" title="Steve Hackett">Steve Hackett</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC08122016_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC08122016-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes2016_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-latimes2016-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Wetton" title="John Wetton">John Wetton</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teamrock2016_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-teamrock2016-50">[50]</a></sup> and ELP drummer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Palmer" title="Carl Palmer">Carl Palmer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teamrock2016_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-teamrock2016-50">[50]</a></sup> With Lake's death and that of <a href="/wiki/Keith_Emerson" title="Keith Emerson">Keith Emerson</a> earlier in 2016, Palmer is the last surviving member of the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teamrock2016_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-teamrock2016-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
