<p>A <b>container</b> is a basic <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> consisting of any device creating a partially or fully enclosed space that can be used to contain, store, and transport objects or materials. In <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a>, it includes "any receptacle or enclosure for holding a product used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packaging" title="Packaging">packaging</a> and shipping."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Things kept inside of a container are protected by being inside of its structure. The term is most frequently applied to devices made from materials that are <a href="/wiki/Durable_good" title="Durable good">durable</a> and at least partly <a href="/wiki/Stiffness" title="Stiffness">rigid</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Container&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Humans have used containers for at least 100,000 years, and possibly for millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamble_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gamble-4">[4]</a></sup> The first containers were probably invented for storing <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamble_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gamble-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> allowing early humans to preserve more of their food for a longer time, to carry it more easily, and to protect it from other animals. The development of <a href="/wiki/Food_storage_container" title="Food storage container">food storage containers</a> was "of immense importance to the evolving human populations", and "was a totally innovative behavior" not seen in other primates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The earliest containers were probably objects found in nature such as hollow <a href="/wiki/Gourd" title="Gourd">gourds</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> of which primitive examples have been found in cultures such as those of the <a href="/wiki/Tharu_people" title="Tharu people">Tharu people</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiian" title="Native Hawaiian">native Hawaiian</a> people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These were followed by woven <a href="/wiki/Basket" title="Basket">baskets</a>, carved <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>.</p>
<p>Containers thereafter continued to develop along with related advances in human technology, and with the development of new materials and new means of manufacture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PTS-10">[10]</a></sup> Early glass bottles were produced by the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a>; specimens of Phoenician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent">translucent</a> and transparent glass bottles have been found in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> generally varying in length from three to six inches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These Phoenician examples from the first millennium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ">BC</a> were thought to have been used to contain <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfume</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> learned glass-making from the Phoenicians and produced many extant examples of fine glass bottles, mostly relatively small. By the beginning of the eighteenth century, sizes for retail containers such as glass bottles had become standardized for their markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chains_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chains-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1810, Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Girard" title="Philippe de Girard">Philippe de Girard</a> came to London and used British merchant <a href="/wiki/Peter_Durand" title="Peter Durand">Peter Durand</a> as an agent to patent his own idea for a process for making <a href="/wiki/Tin_can" title="Tin can">tin cans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The canning concept was based on experimental food preservation work in glass containers the year before by the French inventor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholas_Appert" title="Nicholas Appert">Nicholas Appert</a>. Durand did not pursue food canning, but, in 1812, sold his patent to two Englishmen, <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Donkin" title="Bryan Donkin">Bryan Donkin</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Hall_(canner)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Hall (canner) (page does not exist)">John Hall</a>, who refined the process and product, and set up the world's first commercial canning factory on Southwark Park Road, London. By 1813 they were producing their first tin canned goods for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>.</p>
<p>For transportation of goods on a larger scale, larger containers remained a problem, as customs officials inspecting imports had to deal with a lack of standardization in this field, and because predominantly wooden containers in use well into the twentieth century were prone to leaking or breaking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chains_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chains-13">[13]</a></sup> The standardized steel <a href="/wiki/Shipping_container" title="Shipping container">shipping container</a> was developed in the 1950s, and quickly became ubiquitous.</p>
<p>The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> made it possible to design highly specialized containers and container arrangements, and also to make form-fitting labels for containers of unusual shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_characteristics">Modern characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Container&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of considerations go into the design of modern containers:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The product characteristics that create utility for a container go beyond just providing shock and moisture protection for the contents. A well-designed container will also exhibit ease of use, that is, it is easy for the worker to open or close, to insert or extract the contents, and to handle the container in shipment. In addition, a good container will have convenient and legible labeling locations, a shape that is conducive to efficient stacking and storing, and easy recycling at the end of its useful life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variety">Variety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Container&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Practical examples of containers include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">Ceramic</a> cylindrical vessels including
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">Amphoras</a>, made of clay</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">Bottles</a>, similar to a jar in being traditionally symmetrical about the axis perpendicular to its base and made of glass</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jar" title="Jar">Jars</a>, traditionally cylindrical and made of glass</li>
<li>storage big ceramic vessels, <a href="/wiki/Kvevri" title="Kvevri">Kvevri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pithos" title="Pithos">Pithos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolium" title="Dolium">Dolium</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>cylindrical vessels including
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barrel" title="Barrel">Barrels</a>, made of wooden staves bound by rope, wooden or metal hoops.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tin_can" title="Tin can">Cans</a>, traditionally cylindrical and <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet-metallic</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drum_(container)" title="Drum (container)">Drums</a>, similar to a can but definitely cylindrical and not necessarily metallic</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>rectilinear vessels including
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Box" title="Box">Boxes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crate" title="Crate">Crates</a>, a box or rectilinear exoskeleton, designed for hoisting or loading</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wooden_box" title="Wooden box">Wooden boxes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trolley_and_lift_van" title="Trolley and lift van">Lift-vans</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shipping_container" title="Shipping container">Shipping containers</a>, including
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrugated_box" title="Corrugated box">Corrugated boxes</a>, made of <a href="/wiki/Corrugated_fiberboard" title="Corrugated fiberboard">corrugated fiberboard</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Intermodal_container" title="Intermodal container">Intermodal containers</a>, a.k.a. ship container or cargo container
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit" title="Twenty-foot equivalent unit">Twenty-foot equivalent units</a>, an industry standard intermodal container size</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Intermediate_bulk_container" title="Intermediate bulk container">Intermediate bulk containers</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unit_load_device" title="Unit load device">Unit load devices</a>, similar to a crate</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flexible_intermediate_bulk_container" title="Flexible intermediate bulk container">Flexible intermediate bulk containers</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
