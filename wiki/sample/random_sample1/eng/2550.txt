<p><b>Greek numerals</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">system of writing numbers</a> using the letters of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a>. These alphabetic numerals are also known as <b>Ionic</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionian</a> numerals</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Milesian</a> numerals</b>, or <b><a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> numerals</b>. In modern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, they are still used for <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_number_(linguistics)" title="Ordinal number (linguistics)">ordinal numbers</a> and in contexts similar to those in which <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">Roman numerals</a> are still used elsewhere in the West. For ordinary <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_number_(linguistics)" title="Cardinal number (linguistics)">cardinal numbers</a>, however, Greece uses <a href="/wiki/Arabic_numerals" title="Arabic numerals">Arabic numerals</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_numerals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minoans" title="Minoans">Minoan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenaean_civilization" title="Mycenaean civilization">Mycenaean civilizations</a>' <a href="/wiki/Linear_A" title="Linear A">Linear A</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> alphabets used a different system, called <a href="/wiki/Aegean_numerals" title="Aegean numerals">Aegean numerals</a>, which included specialized symbols for numbers: <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐄇</span> = 1, <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐄐</span> = 10, <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐄙</span> = 100, <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐄢</span> = 1000, and <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐄫</span> = 10000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verdun_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verdun-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The present system probably developed around <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionia</a>. 19th-century classicists placed its development in the 3rd century BC, the occasion of its first widespread use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> More thorough modern archaeology has caused the date to be pushed back at least to the 5th century BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> a little before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athens_(city-state)" title="Athens (city-state)">Athens</a> abandoned its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Euclidean_alphabet" title="Pre-Euclidean alphabet">pre-Euclidean alphabet</a> in favor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_alphabet" title="Ionic alphabet">Miletus</a>'s in 402 BC, and it may predate that by a century or two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The present system uses the 24 letters adopted by <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> as well as three <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">Phoenician</a> and Ionic ones that were not carried over: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digamma_(letter)" title="Digamma (letter)">digamma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Koppa_(letter)" title="Koppa (letter)">koppa</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampi_(letter)" title="Sampi (letter)">sampi</a>. The position of those characters within the numbering system imply that the first two were still in use (or at least remembered as letters) while the third was not. The exact dating, particularly for sampi, is problematic since its uncommon value means the first attested representative near Miletus does not appear until the 2nd century BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and its use is unattested in Athens until the 2nd century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (In general, Athens resisted the use of the new numerals for the longest of any of the Greek states but had fully adopted them by AD c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 50</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heman_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-heman-2">[2]</a></sup>)</p>

<p>Greek numerals are <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimal</a>, based on powers of 10. The units from 1 to 9 are assigned to the first nine letters of the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_alphabet" title="Ionic alphabet">Ionic alphabet</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_(letter)" title="Alpha (letter)">alpha</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_(letter)" title="Theta (letter)">theta</a>. Instead of reusing these numbers to form multiples of the higher powers of ten, however, each multiple of ten from 10 to 90 was assigned its own separate letter from the next nine letters of the Ionic alphabet from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iota_(letter)" title="Iota (letter)">iota</a> to <a href="/wiki/Koppa_(letter)" title="Koppa (letter)">koppa</a>. Each multiple of one hundred from 100 to 900 was then assigned its own separate letter as well, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rho_(letter)" title="Rho (letter)">rho</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampi_(letter)" title="Sampi (letter)">sampi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> (The fact that this was <i>not</i> the traditional location of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampi_(letter)" title="Sampi (letter)">sampi</a> or its possible predecessor <a href="/wiki/San_(letter)" title="San (letter)">san</a> has led classicists to conclude that it was no longer in use even locally by the time the system was created.)</p>
<p>This alphabetic system operates on the additive principle in which the numeric values of the letters are added together to obtain the total. For example, 241 was represented as <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Sigma_classical.svg"><img alt="Greek Sigma classical.svg" data-file-height="80" data-file-width="65" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Greek_Sigma_classical.svg/13px-Greek_Sigma_classical.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Greek_Sigma_classical.svg/19px-Greek_Sigma_classical.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Greek_Sigma_classical.svg/26px-Greek_Sigma_classical.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Mu_classical.svg"><img alt="Greek Mu classical.svg" data-file-height="80" data-file-width="70" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Greek_Mu_classical.svg/14px-Greek_Mu_classical.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Greek_Mu_classical.svg/21px-Greek_Mu_classical.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Greek_Mu_classical.svg/28px-Greek_Mu_classical.svg.png 2x" width="14"/></a><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Alpha_classical.svg"><img alt="Greek Alpha classical.svg" data-file-height="80" data-file-width="70" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Greek_Alpha_classical.svg/14px-Greek_Alpha_classical.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Greek_Alpha_classical.svg/21px-Greek_Alpha_classical.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Greek_Alpha_classical.svg/28px-Greek_Alpha_classical.svg.png 2x" width="14"/></a> (200 + 40 + 1). (It was not always the case that the numbers ran from highest to lowest: a 4th-century BC inscription at Athens placed the units to the left of the tens. This practice continued in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> well into the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Greece" title="Roman Greece">Roman period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heman_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-heman-2">[2]</a></sup>) In ancient and medieval manuscripts, these numerals were eventually distinguished from letters using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overbar" title="Overbar">overbars</a>: <span style="text-decoration:overline;">α</span>, <span style="text-decoration:overline;">β</span>, <span style="text-decoration:overline;">γ</span>, etc. In medieval manuscripts of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Number_of_the_Beast" title="Number of the Beast">number of the Beast</a> 666 is written as <span style="text-decoration:overline;">χξς</span> (600 + 60 + 6). (Numbers larger than 1,000 reused the same letters but included various marks to note the change.)</p>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet#Letter_shapes" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> began with only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majuscule" title="Majuscule">majuscule</a> forms, surviving <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> manuscripts from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Egypt" title="Roman Egypt">Egypt</a> show that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uncial" title="Uncial">uncial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cursive" title="Cursive">cursive</a> <a href="/wiki/Greek_minuscule" title="Greek minuscule">minuscule</a> forms began early. These new letter forms sometimes replaced the former ones, especially in the case of the obscure numerals. The numeral for 6 changed several times. During antiquity, the original letter form of digamma (<a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg"><img alt="Greek Digamma oblique.svg" data-file-height="53" data-file-width="43" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg/13px-Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg/19px-Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg/26px-Greek_Digamma_oblique.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a>) came to be avoided in favor of a special numerical one (<a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Digamma_angular.svg"><img alt="Greek Digamma angular.svg" data-file-height="53" data-file-width="43" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Greek_Digamma_angular.svg/13px-Greek_Digamma_angular.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Greek_Digamma_angular.svg/19px-Greek_Digamma_angular.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Greek_Digamma_angular.svg/26px-Greek_Digamma_angular.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a>). <p>In <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">modern Greek</a>, a number of other changes have been made. Instead of extending an overbar over an entire number, the <i><b>keraia</b></i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">κεραία</span>, <small>lit.</small> "hornlike projection") is marked to its upper right, a development of the short marks formerly used for single numbers and fractions. The modern <i>keraia</i> is a symbol (<big>ʹ</big>) similar the <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accent</a> (´) but has its own <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> character as U+0374. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>'s father <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Macedon" title="Philip II of Macedon">Philip II of Macedon</a> is thus known as <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Φίλιππος Βʹ</span> in modern Greek. A lower left <i>keraia</i> (Unicode: U+0375, "Greek Lower Numeral Sign") is now standard for distinguishing thousands: 2015 is represented as ͵ΒΙΕʹ (2000 + 10 + 5).</p>
<p>The declining use of ligatures in the 20th century also means that stigma is frequently written as the separate letters ΣΤʹ, although a single <i>keraia</i> is used for the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The art of assigning Greek letters also being thought of as numerals and therefore giving words/names/phrases a numeric sum that has meaning through being connected to words/names/phrases of similar sum is called <a href="/wiki/Isopsephy" title="Isopsephy">isopsephy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gematria" title="Gematria">gematria</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_numbers">Higher numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_numerals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Higher numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his text <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sand_Reckoner" title="The Sand Reckoner">The Sand Reckoner</a></i>, the natural philosopher <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> gives an upper bound of the number of grains of sand required to fill the entire universe, using a contemporary estimation of its size. This would defy the then-held notion that it is impossible to name a number greater than that of the sand on a beach or on the entire world. In order to do that, he had to devise a <a href="/wiki/The_Sand_Reckoner#Naming_large_numbers" title="The Sand Reckoner">new numeral scheme</a> with much greater range.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zero">Zero</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_numerals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Zero">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P._Lund,_Inv._35a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="550" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/P._Lund%2C_Inv._35a.jpg/300px-P._Lund%2C_Inv._35a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/P._Lund%2C_Inv._35a.jpg/450px-P._Lund%2C_Inv._35a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/P._Lund%2C_Inv._35a.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p><span id="Hellenistic_zero"></span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomers</a> extended alphabetic Greek numerals into a <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">sexagesimal</a> <a href="/wiki/Positional_notation" title="Positional notation">positional</a> <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numbering system</a> by limiting each position to a maximum value of 50 + 9 and including a special symbol for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/0_(number)" title="0 (number)">zero</a>, which was also used alone like today's modern zero, more than as a simple placeholder. However, the positions were usually limited to the fractional part of a number (called <a href="/wiki/Minute" title="Minute">minutes</a>, seconds, thirds, fourths, etc.) — they were not used for the <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integral</a> part of a number. This system was probably adapted from <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_numerals" title="Babylonian numerals">Babylonian numerals</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">Hipparchus</a> c. 140 BC. It was then used by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> (c. 140), <a href="/wiki/Theon_of_Alexandria" title="Theon of Alexandria">Theon</a> (c. 380) and Theon's daughter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypatia_of_Alexandria" title="Hypatia of Alexandria">Hypatia</a> (murdered 415).</p>

<p>Each number in the first column, labeled περιφερειῶν, is the number of degrees of arc on a circle. Each number in the second column, labeled ευθειῶν, is the length of the corresponding chord of the circle, when the diameter is 120. Thus πδ represents an 84° arc, and the ∠' after it means one-half, so that πδ∠' means 84.5°. In the next column we see π μα γ, meaning 80 + 41/60 + 3/60<sup>2</sup>. That is the length of the chord corresponding to an arc of 84.5° when the diameter of the circle is 120. The next column, labeled ὲξηκοστῶν, for "sixtieths", is the number to be added to the chord length for each 1° increase in the arc, over the span of the next 12°. Thus that last column was used for <a href="/wiki/Linear_interpolation" title="Linear interpolation">linear interpolation</a>.</p>
<p>The Greek sexagesimal placeholder or zero symbol changed over time. The symbol used on <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyri</a> during the second century was a very small circle with an overbar several diameters long, terminated or not at both ends in various ways. Later, the overbar shortened to only one diameter, similar to the modern <i>o</i> macron (ō) which was still being used in late medieval Arabic manuscripts whenever alphabetic numerals were used. But the overbar was omitted in <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> manuscripts, leaving a bare <i>ο</i> (omicron). This gradual change from an invented symbol to <i>ο</i> does not support the hypothesis that the latter was the initial of ουδέν meaning "nothing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Note that the letter <i>ο</i> was still used with its original numerical value of 70; however, there was no ambiguity, as 70 could not appear in the fractional part of a number, and zero was usually omitted when it was the integer.</p>
<p>Some of Ptolemy's true zeros appeared in the first line of each of his eclipse tables, where they were a measure of the angular separation between the center of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and either the center of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> (for <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipses</a>) or the center of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s shadow (for <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipses</a>). All of these zeros took the form 0 | 0 0, where Ptolemy actually used three of the symbols described in the previous paragraph. The vertical bar (|) indicates that the integral part on the left was in a separate column labeled in the headings of his tables as <i>digits</i> (of five arc-minutes each), whereas the fractional part was in the next column labeled <i>minute of immersion</i>, meaning sixtieths (and thirty-six-hundredths) of a digit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
