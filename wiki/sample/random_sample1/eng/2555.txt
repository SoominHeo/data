<p><b>Hellenic</b> is the branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_language_family" title="Indo-European language family">Indo-European language family</a> whose principal member is <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In most classifications, Hellenic consists of Greek alone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JosephPhilippaki_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-JosephPhilippaki-5">[5]</a></sup> but some linguists use the term Hellenic to refer to a group consisting of Greek proper and other varieties thought to be related but different enough to be separate languages, either among ancient neighbouring languages<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BJoseph_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BJoseph-6">[6]</a></sup> or among modern spoken dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_and_ancient_Macedonian">Greek and ancient Macedonian</span></h2>
<p>A family under the name "Hellenic" has been suggested to group together Greek proper and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonian_language" title="Ancient Macedonian language">ancient Macedonian language</a>, which is barely attested and whose degree of relatedness to Greek is not well known. The suggestion of a "Hellenic" group with two branches, in this context, represents the idea that Macedonian was not simply a dialect within Greek but a "sibling language" outside the group of Greek varieties proper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BJoseph_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BJoseph-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other approaches include Macedonian as a dialect of Greek proper or as an unclassified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleo-Balkan_language" title="Paleo-Balkan language">Paleo-Balkan language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Hellenic_languages">Modern Hellenic languages</span></h2>
<p>In addition, some linguists use the term "Hellenic" to refer to <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">modern Greek</a> in a narrow sense together with certain other, divergent modern varieties deemed separate languages on the basis of a lack of <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosely_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mosely-10">[10]</a></sup> Separate language status is most often posited for <a href="/wiki/Tsakonian_language" title="Tsakonian language">Tsakonian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosely_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mosely-10">[10]</a></sup> which is thought to be uniquely a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek" title="Doric Greek">Doric</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">Attic Greek</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Greek" title="Pontic Greek">Pontic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cappadocian_Greek" title="Cappadocian Greek">Cappadocian Greek</a> of Anatolia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Griko" title="Griko">Griko</a> or Italiot varieties of southern Italy are also not readily intelligible to speakers of standard Greek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Separate status is sometimes also argued for <a href="/wiki/Cypriot_Greek" title="Cypriot Greek">Cypriot</a>, though this is not as easily justified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roelcke_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-roelcke-13">[13]</a></sup> In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Yevanic_language" title="Yevanic language">Yevanic</a> (Jewish Greek) is mutually intelligible with standard Greek but is sometimes considered a separate language for ethnic and cultural reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roelcke_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-roelcke-13">[13]</a></sup> Greek linguistics traditionally treats all of these as dialects of a single language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Browning-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<p>Hellenic constitutes a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a>. The ancient languages that might have been most closely related to it, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonian_language" title="Ancient Macedonian language">ancient Macedonian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_language" title="Phrygian language">Phrygian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> are not well enough documented to permit detailed comparison. Among Indo-European branches with living descendants, Greek is often argued to have the closest genetic ties with <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> (see also <a href="/wiki/Graeco-Armenian" title="Graeco-Armenian">Graeco-Armenian</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">Indo-Iranian languages</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Graeco-Aryan" title="Graeco-Aryan">Graeco-Aryan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
