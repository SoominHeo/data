<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Greek fire</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Greek fire</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">incendiary</a> weapon developed <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 672 and used by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire</a>. The Byzantines typically used it in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_battles" title="Naval battles">naval battles</a> to great effect, as it could continue burning while floating on water. It provided a technological advantage and was responsible for many key Byzantine military victories, most notably the salvation of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> from two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople" title="Siege of Constantinople">sieges</a>, thus securing the Empire's survival.</p>
<p>The impression made by Greek fire on the western European <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusaders</a> was such that the name was applied to any sort of incendiary weapon, including those used by Arabs, the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a>. These, however, were different mixtures and not the Byzantine formula, which was a closely guarded <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">state secret</a>. Byzantine use of incendiary mixtures was distinguished by the use of pressurized nozzles or <i>siph≈çn</i> to project the liquid onto the enemy.</p>
<p>Although usage of the term "Greek fire" has been general in English and most other languages since the Crusades, original Byzantine sources called the substance a variety of names, such as "sea fire" (<a href="/wiki/Medieval_Greek" title="Medieval Greek">Medieval Greek</a>: œÄ·ø¶œÅ Œ∏Œ±ŒªŒ¨œÉœÉŒπŒøŒΩ <i>p≈∑r thal√°ssion</i>), "Roman fire" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">œÄ·ø¶œÅ ·ø•œâŒºŒ±œäŒ∫œåŒΩ</span> <i>p≈∑r rh≈çma√Øk√≥n</i>), "war fire" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">œÄŒøŒªŒµŒºŒπŒ∫·Ω∏ŒΩ œÄ·ø¶œÅ</span> <i>polemik√≤n p≈∑r</i>), "liquid fire" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">·ΩëŒ≥œÅ·Ω∏ŒΩ œÄ·ø¶œÅ</span> <i>hygr√≤n p≈∑r</i>), "sticky fire" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">œÄ·ø¶œÅ Œ∫ŒøŒªŒªŒ∑œÑŒπŒ∫œåŒΩ</span> <i>p≈∑r kollƒìtik√≥n</i>) or "manufactured fire" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">œÄ·ø¶œÅ œÉŒ∫ŒµœÖŒ±œÉœÑœåŒΩ</span> <i>p≈∑r skeuast√≥n</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006608.E2.80.93609_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006608.E2.80.93609-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195983_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195983-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The composition of Greek fire is unknown. It remains a matter of speculation and debate, with various proposals including combinations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pine_resin" title="Pine resin">pine resin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">quicklime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphide" title="Calcium phosphide">calcium phosphide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Niter" title="Niter">niter</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Early_thermal_weapons" title="Early thermal weapons">Early thermal weapons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_navy" title="Byzantine navy">Byzantine navy</a></div>
<p>Incendiary and flaming weapons were used in warfare for centuries prior to the invention of Greek fire. They included a number of sulfur-, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>-, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a>-based mixtures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeicester197175_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeicester197175-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrosby200288.E2.80.9389_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrosby200288.E2.80.9389-4">[4]</a></sup> Incendiary arrows and pots containing combustible substances were used as early as the 9th century BC by the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Assyrians</a> and were extensively used in the Greco-Roman world as well.</p>
<p>Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">Thucydides</a> mentions that in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Delium" title="Battle of Delium">siege of Delium</a> in 424 BC a long tube on wheels was used which blew flames forward using a large bellows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington19991.E2.80.935_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington19991.E2.80.935-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195970.E2.80.9374_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195970.E2.80.9374-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In naval warfare, the Byzantine Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anastasius_I_(emperor)" title="Anastasius I (emperor)">Anastasius I</a> (r. 491‚Äì518) is recorded by chronicler <a href="/wiki/John_Malalas" title="John Malalas">John Malalas</a> to have been advised by a philosopher from <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> called Proclus to use sulfur to burn the ships of Vitalianus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington19995_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington19995-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>At that time Kallinikos, an artificer from Heliopolis, fled to the Romans. He had devised a sea fire which ignited the Arab ships and burned them with all hands. Thus it was that the Romans returned with victory and discovered the sea fire.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>‚Äî‚ÄâChronicle of <a href="/wiki/Theophanes_the_Confessor" title="Theophanes the Confessor">Theophanes the Confessor</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Anno_Mundi" title="Anno Mundi">Anno Mundi</a></i> 6165<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETheophanesTurtledove198253_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETheophanesTurtledove198253-9">[9]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Greek fire proper, however, was developed in c. 672 and is ascribed by the chronicler <a href="/wiki/Theophanes_the_Confessor" title="Theophanes the Confessor">Theophanes</a> to Kallinikos (Latinized Callinicus), an architect from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Syria)" title="Heliopolis (Syria)">Heliopolis</a> in the former <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phoenice_(Roman_province)" title="Phoenice (Roman province)">Phoenice</a>, by then overrun by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">Muslim conquests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006607.E2.80.93609_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006607.E2.80.93609-10">[10]</a></sup> The accuracy and exact chronology of this account is open to question: Theophanes reports the use of fire-carrying and <i>siph≈çn</i>-equipped ships by the Byzantines a couple of years before the supposed arrival of Kallinikos at Constantinople.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETheophanesTurtledove198252_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETheophanesTurtledove198252-11">[11]</a></sup> If this is not due to chronological confusion of the events of the siege, it may suggest that Kallinikos merely introduced an improved version of an established weapon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992657_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992657-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006608_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006608-13">[13]</a></sup> The historian <a href="/wiki/J._R._Partington" title="J. R. Partington">James Partington</a> further thinks it likely that Greek fire was not in fact the creation of any single person but "invented by chemists in Constantinople who had inherited the discoveries of the <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> chemical school".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington199912.E2.80.9313_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington199912.E2.80.9313-14">[14]</a></sup> Indeed, the 11th-century chronicler <a href="/wiki/George_Kedrenos" title="George Kedrenos">George Kedrenos</a> records that Kallinikos came from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Heliopolis (Ancient Egypt)">Heliopolis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, but most scholars reject this as an error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195980_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195980-15">[15]</a></sup> Kedrenos also records the story, considered rather implausible by modern scholars, that Kallinikos' descendants, a family called <i>Lampros</i>, "brilliant", kept the secret of the fire's manufacture and continued doing so to Kedrenos' time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006608_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006608-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kallinikos' development of Greek fire came at a critical moment in the Byzantine Empire's history: weakened by its long <a href="/wiki/Roman%E2%80%93Persian_Wars" title="Roman‚ÄìPersian Wars">wars</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanid_Persia" title="Sassanid Persia">Sassanid Persia</a>, the Byzantines had been unable to effectively resist the onslaught of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">Muslim conquests</a>. Within a generation, Syria, Palestine, and Egypt had fallen to the Arabs, who in c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">‚Äâ672</span> set out to conquer the imperial capital of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. Greek fire was used to great effect against the Muslim fleets, helping to repel the Muslims at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(674%E2%80%93678)" title="Siege of Constantinople (674‚Äì678)">first</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(718)" title="Siege of Constantinople (718)">second</a> Arab sieges of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200626.E2.80.9327.2C_31.E2.80.9332_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200626.E2.80.9327.2C_31.E2.80.9332-16">[16]</a></sup> Records of its use in later naval battles against the <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">Saracens</a> are more sporadic, but it did secure a number of victories, especially in the phase of Byzantine expansion in the late 9th and early 10th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200661.E2.80.9362.2C_72_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200661.E2.80.9362.2C_72-17">[17]</a></sup> Utilisation of the substance was prominent in Byzantine civil wars, chiefly the revolt of the thematic fleets in 727 and the large-scale rebellion led by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Slav" title="Thomas the Slav">Thomas the Slav</a> in 821‚Äì823. In both cases, the rebel fleets were defeated by the Constantinopolitan Imperial Fleet through the use of Greek fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200632.2C_46.2C_73_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200632.2C_46.2C_73-18">[18]</a></sup> The Byzantines also used the weapon to devastating effect against the various <a href="/wiki/Rus%27_people" title="Rus' people">Rus'</a> raids on the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosporus</a>, especially those of <a href="/wiki/Rus%27%E2%80%93Byzantine_War_(941)" title="Rus'‚ÄìByzantine War (941)">941</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rus%27%E2%80%93Byzantine_War_(1043)" title="Rus'‚ÄìByzantine War (1043)">1043</a>, as well as during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rus%27%E2%80%93Byzantine_War_(970%E2%80%93971)" title="Rus'‚ÄìByzantine War (970‚Äì971)">Bulgarian war of 970‚Äì971</a>, when the fire-carrying Byzantine ships blockaded the Danube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200686.2C_189_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys200686.2C_189-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The importance placed on Greek fire during the Empire's struggle against the Arabs would lead to its discovery being ascribed to divine intervention. The Emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantine_VII" title="Constantine VII">Constantine Porphyrogennetos</a> (r. 945‚Äì959), in his book <i><a href="/wiki/De_Administrando_Imperio" title="De Administrando Imperio">De Administrando Imperio</a></i>, admonishes his son and heir, <a href="/wiki/Romanos_II" title="Romanos II">Romanos II</a> (r. 959‚Äì963), to never reveal the secrets of its composition, as it was "shown and revealed by an angel to the great and holy first Christian emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine</a>" and that the angel bound him "not to prepare this fire but for Christians, and only in the imperial city". As a warning, he adds that one official, who was bribed into handing some of it over to the Empire's enemies, was struck down by a "flame from heaven" as he was about to enter a church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoravcsikJenkins196768.E2.80.9371_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoravcsikJenkins196768.E2.80.9371-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195982_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195982-21">[21]</a></sup> As the latter incident demonstrates, the Byzantines could not avoid capture of their precious secret weapon: the Arabs captured at least one fireship intact in 827, and the Bulgars captured several <i>siph≈çns</i> and much of the substance itself in 812/814. This, however, was apparently not enough to allow their enemies to copy it (see <a href="#General_characteristics">below</a>). The Arabs, for instance, employed a variety of incendiary substances similar to the Byzantine weapon, but they were never able to copy the Byzantine method of deployment by <i>siph≈çn</i>, and used catapults and grenades instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006609.E2.80.93611_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006609.E2.80.93611-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992660.2C_663.E2.80.93664_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992660.2C_663.E2.80.93664-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greek fire continued to be mentioned during the 12th century, and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Komnene" title="Anna Komnene">Anna Komnene</a> gives a vivid description of its use in a naval battle against the <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisans</a> in 1099.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006110_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006110-24">[24]</a></sup> However, although the use of hastily improvised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fireships" title="Fireships">fireships</a> is mentioned during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(1203)" title="Siege of Constantinople (1203)">1203 siege</a> of Constantinople by the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a>, no report confirms the use of the actual Greek fire. This might be because of the general disarmament of the Empire in the 20 years leading up to the sacking, or because the Byzantines had lost access to the areas where the primary ingredients were to be found, or even perhaps because the secret had been lost over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006630.E2.80.93631_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006630.E2.80.93631-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006316_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006316-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Records of a 13th-century event in which "Greek fire" was used by the Saracens against the Crusaders can be read through the Memoirs of the Lord of Joinville during the <a href="/wiki/Seventh_Crusade" title="Seventh Crusade">Seventh Crusade</a>. One description of the memoir says "the tail of fire that trailed behind it was as big as a great spear; and it made such a noise as it came, that it sounded like the thunder of heaven. It looked like a dragon flying through the air. Such a bright light did it cast, that one could see all over the camp as though it were day, by reason of the great mass of fire, and the brilliance of the light that it shed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, it is reported that an <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenian</a> by the name of Kavafian approached the government of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> with a new type of Greek fire he claimed to have developed. Kavafian refused to reveal its composition when asked by the government, insisting that he be placed in command of its use during naval engagements. Not long after this, he was poisoned by imperial authorities, without their ever having found out his secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>As Constantine Porphyrogennetos' warnings show, the ingredients and the processes of manufacture and deployment of Greek fire were carefully guarded military secrets. So strict was the secrecy that the composition of Greek fire was lost forever and remains a source of speculation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006290_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006290-29">[29]</a></sup> Consequently, the "mystery" of the formula has long dominated the research into Greek fire. Despite this almost exclusive focus, however, Greek fire is best understood as a complete weapon system of many components, all of which were needed to operate together to render it effective. This comprised not only the formula of its composition, but also the specialized <a href="/wiki/Dromon" title="Dromon">dromon</a> ships that carried it into battle, the device used to prepare the substance by heating and pressurizing it, the <i>siph≈çn</i> projecting it; and the special training of the <i>siph≈çnarioi</i> who used it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992660.2C_663_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992660.2C_663-30">[30]</a></sup> Knowledge of the whole system was highly <a href="/wiki/Compartmentalization_(information_security)" title="Compartmentalization (information security)">compartmentalised</a>, with operators and technicians aware of the secrets of only one component, ensuring that no enemy could gain knowledge of it in its entirety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992663.E2.80.93664_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992663.E2.80.93664-31">[31]</a></sup> This accounts for the fact that when the <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarians</a> took <a href="/wiki/Nesebar" title="Nesebar">Mesembria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debeltos" title="Debeltos">Debeltos</a> in 814, they captured 36 <i>siph≈çns</i> and even quantities of the substance itself,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETheophanesTurtledove1982178_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETheophanesTurtledove1982178-32">[32]</a></sup> but were unable to make any use of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992663_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992663-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006609_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006609-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The information available on Greek fire is exclusively indirect, based on references in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_military_manuals" title="Byzantine military manuals">Byzantine military manuals</a> and a number of secondary historical sources such as <a href="/wiki/Anna_Komnene" title="Anna Komnene">Anna Komnene</a> and Western European chroniclers, which are often inaccurate. In her <i><a href="/wiki/Alexiad" title="Alexiad">Alexiad</a></i>, Anna Komnene provides a description of an incendiary weapon, which was used by the Byzantine garrison of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyrrhachium" title="Dyrrhachium">Dyrrhachium</a> in 1108 against the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>. It is often regarded as an at least partial "recipe" for Greek fire:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington199919.2C_29_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington199919.2C_29-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197364_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197364-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>This fire is made by the following arts. From the pine and the certain such evergreen trees inflammable resin is collected. This is rubbed with sulfur and put into tubes of reed, and is blown by men using it with violent and continuous breath. Then in this manner it meets the fire on the tip and catches light and falls like a fiery whirlwind on the faces of the enemies.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the same time, the reports by Western chroniclers of the famed <i>ignis graecus</i> are largely unreliable, since they apply the name to any and all sorts of incendiary substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006290_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006290-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In attempting to reconstruct the Greek fire system, the concrete evidence, as it emerges from the contemporary literary references, provides the following characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li>It burned on water, and, according to some interpretations, was ignited by water. In addition, as numerous writers testify, it could be extinguished only by a few substances, such as sand (which deprived it of oxygen), strong vinegar, or old urine, presumably by some sort of chemical reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>It was a liquid substance, and not some sort of projectile, as verified both by descriptions and the very name "liquid fire".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>At sea, it was usually ejected from a <i>siph≈çn</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363-39">[39]</a></sup> although earthenware pots or grenades filled with it or similar substances were also used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington199914_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington199914-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>The discharge of Greek fire was accompanied by "thunder" and "much smoke".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992657.E2.80.93658-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECheronis1937362.E2.80.93363-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_on_composition">Theories on composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theories on composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first and, for a long time, most popular theory regarding the composition of Greek fire held that its chief ingredient was <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">saltpeter</a>, making it an early form of <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197792_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197792-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197369.E2.80.9370_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197369.E2.80.9370-44">[44]</a></sup> This argument was based on the "thunder and smoke" description, as well as on the distance the flame could be projected from the <i>siph≈çn</i>, which suggested an explosive discharge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992659_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992659-45">[45]</a></sup> From the times of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Vossius" title="Isaac Vossius">Isaac Vossius</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195983_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195983-2">[2]</a></sup> several scholars adhered to this position, most notably the so-called "French school" during the 19th century, which included chemist <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot" title="Marcellin Berthelot">Marcellin Berthelot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992658.E2.80.93659_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992658.E2.80.93659-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197369_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197369-47">[47]</a></sup> This view has been rejected since, as saltpeter does not appear to have been used in warfare in Europe or the Middle East before the 13th century, and is absent from the accounts of the Muslim writers‚Äîthe <a href="/wiki/Alchemy_and_chemistry_in_medieval_Islam" title="Alchemy and chemistry in medieval Islam">foremost chemists</a> of the early medieval world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEal-Hassan200141.E2.80.9383_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEal-Hassan200141.E2.80.9383-48">[48]</a></sup>‚Äîbefore the same period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington199921.E2.80.9322_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington199921.E2.80.9322-49">[49]</a></sup> In addition, the nature of the proposed mixture would have been radically different from the <i>siph≈çn</i>-projected substance described by Byzantine sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195983.E2.80.9384_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195983.E2.80.9384-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second view, based on the fact that Greek fire was inextinguishable by water (some sources suggest that water intensified the flames) suggested that its destructive power was the result of the explosive reaction between water and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quicklime" title="Quicklime">quicklime</a>. Although quicklime was certainly known and used by the Byzantines and the Arabs in warfare,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington19996.E2.80.9310.2C_14_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington19996.E2.80.9310.2C_14-51">[51]</a></sup> the theory is refuted by literary and empirical evidence. A quicklime-based substance would have to come in contact with water to ignite, while Emperor Leo's <i><a href="/wiki/Tactica_of_Emperor_Leo_VI_the_Wise" title="Tactica of Emperor Leo VI the Wise">Tactica</a></i> indicate that Greek fire was often poured directly on the decks of enemy ships,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> although admittedly, decks were kept wet due to lack of sealants. Likewise, Leo describes the use of grenades,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> which further reinforces the view that contact with water was not necessary for the substance's ignition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992660_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992660-54">[54]</a></sup> Furthermore, C. Zenghelis pointed out that, based on experiments, the actual result of the water-quicklime reaction would be negligible in the open sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZenghelis1932270_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZenghelis1932270-55">[55]</a></sup> Another similar proposition suggested that Kallinikos had in fact discovered <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphide" title="Calcium phosphide">calcium phosphide</a>, which can be made by boiling bones in urine within a sealed vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> On contact with water, calcium phosphide releases <a href="/wiki/Phosphine" title="Phosphine">phosphine</a>, which ignites spontaneously. However, extensive experiments with it also failed to reproduce the described intensity of Greek fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECheronis1937363_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECheronis1937363-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197370_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197370-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the presence of either quicklime or saltpeter in the mixture cannot be entirely excluded, they were consequently not the primary ingredient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197370_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197370-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoland1992659_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoland1992659-45">[45]</a></sup> Most modern scholars agree that Greek fire was based on <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, either crude or refined; comparable to modern <a href="/wiki/Napalm" title="Napalm">napalm</a>. The Byzantines had easy access to crude oil from the naturally occurring wells around the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> (e.g., the wells around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tmutorakan" title="Tmutorakan">Tmutorakan</a> noted by Constantine Porphyrogennetos) or in various locations throughout the Middle East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197792_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197792-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington19994_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington19994-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195982.E2.80.9384_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195982.E2.80.9384-60">[60]</a></sup> An alternate name for Greek fire was "<a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Median</a> fire" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ŒºŒ∑Œ¥ŒπŒ∫·Ω∏ŒΩ œÄ·ø¶œÅ</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195983_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195983-2">[2]</a></sup> and the 6th-century historian <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>, records that crude oil, called "<a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>" (in Greek: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ŒΩŒ¨œÜŒ∏Œ±</span> <i>naphtha</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">Old Persian</a> <span lang="peo" xml:lang="peo"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%F0%90%8E%B4%F0%90%8E%B3%F0%90%8E%AB" title="wikt:êé¥êé≥êé´">êé¥êé≥êé´</a></span> <i>naft</i>) by the Persians, was known to the Greeks as "Median oil" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ŒºŒ∑Œ¥ŒπŒ∫·Ω∏ŒΩ ·ºîŒªŒ±ŒπŒøŒΩ</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> This seems to corroborate the use of naphtha as a basic ingredient of Greek fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197362_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197362-62">[62]</a></sup> Naphtha was also used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasids" title="Abbasids">Abbasids</a> in the 9th century, with special troops, the <i>naffƒÅ·π≠≈´n</i>, who wore thick protective suits and used small copper vessels containing burning oil, which they threw onto the enemy troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPartington199922_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPartington199922-63">[63]</a></sup> There is also a surviving 9th Century <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> text, preserved at <a href="/wiki/Wolfenb%C3%BCttel" title="Wolfenb√ºttel">Wolfenb√ºttel</a> in Germany, which mentions the ingredients of what appears to be Greek fire and the operation of the <i>siph≈çn</i>s used to project it. Although the text contains some inaccuracies, it clearly identifies the main component as naphtha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195983_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195983-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006614.E2.80.93616_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006614.E2.80.93616-64">[64]</a></sup> Resins were probably added as a thickener (the <i><a href="/wiki/Praecepta_Militaria" title="Praecepta Militaria">Praecepta Militaria</a></i> refer to the substance as <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">œÄ·ø¶œÅ Œ∫ŒøŒªŒªŒ∑œÑŒπŒ∫œåŒΩ</span>, "sticky fire"), and to increase the duration and intensity of the flame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006310_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006310-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006618_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006618-66">[66]</a></sup> A modern theoretical concoction included the use of <a href="/wiki/Pine_tar" title="Pine tar">pine tar</a> and animal fat along with other ingredients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-video_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-video-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 12th Century treatise prepared by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mardi_bin_Ali_al-Tarsusi" title="Mardi bin Ali al-Tarsusi">Mardi bin Ali al-Tarsusi</a> for <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a> records an Arab version of Greek fire, called <i>naft</i>, which also had a petroleum base, with sulfur and various resins added. Any direct relation with the Byzantine formula is unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006610.E2.80.93611_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006610.E2.80.93611-68">[68]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Cortese" title="Isabella Cortese">Italian</a> recipe from the 16th century has been recorded for recreational use; it includes coal from a willow tree, alcohol, incense, sulfur, wool and camphor as well as two undetermined components (burning salt and <i>pegola</i>), the concoction was guaranteed to "burn under water" and to be "beautiful".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_deployment">Methods of deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methods of deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire,_medieval_illumination.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1392" data-file-width="2000" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire%2C_medieval_illumination.jpg/220px-Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire%2C_medieval_illumination.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire%2C_medieval_illumination.jpg/330px-Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire%2C_medieval_illumination.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire%2C_medieval_illumination.jpg/440px-Hand-siphon_for_Greek_fire%2C_medieval_illumination.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

</div>
<p>The chief method of deployment of Greek fire, which sets it apart from similar substances, was its projection through a tube (<i>siph≈çn</i>), for use aboard ships or in sieges. Portable projectors (<i>cheirosiph≈çnes</i>) were also invented, reputedly by Emperor Leo VI. The <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_military_manuals" title="Byzantine military manuals">Byzantine military manuals</a> also mention that jars (<i>chytrai</i> or <i>tzykalia</i>) filled with Greek fire and <a href="/wiki/Caltrop" title="Caltrop">caltrops</a> wrapped with <a href="/wiki/Tow" title="Tow">tow</a> and soaked in the substance were thrown by catapults, while pivoting <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a> (<i>gerania</i>) were employed to pour it upon enemy ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006378.E2.80.93379.2C_609_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006378.E2.80.93379.2C_609-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes195986.E2.80.9387_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes195986.E2.80.9387-71">[71]</a></sup> The <i>cheirosiph≈çnes</i> especially were prescribed for use at land and in sieges, both against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_machines" title="Siege machines">siege machines</a> and against defenders on the walls, by several 10th-century military authors, and their use is depicted in the <i><a href="/wiki/Poliorcetica" title="Poliorcetica">Poliorcetica</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Byzantium" title="Hero of Byzantium">Hero of Byzantium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617.E2.80.93619_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617.E2.80.93619-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006295_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006295-73">[73]</a></sup> The Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Dromon" title="Dromon">dromons</a> usually had a <i>siph≈çn</i> installed on their prow under the forecastle, but additional devices could also on occasion be placed elsewhere on the ship. Thus in 941, when the Byzantines were facing the vastly more numerous Rus' fleet, <i>siph≈çn</i>s were placed also amidships and even astern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006203.2C_618_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006203.2C_618-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projectors">Projectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Projectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of tubular projectors (œÉŒØœÜœâŒΩ, <i>siph≈çn</i>) is amply attested in the contemporary sources. Anna Komnene gives this account of beast-shaped Greek fire projectors being mounted to the bow of warships:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawes1928292_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawes1928292-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>As he [the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Alexios_I_Komnenos" title="Alexios I Komnenos">Alexios I</a>] knew that the <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisans</a> were skilled in sea warfare and dreaded a battle with them, on the prow of each ship he had a head fixed of a lion or other land-animal, made in brass or iron with the mouth open and then gilded over, so that their mere aspect was terrifying. And the fire which was to be directed against the enemy through tubes he made to pass through the mouths of the beasts, so that it seemed as if the lions and the other similar monsters were vomiting the fire.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Some sources provide more information on the composition and function of the whole mechanism. The Wolfenb√ºttel manuscript in particular provides the following description:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006614.E2.80.93616_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006614.E2.80.93616-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>...having built a furnace right at the front of the ship, they set on it a copper vessel full of these things, having put fire underneath. And one of them, having made a bronze tube similar to that which the rustics call a <i>squitiatoria</i>, "squirt", with which boys play, they spray [it] at the enemy.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another, possibly first-hand, account of the use of Greek fire comes from the 11th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Yngvars_saga_v%C3%AD%C3%B0f%C3%B6rla" title="Yngvars saga v√≠√∞f√∂rla">Yngvars saga v√≠√∞f√∂rla</a></i>, in which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> <a href="/wiki/Ingvar_the_Far-Travelled" title="Ingvar the Far-Travelled">Ingvar the Far-Travelled</a> faces ships equipped with Greek fire weapons:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006616.E2.80.93617_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006616.E2.80.93617-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>[They] began blowing with smiths‚Äô bellows at a furnace in which there was fire and there came from it a great din. There stood there also a brass [or bronze] tube and from it flew much fire against one ship, and it burned up in a short time so that all of it became white ashes...</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The account, albeit embellished, corresponds with many of the characteristics of Greek fire known from other sources, such as a loud roar that accompanied its discharge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197372_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis_Davidson197372-77">[77]</a></sup> These two texts are also the only two sources that explicitly mention that the substance was heated over a furnace before being discharged; although the validity of this information is open to question, modern reconstructions have relied upon them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006628.E2.80.93629_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006628.E2.80.93629-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006315_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006315-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="500" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg/220px-Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg/330px-Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg/440px-Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Fire_mechanism_after_Haldon_and_Byrne.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Proposed reconstruction of the Greek fire mechanism by Haldon and Byrne</div>

<p>Based on these descriptions and the Byzantine sources, John Haldon and Maurice Byrne designed a hypothetical apparatus as consisting of three main components: a bronze pump, which was used to pressurize the oil; a brazier, used to heat the oil (œÄœÅœåœÄœÖœÅŒøŒΩ, <i>propyron</i>, "pre-heater"); and the nozzle, which was covered in bronze and mounted on a swivel (œÉœÑœÅŒµœÄœÑœåŒΩ, <i>strepton</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197793_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197793-80">[80]</a></sup> The brazier, burning a match of linen or flax that produced intense heat and the characteristic thick smoke, was used to heat oil and the other ingredients in an airtight tank above it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197794_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197794-81">[81]</a></sup> a process that also helped to dissolve the resins into a fluid mixture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006310_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldon2006310-65">[65]</a></sup> The substance was pressurized by the heat and the usage of a force pump. After it had reached the proper pressure, a valve connecting the tank with the swivel was opened and the mixture was discharged from its end, being ignited at its mouth by some source of flame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197795_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197795-82">[82]</a></sup> The intense heat of the flame made necessary the presence of heat shields made of iron (Œ≤ŒøœÖŒ∫œåŒªŒπŒ±, <i>boukolia</i>), which are attested in the fleet inventories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006624.E2.80.93626_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006624.E2.80.93626-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The process of operating Haldon and Byrne's design was fraught with danger, as the mounting pressure could easily make the heated oil tank explode, a flaw which was not recorded as a problem with the historical fire weapon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197796_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197796-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006627.E2.80.93628_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006627.E2.80.93628-85">[85]</a></sup> In the experiments conducted by Haldon in 2002 for the episode "Fireship" of the television series <i>Machines Times Forgot</i>, even modern welding techniques failed to secure adequate insulation of the bronze tank under pressure. This led to the relocation of the pressure pump between the tank and the nozzle. The full-scale device built on this basis established the effectiveness of the mechanism's design, even with the simple materials and techniques available to the Byzantines. The experiment used crude oil mixed with wood resins, and achieved a flame temperature of over 1,000¬†¬∞C (1,830¬†¬∞F) and an effective range of up to 15 meters (49¬†ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hand-held_projectors">Hand-held projectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hand-held projectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The portable <i>cheirosiph≈çn</i> ("hand-<i>siph≈çn</i>"), the earliest analogue to a modern <a href="/wiki/Flamethrower" title="Flamethrower">flamethrower</a>, is extensively attested in the military documents of the 10th century, and recommended for use in both sea and land. They first appear in the <i><a href="/wiki/Tactica_of_Emperor_Leo_VI_the_Wise" title="Tactica of Emperor Leo VI the Wise">Tactica</a></i> of emperor <a href="/wiki/Leo_VI_the_Wise" title="Leo VI the Wise">Leo VI the Wise</a>, who claims to have invented them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617-40">[40]</a></sup> Subsequent authors continued to refer to the <i>cheirosiph≈çnes</i>, especially for use against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_towers" title="Siege towers">siege towers</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Nikephoros_II_Phokas" title="Nikephoros II Phokas">Nikephoros II Phokas</a> also advises their use in field armies, with the aim of disrupting the enemy formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617.E2.80.93619_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617.E2.80.93619-72">[72]</a></sup> Although both Leo VI and Nikephoros Phokas claim that the substance used in the <i>cheirosiph≈çnes</i> was the same as in the static devices used on ships, Haldon and Byrne consider that the former were manifestly different from their larger cousins, and theorize that the device was fundamentally different, "a simple syringe [that] squirted both liquid fire (presumably unignited) and noxious juices to repel enemy troops." However the illustrations of Hero's <i>Poliorcetica</i> show the <i>cheirosiph≈çn</i> also throwing the ignited substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197797_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldonByrne197797-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006627_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006627-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grenades">Grenades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grenades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In its earliest form, Greek fire was hurled onto enemy forces by firing a burning cloth-wrapped ball, perhaps containing a flask, using a form of <a href="/wiki/Catapult" title="Catapult">light catapult</a>, most probably a seaborne variant of the Roman light catapult or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onager_(siege_weapon)" title="Onager (siege weapon)">onager</a>. These were capable of hurling light loads‚Äîaround 6 to 9¬†kg (13 to 20¬†lb)‚Äîa distance of 350‚Äì450¬†m (380‚Äì490¬†yd).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effectiveness_and_countermeasures">Effectiveness and countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effectiveness and countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the destructiveness of Greek fire is indisputable, it did not make the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_navy" title="Byzantine navy">Byzantine navy</a> invincible. It was not, in the words of naval historian John Pryor, a "ship-killer" comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Naval_ram" title="Naval ram">naval ram</a>, which, by then, had fallen out of use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryor200397_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryor200397-89">[89]</a></sup> While Greek fire remained a potent weapon, its limitations were significant when compared to more traditional forms of artillery: in its <i>siph≈çn</i>-deployed version, it had a limited range, and it could be used safely only in a calm sea and with favourable wind conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006384_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006384-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Muslim navies eventually adapted themselves to it by staying out of its effective range and devising methods of protection such as felt or hides soaked in vinegar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPryorJeffreys2006617-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Greek fire made an appearance in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_On_Stranger_Tides" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: On Stranger Tides">Pirates of the Caribbean: On Stranger Tides</a></i> (2011) as a naval weapon used by the character Blackbeard.</li>
<li>Greek fire also appeared in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Timeline_(film)" title="Timeline (film)">Timeline</a></i> (2003) where an archaeologist from the modern day gives the English the secrets of Greek Fire for his life. In the film, water applied in an effort to extinguish it spread the fire instead.</li>
<li>Greek fire was used in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Sinbad_the_Sailor_(1947_film)" title="Sinbad the Sailor (1947 film)">Sinbad the Sailor</a></i> (1947, Technicolor) by Sinbad (Douglas Fairbanks, Jr.) to vanquish the Emir of Daibul (Anthony Quinn) in a rowboat while saving Shireen (Maureen O'Hara) and the lost treasure of Alexander the Great (the movie referred to it as the "eighth" voyage of Sinbad).</li>
<li>Greek fire was used in naval battles in the 2014 movie <i><a href="/wiki/300:_Rise_of_an_Empire" title="300: Rise of an Empire">300: Rise of an Empire</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Greek fire has appeared in multiple video games, including <i><a href="/wiki/Defender_of_the_Crown" title="Defender of the Crown">Defender of the Crown</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Assassin%27s_Creed:_Revelations" title="Assassin's Creed: Revelations">Assassin's Creed: Revelations</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Total_War:_Attila" title="Total War: Attila">Total War: Attila</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Empires_II" title="Age of Empires II">Age of Empires II</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Medieval_II:_Total_War:_Kingdoms" title="Medieval II: Total War: Kingdoms">Medieval II: Total War: Kingdoms</a></i>. Greek fire also makes an appearance in the game <i><a href="/wiki/Rise_of_the_Tomb_Raider" title="Rise of the Tomb Raider">Rise of the Tomb Raider</a></i>, wherein the formula is found in the modern day in an excavation and used to increase the potency of fire-related armaments, because of which, it was given the name "Holy Fire" in the game.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Both the academic discussion of Greek fire and a medieval demonstration of it appear in Michael Crichton's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Timeline_(novel)" title="Timeline (novel)">Timeline</a></i> (1999) and its 2003 <a href="/wiki/Timeline_(film)" title="Timeline (film)">film adaptation</a>.</li>
<li>A similar substance, known as "wildfire", is used in a naval battle in <a href="/wiki/George_R._R._Martin" title="George R. R. Martin">George R. R. Martin</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Clash_of_Kings" title="A Clash of Kings">A Clash of Kings</a></i>. This green substance is deployed in fragile clay jars, never from tubes, and is apparently partly magical in nature, but, like Greek fire, it burns on water.</li>
<li>Greek fire is a common weapon used in <a href="/wiki/Rick_Riordan" title="Rick Riordan">Rick Riordan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Percy_Jackson_%26_the_Olympians" title="Percy Jackson &amp; the Olympians">Percy Jackson &amp; the Olympians</a></i> series as well as its spin-off series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heroes_of_Olympus" title="The Heroes of Olympus">The Heroes of Olympus</a></i>. However, the literature's version of the weapon is green.</li>
<li>Greek fire also plays a part in saving the city of Boston from destruction in Matthew Pearl's novel, <i>The Technologists</i> (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the "Notes from the Historical Record" section of his tenth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigma_Force" title="Sigma Force">Sigma Force</a> novel (page 1 of 2) <i>The Sixth Extinction</i>, <a href="/wiki/James_Rollins" title="James Rollins">James Rollins</a> calls Greek fire's modern day equivalent <a href="/wiki/Napalm" title="Napalm">napalm</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/C._J._Sansom" title="C. J. Sansom">C. J. Sansom</a>'s historical mystery novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Fire_(Sansom_novel)" title="Dark Fire (Sansom novel)">Dark Fire</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cromwell" title="Thomas Cromwell">Thomas Cromwell</a> sends the lawyer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthew_Shardlake" title="Matthew Shardlake">Matthew Shardlake</a> to recover the secret of Greek fire, following its discovery in the library of a dissolved London monastery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Chapter 96, "The Try-Works", of <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">Herman Melville</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Moby-Dick" title="Moby-Dick">Moby-Dick</a></i>, the nighttime light effects aboard the whale ship <i>Pequod</i> whilst engaged in rendering blubber at sea are likened to "the famed Greek fire".</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Caine" title="Rachel Caine">Rachel Caine</a>'s The Great Library series, Greek fire is a liquid weapon used by Burners, the Welsh army, the High Garda, and the Great Library itself. It is described as green in appearance and the smoke is also mentioned as being toxic to breathe.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Raymond_E._Feist" title="Raymond E. Feist">Raymond E. Feist</a>'s fiction Riftwar Chronicles, the island nation of Queg (somewhat based on the real-world Greece) produces a substance known as "(Quegan) Fire Oil" which can burn on water and can only be doused with sand. The Fire Oil is created from naptha mixed with some other ingredients.</li>
</ul>
