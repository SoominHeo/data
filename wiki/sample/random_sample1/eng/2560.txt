<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hellenization</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Hellenism_(disambiguation)" title="Hellenism (disambiguation)">Hellenism (disambiguation)</a></div>
<p><b>Hellenization</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) or <b>Hellenisation</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British</a>) is the historical spread of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> and, to a lesser extent, language, over foreign peoples conquered by Greeks or brought into their <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">sphere of influence</a>, particularly during the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> following the campaigns of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> (king of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedon</a>, r. 336–323 BCE). The result of Hellenisation was that elements of Greek origin combined in various forms and degrees with local elements; these Greek influences spread from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_basin" title="Mediterranean basin">Mediterranean basin</a> as far east as modern-day <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. In modern times, Hellenisation has been associated with the adoption of modern Greek culture and the ethnic and cultural homogenization of Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zacharia2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zacharia2008-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koliopoulos-Veremis_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koliopoulos-Veremis-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_usage">Historic usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historic usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_period">Classical period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="684" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png/350px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png/525px-Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f7/Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greek_Colonization_Archaic_Period.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map showing the Greek territories and colonies during the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">Archaic</a> period.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The concept applies to a number of other ancient historical contexts, starting with the Hellenisation of the earliest inhabitants of Greece such as the <a href="/wiki/Pelasgians" title="Pelasgians">Pelasgians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Leleges" title="Leleges">Leleges</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lemnian_language" title="Lemnian language">Lemnians</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eteocypriot" title="Eteocypriot">Eteocypriots</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eteocretan" title="Eteocretan">Eteocretans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minoans" title="Minoans">Minoans</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> (prior to <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical antiquity</a>), as well as the <a href="/wiki/Sicels" title="Sicels">Sicels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elymians" title="Elymians">Elymians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicani" title="Sicani">Sicani</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oenotrians" title="Oenotrians">Oenotrians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brutii" title="Brutii">Brutii</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucani_(ancient_people)" title="Lucani (ancient people)">Lucani</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messapii" title="Messapii">Messapii</a> and many others in territories constituting <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_period">Hellenistic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hellenistic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MacedonEmpire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="951" data-file-width="1990" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/MacedonEmpire.jpg/400px-MacedonEmpire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/MacedonEmpire.jpg/600px-MacedonEmpire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/MacedonEmpire.jpg/800px-MacedonEmpire.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MacedonEmpire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Greek Empire established by the military conquests of Alexander the Great, king of Macedon, 334–323 BC.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic civilization</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Judaism" title="Hellenistic Judaism">Hellenistic Judaism</a></div>
<p>During the Hellenistic period, following the death of Alexander the Great, considerable numbers of <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persis" title="Persis">Persians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenians</a>, and a number of other ethnic groups along the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, South-Eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> were Hellenized. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bactrians" title="Bactrians">Bactrians</a>, an Iranian ethnic group who lived in <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a> (northern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>), were Hellenized during the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a> and soon after various tribes in northwestern regions of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>) underwent Hellenization during the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">Indo-Greek Kingdom</a>. Other tribes that underwent varying degrees of Hellenisation included <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracians</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dardanians_(Balkans)" title="Dardanians (Balkans)">Dardanians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paeonians" title="Paeonians">Paeonians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illyrians" title="Illyrians">Illyrians</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> south of the <a href="/wiki/Jire%C4%8Dek_Line" title="Jireček Line">Jireček Line</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Getae" title="Getae">Getae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hellenisation during the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, however, had its limitations. For example, areas of southern <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> that were affected by Greek culture mostly entailed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucid" title="Seleucid">Seleucid</a> urban centers where Greek was commonly spoken. The countryside, on the other hand, was largely unaffected since most of its inhabitants spoke <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> and continued to maintain their native traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Moreover, Hellenisation did not necessarily involve assimilation of non-Greek ethnic groups since Hellenistic Greeks in regions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> were conscious of their ancestral lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a> there was a sharp polarization between Hellenizers and anti-Hellenizers, especially manifested during the rebellion of the <a href="/wiki/Maccabees" title="Maccabees">Maccabees</a> against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucid" title="Seleucid">Seleucid</a> rule. The word "Mityavnim" (מתיוונים) i.e. "Hellenizers", remains up to the present in use among radical Orthodox Jews as a pejorative term for Jews seeking to assimilate in whatever world culture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantine Greeks</a></div>
<p>Hellenisation can also refer to the medieval <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine's</a> founding of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> that was Hellenized). Moreover, it can refer to the primacy of Greek culture and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek language</a> after the reign of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Heraclius" title="Heraclius">Heraclius</a> (r. 610–641) in the 7th century AD.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_period">Ottoman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ottoman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rum_millet" title="Rum millet">Rum millet</a></div>
<p>Hellenisation during the period of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman imperial rule</a> entailed the higher status which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_culture" title="Greek culture">Greek culture</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church_of_Constantinople" title="Orthodox Church of Constantinople">Greek Orthodox Church</a> enjoyed among the Christian Orthodox populations of the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geographical_name_changes_in_Greece" title="Geographical name changes in Greece">Geographical name changes in Greece</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grecomans" title="Grecomans">Grecomans</a></div>
<p>In 1909, a commission appointed by the Greek government reported that one third of the villages of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> should have their names changed, often because of their non-Greek origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zacharia2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zacharia2008-1">[1]</a></sup> In other instances, names were changed from a contemporary name of Greek origin to the ancient Greek toponym. Some village names were formed from a Greek root word with a foreign suffix, or vice versa. The majority of the name changes took place in areas populated by ethnic Greeks, where a stratum of foreign, or divergent, toponyms had accumulated over the centuries. However, in some parts of northern Greece the population was not Greek-speaking and many of the former toponyms reflected the diverse ethnic and linguistic origins of their inhabitants.</p>
<p>The process of the change of toponyms in modern Greece has been described as a process of Hellenisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zacharia2008_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zacharia2008-1">[1]</a></sup> A modern use is in connection with policies pursuing "cultural harmonization and education of the linguistic minorities residing within the modern Greek state" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenic_Republic" title="Hellenic Republic">Hellenic Republic</a>), i.e. the Hellenization of minority groups in modern Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koliopoulos-Veremis_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Koliopoulos-Veremis-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1870, the Greek government abolished all Italian schools in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionian_islands" title="Ionian islands">Ionian islands</a>, annexed to Greece six years earlier. This led to the diminution of the community of <a href="/wiki/Corfiot_Italians" title="Corfiot Italians">Corfiot Italians</a>, resident in <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a> since the Middle Ages; by the 1940s there were only four hundred Corfiot Italians left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Wars" title="Balkan Wars">Balkan Wars</a> in 1912 Greece took control of the areas today referred to as <a href="/wiki/Northern_Greece" title="Northern Greece">Northern Greece</a> where the Greek-speaking villages were outnumbered by Slavs, Turks, Albanians and Vlachs, with those still residing in Greece after the war becoming Hellenised in the subsequent years by the actions of the government of Greece which included changes of personal names to make them Greek-like, suppression of local languages, and erasure of non-Greek inscriptions from old gravestones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noel_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-noel-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_scholarship">Modern scholarship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hellenization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern scholarship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The twentieth century witnessed a lively debate over the extent of Hellenisation in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and particularly among the ancient <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> that has continued until today. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Religions" title="History of Religions">History of Religions</a>' interpretations of the rise of <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Early Christianity</a> (applied most famously by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Bultmann" title="Rudolf Bultmann">Rudolf Bultmann</a>) were wont to see <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> as largely unaffected by Hellenism, while the Judaism of the diaspora was thought to have succumbed thoroughly to its influences. <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Bultmann" title="Rudolf Bultmann">Rudolf Bultmann</a> thus argued that Christianity arose almost completely within those Hellenistic confines and should be read against that background as opposed to a more traditional Jewish background. With the publication of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hengel" title="Martin Hengel">Martin Hengel</a>'s two volume study <i>Hellenism and Judaism</i> (1974, German original 1972) and subsequent studies <i>Jews, Greeks and Barbarians: Aspects of the Hellenisation of Judaism in the pre-Christian Period</i> (1980, German original 1976), and <i>The 'Hellenisation' of Judaea in the First Century after Christ</i> (1989, German original 1989) the tide began to turn decisively. Hengel argued that virtually all of Judaism was highly Hellenized well before the beginning of the Christian era, and even the Greek language was well known throughout the cities and even smaller towns of Jewish Palestine. Scholars have continued to nuance Hengel's views, but very few continue to doubt the strong Hellenistic influences throughout the Levant, even among the conservative Jewish communities who were the most nationalistic.</p>
