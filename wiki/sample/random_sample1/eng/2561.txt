<p><i><b>Grizzly Man</b></i> is a 2005 American <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary film</a> by German director <a href="/wiki/Werner_Herzog" title="Werner Herzog">Werner Herzog</a>. It chronicles the life and death of bear enthusiast <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Treadwell" title="Timothy Treadwell">Timothy Treadwell</a>. The film includes some of Treadwell's own footage of his interactions with <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bears</a> before 2003, and of interviews with people who knew, or were involved with Treadwell, as well as professionals dealing with wild bears.</p>
<p>He and his girlfriend Amie Huguenard were killed and eaten by a grizzly bear in 2003. Treadwell's footage was found after his death. The bear that killed Treadwell and Huguenard was later encountered and killed by the group retrieving the remains of the victims. The final film was co-produced by Discovery Docs, the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>'s theatrical documentary unit, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lions_Gate_Entertainment" title="Lions Gate Entertainment">Lions Gate Entertainment</a>. <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_Man_(soundtrack)" title="Grizzly Man (soundtrack)">The film's soundtrack</a> is by British <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a> and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Thompson_(musician)" title="Richard Thompson (musician)">Richard Thompson</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herzog used sequences extracted from more than 100 hours of video footage shot by Treadwell during the last five years of his life. He also conducted and filmed interviews with Treadwell's family and friends, and bear and nature experts. Park rangers and bear experts commented on statements and actions by Treadwell, such as his repeated claims that he was defending the bears from poachers. Park rangers noted that there had never been a recorded incident of poaching at this national park.</p>
<p>As another example, Treadwell claimed he had "gained the trust" of certain bears, sufficient to approach and pet them. Park rangers pointed out that bears are wild and potentially dangerous animals; given that, Treadwell was lucky to have survived so long as he did without being mauled. One park ranger suggested that the bears were so confused by Treadwell's direct, casual contact that they weren't sure how to react to him. Other park rangers point out that the bears were not threatened by poachers, but Treadwell's actions put them at real risk of harm and death. By <a href="/wiki/Habituation" title="Habituation">familiarizing them with</a> human contact, he increased the likelihood that they would approach human habitation seeking food, and cause a confrontation in which humans would kill them.</p>
<p>In 2003, Treadwell was camping in Katmai National Park with his girlfriend, Amie Huguenard. Treadwell usually left the park at the end of summer but that year stayed into early October. This put him and Huguenard at greater risk, as in this period, bears are aggressive about searching for food to store up calories for <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">hibernation</a> during the winter. Herzog speculates that their staying later in the season ultimately resulted in the deaths of Treadwell and Huguenard.</p>
<p>In addition to presenting views from friends and professionals, Herzog narrates and offers his own interpretations of events. He concluded that Treadwell had a sentimental view of nature, thinking he could tame the wild bears. Herzog notes that nature is cold and harsh. Treadwell's view clouded his thinking and led him to underestimate danger, resulting in his death and that of his girlfriend.</p>
<p>Treadwell's video camera captured an audio record of the bear attack. Herzog refrained from making this a part of the film, but he is shown listening to it, clearly disturbed. The director advised Palovak (the owner of the tape) to destroy it rather than listen to it. He later repudiated his own advice, saying it was</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Stupid... silly advice born out of the immediate shock of hearing—I mean, it’s the most terrifying thing I’ve ever heard in my life. Being shocked like that, I told her, ‘You should never listen to it, and you should rather destroy it. It should not be sitting on your shelf in your living room all the time.’ [But] she slept over it and decided to do something much wiser. She did not destroy it but separated herself from the tape, and she put it in a bank vault."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The coroner gave Palovak Treadwell's wristwatch, which had been retrieved from his left arm, one of the few remains found. Willy Fulton, the pilot who discovered the remains of Treadwell and his girlfriend, had noted seeing the lone arm with the wristwatch and not being able to keep the image out of his mind.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Treadwell spent 13 summers in <a href="/wiki/Katmai_National_Park_and_Preserve" title="Katmai National Park and Preserve">Katmai National Park and Preserve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. Over time, he believed the bears grew to trust him; they allowed him to approach them and he had even touched them. He gained some national notoriety for his work with the bears and founded Grizzly People with his friend Jewel Palovak. They worked to protect bears in national parks by raising awareness.</p>
<p>Park officials repeatedly warned him that his interaction with the bears was unsafe to both him and to the bears. "At best, he's misguided," Deb Liggett, superintendent at Katmai and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Clark_National_Park_and_Preserve" title="Lake Clark National Park and Preserve">Lake Clark</a> national parks, told the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchorage_Daily_News" title="Anchorage Daily News">Anchorage Daily News</a></i> in 2001. "At worst, he's dangerous. If Timothy models unsafe behavior, that ultimately puts bears and other visitors at risk." Treadwell filmed his exploits, and used the films to raise public awareness of the problems faced by bears in North America. In 2003, at the end of his 13th visit, he and his girlfriend Amie Huguenard were attacked, killed, and partially eaten by a bear. The events that led to the attack are unknown.</p>
<p>Jewel Palovak, co-founder of Grizzly People and a close friend of Treadwell’s, had to give her approval for the film to be produced, as she controlled his video archives. The filmmakers had to deal with logistical as well as sentimental factors related to Treadwell's footage of his bear interactions. Grizzly People is a “grassroots organization” concerned with the treatment of bears in the United States. After her friend's death, Palovak was left with control of Grizzly People and Treadwell's 100 hours of archival footage. As his close friend, former girlfriend, and confidante, she had a large emotional stake in the production. She had known Treadwell since 1985 and felt a deep sense of responsibility to her late friend and his legacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herzog_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-herzog-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>She said that he had often discussed his video archives with her. "Timothy was very dramatic," she once said. She quoted Treadwell as saying, "'If I die, if something happens to me, make that movie. You make it. You show ’em.' I thought that Werner Herzog could definitely do that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herzog_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-herzog-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibition">Exhibition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exhibition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Grizzly Man</i> premiered at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Sundance_Film_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="2005 Sundance Film Festival (page does not exist)">2005 Sundance Film Festival</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Limited_release" title="Limited release">limited US theatrical release</a> began on August 12, 2005. It was later released on DVD in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> on December 26, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Discovery Channel aired <i>Grizzly Man</i> on television on February 3, 2006; its three-hour presentation of the film included a 30-minute companion special that delved deeper into Treadwell's relationship with the bears and addressed controversies related to the film.</p>
<p>The DVD release lacks an interview with Treadwell by <a href="/wiki/David_Letterman" title="David Letterman">David Letterman</a>, which was shown in the original theatrical release. Letterman had joked that Treadwell would be eaten by a bear. The versions televised on the Discovery Channel and <a href="/wiki/Animal_Planet" title="Animal Planet">Animal Planet</a> both retain this scene.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Response">Response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Grizzly Man</i> opened on August 12, 2005 in 29 North America venues. It grossed US$269,131 ($9,280 per screen) in its opening weekend, ranking number 26 in the box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At its widest point, it played at 105 theatres, and made US$3,178,403 in North America during its run, with $882,902 overseas for a worldwide total of $4,061,305.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon its North American theatrical release, <i>Grizzly Man</i> was acclaimed by critics. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film has a 93% "Certified Fresh" score based on 136 reviews, with an average rating of 8/10. The site's consensus states: "Whatever opinion you come to have of the obsessive Treadwell, Herzog has once again found a fascinating subject."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> reports an 87 out of 100 rating based on 35 critics, indicating "universal acclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Denby_(film_critic)" title="David Denby (film critic)">David Denby</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Narrating in his extraordinary German-accented English, Herzog is fair-minded and properly respectful of Treadwell's manic self-invention. He even praises Treadwell as a good filmmaker: as Treadwell stands talking in the foreground of the frame, the bears play behind him or scoop up salmon in sparkling water; in other shots, a couple of foxes leap across the grass in the middle of a Treadwell monologue. The footage is full of stunning incidental beauties."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Film critic <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>, a longtime supporter of Herzog's work, awarded the film four out of four stars.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"'I will protect these bears with my last breath', Treadwell says. After he and Amie become the first and only people to be killed by bears in the park, the bear that is guilty is shot dead. Treadwell's watch, still ticking, is found on his severed arm. I have a certain admiration for his courage, recklessness, idealism, whatever you want to call it, but here is a man who managed to get himself and his girlfriend eaten, and you know what? He deserves Werner Herzog."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charlie_Russell_(naturalist)" title="Charlie Russell (naturalist)">Charlie Russell</a>, a naturalist who studied bears for many years, lived near them and raised them for a decade in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamchatka" title="Kamchatka">Kamchatka</a>. He corresponded with Treadwell and wrote about the film:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Herzog is a skillful filmmaker so a large percentage of those who watch the movie <i>Grizzly Man</i>, overlook Timothy's amazing way with animals even though to me this stands out very strongly. The fact that Timothy spent an incredible 35,000 hours, spanning 13 years, living with the bears in Katmai National Park, without any previous mishap, escapes people completely. Even with his city-kid background, I found myself mesmerized by what he could do with animals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The film placed at No. 94 on <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a>'s best 100 films of the 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grizzly_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Gotham_Awards" title="Gotham Awards">Gotham Award</a> for Best Documentary</li>
<li>Won the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Film_Critics_Association" title="Los Angeles Film Critics Association">Los Angeles Film Critics Association</a> Award for Best Documentary/ Non-Fiction Film</li>
<li>Won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Film_Critics_Circle_Award" title="New York Film Critics Circle Award">New York Film Critics Circle Award</a> for Best Non-Fiction Film</li>
<li>Won the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Film_Critics_Circle" title="San Francisco Film Critics Circle">San Francisco Film Critics Circle</a> Award for Best Documentary</li>
<li>Won the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_P._Sloan_Prize" title="Alfred P. Sloan Prize">Alfred P. Sloan Prize</a> and was nominated for the Grand Jury Prize at the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a></li>
<li>Won the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Film_Critics_Association" title="Toronto Film Critics Association">Toronto Film Critics Association</a> Award for Best Documentary</li>
<li>Won the Anugerah Seri Angkasa 2008 Angkasapuri.</li>
</ul>
