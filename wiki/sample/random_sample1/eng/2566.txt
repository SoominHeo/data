<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coat of arms of Greenland</h1>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a></b> is a blue shield featuring a silver <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a>. This symbol was first introduced in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Denmark" title="Coat of arms of Denmark">coat of arms of Denmark</a> in 1666<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and it is still represented in the arms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_royal_family" title="Danish royal family">Danish royal family</a>. In a Danish context, the bear was originally shown walking naturally, but an upright position was specified in 1819. The 1470 <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=London_Roll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="London Roll (page does not exist)">London Roll</a></i> shows an arms captioned <i>Le Roy de Greneland</i> featuring a shield depicting a polar bear surrounded by three birds. This royal title did not reflect any official title, but merely that the arms could be used by anyone controlling Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The version currently used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Greenland" title="Government of Greenland">government of Greenland</a> was designed by Greenlandic artist <a href="/wiki/Jens_Rosing" title="Jens Rosing">Jens Rosing</a> and adopted on May 1, 1989 by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Greenland" title="Parliament of Greenland">Landsting</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a> symbolizes the <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> of Greenland and the blue (azure) colour designates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> Greenland is washed by. Instead of the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Denmark#Royal_coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms of Denmark">version in the royal arms</a> which follows the <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldic</a> tradition in raising the right forepaw, the polar bear on the Greenlandic coat of arms raises the left forepaw, due to the traditional <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> belief that polar bears are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-handed" title="Left-handed">left-handed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A similar arms is used by the official Danish government representative in Greenland. In this case, the bear raises its right paw, and the shield is crowned with the royal crown.</p>
<p>The official Danish specification of the arms does not specify which forepaw is raised, so there is no conflict between the different versions. The adherents of the full independence of Greenland use a green background.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">blazon</a> in heraldic terms is: <i>Azure, a polar bear rampant argent</i>.</p>
<p>The polar bear was first included as a symbol of Greenland in the Danish coat of arms during the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III_of_Denmark" title="Frederick III of Denmark">Frederick III of Denmark</a>, but did not gain widespread use on its own until the early 20th century.</p>
