<p><b>Greenhall's dog-faced bat</b> (<i><b>Cynomops greenhalli</b></i>) is a <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bat</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molossidae" title="Molossidae">Molossidae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-2">[2]</a></sup> It is found in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guianas" title="Guianas">Guianas</a>, northeastern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is an insect-eating bat, 40–97 mm in length. Yellowish brown to black above, grey underneath, it is broad-faced with widely separated eyes. Its ears are short and rounded; the <a href="/wiki/Antitragus" title="Antitragus">antitragus</a> is square; its lips are not wrinkled; it has a broad snout. Mostly it is found at low elevations. Colonies of 50–77 roost in hollow branches of large trees. Males and females stay together throughout the year. Named after Arthur Greenhall, a scientist who was in charge of the <a href="/wiki/Rabies" title="Rabies">rabies</a> program at the <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_Regional_Virus_Laboratory" title="Trinidad Regional Virus Laboratory">Trinidad Regional Virus Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Spain" title="Port of Spain">Port of Spain</a>, Trinidad.</p>
