<p><i><b>The Brothers Grimm</b></i> is a 2005 <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Matt_Damon" title="Matt Damon">Matt Damon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heath_Ledger" title="Heath Ledger">Heath Ledger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lena_Headey" title="Lena Headey">Lena Headey</a> in an exaggerated and fictitious portrait of the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a> as traveling <a href="/wiki/Confidence_trick" title="Confidence trick">con-artists</a> in French-occupied Germany, during the early 19th century. However, the brothers eventually encounter a genuine <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tale</a> curse which requires real courage instead of their usual bogus <a href="/wiki/Exorcism" title="Exorcism">exorcisms</a>. Supporting characters are played by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stormare" title="Peter Stormare">Peter Stormare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Pryce" title="Jonathan Pryce">Jonathan Pryce</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monica_Bellucci" title="Monica Bellucci">Monica Bellucci</a>.</p>
<p>In February 2001, <a href="/wiki/Ehren_Kruger" title="Ehren Kruger">Ehren Kruger</a> sold his <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">spec script</a> to <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a> (MGM). With Gilliam's hiring as director, the script was rewritten by Gilliam and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Grisoni" title="Tony Grisoni">Tony Grisoni</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">Writers Guild of America</a> refused to credit them for their work, thus Kruger received sole credit. MGM eventually dropped out as distributor, but decided to co-finance <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> with <a href="/wiki/Dimension_Films" title="Dimension Films">Dimension Films</a> and <a href="/wiki/Summit_Entertainment" title="Summit Entertainment">Summit Entertainment</a>, while Dimension took over distribution duties.</p>
<p>The film was shot entirely in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>. Gilliam often had on-set tensions with brothers <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weinstein" title="Bob Weinstein">Bob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Weinstein" title="Harvey Weinstein">Harvey Weinstein</a>, which caused the original theatrical release date to be delayed nearly ten months. <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> was finally released on 26 August 2005 with mixed reviews and a $105.3 million box office performance.</p>
<p>This also marks Terry Gilliam's first film to receive a PG-13 rating by the MPAA.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Grimm" title="Wilhelm Grimm">Wilhelm Grimm</a> (Matt Damon) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakob_Grimm" title="Jakob Grimm">Jakob Grimm</a> (Heath Ledger) arrive in <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">French-occupied Germany during the early 1800s</a>. They go to <a href="/wiki/Karlstadt_am_Main" title="Karlstadt am Main">Karlstadt</a> to rid the town of a witch's ghost. After killing the "ghost", it is revealed that the Brothers Grimm have actually set up a fake witch to trick the town. Afterwards, as they are celebrating, Italian torturer Mercurio Cavaldi (Peter Stormare) takes them to the French General Delatombe (Jonathan Pryce).</p>
<p>Delatombe forces them to solve a mystery: The girls of the small village of Marbaden are disappearing, and the villagers are convinced that supernatural beings are responsible. The Grimms are charged with finding who is responsible, and they soon discover that it is the work of a real supernatural force: a beautiful, yet evil, 500-year-old <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringian</a> Queen (Monica Bellucci) stealing young girls to restore her own beauty.</p>
<p>Long ago, King <a href="/wiki/Childeric_I" title="Childeric I">Childeric I</a> came to the forest to build a city while the Queen experimented with <a href="/wiki/Black_magic" title="Black magic">black magic</a> to gain eternal life. A plague swept through the land, and she hid in her tower, while her husband and everyone below her perished. Her spell granted her immortal life, but not the youth and beauty to go along with it. Her youthful appearance now only exists in her mirror, the source of her life, as an illusion and nothing more.</p>
<p>She needs to drink the blood of twelve young girls to regain her beauty; ten have already been reported missing. The Queen is working an enchantment to regain her beauty with the aid of her <a href="/wiki/Werewolf" title="Werewolf">werewolf</a> huntsman and his magic axe, crow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Familiar" title="Familiar">familiars</a>, and various creatures in the forest. The Grimms, with the help of Cavaldi and Angelika (Lena Headey), a knowing huntress from the village, intend to destroy the Mirror Queen.</p>
<p>After another girl goes missing, Cavaldi takes the Grimms and Angelika back to Delatombe. Because they have failed, Cavaldi is ordered to kill both the Grimms; but, after convincing Delatombe that the magic in the forest is actually caused by German rebels, he sends them back. While Cavaldi stays behind with Angelika in the village, the brothers attempt to get into the tower. Jake succeeds and discovers the Queen and the power of her mirror. Meanwhile, another girl, named Sasha, is captured despite Angelika and Cavaldi's efforts to save her.</p>
<p>Jake rides into the forest alone after a spat with Will, who follows him. After mistaking a dummy that is smashed into the tower for Jake, Will realizes that Jake needs him to believe in him and assists Jake in climbing up the tower. On the roof of the tower, Jake notices twelve crypts in which the twelve victims must lie.</p>
<p>When Sasha's body comes up from a well, the werewolf takes her to a tomb. After rescuing Sasha and taking the werewolf's magic axe, the Grimms return to the village. Delatombe captures the brothers and believes them to be frauds. French soldiers begin burning down the forest, and Cavaldi represses his sympathy to the brothers, but they are eventually saved by Angelika.</p>
<p>The werewolf is revealed to be Angelika's father, who is under the Queen's spell. It turns out that he is only able to keep on living due to an enchanted spike that is lodged into his chest and, without such, the spell is broken. Angelika is drowned by her father, becoming the 12th victim. The Brothers reach the tower while the Queen breathes an ice wind that puts out the forest fire. Delatombe notices that the Grimms have escaped and goes after them with Cavaldi. When Cavaldi refuses to kill the Grimms, Delatombe shoots him but is later impaled by Will.</p>
<p>Will and Jake enter the tower, where Will falls under the Queen's spell when the Queen takes the enchanted spike from Angelika's father and thrusts it into Will's chest. Jake shatters the enchanted mirror in the tower, preventing the Queen from completing the spell that will restore her youth. With the last of his strength, Angelika's father destroys the rest of the mirror by jumping out of the window with it; and Will, attempting to save the Queen, tries to take back the mirror and falls with him, and both men are killed.</p>
<p>Outside, Cavaldi survives, having donned the Grimm's faux-magic armor. He finds Will's body and recites an Italian curse, and the tower falls apart. Jake escapes, and Cavaldi informs Jake that he can break the spell and awaken Angelika with a kiss, which in turn resurrects the other girls and Will. With the menace gone and their daughters returned to them, the villagers of Marbaden celebrate and give their heart-felt thanks to the brothers.</p>
<p>Cavaldi stays in the village and joins the villagers for the feast. Angelika kisses both the Grimms and tells them that they are always welcome at the village. The Brothers Grimm decide to pursue a new profession, presumably recording fairy tales, although they are now wanted criminals of the state. One of the Queen's crows is seen flying off with the last shard of her mirror, still holding the Queen's watchful eye and presumably, her living soul.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Brothers">The Brothers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Brothers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Will and Jake have a complicated relationship; Jake is the smaller, younger, more sensitive one that Will feels he needs to protect. Will is often very hard on Jake (dating all the way back to their childhood, when Jake spent their money that was to be used for medicine for their dying sister on "magic beans") and orders him around. Will is somewhat of a womanizer and wants to make money, whereas Jake is more interested in fairy tales and adventures. Jake feels that Will doesn't care about or believe in him; but Will is just frustrated about the way Jake acts so spontaneously, making it hard for Will to protect him.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ehren_Kruger" title="Ehren Kruger">Ehren Kruger</a>'s screenplay was written as a <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">spec script</a>; in February 2001, <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a> (MGM) purchased the script, with <a href="/wiki/Summit_Entertainment" title="Summit Entertainment">Summit Entertainment</a> to co-finance the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In October 2002, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a> entered negotiations to direct,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and rewrote Kruger's script alongside frequent collaborator <a href="/wiki/Tony_Grisoni" title="Tony Grisoni">Tony Grisoni</a>. However, the <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">Writers Guild of America</a> refused to credit Gilliam and Grisoni for their rewrite work, and Kruger received sole credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staff_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-staff-10">[10]</a></sup> After Gilliam's hiring, production was put on fast track for a target November 2004 theatrical release date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recast_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-recast-5">[5]</a></sup> The budget, originally projected at $75 million, was to be Dimension Films' most expensive film ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lion_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-lion-11">[11]</a></sup> The studio had trouble financing the film, and dropped out as main distributor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guy_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-guy-12">[12]</a></sup> Weeks later, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weinstein" title="Bob Weinstein">Bob Weinstein</a>, under his <a href="/wiki/Dimension_Films" title="Dimension Films">Dimension Films</a> production company, made a deal with MGM and Summit to co-finance <i>The Brothers Grimm</i>, and become the lead distributor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lion_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-lion-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original start date was April 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> but filming did not begin until 30 June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It was decided to shoot <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> entirely in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> over budget constraints. Gilliam reasoned that "this is an $80 million movie, which would probably cost $120—$140 million in America".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The majority of filming required <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">sound stages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backlot" title="Backlot">backlots</a> from <a href="/wiki/Barrandov_Studios" title="Barrandov Studios">Barrandov Studios</a> in Prague. Filming at Barrandov ended on 23 October. Location filming began afterwards, which included the <a href="/wiki/K%C5%99ivokl%C3%A1t_Castle" title="Křivoklát Castle">Křivoklát Castle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staff_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-staff-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Along with <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_vs._Predator_(film)" title="Alien vs. Predator (film)">Alien vs. Predator</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Van_Helsing_(film)" title="Van Helsing (film)">Van Helsing</a></i>, <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> provided work for hundreds of local jobs and contributed over $300 million into the Czech Republic's economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Gilliam hired <a href="/wiki/Guy_Hendrix_Dyas" title="Guy Hendrix Dyas">Guy Hendrix Dyas</a> as <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">production designer</a> after he was impressed with Dyas' work on <i><a href="/wiki/X2_(film)" title="X2 (film)">X2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guy_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-guy-12">[12]</a></sup> Gilliam often disputed with executive producers Bob and <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Weinstein" title="Harvey Weinstein">Harvey Weinstein</a> during production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Weinstein Brothers fired <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">cinematographer</a> and regular Gilliam collaborator <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Pecorini" title="Nicola Pecorini">Nicola Pecorini</a> after six weeks. Pecorini was then replaced by <a href="/wiki/Newton_Thomas_Sigel" title="Newton Thomas Sigel">Newton Thomas Sigel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-time-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>"I'm used to riding roughshod over <a href="/wiki/Studio_executive" title="Studio executive">studio executives</a>," Gilliam explained, "but the Weinsteins rode roughshod over me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-time-4">[4]</a></sup> Gilliam got so upset, filming was shut down for nearly two weeks. Matt Damon reflected on the situation: "I've never been in a situation like that. Terry was spitting rage at <a href="/wiki/Studio_system" title="Studio system">the system</a>, at the Weinsteins. You can't try and impose big compromises on a visionary director like him. If you try to force him to do what you want creatively, he'll go nuclear."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-time-4">[4]</a></sup> The feud between Gilliam and the Weinsteins was eventually settled, although Bob Weinstein blamed the entire situation on <a href="/wiki/Yellow_journalism" title="Yellow journalism">yellow journalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Filming was scheduled to end in October, but due to various problems during filming, principal photography did not end until the following 27 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the tensions between the filmmaker and the producers during production, Gilliam said in retrospect about the film, "<i>[I]</i>t's not the film they wanted and it's not quite the film I wanted. It's the film that is a result of <i>[...]</i> two groups of people, who aren’t working well together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senses_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-senses-21">[21]</a></sup> With regards to the Weinsteins also producing <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Gangs_of_New_York" title="Gangs of New York">Gangs of New York</a></i> (2002), Gilliam stated: "Marty [Scorsese] said almost the exact same quote I said, without us knowing it: 'They took the joy out of filmmaking.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senses_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-senses-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_effects">Visual effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Visual effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">Post-production</a> was severely delayed when Gilliam disagreed with the Weinsteins over the <a href="/wiki/Final_cut_privilege" title="Final cut privilege">final cut privilege</a>. In the meantime, the conflict lasted so long that Gilliam had enough time to shoot another feature film, <i><a href="/wiki/Tideland_(film)" title="Tideland (film)">Tideland</a></i>. To create the visual effects, Gilliam awarded the shots to Peerless Camera, the London-based effects studio he founded in the late-1970s with <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects_supervisor" title="Visual effects supervisor">visual effects supervisor</a> Kent Houston. However, two months into filming, Houston said that Peerless "ran into a number of major issues with <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> and with the Weinstein Brothers". He continued that "the main problem was the fact that the number of effects shots had dramatically increased, mainly because of issues that arose during shooting with the physical effects."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soft_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-soft-22">[22]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the Queen's chamber inside the tower was actually built by the Art Department as 2 sets. One set was resplendent and new while the other was old and decrepit. The sets were joined to each other by the central mirror, a piece of transparent glass giving the illusion that a single set was reflected and used to create the effect.</p>
<p>There were originally to be about 500 effect shots, but it increased to 800. The post-production conflict between Gilliam and the Weinsteins also gave enough time for Peerless to work on another film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zorro" title="The Legend of Zorro">The Legend of Zorro</a></i>. Four different creatures were required for <a href="/wiki/Computer_animation" title="Computer animation">computer animation</a>: a Wolfman, a mud creature, the Mirror Queen, and a living tree. John Paul Docherty, who headed the digital visual effects unit, studied the animation of the computer-generated <a href="/wiki/Morlock" title="Morlock">Morlocks</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Time_Machine_(2002_film)" title="The Time Machine (2002 film)">The Time Machine</a></i> for the Wolfman. Docherty depicted the Morlocks "as a nice mix between human and animal behaviors".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soft_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-soft-22">[22]</a></sup> The death of The Mirror Queen was the most complex effect of the film. In the sequence, the Queen turns into hundreds of shards of glass and shatters. With <a href="/wiki/Rendering_(computer_graphics)" title="Rendering (computer graphics)">computerized rendering</a>, this could not happen, as the 3D volume of the body suddenly turns into 2D pieces of glass. The problem was eventually solved due to sudden advances that occurred with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Softimage_XSI" title="Softimage XSI">Softimage XSI</a> software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soft_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-soft-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original theatrical release date was due in November 2004 before being changed many times; the dates had been moved to February 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nov_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nov-23">[23]</a></sup> 29 July,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> 23 November,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nov_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nov-23">[23]</a></sup> and finally 26 August. Executive producer <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weinstein" title="Bob Weinstein">Bob Weinstein</a> blamed the pushed back release dates on budgetary concerns. To help promote <i>The Brothers Grimm</i>, a three-minute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_trailer" title="Film trailer">film trailer</a> was shown at the <a href="/wiki/2004_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2004 Cannes Film Festival">2004 Cannes Film Festival</a>, while twenty minutes of footage was shown at the <a href="/wiki/2005_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2005 Cannes Film Festival">2005 event</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Brothers Grimm</i> was released in the United States in 3,087 theaters, earning $15,092,079 in its opening weekend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-box-26">[26]</a></sup> The film eventually grossed $37,916,267 in the United States and $67.4 million internationally, coming to a worldwide total of $105,316,267.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-box-26">[26]</a></sup> <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> was shown at the <a href="/wiki/62nd_Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="62nd Venice International Film Festival">62nd Venice International Film Festival</a> on 4 September 2005, while in competition for the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Lion" title="Golden Lion">Golden Lion</a>, but lost to <i><a href="/wiki/Brokeback_Mountain" title="Brokeback Mountain">Brokeback Mountain</a></i>, also starring Ledger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Brothers Grimm</i> was released with mixed reviews from critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotten_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-rotten-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-29">[29]</a></sup> Based on 179 reviews collected by <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, 38% of the critics enjoyed the film, with an average score of 5.2/10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotten_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-rotten-28">[28]</a></sup> By comparison, <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> collected a score of 51/100 based on 36 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-meta-29">[29]</a></sup> The majority of critics believed Gilliam sacrificed the storyline in favor of the visual design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotten_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-rotten-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> called the film "an invention without pattern, chasing itself around the screen without finding a plot. The movie seems like a style in search of a purpose, with a story we might not care about."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hunter" title="Stephen Hunter">Stephen Hunter</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> wrote that "<i>The Brothers Grimm</i> looks terrific, yet it remains essentially inert. You keep waiting for something to happen, and after a while your mind wanders from the hollow frenzy up there with all its filigrees and fretwork."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mick_LaSalle" title="Mick LaSalle">Mick LaSalle</a> from the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> felt "despite an appealing actor in each role, the entire cast comes across as repellent. Will and Jake Grimm are two guys in the woods, surrounded by computerized animals, putting audiences to sleep all over America."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a>, writing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_(magazine)" title="Rolling Stone (magazine)"><i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine</a>, largely enjoyed <i>The Brothers Grimm</i>. He explained that "if you're a Gilliam junkie, as I am, you go with it, even when the script loses its shaky hold on coherence." Travers added, "even when Gilliam flies off the rails, his images stick with you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Gene Seymour of <i><a href="/wiki/Newsday" title="Newsday">Newsday</a></i> called the film "a great compound of rip-snorting Gothic fantasy and <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python" title="Monty Python">Python</a></i>-esque <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">dark comedy</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Brothers_Grimm_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, <a href="/wiki/Miramax" title="Miramax">Miramax</a> owns the home video rights, while <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a> holds the television rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nov_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nov-23">[23]</a></sup> The DVD release of <i>The Brothers Grimm</i> in December 2005 includes <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">audio commentary</a> by Gilliam, two "making-of" featurettes, and <a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">deleted scenes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The film was released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> format in October 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Both the DVD and Blu-ray were released by <a href="/wiki/Lionsgate_Home_Entertainment" title="Lionsgate Home Entertainment">Lionsgate Home Entertainment</a>, under license from Miramax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
