<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Picture book</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>picture book</b> combines visual and verbal narratives in a book format, most often aimed at young children. The images in picture books use a range of media such as oil paints, acrylics, watercolor, and pencil, among others. Two of the earliest books with something like the format picture books still retain now were <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hoffmann_(author)" title="Heinrich Hoffmann (author)">Heinrich Hoffmann</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Struwwelpeter" title="Struwwelpeter">Struwwelpeter</a></i> from 1845 and <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Potter" title="Beatrix Potter">Beatrix Potter</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Peter_Rabbit" title="The Tale of Peter Rabbit">The Tale of Peter Rabbit</a></i> from 1902. Some of the best-known picture books are <a href="/wiki/Robert_McCloskey" title="Robert McCloskey">Robert McCloskey</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Make_Way_for_Ducklings" title="Make Way for Ducklings">Make Way for Ducklings</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Seuss" title="Dr. Seuss">Dr. Seuss</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Cat_In_The_Hat" title="The Cat In The Hat">The Cat In The Hat</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Sendak" title="Maurice Sendak">Maurice Sendak</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Where_the_Wild_Things_Are" title="Where the Wild Things Are">Where the Wild Things Are</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Caldecott_Medal" title="Caldecott Medal">Caldecott Medal</a> (established 1938) and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Greenaway_Medal" title="Kate Greenaway Medal">Kate Greenaway Medal</a> (established 1955) are awarded annually for illustrations in children's literature. From the mid-1960s several children's literature awards include a category for picture books.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Target_audiences">Target audiences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picture_book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Target audiences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Picture books are most often aimed at young children, and while some may have very basic language especially designed to help children develop their reading skills, most are written with vocabulary a child can understand but not necessarily read. For this reason, picture books tend to have two functions in the lives of children: they are first read to young children by adults, and then children read them themselves once they begin learning to read.</p>
<p>Some picture books are published with content aimed at older children or even adults. <i>Tibet: Through the Red Box</i>, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Sis" title="Peter Sis">Peter Sis</a>, is one example of a picture book aimed at an adult audience.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subgenres">Subgenres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picture_book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subgenres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several subgenres among picture books, including <a href="/wiki/Alphabet_book" title="Alphabet book">alphabet books</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Concept_book&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Concept book (page does not exist)">concept books</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_reader" title="Early reader">early readers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">nursery rhymes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toy_book" title="Toy book">toy books</a>. <a href="/wiki/Board_book" title="Board book">Board books</a> - picture books published on a hard <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">cardboard</a> - are often intended for small children to use and play with; cardboard is used for the cover as well as the pages, and is more durable than paper. Another category is movable books, such as <a href="/wiki/Pop-up_book" title="Pop-up book">pop-up books</a>, which employ paper engineering to make parts of the page pop up or stand up when pages are opened. <i>The Wheels on the Bus</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Paul_O._Zelinsky" title="Paul O. Zelinsky">Paul O. Zelinsky</a>, is one example of a bestseller pop-up picture book.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_picture_books">Early picture books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picture_book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early picture books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbispictus.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="571" data-file-width="800" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Orbispictus.JPG/220px-Orbispictus.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Orbispictus.JPG/330px-Orbispictus.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Orbispictus.JPG/440px-Orbispictus.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

A reprint of the 1658 illustrated <i><a href="/wiki/Orbis_Pictus" title="Orbis Pictus">Orbis Pictus</a></i></div>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Orbis_Pictus" title="Orbis Pictus">Orbis Pictus</a></i> from 1658 by <a href="/wiki/John_Amos_Comenius" title="John Amos Comenius">John Amos Comenius</a> was the earliest illustrated book specifically for children. It is something of a children's <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia" title="Encyclopedia">encyclopedia</a> and is illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcuts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Little_Pretty_Pocket-Book" title="A Little Pretty Pocket-Book">A Little Pretty Pocket-Book</a></i> from 1744 by <a href="/wiki/John_Newbery" title="John Newbery">John Newbery</a> was the earliest illustrated storybook marketed as pleasure reading in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The German children's books <i><a href="/wiki/Struwwelpeter" title="Struwwelpeter">Struwwelpeter</a></i> (literally "Shaggy-Peter") from 1845 by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hoffmann_(author)" title="Heinrich Hoffmann (author)">Heinrich Hoffmann</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Max_and_Moritz" title="Max and Moritz">Max and Moritz</a> from 1865 by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Busch" title="Wilhelm Busch">Wilhelm Busch</a>, were among the earliest examples of modern picturebook design. Collections of <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">Fairy tales</a> from early nineteenth century, like those by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_Fairy_Tales" title="Grimm's Fairy Tales">Brothers Grimm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a> were sparsely illustrated, but beginning in the middle of the century, collections were published with images by illustrators like <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Dor%C3%A9" title="Gustave Doré">Gustave Doré</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fedor_Flinzer" title="Fedor Flinzer">Fedor Flinzer</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Cruikshank" title="George Cruikshank">George Cruikshank</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Pedersen" title="Vilhelm Pedersen">Vilhelm Pedersen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Bilibin" title="Ivan Bilibin">Ivan Bilibin</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Bauer_(illustrator)" title="John Bauer (illustrator)">John Bauer</a>. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lang%27s_Fairy_Books" title="Andrew Lang's Fairy Books">Andrew Lang's twelve Fairy Books</a> published between 1889 and 1910 were illustrated by among others <a href="/wiki/Henry_Justice_Ford" title="Henry Justice Ford">Henry J. Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancelot_Speed" title="Lancelot Speed">Lancelot Speed</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i>, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/John_Tenniel" title="John Tenniel">John Tenniel</a> in 1866 was one of the first highly successful entertainment books for children.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Toy_book" title="Toy book">Toy books</a> were introduced in the latter half of the 19th century, small paper bound books with art dominating the text. These had a larger proportion of pictures to words than earlier books, and many of their pictures were in color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The best of these were illustrated by the triumvirate of English illustrators <a href="/wiki/Randolph_Caldecott" title="Randolph Caldecott">Randolph Caldecott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Crane" title="Walter Crane">Walter Crane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Greenaway" title="Kate Greenaway">Kate Greenaway</a> whose association with colour printer and wood engraver Edmund Evans produced books of great quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the late 19th and early 20th century a small number of American and British artists made their living illustrating children's books, like <a href="/wiki/Rose_O%27Neill" title="Rose O'Neill">Rose O'Neill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rackham" title="Arthur Rackham">Arthur Rackham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cicely_Mary_Barker" title="Cicely Mary Barker">Cicely Mary Barker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willy_Pogany" title="Willy Pogany">Willy Pogany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Dulac" title="Edmund Dulac">Edmund Dulac</a>, <a href="/wiki/W._Heath_Robinson" title="W. Heath Robinson">W. Heath Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Pyle" title="Howard Pyle">Howard Pyle</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Charles_Robinson_(illustrator)" title="Charles Robinson (illustrator)">Charles Robinson</a>. Generally, these illustrated books had eight to twelve pages of illustrated pictures or plates accompanying a classic children's storybook.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Potter" title="Beatrix Potter">Beatrix Potter</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Peter_Rabbit" title="The Tale of Peter Rabbit">The Tale of Peter Rabbit</a></i> was published in 1902 to immediate success. <i>Peter Rabbit</i> was Potter's first of many <i>The Tale of...,</i> including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Squirrel_Nutkin" title="The Tale of Squirrel Nutkin">The Tale of Squirrel Nutkin</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Benjamin_Bunny" title="The Tale of Benjamin Bunny">The Tale of Benjamin Bunny</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Tom_Kitten" title="The Tale of Tom Kitten">The Tale of Tom Kitten</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Jemima_Puddle-Duck" title="The Tale of Jemima Puddle-Duck">The Tale of Jemima Puddle-Duck</a></i>, to name but a few which were published in the years leading up to 1910. Swedish author <a href="/wiki/Elsa_Beskow" title="Elsa Beskow">Elsa Beskow</a> wrote and illustrated some 40 children's stories and picture books between 1897–1952. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lang%27s_Fairy_Books" title="Andrew Lang's Fairy Books">Andrew Lang's twelve Fairy Books</a> published between 1889 and 1910 were illustrated by among others <a href="/wiki/Henry_Justice_Ford" title="Henry Justice Ford">Henry J. Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancelot_Speed" title="Lancelot Speed">Lancelot Speed</a>. In the US, illustrated stories for children appeared in magazines like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladies_Home_Journal" title="Ladies Home Journal">Ladies Home Journal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Housekeeping" title="Good Housekeeping">Good Housekeeping</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_(magazine)" title="Cosmopolitan (magazine)">Cosmopolitan</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Woman%27s_Home_Companion" title="Woman's Home Companion">Woman's Home Companion</a></i> intended for mothers to read to their children. Some cheap periodicals appealing to the juvenile reader started to appear in the early 20th century, often with uncredited illustrations. <a href="/wiki/Helen_Bannerman" title="Helen Bannerman">Helen Bannerman</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Black_Sambo" title="Little Black Sambo">Little Black Sambo</a></i> was published in 1899, and went through numerous printings and versions during the first decade of the 20th century. <i>Little Black Sambo</i> was part of a series of small-format books called <a href="/wiki/Dumpy_books" title="Dumpy books">The Dumpy Books for Children</a>, published by British publisher Grant Richards between 1897 and 1904.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_to_mid_20th_century">Early to mid 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picture_book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early to mid 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wizard_title_page.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="288" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Wizard_title_page.jpg/170px-Wizard_title_page.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Wizard_title_page.jpg/255px-Wizard_title_page.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d2/Wizard_title_page.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

Title page from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/L._Frank_Baum" title="L. Frank Baum">L. Frank Baum</a> from 1900</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/L._Frank_Baum" title="L. Frank Baum">L. Frank Baum</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> was published in 1900, and Baum created a number of other successful Oz-oriented books in the period from 1904 to 1920. Frank Baum wanted to create a modern-day fairy tale since he loved fairy tales as a child. In 1910, American illustrator and author <a href="/wiki/Rose_O%27Neill" title="Rose O'Neill">Rose O'Neill</a>'s first children’s book was published, <i>The Kewpies and Dottie Darling</i>. More books in the <a href="/wiki/Kewpie" title="Kewpie">Kewpie</a> series followed: <i>The Kewpies Their Book</i> in 1912 and <i>The Kewpie Primer</i> 1916. In 1918, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Gruelle" title="Johnny Gruelle">Johnny Gruelle</a> wrote and illustrated <i><a href="/wiki/Raggedy_Ann" title="Raggedy Ann">Raggedy Ann</a></i> and in 1920 followed up with <i>Raggedy Andy Stories</i>. Other Gruelle books included <i>Beloved Belinda</i>, <i>Eddie Elephant</i>, and <i>Friendly Fairies</i>.</p>
<p>In 1913, Cupples &amp; Leon published a series of 15 <i>All About</i> books, emulating the form and size of the <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Potter" title="Beatrix Potter">Beatrix Potter</a> books, <i>All About Peter Rabbit</i>, <i>All About The Three Bears</i>, <i>All About <a href="/wiki/Mother_Goose" title="Mother Goose">Mother Goose</a></i>, and <i>All About <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Red_Hen" title="The Little Red Hen">Little Red Hen</a></i>. The latter, along with several others, was illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Gruelle" title="Johnny Gruelle">Johnny Gruelle</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wanda_G%C3%A1g" title="Wanda Gág">Wanda Gág</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Millions_of_Cats" title="Millions of Cats">Millions of Cats</a></i> was published in 1928 and became the first picture book to receive a <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Medal</a> runner-up award. Wanda Gág followed with <i>The Funny Thing</i> in 1929, <i>Snippy and Snappy</i> in 1931, and then <i>The ABC Bunny</i> in 1933, which garnered her a second Newbery runner-up award.</p>
<p>In 1931, <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Brunhoff" title="Jean de Brunhoff">Jean de Brunhoff</a>'s first <a href="/wiki/Babar_the_Elephant" title="Babar the Elephant">Babar</a> book, <i>The Story Of Babar</i> was published in France, followed by <i>The Travels of Babar</i> then <i>Babar The King</i>. In 1930, <a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Flack" title="Marjorie Flack">Marjorie Flack</a> authored and illustrated <i>Angus and the Ducks</i>, followed in 1931 by <i>Angus And The Cats</i>, then in 1932, <i>Angus Lost</i>. Flack authored another book in 1933, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_about_Ping" title="The Story about Ping">The Story about Ping</a></i>, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Wiese" title="Kurt Wiese">Kurt Wiese</a>. The <i>Elson Basic Reader</i> was published in 1930 and introduced the public to <i><a href="/wiki/Dick_and_Jane" title="Dick and Jane">Dick and Jane</a></i>. In 1930 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Engine_That_Could" title="The Little Engine That Could">The Little Engine That Could</a></i> was published, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Lois_Lenski" title="Lois Lenski">Lois Lenski</a>. In 1954 it was illustrated anew by <a href="/wiki/George_and_Doris_Hauman" title="George and Doris Hauman">George and Doris Hauman</a>. It spawned an entire line of books and related paraphernalia and coined the refrain "I think I can! I think I can!". In 1936, <a href="/wiki/Munro_Leaf" title="Munro Leaf">Munro Leaf</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_Ferdinand" title="The Story of Ferdinand">The Story of Ferdinand</a></i> was published, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lawson_(author)" title="Robert Lawson (author)">Robert Lawson</a>. <i>Ferdinand</i> was the first picture book to crossover into <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">pop culture</a>. <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> produced an animated feature film along with corresponding merchandising materials. In 1938 to <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_P._Lathrop" title="Dorothy P. Lathrop">Dorothy Lathrop</a> was awarded the first <a href="/wiki/Caldecott_Medal" title="Caldecott Medal">Caldecott Medal</a> for her illustrations in <i><a href="/wiki/Animals_of_the_Bible" title="Animals of the Bible">Animals of the Bible</a></i>, written by Helen Dean Fish. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Handforth" title="Thomas Handforth">Thomas Handforth</a> won the second Caldecott Medal in 1939, for <i><a href="/wiki/Mei_Li" title="Mei Li">Mei Li</a></i>, which he also wrote. <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Bemelmans" title="Ludwig Bemelmans">Ludwig Bemelmans</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Madeline" title="Madeline">Madeline</a></i> was published in 1939 and was selected as a Caldecott Medal runner-up, today known as a Caldecott Honor book.</p>
<p>In 1942, <a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">Simon &amp; Schuster</a> began publishing the <a href="/wiki/Little_Golden_Books" title="Little Golden Books">Little Golden Books</a>, a series of inexpensive, well illustrated, high quality children's books. The eighth book in the series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Poky_Little_Puppy" title="The Poky Little Puppy">The Poky Little Puppy</a></i>, is the top selling children's book of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PW-Roback_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PW-Roback-6">[6]</a></sup> Many of the books were bestsellers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PW-Roback_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-PW-Roback-6">[6]</a></sup> including <i>The Poky Little Puppy</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tootle" title="Tootle">Tootle</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Scuffy_the_Tugboat" title="Scuffy the Tugboat">Scuffy the Tugboat</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Red_Hen" title="The Little Red Hen">The Little Red Hen</a></i>. Several of the illustrators for the Little Golden Books later became staples within the picture book industry. <a href="/wiki/Corinne_Malvern" title="Corinne Malvern">Corinne Malvern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibor_Gergely" title="Tibor Gergely">Tibor Gergely</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Tenggren" title="Gustaf Tenggren">Gustaf Tenggren</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feodor_Rojankovsky" title="Feodor Rojankovsky">Feodor Rojankovsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Scarry" title="Richard Scarry">Richard Scarry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eloise_Wilkin" title="Eloise Wilkin">Eloise Wilkin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Garth_Williams" title="Garth Williams">Garth Williams</a>. In 1947 <i><a href="/wiki/Goodnight_Moon" title="Goodnight Moon">Goodnight Moon</a></i> written by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Wise_Brown" title="Margaret Wise Brown">Margaret Wise Brown</a> and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Clement_Hurd" title="Clement Hurd">Clement Hurd</a> was published. By 1955, such picture book classics as <i><a href="/wiki/Make_Way_for_Ducklings" title="Make Way for Ducklings">Make Way for Ducklings</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_House" title="The Little House">The Little House</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Curious_George" title="Curious George">Curious George</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eloise_(books)" title="Eloise (books)">Eloise</a></i>, had all been published. In 1955 the first book was published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Miffy" title="Miffy">Miffy</a></i> series by Dutch author and illustrator <a href="/wiki/Dick_Bruna" title="Dick Bruna">Dick Bruna</a>.</p>
<p>In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Seuss" title="Dr. Seuss">Dr. Seuss</a> (Theodor Seuss Geisel), at the time a successful graphic artist and humorist, published his first book for children, <i><a href="/wiki/And_to_Think_That_I_Saw_It_on_Mulberry_Street" title="And to Think That I Saw It on Mulberry Street">And to Think That I Saw It on Mulberry Street</a></i>. It was immediately successful, and Seuss followed up with <i><a href="/wiki/The_500_Hats_of_Bartholomew_Cubbins" title="The 500 Hats of Bartholomew Cubbins">The 500 Hats of Bartholomew Cubbins</a></i> in 1938, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_Stilts" title="The King's Stilts">The King's Stilts</a></i> in 1939, and <i><a href="/wiki/Horton_Hatches_the_Egg" title="Horton Hatches the Egg">Horton Hatches the Egg</a></i> in 1940, all published by <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>. From 1947 to 1956 Seuss had twelve children's picture books published. Dr. Seuss created <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cat_in_the_Hat" title="The Cat in the Hat">The Cat in the Hat</a></i> in reaction to a <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life magazine</a> article by <a href="/wiki/John_Hersey" title="John Hersey">John Hersey</a> in lamenting the unrealistic children in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_Primers" title="School Primers">school primers</a> books. Seuss rigidly limited himself to a small set of words from an elementary school vocabulary list, then crafted a story based upon two randomly selected words—cat and hat. Up until the mid-1950s, there was a degree of separation between illustrated educational books and illustrated picture books. That changed with <i>The Cat in the Hat</i> in 1957.</p>
<p>Because of the success of <i>The Cat In The Hat</i> an independent publishing company was formed, called <a href="/wiki/Beginner_Books" title="Beginner Books">Beginner Books</a>. The second book in the series was nearly as popular, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cat_in_the_Hat_Comes_Back" title="The Cat in the Hat Comes Back">The Cat in the Hat Comes Back</a></i>, published in 1958. Other books in the series were <i><a href="/wiki/Sam_and_the_Firefly" title="Sam and the Firefly">Sam and the Firefly</a></i> (1958), <i><a href="/wiki/Green_Eggs_and_Ham" title="Green Eggs and Ham">Green Eggs and Ham</a></i> (1960), <i><a href="/wiki/Are_You_My_Mother%3F" title="Are You My Mother?">Are You My Mother?</a></i> (1960), <i><a href="/wiki/Go,_Dog._Go!" title="Go, Dog. Go!">Go, Dog. Go!</a></i> (1961), <i><a href="/wiki/Hop_on_Pop" title="Hop on Pop">Hop on Pop</a></i> (1963), and <i><a href="/wiki/Fox_in_Socks" title="Fox in Socks">Fox in Socks</a></i> (1965). Creators in the Beginner Book series were <a href="/wiki/Stan_and_Jan_Berenstain" title="Stan and Jan Berenstain">Stan and Jan Berenstain</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_D._Eastman" title="Philip D. Eastman">P. D. Eastman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roy_McKie" title="Roy McKie">Roy McKie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Helen_Palmer_Geisel" title="Helen Palmer Geisel">Helen Palmer Geisel</a> (Seuss' wife). The Beginner Books dominated the children's picture book market of the 1960s.</p>
<p>Between 1957 and 1960 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harper_%26_Brothers" title="Harper &amp; Brothers">Harper &amp; Brothers</a> published a series of sixteen "I Can Read" books. <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Bear_(book)" title="Little Bear (book)">Little Bear</a></i> was the first of the series. Written by <a href="/wiki/Else_Holmelund_Minarik" title="Else Holmelund Minarik">Else Holmelund Minarik</a> and illustrated by a then relatively unknown <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Sendak" title="Maurice Sendak">Maurice Sendak</a>, the two collaborated on three other "I Can Read" books over the next three years. From 1958 to 1960, <a href="/wiki/Syd_Hoff" title="Syd Hoff">Syd Hoff</a> wrote and illustrated four "I Can Read" books: <i><a href="/wiki/Danny_and_the_Dinosaur" title="Danny and the Dinosaur">Danny and the Dinosaur</a></i>, <i>Sammy The Seal</i>, <i>Julius</i>, and <i>Oliver</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid_to_late_20th_century">Mid to late 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picture_book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mid to late 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1949 American writer and illustrator <a href="/wiki/Richard_Scarry" title="Richard Scarry">Richard Scarry</a> began his career working on the <a href="/wiki/Little_Golden_Books" title="Little Golden Books">Little Golden Books</a> series. His <i><a href="/wiki/Best_Word_Book_Ever" title="Best Word Book Ever">Best Word Book Ever</a></i> from 1963 has sold 4 million copies. In total Scarry wrote and illustrated more than 250 books and more than 100 million of his books have been sold worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1963, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Where_The_Wild_Things_Are" title="Where The Wild Things Are">Where The Wild Things Are</a></i> by American writer and illustrator <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Sendak" title="Maurice Sendak">Maurice Sendak</a> was published. It has been adapted into other media several times, including an animated short in 1973, a <a href="/wiki/Where_the_Wild_Things_Are_(opera)" title="Where the Wild Things Are (opera)">1980 opera</a>, and, in 2009, a live-action <a href="/wiki/Where_the_Wild_Things_Are_(film)" title="Where the Wild Things Are (film)">feature film adaptation</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Spike_Jonze" title="Spike Jonze">Spike Jonze</a>. By 2008 it had sold over 19 million copies worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornton_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thornton-8">[8]</a></sup> American illustrator and author <a href="/wiki/Gyo_Fujikawa" title="Gyo Fujikawa">Gyo Fujikawa</a> created more than 50 books between 1963 and 1990. Her work has been translated into 17 languages and published in 22 countries. Her most popular books, <i>Babies</i> and <i>Baby Animals</i>, have sold over 1.7 million copies in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Fujikawa is recognized for being the earliest mainstream illustrator of picture books to include children of many races in her work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Moomin" title="Moomin">Moomin</a> books by Finnish author <a href="/wiki/Tove_Jansson" title="Tove Jansson">Tove Jansson</a> were novels, but several Moomin picture books were also published between 1952 and 1980, like <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Will_Comfort_Toffle%3F" title="Who Will Comfort Toffle?">Who Will Comfort Toffle?</a></i> (1960) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dangerous_Journey" title="The Dangerous Journey">The Dangerous Journey</a></i> (1977). The <a href="/wiki/Barbapapa" title="Barbapapa">Barbapapa</a> series of books by <a href="/wiki/Annette_Tison" title="Annette Tison">Annette Tison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Talus_Taylor" title="Talus Taylor">Talus Taylor</a> was published in France in the 1970s. They feature the shapeshifting pink blob Barbapapa and his numerous colorful children. The <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Men" title="Mr. Men">Mr. Men</a></i> series of 40-some books by English author and illustrated <a href="/wiki/Roger_Hargreaves" title="Roger Hargreaves">Roger Hargreaves</a> started in 1971. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Snowman" title="The Snowman">The Snowman</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Briggs" title="Raymond Briggs">Raymond Briggs</a> was published in Britain in 1978 and was entirely wordless. It was made into an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscar nominated</a> <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animated cartoon</a> that has been shown every year since on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_television" title="British television">British television</a>.</p>
<p>Japanese author and illustrator <a href="/wiki/Mitsumasa_Anno" title="Mitsumasa Anno">Mitsumasa Anno</a> has published a number of picture books beginning in 1968 with <i>Mysterious Pictures</i>. In his "Journey" books a tiny character travels through depictions of the culture of various countries. <i><a href="/wiki/Everyone_Poops" title="Everyone Poops">Everyone Poops</a></i> was first published in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in 1977, written and illustrated by the prolific children's author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taro_Gomi" title="Taro Gomi">Tarō Gomi</a>. It has been translated into several languages. Australian author <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Wild" title="Margaret Wild">Margaret Wild</a> has written more than 40 books since 1984 and won several awards. In 1987 the first book was published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Where%27s_Wally%3F" title="Where's Wally?">Where's Wally?</a></i> (known as <i>Where's Waldo?</i> in the United States and Canada) series by the British illustrator <a href="/wiki/Martin_Handford" title="Martin Handford">Martin Handford</a>. The books were translated into many languages and the franchise also spawned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Where%27s_Waldo%3F_(TV_series)" title="Where's Waldo? (TV series)">a TV series</a>, a comic strip and a series of video games. Since 1989 over 20 books have been created in the <i><a href="/wiki/Elmer_the_Patchwork_Elephant" title="Elmer the Patchwork Elephant">Elmer the Patchwork Elephant</a></i> series by the British author <a href="/wiki/David_McKee" title="David McKee">David McKee</a>. They have been translated in 40 languages and adapted into a children's TV series.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picture_book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1938, the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> (ALA) began presenting annually the <a href="/wiki/Caldecott_Medal" title="Caldecott Medal">Caldecott Medal</a> to the most distinguished children's book illustration published in the year. The Caldecott Medal was established as a sister award to the ALA's <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Medal</a>, which was awarded to a children's books "for the most distinguished American children's book published the previous year" and presented annually beginning in 1922. During the mid-forties to early-fifties honorees included <a href="/wiki/Marcia_Brown" title="Marcia Brown">Marcia Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Cooney" title="Barbara Cooney">Barbara Cooney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Duvoisin" title="Roger Duvoisin">Roger Duvoisin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berta_and_Elmer_Hader" title="Berta and Elmer Hader">Berta and Elmer Hader</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lawson_(author)" title="Robert Lawson (author)">Robert Lawson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_McCloskey" title="Robert McCloskey">Robert McCloskey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Seuss" title="Dr. Seuss">Dr. Seuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Sendak" title="Maurice Sendak">Maurice Sendak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingri_and_Edgar_Parin_d%27Aulaire" title="Ingri and Edgar Parin d'Aulaire">Ingri and Edgar Parin d'Aulaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Politi" title="Leo Politi">Leo Politi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasha_Tudor" title="Tasha Tudor">Tasha Tudor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Weisgard" title="Leonard Weisgard">Leonard Weisgard</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kate_Greenaway_Medal" title="Kate Greenaway Medal">Kate Greenaway Medal</a> was established in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in 1955 in honour of the children's illustrator, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Greenaway" title="Kate Greenaway">Kate Greenaway</a>. The medal is given annually to an outstanding work of illustration in <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children's literature">children's literature</a>. It is awarded by <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Institute_of_Library_and_Information_Professionals" title="Chartered Institute of Library and Information Professionals">Chartered Institute of Library and Information Professionals</a> (CILIP). Since 1965 the <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Jugendliteraturpreis" title="Deutscher Jugendliteraturpreis">Deutscher Jugendliteraturpreis</a> (German Youth literature prize) includes a category for picture books. The IBBY <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen_Award" title="Hans Christian Andersen Award">Hans Christian Andersen Award</a> for Illustration has been awarded since 1966. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Globe-Horn_Book_Award" title="Boston Globe-Horn Book Award">Boston Globe-Horn Book Award</a>, first presented in 1967, includes a category for picture books. In 2006, the ALA started awarding the <a href="/wiki/Geisel_Award" title="Geisel Award">Geisel Award</a>, named after <a href="/wiki/Dr._Seuss" title="Dr. Seuss">Dr. Seuss</a>, to the most distinguished beginning reader book. The award is presented to both the author and illustrator, in "literary and artistic achievements to engage children in reading."</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Golden_Pinwheel_Young_Illustrators_Award" title="Golden Pinwheel Young Illustrators Award">Golden Pinwheel Young Illustrators Award</a> was established in China in 2015. It received almost 1000 submitted works, from over 250 illustrators from around the world.</p>
