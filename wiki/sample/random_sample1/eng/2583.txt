<p><i><b>Pokémon: The First Movie: Mewtwo Strikes Back</b></i>, commonly referred to as <i><b>Pokémon: The First Movie</b></i>, originally released as <i><b>Pocket Monsters the Movie: Mewtwo Strikes Back!</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">劇場版ポケットモンスター ミュウツーの逆襲</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Gekijōban Poketto Monsutā: Myūtsū no Gyakushū</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, is a 1998 Japanese <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> film<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> directed by <a href="/wiki/Kunihiko_Yuyama" title="Kunihiko Yuyama">Kunihiko Yuyama</a>, the chief director of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(anime)" title="Pokémon (anime)">Pokémon</a></i> television series. It is the first theatrical release in the <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Pokémon">Pokémon</a></i> franchise.</p>
<p>It was released in Japan on July 18, 1998. The English-language adaptation, produced by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4Kids_Entertainment" title="4Kids Entertainment">4Kids Entertainment</a>, was released in North America on November 10, 1999.</p>
<p>The film primarily consists of three segments: <i>Pikachu's Vacation</i>, a 21-minute feature focusing on the series mascot <a href="/wiki/Pikachu" title="Pikachu">Pikachu</a>; <i>Origin of Mewtwo</i>, a 10-minute featurette that functions as a prologue to the main feature; and <i>Mewtwo Strikes Back</i>, the main 75 minute film feature. The featurette was added on for later releases and eventually dubbed as a special feature in the U.S. release of the TV special (that was released as a direct-to-video follow-up film sequel in the U.S.A.) <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon:_Mewtwo_Returns" title="Pokémon: Mewtwo Returns">Pokémon: Mewtwo Returns</a></i> as <i>The Uncut Story of Mewtwo's Origin</i>.</p>
<p>Although <i>Pokémon</i> was extremely popular when the film was released, the English-language version received negative reviews from film critics. Despite the reviews, it was a box office success worldwide, topping the box office charts in its opening weekend, and eventually grossing <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>163.6 million worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pikachu.27s_Vacation"><i>Pikachu's Vacation</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pikachu's Vacation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an animated short called "Pikachu's Vacation", the Pokémon of <a href="/wiki/Ash_Ketchum" title="Ash Ketchum">Ash Ketchum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Misty_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Misty (Pokémon)">Misty</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brock_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Brock (Pokémon)">Brock</a> are sent to spend a day at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">theme park</a> built for Pokémon. <a href="/wiki/Pikachu" title="Pikachu">Pikachu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Togepi" title="Togepi">Togepi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulbasaur" title="Bulbasaur">Bulbasaur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Squirtle" title="Squirtle">Squirtle</a> cross paths with a group of bullies consisting of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raichu" title="Raichu">Raichu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubone" title="Cubone">Cubone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marill" title="Marill">Marill</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snubbull" title="Snubbull">Snubbull</a>. The two groups compete against each other in sports, but it leads to Ash’s <a href="/wiki/Charizard" title="Charizard">Charizard</a> getting its head stuck in a pipe. Pikachu, his friends, and the bullies work together and successfully free Charizard and rebuild the park, spending the rest of the day playing before parting ways when their trainers return.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mewtwo_Strikes_Back"><i>Mewtwo Strikes Back</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mewtwo Strikes Back">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A group of scientists obtain a fossilised eyelash of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendary_Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Legendary Pokémon">legendary Pokémon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mew_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Mew (Pokémon)">Mew</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">clone</a> it to create a <a href="/wiki/Supersoldier" title="Supersoldier">supersoldier</a> named <a href="/wiki/Mewtwo" title="Mewtwo">Mewtwo</a>. The project is successful as Mewtwo awakens, but upon learning the scientists plan to treat him as an experiment, Mewtwo unleashes his <a href="/wiki/Psychokinesis" title="Psychokinesis">psychic powers</a> and destroys the laboratory.</p>
<p>Giovanni, leader of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_Rocket_(anime)" title="Team Rocket (anime)">Team Rocket</a> and the project's benefactor, convinces Mewtwo to work with him to hone his powers. However, Mewtwo learns his purpose is to be a weapon for Giovanni's benefit and escapes back to New Island where he plots revenge against humanity. Several Pokémon trainers are invited to New Island to meet and battle the world's greatest Pokémon Master. Ash, Misty, and Brock accept the challenge, but when they arrive at the port city Old Shore Wharf, a powerful storm whips up, preventing the trainers from sailing to the island. However, several trainers use their Pokémon to travel across the sea. Ash's group are picked up by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_Rocket_(anime)#Team_Rocket_trio" title="Team Rocket (anime)">Team Rocket</a> disguised as Vikings, but the storm sinks their boat, and they individually make it to New Island.</p>
<p>Escorted into the island's palace by the woman from the invitation, the trainers encounter Mewtwo, who releases the woman from his mind control; the woman is revealed to be a brainwashed <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pok%C3%A9mon_anime_characters#Recurring_characters" title="List of Pokémon anime characters">Nurse Joy</a>. Mewtwo plots to use the storm to wipe out humanity, leaving only wild and cloned Pokémon alive. Ash challenges Mewtwo's power, leading to a battle between the trainers' Pokémon and Mewtwo's clones who prove to be vastly superior in combat. Mewtwo captures all of the Pokémon to clone them, Ash chasing the captured Pikachu down into the rebuilt lab, where Team Rocket's <a href="/wiki/Meowth" title="Meowth">Meowth</a> is also cloned. Ash destroys the cloning machine, freeing the Pokémon, and leads them to confront Mewtwo. Mew appears and confronts Mewtwo.</p>
<p>All of the Pokémon battle save a defiant Pikachu, and Meowth, who makes peace with his own clone. Mew and Mewtwo's psychic battle wounds all of the Pokémon, forcing a desperate Ash to charge into the firing line of their attacks and is <a href="/wiki/Petrifaction_in_mythology_and_fiction" title="Petrifaction in mythology and fiction">petrified</a> by the blast. Pikachu tries to revive Ash with his thunderbolt but it fails. However, the tears of the Pokémon, as per a legend mentioned earlier in the film, are able to heal and revive Ash. Moved by Ash's sacrifice, Mewtwo realizes that he should not have to be judged by his origins but rather his choices in life. Departing with Mew and the clones, Mewtwo erases everyone's memories of the event.</p>
<p>Ash, Misty, and Brock find themselves back in Old Shore Wharf unsure how they got there. The storm outside clears up, Ash spotting Mew flying through the clouds and tells his friends of how he saw another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ho-Oh" title="Ho-Oh">legendary Pokémon</a> the day he left Pallet Town. Meanwhile, Team Rocket find themselves stranded on New Island but enjoy their time there.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kunihiko Yuyama directed the original Japanese version of the film, while Choji Yoshikawa served as producer and Takeshi Shudo as the writer. <a href="/wiki/Norman_J._Grossfeld" title="Norman J. Grossfeld">Norman J. Grossfeld</a>, former president of <a href="/wiki/4K_Media_Inc." title="4K Media Inc.">4Kids Productions</a>, served as the film's producer for the English-language North American version. Grossfeld, Michael Haigney, and John Touhey wrote the English adaptation, and Haigney served as the English version's voice director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The English version was heavily edited from the original Japanese one ; along with various content edits, Mewtwo was changed from a morally confused character into a more straightforward villain like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Pok%C3%A9mon_(599%E2%80%93649)#Genesect" title="List of Pokémon (599–649)">Genesect</a> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_the_Movie:_Genesect_and_the_Legend_Awakened" title="Pokémon the Movie: Genesect and the Legend Awakened">Pokémon the Movie: Genesect and the Legend Awakened</a></i>) so the audience would be able to identify it as the villain more easily. Furthermore, the moral message of the movie was changed from "all life is equal" to "fighting is wrong".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The English version editors translated various Japanese texts, including those on signs and on buildings, into English. <a href="/wiki/Shogakukan" title="Shogakukan">Shogakukan</a> digitally altered the backgrounds for both the American English and Kanzenban versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pheonomenon_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pheonomenon-4">[4]</a></sup> In the English dub, three Pokémon are referred to by the wrong name. Pidgeot was called <b>Pidgeotto</b>, Scyther was called <b>Alakazam</b>, and Sandslash was called <b>Sandshrew</b>. 4Kids said that they decided to leave the Alakazam and Sandshrew errors when they noticed it as something for the children watching to notice and because they felt it was plausible in context that Team Rocket could make a mistake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grossfeld also had new music re-recorded for the film's release, citing that it "would better reflect what American kids would respond to". John Loeffler of Rave Music produced the English-language music and composed the <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">film score</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Schuckett" title="Ralph Schuckett">Ralph Schuckett</a>. Loeffler also collaborated with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Lissauer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Lissauer (page does not exist)">John Lissauer</a> and Manny Corallo to produce the English-language "Pikachu's Vacation" score. Grossfeld also revealed that the English version of the film "combines the visual sense of the best Japanese animation with the musical sensibility of Western pop culture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pheonomenon_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pheonomenon-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_campaign">Marketing campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marketing campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first trailer was released in August 1999 and was shown before <i><a href="/wiki/The_Iron_Giant" title="The Iron Giant">The Iron Giant</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mystery_Men" title="Mystery Men">Mystery Men</a></i>. The second trailer was released in the fall of 1999 and was attached to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bachelor_(1999_film)" title="The Bachelor (1999 film)">The Bachelor</a></i>.</p>
<p>For the film's theatrical release, select theaters would give away exclusive <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Trading_Card_Game" title="Pokémon Trading Card Game">Pokémon trading cards</a>, to capitalize on the success of the trading card game. The cards featured likenesses of Electabuzz, Pikachu, Mewtwo, and Dragonite, and were dispensed in random order for each week it was in that particular theater. The subsequent releases of <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_Movie_2000" title="Pokémon: The Movie 2000">Pokémon: The Movie 2000</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_3:_The_Movie" title="Pokémon 3: The Movie">Pokémon 3: The Movie</a></i> featured a similar marketing campaign. For the 2000 home video release of <i>The First Movie</i>, a limited edition Mewtwo card (different from that used for the theatrical release) was packaged with the video.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toshihiro_Ono" title="Toshihiro Ono">Toshihiro Ono</a>, author of <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_Electric_Tale_of_Pikachu" title="Pokémon: The Electric Tale of Pikachu">Pokémon: The Electric Tale of Pikachu</a></i>, created a manga version of the film. He received the scripts and continuity in April 1998. The manga was released in May of that year. Ono's editors asked him to draw Mewtwo's birth, which was not included in the film. At a later point, the anime staff wrote the <i>Origin of Mewtwo</i> special, which does not match his story. Ono said that "there's not much connection between the manga and the movie".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onointerview_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Onointerview-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was released into cinemas in the United States from <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Family_Entertainment" title="Warner Bros. Family Entertainment">Warner Bros. Family Entertainment</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Kids%27_WB" title="Kids' WB">Kids' WB</a> banner (which broadcast the anime in the US).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-release">Re-release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Re-release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gave the film's English adaptation a 14% approval rating based on 79 reviews, with the consensus being: "Audiences other than children will find very little to entertain them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">weighted average</a> score of 35 out of 100 based on 25 reviews, meaning "generally unfavorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anime_News_Network" title="Anime News Network">Anime News Network</a> review called the main feature "contradictory", stating that "the anti-violent message that is pretty much crammed down our throats works directly against the entire point of the franchise" and criticized <i>Pikachu's Summer Vacation</i> for being "incoherent, pointless and fluffy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Patrick Butters, of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Times" title="The Washington Times">The Washington Times</a></i>, accused <i>Pokémon: The First Movie</i> of taking ideas from other films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> and being "just another cog in the mighty Nintendo machine".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Michael Wood, of England's <i>Coventry Evening Telegraph</i> said that <i>Pikachu's Summer Vacation</i> "can only be described as a mind-numbingly tedious piece, with no discernible storyline and lots of trippy images and silly voices". Wood did note that the main feature had a "mildly intriguing premise", but said that the rest of the film "was like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martial_arts_movie" title="Martial arts movie">martial arts movie</a> without the thrills".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Pokémon: The First Movie</i> was an instant commercial success, debuting at number one on the U.S. box office charts and making $10,096,848 on its Wednesday opening day. During its first weekend, it grossed $31,036,678 and went on to generate a total of $50,754,104 since its Wednesday launch in 3,043 theaters, averaging to about $10,199 per venue over the three-day span. It also held the record for being the animated feature with the highest opening weekend in November, which would be broken two weeks later by <i><a href="/wiki/Toy_Story_2" title="Toy Story 2">Toy Story 2</a></i>. Despite a 59.72% drop in its second weekend to $12,502,869, the film made $67,372,092 within 12 days. It closed on February 27, 2000, earning $85,744,662 in North America, and $77,900,000 in other territories. Worldwide, the film made $163,644,662, making it the highest-grossing anime film in the United States and the fourth highest-grossing animated film based on a television show worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was also the highest-grossing film based on a video game at the time, until 2001's <i><a href="/wiki/Lara_Croft:_Tomb_Raider" title="Lara Croft: Tomb Raider">Lara Croft: Tomb Raider</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="HD_remaster">HD remaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: HD remaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movie was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digitally_remastered" title="Digitally remastered">Digitally remastered</a> for high definition and aired by <a href="/wiki/TV_Tokyo" title="TV Tokyo">TV Tokyo</a> on May 3, 2013. It was also aired by other TV stations in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It aired on Cartoon Network in the United States on January 4, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The HD version came to Blu-ray on the Pikachu Movie Premium 1998-2010 box set in Japan on November 28, 2012.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Viz_Media" title="Viz Media">Viz Media</a> released a limited edition Blu-ray Steelbook containing the first three <i>Pokémon</i> films would be released on February 9, 2016, along with single releases on DVD.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In accommodation with the 20th anniversary of the <i>Pokémon</i> franchise, a digitally remastered version of the film was released on iTunes, Amazon and Google Play on February 27, 2016.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The film received a limited theatrical re-release, through select Cinemark theaters in the United States, on October 29 and November 1, 2016, to help celebrate Pokémon's 20th anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p><i><b>Pokémon: The First Movie Music From and Inspired by the Motion Picture</b></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a> to the first <i>Pokémon</i> film in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, It was released on November 10, 1999, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Disc" title="Compact Disc">Compact Disc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">Compact Cassette</a>. Some of the songs were featured in the animated short <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pikachu%27s_Vacation" title="Pikachu's Vacation">Pikachu's Vacation</a></i>, and some songs did not feature in either the short or the movie at all. The CD contains extra features, such as Pokémon videos and a screensaver. When it was released it included a promotion for one to send a Proof of Purchase to receive an exclusive promo <a href="/wiki/Jigglypuff" title="Jigglypuff">Jigglypuff</a> card from the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Trading_Card_Game" title="Pokémon Trading Card Game">Pokémon TCG</a> that lasted for a few months. <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Say_You_Love_Me_(M2M_song)" title="Don't Say You Love Me (M2M song)">Don't Say You Love Me</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/M2M_(band)" title="M2M (band)">M2M</a> was released as a single from the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

The second disc contains full score of the original Japanese release of the film in addition to two theme songs sung in Japanese.</p>
