<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cryogenic fuel</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Cryogenic fuels</b> are <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a> that require storage at extremely low temperatures in order to maintain them in a liquid state. These fuels are used in machinery that operates in space (e.g. rocket ships and satellites) because ordinary fuel cannot be used there, due to absence of an environment that supports <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> (on earth, oxygen is abundant in the atmosphere, whereas in human-explorable space, oxygen is virtually non-existent). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">Cryogenic</a> fuels most often constitute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquification" title="Liquification">liquefied</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cryogenic_Engine_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cryogenic_Engine-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a> use <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_cooling_(rocket)" title="Regenerative cooling (rocket)">regenerative cooling</a>, the practice of circulating their cryogenic fuel around the <a href="/wiki/Nozzle" title="Nozzle">nozzles</a> before the fuel is pumped into the <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a> and ignited. This arrangement was first suggested by <a href="/wiki/Eugen_S%C3%A4nger" title="Eugen Sänger">Eugen Sänger</a> in the 1940s. The <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket that sent the first manned missions to the moon used this design element, which is still in use today.</p>
<p>Quite often, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> is mistakenly called cryogenic "fuel", though it is actually an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> and not a fuel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Russian aircraft manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Tupolev" title="Tupolev">Tupolev</a> developed a version of its popular Tu-154 design but with a cryogenic fuel system, designated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu-155" title="Tu-155">Tu-155</a>. Using a fuel referred to as <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> (LNG), its first flight was in 1989.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cryogenic fuels can be placed into two categories: inert and flammable or combustible. Both types exploit the large liquid to gas volume ratio that occurs when liquid transitions to gas phase. The feasibility of cryogenic fuels is associated with what is known as a high mass flow rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-470-08024-8_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-470-08024-8-2">[2]</a></sup> With regulation, the high-density energy of cryogenic fuels is utilized to produce thrust in rockets and controllable consumption of fuel. The following sections provide further detail.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inert">Inert</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inert">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These types of fuels typically use the regulation of gas production and flow to power pistons in an engine. The large increases in pressure are controlled and directed toward the engine's pistons. The pistons move due to the mechanical power transformed from the monitored production of gaseous fuel. A notable example can be seen in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Dearman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Dearman (page does not exist)">Peter Dearman</a>'s liquid air vehicle. Some common inert fuels include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">Liquid nitrogen</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">Liquid air</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">Liquid helium</a></li>
<li>Liquid neon</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustible">Combustible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Combustible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These fuels utilize the beneficial liquid cryogenic properties along with the flammable nature of the substance as a source of power. These types of fuel are well known primarily for their use in rockets including the <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">Intercontinental ballistic missile</a>. Some common combustible fuels include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">Liquid hydrogen</a></li>
<li>Liquid natural gas (LNG)</li>
<li>Liquid methane</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine_combustion">Engine combustion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Engine combustion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Combustible cryogenic fuels offer much more utility than most inert fuels can. Liquefied natural gas, as with any fuel, will only combust when properly mixed with right amounts of air. As for LNG, the bulk majority of efficiency depends on the methane number, which is the gas equivalent of the octane number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Statoil_LNG_Combustion_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Statoil_LNG_Combustion-3">[3]</a></sup> This is determined based on the methane content of the liquefied fuel and any other dissolved gas, and varies as a result of experimental efficiencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Statoil_LNG_Combustion_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Statoil_LNG_Combustion-3">[3]</a></sup> Maximizing efficiency in combustion engines will be a result of determining the proper fuel to air ratio and utilizing the addition other hydrocarbons for added optimal combustion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_efficiency">Production efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gas liquefying processes have been improving over the past decades with the advent of better machinery and control of system heat losses. Typical techniques take advantage of the temperature of the gas dramatically cooling as the controlled pressure of a gas is released. Enough pressurization and then subsequent depressurization can liquefy most gases, as exemplified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joule-Thomson_effect" title="Joule-Thomson effect">Joule-Thomson effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNG_Liquefaction_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNG_Liquefaction-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquefied_natural_gas">Liquefied natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liquefied natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While it is cost effective to liquefy natural gas for storage, transport, and use, roughly 10 to 15 percent of the gas gets consumed during the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNG_Need_to_Know_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNG_Need_to_Know-5">[5]</a></sup> The optimal process contains four stages of <a href="/wiki/Propane_refrigeration" title="Propane refrigeration">propane refrigeration</a> and two stages of ethylene refrigeration. There can be the addition of an additional <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a> stage, but the additional costs of equipment are not economically justifiable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Efficiency can be tied to the pure component cascade processes which minimize the overall source to sink temperature difference associated with refrigerant condensing. The optimized process incorporates optimized heat recovery along with the use of pure refrigerants. All process designers of liquefaction plants using proven technologies face the same challenge: to efficiently cool and condense a mixture with a pure refrigerant. In the optimized Cascade process, the mixture to be cooled and condensed is the feed gas. In the propane mixed refrigerant processes, the two mixtures requiring cooling and condensing are the feed gas and the mixed refrigerant. The chief source of inefficiency lies in the heat exchange train during the liquefaction process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNG_Plant_Efficiency_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNG_Plant_Efficiency-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Cryogenic fuels are environmentally cleaner than gasoline or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. Among other things, the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> rate could potentially be reduced by 11%-20% using LNG as opposed to gasoline when transporting goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What_are_the_Benefits_of_LNG_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-What_are_the_Benefits_of_LNG-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Along with their eco-friendly nature, they have the potential to significantly decrease transportation costs of inland products because of their abundance compared to that of fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What_are_the_Benefits_of_LNG_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-What_are_the_Benefits_of_LNG-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cryogenic fuels have a higher mass flow rate than fossil fuels and therefore produce more <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> and power when combusted for use in an engine. This means that engines will run farther on less fuel overall than modern <a href="/wiki/Gas_engine" title="Gas engine">gas engines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cryo_basics_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cryo_basics-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cryogenic fuels are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Non-pollutants&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Non-pollutants (page does not exist)">non-pollutants</a> and therefore, if spilled, are no risk to the environment. There will be no need to clean up hazardous waste after a spill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Methane_Fuel_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Methane_Fuel-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_drawbacks">Potential drawbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Potential drawbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Some cryogenic fuels, like LNG, are naturally combustible. Ignition of fuel spills could result in a large explosion. This is possible in the case of a car crash with an LNG engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Methane_Fuel_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Methane_Fuel-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cryogenic storage tanks must be able to withstand high pressure. High-pressure <a href="/wiki/Propellant_tank" title="Propellant tank">propellant tanks</a> require thicker walls and stronger alloys which make the vehicle tanks heavier, thereby reducing performance and practicality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advantages_and_Disadvantages_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Advantages_and_Disadvantages-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Despite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-toxic" title="Non-toxic">non-toxic</a> tendencies, cryogenic fuels are denser than air. As such, they can lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a>. If leaked, the liquid will boil into a very dense, cold gas and if inhaled, could be fatal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canadian_Centre_for_Occupational_Health_.26_Safety_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian_Centre_for_Occupational_Health_.26_Safety-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
