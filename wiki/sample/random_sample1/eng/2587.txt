<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Neighbourhood</h1>

<p>A <b>neighbourhood</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences" title="American and British English spelling differences">Commonwealth English</a>), or <b>neighborhood</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences" title="American and British English spelling differences">American English</a>), is a geographically localized <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> within a larger <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburb</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural area</a>. Neighbourhoods are often social communities with considerable <a href="/wiki/Face-to-face_interaction" title="Face-to-face interaction">face-to-face interaction</a> among members. Researchers have not agreed on an exact definition, but the following may serve as a starting point: "…Neighborhood is generally defined spatially as a specific geographic area and functionally as a set of social networks. Neighborhoods, then, are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_unit" title="Neighborhood unit">spatial units</a> in which face-to-face social interactions occur—the personal settings and situations where residents seek to realize common values, socialize youth, and maintain effective social control."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word for "neighbourhood" was <i>neahdæl</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preindustrial_cities">Preindustrial cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preindustrial cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the words of the urban scholar <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">Lewis Mumford</a>, “Neighbourhoods, in some primitive, inchoate fashion exist wherever human beings congregate, in permanent family dwellings; and many of the functions of the city tend to be distributed naturally—that is, without any theoretical preoccupation or political direction—into neighbourhoods.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Most of the earliest cities around the world as excavated by archaeologists have evidence for the presence of social neighbourhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Historical documents shed light on neighbourhood life in numerous historical preindustrial or nonwestern cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neighbourhoods are typically generated by social interaction among people living near one another. In this sense they are local social units larger than <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">households</a> not directly under the control of city or state officials. In some preindustrial urban traditions, basic municipal functions such as protection, social regulation of births and marriages, cleaning and upkeep are handled informally by neighbourhoods and not by urban governments; this pattern is well documented for historical Islamic cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to social neighbourhoods, most ancient and historical cities also had administrative districts used by officials for taxation, record-keeping, and social control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Administrative districts are typically larger than neighbourhoods and their boundaries may cut across neighbourhood divisions. In some cases, however, administrative districts coincided with neighbourhoods, leading to a high level of regulation of social life by officials. For example, in the T’ang period Chinese capital city Chang’an, neighbourhoods were districts and there were state officials who carefully controlled life and activity at the neighbourhood level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neighbourhoods in preindustrial cities often had some degree of social specialization or differentiation. Ethnic neighbourhoods were important in many past cities and remain common in cities today. Economic specialists, including craft producers, merchants, and others, could be concentrated in neighbourhoods, and in societies with religious pluralism neighbourhoods were often specialized by religion. One factor contributing to neighbourhood distinctiveness and social cohesion in past cities was the role of rural to urban migration. This was a continual process in preindustrial cities, and migrants tended to move in with relatives and acquaintances from their rural past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighbourhoods_and_service_delivery">Neighbourhoods and service delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Neighbourhoods and service delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neighbourhoods have been the site of service delivery or "service interventions" in part as efforts to provide local, quality services, and to increase the degree of local control and ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Alfred Kahn, as early as the mid-1970s, described the "experience, theory and fads" of neighbourhood service delivery over the prior decade, including discussion of income transfers and poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Neighbourhoods, as a core aspect of community, also are the site of services for youth, including children with disabilities <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and coordinated approaches to low-income populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> While the term neighbourhood organization <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> is not as common in 2015, these organizations often are non-profit, sometimes grassroots or even core funded community development centres or branches.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighbourhoods_and_economic_development">Neighbourhoods and economic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neighbourhoods and economic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Community and economic development activists have pressured for reinvestment in local communities and neighbourhoods. In the early 2000s, Community Development Corporations, Rehabilitation Networks, Neighbourhood Development Corporations, and Economic Development organizations would work together to address the housing stock and the infrastructures of communities and neighbourhoods (e.g., community centres).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Community and Economic Development may be understood in different ways, and may involve "faith-based" groups and congregations in cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighbourhoods_and_community_research">Neighbourhoods and community research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neighbourhoods and community research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Urban sociology even has a subset termed neighbourhood sociology which supports the study of local communities <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the diversity of urban neighbourhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Neighbourhoods are also used in research studies from postal codes and health disparities, to correlations with school drop out rates or use of drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Central yet today are "community values" which have changed dramatically in larger cities, and sometimes not as much in rural regions of the USA; these values are reflected and shaped in part by the media bridging the gap between citizens and government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Neighbourhoods have several advantages as areas for policy analysis as well as an arena for social action:</p>
<ol>
<li>Neighbourhoods are common, and perhaps close to universal, since most people in <a href="/wiki/Urban_sociology" title="Urban sociology">urbanized</a> areas would probably consider themselves to be living in one.</li>
<li>Neighbourhoods are convenient, and always accessible, since you are already in your neighbourhood when you walk out your door.</li>
<li>Successful neighbourhood action frequently requires little specialized technical skill, and often little or no money. Action may call for an investment of time, but material costs are often low.</li>
<li>With neighbourhood action, compared to activity on larger scales, results are more likely to be visible and quickly forthcoming. The streets are cleaner; the crosswalk is painted; the trees are planted; the festival draws a crowd.</li>
<li>Visible and swift results are indicators of success; and since success is reinforcing, the probability of subsequent neighbourhood action is increased.</li>
<li>Because neighbourhood action usually involves others, such actions create or strengthen connections and relationships with other neighbours, leading in turn to a variety of potentially positive effects, often hard to predict.</li>
<li>Over and above these community advantages, neighbourhood activity may simply be enjoyable and fun for those taking part; and can often tighten security for those partaking in neighbourhood watch communities.</li>
</ol>
<p>But in addition to these benefits, considerable research indicates that strong and cohesive neighbourhoods and communities are linked—quite possibly causally linked—to decreases in crime, better outcomes for children, and improved physical and mental health. The social support that a strong neighbourhood may provide can serve as a buffer against various forms of adversity.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, the term is generally used for the urban <a href="/wiki/Administrative_division" title="Administrative division">administrative division</a> found immediately below the district level, although an intermediate, subdistrict level exists in some cities. They are also called streets (administrative terminology may vary from city to city). Neighbourhoods encompass 2,000 to 10,000 families. Within neighbourhoods, families are grouped into smaller residential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Residential_unit" title="Residential unit">units</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Residential_quarter" title="Residential quarter">quarters</a> of 100 to 600 families and supervised by a residents' committee; these are subdivided into residents' small groups of fifteen to forty families. In most urban areas of China, <b>neighbourhood'<i>,</i></b> <i><a href="/wiki/Residential_community" title="Residential community">community</a><b>,</b> residential community<b>,</b> residential unit<b>,</b> residential quarter'</i> have the same meaning: 社区 or 小区 or 居民区 or 居住区, and is the direct sublevel of a <a href="/wiki/Subdistricts_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Subdistricts of the People's Republic of China">subdistrict</a> (街道办事处), which is the direct sublevel of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Districts_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Districts of the People's Republic of China">district</a> (区), which is the direct sublevel of a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cities_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cities of the People's Republic of China (page does not exist)">city</a> (市). (See <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_China" title="Administrative divisions of China">Administrative divisions of the People's Republic of China</a>)</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The term has no general official or statistical purpose in the United Kingdom, but is often used by local <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">boroughs</a> for self-chosen sub-divisions of their area for the delivery of various services and functions, as for example in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingston-upon-Thames" title="Kingston-upon-Thames">Kingston-upon-Thames</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> or is used as an informal term to refer to a small area within a town or city. The label is commonly used to refer to organizations which relate to such a very local structure, such as neighbourhood policing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighbourhood_Watch_(UK)" title="Neighbourhood Watch (UK)">Neighbourhood watch</a> schemes. In addition, government statistics for local areas are often referred to as neighbourhood statistics, although the data themselves are broken down usually into <a href="/wiki/District" title="District">districts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" title="Ward (politics)">wards</a> for local purposes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, neighbourhoods are often given official or semi-official status through <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_association" title="Neighborhood association">neighbourhood associations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_watch" title="Neighborhood watch">neighbourhood watches</a>, or block watches. These may regulate such matters as <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">lawn</a> care and <a href="/wiki/Fence" title="Fence">fence</a> height, and they may provide such services as <a href="/wiki/Block_party" title="Block party">block parties</a>, neighbourhood <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parks" title="Parks">parks</a>, and community <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a>. In some other places the equivalent organization is the <a href="/wiki/Parish_(administrative_division)" title="Parish (administrative division)">parish</a>, though a parish may have several neighbourhoods within it depending on the area.</p>
<p>In localities where neighbourhoods do not have an official status, questions can arise as to where one neighbourhood begins and another ends. Many cities use <i>districts</i> and <i>wards</i> as official divisions of the city, rather than traditional neighbourhood boundaries.</p>
