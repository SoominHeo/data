<p><b>Gloucester</b> is a traditional, semi-hard <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> which has been made in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, since the 16th century, at one time made only with the milk of the once nearly extinct <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_cattle" title="Gloucester cattle">Gloucester cattle</a>.</p>
<p>There are two types of gloucester cheese: single and double; both are traditionally made from milk from Gloucestershire breed cows farmed within the English county of Gloucestershire.</p>
<p>Both types have a natural rind (outer layer) and a hard texture, but single gloucester is more crumbly, lighter in texture and lower in fat. Double gloucester is allowed to age for longer periods than single, and it has a stronger and more savoury flavour. It is also slightly firmer. The flower known as lady’s bedstraw (<i><a href="/wiki/Galium_verum" title="Galium verum">Galium verum</a></i>), was responsible for the distinctively yellow colour of double gloucester cheese. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> today, of these two types of cheese, it is the double gloucester which is more likely to be sold in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermarkets" title="Supermarkets">supermarkets</a>. Both types are produced in round shapes, but double gloucester rounds are larger. Traditionally whereas the double gloucester was a prized cheese comparable in quality to the best cheddar or cheshire, and was exported out of the county, single gloucester tended to be consumed within Gloucestershire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most double gloucester sold in UK supermarkets is slab cheese, made in large <a href="/wiki/Creamery" title="Creamery">creameries</a> operated by major dairy companies such as <a href="/wiki/Dairy_Crest" title="Dairy Crest">Dairy Crest</a>. It is normally sold as a supermarket <a href="/wiki/Store_brand" title="Store brand">own brand</a>. This version of the cheese is pasteurised but not <a href="/wiki/Processed_cheese" title="Processed cheese">processed</a>.</p>

<p>Double gloucester is often blended with other ingredients. One variety made by blending with chives and spring onions has been marketed as Cotswold cheese, though this is not a traditional English cheese name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ns1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ns1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This cheese is supposedly coloured similarly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotswold_stone" title="Cotswold stone">Cotswold stone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Huntsman cheese, also known as Stilchester, is made with alternating layers of double gloucester and <a href="/wiki/Stilton_cheese" title="Stilton cheese">Stilton</a>.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_double_and_single_names">Origin of double and single names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucester_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of double and single names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reason is not known for the two types of Gloucester cheese being called 'double' and 'single'. The main theories are:</p>
<ul>
<li>because the creamy milk had to be skimmed twice to make the double variety, or</li>
<li>because cream from the morning milk was added to the evening milk, or</li>
<li>because a double gloucester cheese is typically twice the height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cheese-rolling">Cheese-rolling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucester_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cheese-rolling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Double gloucester cheese is also used every <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Cooper%27s_Hill_Cheese-Rolling_and_Wake" title="Cooper's Hill Cheese-Rolling and Wake">Cooper's Hill Cheese-Rolling and Wake</a>, a sport considered to be dangerous due to the lengthy, steep Gloucestershire hillside<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> (50% downhill gradient at over 200 m) in which the event takes place.</p>
