<p><b>Glynis Leanne Nunn-Cearns</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" title="Née">née</a> <b>Saunders</b>, formerly <b>Nunn</b>), <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">OAM</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oam_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oam-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (born 4 December 1960) is a former <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Heptathlon" title="Heptathlon">heptathlete</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> champion in the event. Born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toowoomba,_Queensland" title="Toowoomba, Queensland">Toowoomba, Queensland</a>, she began competing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a> at age 9, when she was a student at <a href="/wiki/Toowoomba_South_State_School" title="Toowoomba South State School">Toowoomba South State School</a>.</p>
<p>She starred in several events, and was thus a natural competitor in the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_pentathlon" title="Women's pentathlon">pentathlon</a> (which was replaced by the heptathlon in 1981). In 1978, she qualified for the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Games" title="Commonwealth Games">Commonwealth Games</a>, but could not compete because of an injury.</p>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/1982_Commonwealth_Games" title="1982 Commonwealth Games">1982 Commonwealth Games</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, she had married decathlete <a href="/wiki/Chris_Nunn" title="Chris Nunn">Chris Nunn</a>, and that year the couple moved to <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> where Chris was studying for a physical education degree at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Australia" title="University of South Australia">South Australian College of Advanced Education</a>. In the first heptathlon competition at the Games, she upset the English favourite and took the title. At the inaugural <a href="/wiki/IAAF_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="IAAF World Championships in Athletics">World Championships</a> a year later, she was placed 7th.</p>
<p>Because of the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics_boycott" title="1984 Summer Olympics boycott">1984 Summer Olympics boycott</a>, Nunn was one of the medal candidates for the Olympic title, too. The competition was incredibly close, with five athletes fighting for the medals. After the competition, there was confusion about who had won, but when the smoke cleared, Nunn had scored 6390 points, five more than runner-up <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Joyner-Kersee" title="Jackie Joyner-Kersee">Jackie Joyner-Kersee</a>. In addition to her gold medal, Nunn was also placed fifth in the 100 m hurdles event, and seventh in the <a href="/wiki/Long_jump" title="Long jump">long jump</a>.</p>
<p>After the Olympics, Nunn abandoned the heptathlon, and switched to hurdling. She was hampered by many injuries, but managed to win a bronze medal in the high hurdles event at the 1986 Commonwealth Games. She quit sports in 1990.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glynis_Nunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1985, Nunn received a Medal of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">Order of Australia</a> and was inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_Australia_Hall_of_Fame" title="Sport Australia Hall of Fame">Sport Australia Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oam_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oam-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She received an <a href="/wiki/Australian_Sports_Medal" title="Australian Sports Medal">Australian Sports Medal</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
