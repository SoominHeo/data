<p><b>Glycerides</b>, more correctly known as <b>acylglycerols</b>, are <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> formed from <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>.</p>
<p>Glycerol has three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional groups</a>, which can be esterified with one, two, or three fatty acids to form <a href="/wiki/Monoglyceride" title="Monoglyceride">monoglycerides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diglyceride" title="Diglyceride">diglycerides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">Vegetable oils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_fat" title="Animal fat">animal fats</a> contain mostly triglycerides, but are broken down by natural <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lipase" title="Lipase">lipases</a>) into mono and diglycerides and free fatty acids and glycerol.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">Soaps</a> are formed from the reaction of glycerides with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. The product of the reaction is glycerol and salts of fatty acids. Fatty acids in the soap <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsify</a> the oils in dirt, enabling the removal of oily dirt with water.</p>
<p><b>Partial glycerides</b> are esters of glycerol with fatty acids, where not all the hydroxyl groups are esterified. Since some of their hydroxyl groups are free their molecules are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity#Large_molecules_with_both_a_polar_and_a_non-polar_end" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a>. Partial glycerides may be <a href="/wiki/Monoglyceride" title="Monoglyceride">monoglycerides</a> (two hydroxyl groups free) or <a href="/wiki/Diglyceride" title="Diglyceride">diglycerides</a> (one hydroxyl group free). Short chain partial glycerides are more strongly polar than long chain partial glycerides, and have excellent solvent properties for many hard-to-solubilize drugs, making them valuable as <a href="/wiki/Excipient" title="Excipient">excipients</a> in improving the formulation of certain pharmaceuticals. The most common forms of acylglycerol are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triglycerides" title="Triglycerides">triglycerides</a>, having high caloric value and usually yielding twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrate. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acylglyceride_linkage">Acylglyceride linkage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glyceride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Acylglyceride linkage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <b>acylglyceride linkage</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bond</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Organic_acid" title="Organic acid">organic acid</a> groups (such as <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acid</a>) and one of the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_groups" title="Hydroxyl groups">hydroxyl groups</a> of <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
