<p><b>Gliese 832</b> (<b>Gl 832</b> or <b>GJ 832</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">spectral type</a> M2V in the southern <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Grus_(constellation)" title="Grus (constellation)">Grus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_visual_magnitude" title="Apparent visual magnitude">apparent visual magnitude</a> of 8.66<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailey08_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-bailey08-2">[2]</a></sup> means that it is too faint to be seen with the naked eye. It is located relatively close to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, at a distance of 16.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup> and has a high <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a> of 818.93 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliarcsecond" title="Milliarcsecond">mas</a> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa575_A35_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aa575_A35-12">[10]</a></sup> Gliese 832 has just under half the mass and radius of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup> Its estimated rotation period is a relatively leisurely 46 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnras452_3_2745_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mnras452_3_2745-3">[3]</a></sup> The star is roughly 9.5 billion years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-age-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Gliese 832 was announced to be hosting the closest potentially <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">habitable</a> Earth-mass-range <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanet</a> to the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup> This star achieved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> some 52,920 years ago when it came within an estimated 15.71 ly (4.817 pc) of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa575_A35_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-aa575_A35-12">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_system">Planetary system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_832&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Planetary system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gliese 832 hosts two known planets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_Jupiter_mass_planet">Discovery of Jupiter mass planet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_832&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery of Jupiter mass planet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2008, it was announced that a <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>-like planet, now designated as <a href="/wiki/Gliese_832_b" title="Gliese 832 b">Gliese 832 b</a>, had been detected in a long-period, near-circular orbit around this star (false alarm probability thus far: a negligible 0.05%). It would induce an <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometric</a> perturbation on its star of at least 0.95 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">milliarcseconds</a> and is thus a good candidate for being detected by astrometric observations. Despite its relatively large angular distance, direct imaging is problematic due to the star–planet contrast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailey08_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-bailey08-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_Gliese_832_c_.28super-Earth_mass_planet.29_in_habitable_zone">Discovery of Gliese 832 c (super-Earth mass planet) in habitable zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_832&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery of Gliese 832 c (super-Earth mass planet) in habitable zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, a second planet was discovered by astronomers at the University of New South Wales. This one is believed to be of super-Earth mass<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup> and has since been given the scientific name <a href="/wiki/Gliese_832_c" title="Gliese 832 c">Gliese 832 c</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup> It was announced to orbit in the optimistic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> but outside the conservative habitable zone of its parent star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittenmyer14_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wittenmyer14-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The planet is believed to be in, or very close to, the right distance from its sun to allow liquid water to exist on its surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Wall_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Wall-11">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_for_cometary_disc">Search for cometary disc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_832&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Search for cometary disc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If this system has a comet disc, it is undetectable "brighter than the fractional dust luminosity 10<sup>−5</sup>" of a recent Herschel study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nodebris_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nodebris-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
