<p><b>Gliese 876</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a> approximately 15 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aquarius_(constellation)" title="Aquarius (constellation)">Aquarius</a>.</p>
<p>It is the second-closest-known star to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> confirmed to possess a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary system</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Gliese_674" title="Gliese 674">Gliese 674</a>) and the closest such system known to consist of multiple planets. As of 2011, four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planets</a> have been found to orbit the star.</p>
<p>The planetary system is also notable for the orbital properties of its planets. It is the only known system of orbital companions to exhibit a triple conjunction in the rare phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance#Types_of_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">Laplace resonance</a> (a type of resonance first noted in <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>'s inner three <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">Galilean moons</a>). It is also the first extrasolar system around a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_star" title="Normal star">normal star</a> with measured <a href="/wiki/Coplanarity" title="Coplanarity">coplanarity</a>.</p>
<p>Two of the middle planets are located in the system's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a>; however, they are <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a> believed to be analogous to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_and_visibility">Distance and visibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_876&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distance and visibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gliese 876 is located fairly close to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometric</a> measurements made by the <a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">Hipparcos</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>, the star shows a <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> of 213.28 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">milliarcseconds</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Leeuwen2007_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-van_Leeuwen2007-1">[1]</a></sup> which corresponds to a distance of 4.69 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> (15.3 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">ly</a>), currently making it the third-closest known star with orbiting planets, after <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_Eridani" title="Epsilon Eridani">Epsilon Eridani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gliese_674" title="Gliese 674">Gliese 674</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-correia2010_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-correia2010-8">[6]</a></sup> Despite being located so close to Earth, the star is so faint that it is invisible to the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a> and can only be seen using a <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_characteristics">Stellar characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_876&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stellar characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a red dwarf, Gliese 876 is much less massive than the Sun: estimates suggest it has only 32% of the mass of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marcy1998_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-marcy1998-10">[8]</a></sup> The surface <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of Gliese 876 is cooler than the Sun and the star has a smaller radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson1983_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-johnson1983-11">[9]</a></sup> These factors combine to make the star only 1.24% as <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminous</a> as the Sun, and most of this is at <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a>.</p>
<p>Estimating the age and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-rich" title="Metal-rich">metallicity</a> of cool stars is difficult due to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecule" title="Diatomic molecule">diatomic molecules</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheres</a>, which makes the <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> extremely complex. By fitting the observed spectrum to model spectra, it is estimated that Gliese 876 has a slightly lower abundance of heavy elements compared to the Sun (around 75% the solar abundance of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bean2006_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-bean2006-6">[4]</a></sup> Based on <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">chromospheric</a> activity the star is likely to be around 6.5 to 9.9 billion years old, depending on the theoretical model used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saffe2005_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-saffe2005-9">[7]</a></sup> However, the low rotational period of the star as well as its membership among the young disk population suggest that the star is between 0.1–5 billion years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-correia2010_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-correia2010-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like many low-mass stars, Gliese 876 is a <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable star</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Variable_star_designation" title="Variable star designation">variable star designation</a> is IL Aquarii and it is classified as a <a href="/wiki/BY_Draconis_variable" title="BY Draconis variable">BY Draconis variable</a>. Its brightness fluctuates by around 0.04 <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitudes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcvs_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gcvs-12">[10]</a></sup> This type of variability is thought to be caused by large <a href="/wiki/Starspot" title="Starspot">starspots</a> moving in and out of view as the star rotates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bopp1973_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bopp1973-13">[11]</a></sup> Gliese 876 emits X-rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmitt1995_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmitt1995-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_system">Planetary system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_876&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Planetary system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_history">Observation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_876&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Observation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 23, 1998, an extrasolar planet was announced in <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around Gliese 876 by two independent teams led by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Marcy" title="Geoffrey Marcy">Geoffrey Marcy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marcy1998_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-marcy1998-10">[8]</a></sup> and Xavier Delfosse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delfosse1998_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-delfosse1998-15">[13]</a></sup> The planet was designated <a href="/wiki/Gliese_876_b" title="Gliese 876 b">Gliese 876 b</a> and was detected by <a href="/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy" title="Doppler spectroscopy">Doppler spectroscopy</a>.</p>
<p>Based on luminosity measurement, the circumstellar habitable zone (CHZ) is believed to be located between 0.116 and 0.227 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones2005_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones2005-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 4, 2001, a second planet designated Gliese 876 c was detected, inside the orbit of the previously-discovered planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marcy2001_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-marcy2001-17">[15]</a></sup> The relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital periods</a> initially disguised the planet's radial velocity signature as an increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">orbital eccentricity</a> of the outer planet. Eugenio Rivera and J. Lissauer found that the two planets undergo strong gravitational interactions as they orbit the star, causing the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_elements" title="Orbital elements">orbital elements</a> to change rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rivera2001_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-rivera2001-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 13, 2005, further observations by a team led by Rivera revealed a third planet, designated <a href="/wiki/Gliese_876_d" title="Gliese 876 d">Gliese 876 d</a> inside the orbits of the two Jupiter-size planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rivera2005_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rivera2005-19">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2009, the mutual inclination between planets b and c was determined using a combination of radial velocity and <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometric</a> measurements. The planets were found to be almost coplanar, with an angle of only 5.0<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+3.9<br/>
−2.3</span>° between their orbital planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bean2009_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-bean2009-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 23, 2010, astronomers announced a fourth planet, designated <a href="/wiki/Gliese_876_e" title="Gliese 876 e">Gliese 876 e</a>. This discovery better constrained the mass and orbital properties of the other three planets, including the high eccentricity of the innermost planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rivera2010_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-rivera2010-21">[19]</a></sup> This also filled out the system inside e's orbit; additional planets there would be unstable at this system's age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerlach_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerlach-22">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, evidence of the presence of newly Gliese 876 f and Gliese 876 g was discovered. These planets have been having almost the same mass as Gliese 876d.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>If this system has a comet disc, it is undetectable "brighter than the fractional dust luminosity 10<sup>−5</sup>" of a recent Herschel study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nodebris_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nodebris-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>None of these planets <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transit</a> the star from the perspective of Earth, making it difficult to study their properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_arrangement">Orbital arrangement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gliese_876&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orbital arrangement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gliese876Orbits.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="640" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gliese876Orbits.svg/220px-Gliese876Orbits.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gliese876Orbits.svg/330px-Gliese876Orbits.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gliese876Orbits.svg/440px-Gliese876Orbits.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gliese876Orbits.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The orbits of the planets of Gliese 876. Note that the strong gravitational interactions between the planets causes rapid orbital precession, so this diagram is only valid at the stated epoch.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Gliese 876 has a notable orbital arrangement. It is the first planetary system around a normal star to have mutual inclination between planets measured without transits (previously the mutual inclination of the planets orbiting the <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a> <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1257%2B12" title="PSR B1257+12">PSR B1257+12</a> had been determined by measuring their gravitational interactions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-konacki2003_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-konacki2003-26">[24]</a></sup>). Later measurements reduced the value of the mutual inclination,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-correia2010_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-correia2010-8">[6]</a></sup> and in the latest four-planet models the incorporation mutual inclinations does not result in significant improvements relative to coplanar solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rivera2010_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-rivera2010-21">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The system has the second known example of a Laplace resonance with a 1:2:4 resonance of its planets. The first known example was Jupiter's closest <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">Galilean moons</a> - <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>. Numerical integration indicates that the coplanar, four-planet system is stable for at least another billion years. This planetary system comes close to a triple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">conjunction</a> between the three outer planets once per orbit of the outermost planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rivera2010_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-rivera2010-21">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Gliese 876 c, discovered in 2001, is a giant planet at 0.62 Jupiter-mass planet. It is in a 1:2 <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonance</a> with the outermost known planet, taking 30.340 days to orbit the star. The planet orbits within the habitable zone. Its mass makes it more likely to be a Class II planet in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudarsky_extrasolar_planet_classification" title="Sudarsky extrasolar planet classification">Sudarsky extrasolar planet classification</a>. The presence of surface liquid water is possible on sufficiently massive satellites should they exist.</p>

<p>Gliese 876 b, discovered in 1998 is around twice the mass of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and revolves around its star in an orbit taking approximately 61 <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a> to complete, at a distance of only 0.208 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>, less than the distance from the Sun to <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler2006_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-butler2006-30">[27]</a></sup> Its mass makes it more likely to be a Class II or Class III planet in the Sudarsky model. The presence of surface liquid water is possible on sufficiently massive satellites should they exist.</p>
