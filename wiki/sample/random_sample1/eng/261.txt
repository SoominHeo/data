<p>The <b>1974 World Series</b> matched the two-time defending champions <a href="/wiki/1974_Oakland_Athletics_season" title="1974 Oakland Athletics season">Oakland Athletics</a> against the <a href="/wiki/1974_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1974 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> with the A’s winning the Series in five games.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> figured in three of the four Oakland victories, posting a win and two saves, and was honored as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Series_MVP" title="World Series MVP">Series MVP</a>. Oakland became the first team to win three consecutive Series since the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> won five in a row between <a href="/wiki/1949_World_Series" title="1949 World Series">1949</a> and <a href="/wiki/1953_World_Series" title="1953 World Series">1953</a>; the win secured the Athletics' status as one of the truly dominant teams of the 1970s. (The other "team of the decade," the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>, would check in with consecutive Series victories in <a href="/wiki/1975_World_Series" title="1975 World Series">1975</a> and <a href="/wiki/1976_World_Series" title="1976 World Series">1976</a>.)</p>
<p>The 1974 Fall Classic was the first all-<a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> World Series. These two teams would meet again in the fall classic 14 years later in <a href="/wiki/1988_World_Series" title="1988 World Series">1988</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1974_World_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1974_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1974 Los Angeles Dodgers season">1974 Los Angeles Dodgers</a> were the second (after the runner-up 1962 squad) Dodger team to win at least 100 games since moving west from Brooklyn; they won the <a href="/wiki/National_League_West" title="National League West">National League West</a> division after a season long battle by four games over the <a href="/wiki/1974_Cincinnati_Reds_season" title="1974 Cincinnati Reds season">Cincinnati Reds</a>, then defeated the <a href="/wiki/1974_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1974 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>, three games to one, in the <a href="/wiki/1974_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1974 National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a>. The <a href="/wiki/1974_Oakland_Athletics_season" title="1974 Oakland Athletics season">Oakland A's</a> won the <a href="/wiki/American_League_West" title="American League West">American League West</a> division by five games over the <a href="/wiki/1974_Texas_Rangers_season" title="1974 Texas Rangers season">Texas Rangers</a>, then defeated the <a href="/wiki/1974_Baltimore_Orioles_season" title="1974 Baltimore Orioles season">Baltimore Orioles</a>, three games to one, in the <a href="/wiki/1974_American_League_Championship_Series" title="1974 American League Championship Series">American League Championship Series</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oakland_A.27s">Oakland A's</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1974_World_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Oakland A's">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland A's</a>, dynasty or not, had become something of a running joke in the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>, consistently matching their on-field heroics with locker-room meltdowns and intra-squad theatrics. After yet another banner year (<a href="/wiki/Catfish_Hunter" title="Catfish Hunter">Catfish Hunter</a> won 25 games, <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a> knocked in 105 runs, and <a href="/wiki/Billy_North" title="Billy North">Billy North</a> stole 54 bases), the team seemed to be winning more attention for its dysfunctional communication patterns than for its superior lineup. Hunter was threatening to file for free agency in 1975 if owner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_O._Finley" title="Charles O. Finley">Charles O. Finley</a> didn't come through with back pay Hunter claimed he had coming. Traumatized former second baseman <a href="/wiki/Mike_Andrews" title="Mike Andrews">Mike Andrews</a> was considering legal action for the shabby treatment he received in the previous year's <a href="/wiki/1973_World_Series" title="1973 World Series">World Series</a>. Teammates <a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue_Moon_Odom" title="Blue Moon Odom">Blue Moon Odom</a> had gotten into a fistfight. And the Series-winning manager of the previous year, <a href="/wiki/Dick_Williams" title="Dick Williams">Dick Williams</a> was long gone, having had one personality clash too many with the A's front office. Despite all the melodrama, or perhaps because of it, the A's won the A.L. pennant for the third straight year, dispatching the <a href="/wiki/1974_Baltimore_Orioles_season" title="1974 Baltimore Orioles season">Baltimore Orioles</a> in a <a href="/wiki/1974_American_League_Championship_Series" title="1974 American League Championship Series">four-game ALCS</a>. Despite all of the off field troubles, between the lines. They were fundamentally sound, had strong pitching, and combined speed and power.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Los_Angeles_Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1974_World_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Los Angeles Dodgers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The businesslike <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>, for their part, looked downright corporate, and seemed, to many, to be the better team. On paper, at least, they were. L.A. won 102 games, boasted the best team ERA in the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>, and led their league in runs scored. <a href="/wiki/Steve_Garvey" title="Steve Garvey">Steve Garvey</a> hit .312, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Wynn" title="Jimmy Wynn">Jimmy Wynn</a> knocked in 108 runs, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Buckner" title="Bill Buckner">Bill Buckner</a> hit .314 in one of the best years of his career, <a href="/wiki/Davey_Lopes" title="Davey Lopes">Davey Lopes</a> stole 59 bases, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Messersmith" title="Andy Messersmith">Andy Messersmith</a> won 20 games, and <a href="/wiki/Don_Sutton" title="Don Sutton">Don Sutton</a> won 19. It was all very brisk and efficient—Big (Dodger) Blue, with the unflappable, perennially studious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Alston" title="Walt Alston">Walt Alston</a>, in his twenty-first year as manager of the club, holding everything together. Unlike the Oakland squad, nobody in the Dodger clubhouse appeared to need therapy to work out any simmering interpersonal issues. The Series promised, at the very least, to be a memorable conflict of worldviews. However, as the World Series would prove, there was no substitute for experience; the A's did not beat themselves and the Dodgers made a number of mental errors.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a> put the A's on the board first with a solo homer in the top of the second off 20-game winner <a href="/wiki/Andy_Messersmith" title="Andy Messersmith">Andy Messersmith</a>. The A's added another run in the fifth when starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Ken_Holtzman" title="Ken Holtzman">Ken Holtzman</a>, batting for the first time all season because of the <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> rule, doubled to left, went to third on a Messersmith wild pitch, and scored on a <a href="/wiki/Bert_Campaneris" title="Bert Campaneris">Bert Campaneris</a> suicide squeeze bunt.</p>
<p>The Dodgers <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_baseball_(C)#crawl_back_into_the_game" title="Glossary of baseball (C)">crept back</a> with a run in their half of the fifth when <a href="/wiki/Davey_Lopes" title="Davey Lopes">Davey Lopes</a> reached first on an error by shortstop Campaneris. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Buckner" title="Bill Buckner">Bill Buckner</a> then bounced a single to right that Jackson misplayed, allowing Lopes to score.</p>
<p>The A's scored their final run in the eighth when Campaneris singled to shallow center, was sacrificed to second by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_North" title="Bill North">Bill North</a>, and scored when Dodger third baseman <a href="/wiki/Ron_Cey" title="Ron Cey">Ron Cey</a> threw wildly to first on a grounder hit by <a href="/wiki/Sal_Bando" title="Sal Bando">Sal Bando</a>. Bando reached third on the error, and attempted to score on a flyout to right by Jackson, but right fielder <a href="/wiki/Joe_Ferguson_(baseball)" title="Joe Ferguson (baseball)">Joe Ferguson</a> gunned him down at the plate.</p>
<p>In the bottom of the ninth, with <a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> on the mound, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Wynn" title="Jimmy Wynn">Jimmy Wynn</a> hit a solo homer that just escaped the reach of <a href="/wiki/Joe_Rudi" title="Joe Rudi">Joe Rudi</a> and North in left center. <a href="/wiki/Catfish_Hunter" title="Catfish Hunter">Catfish Hunter</a> relieved Fingers and made the final out by striking out Ferguson. Fingers got the win with <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">4<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> innings of relief, having relieved Holtzman in the fifth.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Don_Sutton" title="Don Sutton">Don Sutton</a> pitched superbly, shutting out the A's on four hits through eight innings. The Dodgers scored first in the second off <a href="/wiki/Vida_Blue" title="Vida Blue">Vida Blue</a> when <a href="/wiki/Ron_Cey" title="Ron Cey">Ron Cey</a> walked, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Russell_(baseball)" title="Bill Russell (baseball)">Bill Russell</a> singled, and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Yeager" title="Steve Yeager">Steve Yeager</a> singled home Cey. <a href="/wiki/Joe_Ferguson_(baseball)" title="Joe Ferguson (baseball)">Joe Ferguson</a> slammed a two-run homer off Blue in the sixth to make it 3–0.</p>
<p>The A's threatened in the eighth when <a href="/wiki/Pinch_hitter" title="Pinch hitter">pinch hitters</a> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Holt_(baseball)" title="Jim Holt (baseball)">Jim Holt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claudell_Washington" title="Claudell Washington">Claudell Washington</a> hit back-to-back singles with one out. <a href="/wiki/Bert_Campaneris" title="Bert Campaneris">Bert Campaneris</a> reached on an infield error to load the bases. The rally was squelched when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_North" title="Bill North">Bill North</a> hit into a double play in which first baseman <a href="/wiki/Steve_Garvey" title="Steve Garvey">Steve Garvey</a> made an exceptional pickup of a low throw by Russell.</p>
<p>In the ninth, the A's finally got on the board when <a href="/wiki/Sal_Bando" title="Sal Bando">Sal Bando</a> led off with being hit by a pitch, <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a> followed with a double, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Rudi" title="Joe Rudi">Joe Rudi</a> singled both runners home. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Marshall_(pitcher)" title="Mike Marshall (pitcher)">Mike Marshall</a> then relieved Sutton and struck out <a href="/wiki/Gene_Tenace" title="Gene Tenace">Gene Tenace</a>. <a href="/wiki/Herb_Washington" title="Herb Washington">Herb Washington</a> was sent in to pinch-run for Rudi, and Marshall picked him off. Herb Washington was the only "designated runner" to appear in a World Series. Washington was a track star with no baseball experience whom the A's <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Finley" title="Charlie Finley">Charles Finley</a> signed solely to appear as a pinch runner in late game situations. His stats for 1974 were 92 games, zero at bats, 29 runs scored, 29 stolen bases, but he was caught stealing 16 times. Sure enough, with the A's trailing 3–2 in the ninth inning of Game 2, Washington pinch ran. Just after announcer Vin Scully said that Washington better be careful because pitcher Mike Marshall has a good pickoff move, he was promptly picked off first base. Marshall then retired <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_Mangual" title="Ángel Mangual">Ángel Mangual</a> for the final out.</p>

<p>With the Series shifting back to Oakland, it was A's ace <a href="/wiki/Catfish_Hunter" title="Catfish Hunter">Catfish Hunter</a>'s turn to be brilliant. Hunter shut out the Dodgers on four hits through seven innings, and his teammates provided him with two runs in the third when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_North" title="Bill North">Bill North</a> scored on an error by catcher <a href="/wiki/Joe_Ferguson_(baseball)" title="Joe Ferguson (baseball)">Joe Ferguson</a> and an RBI single by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Rudi" title="Joe Rudi">Joe Rudi</a>. The A's added another run in the fourth on <a href="/wiki/Bert_Campaneris" title="Bert Campaneris">Bert Campaneris</a>' RBI single.</p>
<p>The Dodgers got their only runs on solo homers by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Buckner" title="Bill Buckner">Bill Buckner</a> in the eighth and <a href="/wiki/Willie_Crawford" title="Willie Crawford">Willie Crawford</a> in the ninth.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> was not awarded the save because in 1974, tougher criteria were adopted for saves where the tying run had to be on base or at the plate when the reliever entered to qualify for a save.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The A's struck first on a solo homer by the surprising <a href="/wiki/Ken_Holtzman" title="Ken Holtzman">Ken Holtzman</a> in the third. The Dodgers came right back in their half of the fourth on a two-run triple by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Russell_(baseball)" title="Bill Russell (baseball)">Bill Russell</a>. Holtzman settled into a groove after that, surrendering no runs before yielding to <a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> in the eighth.</p>
<p>The A's took control of the game with four runs in the sixth off <a href="/wiki/Andy_Messersmith" title="Andy Messersmith">Andy Messersmith</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_North" title="Bill North">Bill North</a> led off with a walk and went to second on a wild pickoff throw by Messersmith. <a href="/wiki/Sal_Bando" title="Sal Bando">Sal Bando</a> singled home North to tie it. After a <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a> walk, a <a href="/wiki/Joe_Rudi" title="Joe Rudi">Joe Rudi</a> sacrifice bunt, and an intentional walk to <a href="/wiki/Claudell_Washington" title="Claudell Washington">Claudell Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Holt_(baseball)" title="Jim Holt (baseball)">Jim Holt</a> pinch-hit for <a href="/wiki/Ray_Fosse" title="Ray Fosse">Ray Fosse</a> and singled in two runs. An RBI groundout by <a href="/wiki/Dick_Green" title="Dick Green">Dick Green</a> capped off the rally. Fingers pitched the final <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>2</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> innings for his first save of the series.</p>

<p>The A's staked <a href="/wiki/Vida_Blue" title="Vida Blue">Vida Blue</a> to a 2–0 lead with single runs in the first and second innings on a sacrifice fly by <a href="/wiki/Sal_Bando" title="Sal Bando">Sal Bando</a> and a solo homer by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Fosse" title="Ray Fosse">Ray Fosse</a>. The Dodgers tied it in the sixth with two runs on a sacrifice fly by <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Wynn" title="Jimmy Wynn">Jimmy Wynn</a> and an RBI single by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Garvey" title="Steve Garvey">Steve Garvey</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joe_Rudi" title="Joe Rudi">Joe Rudi</a> got what would turn out to be the game-winning RBI when he tagged Dodger ace reliever <a href="/wiki/Mike_Marshall_(pitcher)" title="Mike Marshall (pitcher)">Mike Marshall</a> with a solo homer in the seventh. On a cool, damp night in Oakland, Marshall, who always pitched in short sleeves, disdained his allowance of eight warm up pitches after coming in from the bullpen. Rudi hit his first pitch out. In the Dodgers half of the eighth, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Buckner" title="Bill Buckner">Bill Buckner</a> led off with a base hit to center that got past center fielder <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_North" title="Bill North">Bill North</a>. Buckner had an easy double, but tried to stretch it to a triple and <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a>, backing up North, fired a perfect throw to <a href="/wiki/Dick_Green" title="Dick Green">Dick Green</a>, who relayed to <a href="/wiki/Sal_Bando" title="Sal Bando">Sal Bando</a> at third to nail Buckner and squelch the last Dodger threat. In his youthful exuberance, Buckner broke the unwritten baseball rule to never make the first or third out at third base. It was even more critical given that he was the potential tying run. <a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> got his second save and was named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Series_MVP" title="World Series MVP">Series MVP</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1974_World_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Four of the five games had the score of 3–2, and in all five games the losing team scored two runs. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Marshall_(pitcher)" title="Mike Marshall (pitcher)">Mike Marshall</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> was the only pitcher to appear in all five games of a five-game World Series, as of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1974_World_Series&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The Dodgers got six more hits than the A's but scored five fewer runs, showing their lack of timely hitting. They also made six errors in five games, in addition to a number of mental errors.</p>
<p>The A's won the Series despite batting only .211 in the Series, the lowest in a five-game Series since the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> hit .146 in the <a href="/wiki/1969_World_Series" title="1969 World Series">1969</a> Series.</p>
<p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1974_World_Series&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the A's are the only non-Yankee team to win three consecutive World Series.</p>
