<p>The <b>Ny Carlsberg Glyptotek</b> (<i>Glypto</i>-, from the Greek root glyphein, to carve and <i>theke</i>, a storing-place) is an <a href="/wiki/Art_museum" title="Art museum">art museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. The collection is built around the personal collection of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jacobsen" title="Carl Jacobsen">Carl Jacobsen</a> (1842â1914), the son of the founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlsberg_Breweries" title="Carlsberg Breweries">Carlsberg Breweries</a>.</p>
<p>Primarily a sculpture museum as indicated by the name, the focal point of the museum is antique sculpture from the ancient cultures around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> including Egypt, Rome and Greece, as well as more modern sculptures such as a collection of <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">Rodin</a> works which is considered the most important outside France<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (September 2016)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. However, the museum is equally noted for its collection of painting that includes an extensive collection of <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">French impressionists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">Post-impressionists</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Danish_Painting" title="Golden Age of Danish Painting">Danish Golden Age paintings</a>.</p>
<p>The French Collection includes works by painters such as <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monet" title="Monet">Monet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camille_Pissarro" title="Camille Pissarro">Pissarro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Renoir</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degas" title="Degas">Degas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul CÃ©zanne">CÃ©zanne</a>, as well as those by <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">Post-impressionists</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Gogh" title="Van Gogh">van Gogh</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Toulouse-Lautrec">Toulouse-Lautrec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">Bonnard</a>. The museum's collection includes all the bronze sculptures of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degas" title="Degas">Degas</a>, including the series of dancers. Numerous works by Norwegian-Danish sculptor <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Sinding" title="Stephan Sinding">Stephan Sinding</a> are featured prominently in various sections of the museum.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_Glyptoteque">The first Glyptoteque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The first Glyptoteque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Jacobsen" title="Carl Jacobsen">Carl Jacobsen</a> was a dedicated art collector. He was particularly interested in antique art, but over the years he also acquired a considerable collection of French and Danish sculptures. When his <a href="/wiki/Carlsberg_(district)#Carl.27s_Villa" title="Carlsberg (district)">private villa</a> in 1882 was extended with a winter garden, sculptures soon outnumbered plants in it. The same year the collection was opened to the public. In the following years the museum was expanded on a number of occasions to meet the need for more space for his steadily growing collections. In 1885 his 'house museum' had grown to a total of 19 <a href="/wiki/Art_museum#Galleries_in_museums" title="Art museum">galleries</a>, the first 14 of which had been designed by Vilhelm Dahlerup while Hack Kampmann had built the last four as well as conducted a redesign of the winter garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_new_museum">The new museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The new museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In spite of the many extensions, it was finally clear the existing premises were inadequate and that a new building was needed. On 8 March 1888 Carl Jacobsen donated his collection to the Danish State and the City of Copenhagen on condition that they provided a suitable building for its exhibition. Copenhagen's <a href="/wiki/Fortifications_of_Copenhagen_(17th_century)" title="Fortifications of Copenhagen (17th century)">old fortifications</a> had recently been abandoned and a site was chosen on a <a href="/wiki/Ravelin" title="Ravelin">ravelin</a> outside <i>Holcks Bastion</i> in the city's <a href="/wiki/Fortifications_of_Copenhagen_(17th_century)#Vestervold" title="Fortifications of Copenhagen (17th century)">Western Rampart</a>, just south of the <a href="/wiki/Tivoli_Gardens" title="Tivoli Gardens">Tivoli Gardens</a> which had been founded in 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Jacobsen was displeased with the location which he found to be too far from the city centre and he had also reservations about the proximity of Tivoli which he found common. Instead he wanted a building on the emerging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_City_Hall_Square,_Copenhagen" title="The City Hall Square, Copenhagen">new city hall square</a>, yet in the end he accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was Carl Jacobsen who chose the name for the museum, with inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Glyptothek" title="Glyptothek">Glyptothek</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, as well as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wilhelm_Dahlerup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wilhelm Dahlerup (page does not exist)">Wilhelm Dahlerup</a> as the architect for the assignment. The <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a> around the radan was filled and the new museum opened first on 1 May 1897. At first it only included Jacobsen's modern collection with French and Danish works from the 18th century.</p>
<p>In January 1899 Carl Jacobsen donated his collection of Antique art to the museum which made an expansion necessary. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Hack_Kampmann" title="Hack Kampmann">Hack Kampmann</a> while Dahlerup designed a winter garden which connected the new wing to the old building. It was inaugurated in 1906.</p>
<p>In 1996 the museum was once again extended, this time with an infill constructed in one of its courtyards to the design of <a href="/wiki/Henning_Larsen" title="Henning Larsen">Henning Larsen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2006, the building underwent a major renovation programme under the direction of Danish architects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissing_%2B_Weitling" title="Dissing + Weitling">Dissing + Weitling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bonde_Ljungar_Arkitekter_MAA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bonde Ljungar Arkitekter MAA (page does not exist)">Bonde Ljungar Arkitekter MAA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The building is often noted for its elegance in its own right and the synthesis it creates with the works of art.</p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Dahlerup" title="Vilhelm Dahlerup">Dahlerup</a> Wing</b>, the oldest part of the museum, is a lavish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historicism_(architecture)" title="Historicism (architecture)">historicist</a> building. The faÃ§ade is in red brick with polished <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> <a href="/wiki/Column" title="Column">columns</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Venetian renaissance</a> style. It houses the French and Danish collections.</p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Hack_Kampmann" title="Hack Kampmann">Kampmann</a> Wing</b> is a more simple, <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neo-classical</a> building, built as a series of galleries around a central auditorium used for lectures, small concerts, symposiums and poetry readings.</p>
<p>The two wings are connected by the <b>Winter Garden</b> with <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> floors, tall palms, a fountain and topped by a dome made in copper and wrought iron.</p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Henning_Larsen" title="Henning Larsen">Henning Larsen</a> Wing</b> is a minimalistic infill, built in a former inner courtyard and affording access to the roof. Official meetings and banquets sometimes take place in the Glyptotek, such as the certification of Polio-free Europe, 21 June 2002.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections">Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ny Carlsberg Glyptotek's collections comprise more than 10,000 works of art.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antique_collection">Antique collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Antique collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Antique collection displays sculptures and other antiquities from the ancient cultures around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>.</p>
<p>The extensive <b>Greek, Roman and Etruscan Collection</b> comprises marble statues, small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terra_cotta" title="Terra cotta">terra cotta</a> statues, <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">reliefs</a>, pottery and other artifacts. The Etruscan collection is the largest outside Italy. The German archeologist <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Helbig" title="Wolfgang Helbig">Wolfgang Helbig</a> was Carl Jacobsen's broker in Rome for 25 years, acquiring more than 950 sculptures and Etruscan antiquities for the Ny Carlsberg Museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>Egyptian Collection</b> comprises more than 1,900 pieces, dating from 3000 BCE to the 1st century CE and representing both <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Egypt_(Roman_province)" title="Egypt (Roman province)">Roman Period</a>. It was founded in 1882 when Carl Jacobsen made his first Egyptian acquisition, a <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">Sarcophagus</a> purchased from the Egyptian Museum in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. Many of the objects in the collection were augmented when the Ny Carlsberg Foundation sponsored excavations in Egypt in the beginning of the 20th century led by the English <a href="/wiki/Egyptology" title="Egyptology">Egyptologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Flinders_Petrie" title="Flinders Petrie">W. M. F. Petrie</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The holdings include several <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummies</a>, displayed in a <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a>-like gallery below the normal galleries.</p>
<p>The <b>Near Eastern Collection</b> spans a period of 7150 years, the oldest artifact being from 6500 BCE and the youngest being from 650 CE, featuring such cultures as the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>.</p>

<p>The main focus of the <b>French Collection</b> is 19th-century French painting and sculpture. The painting collection contains works by such painters as <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">David</a> and Manet, as well as a large collection of Impressionist painters such as Monet, CÃ©zanne and Bonnard. The single painter represented with most paintings is <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a> with more than 40 works. The museum also holds a large collection of French 19th-century sculpture by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Carpeaux" title="Jean-Baptiste Carpeaux">Carpeaux</a> and Rodin, the Rodin collection being one of the largest in the world, as well as a complete collection of Degas' bronze sculptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Danish_Collection">Danish Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Danish Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Danish Collection contains a large collection of Danish Golden Age paintings by painters such as <a href="/wiki/Christoffer_Wilhelm_Eckersberg" title="Christoffer Wilhelm Eckersberg">Eckersberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christen_K%C3%B8bke" title="Christen KÃ¸bke">KÃ¸bke</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johan_Lundbye" title="Johan Lundbye">Lundbye</a>. It also contains the largest representation of <a href="/wiki/Danish_sculpture" title="Danish sculpture">Danish Golden Age Sculpture</a> in the country.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Collection">European Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: European Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The European Collection comprises works from the 18th to the 20th century. Represented sculptors include Neoclassicists such as <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Canova" title="Antonio Canova">Canova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johan_Tobias_Sergel" title="Johan Tobias Sergel">Sergel</a>, Carstens, Flaxman, Rauch and Baily, as well as Modernists like Meunier, Klinger, Picasso and Giacometti.</p>
<p>The collection also comprises a small collection of Modern paintings of artists such as Arp, Ernst, MirÃ³, Poliakoff and Gilioli.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Auditorium">Auditorium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Auditorium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerts">Concerts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Concerts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Auditorium is mainly used for classical concerts, including the <i>Helge Jacobsen concert series</i>. <i>Helge Jacobsen Concerts</i> have included the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Hagen_Quartet" title="Hagen Quartet">Hagen Quartet</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the Russian violinist <a href="/wiki/Alina_Ibragimova" title="Alina Ibragimova">Alina Ibragimova</a>, the French pianist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%A9dric_Tiberghien&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CÃ©dric Tiberghien (page does not exist)">CÃ©dric Tiberghien</a>, the Russian bariton <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Leiferkus" title="Sergei Leiferkus">Sergei Leiferkus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the French <a href="/wiki/Ysa%C3%BFe_Quartet_(1984)" title="YsaÃ¿e Quartet (1984)">YsaÃ¿e Quartet</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and German tenor <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Kaufmann" title="Jonas Kaufmann">Jonas Kaufmann</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> among others.</p>
<p>The Ny Carlsberg Glyptotek is in general noted for its good acoustics, both in the auditorium and in the surrounding long halls. The Auditorium has been used as a rehearsal room by the <a href="/wiki/Early_music" title="Early music">Early music</a> vocal ensemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musica_Ficta_(Denmark)" title="Musica Ficta (Denmark)">Musica Ficta</a>, often within opening hours of the museum, occasionally adding music to the museum experience, and it has also regularly performed concerts, both in the Auditorium and the surrounding halls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MyCPH_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-MyCPH-15">[15]</a></sup> Pioneer <a href="/wiki/Overtone_singing" title="Overtone singing">overtone singer</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Hykes" title="David Hykes">David Hykes</a> in the Ny Carlsberg Glyptotek in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MyCPH_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-MyCPH-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally the Auditorium is also used for other musical genres, such as the Danish <a href="/wiki/Klezmer" title="Klezmer">Klezmer</a> group <a href="/wiki/Mames_Babegenush" title="Mames Babegenush">Mames Babegenush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_cultural_events">Other cultural events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ny_Carlsberg_Glyptotek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other cultural events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Auditorium is also used for other cultural events, such as poetry readings, lectures and debates.</p>
