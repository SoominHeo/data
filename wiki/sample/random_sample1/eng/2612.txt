<p><b>Emperor Taizu of Jin</b> (August 1, 1068 – September 19, 1123), personal name <b>Aguda</b>, <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">sinicised</a> name <b>Wanyan Min</b>, was the founder and first emperor of the <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_people" title="Jurchen people">Jurchen</a>-led <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(1115-1234)" title="Jin dynasty (1115-1234)">Jin dynasty</a>, which ruled northern China between the 12th and 13th centuries. He was initially the chieftain of the <a href="/wiki/Wanyan" title="Wanyan">Wanyan</a> tribe, the most dominant among the Jurchen tribes which were subjects of the <a href="/wiki/Khitan_people" title="Khitan people">Khitan</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Liao_dynasty" title="Liao dynasty">Liao dynasty</a>. Starting in 1114, Aguda united the Jurchen tribes under his rule and rebelled against the Liao dynasty. A year later, he declared himself emperor and established the Jin dynasty. By the time of his death, the Jin dynasty had conquered most of the Liao dynasty's territories and emerged as a major power in northern China. In 1145, he was posthumously honoured with the <a href="/wiki/Temple_name" title="Temple name">temple name</a> <b>Taizu</b> by his descendant, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xizong_of_Jin" title="Emperor Xizong of Jin">Emperor Xizong</a>.</p>
<p>The name [Wanyan] Aguda is transcribed <b>[Wan-yen] A-ku-ta</b> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wade-Giles" title="Wade-Giles">Wade-Giles</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the alternative but official<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="according to who? (August 2016)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> spelling <b>Akutta</b> (possibly from reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_language" title="Jurchen language">Jurchen language</a>) appears in a very small number of books as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taizu_of_Jin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aguda was the second son of <a href="/wiki/Helibo" title="Helibo">Helibo</a>, the chieftain of the <a href="/wiki/Wanyan" title="Wanyan">Wanyan</a> tribe. His mother was a daughter of the chieftain of the Nalan (拏懶) tribe. He was born in 1068 near the <a href="/wiki/Ashi_River" title="Ashi River">Ashi River</a> within present-day <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heilongjiang" title="Heilongjiang">Heilongjiang Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was well-known within his tribe for his bravery, and had participated in numerous campaigns against rival Jurchen tribes at the command of the <a href="/wiki/Khitan_people" title="Khitan people">Khitan</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Liao_dynasty" title="Liao dynasty">Liao dynasty</a>. In 1109, during the height of a widespread famine, Aguda assisted his father in absorbing famished warriors from other Jurchen tribes to strengthen his own tribe. Later, he fought wars against other Jurchen tribes and succeeded to unify all Jurchens under the Wanyan tribe's leadership. In 1113, Aguda succeeded his elder brother, <a href="/wiki/Wuyashu" title="Wuyashu">Wuyashu</a>, as the leader of his tribe. Like other Jurchens, Aguda loathed what he considered the exploitation of his tribesmen by corrupt Liao officials. In 1122, when the Liao ruler, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Tianzuo_of_Liao" title="Emperor Tianzuo of Liao">Emperor Tianzuo</a>, went on a fishing expedition in Jurchen territory, he ordered all the chieftains to dance for him. Aguda became famous among the Jurchens when he was the only person who defied the order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1114, Aguda sent spies into Liao territory and prepared to revolt against the Khitan regime, which he considered decadent. His chief advisors were <a href="/wiki/Wanyan_Zonghan" title="Wanyan Zonghan">Wanyan Zonghan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wanyan_Xiyin" title="Wanyan Xiyin">Wanyan Xiyin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-franke154_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-franke154-6">[6]</a></sup> In September, Aguda rallied his tribesmen (around 2,500 men) at Liushui (流水; near present-day Lalin River in <a href="/wiki/Fuyu,_Jilin" title="Fuyu, Jilin">Fuyu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin Province</a>) and openly rebelled against the Liao dynasty. His cavalry captured Ningjiangzhou (寧江州; present-day Fuyu, Jilin Province) and defeated a 7,000-strong Liao army at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Chuhedian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battle of Chuhedian (page does not exist)">Battle of Chuhedian</a> in November. In January 1115, following a series of military successes, Aguda proclaimed himself emperor, established the Jin dynasty, and adopted the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_era_name" title="Chinese era name">regnal name</a> "Shouguo" (收國). In August, his army conquered Huanglong Prefecture (黄龍府; present-day <a href="/wiki/Nong%27an_County" title="Nong'an County">Nong'an County</a>, Jilin Province) and defeated 700,000 Liao troops with only 20,000 horsemen at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Hubudagang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battle of Hubudagang (page does not exist)">Battle of Hubudagang</a>. By 1116, Aguda had completed the conquest of the entire <a href="/wiki/Liaodong_Peninsula" title="Liaodong Peninsula">Liaodong Peninsula</a>. Between 1119 and 1122, his army repeatedly defeated Liao forces and captured all of the Liao dynasty's five capitals.</p>
<p>Since the Jin dynasty was an enemy of the Liao dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a>-led <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Song_dynasty" title="Northern Song dynasty">Northern Song dynasty</a> considered the Jin dynasty to be their natural allies. In 1117, the Song dynasty sent emissaries to the Jin dynasty, ostensibly to buy horses, but in reality to negotiate an alliance against the Liao dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cht224_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cht224-7">[7]</a></sup> Between 1117 and 1123, seven Song delegations visited the Jurchens, and six Jin embassies went to the Song capital, Bianjing (present-day <a href="/wiki/Kaifeng" title="Kaifeng">Kaifeng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan Province</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cht224_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-cht224-7">[7]</a></sup> Between 1115 and 1123, the Jin and Song dynasties negotiated and formed the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Conducted_at_Sea" title="Alliance Conducted at Sea">Alliance Conducted at Sea</a> against the Liao dynasty. Under the conditions of the alliance, the Song dynasty would attack the Liao dynasty from the south, while in return, the Jin dynasty would hand over control of the Liao dynasty's <a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Prefectures" title="Sixteen Prefectures">Sixteen Prefectures</a> to the Song dynasty.</p>
<p>During the war against the Liao dynasty, Aguda also took time to establish the new feudal governmental system based on Jurchen tribal customs. He also organised the national agriculture with a collectivist system known as the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meng%27an-Mouke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meng'an-Mouke (page does not exist)">Meng'an-Mouke</a></i> (猛安謀克). Furthermore, Aguda absorbed elements of Han Chinese culture and ordered his chancellor <a href="/wiki/Wanyan_Xiyin" title="Wanyan Xiyin">Wanyan Xiyin</a> to develop a unique <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_script" title="Jurchen script">Jurchen writing system</a>.</p>
<p>Aguda died in August 1123, at the age of 56. His death came a few months after the Jin and Song dynasties signed a treaty which recognised each other as equals and required the Song to pay the Jin an annual tribute of 200,000 <a href="/wiki/Tael" title="Tael">taels</a> of silver and 300,000 bolts of silk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cht224_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-cht224-7">[7]</a></sup> Aguda was succeeded by his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taizong_of_Jin" title="Emperor Taizong of Jin">Wuqimai (Emperor Taizong)</a>. Wuqimai continued the campaign against the Liao dynasty and captured the Liao emperor, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Tianzuo_of_Liao" title="Emperor Tianzuo of Liao">Emperor Tianzuo</a> in 1125, thereby ending the Liao dynasty's existence. Soon after conquering the Liao dynasty, the Jin dynasty <a href="/wiki/Jin%E2%80%93Song_Wars" title="Jin–Song Wars">waged war</a> against the Northern Song dynasty. Aguda was buried in the Rui Mausoleum (睿陵) at Dafangshan (大房山) outside Zhongdu (中都; present-day <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taizu_of_Jin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commemoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mounted statues of Aguda and his chief commander, <a href="/wiki/Wanyan_Zonghan" title="Wanyan Zonghan">Wanyan Zonghan</a>, have been erected on the grounds of the Jin Dynasty History Museum (金上京歷史博物馆) at the former location of the old Jin capital, Shangjing (上京),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which is near present-day <a href="/wiki/Acheng_District" title="Acheng District">Acheng District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heilongjiang" title="Heilongjiang">Heilongjiang Province</a>.</p>
