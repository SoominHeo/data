<p><b>Geumgwan Gaya</b> (43 - 532), also known as <b>Bon-Gaya</b> (본가야, 本伽倻, "original Gaya") or <b>Garakguk</b> (가락국, "Garak State"), was the ruling city-state of the <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Gaya confederacy</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms Period</a> in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. It is believed to have been located around the modern-day city of <a href="/wiki/Gimhae" title="Gimhae">Gimhae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeongsangnam-do" title="Gyeongsangnam-do">Southern Gyeongsang province</a>, near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Nakdong_River" title="Nakdong River">Nakdong River</a>. Due to its geographic location, this kingdom played a dominant role in the regional affairs from the <a href="/wiki/Byeonhan_confederacy" title="Byeonhan confederacy">Byeonhan</a> period onward to the end of the Gaya confederacy.</p>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samguk_Yusa" title="Samguk Yusa">Samguk Yusa</a>, Geumgwan Kaya was made of 9 villages united by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suro_of_Gaya" title="Suro of Gaya">King Suro of Gaya</a>. His wife and queen <a href="/wiki/Heo_Hwang-ok" title="Heo Hwang-ok">Heo Hwang-ok</a>, whom he married in 48 AD, is believed to be a princess from the <a href="/wiki/Ayodhya" title="Ayodhya">Ayodhya</a> region in what is now <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> although this may have been an embellishment made during later Buddhist times.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>During this early time in the history of Gaya, several waves of migration from the north, including the earlier-extant <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buyeo_(state)" title="Buyeo (state)">Buyeo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, arrived and integrated with existing populations and stimulated cultural and political developments. A sharp break in burial styles is found in archaeological sites dated near the late 3rd century AD, when these migrations are to have taken place. Burial forms associated with North Asian nomadic peoples, such as the burial of horses with the dead, suddenly replace earlier forms in the tombs of the elite (Cheol 2000). In addition, evidence exists indicating that earlier burials were systematically destroyed. In the early 1990s, a royal tomb complex was unearthed in Daeseong-dong, <a href="/wiki/Gimhae" title="Gimhae">Gimhae</a>, attributed to Geumgwan Gaya but apparently used since Byeonhan times.</p>
<p>After Geumgwan Gaya capitulated to <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> in 532 AD, its royal house was accepted into the Sillan aristocracy (probably because by that time, a major house of Silla, of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeongju_Kim" title="Gyeongju Kim">Gyeongju Kim</a> clan, was related to the Gaya royal house, which was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gimhae_Kim" title="Gimhae Kim">Gimhae Kim</a> clan) and given the rank of "true bone," the second-highest level of the Silla <a href="/wiki/Bone_rank_system" title="Bone rank system">bone rank system</a>. General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Yu-shin" title="Kim Yu-shin">Kim Yu-shin</a> of Silla (also of the Gimhae Kim clan) was a descendant of the last king of Gaya.</p>
