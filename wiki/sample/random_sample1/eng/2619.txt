<p><b>Chinese bronze inscriptions</b>, also commonly referred to as <b>Bronze script</b> or <b>Bronzeware script</b> are writing in a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_writing" title="Chinese writing">Chinese scripts</a> on <a href="/wiki/Chinese_ritual_bronzes" title="Chinese ritual bronzes">Chinese ritual bronzes</a> such as <i>zhōng</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_(instrument)#Ancient_Chinese_bells" title="Bell (instrument)">bells</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ding_(vessel)" title="Ding (vessel)">dǐng</a></i> tripodal cauldrons from the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a> and even later. Early bronze inscriptions were almost always <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">cast</a> (that is, the writing was done with a stylus in the wet clay of the piece-mold from which the bronze was then cast), while later inscriptions were often engraved after the bronze was cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The bronze inscriptions are one of the earliest scripts in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_family_of_scripts" title="Chinese family of scripts">Chinese family of scripts</a>, preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracle bone script</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the early <a href="/wiki/Western_Zhou" title="Western Zhou">Western Zhou</a> to early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a> period, the bulk of writing which has been unearthed has been in the form of bronze inscriptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> As a result, it is common to refer to the variety of scripts of this period as "bronze script", even though there is no single such script. The term usually includes bronze inscriptions of the preceding <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[b]</a></sup> However, there are great differences between the highly pictorial Shang emblem (aka "identificational") characters on bronzes (see "ox" clan insignia at left), typical Shang bronze graphs, writing on bronzes from the middle of the Zhou dynasty, and that on late Zhou to <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a> and subsequent period bronzes. Furthermore, starting in the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a>, the writing in each region gradually evolved in different directions, such that the script styles in the Warring States of <a href="/wiki/Chu_(state)" title="Chu (state)">Chu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qin_(state)" title="Qin (state)">Qin</a> and the eastern regions, for instance, were strikingly divergent. In addition, artistic scripts also emerged in the late Spring and Autumn to early Warring States, such as <a href="/wiki/Bird-worm_seal_script" title="Bird-worm seal script">Bird Script</a> (鳥書 <i>niǎoshū</i>), also called Bird Seal Script (<i>niǎozhuàn</i> 鳥篆 ), and <a href="/wiki/Bird-worm_seal_script" title="Bird-worm seal script">Worm Script</a> (<i>chóngshū</i> 蟲書).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inscribed_bronzes">Inscribed bronzes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inscribed bronzes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1260" data-file-width="1965" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg/250px-Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg/375px-Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg/500px-Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Western_Zhou_Ritual_Containers3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Left: Bronze <i>fāng zūn</i> ritual wine container, c. 1000 BCE. The inscription commemorates a gift of cowrie shells to its owner. Right: Bronze <i>fāng yí</i> ritual container c. 1000 BCE. An inscription of some 180 characters appears twice on it, commenting on state rituals that accompany court ceremony, recorded by an official scribe.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Of the abundant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_ritual_bronze" title="Chinese ritual bronze">Chinese ritual bronze</a> artifacts extant today, about 12,000 have inscriptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilkinson428_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilkinson428-4">[2]</a></sup> These have been periodically unearthed ever since their creation, and have been systematically collected and studied since at least the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.62_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.62-5">[3]</a></sup> The inscriptions tend to grow in length over time, from only one to six or so characters for the earlier Shang examples, to forty or so characters in the longest, late-Shang case,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.62_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.62-5">[3]</a></sup> and frequently a hundred or more on Zhou bronzes, with the longest up to around 500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[c]</a></sup></p>
<p>In general, characters on ancient Chinese bronze inscriptions were arranged in vertical columns, written top to bottom, in a fashion thought to have been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> books, which are believed to have been the main medium for writing in the Shang and Zhou dynasties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[5]</a></sup> The very narrow, vertical bamboo slats of these books were not suitable for writing wide characters, and so a number of graphs were rotated 90 degrees; this style then carried over to the Shang and Zhou oracle bones and bronzes. Examples:</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="99" data-file-width="786" height="57" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg/450px-Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg/675px-Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg/900px-Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chinese_characters_from_bronzes_rotated_90_degrees.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Examples of bronze inscription graphs rotated 270°: From left, <i>mǎ</i> 馬 "horse"; <i>hǔ</i> 虎 "tiger", <i>shĭ</i> 豕 "swine", <i>quǎn</i> 犬 "dog", <i>xiàng</i> 象 "elephant", <i>guī</i> 龜 "turtle", <i>wèi</i> 為 "to lead" (now "do"), <i>jí</i> 疾 "illness".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Of the 12,000 inscribed bronzes extant today, roughly 3,000 date from the Shang dynasty, 6,000 from the Zhou dynasty, and the final 3,000 from the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilkinson428_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-wilkinson428-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shang_bronze_inscriptions">Shang bronze inscriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shang bronze inscriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Inscriptions on <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang</a> bronzes are of a fairly uniform style, making it possible to discuss a "Shang bronze script", although great differences still exist between typical characters and certain instances of clan names or emblems. Like early period <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracle bone script</a>, the structures and orientations of individual graphs varied greatly in the Shang bronze inscriptions, such that one may find a particular character written differently each time rather than in a standardized way (see the many examples of "tiger" graph to the lower left).</p>

//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Chinese_oracle_bone_script_character_hou2_%E4%BE%AF_marquis.gif/26px-Chinese_oracle_bone_script_character_hou2_%E4%BE%AF_marquis.gif 2x" width="13"/></a> and <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg"><img alt="侯-oracle.svg" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg/13px-%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg/20px-%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg/26px-%E4%BE%AF-oracle.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> are both <i>hóu</i> 侯 "marquis". This was true of normal as well as extra complex identificational graphs, such as the <i>hǔ</i> 虎 "tiger" clan emblem at right, which was turned 90 degrees clockwise on its bronze.</p>
<p>These inscriptions are almost all cast (as opposed to engraved),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[7]</a></sup> and are relatively short and simple. Some were mainly to identify the name of a clan or other name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.64_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.64-11">[8]</a></sup> while typical inscriptions include the maker's clan name and the posthumous title of the ancestor who is commemorated by the making and use of the vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup> These inscriptions, especially those late period examples identifying a name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.64_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.64-11">[8]</a></sup> are typically executed in a script of highly pictographic flavor, which preserves the formal, complex Shang writing as would have primarily been written on bamboo or wood books,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.63_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.63-13">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[d]</a></sup> as opposed to the concurrent simplified, linearized and more rectilinear form of writing as seen on the oracle bones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.70_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.70-15">[11]</a></sup> A few Shang inscriptions have been found which were brush-written on pottery, stone, jade or bone artifacts, and there are also some bone engravings on non-divination matters written in a complex, highly pictographic style;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.64_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.64-11">[8]</a></sup> the structure and style of the bronze inscriptions is consistent with these.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.63_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.63-13">[10]</a></sup> The soft clay of the piece-molds used to produce the Shang to early Zhou bronzes was suitable for preserving most of the complexity of the brush-written characters on such books and other media, whereas the hard, bony surface of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oracle_bones" title="Oracle bones">oracle bones</a> was difficult to engrave, spurring significant simplification and conversion to rectilinearity. Furthermore, some of the characters on the Shang bronzes may have been more complex than normal due to particularly conservative<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[12]</a></sup> usage in this ritual medium, or when recording identificational inscriptions (clan or personal names); some scholars instead attribute this to purely decorative considerations. Shang bronze script may thus be considered a <i>formal</i> script, similar to but sometimes even more complex than the unattested daily Shang script on bamboo and wood books and other media, yet far more complex than the <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">Shang script on the oracle bones</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zhou_dynasty_inscriptions">Zhou dynasty inscriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Zhou dynasty inscriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Zhou">Western Zhou</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western Zhou">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Western Zhou dynasty characters (as exemplified by bronze inscriptions of that time) basically continue from the Shang writing system; that is, early W. Zhou forms resemble Shang bronze forms (both such as clan names,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[e]</a></sup> and typical writing), without any clear or sudden distinction. They are, like their Shang predecessors in all media, often irregular in shape and size, and the structures and details often vary from one piece of writing to the next, and even within the same piece. Although most are not pictographs in function, the early Western Zhou bronze inscriptions have been described as more pictographic in flavor than those of subsequent periods. During the Western Zhou, many graphs begin to show signs of simplification and linearization (the changing of rounded elements into squared ones, solid elements into short line segments, and thick, variable-width lines into thin ones of uniform width), with the result being a decrease in pictographic quality, as depicted in the chart below.</p>

<div class="thumb tnone">

Linearization of characters during the Western Zhou</div>

<p>Some flexibility in orientation of graphs (rotation and reversibility) continues in the Western Zhou, but this becomes increasingly scarce throughout the Zhou dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[13]</a></sup> The graphs start to become slightly more uniform in structure, size and arrangement by the time of the third Zhou sovereign, <a href="/wiki/King_Kang_of_Zhou" title="King Kang of Zhou">King Kāng</a>, and after the ninth, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Yi_of_Zhou_(Ji_Xie)" title="King Yi of Zhou (Ji Xie)">King Yì</a>, this trend becomes more obvious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some have used the problematic term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_seal_script" title="Large seal script">large seal</a>" (大篆 <i>dàzhuàn</i>) to refer to the script of this period. This term dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[15]</a></sup> when (small) <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">seal script</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clerical_script" title="Clerical script">clerical script</a> were both in use. It thus became necessary to distinguish between the two, as well as any earlier script forms which were still accessible in the form of books and inscriptions, so the terms "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_seal_script" title="Large seal script">large seal</a>" (大篆 <i>dàzhuàn</i>) and "small seal" (小篆 <i>xiǎozhuàn</i>, aka 秦篆 <i>Qín zhuàn</i>) came into being. However, since the term "large seal" is <i>variously</i> used to describe <i>zhòuwén</i> (籀文) examples from the ca. 800 BCE <a href="/wiki/Shizhoupian" title="Shizhoupian">Shizhoupian</a> compendium, <i>or</i> inscriptions on both late W. Zhou bronze inscriptions and the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Drums_of_Qin" title="Stone Drums of Qin">Stone Drums of Qin</a>, <i>or</i> all forms (including <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracle bone script</a>) predating small seal, the term is best avoided entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Zhou">Eastern Zhou</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern Zhou">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_and_Autumn">Spring and Autumn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spring and Autumn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>By the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Zhou" title="Eastern Zhou">Eastern Zhou</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a>, many graphs are fully linearized, as seen in the chart above; additionally, curved lines are straightened, and disconnected lines are often connected, with the result of greater convenience in writing, but a marked decrease in pictographic quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.70_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.70-15">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Eastern Zhou, the various states initially continued using the same forms as in the late Western Zhou. However, regional forms then began to diverge stylistically as early as the Spring and Autumn period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[17]</a></sup> with the forms in the <a href="/wiki/Qin_(state)" title="Qin (state)">state of Qin</a> remaining more conservative. At this time, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(Chinese)" title="Seal (Chinese)">seals</a> and minted coins, both probably primarily of bronze, were already in use, according to traditional documents, but none of the extant seals have yet been indisputably dated to that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the mid to late Spring and Autumn period, artistic derivative scripts with vertically elongated forms appeared on bronzes, especially in the eastern and southern states, and remained in use into the Warring States period (see detail of inscription from the Warring States <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Marquis_Yi_of_Zeng" title="Tomb of Marquis Yi of Zeng">Tomb of Marquis Yĭ of Zēng</a> below left). In the same areas, in the late Spring and Autumn to early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a>, scripts which embellished basic structures with decorative forms such as birds or worms also appeared. These are known as Bird Script (<i>niǎoshū</i> 鳥書) and Worm Script (<i>chóngshū</i> 蟲書), and collectively as <a href="/wiki/Bird-worm_seal_script" title="Bird-worm seal script">Bird-worm scripts</a>, (<i>niǎochóngshū</i> 鳥蟲書; see Bronze sword of King Gōujiàn to right); however, these were primarily decorative forms for inscriptions on bronzes and other items, and not scripts in daily use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[19]</a></sup> Some bronzes of the period were incised in a rough, casual manner, with graph structures often differing somewhat from typical ones. It is thought that these reflected the popular (vulgar) writing of the time which coexisted with the formal script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Warring_States_period">Warring States period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_bronze_inscriptions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Warring States period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(Chinese)" title="Seal (Chinese)">Seals</a> have been found from the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a>, mostly cast in bronze,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[21]</a></sup> and minted bronze coins from this period are also numerous. These form an additional, valuable resource for the study of Chinese bronze inscriptions. It is also from this period that the first surviving bamboo and silk manuscripts have been uncovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early Warring States period, typical bronze inscriptions were similar in content and length to those in the late <a href="/wiki/Western_Zhou" title="Western Zhou">Western Zhou</a> to <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.79_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.79-28">[23]</a></sup> One of the most famous sets of bronzes ever discovered dates to the early Warring States: a large set of <i><a href="/wiki/Bianzhong" title="Bianzhong">biānzhōng</a></i> bells from the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Marquis_Yi_of_Zeng" title="Tomb of Marquis Yi of Zeng">tomb of Marquis Yĭ of the state of Zēng</a>, unearthed in 1978. The total length of the inscriptions on this set was almost 2,800 characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.79_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.79-28">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bianzhong.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2752" data-file-width="4768" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Bianzhong.jpg/350px-Bianzhong.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Bianzhong.jpg/525px-Bianzhong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Bianzhong.jpg/700px-Bianzhong.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bianzhong.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bronze biānzhōng bells from the early Warring States Tomb of Marquis Yĭ of Zēng</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the mid to late Warring States period, the average length of inscriptions decreased greatly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[24]</a></sup> Many, especially on weapons, recorded only the date, maker and so on, in contrast with earlier narrative contents. Beginning at this time, such inscriptions were typically engraved onto the already cast bronzes, rather than being written into the wet clay of piece-molds as had been the earlier practice. The engraving was often roughly and hastily executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Warring States period bronze inscriptions, trends from the late Spring and Autumn period continue, such as the use of artistically embellished scripts (e.g., Bird and Insect Scripts) on decorated bronze items. In daily writing, which was not embellished in this manner, the typical script continued evolving in different directions in various regions, and this divergence was accelerated by both a lack of central political control as well as the spread of writing outside of the nobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.78_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.78-31">[26]</a></sup> In the state of Qin, which was somewhat culturally isolated from the other states, and which was positioned on the old Zhou homeland, the script became more uniform and stylistically symmetrical,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.97_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.97-32">[27]</a></sup> rather than changing much structurally. Change in the script was slow, so it remained more similar to the typical late Western Zhou script as found on bronzes of that period and the <a href="/wiki/Shizhoupian" title="Shizhoupian">Shĭ Zhoù Piān (史籀篇)</a> compendium of ca. 800 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.97_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.97-32">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[28]</a></sup> As a result, it was not until around the middle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a> period that popular (aka common or vulgar) writing gained momentum in Qin, and even then, the vulgar forms remained somewhat similar to traditional forms, changing primarily in terms of becoming more rectilinear. Traditional forms in Qin remained in use as well, so that two forms of writing coexisted. The traditional forms in Qin evolved slowly during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Zhou" title="Eastern Zhou">Eastern Zhou</a>, gradually becoming what is now called (small) <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">seal script</a> during that period, without any clear dividing line (it is not the case, as is commonly believed, that small seal script was a sudden invention by <a href="/wiki/Li_Si" title="Li Si">Li Si</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[29]</a></sup>). Meanwhile, the Qin vulgar writing evolved into early clerical (or proto-clerical) in the late Warring States to Qin dynasty period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[30]</a></sup> which would then evolve further into the <a href="/wiki/Clerical_script" title="Clerical script">clerical script</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Wei</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265–420)">Jin</a> periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[f]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, in the eastern states, vulgar forms had become popular sooner; they also differed more radically from and more completely displaced the traditional forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.78_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi.C3.BA_2000.2C_p.78-31">[26]</a></sup> These eastern scripts, which also varied somewhat by state or region, were later misunderstood by <a href="/wiki/Xu_Shen" title="Xu Shen">Xu Shen</a>, author of the Han dynasty etymological dictionary <i><a href="/wiki/Shuowen_Jiezi" title="Shuowen Jiezi">Shuowen Jiezi</a></i>, who thought they predated the Warring States Qin forms, and thus labeled them <i>gǔwén</i> (古文), or "ancient script".</p>

<li id="cite_note-14"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-14">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The main writing medium of the time is believed to have been the writing brush and bamboo or wood books; although they have not survived, the character for such books, <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png"><img alt="Ce4 book from Shang1 dynasty bronze.png" data-file-height="274" data-file-width="278" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png/16px-Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png/24px-Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png/32px-Ce4_book_from_Shang1_dynasty_bronze.png 2x" width="16"/></a> 冊 <i>cè</i>, showing their vertical slats bound with a horizontal string, is found on Shang and Zhou bronzes, as is the graph for the writing brush (see Qiú 2000, p.63, and Xǔ Yǎhuì, p.12). Historical references to use by the Shang of such books also exist, e.g., in the Duōshì chapter of the Shàngshū (Qiú pp.62-3)</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Qiú 2000, p.69; p.65 footnote 5 also mentions that highly pictographic clan emblems and other identificational forms are still found in the Zhou period, especially the beginning of the W. Zhou.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-36"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-36">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although popularly associated only with the Han dynasty, clerical actually remained in use alongside cursive, neo-clerical and semi-cursive scripts until <i>after</i> the Wei-Jin period, when the modern standard script became dominant; see Qiu 2000, p.113</span></li>
</ol>
