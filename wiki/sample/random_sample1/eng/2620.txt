<p>The <b>goldfish</b> (<i>Carassius auratus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">freshwater fish</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Cyprinidae" title="Cyprinidae">Cyprinidae</a> of <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Cypriniformes" title="Cypriniformes">Cypriniformes</a>. It was one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> to be <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a>, and is one of the most commonly kept <a href="/wiki/List_of_freshwater_aquarium_fish_species" title="List of freshwater aquarium fish species">aquarium fish</a>.</p>
<p>A relatively small member of the <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a> family (which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Koi" title="Koi">koi carp</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crucian_carp" title="Crucian carp">crucian carp</a>), the goldfish is a domesticated version of a less-colourful carp (<i>Carassius auratus</i>) native to <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">east Asia</a>. It was first domesticated in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> more than a thousand years ago, and several distinct <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breeds</a> have since been developed. Goldfish breeds vary greatly in size, body shape, fin configuration and colouration (various combinations of white, yellow, orange, red, brown, and black are known).</p>
<p>The mutation that gave rise to the domestic goldfish is also known from other <a href="/wiki/Cyprinidae" title="Cyprinidae">cyprinid</a> species, such as <a href="/wiki/Common_carp" title="Common carp">common carp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tench" title="Tench">tench</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="painting of many swimming fish, mostly in shades of tan" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="519" data-file-width="1024" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Goldfish_in_Fish_Swimming_Amid_Falling_Flowers_by_Liu_Cai_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Starting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">ancient China</a>, various species of <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a> (collectively known as <a href="/wiki/Asian_carp" title="Asian carp">Asian carp</a>) have been <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> and reared as <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">food fish</a> for thousands of years. Some of these normally gray or silver species have a tendency to produce red, orange or yellow colour <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a>; this was first recorded during the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265–420)">Jin dynasty (265–420)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ocean_Park_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ocean_Park-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roots_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-roots-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> (618–907), it was popular to raise carp in ornamental ponds and <a href="/wiki/Water_garden" title="Water garden">watergardens</a>. A natural genetic mutation produced gold (actually yellowish orange) rather than silver colouration. People began to breed the gold variety instead of the silver variety, keeping them in ponds or other bodies of water. On special occasions at which guests were expected, they would be moved to a much smaller container for display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bristol_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bristol-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279), the domestication of goldfish was firmly established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smartt_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-smartt-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1162, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress" title="Empress">empress</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> ordered the construction of a pond to collect the red and gold variety. By this time, people outside the imperial family were forbidden to keep goldfish of the gold (yellow) variety, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variations_of_yellow#Royal_yellow" title="Variations of yellow">yellow being the imperial colour</a>. This is probably the reason why there are more orange goldfish than yellow goldfish, even though the latter are genetically easier to breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The occurrence of other colours (apart from red and gold) was first recorded in 1276.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (1368–1644), goldfish also began to be raised indoors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roots_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-roots-4">[4]</a></sup> which permitted selection for mutations that would not be able to survive in ponds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bristol_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bristol-5">[5]</a></sup> The first occurrence of fancy-tailed goldfish was recorded in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>. In 1603, goldfish were introduced to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bristol_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-bristol-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1611, goldfish were introduced to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and from there to other parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bristol_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-bristol-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1620s, goldfish were highly regarded in southern Europe because of their metallic scales, and symbolised good luck and fortune. It became tradition for married men to give their wives a goldfish on their first anniversary, as a symbol for the prosperous years to come. This tradition quickly died, as goldfish became more available, losing their status. Goldfish were first introduced to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> around 1850 and quickly became popular in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunner_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brunner-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_goldfish_varieties" title="List of goldfish varieties">List of goldfish varieties</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">Selective breeding</a> over centuries has produced several colour variations, some of them far removed from the "<a href="/wiki/Gold_(color)" title="Gold (color)">golden</a>" colour of the originally domesticated fish. There are also different body shapes, <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">eye</a> configurations. Some extreme versions of the goldfish live only in <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquariums</a>—they are much less hardy than varieties closer to the "wild" original. However, some variations are hardier, such as the Shubunkin. Currently, there are about 300 breeds recognised in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roots_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-roots-4">[4]</a></sup> The vast majority of goldfish breeds today originated from China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roots_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-roots-4">[4]</a></sup> Some of the main varieties are:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_goldfish_classification">Chinese goldfish classification</span></h3>
<p>Chinese tradition classifies goldfish into four main types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These classifications are not commonly used in the West.</p>
<ul>
<li>Crucian (may also be called "grass")—Goldfish without fancy anatomical features. These include the common goldfish, comet goldfish and Shubunkin.</li>
<li>Wen—Goldfish have a fancy tail, e.g., Fantails and Veiltails ("Wen" is also the name of the characteristic headgrowth on such strains as Oranda and Lionhead)</li>
<li>Dragon Eye—Goldfish have extended eyes, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Moor_(fish)" title="Black Moor (fish)">Black Moor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bubble_Eye" title="Bubble Eye">Bubble Eye</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telescope_Eye" title="Telescope Eye">Telescope Eye</a></li>
<li>Egg—Goldfish have no dorsal fin, and usually have an 'egg-shaped' body, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lionhead_(goldfish)" title="Lionhead (goldfish)">Lionhead</a> (note that a Bubble Eye without a dorsal fin belongs to this group)</li>
</ul>

<p>Goldfish were bred from <a href="/wiki/Prussian_carp" title="Prussian carp">Prussian carp</a> (<i>Carassius auratus gibelio</i>) in China, and these usually olive green fish remain the closest wild relative of the goldfish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evolutionary_origin_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evolutionary_origin-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-matsui_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-matsui-20">[20]</a></sup> Previously, some sources claimed the <a href="/wiki/Crucian_carp" title="Crucian carp">Crucian carp</a> (<i>Carassius carassius</i>) as the wild version of the goldfish. However, they are differentiated by several characteristics. <i>C. auratus</i> have a more pointed snout while the snout of a <i>C. carassius</i> is well rounded. <i>C. a. gibelio</i> often has a grey/greenish colour, while crucian carp are always golden bronze. Juvenile crucian carp have a black spot on the base of the tail which disappears with age. In C. auratus this tail spot is never present. <i>C. auratus</i> have fewer than 31 scales along the lateral line while crucian carp have 33 scales or more.</p>
<p>Like their wild ancestors, common and comet goldfish as well as Shubunkin can survive, and even thrive, in any climate that can support a pond, whereas fancy goldfish are unlikely to survive in the wild as their bright colours and long fins make them easy prey. Goldfish can hybridise with certain other species of carp as well as <i>C. a. gibelio</i>. Within three breeding generations, the vast majority of the hybrid spawn revert to the <a href="/wiki/Wild_type" title="Wild type">wild type</a> colour. Koi may also interbreed with the goldfish to produce sterile hybrids.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span></h3>
<p>As of April 2008, the largest goldfish in the world was believed by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> to measure 19 inches (48 cm), and be living in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17Apr2008_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17Apr2008-21">[21]</a></sup> At the time, a goldfish named "Goldie", kept as a pet in a tank in <a href="/wiki/Folkestone" title="Folkestone">Folkestone</a>, England, was measured as 15 inches (38 cm) and over 2 pounds (0.91 kg), and named as the second largest in the world behind the Netherlands fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17Apr2008_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17Apr2008-21">[21]</a></sup> The secretary of the Federation of British Aquatic Societies (FBAS) stated of Goldie's size that "I would think there are probably a few bigger goldfish that people don't think of as record holders, perhaps in ornamental lakes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17Apr2008_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17Apr2008-21">[21]</a></sup> In July 2010, a goldfish measuring 16 inches (41 cm) and 5 pounds (2.3 kg) was caught in a pond in <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a>, England, thought to have been abandoned there after outgrowing a tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC15Jul2010_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC15Jul2010-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision">Vision</span></h3>
<p>Goldfish have one of the most studied senses of <a href="/wiki/Vision_in_fishes" title="Vision in fishes">vision in fishes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neumeyer_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neumeyer-23">[23]</a></sup> Goldfish have four kinds of <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a>, which are respectively sensitive to different colours: red, green, blue and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>. The ability to distinguish between four different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_colour" title="Primary colour">primary colours</a> classifies them as <a href="/wiki/Tetrachromacy" title="Tetrachromacy">tetrachromats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_abilities">Cognitive abilities</span></h3>
<p>Goldfish have strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associative_learning" title="Associative learning">associative learning</a> abilities, as well as <a href="/wiki/Observational_learning" title="Observational learning">social learning</a> skills. In addition, their <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">visual</a> acuity allows them to distinguish between individual humans. Owners may notice that fish react favorably to them (swimming to the front of the glass, swimming rapidly around the tank, and going to the surface mouthing for food) while hiding when other people approach the tank. Over time, goldfish learn to associate their owners and other humans with food, often "begging" for food whenever their owners approach.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Goldfish that have constant visual contact with humans also stop considering them to be a threat. After being kept in a tank for several weeks, sometimes months, it becomes possible to feed a goldfish by hand without it shying away.</p>
<p>Goldfish have a memory-span of at least three months and can distinguish between different shapes, colours and sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> By using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_Reinforcement" title="Negative Reinforcement">positive reinforcement</a>, goldfish can be trained to recognise and to react to light signals of different colours<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> or to perform tricks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Fish respond to certain colours most evidently in relation to feeding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Fish learn to anticipate feedings provided they occur at around the same time every day.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h3>
<p>Goldfish are gregarious, displaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swarm" title="Swarm">schooling</a> behaviour, as well as displaying the same types of feeding behaviours. Goldfish may display similar behaviours when responding to their reflections in a mirror.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Goldfish have learned behaviours, both as groups and as individuals, that stem from native carp behaviour. They are a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generalist_(ecology)" title="Generalist (ecology)">generalist</a> species with varied feeding, breeding, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predator</a> avoidance behaviours that contribute to their success. As fish they can be described as "friendly" towards each other. Very rarely does a goldfish harm another goldfish, nor do the males harm the females during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_reproduction" title="Biological reproduction">breeding</a>. The only real threat that goldfish present <i>to each other</i> is competing for food. <a href="/wiki/Common_goldfish" title="Common goldfish">Commons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comet_(goldfish)" title="Comet (goldfish)">comets</a>, and other faster varieties can easily eat all the food during a feeding before fancy varieties can reach it. This can lead to stunted growth or possible <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> of fancier varieties when they are kept in a pond with their single-tailed brethren. As a result, care should be taken to combine only breeds with similar body type and swim characteristics.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_food" title="Fish food">Fish food</a></div>
<p>In the wild, the diet of goldfish consists of <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, and various <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> matter. Like most fish, they are opportunistic feeders and do not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satiety" title="Satiety">stop eating</a> on their own accord. Overfeeding can be deleterious to their health, typically by blocking the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestines" title="Intestines">intestines</a>. This happens most often with selectively bred goldfish, which have a convoluted intestinal tract. When excess food is available, they produce more waste and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faeces" title="Faeces">faeces</a>, partly due to incomplete <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> digestion. Overfeeding can sometimes be diagnosed by observing faeces trailing from the fish's <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a>.</p>
<p>Goldfish-specific food has less protein and more <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> than conventional fish food. Enthusiasts may supplement this diet with shelled <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a> (with outer skins removed), <a href="/wiki/Blanching_(cooking)" title="Blanching (cooking)">blanched</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_leafy_vegetable" title="Green leafy vegetable">green leafy vegetables</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chironomidae" title="Chironomidae">bloodworms</a>. Young goldfish benefit from the addition of <a href="/wiki/Brine_shrimp" title="Brine shrimp">brine shrimp</a> to their diet. As with all animals, goldfish preferences vary.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h4>
<p>Goldfish may only grow to sexual maturity with enough water and the right nutrition. Most goldfish breed in captivity, particularly in pond settings. Breeding usually happens after a significant temperature change, often in spring. Males chase gravid female goldfish (females carrying eggs), and prompt them to release their eggs by bumping and nudging them.</p>
<p>Goldfish, like all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyprinid" title="Cyprinid">cyprinids</a>, are egg-layers. Their eggs are adhesive and attach to aquatic vegetation, typically dense plants such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cabomba" title="Cabomba">Cabomba</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Elodea" title="Elodea">Elodea</a></i> or a spawning mop. The eggs hatch within 48 to 72 hours.</p>
<p>Within a week or so, the <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">fry</a> begins to assume its final shape, although a year may pass before they develop a mature goldfish colour; until then they are a metallic brown like their wild ancestors. In their first weeks of life, the fry grow quickly—an adaptation born of the high risk of getting devoured by the adult goldfish (or other fish and insects) in their environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some highly bred goldfish can no longer breed naturally due to their altered shape. The artificial breeding method called "hand stripping" can assist nature, but can harm the fish if not done correctly. In captivity, adults may also eat young that they encounter.</p>
<p>Breeding goldfish by the hobbyist is the process of selecting adult fish to reproduce, allowing them to reproduce and then raising the resulting offspring while continually removing fish that do not approach the desired pedigree. The process is described here in detail. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_aquaria">In aquaria</span></h3>
<p>Like most species in the carp family, goldfish produce a large amount of waste both in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faeces" title="Faeces">faeces</a> and through their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gills" title="Gills">gills</a>, releasing harmful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemicals</a> into the water. Build-up of this waste to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> levels can occur in a relatively short period of time, and can easily cause a goldfish's death. For common and comet varieties, each goldfish should have about 20 US gallons (76 l; 17 imp gal) of water. Fancy goldfish (which are smaller) should have about 10 US gallons (38 l; 8.3 imp gal) per goldfish. The water surface area determines how much <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> diffuses and dissolves into the water. A general rule is have 1 square foot (0.093 m<sup>2</sup>). Active <a href="/wiki/Aeration" title="Aeration">aeration</a> by way of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pump" title="Water pump">water pump</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(water)" title="Filter (water)">filter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fountain" title="Fountain">fountain</a> effectively increases the surface area.</p>
<p>The goldfish is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Coldwater_fish" title="Coldwater fish">coldwater fish</a>, and can live in unheated <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a> at a temperature comfortable for humans. However, rapid changes in temperature (for example in an office building in winter when the heat is turned off at night) can kill them, especially if the tank is small. Care must also be taken when adding water, as the new water may be of a different temperature. Temperatures under about 10 °C (50 °F) are dangerous to fancy varieties, though commons and comets can survive slightly lower temperatures. Extremely high temperatures (over 30 °C (86 °F) can also harm goldfish. However, higher temperatures may help fight <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoan</a> infestations by accelerating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasite</a>'s life-cycle—thus eliminating it more quickly. The optimum temperature for goldfish is between 20 °C (68 °F) and 22 °C (72 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like all fish, goldfish do not like to be petted. In fact, touching a goldfish can endanger its health, because it can cause the protective slime coat to be damaged or removed, exposing the fish’s skin to infection from bacteria or water-born parasites. However, goldfish respond to people by surfacing at feeding time, and can be trained or acclimated to taking pellets or flakes from human fingers. The reputation of goldfish dying quickly is often due to poor care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The lifespan of goldfish in captivity can extend beyond 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>If left in the dark for a period of time, goldfish gradually change colour until they are almost gray.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Goldfish produce pigment in response to light, in a similar manner to how human skin becomes <a href="/wiki/Sun_tanning" title="Sun tanning">tanned</a> in the sun. Fish have cells called <a href="/wiki/Chromatophore" title="Chromatophore">chromatophores</a> that produce pigments which reflect light, and give the fish colouration. The colour of a goldfish is determined by which pigments are in the cells, how many pigment molecules there are, and whether the pigment is grouped inside the cell or is spaced throughout the cytoplasm.</p>
<p>Because goldfish eat live plants, their presence in a <a href="/wiki/Aquascaping" title="Aquascaping">planted aquarium</a> can be problematic. Only a few aquarium plant species for example <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptocoryne" title="Cryptocoryne">Cryptocoryne</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Anubias" title="Anubias">Anubias</a></i>, can survive around goldfish, but they require special attention so that they are not uprooted. Plastic plants are more durable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_ponds">In ponds</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="684" data-file-width="912" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg/220px-Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg/330px-Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg/440px-Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_Oranda_goldfish_in_outdoor_pond.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Red Oranda (Wen) goldfish reared in a small outdoor pond with lilies.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Goldfish are popular pond fish, since they are small, inexpensive, colourful and very hardy. In an outdoor pond or <a href="/wiki/Water_garden" title="Water garden">water garden</a>, they may even survive for brief periods if <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> forms on the surface, as long as there is enough <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> remaining in the water and the pond does not freeze solid. Common goldfish, London and Bristol shubunkins, jikin, wakin, comet and some hardier fantail goldfish can be kept in a pond all year round in temperate and subtropical climates. Moor, veiltail, oranda and lionhead can be kept safely in outdoor ponds year-round only in more tropical climates and only in summer elsewhere.</p>
<p>Ponds small and large are fine in warmer areas (although it ought to be noted that goldfish can "overheat" in small volumes of water in summer in tropical climates). In frosty climes the depth should be at least 80 centimeters (31 in) to preclude freezing. During winter, goldfish become sluggish, stop eating and often stay on the bottom of the pond. This is normal; they become active again in the spring. Unless the pond is large enough to maintain its own ecosystem without interference from humans, a filter is important to clear waste and keep the pond clean. Plants are essential as they act as part of the filtration system, as well as a food source for the fish. Plants are further beneficial since they raise oxygen levels in the water.</p>
<p>Compatible fish include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudd" title="Rudd">rudd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tench" title="Tench">tench</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orfe" title="Orfe">orfe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Koi" title="Koi">koi</a>, but the latter require specialised care. <a href="/wiki/Ramshorn_snail" title="Ramshorn snail">Ramshorn snails</a> are helpful by eating any <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> that grows in the pond. Without some form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_population_control" title="Animal population control">animal population control</a>, goldfish ponds can easily become overstocked. Fish such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orfe" title="Orfe">orfe</a> consume goldfish eggs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="For_mosquito_control">For mosquito control</span></h3>
<p>Like some other popular aquarium fish, such as the <a href="/wiki/Guppy" title="Guppy">guppy</a>, goldfish and other carp are frequently added to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stagnant_(water)" title="Stagnant (water)">stagnant</a> bodies of water to reduce <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquito</a> populations. They are used to prevent the spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Nile_Virus" title="West Nile Virus">West Nile Virus</a>, which relies on mosquitoes to migrate. However, <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introducing</a> goldfish has often had negative consequences for local <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Welfare_concerns">Welfare concerns</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goldfish_Scooped_Up.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Japanese game of <a href="/wiki/Goldfish_scooping" title="Goldfish scooping">Goldfish scooping</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fishbowl" title="wikt:fishbowl">Fishbowls</a> are not appropriate housing for goldfish, and are so detrimental to their health and well-being that they are prohibited by <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> legislation in several municipalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Times_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Times-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Council_bans_goldfish_bowls_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Council_bans_goldfish_bowls-36">[36]</a></sup> The practice of using bowls as permanent fish housing originated from a misunderstanding of Chinese "display" vessels: goldfish which were normally housed in ponds were, on occasion, temporarily displayed in smaller containers to be better admired by guests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bristol_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-bristol-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goldfish kept in bowls or "mini aquariums" suffer from death, disease, and <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunting</a>, due primarily to the low oxygen and very high <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>/<a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a> levels inherent in such an environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In comparison to other common aquarium fish, goldfish have high oxygen needs and produce a large amount of waste; therefore they require a substantial volume of well-filtered water to thrive. In addition, all goldfish varieties have the potential to reach 5" (12.7 cm) in total length, with single-tailed breeds often exceeding a foot (30.5 cm); meaning that an adult goldfish kept in a bowl will be approximately as cramped as a five-week-old kitten trapped in a shoebox.</p>
<p>In many countries, <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">carnival</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a> operators commonly give goldfish away in <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> bags as <a href="/wiki/Prize" title="Prize">prizes</a>. In late 2005 <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> banned the use of goldfish and other animals as carnival prizes. Rome has also banned the use of "goldfish bowls", on animal cruelty grounds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Times_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Times-35">[35]</a></sup> as well as Monza, Italy, in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Council_bans_goldfish_bowls_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Council_bans_goldfish_bowls-36">[36]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> proposed banning this practice as part of its Animal Welfare Bill,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> though this has since been amended to only prevent goldfish being given as prizes to unaccompanied minors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, during summer festivals and religious holidays (<a href="/wiki/Ennichi" title="Ennichi">ennichi</a>), a traditional game called <a href="/wiki/Goldfish_scooping" title="Goldfish scooping">goldfish scooping</a> is played, in which a player scoops goldfish from a basin with a special scooper. Sometimes <a href="/wiki/Bouncy_ball" title="Bouncy ball">bouncy balls</a> are substituted for goldfish.</p>
<p>Although edible and closely related to some fairly widely eaten species, goldfish are rarely eaten. A fad among <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> college students for many years was <a href="/wiki/Goldfish_swallowing" title="Goldfish swallowing">swallowing goldfish</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Stunt" title="Stunt">stunt</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_sororities" title="Fraternities and sororities">fraternity</a> initiation process. The first recorded instance was in 1939 at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The practice gradually fell out of popularity over the course of several decades and is rarely practiced today.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and among the international <a href="/wiki/Iranian_diaspora" title="Iranian diaspora">Iranian diaspora</a>, goldfish are a traditional part of <a href="/wiki/Nowruz#Haft_S.C4.ABn" title="Nowruz">Nowruz</a> celebrations. Some animal advocates have called for boycotts of goldfish purchases, citing industrial farming and low survival rates of the fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
