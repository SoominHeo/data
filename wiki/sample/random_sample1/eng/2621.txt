<p><b>Metallurgy</b> is a domain of <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science and engineering</a> that studies the physical and chemical behaviour of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metallic</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a>, their <a href="/wiki/Intermetallic" title="Intermetallic">intermetallic compounds</a>, and their mixtures, which are called <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>. Metallurgy is also the <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> of metals: the way in which science is applied to the production of metals, and the engineering of metal components for usage in products for consumers and manufacturers. The production of metals involves the processing of <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> to extract the metal they contain, and the mixture of metals, sometimes with other elements, to produce alloys. Metallurgy is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a> of <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a>, although metalworking relies on metallurgy, as <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> relies on medical science, for technical advancement.</p>
<p>Metallurgy is subdivided into <i><a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy" title="Ferrous metallurgy">ferrous metallurgy</a></i> (sometimes also known as black metallurgy) and <i><a href="/wiki/Non-ferrous_metal" title="Non-ferrous metal">non-ferrous metallurgy</a></i> or <i>colored metallurgy</i>. Ferrous metallurgy involves processes and alloys based on <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> while non-ferrous metallurgy involves processes and alloys based on other metals. The production of ferrous metals accounts for 95 percent of world metal production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_pronunciation">Etymology and pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The roots of <i>metallurgy</i> derive from Ancient Greek: μεταλλουργός, <i>metallourgós</i>, "worker in metal", from μέταλλον, <i>métallon</i>, "metal" + ἔργον, <i>érgon</i>, "work".</p>
<p>The word was originally an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alchemist" title="Alchemist">alchemist</a>'s term for the extraction of metals from minerals, the ending <i>-urgy</i> signifying a process, especially manufacturing: it was discussed in this sense in the 1797 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_Britannica" title="Encyclopaedia Britannica">Encyclopaedia Britannica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-2">[2]</a></sup> In the late 19th century it was extended to the more general scientific study of metals, alloys, and related processes.</p>
<p>In English, the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/meˈtælədʒi/</span> pronunciation is the more common one in the UK and Commonwealth. The <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈmetələrdʒi/</span> pronunciation is the more common one in the USA, and is the first-listed variant in various American dictionaries (e.g., <i>Merriam-Webster Collegiate</i>, <i>American Heritage</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_ferrous_metallurgy" title="History of ferrous metallurgy">History of ferrous metallurgy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy_in_pre-Columbian_America" title="Metallurgy in pre-Columbian America">Metallurgy in pre-Columbian America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy_in_pre-Columbian_Mesoamerica" title="Metallurgy in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica">Metallurgy in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_metallurgy_in_the_Indian_subcontinent" title="History of metallurgy in the Indian subcontinent">History of metallurgy in the Indian subcontinent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Non-ferrous_extractive_metallurgy" title="Non-ferrous extractive metallurgy">Non-ferrous extractive metallurgy</a></div>
<p>The earliest recorded metal employed by humans appears to be <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, which can be found free or "native". Small amounts of natural gold have been found in Spanish caves used during the late <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> period, c. 40,000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">Silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and meteoric <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> can also be found in native form, allowing a limited amount of <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a> in early cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ephotos_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ephotos-4">[4]</a></sup> Egyptian weapons made from <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">meteoric iron</a> in about 3000 BC were highly prized as "daggers from heaven".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keller_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-keller-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Certain metals, notably tin, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and (at a higher temperature) copper, can be recovered from their ores by simply heating the rocks in a fire or blast furnace, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a>. The first evidence of this extractive metallurgy dates from the 5th and 6th millennia BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and was found in the archaeological sites of <a href="/wiki/Majdanpek" title="Majdanpek">Majdanpek</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yarmovac&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yarmovac (page does not exist)">Yarmovac</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plocnik" title="Plocnik">Plocnik</a>, all three in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>. To date, the earliest evidence of copper smelting is found at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belovode" title="Belovode">Belovode</a> site,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> including a copper axe from 5500 BC belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_culture" title="Vinča culture">Vinča culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other signs of early metals are found from the third millennium BC in places like <a href="/wiki/Palmela" title="Palmela">Palmela</a> (Portugal), <a href="/wiki/Los_Millares" title="Los Millares">Los Millares</a> (Spain), and <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> (United Kingdom). However, the ultimate beginnings cannot be clearly ascertained and new discoveries are both continuous and ongoing.</p>

<p>These first metals were single ones or as found. About 3500 BC, it was discovered that by combining copper and tin, a superior metal could be made, an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> called <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>, representing a major technological shift known as the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.</p>
<p>The extraction of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> from its ore into a workable metal is much more difficult than for copper or tin. The process appears to have been invented by the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> in about 1200 BC, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. The secret of extracting and working iron was a key factor in the success of the <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keller_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-keller-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historical developments in ferrous metallurgy can be found in a wide variety of past cultures and civilizations. This includes the ancient and medieval kingdoms and empires of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>, ancient <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, ancient <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, ancient <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nok_culture" title="Nok culture">Ancient Nok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> of ancient <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, medieval Europe, ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, amongst others. Many applications, practices, and devices associated or involved in metallurgy were established in ancient China, such as the innovation of the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammers</a>, and double acting piston <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temple-r_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-temple-r-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 16th century book by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Agricola" title="Georg Agricola">Georg Agricola</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/De_re_metallica" title="De re metallica">De re metallica</a></i> describes the highly developed and complex processes of mining metal ores, metal extraction and metallurgy of the time. Agricola has been described as the "father of metallurgy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">Extractive metallurgy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">Extractive metallurgy</a> is the practice of removing valuable metals from an <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> and refining the extracted raw metals into a purer form. In order to convert a metal <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> to a purer metal, the ore must be <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> physically, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemically</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytically</a>.</p>
<p>Extractive metallurgists are interested in three primary streams: feed, concentrate (valuable metal oxide/sulfide), and <a href="/wiki/Tailings" title="Tailings">tailings</a> (waste). After mining, large pieces of the ore feed are broken through crushing and/or grinding in order to obtain particles small enough where each particle is either mostly valuable or mostly waste. Concentrating the particles of value in a form supporting separation enables the desired metal to be removed from waste products.</p>
<p>Mining may not be necessary if the ore body and physical environment are conducive to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_leaching" title="In-situ leaching">leaching</a>. Leaching dissolves minerals in an ore body and results in an enriched solution. The solution is collected and processed to extract valuable metals.</p>
<p>Ore bodies often contain more than one valuable metal. Tailings of a previous process may be used as a feed in another process to extract a secondary product from the original ore. Additionally, a concentrate may contain more than one valuable metal. That concentrate would then be processed to separate the valuable metals into individual constituents.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Common engineering <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> include <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>. These are most often used as alloys. Much effort has been placed on understanding the iron-carbon alloy system, which includes <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast irons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">Plain carbon steels</a> (those that contain essentially only carbon as an alloying element) are used in low-cost, high-strength applications where weight and <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> are not a problem. Cast irons, including <a href="/wiki/Ductile_iron" title="Ductile iron">ductile iron</a>, are also part of the iron-carbon system.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">Stainless steel</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galvanized_steel" title="Galvanized steel">galvanized steel</a> are used where resistance to corrosion is important. Aluminium alloys and magnesium alloys are used for applications where strength and lightness are required.</p>
<p>Copper-nickel alloys (such as <a href="/wiki/Monel" title="Monel">Monel</a>) are used in highly corrosive environments and for non-magnetic applications. Nickel-based <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> like <a href="/wiki/Inconel" title="Inconel">Inconel</a> are used in high-temperature applications such as <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbochargers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessels</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_exchangers" title="Heat exchangers">heat exchangers</a>. For extremely high temperatures, <a href="/wiki/Single_crystal" title="Single crystal">single crystal</a> alloys are used to minimize <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">production engineering</a>, metallurgy is concerned with the production of metallic components for use in consumer or <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> products. This involves the production of alloys, the shaping, the heat treatment and the surface treatment of the product. The task of the metallurgist is to achieve balance between material properties such as cost, <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">strength</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">toughness</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardness_(materials_science)" title="Hardness (materials science)">hardness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a> resistance, and performance in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> extremes. To achieve this goal, the operating environment must be carefully considered. In a saltwater environment, ferrous metals and some aluminium alloys corrode quickly. Metals exposed to cold or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> conditions may endure a ductile to brittle transition and lose their toughness, becoming more brittle and prone to cracking. Metals under continual cyclic loading can suffer from metal fatigue. Metals under constant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> at elevated temperatures can <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metalworking_processes">Metalworking processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metalworking processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">Metalworking</a></div>
<p>Metals are shaped by processes such as:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Casting_(metalworking)" title="Casting (metalworking)">casting</a> – molten metal is poured into a shaped <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">mold</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">forging</a> – a red-hot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billet_(manufacturing)" title="Billet (manufacturing)">billet</a> is hammered into shape.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rolling_(metalworking)" title="Rolling (metalworking)">rolling</a> – a billet is passed through successively narrower rollers to create a sheet.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cladding_(metalworking)" title="Cladding (metalworking)">laser cladding</a> – metallic powder is blown through a movable laser beam (e.g. mounted on a NC 5-axis machine). The resulting melted metal reaches a substrate to form a melt pool. By moving the laser head, it is possible to stack the tracks and build up a three-dimensional piece.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extrusion" title="Extrusion">extrusion</a> – a hot and malleable metal is forced under pressure through a <a href="/wiki/Die_(manufacturing)" title="Die (manufacturing)">die</a>, which shapes it before it cools.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a> – a <a href="/wiki/Powder_metallurgy" title="Powder metallurgy">powdered metal</a> is heated in a non-oxidizing environment after being compressed into a die.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machining</a> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lathe_(tool)" title="Lathe (tool)">lathes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milling_machine" title="Milling machine">milling machines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Drill" title="Drill">drills</a> cut the cold metal to shape.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrication_(metal)" title="Fabrication (metal)">fabrication</a> – sheets of metal are cut with <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotines</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_welding" title="Gas welding">gas cutters</a> and bent and welded into structural shape.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a> – Sintering or melting powder metal in a very small point on a moving 'print head' moving in 3D space to make any object to shape.</li>
</ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_work" title="Cold work">Cold-working</a> processes, in which the product’s shape is altered by rolling, fabrication or other processes while the product is cold, can increase the strength of the product by a process called <a href="/wiki/Work_hardening" title="Work hardening">work hardening</a>. Work hardening creates <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">microscopic defects</a> in the metal, which resist further changes of shape.</p>
<p>Various forms of casting exist in industry and academia. These include <a href="/wiki/Sand_casting" title="Sand casting">sand casting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Investment_casting" title="Investment casting">investment casting</a> (also called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_wax_process" title="Lost wax process">lost wax process</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Die_casting" title="Die casting">die casting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Continuous_casting" title="Continuous casting">continuous casting</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_treatment">Heat treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heat treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Metals can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_treatment" title="Heat treatment">heat-treated</a> to alter the properties of strength, ductility, toughness, hardness and/or resistance to corrosion. Common heat treatment processes include <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_strengthening" title="Precipitation strengthening">precipitation strengthening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quenching" title="Quenching">quenching</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tempering_(metallurgy)" title="Tempering (metallurgy)">tempering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <b>annealing</b> process softens the metal by heating it and then allowing it to cool very slowly, which gets rid of stresses in the metal and makes the grain structure large and soft-edged so that when the metal is hit or stressed it dents or perhaps bends, rather than breaking; it is also easier to sand, grind, or cut annealed metal. <b>Quenching</b> is the process of cooling a high-carbon steel very quickly after heating, thus "freezing" the steel's molecules in the very hard martensite form, which makes the metal harder. There is a balance between hardness and toughness in any steel; the harder the steel, the less tough or impact-resistant it is, and the more impact-resistant it is, the less hard it is. <b>Tempering</b> relieves stresses in the metal that were caused by the hardening process; tempering makes the metal less hard while making it better able to sustain impacts without breaking.</p>
<p>Often, mechanical and thermal treatments are combined in what are known as thermo-mechanical treatments for better properties and more efficient processing of materials. These processes are common to high-alloy special steels, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_alloys" title="Super alloys">super alloys</a> and titanium alloys.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plating">Plating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Plating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plating" title="Plating">Plating</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">Electroplating</a> is a chemical surface-treatment technique. It involves bonding a thin layer of another metal such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> to the surface of the product. It is used to reduce corrosion as well as to improve the product's aesthetic appearance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_spraying">Thermal spraying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Thermal spraying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_spraying" title="Thermal spraying">Thermal spraying</a></div>
<p>Thermal spraying techniques are another popular finishing option, and often have better high temperature properties than electroplated coatings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microstructure">Microstructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Microstructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="960" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png/220px-AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png/330px-AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png/440px-AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Metallography allows the metallurgist to study the microstructure of metals.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Metallurgists study the microscopic and macroscopic properties using <a href="/wiki/Metallography" title="Metallography">metallography</a>, a technique invented by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clifton_Sorby" title="Henry Clifton Sorby">Henry Clifton Sorby</a>. In metallography, an alloy of interest is ground flat and polished to a mirror finish. The sample can then be etched to reveal the microstructure and macrostructure of the metal. The sample is then examined in an optical or <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscope</a>, and the image contrast provides details on the composition, mechanical properties, and processing history.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">Crystallography</a>, often using <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> of <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, is another valuable tool available to the modern metallurgist. Crystallography allows identification of unknown materials and reveals the crystal structure of the sample. Quantitative crystallography can be used to calculate the amount of phases present as well as the degree of strain to which a sample has been subjected.</p>

The European Metallurgical Conference is organized by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=GDMB_Society_of_Metallurgists_and_Miners&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="GDMB Society of Metallurgists and Miners (page does not exist)">GDMB Society of Metallurgists and Miners</a>.</p>
