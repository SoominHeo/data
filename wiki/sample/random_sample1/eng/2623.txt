<p><b>Finance capitalism</b> or <b>financial capitalism</b> is the subordination of processes of <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> to the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> profits in a <a href="/wiki/Financial_system" title="Financial system">financial system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Financial capitalism is thus a form of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> where the intermediation of saving to investment becomes a dominant function in the economy, with wider implications for the political process and social evolution:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> since the late 20th century it has become the predominant force in the global economy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> whether in <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal</a> or other form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finance_capitalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Finance capitalism is characterized by a predominance of the pursuit of profit from the purchase and sale of, or <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a> in, <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currencies</a> and financial products such as <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">futures</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivatives</a>. It also includes <a href="/wiki/Usury" title="Usury">the lending of money at interest</a>; and is seen by <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist analysts</a> (from whom the term finance capitalism originally derived) as being exploitative by supplying income to non-laborers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Academic defenders of the economic concept of capitalism, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugen_von_B%C3%B6hm-Bawerk" title="Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk">Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk</a>, see such profits as part of the <a href="/wiki/Roundaboutness" title="Roundaboutness">roundabout</a> process by which it grows and <a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)" title="Hedge (finance)">hedges</a> against inevitable risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In financial capitalism, financial intermediaries become large concerns, ranging from banks to investment firms. Where deposit banks attract savings and lend out money, while investment banks obtain funds on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbank_market" title="Interbank market">interbank market</a> to re-lend for investment purposes, investment firms, by comparison, act on behalf of other concerns, by selling their equities or securities to investors, for investment purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_implications">Social implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finance_capitalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The meaning of the term financial capitalism goes beyond the importance of financial intermediation in the modern capitalist economy. It also encompasses the significant influence of the wealth holders on the political process and the aims of economic policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Palley" title="Thomas Palley">Thomas Palley</a> has argued that the 21st century predominance of finance capital has led to a preference for speculation – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casino_Capitalism" title="Casino Capitalism">Casino Capitalism</a> – over investment for entrepreneurial growth in the global economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_developments">Historical developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finance_capitalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Hilferding" title="Rudolf Hilferding">Rudolf Hilferding</a> is credited with first bringing the term finance capitalism into prominence, with his (1910) study of the links between German trusts, banks, and monopolies before <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> – a study subsumed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lenin" title="Lenin">Lenin</a> into his wartime analysis of the imperialist relations of the great world powers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Lenin concluded of the banks at that time that they were “the chief nerve centres of the whole capitalist system of national economy”:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern">Comintern</a>, the phrase "dictatorship of finance capitalism"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> became a regular one.</p>
<p>In such a traditional Marxist perspective, finance capitalism is seen as a dialectical outgrowth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_capitalism" title="Industrial capitalism">industrial capitalism</a>, and part of the process by which the whole capitalist phase of history comes to an end. In a fashion similar to the views of <a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen" title="Thorstein Veblen">Thorstein Veblen</a>, finance capitalism is contrasted with industrial capitalism, where profit is made from the manufacture of goods.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Braudel" title="Braudel">Braudel</a> would later point to two earlier periods when finance capitalism had emerged in human history – with the Genoese in the 16th century and the Dutch in the 17th and 18th centuries – although at those points it was from commercial capitalism that it developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Arrighi" title="Giovanni Arrighi">Giovanni Arrighi</a> extended Braudel's analysis to suggest that a predominance of finance capitalism is a recurring, long-term phenomenon, whenever a previous phase of commercial/industrial capitalist expansion reaches a plateau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whereas by mid-century the industrial corporation had displaced the banking system as the prime economic symbol of success,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the late twentieth-century growth of derivatives and of a novel banking model<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> ushered in a new (and historically fourth) period of finance capitalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fredric_Jameson" title="Fredric Jameson">Fredric Jameson</a> has seen the <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalised</a> abstractions of this current phase of financial capitalism as underpinning the cultural manifestations of <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opponents">Opponents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finance_capitalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opponents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fascist" title="Fascist">Fascists</a> were vocal in their opposition to finance capitalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/C._H._Sisson" title="C. H. Sisson">C. H. Sisson</a> saw the underlying theme of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cantos" title="The Cantos">The Cantos</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a> as the depredations of finance capital: “the monstrous aberration of a world in which reality is distorted, down to a detail never so comprehensively implicated before, by the pull of a fictitious money”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Feder" title="Gottfried Feder">Gottfried Feder</a> opposed financial capitalism and the concentration of capital by bankers in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</p>
