<p>The (c. 40 BCE) <i><b>Jijiupian</b></i> 急就篇, which was compiled by <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> scholar Shi You 史游 (fl. 48-33 BCE), was a Chinese <a href="/wiki/Primer_(textbook)" title="Primer (textbook)">primer</a> and a prototype for <a href="/wiki/Chinese_dictionary" title="Chinese dictionary">Chinese dictionaries</a>. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese character</a> <a href="/wiki/Abecedarium" title="Abecedarium">abecedarium</a> contains a series of <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthographic</a> word lists, categorized according to <a href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese_characters)" title="Radical (Chinese characters)">character radical</a>, and briefly explained in rhymed lines. In the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin</a> and Han dynasties, several similar othographic primers were in circulation, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cangjiepian" title="Cangjiepian">Cangjiepian</a></i>, but the <i>Jijiupian</i> is the only one that survived for two millennia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jijiupian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Jíjiùpiān</i> "Quickly Master [Character] Chapters" is also called the <i>Jíjiùzhāng</i> 急就章 "Quickly Master [Character] Sections" and simply <i>Jíjiù</i> 急就.</p>
<p>The title <i>Jíjiùpiān</i> uses the word <i>piān</i> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AF%87" title="wikt:篇">篇</a>, which originally meant "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bamboo_strips" title="Bamboo strips">bamboo strips</a> used for writing (before the invention of paper)" and was semantically extended to "sheet (of paper/etc.); piece of writing; article; chapter; section; book". Several other Chinese dictionary titles use <i>pian</i>, for example, the (c. 500? BCE) <i><a href="/wiki/Shizhoupian" title="Shizhoupian">Shizhoupian</a></i> "Historian Zhou's Chapters", (c. 220 BCE) <i><a href="/wiki/Cangjiepian" title="Cangjiepian">Cangjiepian</a></i> "<a href="/wiki/Cangjie" title="Cangjie">Cangjie</a>'s Chapters", (c. 543) <i><a href="/wiki/Yupian" title="Yupian">Yupian</a></i> "Jade Chapters", and (1066) <i><a href="/wiki/Leipian" title="Leipian">Leipian</a></i> "Categorized Chapters".</p>
<p><i>Jíjiù</i> has several possible interpretations, depending on the meanings of <i>jí</i> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%80%A5" title="wikt:急">急</a> "urgent; hurrying; rapid; fast; hasty; distress" and <i>jiù</i> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B0%B1" title="wikt:就">就</a> "proceed; advance; accomplish; achieve; accomplish; finish". The <i><a href="/wiki/Science_and_Civilisation_in_China" title="Science and Civilisation in China">Science and Civilisation in China</a></i> collaborators <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Needham" title="Joseph Needham">Joseph Needham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lu_Gwei-djen" title="Lu Gwei-djen">Lu Gwei-djen</a>, and Huang Hsing-Tsung (1986: 194) say, "One hardly knows how to render its title, unless 'Handy Primer'." Thomas Lee (2000: 437) translates <i>jijiu</i> as "quickly getting to". The Chinese lexicographers Heming Yong and Jing Peng (2008: 57) say that <i>jijiu</i> 急就 "instant success" suggested "fast learning", as seen in the first words of the <i>Jijiupian</i> preface.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Quickly learn the rarely seen drinking vessels and many different things: listing the names of objects, people, and family names; classify them into different sections so that they will not be easily mixed up. Occasional consultation will definitely be a great delight—for it is quick to retrieve, and, if hard effort is put into it, there will surely be surprising rewards. Please follow the guidelines in each chapter.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This passage is notably the earliest recorded discussion of how to classify characters into different textual sections (Yong and Peng 2008: 144).</p>
<p>While the title is usually <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterated</a> <i>Jijiupian</i>, <i>Chi-chiu-p'ien</i>, etc., some English translations are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Handy Primer</i> (Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 562)</li>
<li><i>Quick Access [to Characters]</i> (Theobald 2010)</li>
<li><i>Quick mastery of the characters</i> (Wilkinson 2000: 49)</li>
<li><i>The Instant Primer</i> (Yong and Peng 2008: 25)</li>
<li><i>Primer for Quickly Learning Chinese Characters</i> (Cai 2013: 85)</li>
<li><i>For Urgent Use</i> (Cheung and Lin 2014: n.p.)</li>
<li><i>Wood-Prism Bundles for Rapid Attainment</i> (Barbieri-Low and Yates 2015: 1102)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jijiupian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jijiupian.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2152" data-file-width="1569" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Jijiupian.png/220px-Jijiupian.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Jijiupian.png/330px-Jijiupian.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Jijiupian.png/440px-Jijiupian.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jijiupian.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1875 edition Jijiupianfuzhu 急就篇補注 with the Yan Shigu and Wang Yilin commentaries, <a href="/wiki/Keio_Media_Centers_(Libraries)" title="Keio Media Centers (Libraries)">Keio University Library</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first reference to the <i>Jijiupian</i> and Shi You 史游 is found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Yiwenzhi" title="Yiwenzhi">Yiwenzhi</a></i> "Treatise on Literature" bibliographical section of the (111 CE) <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanshu" title="Hanshu">Hanshu</a></i> "Book of Han", listed among early dictionaries: "In the time of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Yuan_of_Han" title="Emperor Yuan of Han">Emperor Yuan of Han</a> (r. 48-33 BCE), the court scholar Shi You composed the <i>Jijiupian</i>" (元帝时黄门令史游作急就篇). This section also uses the <i>Jijiu</i> title; <i>Jijiu yipian</i> 急就一篇 "The <i>Jijiu</i>, in one chapter" (tr. Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 194).</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Han_Dynasty" title="Western Han Dynasty">Western Han Dynasty</a> (206 BCE-25 CE), the teaching of characters was emphasized, and scholars compiled other character glossaries and wordbooks besides the <i>Jijiupian</i>, for instance, the <i>Fanjiangpian</i> 凡將篇 <i>The General Primer</i> by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Xiangru" title="Sima Xiangru">Sima Xiangru</a> and <i>Yuanshangpian</i> 元尚篇 <i>The Yuanshang Primer</i> by Li Chang 李長, both of which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_works" title="Lost works">lost works</a>. These proto-dictionaries facilitated the development of Chinese <i>xiǎoxué</i> 小學 "minor learning; pre-modern 'linguistics'; philology" (which now means "primary school"), and laid the academic foundation for the compilation of wordbooks, vocabularies, and dictionaries (tr. Yong and Peng 2008: 25). The Han dynasty experienced the transition of Chinese lexicography from wordlists and glossaries to character dictionaries and word dictionaries. The <i>Jijiupian</i> generalized the practice of logically classifying characters into different sections, which inspired the "macro-level stylistic format of the Chinese dictionary" (Yong and Peng 2008: 144).</p>
<p>From the Han to the <a href="/wiki/Six_Dynasties" title="Six Dynasties">Six Dynasties</a> (220-589), the most popular character textbook was the <i>Jijiupian</i> (Wilkinson 2000: 49). During the <a href="/wiki/Northern_and_Southern_dynasties" title="Northern and Southern dynasties">Northern and Southern dynasties</a> (420-589), several other popular textbooks appeared, such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qianziwen" title="Qianziwen">Qianziwen</a></i> "Thousand Characters Text", <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baijiaxing" title="Baijiaxing">Baijiaxing</a></i> "Myriad Family Surnames", and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanzijing" title="Sanzijing">Sanzijing</a></i> "Three-character Classic" (Yong and Peng 2008: 57). By the Tang, the <i>Jijiupian</i> had been replaced by the <i>Qianziwen</i> and <i>Baijiaxing</i>, both of which were deliberately written so that few characters they contain occur more than once. They were memorized generation after generation for over 1,000 years. In the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> (1271-1368) and <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> (1644-1912), the most popular primer was the <i>Sanzijing</i> (Wilkinson 2000: 49). Contemporary Chinese scholarship admires the <i>Jijiupian</i> because of its high factual content as contrasted with the much more moralistic tendencies of similar later works such as the <i>Sanzijing</i> (Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 195).</p>
<p>The <i>Jijiupian</i> was one of several similar wordbooks that circulated widely during the Qin and Han periods, but it alone survived to the present day, owing to several factors. One reason for its preservation was a model version written by the famous Jin dynasty <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">Chinese calligrapher</a> <a href="/wiki/Wang_Xizhi" title="Wang Xizhi">Wang Xizhi</a> (303–361), which was copied by generation after generation of literati eager to perfect their calligraphy (Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 195). The Yuan dynasty calligrapher <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Mengfu" title="Zhao Mengfu">Zhao Mengfu</a> (1254–1322) also produced an orthographic model <i>Jijiupian</i>. Another factor was the textual explanations written by famous authors in later dynasties. The Tang scholar <a href="/wiki/Yan_Shigu" title="Yan Shigu">Yan Shigu</a> wrote a (620) commentary, and the Song scholar Wang Yinglin 王應麟 wrote the (1280) <i>Xingshi Jijiupian</i> 姓氏急就篇 (Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 195).</p>
<p>Modern archeological excavations have found fragments of the <i>Jijiupian</i>, and even some tablets on which the inscriptions were evidently exercises in copying characters (Loewe 1967 2: 418; Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 197).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Text">Text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jijiupian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original <i>Jijiupian</i> consisted of 32 sections (<i>zhang</i> 章), each with 63 <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>, totaling 2,016. Some later editions of the text add two more sections, each with 64 characters, thus totaling 2,144. The text was designed to include as many different characters as possible, with little repetition, in order to maximize the student's exposure to and learning of new words. The text is described as a sort of <a href="/wiki/Abecedarium" title="Abecedarium">abecedarium</a> (Goodrich 1975: 220, Yates 2011: 358).</p>
<p>The <i>Jijiupian</i> <a href="/wiki/Collation" title="Collation">collation</a> puts together characters written with the same <a href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese_characters)" title="Radical (Chinese characters)">radical</a> or signific, and then divides them into chapters. Within each chapter, the style consisted mainly of 3-, 4-, or 7-syllable rhyming lines, as in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_poetry" title="Chinese poetry">Chinese poetry</a>. Rhyme makes it easy to read, memorize, and recite (Yong and Peng 2008: 57).</p>
<p>China's first "word books" were written to meet the needs of literacy education (Allan 2013: 212). Shi You's <i>Jijiupian</i> was intended to be used for learning the meanings of basic characters and how they should properly be written. It formed a basis for verbal elaborations by teachers, and could have served as a handy reference manual for scribes and copyists (Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 195). The original Han edition <i>Jijiupian</i> was written in <a href="/wiki/Clerical_script" title="Clerical script">clerical script</a>, but it was later used for learning to write in other calligraphic styles of characters, such as <a href="/wiki/Regular_script" title="Regular script">regular script</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cursive_script_(East_Asia)" title="Cursive script (East Asia)">cursive script</a>.</p>
<p>The <i>Jijiupian</i> has <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">historical linguistic</a> value as a record of common words that were current during the Han dynasty. It preserved many technical terms and names of plants, animals, tools, and objects, which are important for the histories of science, medicine, and technology. For instance, the <i>Jijiupian</i> was the first text to describe the <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_of_the_Han_dynasty#Uses_of_the_waterwheel_and_water_clock" title="Science and technology of the Han dynasty">waterwheel</a>.</p>
<p>To illustrate the types of lists that the <i>Jijiupian</i> contains, and their value for those who wished to write correctly, consider the Section 24 list of <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional Chinese herbal medicines</a>, written in rhymed 7-character lines (tr. Needham, Lu, and Huang 1986: 195).</p>
<blockquote>
<p>灸刺和药逐去邪 By moxa, acupuncture and the compounding of drugs we may drive out the malign (<i><a href="/wiki/Qi" title="Qi">qi</a></i> that cause illness). (Of drugs and drug-plants there are:) <i>Huángqín</i> 黄芩 <i><a href="/wiki/Scutellaria_lateriflora" title="Scutellaria lateriflora">Scutellaria lateriflora</a></i>, <i>fúlíng</i> 伏苓 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolfiporia_cocos" title="Wolfiporia cocos">Wolfiporia cocos</a></i>, <i>yù</i> 礜 <a href="/wiki/Arsenolite" title="Arsenolite">arsenolite</a>, and <i>cháihú</i> 茈胡 <i><a href="/wiki/Bupleurum_falcatum" title="Bupleurum falcatum">Bupleurum falcatum</a></i>. <i>Mǔméng</i> 牡蒙 <i><a href="/wiki/Rubia" title="Rubia">Rubia yunnanensis</a></i>, <i>gāncǎo</i> 甘草 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycyrrhiza_glabra" title="Glycyrrhiza glabra">Glycyrrhiza glabra</a></i>, <i>wǎn</i> 菀 <i><a href="/wiki/Aster_tataricus" title="Aster tataricus">Aster tataricus</a></i>, and <i>lílú</i> 藜蘆 <i><a href="/wiki/Veratrum" title="Veratrum">Veratrum niqrum</a></i>. <i>Wūhuì</i> 烏喙 and <i>fùzǐ</i> 附子 both <i><a href="/wiki/Aconitum_carmichaelii" title="Aconitum carmichaelii">Aconitum carmichaelii</a></i>, <i>jiāo</i> 椒 , <i><a href="/wiki/Zanthoxylum_piperitum" title="Zanthoxylum piperitum">Zanthoxylum piperitum</a></i>, and <i>yánhuá</i> 芫花 <i><a href="/wiki/Daphne_genkwa" title="Daphne genkwa">Daphne genkwa</a></i>. <i>Bànxià</i> 半夏 <i><a href="/wiki/Pinellia_ternata" title="Pinellia ternata">Pinellia ternata</a></i>, <i>zàojiá</i> 皂莢 <i><a href="/wiki/Gleditsia_sinensis" title="Gleditsia sinensis">Gleditsia sinensis</a></i>, <i>ài</i> 艾 <i><a href="/wiki/Artemisia_argyi" title="Artemisia argyi">Artemisia argyi</a></i>, and <i>tuówú</i> 橐吾 <i><a href="/wiki/Ligularia_sibirica" title="Ligularia sibirica">Ligularia sibirica</a></i>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The rhyme-words (Old Chinese reconstructions from <a href="/wiki/Reconstructions_of_Old_Chinese#Baxter.E2.80.93Sagart_.282014.29" title="Reconstructions of Old Chinese">Baxter-Sagart 2014</a>) are: *<i>sə.ɢa</i> 邪, *<i>ɡˁa</i> 胡, *<i>rˁa</i> 蘆, *<i>qʷʰˁra</i> 花, and *<i>ŋˁa</i> 吾. With the exception of the mineral arsenolite (arsenic oxide), most of these names are written with the "<a href="/wiki/Radical_140" title="Radical 140">plant radical</a>" 艸 or 艹, which is commonly used in characters for plants and trees.</p>
<p>The text "impresses readers with its balanced content and ingenuity" (Lee 2000: 437), and some examples are:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>To buy on credit, to borrow, to sell and to buy, these activities give convenience to merchants and markets….To cut, to mince, to broil and to cook a whole piece of meat, each has its own shape….Rooms, houses, and inns are [for people] to rest and there are also towers, palaces, and halls….Various ranked lords have their fiefs, lands, and household vassals; these [privileges] come from hard studies, but not from [the help of] ghosts or spirits. (tr. Lee 2000: 438)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <i>Jijiupian</i> teaches students basic vocabulary for daily life, with occasional moral lessons.</p>
