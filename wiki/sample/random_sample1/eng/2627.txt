<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gigabit Ethernet</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networking</a>, <b>Gigabit Ethernet</b> (<b>GbE</b> or <b>1 GigE</b>) is a term describing various technologies for transmitting <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_frame" title="Ethernet frame">Ethernet frames</a> at a rate of a <a href="/wiki/Data_rate_units#Gigabit_per_second" title="Data rate units">gigabit per second</a> (1,000,000,000 bits per second), as defined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3-2008" title="IEEE 802.3-2008">IEEE 802.3-2008</a> standard. It came into use beginning in 1999, gradually supplanting <a href="/wiki/Fast_Ethernet" title="Fast Ethernet">Fast Ethernet</a> in wired local networks, as a result of being considerably faster. The cables and equipment are very similar to previous standards and have been very common and economical since 2010.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-duplex" title="Half-duplex">Half-duplex</a> gigabit links connected through <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_hub" title="Ethernet hub">repeater hubs</a> were part of the IEEE specification,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but the specification is not updated anymore and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-duplex" title="Full-duplex">full-duplex</a> operation with <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">switches</a> is used exclusively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> was the result of the research done at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> in the early 1970s. Ethernet later evolved into a widely implemented <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_link_layer" title="Data link layer">link</a> <a href="/wiki/OSI_model" title="OSI model">layer</a> protocol. <a href="/wiki/Fast_Ethernet" title="Fast Ethernet">Fast Ethernet</a> increased speed from 10 to 100 megabits per second (Mbit/s). Gigabit Ethernet was the next iteration, increasing the speed to 1000 Mbit/s. The initial standard for Gigabit Ethernet was produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> in June 1998 as <b>IEEE 802.3z</b>, and required <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a>. 802.3z is commonly referred to as 1000BASE-X, where -X refers to either -CX, -SX, -LX, or (non-standard) -ZX. For the history behind the "X" see <a href="/wiki/Fast_Ethernet#General_design" title="Fast Ethernet">Fast Ethernet</a>.</p>
<p><b>IEEE 802.3ab</b>, ratified in 1999, defines Gigabit Ethernet transmission over <a href="/wiki/Shielded_cable" title="Shielded cable">unshielded</a> <a href="/wiki/Twisted_pair" title="Twisted pair">twisted pair</a> (UTP) <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">category 5, 5e</a> or <a href="/wiki/Category_6_cable" title="Category 6 cable">6</a> cabling, and became known as 1000BASE-T. With the ratification of 802.3ab, Gigabit Ethernet became a desktop technology as organizations could use their existing copper cabling infrastructure.</p>
<p><b>IEEE 802.3ah</b>, ratified in 2004 added two more gigabit fiber standards, 1000BASE-LX10 (which was already widely implemented as vendor specific extension) and 1000BASE-BX10. This was part of a larger group of protocols known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethernet_in_the_First_Mile" title="Ethernet in the First Mile">Ethernet in the First Mile</a>.</p>
<p>Initially, Gigabit Ethernet was deployed in high-capacity <a href="/wiki/Backbone_network" title="Backbone network">backbone network</a> links (for instance, on a high-capacity campus network). In 2000, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple's</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_Mac_G4" title="Power Mac G4">Power Mac G4</a> and <a href="/wiki/PowerBook_G4" title="PowerBook G4">PowerBook G4</a> were the first mass-produced personal computers featuring the 1000BASE-T connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It quickly became a built-in feature in many other computers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are five <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a> standards for Gigabit Ethernet using <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a> (1000BASE-X), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twisted_pair_cable" title="Twisted pair cable">twisted pair cable</a> (1000BASE-T), or shielded balanced copper cable (1000BASE-CX).</p>
<p>The IEEE 802.3z standard includes 1000BASE-SX for transmission over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-mode_fiber" title="Multi-mode fiber">multi-mode fiber</a>, 1000BASE-LX for transmission over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-mode_fiber" title="Single-mode fiber">single-mode fiber</a>, and the nearly obsolete 1000BASE-CX for transmission over shielded balanced copper cabling. These standards use <a href="/wiki/8b/10b_encoding" title="8b/10b encoding">8b/10b encoding</a>, which inflates the line rate by 25%, from 1000 Mbit/s to 1250 Mbit/s, to ensure a DC balanced signal. The symbols are then sent using <a href="/wiki/Non-return-to-zero" title="Non-return-to-zero">NRZ</a>.</p>
<p>Optical fiber transceivers are most often implemented as user-swappable modules in <a href="/wiki/Small_form-factor_pluggable_transceiver" title="Small form-factor pluggable transceiver">SFP</a> form or <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_interface_converter" title="Gigabit interface converter">GBIC</a> on older devices.</p>
<p>IEEE 802.3ab, which defines the widely used 1000BASE-T interface type, uses a different encoding scheme in order to keep the symbol rate as low as possible, allowing transmission over twisted pair.</p>
<p>IEEE 802.3ap defines Ethernet Operation over Electrical Backplanes at different speeds.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethernet_in_the_First_Mile" title="Ethernet in the First Mile">Ethernet in the First Mile</a> later added 1000BASE-LX10 and -BX10.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-X">1000BASE-X</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-X">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1000BASE-X is used in industry to refer to Gigabit Ethernet transmission over fiber, where options include 1000BASE-SX, 1000BASE-LX, 1000BASE-LX10, 1000BASE-BX10 or the non-standard -EX and -ZX implementations.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-CX">1000BASE-CX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-CX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-CX is an initial standard for Gigabit Ethernet connections with maximum distances of 25 meters using balanced shielded twisted pair and either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DE-9" title="DE-9">DE-9</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8P8C" title="8P8C">8P8C</a> connector (with a pinout different from 1000BASE-T). The short segment length is due to very high signal transmission rate. Although it is still used for specific applications where cabling is done by IT professionals, for instance the IBM BladeCenter uses 1000BASE-CX for the Ethernet connections between the blade servers and the switch modules, 1000BASE-T has succeeded it for general copper wiring use.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-KX">1000BASE-KX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-KX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-KX is part of the IEEE 802.3ap standard for Ethernet Operation over Electrical Backplanes. This standard defines one to four lanes of backplane links, one RX and one TX differential pair per lane, at link bandwidth ranging from 100Mbit to 10Gbit per second (from 100BASE-KX to 10GBASE-KX4). The 1000BASE-KX variant uses 1.25 GBd electrical (not optical) signalling speed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-SX">1000BASE-SX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-SX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-SX is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" title="Fiber optic">fiber optic</a> Gigabit Ethernet standard for operation over multi-mode fiber using a 770 to 860 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">near infrared</a> (NIR) <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>.</p>
<p>The standard specifies a distance capability between 220 metres (62.5/125 µm fiber with low <a href="/wiki/Modal_bandwidth" title="Modal bandwidth">modal bandwidth</a>) and 550 metres (50/125 µm fiber with high modal bandwidth). In practice, with good quality fiber, optics, and terminations, 1000BASE-SX will usually work over significantly longer distances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This standard is highly popular for intra-building links in large office buildings, co-location facilities and carrier-neutral Internet exchanges.</p>
<p>Optical power specifications of SX interface: Minimum output power = −9.5 <a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a>. Minimum receive sensitivity = −17 dBm.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-LX">1000BASE-LX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-LX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-LX is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" title="Fiber optic">fiber optic</a> Gigabit Ethernet standard specified in IEEE 802.3 Clause 38 which uses a long wavelength laser (1,270–1,355 nm), and a maximum RMS spectral width of 4 nm.</p>
<p>1000BASE-LX is specified to work over a distance of up to 5 km over 10 µm single-mode fiber.</p>
<p>1000BASE-LX can also run over all common types of multi-mode fiber with a maximum segment length of 550 m. For link distances greater than 300 m, the use of a special launch conditioning patch cord may be required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This launches the laser at a precise offset from the center of the fiber which causes it to spread across the diameter of the fiber core, reducing the effect known as differential mode delay which occurs when the laser couples onto only a small number of available modes in multi-mode fiber.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-LX10">1000BASE-LX10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-LX10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-LX10 was standardized six years after the initial gigabit fiber versions as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethernet_in_the_First_Mile" title="Ethernet in the First Mile">Ethernet in the First Mile</a> task group. It is practically identical to 1000BASE-LX, but achieves longer distances up to 10 km over a pair of single-mode fiber due to higher quality optics. Before it was standardized, 1000BASE-LX10 was essentially already in widespread use by many vendors as a proprietary extension called either 1000BASE-LX/LH or 1000BASE-LH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-EX">1000BASE-EX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-EX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-EX is a non-standard but industry accepted<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> term to refer to Gigabit Ethernet transmission. It is very similar to 1000BASE-LX10 but achieves longer distances up to 40 km over a pair of single-mode fibers due to higher quality optics than a LX10, running on 1310 nm wavelength lasers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is sometimes referred to as LH (Long Haul), and is easily confused with 1000BASE-LX10 or 1000BASE-ZX because the use of -LX(10), -LH, -EX, and -ZX is ambiguous between vendors. <a href="#1000BASE-ZX">1000BASE-ZX</a> is a very similar non-standard longer-reach variant that uses 1550 nm wavelength optics.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-BX10">1000BASE-BX10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-BX10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-BX10 is capable of up to 10 km over a single strand of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-mode_fiber" title="Single-mode fiber">single-mode fiber</a>, with a different wavelength going in each direction. The terminals on each side of the fibre are not equal, as the one transmitting downstream (from the center of the network to the outside) uses the 1490 nm wavelength, and the one transmitting upstream uses the 1310 nm wavelength. This is accomplished using a passive splitter prism inside each transceiver.</p>
<p>Other, non-standard higher-powered single-strand optics commonly known as "BiDi" (bi-directional) utilize wavelength pairs in the 1490/1550 nm range, and are capable of reaching distances of 20, 40 and 80 km, or greater depending on module cost, fiber path loss, splices, connectors and patch panels. Very long reach BiDi optics may use 1510/1590 nm wavelength pairs.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-ZX">1000BASE-ZX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-ZX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>1000BASE-ZX is a non-standard but multi-vendor<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> term to refer to Gigabit Ethernet transmission using 1,550 nm wavelength to achieve distances of at least 70 kilometres (43 miles) over single-mode fiber. Some vendors specify distances up to 120 kilometres (75 miles) over single-mode fiber, sometimes called 1000BASE-EZX. Ranges beyond 80 km are highly dependent upon the path loss of the fiber in use, specifically the attenuation figure in dB per km, the number and quality of connectors/patch panels and splices located between transceivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-T">1000BASE-T</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-T">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>1000BASE-T (also known as IEEE 802.3ab) is a standard for Gigabit Ethernet over <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> wiring.</p>
<p>Each 1000BASE-T network segment can be a maximum length of 100 meters (330 feet), and must use <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">Category 5 cable</a> or better (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_5e_cable" title="Category 5e cable">Cat 5e</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category_6_cable" title="Category 6 cable">Cat 6</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Autonegotiation" title="Autonegotiation">Autonegotiation</a> is a requirement for using 1000BASE-T<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> according to <i>Section 28D.5 Extensions required for Clause40 (1000BASE-T)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At least the clock source has to be negotiated, as one endpoint must be master and the other endpoint must be slave.</p>
<p>In a departure from both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T">10BASE-T</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/100BASE-TX" title="100BASE-TX">100BASE-TX</a>, 1000BASE-T uses four lanes over all four cable pairs for simultaneous transmission in both directions through the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echo_cancellation" title="Echo cancellation">echo cancellation</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptive_equalization" title="Adaptive equalization">adaptive equalization</a> and five-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulse_amplitude_modulation" title="Pulse amplitude modulation">pulse amplitude modulation</a> (PAM-5). The symbol rate is identical to that of 100BASE-TX (125 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megabaud" title="Megabaud">megabaud</a>) and the noise immunity of the five-level signaling is also identical to that of the three-level signaling in 100BASE-TX, since 1000BASE-T uses four-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Trellis_modulation" title="Trellis modulation">trellis coded modulation</a> (TCM) to achieve a 6 <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a> <a href="/wiki/Coding_gain" title="Coding gain">coding gain</a> across the four pairs.</p>
<p>Since negotiation takes place on only two pairs, if two gigabit devices are connected through a cable with only two pairs, the devices will successfully choose 'gigabit' as the highest common denominator (HCD), but the link will never come up. Most gigabit physical devices have a specific register to diagnose this behaviour. Some drivers offer an "Ethernet@Wirespeed" option where this situation leads to a slower yet functional connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The data is transmitted over four copper pairs, eight <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bits</a> at a time. First, eight bits of data are expanded into four three-bit symbols through a non-trivial scrambling procedure based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_feedback_shift_register" title="Linear feedback shift register">linear feedback shift register</a>; this is similar to what is done in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/100BASE-T2" title="100BASE-T2">100BASE-T2</a>, but uses different parameters. The three-bit symbols are then mapped to voltage levels which vary continuously during transmission. An example mapping is as follows:</p>

<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto-MDIX" title="Auto-MDIX">Automatic MDI/MDI-X Configuration</a></i> is specified as an optional feature in the 1000BASE-T standard,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> meaning that straight-through cables will often work between gigabit-capable interfaces. This feature eliminates the need for <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_crossover_cable" title="Ethernet crossover cable">crossover cables</a>, making obsolete the uplink/normal ports and manual selector switches found on many older hubs and switches and greatly reduces installation errors.</p>
<p>In order to extend and maximize the use of existing Cat-5e and Cat-6 cabling, additional next-generation standards <a href="/wiki/2.5GBASE-T_and_5GBASE-T" title="2.5GBASE-T and 5GBASE-T">2.5GBASE-T and 5GBASE-T</a> will operate at 2.5 and 5.0 Gbit/s, respectively, on existing copper infrastructure designed for use with 1000BASE-T.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10GBASE-T" title="10GBASE-T">10GBASE-T</a> but uses lower signaling frequencies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-T1">1000BASE-T1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-T1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>IEEE 802.3 standardized 1000BASE-T1 in IEEE Std 802.3bp-2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It defines Gigabit Ethernet over a single twisted pair for automotive and industrial applications. It includes cable specifications for 15 meters or 40 meters reach.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1000BASE-TX">1000BASE-TX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigabit_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 1000BASE-TX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Industry_Association" title="Telecommunications Industry Association">Telecommunications Industry Association</a> (TIA) created and promoted a standard similar to 1000BASE-T that was simpler to implement, calling it 1000BASE-TX (TIA/EIA-854).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The simplified design would have, in theory, reduced the cost of the required electronics by only using two unidirectional pairs (one pair TX, one RX) instead of four bidirectional pairs. However, this solution has been a commercial failure, likely due to the required Category 6 cabling and the rapidly falling cost of 1000BASE-T products.</p>
