<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bronchospasm</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Bronchospasm</b> or a bronchial spasm is a sudden constriction of the muscles in the walls of the <a href="/wiki/Bronchiole" title="Bronchiole">bronchioles</a>. It is caused by the release (<a href="/wiki/Degranulation" title="Degranulation">degranulation</a>) of substances from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mast_cells" title="Mast cells">mast cells</a> or <a href="/wiki/Basophil" title="Basophil">basophils</a> under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Anaphylatoxin" title="Anaphylatoxin">anaphylatoxins</a>. It causes difficulty in breathing which can be very mild to severe.</p>

<p>Bronchospasms appear as the feature of <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, chronic <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anaphylaxis" title="Anaphylaxis">anaphylaxis</a>. Bronchospasms are a possible side effect of some drugs: <a href="/wiki/Pilocarpine" title="Pilocarpine">pilocarpine</a> (which is used to treat illness resulting from the ingestion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deadly_nightshade" title="Deadly nightshade">deadly nightshade</a>, as well as other things), <a href="/wiki/Beta_blocker" title="Beta blocker">beta blockers</a> (used to treat hypertension), a <a class="external text" href="http://www.perforomist.com/en/hcp/about/laba" rel="nofollow">paradoxical result</a> of using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_acting_beta-adrenoceptor_agonist" title="Long acting beta-adrenoceptor agonist">LABA</a> drugs (to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COPD" title="COPD">COPD</a>) and other drugs. Bronchospasms can present as a sign of <a href="/wiki/Giardiasis" title="Giardiasis">giardiasis</a>.</p>
<p>Bronchospasms are one of several conditions associated with cold housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the things that can cause bronchospasms are: consuming foods, taking medicines, allergic responses to insects, and fluctuating hormone levels, particularly in women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haggerty2003_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haggerty2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatfield_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatfield-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few of the more common allergens are foods such as eggs, milk, peanuts, walnuts, tree and other nuts, fish, especially shellfish, soy and wheat; insect bites and stings, especially bee stings; and other medicines, especially penicillin and its derivatives.</p>
<p>The overactivity of the bronchioles' muscle is a result of exposure to a stimulus which under normal circumstances would cause little or no response. The resulting constriction and inflammation causes a narrowing of the airways and an increase in <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> production; this reduces the amount of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> that is available to the individual causing breathlessness, coughing and <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>.</p>
<p>Bronchospasms are a serious potential complication of placing a <a href="/wiki/Tracheal_tube" title="Tracheal tube">breathing tube</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_anesthesia" title="General anesthesia">general anesthesia</a>. When the airways spasm or constrict in response to the irritating stimulus of the breathing tube, it is difficult to maintain the airway and the patient can become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apneic" title="Apneic">apneic</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronchospasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bronchospasm are caused by a number of reasons. Lower respiratory tract conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a> (COPD), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> can result in contraction of the airways. Other causes are side effects of topical decongestants such as <a href="/wiki/Oxymetazoline" title="Oxymetazoline">oxymetazoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenylephrine" title="Phenylephrine">phenylephrine</a>. Both of these medications activate alpha-1 adrenergic receptors that result in smooth muscle constriction. Non-selective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_blockers" title="Beta blockers">beta blockers</a> are known to facilitate bronchospasm as well. Beta blockers bind into the β2 receptors and block the action of epinephrine and norepinephrine from binding to its receptors, causing shortness of breath.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscarinic_Acetylcholine_receptor_antagonist">Muscarinic Acetylcholine receptor antagonist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronchospasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Muscarinic Acetylcholine receptor antagonist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>See also</i>: <a href="/wiki/Parasympathetic_nervous_system" title="Parasympathetic nervous system">Parasympathetic nervous system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system" title="Sympathetic nervous system">Sympathetic nervous system</a></p>
<p>The neurotransmitter <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a> is known to decrease sympathetic response by slowing the heart rate and constricting the <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle_tissue" title="Smooth muscle tissue">smooth muscle tissue</a>. Ongoing research and successful clinical trials have shown that agents such as <a href="/wiki/Diphenhydramine" title="Diphenhydramine">diphenhydramine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atropine" title="Atropine">atropine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ipratropium_bromide" title="Ipratropium bromide">Ipratropium bromide</a> (all of which act as <a href="/wiki/Receptor_antagonist" title="Receptor antagonist">receptor antagonists</a> of <a href="/wiki/Muscarinic_acetylcholine_receptor" title="Muscarinic acetylcholine receptor">muscarinic acetylcholine receptors</a>) are effective for treating asthma and COPD-related symptoms<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
