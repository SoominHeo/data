<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Functional food</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>functional food</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> given an additional <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/function" title="wikt:function">function</a> (often one related to health-promotion or disease prevention) by adding new ingredients or more of existing ingredients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term may also apply to traits purposely <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">bred</a> into existing edible plants, such as purple or gold potatoes having enriched <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> contents, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-usda-2">[2]</a></sup> Functional foods may be "designed to have physiological benefits and/or reduce the risk of chronic disease beyond basic nutritional functions, and may be similar in appearance to conventional food and consumed as part of a regular diet".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term was first used in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in the 1980s where there is a government approval process for functional foods called Foods for Specified Health Use (FOSHU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Functional_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The functional food industry, consisting of food, beverage and supplement sectors, is one of the several areas of the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a> that is experiencing fast growth in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roberts_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-roberts-5">[5]</a></sup> It is estimated that the global market of functional food industry will reach 176.7 billion in 2013 with a <a href="/wiki/Compound_annual_growth_rate" title="Compound annual growth rate">compound annual growth rate</a> (CAGR) of 7.4%. Specifically, the functional food sector will experience 6.9% CAGR, the supplement sector will rise by 3.8% and the functional beverage sector will be the fastest growing segment with 10.8% CAGR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roberts_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-roberts-5">[5]</a></sup> This kind of growth is fueled not only by industrial innovation and development of new products that satisfy the demand of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_industry" title="Health industry">health</a> conscious <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> but also by <a href="/wiki/Health_claim" title="Health claim">health claims</a> covering a wide range of health issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-scholan-6">[6]</a></sup> Yet, consumer <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a> persists mainly because benefits associated with consuming the products may be difficult to be detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholan_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-scholan-6">[6]</a></sup> Strict examination of some of the functional food claims may discourage some companies from launching their products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholan_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-scholan-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
