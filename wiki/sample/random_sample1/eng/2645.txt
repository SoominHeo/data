<p><a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a> believe that <b>Jesus</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Messiah#Christianity" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> (<a href="/wiki/Christ_(title)" title="Christ (title)">Christ</a>) and believe that through <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">his death and resurrection</a>, humans can be reconciled to <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">God</a> and thereby are offered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvation_(Christianity)" title="Salvation (Christianity)">salvation</a> and the promise of <a href="/wiki/Eternal_life_(Christianity)" title="Eternal life (Christianity)">eternal life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These teachings emphasize that as the <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">willing</a> <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God" title="Lamb of God">Lamb of God</a>, Jesus <i>chose to</i> suffer on the cross at <a href="/wiki/Calvary" title="Calvary">Calvary</a> as a sign of his full obedience to the will of the <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">Eternal Father</a>, as an "agent and servant of God".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anselm_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-anselm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cullmann_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cullmann-4">[4]</a></sup> The choice Jesus made thus counter-positions him as a new man of morality and obedience, in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam</a>'s disobedience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pannenberg_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pannenberg-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most Christians believe that Jesus was both human and divine—the <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God_(Christianity)" title="Son of God (Christianity)">Son of God</a>. While there has been theological debate over the nature of Jesus, <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinitarian</a> Christians believe that Jesus is the <a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">Logos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity)" title="Incarnation (Christianity)">God incarnate</a>, <a href="/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">God the Son</a>, and "<a href="/wiki/Hypostatic_union" title="Hypostatic union">true God and true man</a>"—both fully divine and fully human. Jesus, <a href="/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity)" title="Incarnation (Christianity)">having become fully human</a> in all respects, suffered the pains and temptations of a mortal man, yet he did not <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_sin" title="Christian views on sin">sin</a>. As fully God, he defeated death and rose to life again. According to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, God raised him from the dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">ascended to heaven</a> to <a href="/wiki/Session_of_Christ" title="Session of Christ">sit</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_Hand_of_God" title="Right Hand of God">Right Hand of God</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and he will <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">return to earth again</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgment" title="Last Judgment">Last Judgment</a> and the establishment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_God" title="Kingdom of God">Kingdom of God</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_to_Come" title="World to Come">World to Come</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Although Christian views of Jesus vary, it is possible to summarize key elements of the shared beliefs among major denominations based on their <a href="/wiki/Catechism" title="Catechism">catechetical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Confessionalism_(religion)" title="Confessionalism (religion)">confessional</a> texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CathLuther1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CathLuther1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Christian views of Jesus are derived from various biblical sources, particularly from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonical_Gospels" title="Canonical Gospels">canonical Gospels</a> and New Testament letters such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauline_Epistles" title="Pauline Epistles">Pauline Epistles</a>. Christians predominantly hold that these works are historically true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Those groups or denominations committed to what are considered biblically <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodox</a> Christianity nearly all agree on the following points:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Christians believe that Jesus was a human being who was also fully God.</li>
<li>Christians believe that Jesus came into the world as the son of only one earthly parent, Mary.</li>
<li>Christians believe that Jesus never sinned or did anything wrong.</li>
<li>Christians believe that Jesus was eventually martyred as a religious heretic, was buried in a tomb, and then on the third day came back to life.</li>
<li>Christians believe that because he rose from the tomb on the third day, that he lives and has a body of flesh and bone today.</li>
<li>Christians believe that Jesus eventually ascended back to God the Father.</li>
<li>Christians believe that Jesus will come back to earth a second time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The five major milestones in the gospel narrative of the life of Jesus are his <a href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus" title="Baptism of Jesus">Baptism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transfiguration_of_Jesus" title="Transfiguration of Jesus">Transfiguration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">Crucifixion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">Resurrection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">Ascension</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digby_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-digby-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBreck12_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-JBreck12-17">[17]</a></sup> These are usually bracketed by two other episodes: his <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Jesus" title="Nativity of Jesus">Nativity</a> at the beginning and the sending of the <a href="/wiki/Paraclete" title="Paraclete">Paraclete</a> (Holy Spirit) at the end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digby_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-digby-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBreck12_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-JBreck12-17">[17]</a></sup> The gospel accounts of the teachings of Jesus are often presented in terms of specific categories involving his "works and words", e.g., his <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">ministry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parables_of_Jesus" title="Parables of Jesus">parables</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miracles_of_Jesus" title="Miracles of Jesus">miracles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zuck100_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zuck100-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPent212_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPent212-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christians not only attach theological significance to the <i>works</i> of Jesus, but also to his <i>name</i>. Devotions to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Name_of_Jesus" title="Holy Name of Jesus">name of Jesus</a> go back to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">earliest days of Christianity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sylvester_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sylvester-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houlden_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houlden-21">[21]</a></sup> These exist today both in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Church" title="Western Church">Western Christianity</a>—both Catholic and Protestant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houlden_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Houlden-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christians predominantly profess that through Jesus' life, death, and resurrection, he restored humanity's communion with God with the blood of the <a href="/wiki/New_Covenant" title="New Covenant">New Covenant</a>. His death on a cross is understood as a redemptive sacrifice: the source of humanity's <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atonement_in_Christianity" title="Atonement in Christianity">atonement</a> for <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> which had entered human history through <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">the sin of Adam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christ.2C_Logos_and_Son_of_God">Christ, Logos and Son of God</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Christ, Logos and Son of God">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">Logos (Christianity)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sargis_Pitsak.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2530" data-file-width="1808" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sargis_Pitsak.jpg/130px-Sargis_Pitsak.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sargis_Pitsak.jpg/195px-Sargis_Pitsak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sargis_Pitsak.jpg/260px-Sargis_Pitsak.jpg 2x" width="130"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sargis_Pitsak.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
First page of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Mark</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Sargis_Pitsak" title="Sargis Pitsak">Sargis Pitsak</a> (14th century): "The beginning of the gospel of Jesus Christ, the Son of God".</div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>But who do you say that I am?</i> Only Simon Peter answered him: <i>You are the Christ, the Son of the living God</i> — <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#16:15" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 16:15-16</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Jesus is mediator, but…the title means <i>more</i> that someone <i>between</i> God and man. He is not just a third party between God and humanity…. As true God he brings God to mankind. As true man he brings mankind to God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stagg_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stagg-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Most Christians generally consider Jesus to be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a>, the long awaited <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a>, as well as the one and only <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a>. The opening words in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Gospel of Mark</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Mark#1:1" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Mark">1:1</a>), "The beginning of the gospel of Jesus Christ, the Son of God", provide Jesus with the two distinct attributions as Christ and as the Son of God. His divinity is again re-affirmed in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Mark#1:11" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Mark">Mark 1:11</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#1:1" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 1:1</a> which begins by calling Jesus the Christ and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#1:16" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">in verse 16</a> explains it again with the affirmation: "Jesus, who is called Christ".</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauline_Epistles" title="Pauline Epistles">Letters of Saint Paul</a>, the word "Christ" is so closely associated with Jesus that apparently for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Christians" title="Early Christians">early Christians</a> there was no need to claim that Jesus was Christ, for that was considered widely accepted among them. Hence Paul could use the term <i>Christos</i> with no confusion about who it referred to, and as in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/1_Corinthians#4:15" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/1 Corinthians">1 Corinthians 4:15</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Romans#12:5" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Romans">Romans 12:5</a> he could use expressions such as "in Christ" to refer to the followers of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, the title "Son of God" is applied to Jesus on many occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cathenc_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cathenc-28">[28]</a></sup> It is often used to refer to his divinity, from the beginning in the <a href="/wiki/Annunciation" title="Annunciation">Annunciation</a> up to the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">Crucifixion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cathenc_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cathenc-28">[28]</a></sup> The declaration that Jesus is the Son of God is made by many individuals in the New Testament, and on two separate occasions by <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">God the Father</a> as a voice from Heaven, and is asserted by Jesus himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cathenc_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cathenc-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OneTeacher_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-OneTeacher-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pentecost_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pentecost-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bromiley571_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bromiley571-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a>, the concept that <i>the Christ</i> is the <i><a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">Logos</a></i> (i.e., "The Word") has been important in establishing the doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Divinity" title="Divinity">divinity</a> of Christ and his position as <a href="/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">God the Son</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> as set forth in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_Creed" title="Chalcedonian Creed">Chalcedonian Creed</a>. This derives from the <a href="/wiki/John_1:1" title="John 1:1">opening of the Gospel of John</a>, commonly translated into English as: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." In the original Greek, <i><a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">Logos</a></i> (λόγος) is used for "Word," and in theological discourse, this is often left in its English transliterated form, "Logos".</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Pre-existence_of_Christ" title="Pre-existence of Christ">pre-existence of Christ</a></i> refers to the doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Person_of_Christ" title="Person of Christ">personal</a> existence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a> before his conception. One of the relevant <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> passages is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#1:1" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 1:1-18</a> where, in the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinitarian</a> view, Christ is identified with a pre-existent divine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(philosophy)" title="Hypostasis (philosophy)">hypostasis</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">Logos</a> or Word. This doctrine is reiterated in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#17:5" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 17:5</a> when Jesus refers to the glory which he had with the Father "before the world was" during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farewell_discourse" title="Farewell discourse">Farewell discourse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Endo_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Endo-32">[32]</a></sup> <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+17%3A24&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">John 17:24</a></span> also refers to the Father loving Jesus "before the foundation of the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Endo_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Endo-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">Non-Trinitarian</a> views doubt the aspect of personal pre-existence or the aspect of divinity, or both.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Age" title="Apostolic Age">Apostolic Age</a>, from the 2nd century forward, several controversies developed about how the human and divine are related within the person of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Editors1999_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Editors1999-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rausch2003_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rausch2003-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrath2007_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGrath2007-35">[35]</a></sup> Eventually in 451, the concept of a <i><a href="/wiki/Hypostatic_union" title="Hypostatic union">Hypostatic union</a></i> was decreed, namely that Jesus is both fully divine and fully human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Editors1999_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Editors1999-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rausch2003_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rausch2003-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, differences among Christian denominations continued thereafter. See the article on <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a> for details.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incarnation.2C_Nativity_and_Second_Adam">Incarnation, Nativity and Second Adam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Incarnation, Nativity and Second Adam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity)" title="Incarnation (Christianity)">Incarnation (Christianity)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Jesus" title="Nativity of Jesus">Nativity of Jesus</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Adam" title="Second Adam">Second Adam</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans,_The_Nativity_at_Night,_c_1490.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4225" data-file-width="3121" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans%2C_The_Nativity_at_Night%2C_c_1490.jpg/180px-Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans%2C_The_Nativity_at_Night%2C_c_1490.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans%2C_The_Nativity_at_Night%2C_c_1490.jpg/270px-Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans%2C_The_Nativity_at_Night%2C_c_1490.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans%2C_The_Nativity_at_Night%2C_c_1490.jpg/360px-Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans%2C_The_Nativity_at_Night%2C_c_1490.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans,_The_Nativity_at_Night,_c_1490.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Jesus" title="Nativity of Jesus">Nativity of Jesus</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Geertgen_tot_Sint_Jans" title="Geertgen tot Sint Jans">Geertgen tot Sint Jans</a>, c. 1490</div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation. For by him all things were created, in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible</i>. — <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Colossians#1:15" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Colossians">Colossians 1:15-16</a></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The above verse from Colossians regards the birth of Jesus as the model for all creation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_Paul" title="Apostle Paul">Apostle Paul</a> viewed the birth of Jesus as an event of cosmic significance which brought forth a "new man" who undid the damage caused by the fall of the first man, <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam</a>. Just as the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Johannine</a> view of Jesus as the incarnate <a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">Logos</a> proclaims the universal relevance of his birth, the Pauline perspective emphasizes the birth of a new man and a new world in the birth of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pannenberg_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pannenberg-5">[5]</a></sup> Paul's <a href="/wiki/Eschatology" title="Eschatology">eschatological</a> view of Jesus counter-positions him as a new man of morality and obedience, in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>. Unlike Adam, the new man born in Jesus obeys God and ushers in a world of morality and salvation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pannenberg_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pannenberg-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Pauline view, Adam is positioned as the first man and Jesus as the second: Adam, having corrupted himself by his disobedience, also infected humanity and left it with a curse as its inheritance. The birth of Jesus counterbalanced the fall of Adam, bringing forth redemption and repairing the damage done by Adam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daille_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daille-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2nd century Church Father <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a> writes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"When He became incarnate and was made man, He commenced afresh the long line of human beings, and furnished us, in a brief, comprehensive manner, with salvation; so that what we had lost in Adam—namely to be according to the image and likeness of God- that we might recover in Christ Jesus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bethune_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bethune-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williston_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williston-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patristic" title="Patristic">patristic</a> theology, Paul's contrasting of Jesus as the new man versus Adam provided a framework for discussing the uniqueness of the birth of Jesus and the ensuing events of his life. The Nativity of Jesus thus began to serve as the starting point for "cosmic Christology" in which the birth, life and Resurrection of Jesus have universal implications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pannenberg_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pannenberg-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The concept of Jesus as the "new man" repeats in the cycle of birth and rebirth of Jesus from his Nativity to his <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">Resurrection</a>: following his birth, through his morality and obedience to the Father, Jesus began a "new harmony" in the relationship between God the Father and man. The Nativity and Resurrection of Jesus thus created the author and exemplar of a new humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In this view, the birth, death and Resurrection of Jesus brought about salvation, undoing the damage of Adam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ministry">Ministry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ministry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">Ministry of Jesus</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/New_Testament_places_associated_with_Jesus" title="New Testament places associated with Jesus">New Testament places associated with Jesus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

The <i>Communion of the Apostles</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Signorelli" title="Luca Signorelli">Luca Signorelli</a>, 1512.</div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>The thief comes only in order to steal and kill and destroy. I came that they may have and enjoy life, and have it in abundance (to the full, till it overflows).</i>—John 10:10 (Ampl)</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Jesus seemed to have two basic concerns with reference to people and the material: (1) that they be freed from the tyranny of things and (2) that they be actively concerned for the needs of others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stagg_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stagg-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the Canonical gospels, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">Ministry of Jesus</a> begins with <a href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus" title="Baptism of Jesus">his Baptism</a> in the countryside of <a href="/wiki/Judea" title="Judea">Judea</a>, near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Jordan" title="River Jordan">River Jordan</a> and ends in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_in_Christianity" title="Jerusalem in Christianity">Jerusalem</a>, following the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alister16_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alister16-49">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Luke#3:23" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Luke">3:23</a>) states that Jesus was "about 30 years of age" at the start of his ministry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kostenberger140_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kostenberger140-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChronosPaul_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChronosPaul-51">[51]</a></sup> The date of the start of his ministry has been estimated at around AD 27-29 and the end in the range AD 30-36.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kostenberger140_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kostenberger140-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChronosPaul_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChronosPaul-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChronosPaul_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-ChronosPaul-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett19_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett19-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanders_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jesus' <i>Early Galilean ministry</i> begins when after his Baptism, he goes <a href="/wiki/Return_of_Jesus_to_Galilee" title="Return of Jesus to Galilee">back to Galilee</a> from his time in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temptation_of_Jesus" title="Temptation of Jesus">Judean desert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In this early period he preaches around Galilee and recruits <a href="/wiki/First_disciples_of_Jesus" title="First disciples of Jesus">his first disciples</a> who begin to travel with him and eventually form the core of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">early Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alister16_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alister16-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redford117_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Redford117-55">[55]</a></sup> The <i>Major Galilean ministry</i> which begins in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_8" title="Matthew 8">Matthew 8</a> includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commissioning_the_twelve_Apostles" title="Commissioning the twelve Apostles">Commissioning the twelve Apostles</a>, and covers most of the ministry of Jesus in Galilee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redford143_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Redford143-57">[57]</a></sup> The <i>Final Galilean ministry</i> begins after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_of_John_the_Baptist" title="Death of John the Baptist">death of John the Baptist</a> as Jesus prepares to go to Jerusalem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redford165_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Redford165-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i>Later Judean ministry</i> Jesus starts his final journey to Jerusalem through Judea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingsburyMark_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-KingsburyMark-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barton132_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barton132-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> As Jesus travels towards Jerusalem, in the <i>Later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perea_(Bible)" title="Perea (Bible)">Perean</a> ministry</i>, about one third the way down from the Sea of Galilee along the River Jordan, he returns to the area where he was baptized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Final ministry in Jerusalem</i> is sometimes called the <i>Passion Week</i> and begins with the Jesus' <a href="/wiki/Triumphal_entry_into_Jerusalem" title="Triumphal entry into Jerusalem">triumphal entry into Jerusalem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox155_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cox155-67">[67]</a></sup> The gospels provide more details about the final ministry than the other periods, devoting about one third of their text to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">last week of the life of Jesus in Jerusalem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner613_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner613-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teachings.2C_parables_and_miracles">Teachings, parables and miracles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Teachings, parables and miracles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Parables_of_Jesus" title="Parables of Jesus">Parables of Jesus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miracles_of_Jesus" title="Miracles of Jesus">Miracles of Jesus</a></div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>The words that I say unto you I speak not from myself: but the Father who dwells in me does his works.</i> — <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#14:10" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 14:10</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated98_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated98-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the New Testament the teachings of Jesus are presented in terms of his "words and works".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zuck100_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zuck100-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPent212_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPent212-19">[19]</a></sup> The words of Jesus include several sermons, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Parables_of_Jesus" title="Parables of Jesus">parables</a> that appear throughout the narrative of the Synoptic Gospels (the gospel of John includes no parables).</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="650" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg/120px-Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg/180px-Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg/240px-Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ostromir_Gospel_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a>, 1056</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The works include the <a href="/wiki/Miracles_of_Jesus" title="Miracles of Jesus">miracles</a> and other acts performed during his ministry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPent212_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-WPent212-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonical_Gospel" title="Canonical Gospel">Canonical Gospels</a> are the major source of the teachings of Jesus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauline_Epistles" title="Pauline Epistles">Pauline Epistles</a>, which were likely written decades before the gospels, provide some of the earliest written accounts of the teachings of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomb442_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blomb442-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The New Testament does not present the teachings of Jesus as merely his own teachings, but equates the words of Jesus with divine revelation, with <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> stating in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(NIV)/John#3:34" title="s:Bible (NIV)/John">John 3:34</a>: "For the one whom God has sent speaks the words of God, for God gives the Spirit without limit." and Jesus stating in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(NIV)/John#7:16" title="s:Bible (NIV)/John">John 7:16</a>: “My teaching is not my own. It comes from the one who sent me".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated98_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated98-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-And108_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-And108-71">[71]</a></sup> In <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#11:27" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 11:27</a> Jesus claims divine knowledge, stating: "No one knows the Son except the Father and no one knows the Father except the Son", asserting the mutual knowledge he has with the Father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bromiley571_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bromiley571-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discourses">Discourses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discourses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Five_Discourses_of_Matthew" title="Five Discourses of Matthew">Five Discourses of Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">Sermon on the Mount</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="744" data-file-width="850" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg/200px-Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg/300px-Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg/400px-Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christ_Taking_Leave_of_the_Apostles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jesus' <i><a href="/wiki/Farewell_Discourse" title="Farewell Discourse">Farewell Discourse</a></i> to his eleven remaining disciples after the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Maest%C3%A0_(Duccio)" title="Maestà (Duccio)">Maesta</a> by <a href="/wiki/Duccio" title="Duccio">Duccio</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The gospels include several discourses by Jesus on specific occasions, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farewell_discourse" title="Farewell discourse">Farewell discourse</a> delivered after the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper</a>, the night before his crucifixion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gail142_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gail142-73">[73]</a></sup> Although some of the teachings of Jesus are reported as taking place within the formal atmosphere of a synagogue (e.g., in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+4%3A23&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Matthew 4:23</a></span>) many of the discourses are more like conversations than formal lectures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howick7_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howick7-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> has a structured set of sermons, often grouped as the <a href="/wiki/Five_Discourses_of_Matthew" title="Five Discourses of Matthew">Five Discourses of Matthew</a> which present many of the key teachings of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cradle194_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cradle194-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keener37_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keener37-76">[76]</a></sup> Each of the five discourses has some parallel passages in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Gospel of Mark</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RTFrance_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-RTFrance-77">[77]</a></sup> The five discourses in Matthew begin with the <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">Sermon on the Mount</a>, which encapsulates many of the moral teaching of Jesus and which is one of the best known and most quoted elements of the New Testament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howick7_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Howick7-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-78">[78]</a></sup> The Sermon on the Mount includes the <i><a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a></i> which describe the character of the people of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_God" title="Kingdom of God">Kingdom of God</a>, expressed as "blessings".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The Beatitudes focus on love and humility rather than force and exaction and echo the key ideals of Jesus' teachings on spirituality and compassion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haste_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haste-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peace_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peace-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Synop_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Synop-82">[82]</a></sup> The other discourses in Matthew include the <i>Missionary Discourse</i> in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_10" title="Matthew 10">Matthew 10</a> and the <i>Discourse on the Church</i> in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_18" title="Matthew 18">Matthew 18</a>, providing instructions to the disciples and laying the foundation of the codes of conduct for the anticipated community of followers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toussaint_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toussaint-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parables">Parables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Parables_of_Jesus" title="Parables of Jesus">parables of Jesus</a> represent a major component of his teachings in the gospels, the approximately thirty parables forming about one third of his recorded teachings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lockyer174_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lockyer174-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JDPentecost10_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-JDPentecost10-87">[87]</a></sup> The parables may appear within longer sermons, as well as other places within the narrative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howick7_74-2"><a href="#cite_note-Howick7-74">[74]</a></sup> Jesus' parables are seemingly simple and memorable stories, often with imagery, and each conveys a teaching which usually relates the physical world to the <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a> world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lisco9-11_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lisco9-11-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, Lisco and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Fairbairn" title="Patrick Fairbairn">Fairbairn</a> stated that in the parables of Jesus, "the image borrowed from the visible world is accompanied by a truth from the invisible (spiritual) world" and that the parables of Jesus are not "mere similitudes which serve the purpose of illustration, but are internal analogies where nature becomes a witness for the spiritual world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lisco9-11_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lisco9-11-88">[88]</a></sup> Similarly, in the 20th century, calling a parable "an earthly story with a heavenly meaning",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barclay12_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barclay12-90">[90]</a></sup> William Barclay states that the parables of Jesus use familiar examples to lead others' minds towards heavenly concepts. He suggests that Jesus did not form his parables merely as analogies but based on an "inward affinity between the natural and the spiritual order."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barclay12_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barclay12-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miracles_of_Jesus">Miracles of Jesus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Miracles of Jesus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Believe the miracles, that you may know and understand that the Father is in me, and I in the Father</i>. —<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#10:38" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 10:38</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In Christian teachings, the <a href="/wiki/Miracles_of_Jesus" title="Miracles of Jesus">miracles of Jesus</a> were as much a vehicle for his message as were his words. Many of the miracles emphasize the importance of faith, for instance in <a href="/wiki/Cleansing_ten_lepers" title="Cleansing ten lepers">cleansing ten lepers</a>,<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Lk+17%3A19&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[Lk 17:19]</a></sup> Jesus did not say: "My power has saved you" but says "Rise and go; your faith has saved you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Similarly, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_walks_on_water" title="Jesus walks on water">Walking on Water</a> miracle, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_Peter" title="Apostle Peter">Apostle Peter</a> learns an important lesson about faith in that as his faith wavers, he begins to sink.<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mt+14%3A34-36&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[Mt 14:34-36]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>

<p>One characteristic shared among all miracles of Jesus in the Gospel accounts is that he delivered benefits freely and never requested or accepted any form of payment for his healing miracles, unlike some high priests of his time who charged those who were healed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomberg197_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blomberg197-95">[95]</a></sup> In <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(NIV)/Matthew#10:8" title="s:Bible (NIV)/Matthew">Matthew 10:8</a> he advised his disciples to heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse those who have leprosy, and drive out demons without payment and stated: "Freely you have received; freely give".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomberg197_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blomberg197-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christians in general believe that Jesus' miracles were actual historical events and that his miraculous works were an important part of his life, attesting to his divinity and the <a href="/wiki/Hypostatic_union" title="Hypostatic union">Hypostatic union</a>, i.e., the dual natures of Christ's humanity and divinity in one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(philosophy)" title="Hypostasis (philosophy)">hypostasis.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catholic_Encyclopedia_on_Miracles_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic_Encyclopedia_on_Miracles-96">[96]</a></sup> Christians believe that while Jesus' experiences of hunger, weariness, and death were evidences of his humanity, the miracles were evidences of his deity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christian authors also view the miracles of Jesus not merely as acts of power and omnipotence, but as works of love and mercy: they were performed to show compassion for sinful and suffering humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catholic_Encyclopedia_on_Miracles_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic_Encyclopedia_on_Miracles-96">[96]</a></sup> Authors Ken and Jim Stocker state that "every single miracle Jesus performed was an act of love".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> And each miracle involves specific teachings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since according to the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a><sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Jn+20%3A30&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[20:30]</a></sup> it was impossible to narrate all the miracles performed by Jesus, the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a> states that the miracles presented in the Gospels were selected for a twofold reason: first for the manifestation of God's glory, and then for their evidential value. Jesus referred to his "works" as evidences of his mission and his divinity, and in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+5%3A36&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">John 5:36</a></span> he declared that his miracles have greater evidential value than the testimony of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catholic_Encyclopedia_on_Miracles_96-2"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic_Encyclopedia_on_Miracles-96">[96]</a></sup></p>

<p>The accounts of the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">crucifixion</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">resurrection of Jesus</a> provide a rich background for <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christological</a> analysis, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonical_gospels" title="Canonical gospels">canonical gospels</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Pauline_epistles" title="Pauline epistles">Pauline epistles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johannine" title="Johannine">Johannine</a> "agency christology" combines the concept that Jesus is the Son of his Father with the idea that he has come into the world as his Father's agent, commissioned and sent by the Father to represent the Father and to accomplish his Father's work. Implied in each Synoptic portrayal of Jesus is the doctrine that the salvation Jesus gives is inseparable from Jesus himself and his divine identity. Sonship and agency come together in the Synoptic gospels only in the Parable of the Vineyard (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+21%3A37&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Matthew 21:37</a></span>; <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+12%3A6&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Mark 12:6</a></span>; <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+20%3A13&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Luke 20:13</a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> The submission of Jesus to crucifixion is a sacrifice made as an <i>agent of God</i> or <i>servant of God</i>, for the sake of eventual victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cullmann_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cullmann-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> This builds upon the <a href="/wiki/Salvation_in_Christianity" title="Salvation in Christianity">salvific</a> theme of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a> which begins in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#1:36" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 1:36</a> with <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>'s proclamation: "The Lamb of God who takes away the sins of the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel371_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel371-107">[107]</a></sup> Further reinforcement of the concept is provided in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Revelation#21:14" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Revelation">Revelation 21:14</a> where the "<a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God" title="Lamb of God">lamb slain but standing</a>" is the only one worthy of handling the scroll (i.e., the book) containing the names of those who are to be saved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeHaan_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeHaan-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>A central element in the Christology presented in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a> is the affirmation of the belief that the death of Jesus by crucifixion happened "with the foreknowledge of God, according to a definite plan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matera67_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matera67-109">[109]</a></sup> In this view, as in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Acts#2:23" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Acts">Acts 2:23</a>, the cross is not viewed as a scandal, for the crucifixion of Jesus "at the hands of the lawless" is viewed as the fulfilment of the plan of God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matera67_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matera67-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>Paul's Christology has a specific focus on the death and resurrection of Jesus. For Paul, the crucifixion of Jesus is directly related to his resurrection and the term "the cross of Christ" used in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Galatians#6:12" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Galatians">Galatians 6:12</a> may be viewed as his abbreviation of the message of the gospels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarz132_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarz132-111">[111]</a></sup> For Paul, the crucifixion of Jesus was not an isolated event in history, but a cosmic event with significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eschatological" title="Eschatological">eschatological</a> consequences, as in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/1_Corinthians#2:8" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/1 Corinthians">1 Corinthians 2:8</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarz132_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarz132-111">[111]</a></sup> In the Pauline view, Jesus, obedient to the point of death (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Philippians#2:8" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Philippians">Philippians 2:8</a>) died "at the right time" (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Romans#4:25" title="s:Bible (American Standard)/Romans">Romans 4:25</a>) based on the plan of God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarz132_111-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarz132-111">[111]</a></sup> For Paul the "power of the cross" is not separable from the Resurrection of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarz132_111-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarz132-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> supported the "agent of God" Christology and argued that in his trial in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilate%27s_Court" title="Pilate's Court">Pilate's Court</a> Jesus could have successfully argued for his innocence, but instead submitted to crucifixion in obedience to the Father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CalvinC_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-CalvinC-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> This Christological theme continued into the 20th century, both in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Church" title="Eastern Church">Eastern</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Church" title="Western Church">Western Churches</a>. In the Eastern Church <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Bulgakov" title="Sergei Bulgakov">Sergei Bulgakov</a> argued that the crucifixion of Jesus was "<a href="/wiki/Pre-existence_of_Christ" title="Pre-existence of Christ">pre-eternally</a>" determined by the Father before the creation of the world, to redeem humanity from the disgrace caused by the fall of Adam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SBulgakov_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-SBulgakov-114">[114]</a></sup> In the Western Church, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Rahner" title="Karl Rahner">Karl Rahner</a> elaborated on the analogy that the blood of the <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God" title="Lamb of God">Lamb of God</a> (and the water from the side of Jesus) shed at the crucifixion had a cleansing nature, similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptism_in_Christianity" title="Baptism in Christianity">baptismal water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resurrection.2C_Ascension_and_Second_Coming">Resurrection, Ascension and Second Coming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus_in_Christianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Resurrection, Ascension and Second Coming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">Resurrection of Jesus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">Ascension of Jesus</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_coming_of_Christ" title="Second coming of Christ">Second coming of Christ</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> teaches that the resurrection of Jesus is a foundation of the Christian faith.<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1co+15%3A12-20&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[1 Cor 15:12-20]</a></sup> <sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1Peter+1%3A3&amp;version=HCSB" rel="nofollow">[1 Pet 1:3]</a></sup> Christians, through faith in the working of God<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Colossians+2%3A12&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[Col 2:12]</a></sup> are spiritually resurrected with Jesus, and are <a href="/wiki/Redemption_(theology)" title="Redemption (theology)">redeemed</a> so that they may walk in a new way of life.<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom+6%3A4&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[Rom 6:4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Age" title="Apostolic Age">apostolic Church</a>, the resurrection was seen as heralding a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_to_Come" title="World to Come">new era</a>. Forming a theology of the resurrection fell to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_Paul" title="Apostle Paul">Apostle Paul</a>. It was not enough for Paul to simply repeat elementary teachings, but as <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Hebrews+6%3A1&amp;version=NIV" rel="nofollow">Hebrews 6:1</a></span> states, "go beyond the initial teachings about Christ and advance to maturity". Fundamental to Pauline theology is the connection between Christ's Resurrection and <a href="/wiki/Redemption_(theology)" title="Redemption (theology)">redemption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Paul explained the importance of the resurrection of Jesus as the cause and basis of the hope of Christians to share a similar experience in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1co+15%3A20-22&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">1 Corinthians 15:20-22</a></span>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>But Christ has indeed been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep. For since death came through a man, the resurrection of the dead comes also through a man. For as in Adam all die, so in Christ all will be made alive.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>If the cross stands at the center of Paul's theology, so does the Resurrection: unless the one died the death of <i>all</i>, the <i>all</i> would have little to celebrate in the resurrection of the one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> Paul taught that, just as Christians share in Jesus' death in baptism, so they will share in his resurrection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrman_2006_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrman_2006-118">[118]</a></sup> for Jesus was designated the <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a> by his resurrection.<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom.+1%3A4&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[Rom. 1:4]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrman_2006_118-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrman_2006-118">[118]</a></sup> Paul's views went against the thoughts of the Greek philosophers to whom a bodily resurrection meant a new imprisonment in a corporeal body, which was what they wanted to avoid, given that for them the corporeal and the material fettered the spirit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> At the same time, Paul believed that the newly resurrected body would be a heavenly body—immortal, glorified, powerful and pneumatic, in contrast to an earthly body which is mortal, dishonored, weak and psychic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dale Martin contends that Paul's various disagreements with the Corinthians were the result of a fundamental conflict over the ideological construction of the human body (and hence the church as the body of Christ). According to Martin, most Corinthian Christians and Paul himself saw the body as an entity that could be permeated by different pollutions. Other members of the Corinthian church, however, viewed the body as hierarchical—as a microcosm of the universe—and were not particularly concerned about body boundaries. These differing views of the human body (and also of the church as the body of Christ) led to differing opinions on a variety of subjects—including the resurrection of the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> According to theologian <a href="/wiki/Peter_Carnley" title="Peter Carnley">Peter Carnley</a>, the resurrection of Jesus was different from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Lazarus" title="Resurrection of Lazarus">Resurrection of Lazarus</a> as: "In the case of Lazarus, the stone was rolled away so that he could walk out.... the raised Christ didn't have to have the stone rolled away, because he is transformed and can appear anywhere, at any time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Fathers" title="Apostolic Fathers">Apostolic Fathers</a>, discussed the death and resurrection of Jesus, including <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_of_Antioch" title="Ignatius of Antioch">Ignatius</a> (50−115),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Polycarp" title="Polycarp">Polycarp</a> (69−155), and <a href="/wiki/Justin_Martyr" title="Justin Martyr">Justin Martyr</a> (100−165). Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I_and_Christianity" title="Constantine I and Christianity">conversion of Constantine</a> and the liberating <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">Edict of Milan</a> in 313, the <a href="/wiki/First_seven_Ecumenical_Councils" title="First seven Ecumenical Councils">ecumenical councils</a> of the 4th, 5th and 6th centuries, that focused on <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a> helped shape the Christian understanding of the redemptive nature of Resurrection, and influenced both the development of its iconography, and its use within Liturgy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup></p>
