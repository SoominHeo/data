<p>A <b>Christian denomination</b> is a distinct <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> body within <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, identified by traits such as a name, organisation, leadership and doctrine. Individual bodies, however, may use alternative terms to describe themselves, such as <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Church" title="Church">church</a> or sometimes <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fellowship" title="Fellowship">fellowship</a>. Divisions between one group and another are defined by authority and doctrine; issues such as the <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">nature of Jesus</a>, the authority of <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">apostolic succession</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_eschatology" title="Christian eschatology">eschatology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Papal_primacy" title="Papal primacy">papal primacy</a> may separate one denomination from another. Groups of denominations — often sharing broadly similar beliefs, practices, and historical ties — are sometimes known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branches_of_Christianity" title="Branches of Christianity">branches of Christianity</a>" or "denominational families".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_Christian_denominations" title="List of Christian denominations">Individual Christian groups</a> vary widely in the degree to which they recognize one another. Several groups claim to be the direct and <a href="/wiki/One_true_church" title="One true church">sole authentic successor</a> of the church founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_1st_century" title="Christianity in the 1st century">1st century AD</a>. Others, however, believe in denominationalism, where some or all Christian groups are legitimate churches of the same religion regardless of their distinguishing labels, beliefs, and practices. Because of this concept, some Christian bodies reject the term "denomination" to describe themselves, to avoid implying equivalency with other churches or denominations.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, the largest Christian denomination with roughly 1.2 billion members—slightly over half of all Christians worldwide—is the world's second largest <a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">religious denomination</a> after <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, the Catholic Church does not view itself as a denomination, but as the original pre-denominational church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olsen1999_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-olsen1999-2">[2]</a></sup> This view is rejected by other Christian denominations. <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant denominations</a> account for approximately 37 percent of Christians worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pewforum1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pewforum1-3">[3]</a></sup> Together, Catholicism and Protestantism (including <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>, and other denominations sharing historical ties) comprise <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>. Western Christian denominations prevail in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, with an estimated 225–300 million adherents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is the second-largest Christian organization in the world and also considers itself the original pre-denominational church. Unlike the Catholic Church, the Eastern Orthodox Church is itself a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communion_(Christianity)" title="Communion (Christianity)">communion</a> of fully independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autocephalous" title="Autocephalous">autocephalous</a> churches (or "jurisdictions") that mutually recognize each other to the exclusion of others. The Eastern Orthodox Church, together with <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a>, constitutes <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>. (There are also some smaller groups of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholics" title="Eastern Catholics">Eastern Catholics</a> which are in communion with the Latin Rite but have cultural and historical ties with other Eastern Christians.) Eastern Christian denominations are represented mostly in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">North Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Africa" title="Northeast Africa">Northeast Africa</a>.</p>
<p>Christians have various doctrines about the Church (the body of the faithful that they believe Jesus Christ established) and about how the divine church corresponds to Christian denominations. Both Catholics and Eastern Orthodox hold that their own organizations faithfully represent the <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Marks_of_the_Church" title="Four Marks of the Church">One Holy catholic and Apostolic Church</a></i> to the exclusion of the other. Sixteenth-century Protestants separated from the Catholic Church because of <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theologies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">practices</a> that they considered to be in violation of their own interpretation. Generally, members of the various denominations acknowledge each other as Christians, at least to the extent that they have mutually recognized <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptisms</a> and acknowledge historically <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodox</a> views including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divinity_of_Jesus" title="Divinity of Jesus">Divinity of Jesus</a> and doctrines of <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_sin" title="Christian views on sin">sin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salvation_in_Christianity" title="Salvation in Christianity">salvation</a>, even though doctrinal and <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiology" title="Ecclesiology">ecclesiological</a> obstacles hinder <a href="/wiki/Ecumenism" title="Ecumenism">full communion between churches</a>.</p>
<p>Since the reforms surrounding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_II" title="Vatican II">Vatican II</a> of 1962-1965, the Catholic Church has referred to Protestant communities as "denominations", while reserving the term "church" for <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_see" title="Apostolic see">apostolic churches</a>, including the Eastern Orthodox (see <i><a href="/wiki/Subsistit_in" title="Subsistit in">subsistit in</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Branch_theory" title="Branch theory">branch theory</a>). But some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-denominational_Christianity" title="Non-denominational Christianity">non-denominational Christians</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> do not follow any particular branch, though sometimes regarded as Protestants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (May 2016)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church#Related_concepts" title="Christian Church">Christian Church § Related concepts</a></div>
<p>Each group uses different terminology to discuss their beliefs. This section will discuss the definitions of several terms used throughout the article, before discussing the beliefs themselves in detail in following sections.</p>
<p>A denomination within Christianity can be defined as a "recognized autonomous branch of the Christian Church"; major synonyms include "religious group, sect, Church," etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[notes 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> "Church" as a synonym, refers to a "particular Christian organization with its own clergy, buildings, and distinctive doctrines";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> "church" can also more broadly be defined as the entire body of Christians, the "<a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Christian Church</a>".</p>
<p>Some traditional and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelical" title="Evangelical">evangelical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestants" title="Protestants">Protestants</a> draw a distinction between membership in the universal church and fellowship within the local church. Becoming a believer in Christ makes one a member of the universal church; one then may join a fellowship of other local believers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> Some evangelical groups describe themselves as interdenominational fellowships, partnering with local churches to strengthen evangelical efforts, usually targeting a particular group with specialized needs, such as students or ethnic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> A related concept is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denominationalism" title="Denominationalism">denominationalism</a>, the belief that some or all Christian groups are legitimate churches of the same religion regardless of their distinguishing labels, beliefs, and practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-11">[10]</a></sup> (Conversely, "denominationalism" can also refer to "emphasizing of denominational differences to the point of being narrowly exclusive", similar to <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">sectarianism</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Protestant leaders differ greatly from the views of the leaders of the Roman Catholic Church and the Eastern Orthodox Church, the two largest Christian denominations. Each church makes mutually exclusive claims for itself to be the direct continuation of the Church founded by Jesus Christ, from whom other denominations later broke away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olsen1999_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-olsen1999-2">[2]</a></sup> These churches, and a few others, reject denominationalism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_branches">Major branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major branches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> can be taxonomically divided into five main groups: the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>. The latter is a broad umbrella term including many groups which do not share any ecclesiastical governance and have widely diverging beliefs and practices.</p>
<p>Christianity has denominational families (or movements) and also has individual denominations (or communions). The difference between a denomination and a denominational family is sometimes unclear to outsiders. Some denominational families can be considered major branches. Groups that are members of a branch, while sharing historical ties and similar doctrines, are not necessarily in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communion_(Christianity)" title="Communion (Christianity)">communion</a> with one another.</p>
<p>There were some movements considered heresies by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_church" title="Early church">early church</a> which do not exist today and are not generally referred to as denominations. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnostics</a> (who had believed in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esotericism" title="Esotericism">esoteric</a> <a href="/wiki/Dualism" title="Dualism">dualism</a> called <a href="/wiki/Gnosis" title="Gnosis">gnosis</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebionite" title="Ebionite">Ebionites</a> (who denied the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divinity_of_Jesus" title="Divinity of Jesus">divinity of Jesus</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arians</a> (who subordinated the <a href="/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">Son</a> to the <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">Father</a> by denying the <a href="/wiki/Pre-existence_of_Christ" title="Pre-existence of Christ">pre-existence of Christ</a>, thus placing <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> as a created being), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bogumilism" title="Bogumilism">Bogumilism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_Church" title="Bosnian Church">Bosnian Church</a>. The greatest divisions in Christianity today, however, are between the Eastern and Oriental Orthodox, Catholicism, and the various denominations formed during and after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. There also exists a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-Trinitarian" title="Non-Trinitarian">non-Trinitarian</a> groups. There also exist some non-traditional groups that the majority of other Christians view as <a href="/wiki/Apostasy" title="Apostasy">apostate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretical</a>, and not as legitimate versions of Christianity.</p>
<p>Comparisons between denominational churches must be approached with caution. For example, in some churches, congregations are part of a larger church organization, while in other groups, each congregation is an independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_entity" title="Autonomous entity">autonomous</a> organization. This issue is further complicated by the existence of groups of congregations with a common heritage that are officially nondenominational and have no centralized authority or records, but which are identified as denominations by non-adherents. Study of such churches in denominational terms is therefore a more complex proposition.</p>
<p>Some groups count membership based on adult believers and <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptized</a> children of believers, while others only count adult baptized believers. Others may count membership based on those adult believers who have formally affiliated themselves with the congregation. In addition, there may be political motives of advocates or opponents of a particular group to inflate or deflate membership numbers through <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> or outright deception.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denominationalism">Denominationalism <span id="denominationalism"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Denominationalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Denominationalism is the belief that some or all Christian groups are legitimate churches of the same religion regardless of their distinguishing labels, beliefs, and practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-11">[10]</a></sup> The idea was first articulated by <a href="/wiki/Independent_(religion)" title="Independent (religion)">Independents</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> movement. They argued that differences among Christians were inevitable, but that separation based on these differences was not necessarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a>. Christians are obligated to practice their beliefs rather than remain within a church with which they disagree, but they must also recognize their imperfect knowledge and not condemn other Christians as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostate" title="Apostate">apostate</a> over unimportant matters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Christians view denominationalism as a regrettable fact. As of 2011, divisions are becoming less sharp, and there is increasing cooperation between denominations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">Theological</a> denominationalism ultimately denies reality to any apparent doctrinal differences among the "denominations", reducing all differences to mere matters <i>de nomina</i> ("of names").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A denomination in this sense is created when part of a church no longer feel they can accept the leadership of that church as a spiritual leadership due to a different view of doctrine or what they see as immoral behaviour, but the schism does not in any way reflect either group leaving the Church as a theoretical whole.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This particular doctrine is rejected by <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>. In these churches, it is not possible to have a separation over doctrinal or leadership issues, and any such attempts automatically are a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a>. Some Protestant groups reject denominationalism as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_schisms_and_divisions">Historical schisms and divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical schisms and divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Christianity has not been a monolithic faith since the <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_1st_century" title="Christianity in the 1st century">first century</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Age" title="Apostolic Age">Apostolic Age</a>, if ever, and today there exist a large variety of groups that share a common history and tradition within and without <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a> Christianity. Christianity is the largest religion in the world (making up approximately one-third of the population) and the various divisions have commonalities and differences in tradition, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiology" title="Ecclesiology">church government</a>, doctrine, and language.</p>
<p>The largest <a href="/wiki/Schism" title="Schism">schism</a> or division in many classification schemes is between the families of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>. After these two larger families come distinct branches of Christianity. Most classification schemes list six (in order of size: Roman <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>, and the Church of the East, which was originally referred to as <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorianism</a> but in modern times is embodied by the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a>).</p>
<p>Unlike Roman Catholicism, Protestantism is a general movement that has no universal governing authority. As such, diverse groups such as <a href="/wiki/Adventism" title="Adventism">Adventists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptists" title="Anabaptists">Anabaptists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binitarianism" title="Binitarianism">Binitarians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_Christianity" title="Charismatic Christianity">Charismatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregationalists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelical_Christianity" title="Evangelical Christianity">Evangelicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holiness_movement" title="Holiness movement">Holiness churches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Moravians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformed_churches" title="Reformed churches">Reformed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarians</a> (depending on one's classification scheme) are all a part of the same family but have distinct doctrinal variations within each group -- <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutherans" title="Lutherans">Lutherans</a> see themselves not to be a part of the rest of what they call "Reformed Protestantism" due to radical differences in sacramental theology and historical approach to the Reformation itself (both Reformed and Lutherans see their reformation in the sixteenth century to be a 'reforming' of the Catholic Church, not a rejection of it entirely). From these come denominations, which in the West, have independence from the others in their doctrine.</p>
<p>The Eastern and Roman Catholic churches, due to their <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchical</a> structures, are not said to be made up of denominations, rather, they include kinds of regional councils and individual congregations and church bodies, which do not officially differ from one another in doctrine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The initial differences between the East and West traditions stem from socio-cultural and ethno-linguistic divisions in and between the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empires</a>. Since the West (that is, Western Europe) spoke <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as its <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> and the East (Eastern Europe, the Middle East, Asia, and northern Africa) largely used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a> to transmit writings, theological developments were difficult to translate from one branch to the other. In the course of <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">ecumenical councils</a> (large gatherings of Christian leaders), some church bodies split from the larger family of Christianity. Many earlier <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretical</a> groups either died off for lack of followers and/or suppression by the church at large (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollinarianism" title="Apollinarianism">Apollinarians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montanism" title="Montanism">Montanists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ebionites" title="Ebionites">Ebionites</a>).</p>
<p>The first significant, lasting split in historic Christianity came from the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>, who left following the <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christological</a> controversy over <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorianism</a> in 431 (the Assyrians in 1994 released a common Christological statement with the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>). Today, the Assyrian and Roman Catholic Church view this schism as largely linguistic, due to problems of translating very delicate and precise terminology from Latin to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> and vice versa (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Ephesus" title="First Council of Ephesus">Council of Ephesus</a>).</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> in 451, the next large split came with the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Syriac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic</a> churches dividing themselves, with the dissenting churches becoming today's <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>. In modern times, there have also been moves towards healing this split, with common Christological statements being made between <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> and Syriac patriarch <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_Zakka_I_Iwas" title="Ignatius Zakka I Iwas">Ignatius Zakka I Iwas</a>, as well as between representatives of both Eastern and Oriental Orthodoxy.</p>
<p>There has been a claim that the Chalcedonian Creed restored Nestorianism, however this is refuted by maintaining the following distinctions associated with the <i>person</i> of Christ: two hypostases, two natures (Nestorian); one hypostasis, one nature (Monophysite); one hypostasis, two natures (Orthodox/Catholic).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East-West_Schism" title="East-West Schism">East-West Schism</a></div>
<p>In Western Christianity, there were a handful of geographically isolated movements that preceded the spirit of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathars" title="Cathars">Cathars</a> were a very strong movement in medieval southwestern France, but did not survive into modern times. In northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and southeastern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Waldo" title="Peter Waldo">Peter Waldo</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Waldensians" title="Waldensians">Waldensians</a> in the 12th century. This movement has largely been absorbed by modern-day Protestant groups. In <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, a movement in the early 15th century by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a> called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hussite" title="Hussite">Hussites</a> defied Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a> and still exists to this day (alternately known as the <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Moravian Church</a>).</p>
<p>Although the church as a whole did not experience any major divisions for centuries afterward, the Eastern and Western groups drifted until the point where patriarchs from both families <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunicated</a> one another in about 1054 in what is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East-West_Schism" title="East-West Schism">Great Schism</a>. The political and theological reasons for the schism are complex, but one major controversy was the inclusion and acceptance in the West of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filioque_clause" title="Filioque clause">filioque clause</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a>, which the East viewed as erroneous. Another was the definition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primacy_of_the_Roman_Pontiff" title="Primacy of the Roman Pontiff">papal primacy</a>.</p>
<p>Both West and East agreed that the patriarch of Rome was owed a "primacy of honour" by the other patriarchs (those of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_in_Christianity" title="Jerusalem in Christianity">Jerusalem</a>), but the West also contended that this primacy extended to jurisdiction, a position rejected by the Eastern patriarchs. Various attempts at dialogue between the two groups would occur, but it was only in the 1960s, under Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_VI" title="Paul VI">Paul VI</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Athenagoras" title="Patriarch Athenagoras">Patriarch Athenagoras</a>, that significant steps began to be made to mend the relationship between the two.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_Reformation_.2816th_century.29">Protestant Reformation (16th century)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Protestant Reformation (16th century)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a></div>
<p>The Protestant Reformation began with the posting of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ninety-Five_Theses" title="The Ninety-Five Theses">Ninety-Five Theses</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> on October 31, 1517, written as a set of grievances to reform the pre-Reformation Western Church. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_(resources)" title="Martin Luther (resources)">Luther's writings</a>, combined with the work of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> theologian <a href="/wiki/Huldrych_Zwingli" title="Huldrych Zwingli">Huldrych Zwingli</a> and French theologian and politician <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> sought to reform existing problems in doctrine and practice. Due to the reactions of ecclesiastical office holders at the time of the reformers, the Roman Catholic Church separated from them, instigating a rift in <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a> declared himself to be supreme head of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Act_of_Supremacy" title="Act of Supremacy">Act of Supremacy</a> in 1531, founding the Church of England, repressing both Lutheran reformers and those loyal to the pope. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cranmer" title="Thomas Cranmer">Thomas Cranmer</a> as <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> introduced the Reformation, in a form compromising between the Calvinists and Lutherans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_and_Liberal_Catholic_Churches_.2819th-20th_centuries.29">Old and Liberal Catholic Churches (19th-20th centuries)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Old and Liberal Catholic Churches (19th-20th centuries)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholic Church</a> split from the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> in the 1870s because of the promulgation of the <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Infallibility" title="Papal Infallibility">Papal Infallibility</a> as promoted by the <a href="/wiki/First_Vatican_Council" title="First Vatican Council">First Vatican Council</a> of 1869–1870. The term 'Old Catholic' was first used in 1853 to describe the members of the See of Utrecht that were not under Papal authority. The Old Catholic movement grew in America but has not maintained ties with Utrecht, although talks are under way between independent Old Catholic bishops and Utrecht.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Catholic_Church" title="Liberal Catholic Church">Liberal Catholic Church</a> started in 1916 via an Old Catholic bishop in London, bishop Matthew, who consecrated bishop James Wedgwood to the Episcopacy. This stream has in its relatively short existence known many splits, which operate worldwide under several names.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_churches">Eastern churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Eastern churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Eastern world, the largest body of believers in modern times is the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, sometimes imprecisely called "Greek Orthodox" because from the time of Christ through the Byzantine empire, Greek was its common language. However, the term "<a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox</a>" actually refers to only one portion of the entire Eastern Orthodox Church. The Eastern Orthodox Church believes itself to be the continuation of the original Christian Church established by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" title="Twelve Apostles">Apostles</a>. The Eastern Orthodox and the Roman Catholics have been separated since the 11th century, following the <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Schism" title="East–West Schism">East–West Schism</a>, with each of them claiming to represent the original pre-schism Church.</p>
<p>The Eastern Orthodox consider themselves to be spiritually one body, which is administratively grouped into several <a href="/wiki/Autocephaly" title="Autocephaly">autocephalous</a> jurisdictions (also commonly referred to as "Churches", despite being parts of one Church). They do not recognize any single bishop as universal church leader, but rather each bishop governs only his own <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch of Constantinople">Patriarch of Constantinople</a> is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch" title="Ecumenical Patriarch">Ecumenical Patriarch</a>, and holds the title "<a href="/wiki/Primus_inter_pares" title="Primus inter pares">first among equals</a>", meaning only that if a great council is called, the Patriarch sits as president of the council. He has no more power than any other bishop. Currently, the largest <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> with the most members is the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a>. Others include the ancient Patriarchates of <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Greek Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church_of_Antioch" title="Greek Orthodox Church of Antioch">Antioch</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Patriarchate_of_Jerusalem" title="Greek Orthodox Patriarchate of Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Orthodox_Church" title="Georgian Orthodox Church">Georgian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Orthodox_Church" title="Romanian Orthodox Church">Romanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Orthodox_Church" title="Bulgarian Orthodox Church">Bulgarian</a> Orthodox Churches, and several smaller ones.</p>
<p>The second largest Eastern Christian communion is <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>, which is organized in a similar manner, with six national autocephalous groups and two autonomous bodies, although there are greater internal differences than among the Eastern Orthodox (especially in the diversity of <a href="/wiki/Rite" title="Rite">rites</a> being used). The six autocephalous Oriental Orthodox Churches are the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic</a> (Egyptian), <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Syriac</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenian_Orthodox_Church" title="Armenian Orthodox Church">Armenian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malankara_Orthodox_Syrian_Church" title="Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church">Malankara</a> (Indian), <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Ethiopian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrean_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Eritrean</a> Orthodox Churches. In the Aramaic-speaking areas of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, the Syriac Orthodox Church has long been dominant. Although the region of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a> has had a strong body of believers since the infancy of Christianity, these regions only gained autocephaly in 1963 and 1994 respectively. The Oriental Orthodox are distinguished from the Eastern Orthodox by doctrinal differences concerning the union of human and divine natures in the person of Jesus Christ, and the two communions separated as a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> in the year 451, although there have been recent moves towards reconciliation.</p>
<p>Since these groups are relatively obscure in the West, literature on them has sometimes included the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">Assyrian Church of the East</a> as a part of the Oriental Orthodox Communion, but the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>, after adopting Christianity in the 1st century AD, have maintained theological, cultural, and ecclesiastical independence from all other Christian bodies since 431.</p>
<p>The Assyrian Church therefore represents a third Eastern Christian communion in its own right. It is administered in a hierarchical model not entirely unlike the Catholic Church, with the head of the church being the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Patriarchs_of_the_Church_of_the_East" title="List of Patriarchs of the Church of the East">Patriarch Catholicos of the Assyrian Church of the East</a>, since 1976 HH <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mar_Dinkha_IV" title="Mar Dinkha IV">Mar Dinkha IV</a>. Due to oppression, the church's headquarters is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>, rather than the ancient <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_homeland" title="Assyrian homeland">Assyrian homelands</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, northeast <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, southeast <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, though the core of believers remain there. Even within the Assyrian Church, there were two splits, with a number of Assyrians breaking away in 1552 and later forming the <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Church" title="Chaldean Catholic Church">Chaldean Catholic Church</a>, and in the 1960s another group forming the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Church_of_the_East" title="Ancient Church of the East">Ancient Church of the East</a>, with a rival <a href="/wiki/Catholicos" title="Catholicos">Catholicos</a> (Patriarch) in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.</p>
<p>There are also the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, which are counterparts of the various Churches listed above, in that they preserve the same theological and liturgical traditions as they do. But they differ from their Orthodox mother Churches (and <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>) in that they recognize the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Bishop of Rome</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primacy_of_the_Roman_Pontiff" title="Primacy of the Roman Pontiff">universal head of the Church</a>. Though adherents of Eastern Catholicism are fully part of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic communion</a>, most do not to use the term "<a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_(term)" title="Roman Catholic (term)">Roman Catholic</a>" to describe themselves, associating that name instead with members of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> Rather, they prefer to use the name of whichever Church they belong to—Ukrainian Catholic, Coptic Catholic, Assyrian-Chaldean Catholic, etc.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin portion</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, comprise the three major divisions of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> in the Western world. Roman Catholics do not describe themselves as a denomination but rather as the original Holy and Universal Church; which all other branches broke off from in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religious)" title="Schism (religious)">schism</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> churches are generally considered to be Protestant denominations, although strictly speaking, of these three, only the Lutherans took part in the official <a href="/wiki/Protestation_at_Speyer" title="Protestation at Speyer">Protestation at Speyer</a> after the decree of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Diet_of_Speyer" title="Second Diet of Speyer">Second Diet of Speyer</a> mandated the burning of Luther's works and the end of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.</p>
<p>Anglicanism was generally classified as Protestant, but since the "Tractarian" or <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Movement" title="Oxford Movement">Oxford Movement</a> of the 19th century, led by <a href="/wiki/John_Henry_Newman" title="John Henry Newman">John Henry Newman</a>, Anglican writers emphasize a more catholic understanding of the church and characterize it as more properly understood as its own tradition—a <i>via media</i> ("middle way"), <i>both</i> Protestant and Catholic. The American province of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_USA" title="Episcopal Church USA">Episcopal Church USA</a>, describes itself as a modern <i>via media</i> church in this tradition. A case is sometimes also made to regard Lutheranism in a similar way, considering the catholic character of its foundational documents (the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a> and other documents contained in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Concord" title="Book of Concord">Book of Concord</a>) and its existence prior to the Anglican, Anabaptist, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformed" title="Reformed">Reformed</a> churches, from which nearly all other Protestant denominations derive.</p>
<p>One central tenet of Catholicism (which is a common point between Roman Catholic, Anglican, Eastern Orthodox, and some other Churches), is its practice of <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">apostolic succession</a>. "Apostle" means "one who is sent out". Jesus commissioned the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_apostles" title="Twelve apostles">twelve apostles</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical_Figures" title="Biblical Figures">Biblical Figures</a> for the list of the Twelve), and they, in turn laid hands on subsequent church leaders to ordain (commission) them for ministry. In this manner, Roman Catholics and Anglicans trace their ordained ministers all the way back to the original Twelve.</p>
<p>Roman Catholics believe that the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> has authority which can be traced directly to the apostle <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter</a> whom they hold to be the original head of and first Pope of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Church</a>. There are smaller churches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholic Church</a> which rejected the definition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Infallibility" title="Papal Infallibility">Papal Infallibility</a> at the <a href="/wiki/First_Vatican_Council" title="First Vatican Council">First Vatican Council</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Catholicism" title="Anglo-Catholicism">Anglo-Catholics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicans</a> who believe that Anglicanism is a continuation of historical <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> and who incorporate many Catholic beliefs and practices. The Catholic Church refers to itself simply by the terms <i>Catholic</i> and <i>Catholicism</i> (which mean universal).</p>
<p>Sometimes, Catholics, based on a strict interpretation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra_ecclesiam_nulla_salus" title="Extra ecclesiam nulla salus">extra ecclesiam nulla salus</a> ("Outside the Church, there is no salvation"), rejected any notion those outside its communion could be regarded as part of any true Catholic Christian faith, an attitude rejected by the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> (1962-1965).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnitatisRed_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-UnitatisRed-17">[16]</a></sup> Catholicism has a hierarchical structure in which supreme authority for matters of faith and practice are the exclusive domain of the Pope, who sits on the Throne of Peter, and the bishops when acting in union with him. Most Catholics are unaware of the existence of <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholicism</a> which represents a relatively recent split from the Catholic Church and is particularly vocal in rejecting their use of the term <i>Catholic</i>.</p>
<p>Each Protestant movement has developed freely, and many have split over theological issues. For instance, a number of movements grew out of spiritual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revivalism" title="Revivalism">revivals</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostalism</a>. Doctrinal issues and matters of <a href="/wiki/Conscience" title="Conscience">conscience</a> have also divided Protestants. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptist" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptist</a> tradition, made up of the <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hutterites" title="Hutterites">Hutterites</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mennonites" title="Mennonites">Mennonites</a>, rejected the Roman Catholic and Lutheran doctrines of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedobaptism" title="Pedobaptism">infant baptism</a>; this tradition is also noted for its belief in <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifism</a>.</p>
<p>Many churches with roots in <a href="/wiki/Restorationism" title="Restorationism">Restorationism</a> reject being identified as Protestant or even as a denomination at all, as they use only the Bible and not creeds, and model the church after what they feel is the first-century church found in scripture; the <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ" title="Churches of Christ">Churches of Christ</a> are one example; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Initiated_Church" title="African Initiated Church">African Initiated Churches</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Kimbanguism" title="Kimbanguism">Kimbanguism</a>, mostly fall within Protestantism, with varying degrees of syncretism. The measure of mutual acceptance between the denominations and movements varies, but is growing largely due to the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenism" title="Ecumenism">ecumenical movement</a> in the 20th century and overarching Christian bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Council_of_Churches" title="World Council of Churches">World Council of Churches</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christians_with_Jewish_roots">Christians with Jewish roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Christians with Jewish roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messianic_Jews" title="Messianic Jews">Messianic Jews</a> maintain a Jewish identity while accepting Jesus as the <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> as authoritative. After the founding of the church, the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Christian" title="Jewish Christian">disciples of Jesus</a> generally retained their ethnic origins while accepting the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a> message. The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Jerusalem" title="Council of Jerusalem">first church council was called in Jerusalem</a> to address just this issue, and the deciding opinion was written by <a href="/wiki/James,_brother_of_Jesus" title="James, brother of Jesus">James the Just</a>, the first bishop of Jerusalem and a pivotal figure in the Christian movement. The history of Messianic Judaism includes many movements and groups and defies any simple classification scheme.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Nasrani</a> or <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Syrian Malabar Nasrani</a> community in <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> is conscious of their Jewish origins. However, they have lost many of their Jewish traditions due to western influences. The Nasrani are also known as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Syrian Christians</a> or <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">St. Thomas Christians</a>. This is because they follow the traditions of <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Christianity" title="Syriac Christianity">Syriac Christianity</a> and are descendants of the early converts by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle" title="Thomas the Apostle">St. Thomas the Apostle</a>. Today, they belong to various denominations of Christianity but they have kept their unique identity within each of these denominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>An existing community that still maintain their Jewish traditions is the <a href="/wiki/Knanaya" title="Knanaya">Knanaya</a>. They are an endogamous sub-ethnic group among the Syrian Malabar Nasrani and are the descendants of early Jewish Christian settlers who arrived in Kerala in A.D 345. Although affiliated with a variety of Roman Catholic and Oriental Orthodox denominations, they have remained a cohesive community, shunning intermarriage with outsiders (but not with fellow-Knanaya of other denominations).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quakers">Quakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Quakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some denominations which arose alongside the Western Christian tradition consider themselves Christian, but neither Roman Catholic nor wholly Protestant, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Religious Society of Friends</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quakers</a>). Quakerism began as an evangelical Christian movement in 17th century <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, eschewing priests and all formal Anglican or Roman Catholic sacraments in their worship, including many of those practices that remained among the stridently Protestant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritans</a> such as baptism with water. They were known in America for helping with the Underground Railroad, and like the Mennonites, Quakers traditionally refrain from participation in war.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latter_Day_Saint_movement">Latter Day Saint movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Latter Day Saint movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter Day Saint movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mormonism" title="Mormonism">Mormonism</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_sects_in_the_Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="List of sects in the Latter Day Saint movement">List of sects in the Latter Day Saint movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mormonism_and_Christianity" title="Mormonism and Christianity">Mormonism and Christianity</a></div>
<p>Most Latter Day Saint denominations are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Church of Christ (Latter Day Saints)">Church of Christ (Latter Day Saints)</a> established by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a> in 1830. The largest worldwide denomination, and the one publicly recognized as <a href="/wiki/Mormonism" title="Mormonism">Mormonism</a>, is <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>, although there are various considerably smaller sects that broke from it after its relocation to the Rocky Mountains in the mid-1800s. Several of these broke away over the abandonment of practicing <a href="/wiki/Mormonism_and_polygamy" title="Mormonism and polygamy">plural marriage</a> after the <a href="/wiki/1890_Manifesto" title="1890 Manifesto">1890 Manifesto</a>. Most of the "Prairie Saint" denominations (see below) were established after <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Joseph_Smith" title="Death of Joseph Smith">Smith's death</a> by the remnants of the Latter Day Saints who did not go west with <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young" title="Brigham Young">Brigham Young</a>. Many of these opposed some of the 1840s theological developments in favor of 1830s theological understandings and practices. Other denominations are defined by either a belief in Joseph Smith as a <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> or acceptance of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mormon" title="Book of Mormon">Book of Mormon</a> as <a href="/wiki/Religious_text" title="Religious text">scripture</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormons</a> generally consider themselves to be <a href="/wiki/Restorationism" title="Restorationism">restorationist</a>, believing that Smith, as <a href="/wiki/Prophet,_seer,_and_revelator" title="Prophet, seer, and revelator">prophet, seer, and revelator</a>, restored the original and true Church of Christ to the earth. Some Latter Day Saint denominations are regarded by other Christians as being <a href="/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">nontrinitarian</a> or even non-Christian, but the Latter Day Saints are predominantly in disagreement with these claims. Mormons see themselves as believing in a <a href="/wiki/God_in_Mormonism" title="God in Mormonism">Godhead</a> comprising the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost as separate personages united in purpose. Mormons regard traditional definitions of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> as aberrations of true doctrine and emblematic of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Apostasy" title="Great Apostasy">Great Apostasy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> but they do not accept certain trinitarian definitions in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Christian_creeds" title="List of Christian creeds">post-apostolic creeds</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Athanasian_Creed" title="Athanasian Creed">Athanasian Creed</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Great_Awakening">Second Great Awakening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Second Great Awakening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Awakening" title="Second Great Awakening">Second Great Awakening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restorationism" title="Restorationism">Restorationism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Restoration_Movement" title="Restoration Movement">Restoration Movement</a></div>
<p>The Stone-Campbell Restoration Movement began on the American frontier during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Awakening" title="Second Great Awakening">Second Great Awakening</a> (1790–1870) of the early 19th century. The movement sought to restore the church and "the unification of all Christians in a single body patterned after the church of the New Testament."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-I_Just_Want_to_Be_a_Christian_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-I_Just_Want_to_Be_a_Christian-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54</sup> Members do not identify as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> but simply as Christian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neither_Catholic.2C_Protestant_nor_Jew_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neither_Catholic.2C_Protestant_nor_Jew-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_of_Religion_in_the_South_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_of_Religion_in_the_South-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:213</sup></p>
<p>The Restoration Movement developed from several independent efforts to return to <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Age" title="Apostolic Age">apostolic Christianity</a>, but two groups, which independently developed similar approaches to the Christian faith, were particularly important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redigging_the_Wells_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Redigging_the_Wells-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27–32</sup> The first, led by <a href="/wiki/Barton_W._Stone" title="Barton W. Stone">Barton W. Stone</a>, began at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cane_Ridge" title="Cane Ridge">Cane Ridge</a>, Kentucky and called themselves simply as "<a href="/wiki/Christians_(Stone_Movement)" title="Christians (Stone Movement)">Christians</a>". The second began in western Pennsylvania and Virginia (now West Virginia) and was led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Campbell_(clergyman)" title="Thomas Campbell (clergyman)">Thomas Campbell</a> and his son, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Campbell_(clergyman)" title="Alexander Campbell (clergyman)">Alexander Campbell</a>; they used the name "<a href="/wiki/Disciples_of_Christ_(Campbell_Movement)" title="Disciples of Christ (Campbell Movement)">Disciples of Christ</a>". Both groups sought to restore the whole Christian church on the pattern set forth in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, and both believed that <a href="/wiki/Creed" title="Creed">creeds</a> kept Christianity divided. In 1832 they joined in fellowship with a handshake.</p>
<p>Among other things, they were united in the belief that <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> is the Christ, the <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a>; that Christians should celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Lord's Supper</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sabbath_in_Christianity" title="Sabbath in Christianity">first day of each week</a>; and that <a href="/wiki/Believer%27s_baptism" title="Believer's baptism">baptism of adult believers</a> by <a href="/wiki/Immersion_baptism" title="Immersion baptism">immersion in water</a> is a necessary condition for <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a>. Because the founders wanted to abandon all denominational labels, they used the biblical names for the followers of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McAlister_.26_Tucker.2C_1975_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-McAlister_.26_Tucker.2C_1975-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup> Both groups promoted a return to the purposes of the <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_1st_century" title="Christianity in the 1st century">1st-century churches</a> as described in the New Testament. One historian of the movement has argued that it was primarily a unity movement, with the restoration motif playing a subordinate role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_2002_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett_2002-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup></p>
<p>The Restoration Movement has since divided into multiple separate groups. There are three main branches in the US: the <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ" title="Churches of Christ">Churches of Christ</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_churches_and_churches_of_Christ" title="Christian churches and churches of Christ">Christian churches and churches of Christ</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church_(Disciples_of_Christ)" title="Christian Church (Disciples of Christ)">Christian Church (Disciples of Christ)</a>. Other U.S. based groups affiliated with the movement are the <a href="/wiki/International_Churches_of_Christ" title="International Churches of Christ">International Churches of Christ</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Christian_Churches" title="International Christian Churches">International Christian Churches</a>. Non-U.S. groups include the <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ_in_Australia" title="Churches of Christ in Australia">Churches of Christ in Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Christian_Church_in_Canada" title="Evangelical Christian Church in Canada">Evangelical Christian Church in Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ_in_Europe" title="Churches of Christ in Europe">Churches of Christ in Europe</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Brethren" title="Plymouth Brethren">Plymouth Brethren</a> are a similar though historically unrelated group which originated in the United Kingdom. Some churches, such as <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ" title="Churches of Christ">Churches of Christ</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Brethren" title="Plymouth Brethren">Plymouth Brethren</a> reject formal ties with other churches within the movement.</p>
<p>Other groups originating during the Second Great Awakening include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millerites" title="Millerites">Adventist</a> movement, the <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Science" title="Christian Science">Christian Science</a> (which had roots in <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregationalism</a>, but regarded itself as restorative). Each of these groups, founded within fifty years of one another, originally claimed to be an unprecedented, late restoration of the primitive Christian church. Some Baptist churches with <a href="/wiki/Landmarkism" title="Landmarkism">Landmarkist</a> views have similar beliefs concerning their connection with primitive Christianity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_sectarianism">Russian sectarianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Russian sectarianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> has a long history of opposing heresies, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Bogomilism" title="Bogomilism">Bogomilism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Old_Believers" title="Old Believers">Old Believers</a>, a sect opposing the reforms introduced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsarist_Russia" title="Tsarist Russia">Tsarist Russia</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Nikon" title="Patriarch Nikon">Patriarch Nikon</a> in 1666.</p>
<p>In 18th to 19th century <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Imperial Russia</a>, there arose a new type of denominational schism grouped as <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_Christianity" title="Spiritual Christianity">Spiritual Christianity</a> (<span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">духовное христианство</span>). Many heresies, nicknamed by the church or government, called themselves "spiritual Christians", such as: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dukhobor" title="Dukhobor">Dukhobors</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ikonobortsy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ikonobortsy (page does not exist)">Ikonobortsy</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">Iconoclasts</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Khlysts" title="Khlysts">Khlysts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molokan" title="Molokan">Molokans</a>, <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.molokane.org/taxonomy/" rel="nofollow">Pryguny</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Skoptsy" title="Skoptsy">Skoptsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shtundists" title="Shtundists">Shtundists</a>, <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.subbotniki.net" rel="nofollow">Subbotniki</a></i>, etc. These sects often have radically divergent notions of spirituality. Their common denominator is that they sought God in "Spirit and Truth", (<a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a> 4:24) rather than in the Church of official Orthodoxy or ancient rites of <a href="/wiki/Old_Believers" title="Old Believers">Old Believers</a>. Rejecting the official church, they considered their religious organization as a homogeneous community, without division into laymen and clergy.</p>
<p>In the 1830s, Ivan Grigorev Kanygin founded religious communities with communal practices in the <a href="/wiki/Novouzensk" title="Novouzensk">Novouzensk</a> region. They called themselves Communists or Methodists, but from the 1870s became known as "Mormons", by comparison with the contemporaneous American movement. An unrelated community known as "Samara Mormons" developed near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volga" title="Volga">Volga</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Samara,_Russia" title="Samara, Russia">Samara</a>. They avoided alcohol, tobacco, and swearing, cooperated in commercial enterprises, and governed themselves by "apostles" and "prophets".</p>
<p>A more recent charismatic movement in Russia is the "<a href="/wiki/Vissarion" title="Vissarion">Church of the Last Testament</a>", which established a substantial settlement in the Siberian Taiga in the 1990s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iglesia_ni_Cristo">Iglesia ni Cristo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Iglesia ni Cristo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to a number of similarities, some <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> writers describe the doctrines of the Philippines originating <a href="/wiki/Iglesia_ni_Cristo" title="Iglesia ni Cristo">Iglesia ni Cristo</a> as restorationist in outlook and theme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annharper_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-annharper-27">[26]</a></sup> INC, however, does not consider itself to be part of the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_Movement" title="Restoration Movement">Restoration Movement</a>. On the other hand, some Catholic leaders viewed Iglesia ni Cristo as an offshoot of the Roman Catholic Church, since the then first leader or Executive Minister (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felix_Y._Manalo" title="Felix Y. Manalo">Felix Ysagun Manalo</a>) was a former Catholic member. However, INC is working and functioning spiritually and financially on its own, thus, completely independent from any religious body and communion.</p>
<p>The church hierarchical administration (Filipino: <i>Pamamahala</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exec_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-exec-28">[27]</a></sup> centralized church governance, theological orientation, places of worship architectural design, adaptation to modern technology, very strong and strict discipline, and country of origin or establishment, are some of the INC features, polity and organizational structure that identify itself different from Restoration Movement, <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> and mainstream <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. Iglesia ni Cristo members are noted for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voting_block" title="Voting block">bloc voting</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_elections" title="Philippine elections">political elections</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pasugo1986_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pasugo1986-29">[28]</a></sup> which is unique to the church due to their doctrine on unity and a practice that cannot be found outside INC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Thought_Movement">New Thought Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: New Thought Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Thought" title="New Thought">New Thought</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_New_Thought" title="History of New Thought">History of New Thought</a></div>
<p>Another group of churches are known under the banner of "<a href="/wiki/New_Thought" title="New Thought">New Thought</a>". These churches share a <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mystical</a> predisposition and understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and were strongly influenced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcendentalist" title="Transcendentalist">Transcendentalist</a> movement—particularly the work of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Emerson</a>. Another antecedent of this movement was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedenborgianism" title="Swedenborgianism">Swedenborgianism</a>, founded in 1787 on the writings of <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Swedenborg" title="Emanuel Swedenborg">Emanuel Swedenborg</a>, who claimed to have received a new revelation from Jesus Christ through continuous heavenly visions which he experienced over a period of at least twenty-five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The New Thought concept was named by <a href="/wiki/Emma_Curtis_Hopkins" title="Emma Curtis Hopkins">Emma Curtis Hopkins</a> ("teacher of teachers") after Hopkins broke off from <a href="/wiki/Mary_Baker_Eddy" title="Mary Baker Eddy">Mary Baker Eddy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ,_Scientist" title="Church of Christ, Scientist">Church of Christ, Scientist</a>—the movement had been previously known as the Mental Sciences. The New Thought movement includes <a href="/wiki/Religious_Science" title="Religious Science">Religious Science</a> founded by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Holmes" title="Ernest Holmes">Ernest Holmes</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine_Science" title="Divine Science">Divine Science</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Malinda_Cramer" title="Malinda Cramer">Malinda Cramer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nona_L._Brooks" title="Nona L. Brooks">Brook sisters</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Unity_Church" title="Unity Church">Unity</a> founded by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fillmore_(Unity_Church)" title="Charles Fillmore (Unity Church)">Charles Fillmore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myrtle_Fillmore" title="Myrtle Fillmore">Myrtle Fillmore</a>. The founders of these denominations all studied with Emma Curtis Hopkins. Each of one these New Thought Churches has been influenced by a wide variety of ancient spiritual ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Each of these churches identify to different degrees with Christianity, Unity and Divine Science being the most explicit in the use of the Bible.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Christian_Community">The Christian Community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The Christian Community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Community" title="The Christian Community">The Christian Community</a> is a movement for religious renewal. It was founded in 1922 in Switzerland by the Lutheran theologian and minister Friedrich Rittlemeyer, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Steiner" title="Rudolf Steiner">Rudolf Steiner</a>, the Austrian philosopher and founder of <a href="/wiki/Anthroposophy" title="Anthroposophy">anthroposophy</a>. Christian Community congregations exist as financially independent groups with regional and international administrative bodies overseeing their work. There are approximately 350 worldwide. The international headquarters are in Berlin, Germany.</p>
<p>The Christian Community does not require its members to conform to any specific teaching or behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> Seven sacraments are celebrated within the Community: the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>, generally called the <i>Act of Consecration of Man</i>, and six other sacraments: <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">Baptism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">Confirmation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">Marriage</a>, The Last <a href="/wiki/Anointing" title="Anointing">Anointing</a>, Sacramental Consultation (replacing <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">Confession</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">Ordination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_movements">Other movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_denomination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Other movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Protestant denominations have shown a strong tendency towards diversification and fragmentation, giving rise to numerous churches and movements, especially in Anglo-American religious history, where the process is cast in terms of a series of "<a href="/wiki/Great_Awakening" title="Great Awakening">Great Awakenings</a>". The most recent wave of diversification, known as the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Great_Awakening" title="Fourth Great Awakening">Fourth Great Awakening</a> took place during the 1960s to 1980s and resulted in phenomena such as the <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_Movement" title="Charismatic Movement">Charismatic Movement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jesus_movement" title="Jesus movement">Jesus movement</a>, and a great number of <a href="/wiki/Parachurch_organization" title="Parachurch organization">Parachurch organizations</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelicalism</a>.</p>
<p>Many independent churches and movements considered themselves to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nondenominational" title="Nondenominational">non-denominational</a>, but may vary greatly in doctrine. Many of these, like the <a href="/wiki/Local_churches_(affiliation)" title="Local churches (affiliation)">local churches</a> movement, reflect the core teachings of traditional Christianity. Others however, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Way_International" title="The Way International">The Way International</a>, have been denounced as cults by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_anti-cult_movement" title="Christian anti-cult movement">Christian anti-cult movement</a>.</p>
<p>Two movements, which are entirely unrelated in their founding, but share a common element of an additional Messiah (or incarnation of Christ) are the <a href="/wiki/Unification_Church" title="Unification Church">Unification Church</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rastafari_movement" title="Rastafari movement">Rastafari movement</a>. These movements fall outside of traditional <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(general)" title="Taxonomy (general)">taxonomies</a> of Christian groups, though both cite the Christian Bible as a basis for their beliefs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">Syncretism</a> of Christian beliefs with local and tribal religions is a phenomenon that occurs throughout the world. An example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">Native American Church</a>. The ceremonies of this group are strongly tied to the use of <a href="/wiki/Peyote" title="Peyote">peyote</a>. (Parallels may be drawn here with the Rastafari <a href="/wiki/Entheogen" title="Entheogen">spiritual use</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a>.) While traditions vary from tribe to tribe, they often include a belief in Jesus as a Native American cultural hero, an intercessor for man, or a spiritual guardian; belief in the Bible; and an association of Jesus with peyote.</p>
<p>There are also some Christians that reject organized religion altogether. Some <a href="/wiki/Christian_anarchism" title="Christian anarchism">Christian anarchists</a> believe that the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">original teachings of Jesus</a> were corrupted by Roman statism (compare <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Early Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_church_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="State church of the Roman Empire">State church of the Roman Empire</a>), and that earthly authority such as government, or indeed the established Church, do not and should not have power over them. Following "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethic_of_reciprocity" title="Ethic of reciprocity">The Golden Rule</a>", many oppose the use of physical force in any circumstance, and advocate <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>. The Russian novelist <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a> wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_of_God_Is_Within_You" title="The Kingdom of God Is Within You">The Kingdom of God Is Within You</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> and was a Christian anarchist.</p>
