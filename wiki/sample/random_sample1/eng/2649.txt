<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bridge (instrument)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>bridge</b> is a device that supports the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strings_(music)" title="Strings (music)">strings</a> on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stringed_instrument" title="Stringed instrument">stringed musical instrument</a> and transmits the <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a> of those strings to some other structural component of the instrument—typically a <a href="/wiki/Sound_board_(music)" title="Sound board (music)">soundboard</a>, such as the top of a guitar or violin—which transfers the <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> to the surrounding air.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most stringed instruments produce their sound through the application of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> to the strings, which sets them into vibratory motion. The strings alone, however, produce only a faint sound because they <a href="/wiki/Particle_displacement" title="Particle displacement">displace</a> only a small volume of air as they vibrate. Consequently, the sound of the strings alone requires <a href="/wiki/Impedance_matching" title="Impedance matching">impedance matching</a> to the surrounding air by transmitting their vibrations to a larger surface area capable of displacing larger volumes of air (and thus producing louder sounds). This calls for an arrangement that allows for the strings to vibrate freely, but also conducts those vibrations efficiently to the larger surface. A bridge is the customary means by which this is accomplished.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positioning">Positioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Positioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typically, the bridge is perpendicular to the strings and larger surface (which are roughly parallel to one another) with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tension_(mechanics)" title="Tension (mechanics)">tension</a> of the strings pressing down on the bridge and thus on the larger surface beneath it. That larger, more acoustically responsive surface may be coupled to a sound chamber—an enclosure such as the body of a <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>—that provides resonance that helps amplify the sound. Depending on the type of stringed instrument, the <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonant</a> surface the bridge rests on may be made of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Wood, as the top plate of a <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a></li>
<li>Calfskin or plastic, as the head of a <a href="/wiki/Banjo" title="Banjo">banjo</a></li>
<li>Metal, as on certain types of resophonic fretted instruments</li>
<li>Virtually any material that vibrates sympathetically with the strings</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="1446" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg/220px-Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg/330px-Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg/440px-Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Violin_bridge_blank_and_finished.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A violin bridge blank and a finished bridge.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bridges may consist of a single piece of material, most commonly wood, that fits between the strings and the resonant surface. Alternatively, a bridge may consist of multiple parts. One common form is a bridge that incorporates a separate bearing surface on which the strings rest, termed a <i>saddle</i>. This is often of a material harder than the bridge itself, such as bone, ivory, high-density plastic, or metal.</p>
<p>A classical guitar saddle sits loosely in the hardwood bridge and is kept there by string tension. The saddle has shallow grooves in it, at least for the treble strings, which prevents them moving around during hard playing.</p>
<p>Yet another type of multi-part bridge is common on instruments whose sound plate is curved rather than flat. Instruments of this type, such as arch-top <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolins</a>, often have a bridge with a base and separate saddle that can be adjusted for height. On classical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat-top_guitar" title="Flat-top guitar">flat-top guitars</a> the bridge is glued to the top. A bridge held on to the top by string tension, as in archtop guitars, is known as a <b>floating bridge</b>, and requires a separate tailpiece to anchor the strings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guitar_bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="218" data-file-width="330" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/51/Guitar_bridge.jpg" width="330"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">A guitar's bridge holds its strings fast to the instrument; its saddle (white) raises them above the bridge and conveys their vibrations to the bridge. The black fasteners are called string pegs. All are "loose"—held in place only by string tension.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bridge must transfer vibration to the <a href="/wiki/Sound_board_(music)" title="Sound board (music)">sound board</a> or other amplifying surface. As the strings are set in motion, it does this by bending to and fro along the string direction at twice the rate of the string vibration. This causes the sounding board to vibrate at the same frequency as the string producing a wave-like motion and an audible sound.</p>
<p>Bridges are designed to hold the strings at a suitable height above the <a href="/wiki/Fingerboard" title="Fingerboard">fingerboard</a> of the instrument. The ideal bridge height creates sufficient angularity in the string to create enough down force to drive the top, but places the strings sufficiently close to the fingerboard to make noting the strings easy. Bridge height may be fixed or alterable.</p>
<p>In addition to supporting the strings and transmitting their vibrations, the bridge also controls the spacing between strings with shallow grooves cut in the bridge or its saddle. The strings sit in those grooves, thus are held in their proper lateral position. The nut, at the opposite end of the instrument from the bridge or tailpiece (typically where the head holding the tuning pegs joins the fingerboard), serves a similar string-spacing function.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_guitar_bridges">Electric guitar bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electric guitar bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bridges for electric guitars can be divided into two main groups, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibrato_arm" title="Vibrato arm">vibrato</a>" and "non-vibrato" (also called "hard-tail"). Vibrato bridges have an arm (called the vibrato arm, tremolo arm, or "whammy bar") that extends from below the string anchoring point. It acts as a <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">lever</a> that the player can push or pull to change the strings <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tension_(mechanics)" title="Tension (mechanics)">tension</a> and, as a result, the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a>. This means that this type of bridge produces <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> rather than actual <a href="/wiki/Tremolo" title="Tremolo">tremolo</a>, but the term "tremolo" is deeply entrenched in popular usage via some manufacturers naming their vibrato systems as "tremolo".</p>
<p>Non-vibrato bridges supply an anchoring point for the strings but provide no active control over string tension or pitch. A small group of vibrato bridges have an extended tail (also called "longtail"). These guitars have more reverb in their sound, because of the string resonance behind the bridge. The <a href="/wiki/Fender_Jaguar" title="Fender Jaguar">Fender Jaguar</a> is a example of such a guitar.</p>
<p>All bridges have advantages, depending on the playing style, but, in general, a non-vibrato bridge is thought to provide better tuning stability and a solid contact between the guitar body and the strings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vibrato_bridges">Vibrato bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vibrato bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tremolo_arm" title="Tremolo arm">tremolo arm</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kahler_tremolo_system.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A licensed Kahler <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> system on a '87 <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Guitar_Corporation" title="Gibson Guitar Corporation">Gibson</a> <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul" title="Gibson Les Paul">Les Paul</a> Standard</div>

<p>Generally, the more <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contact" title="wikt:contact">contact</a> the bridge has with the body (i.e. the lower the position), the better the sound transfer will be into the body. A "warmer" sound with increased sustain is the result.</p>
<p>Vibrato bridges usually must be suspended in some way, which reduces contact. Most vibrato system designs use a group of <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">springs</a> in the guitar body, which oppose the tension of the strings. Some players feel that the vibration of the springs affects <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonance</a> in a way that makes the guitar sound better, but others disagree. Many electric guitar playing styles require a vibrato system, either "locking" or "non-locking".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Locking_Tremolo.2FVibrato_systems">Non-Locking Tremolo/Vibrato systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-Locking Tremolo/Vibrato systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Non-locking (or vintage) tremolos are the bridges found on guitars manufactured prior to the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Floyd_Rose" title="Floyd Rose">Floyd Rose</a> locking tremolo in the late 1970s and many (typically cheaper) guitars manufactured thereafter. For many playing styles, vintage tremolos are a good choice because they are easy to use and maintain and have very few parts. Some people feel that they can also provide a better degree of sound transfer, especially with tailpiece type tremolos such as the Bigsby. However, "Synchronized Tremolo" type found on the <a href="/wiki/Fender_Musical_Instruments_Corporation" title="Fender Musical Instruments Corporation">Fender</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocaster" title="Stratocaster">Stratocaster</a> is balanced against a set of screws in much the same manner as a locking tremolo and offers little improvement in the way of sounds transfer. Given that this type of tremolo is installed on solid body guitars the degree to which sound transfer affects the sound that the instrument produces is minimal. Also, keeping a guitar with a non-locking tremolo in tune can be difficult. The most common types of non-locking tremolos are the "Synchronized Tremolo" type and an almost endless stream of copies. The <a href="/wiki/Bigsby_vibrato_tailpiece" title="Bigsby vibrato tailpiece">Bigsby vibrato tailpiece</a> is another option.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Locking_Tremolo.2FVibrato_systems">Locking Tremolo/Vibrato systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Locking Tremolo/Vibrato systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another type of tremolo is the locking tremolo. These bridges have a sort of vise in each saddle which clamps the string into place (usually with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_key" title="Allen key">Allen key</a>). The end of the string has a clamp, which holds the string in place, stopping it from slipping. It is especially useful for songs that require tapping or heavy/hard playing styles. Once set up for tuning stability, locking tremolos are very good. However, their fulcrum points offer extremely minute contact with the body, which may disturb sound transfer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Tremolo.2FVibrato_bridges">Non-Tremolo/Vibrato bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridge_(instrument)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-Tremolo/Vibrato bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Badass Bridge on a Martin EB18 Bass guitar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.F._Martin_%26_Company" title="C.F. Martin &amp; Company">C.F. Martin &amp; Company</a></div>

<p>It is generally thought that non-tremolo bridges offer better transfer of string vibration into the body. This is due to direct contact of the bridge to the guitar's body.</p>
<p>These bridges are bolted directly to the guitar body. Assuming the bridge is of good quality, it will limit longitudinal string movement, and therefore tuning stability is assured.</p>
<p>The improved transfer of string vibration into the body has an effect on the sound, so guitars with this type of bridge have different characteristics than those with tremolos even when it is removed. There are no springs in the body or a cavity to accommodate them, which also affects resonance.</p>
