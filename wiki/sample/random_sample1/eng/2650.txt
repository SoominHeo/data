<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Damnatio memoriae</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><i><b>Damnatio memoriae</b></i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin phrase</a> literally meaning "condemnation of memory," meaning that a person must not be remembered.</p>
<p>It was a form of dishonor that could be passed by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman Senate</a> on <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">traitors</a> or others who brought discredit to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_State" title="Roman State">Roman State</a>. The intent was to erase the malefactor from history, a task somewhat easier in ancient times, when documentation was limited.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damnatio_memoriae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Damnatio Memoriae was originally created by the Romans, who viewed it as a punishment worse than death itself. Felons would literally be erased from history for the crimes they had committed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damnatio_memoriae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_(DerHexer)_2012-09-30_008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2755" data-file-width="4149" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_%28DerHexer%29_2012-09-30_008.jpg/220px-R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_%28DerHexer%29_2012-09-30_008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_%28DerHexer%29_2012-09-30_008.jpg/330px-R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_%28DerHexer%29_2012-09-30_008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_%28DerHexer%29_2012-09-30_008.jpg/440px-R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_%28DerHexer%29_2012-09-30_008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:R%C3%B6mermuseum_Osterburken_(DerHexer)_2012-09-30_008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Damnatio memoriae</i> of '<a href="/wiki/Commodus" title="Commodus">Commodus</a>' on an inscription in the Museum of Roman History <a href="/wiki/Osterburken" title="Osterburken">Osterburken</a>. The abbreviation "CO" was later restored with paint.</div>

<p>The sense of the expression <i>damnatio memoriae</i> and of the sanction is to cancel every trace of the person from the life of Rome, as if he or she had never existed, in order to preserve the honour of the city. In a city that stressed social appearance, respectability, and the pride of being a true Roman as a fundamental requirement of the citizen, it was perhaps the most severe punishment.</p>
<p>In Latin, the term <i>damnatio memoriae</i> was not used by the ancient Romans. The first appearance of the phrase is in a dissertation written in Germany in 1689. The term is used in modern scholarship to cover a wide array of official and unofficial sanctions whereby the physical remnants of a deceased individual were destroyed to differing degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice">Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damnatio_memoriae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="392" data-file-width="800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg/300px-Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg/450px-Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg/600px-Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sejanus_Damnatio_Memoriae.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sejanus" title="Sejanus">Lucius Aelius Sejanus</a> suffered <i>damnatio memoriae</i> following a failed conspiracy to overthrow emperor <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> in 31 CE. His statues were destroyed and his name obliterated from all <a href="/wiki/Public_records" title="Public records">public records</a>. The above coin from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augusta_Bilbilis" title="Augusta Bilbilis">Augusta Bilbilis</a>, originally struck to mark the consulship of Sejanus, has the words <i>L. Aelio Seiano</i> obliterated.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In ancient Rome, the practice of <i>damnatio memoriae</i> was the condemnation of Roman elites and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_Emperors" title="List of Roman Emperors">emperors</a> after their deaths. If the senate or a later emperor did not like the acts of an individual, they could have his property seized, his name erased and his statues reworked. Because there is an economic incentive to seize property and rework statues, historians and archaeologists have had difficulty determining when official <i>damnatio memoriae</i> actually took place, although it seems to have been quite rare.</p>
<p>Historians sometimes use the phrase <i>de facto damnatio memoriae</i> when the condemnation is not official. Examples of this may include the destruction of the statues and other images of two close members of <a href="/wiki/Caligula" title="Caligula">Caligula</a>'s family, his wife <a href="/wiki/Milonia_Caesonia" title="Milonia Caesonia">Milonia Caesonia</a> and his daughter <a href="/wiki/Julia_Drusilla_(daughter_of_Caligula)" title="Julia Drusilla (daughter of Caligula)">Julia Drusilla</a>, which occurred after the emperor was assassinated. Among those few who suffered legal damnatio memoriae were <a href="/wiki/Sejanus" title="Sejanus">Sejanus</a>, who had conspired against emperor <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> in 31 CE, and later <a href="/wiki/Livilla" title="Livilla">Livilla</a>, who was revealed to be his accomplice.</p>
<p>It is unknown whether any <i>damnatio memoriae</i> was totally successful as it would not be noticeable to later historians, since, by definition, it would entail the complete and total erasure of the individual in question from the historical record. It was difficult, however, to implement the practice completely. For instance, the senate wanted to condemn the memory of Caligula, but <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> prevented this. <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a> was declared an enemy of the state by the senate, but then given an enormous funeral honoring him after his death by <a href="/wiki/Vitellius" title="Vitellius">Vitellius</a>. While statues of some emperors were destroyed or reworked after their death, others were erected. Also, many coins with the images of the discredited person continued to circulate. A particularly large number exist with Geta's image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>According to the biblical story, when the ancient Israelites entered the land of <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>, they were ordered to destroy several pagan tribes and their property. The tribe of <a href="/wiki/Amalek" title="Amalek">Amalek</a> was specifically singled out for destruction. <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a> would "completely blot out the name of Amalek from under heaven".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> To this day, the Hebrew Bible is the only ancient source that attests the existence of the Amalekites, and no other archaeological or epigraphic evidence of their existence has been found.</li>
<li>In ancient Greece, <a href="/wiki/Herostratus" title="Herostratus">Herostratus</a> set fire to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Artemis_in_Ephesus" title="Temple of Artemis in Ephesus">Temple of Artemis in Ephesus</a> to become famous. To discourage such acts, <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a> leaders decided that his name should never be repeated again, under penalty of death. This attempt was also unsuccessful, however, as illustrated by the fact that his name is still known today.</li>
<li>Ancient Egyptians attached the greatest importance to the preservation of a person's name. The one who destroyed a person's name was thought somehow to have destroyed the person,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and it was thought that this effect extended beyond the grave. Most famously, the <a href="/wiki/Cartouche" title="Cartouche">cartouches</a> of the heretical 18th dynasty pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">Akhenaten</a> and his immediate successors during the <a href="/wiki/Amarna_Period" title="Amarna Period">Amarna Period</a> were mutilated by <a href="/wiki/Horemheb" title="Horemheb">Horemheb</a>, who succeeded in wiping out their names so completely that it took until the 19th century for them to be rediscovered. Among those who were erased was <a href="/wiki/Tutankhamun" title="Tutankhamun">Tutankhamun</a>, ironically today one of the best known pharaohs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Earlier in that same dynasty, a similar attack on <a href="/wiki/Hatshepsut" title="Hatshepsut">Hatshepsut</a> was carried out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damn_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-damn-7">[7]</a></sup> However, only engravings and statuary of her as a crowned king of Egypt were attacked. Anything depicting her as a queen was left unharmed, so this was not strictly speaking <i>damnatio memoriae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Historical_deletion_in_ancient_Egypt" title="Category:Historical deletion in ancient Egypt">Category:Historical deletion in ancient Egypt</a></div>
<ul>
<li>In 10th century <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, after the death of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jarl_(title)" title="Jarl (title)">Jarl</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A1kon_Sigur%C3%B0arson" title="Hákon Sigurðarson">Hákon Sigurðarson</a>, according to <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a> "So great was the enmity of the <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a> people against Earl Hakon, that no man could venture to call him by any other name than "the evil earl"; and he was so called long after those days."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Following the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Julius II</a> said on the day of his election: "I will not live in the same rooms as the Borgias lived. He desecrated the Holy Church as none before. He usurped the papal power by the devil's aid, and I forbid under the pain of excommunication anyone to speak or think of Borgia again. His name and memory must be forgotten. It must be crossed out of every document and memorial. His reign must be obliterated. All paintings made of the Borgias or for them must be covered over with black crepe. All the tombs of the Borgias must be opened and their bodies sent back to where they belong – to Spain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NC2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NC2-10">[10]</a></sup> The Borgias' apartments remained sealed until the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NC2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NC2-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adandozan" title="Adandozan">Adandozan</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Dahomey" title="Dahomey">Dahomey</a> at the beginning of the nineteenth century, had imprisoned his brother Gakpe. Once the latter became king <a href="/wiki/Ghezo" title="Ghezo">Ghezo</a>, he took revenge by erasing the memory of Adandozan. To this day, Adandozan is not officially considered as one of the twelve kings of Dahomey.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marino_Faliero" title="Marino Faliero">Marino Faliero</a>, fifty-fifth <a href="/wiki/Doge_of_Venice" title="Doge of Venice">Doge of Venice</a>, was executed in 1355 after a failed coup d'état. In the aftermath, his portrait displayed in the Sala del Maggior Consiglio (Hall of the Great Council) in the Doge's Palace was removed and the space painted over with a black shroud, which can still be seen in the hall today. An inscription on the painted shroud reads: <i>Hic est locus Marini Faletro decapitati pro criminibus</i> ("This is the space reserved for Marino Faliero, beheaded for his crimes").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g49_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-g49-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>All court documents related to the 1598 process against a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">Parisian</a> <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killer</a> nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Werewolf" title="Werewolf">Werewolf</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2lons" title="Châlons">Châlons</a>" or "the Demon Tailor" were destroyed in a process intended to erase his memory. The killer's name is today unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ivan_VI_of_Russia" title="Ivan VI of Russia">Ivan VI of Russia</a> (1740–64), imprisoned as an infant after a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a> was at the time essentially written out of Russian history, with an attempt made to destroy any documents or coins mentioning him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>The penultimate verse of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">King James Version</a> <i>"And if any man shall take away from the words of the book of this prophecy, God shall take away his part out of the book of life, and out of the holy city, and from the things which are written in this book."</i>) appears to threaten whoever alters its text with a <a href="/wiki/Self-reference" title="Self-reference">self-referent</a> kind of damnatio memoriae.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>More modern examples of <i>damnatio memoriae</i> include the removal of portraits, books, editing people out of pictures, and any other traces of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>'s opponents during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a> (for example, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Soviet_Encyclopedia#Damnatio_memoriae" title="Great Soviet Encyclopedia">Great Soviet Encyclopedia</a></i>). When the Soviet Union <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team lost to <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> at the 1952 Summer Olympics, Stalin ordered that all footage of the event was to be destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Stalin himself was edited out of some propaganda films when <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> became the leader of the Soviet Union, and the city of Tsaritsyn that had earlier been named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalingrad" title="Stalingrad">Stalingrad</a> was renamed <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">Volgograd</a> in 1961.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, it was forbidden to say "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Domingo_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Domingo Perón">Juan Domingo Perón</a>" after the coup that deposed him in 1955, and the media often referred to him as the "deposed tyrant". Hospitals and other public buildings named after him during his presidency were quickly renamed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberating_Revolution" title="Liberating Revolution">Liberating Revolution</a>. Photographs and other representations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine" title="Argentine">Argentine</a> leader were also prohibited.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the official portraits of disgraced <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> governors <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">Spiro Agnew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Mandel" title="Marvin Mandel">Marvin Mandel</a> were absent from the <a href="/wiki/Maryland_State_House" title="Maryland State House">Maryland State House</a> Governor's Reception Room for periods of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In both cases, this followed allegations of corruption. Mandel's portrait was restored when his convictions were overturned on appeal, and while Agnew was also convicted, his portrait was restored after complaints and arguments that no one had the right to change history.</li>
<li>Memorials to continental general <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Saratoga_National_Historical_Park" title="Saratoga National Historical Park">Saratoga National Historical Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Academy" title="United States Military Academy">United States Military Academy</a> bear neither his name nor his likeness, as a result of his <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold#Plotting_to_change_sides" title="Benedict Arnold">treason</a>. The plaque at the military academy says simply, "Major General. Born 1740."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>For a time during the latter part of the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Madame_Tussauds" title="Madame Tussauds">Madame Tussauds</a> Museum in London displayed a statue of Adolf Hitler in the stairway leading from the main floor down to its basement <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Horrors_(Madame_Tussauds)" title="Chamber of Horrors (Madame Tussauds)">Chamber of Horrors</a>. Unlike most other statues in the museum, the Hitler statue had no identifying label.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>In Uruguay during the early-1970s, the <a href="/wiki/Tupamaros" title="Tupamaros">Tupamaros</a>, a local guerrilla group, was making the government and police look foolish with their brazen raids and kidnappings. As a result, in the news a number of terms could not be used to describe them ("commando", "terrorist", etc.), nor could the organization's name itself be mentioned. As a substitute for "Tupamaros" people spoke of "The Nameless Ones."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2007, after it emerged that professional wrestler <a href="/wiki/Chris_Benoit" title="Chris Benoit">Chris Benoit</a> had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Benoit_double_murder_and_suicide" title="Chris Benoit double murder and suicide">murdered his wife and son</a> before committing suicide, <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">WWE</a> removed all mention of him from its television broadcasts, website, and subsequent DVD releases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2014, Benoit's footage was redistributed as part of the <a href="/wiki/WWE_Network" title="WWE Network">WWE Network</a>, sometimes with a viewer advisory warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The incident itself was recapped in <i>WWE 50</i>, an official company retrospective book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2008, two engraved bricks on the "Wall of Fame" at Liverpool's famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavern_Club" title="Cavern Club">Cavern Club</a> were controversially removed because they bore the names of two members of the music industry who have since been disgraced by sexual scandal: singer/songwriter <a href="/wiki/Gary_Glitter" title="Gary Glitter">Gary Glitter</a> and record producer <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_King" title="Jonathan King">Jonathan King</a>. In their place, a metal plaque was installed which simply stated that the names had been removed (albeit without actually identifying the men).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianGlitterBrick_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianGlitterBrick-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2010, after the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Sarath_Fonseka" title="Sarath Fonseka">Sarath Fonseka</a> in 2010 presidential election of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarath_Fonseka" title="Sarath Fonseka">Fonseka</a>'s name, military honors and achievements were removed from the official web site of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka_Army" title="Sri Lanka Army">Sri Lanka Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 10 May 2012, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> announced that it had made a "terrible mistake" by publishing a booklet with a photograph bearing the likeness of convicted murderer and rapist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russell_Williams_(colonel)" title="Russell Williams (colonel)">Russell Williams</a> in the background, and ordered 4,000 copies of the book destroyed. The photograph was incidental to the subject matter of the book, but the image was felt to be offensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2010, the CF had also burned his uniforms and destroyed his medals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>The names of <a href="/wiki/Hosni_Mubarak" title="Hosni Mubarak">Hosni Mubarak</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Mubarak" title="Suzanne Mubarak">Suzanne</a> were erased from all Egyptian monuments after they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Egyptian_revolution" title="2011 Egyptian revolution">deposed</a> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damn_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-damn-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Convicted child rapist and retired assistant coach <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Sandusky" title="Jerry Sandusky">Jerry Sandusky</a> was edited out of a mural at Penn State University, and replaced with a blue ribbon. Famous coach <a href="/wiki/Joe_Paterno" title="Joe Paterno">Joe Paterno</a> also had a statue of him and a backdrop removed, along with the record of his victories from 1998 through 2011 (from a head coaching career spanning from 1966 to 2011) vacated (Paterno does, however, remain on the same mural from which Sandusky was omitted and the wins were restored in 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2012, in the wake of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jimmy_Savile_child_sexual_abuse_scandal" title="Jimmy Savile child sexual abuse scandal">child sexual abuse allegations</a> against the late disc-jockey and TV presenter <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Savile" title="Jimmy Savile">Jimmy Savile</a>, his headstone was removed from his grave site, buildings named after him were renamed, and street signs with his name were taken down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> later announced it would no longer show reruns of <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i> hosted by Savile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Subsequent attempts to expunge public memory of Savile have included the vandalism of commemorative plaques and properties that he once owned.</li>
<li>Following disclosures that widely respected <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a> researcher Sidney Gilman was passing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inside_information" title="Inside information">inside information</a> about ongoing drug trials to <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a> traders, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>, where he taught, deleted all references to him from its website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sidney_Gilman_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sidney_Gilman-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>In December 2013, the high-ranking <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> politician <a href="/wiki/Jang_Sung-taek" title="Jang Sung-taek">Jang Sung-taek</a> was publicly purged from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worker%27s_Party_of_Korea" title="Worker's Party of Korea">Worker's Party of Korea</a> and subsequently executed. In the aftermath, documentary footage first broadcast in October was edited to remove all frames with his presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, it is common to express strong condemnation of a person by adding whenever his name is mentioned the Hebrew words <a href="/wiki/Yimakh_shemo" title="Yimakh shemo">"Yimach Shmo VeZichro"</a> (ימח שמו וזכרו), literally "May his name and memory be erased"—though in present Jewish society, this is not necessarily accompanied by an effort to actually destroy physical records mentioning that person.</li>
<li>In January 2014, after the separation of French president <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Hollande" title="François Hollande">François Hollande</a> and his "first lady" <a href="/wiki/Val%C3%A9rie_Trierweiler" title="Valérie Trierweiler">Valérie Trierweiler</a> 130 pages with 600 pictures of the first lady were deleted from the official internet site of the Elysée on 26 January 2014 at 11:00.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2014, at the beginning of Second <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> Presidency of <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Bachelet" title="Michelle Bachelet">Michelle Bachelet</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Casa_de_Moneda_de_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Casa de Moneda de Chile (page does not exist)">Casa de Moneda</a> was ordered to stop coinage of Presidential Medals of <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a> (medal collection of every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidents_of_Chile" title="Presidents of Chile">Chilean President</a>) and to destroy its rolls.</li>
<li>In July 2015, the <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">WWE</a> removed all references to wrestler <a href="/wiki/Hulk_Hogan" title="Hulk Hogan">Hulk Hogan</a> from its website after eight-year old audio tapes emerged in which Hogan used the word "nigger".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCHogan_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCHogan-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the Dominican Republic, official photos of the local church hierarchy were digitally modified, similar to the Yezhov's case in USSR (see above), to remove disgraced <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Weso%C5%82owski" title="Józef Wesołowski">Wesołowski</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>October 2015 saw the launch of a proposal to remove from the media all references to the names of mass murderers (beginning 48 hours after the first report of a mass-murder) in order to prevent them from gaining permanent fame while the victims are forgotten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The 48-hour proposed delay before a mass-murderer's name is to be expunged from reportage is intended to provide time to alert the public to the killer, in aid of police investigations.</li>
<li>In 2015, there was <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Must_Fall" title="Rhodes Must Fall">movement at several universities to remove anything honoring Cecil Rhodes</a> due to his part in African colonialism. The University of Cape Town removed a statue of Rhodes <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and Rhodes University began talks to change the school's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>Many contemporary novels and films mention a form of <i>damnatio memoriae</i>. Two early examples are the "vaporization" of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unperson" title="Unperson">unpersons</a>" in <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">George Orwell</a>'s 1949 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dystopian" title="Dystopian">dystopian</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four">Nineteen Eighty-Four</a></i> ("He did not exist; he never existed"); and the reference to the Egyptian practice in the 1956 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ten_Commandments_(1956_film)" title="The Ten Commandments (1956 film)">The Ten Commandments</a></i>, in which the Pharaoh Seti orders the name of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> be struck from every building and never mentioned by anyone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>More recent authors who have used <i>damnatio memoriae</i> as a plot device include <a href="/wiki/Milan_Kundera" title="Milan Kundera">Milan Kundera</a> in his 1979 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Laughter_and_Forgetting" title="The Book of Laughter and Forgetting">The Book of Laughter and Forgetting</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.A._Salvatore" title="R.A. Salvatore">R.A. Salvatore</a> in the 1990 novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeland_(R._A._Salvatore_novel)" title="Homeland (R. A. Salvatore novel)">Homeland</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Lois_Lowry" title="Lois Lowry">Lois Lowry</a> in her 1993 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Giver" title="The Giver">The Giver</a></i> (a version in which the damned name is never given to any new baby ever again), and Brian Herbert and Kevin J. Anderson in their 1999 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prelude_to_Dune" title="Prelude to Dune">Prelude to Dune</a></i> trilogy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the TV series, <a href="/wiki/Teen_Wolf_(2011_TV_series)" title="Teen Wolf (2011 TV series)"><i>Teen Wolf</i></a>, the character of Marie-Jeanne Valet uses <i>damnatio memoriae</i> to destroy all record of her brother's existence, after he is found to be a werewolf responsible for hundreds of deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
