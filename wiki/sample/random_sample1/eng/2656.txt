<p>An <b>aerophone</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instrument</a> that produces sound primarily by causing a body of air to vibrate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Randel_1999_p._12_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Randel_1999_p._12-1">[1]</a></sup> without the use of strings or membranes, and without the vibration of the instrument itself adding considerably to the sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Aerophones categorically comprise "the largest and most complex group of instruments in the Americas".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nettl_Miller_Stone_Williams_1998_p._29_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nettl_Miller_Stone_Williams_1998_p._29-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aerophones are one of the four main classes of instruments in the original <a href="/wiki/Hornbostel%E2%80%93Sachs" title="Hornbostel–Sachs">Hornbostel–Sachs</a> system of <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument_classification" title="Musical instrument classification">musical instrument classification</a>, which further classifies aerophones by whether or not the vibrating air is contained within the instrument. The first class (<b>41</b>) includes instruments which, when played, do <i>not</i> contain the vibrating air. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullroarer_(music)" title="Bullroarer (music)">bullroarer</a> is one example. These are called <i>free aerophones</i>. This class includes (<b>412.13</b>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_reed" title="Free reed">free reed</a> instruments, such as the <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">harmonica</a>, but also many instruments unlikely to be called wind instruments at all by most people, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siren_(noisemaker)" title="Siren (noisemaker)">sirens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whip_(instrument)" title="Whip (instrument)">whips</a>. The second class (<b>42</b>) includes instruments which contain the vibrating air when being played. This class includes almost all instruments generally called <a href="/wiki/Wind_instrument" title="Wind instrument">wind instruments</a> — including the <a href="/wiki/Didgeridoo" title="Didgeridoo">didgeridoo</a>, (<b>423</b>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brass_instruments" title="Brass instruments">brass instruments</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">french horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baritone_horn" title="Baritone horn">baritone horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a>), and (<b>421</b> &amp; <b>422</b>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodwind_instruments" title="Woodwind instruments">woodwind instruments</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98-4">[4]</a></sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, very loud sounds can be made by explosions directed into, or being detonated inside of resonant cavities. Detonations inside the <a href="/wiki/Calliope_(music)" title="Calliope (music)">calliope</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Steam_whistle" title="Steam whistle">steam whistle</a>), as well as the <a href="/wiki/Pyrophone" title="Pyrophone">pyrophone</a> might thus be considered as class 42 instruments, despite the fact that the "wind" or "air" may be steam or an air-fuel mixture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg/220px-Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg/330px-Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg/440px-Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neolithic_bone_flute.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Neolithic bone flute</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Identifying the origin of the aerophone is difficult, though it is believed that <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Americans and their descendants</a> developed the largest diversity of aerophones, and they are understood to have been the major non-vocal, melodic instruments of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuss_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuss-6">[6]</a></sup> Archaeological studies have found examples of <a href="/wiki/Globular_Flute" title="Globular Flute">globular flutes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico#Ancient_cultures" title="Mexico">ancient Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia#Pre-Columbian_era" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru#Prehistory_and_Pre-Columbian_period" title="Peru">Peru</a>, and multiple tubular flutes were common among the <a href="/wiki/Maya_peoples" title="Maya peoples">Maya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztec</a>. The use of <a href="/wiki/Conch#Musical_instruments" title="Conch">shells of Conches</a> as an aerophone have also been found to be prevalent in areas such as <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru#Prehistory_and_Pre-Columbian_period" title="Peru">Peru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OlsenSheehy2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OlsenSheehy2007-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of aerophone type instruments in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> can be dated back to the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic period</a>. Fragments of <a href="/wiki/Gudi_(instrument)" title="Gudi (instrument)">bone flutes</a> can be found at the burial sites of the <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu settlements</a> of ancient <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and they represent some of the earliest known examples of playable instruments. The instruments were typically carved from the wing bone of the <a href="/wiki/Red-crowned_crane" title="Red-crowned crane">red-crowned crane</a>, and had five to eight holes. The flutes were efficient enough to produce sound in a nearly accurate <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">octave</a>, and are thought to have been used ceremonially or for ritualistic purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Examples of flutes made out of bamboo in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> date back to 2nd Century BC. These flutes were known as <a href="/wiki/Dizi_(instrument)" title="Dizi (instrument)">Dizi's</a> or simply <i>Di</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AC%9B" title="wiktionary:笛">笛</a>) and typically had 6 holes for playing melodies that were framed by scale-modes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodman2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodman2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flutes including the famous <a href="/wiki/Bansuri" title="Bansuri">Bansuri</a>, have been an integral part of <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_music" title="Indian classical music">Indian classical music</a> since 1500 BC. A major deity of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>, has been associated with the flute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ll_book_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ll_book-12">[12]</a></sup> Some early flutes were made out of <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibias</a> (shin bones). The flute has also always been an essential part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_culture" title="Indian culture">Indian culture</a> and mythology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the cross flute is believed, by several accounts, to originate in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> as Indian literature from 1500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BCE" title="BCE">BCE</a> has made vague references to the cross flute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free">Free</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Free">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Free_reed_aerophone" title="Free reed aerophone">Free reed aerophone</a></div>
<p>Free aerophones are instruments where the vibrating air is not enclosed by the instrument itself.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Displacement">Displacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Displacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The air-stream meets a sharp edge, or a sharp edge is moved through the air.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interruptive">Interruptive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interruptive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The air-stream is interrupted periodically.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plosive">Plosive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Plosive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Occasionally called "percussive aerophones", plosive aerophones are sounded by percussion caused by a single compression and release of air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-See_Sharp_Press_1996_p._85_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-See_Sharp_Press_1996_p._85-17">[17]</a></sup> An example of a plosive aerophone is the "scraper flute" which has tubes with ridged or <a href="/wiki/Serration" title="Serration">serrated</a> edges so that they can be scraped with a rod to produce sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkin1996_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkin1996-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-free">Non-free</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Non-free">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Wind_instrument" title="Wind instrument">Wind instrument</a></div>
<p>Non-free aerophones are instruments where the vibrating air is contained within the instrument. Often called <a href="/wiki/Wind_instrument" title="Wind instrument">wind instruments</a>, they are typically divided into two categories; <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instrument" title="Woodwind instrument">Woodwind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">Brass</a>. It is widely accepted that wind instruments are not classified on the material from which they are made, as a woodwind instrument does not need to be made of wood, nor a brass instrument made of brass. Woodwind instruments are often made with <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> and include the <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">recorder</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophone</a>. Brass instruments are often made with <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horn_(anatomy)" title="Horn (anatomy)">horn</a>, or even <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> and include the <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornet" title="Cornet">cornet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_horn" title="Natural horn">horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombone</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carse2002_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carse2002-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flute">Flute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">Flute</a></div>
<p>A flute is a type of aerophone, as is the <a href="/wiki/Eunuch_flute" title="Eunuch flute">Eunuch flute</a>, also referred to as a mirliton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nzewi_Nzewi_2007_p._98-4">[4]</a></sup> A flute is an aerophone or <a href="/wiki/Reedless_wind_instrument" title="Reedless wind instrument">reedless wind instrument</a> that produces its sound from the flow of air across an opening. According to the instrument classification of <a href="/wiki/Hornbostel%E2%80%93Sachs" title="Hornbostel–Sachs">Hornbostel–Sachs</a>, flutes are categorized as <a href="/wiki/Edge-blown_aerophones" title="Edge-blown aerophones">edge-blown aerophones</a>. Aside from the voice, flutes are the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic_flutes" title="Paleolithic flutes">earliest known musical instruments</a>. A number of flutes dating to about 43,000 to 35,000 years ago have been found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swabian_Alb" title="Swabian Alb">Swabian Alb</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. These flutes demonstrate that a developed musical tradition existed from the earliest period of modern human presence in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jhevol_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-jhevol-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reed">Reed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reed_aerophones" title="Reed aerophones">Reed aerophones</a></div>
<p>A reed aerophone is a <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instrument</a> that produces sound by the player's breath being directed against a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reed_(instrument)" title="Reed (instrument)">lamella</a> or pair of lamellae which periodically interrupt the airflow and cause the air to be set in motion.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brass">Brass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Brass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">Brass instrument</a></div>
<p>A brass aerophone is a <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instrument</a> that produces sound by <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_resonance" title="Sympathetic resonance">sympathetic vibration</a> of air in a tubular <a href="/wiki/Resonator" title="Resonator">resonator</a> in sympathy with the vibration of the player's lips. Brass instruments are also called <i>labrosones</i>, literally meaning "lip-vibrated instruments".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> There are several factors involved in producing different <a href="/wiki/Pitch_of_brass_instruments" title="Pitch of brass instruments">pitches on a brass instrument</a>. Slides, <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">valves</a>, crooks, or keys are used to change vibratory length of tubing, thus changing the available <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_series_(music)" title="Harmonic series (music)">harmonic series</a>, while the player's <a href="/wiki/Embouchure" title="Embouchure">embouchure</a>, lip tension and air flow serve to select the specific harmonic produced from the available series.</p>
