<p><b>Motoo Kimura</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">木村 資生</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kimura Motoo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, November 13, 1924 – November 13, 1994)</span> was a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologist</a> best known for introducing the <a href="/wiki/Neutral_theory_of_molecular_evolution" title="Neutral theory of molecular evolution">neutral theory of molecular evolution</a> in 1968, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Tomoko_Ohta" title="Tomoko Ohta">Tomoko Ohta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimura68_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kimura68-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He became one of the most influential theoretical <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population geneticists</a>. He is remembered in genetics for his innovative use of <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_equation" title="Diffusion equation">diffusion equations</a> to calculate the probability of fixation of beneficial, deleterious, or neutral <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">alleles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watterson96_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watterson96-4">[4]</a></sup> Combining theoretical population genetics with <a href="/wiki/Molecular_evolution" title="Molecular evolution">molecular evolution</a> data, he also developed the neutral theory of molecular evolution in which <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a> is the main force changing <a href="/wiki/Allele_frequency" title="Allele frequency">allele frequencies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohta96_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ohta96-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_F._Crow" title="James F. Crow">James F. Crow</a>, himself a renowned population geneticist, considered Kimura to be one of the two greatest evolutionary geneticists, along with <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Mal%C3%A9cot" title="Gustave Malécot">Gustave Malécot</a>, after the great trio of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_evolutionary_synthesis" title="Modern evolutionary synthesis">Modern evolutionary synthesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.B.S._Haldane" title="J.B.S. Haldane">J.B.S. Haldane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewall_Wright" title="Sewall Wright">Sewall Wright</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crow95_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crow95-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motoo_Kimura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kimura was born in <a href="/wiki/Okazaki,_Aichi" title="Okazaki, Aichi">Okazaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aichi_Prefecture" title="Aichi Prefecture">Aichi Prefecture</a>. From an early age he was very interested in botany, though he also excelled at mathematics (teaching himself geometry and other maths during a lengthy convalescence due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning">food poisoning</a>). After entering a selective high school in <a href="/wiki/Nagoya" title="Nagoya">Nagoya</a>, Kimura focused on plant morphology and cytology; he worked in the laboratory of M. Kumazawa studying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosome_structure" title="Chromosome structure">chromosome structure</a> of <a href="/wiki/Liliaceae" title="Liliaceae">lilies</a>. With Kumazawa, he also discovered how to connect his interests in botany and mathematics: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biometry" title="Biometry">biometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Kimura left high school early to enter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_Imperial_University" title="Kyoto Imperial University">Kyoto Imperial University</a> in 1944. On the advice of the prominent geneticist <a href="/wiki/Hitoshi_Kihara" title="Hitoshi Kihara">Hitoshi Kihara</a>, Kimura entered the botany program rather than cytology because the former, in the Faculty of Science rather than Agriculture, allowed him to avoid military duty. He joined Kihara's laboratory after the war, where he studied the introduction of foreign chromosomes into plants and learned the foundations of population genetics. In 1949, Kimura joined the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Genetics" title="National Institute of Genetics">National Institute of Genetics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mishima,_Shizuoka" title="Mishima, Shizuoka">Mishima, Shizuoka</a>. In 1953 he published his first population genetics paper (which would eventually be very influential), describing a "stepping stone" model for population structure that could treat more complex patterns of immigration than <a href="/wiki/Sewall_Wright" title="Sewall Wright">Sewall Wright</a>'s earlier "island model". After meeting visiting American geneticist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Duncan_McDonald_(geneticist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Duncan McDonald (geneticist) (page does not exist)">Duncan McDonald</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Bomb_Casualty_Commission" title="Atomic Bomb Casualty Commission">Atomic Bomb Casualty Commission</a>), Kimura arranged to enter graduate school at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iowa_State_College" title="Iowa State College">Iowa State College</a> in summer 1953 to study with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._L._Lush" title="J. L. Lush">J. L. Lush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kimura soon found Iowa State College too restricting; he moved to the University of Wisconsin to work on stochastic models with <a href="/wiki/James_F._Crow" title="James F. Crow">James F. Crow</a> and join a strong intellectual community of like-minded geneticists, including Newton Morton and most significantly, <a href="/wiki/Sewall_Wright" title="Sewall Wright">Sewall Wright</a>. Near the end of his graduate study, Kimura gave a paper at the 1955 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_Spring_Harbor_Symposium" title="Cold Spring Harbor Symposium">Cold Spring Harbor Symposium</a>; though few were able to understand it (both because of mathematical complexity and Kimura's English pronunciation) it received strong praise from Wright and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.B.S._Haldane" title="J.B.S. Haldane">J.B.S. Haldane</a>. His accomplishments at Wisconsin included a general model for genetic drift, which could accommodate multiple alleles, selection, migration, and mutations, as well as some work based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.A._Fisher" title="R.A. Fisher">R.A. Fisher</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_natural_selection" title="Fundamental theorem of natural selection">fundamental theorem of natural selection</a>. He also built on the work of Wright with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fokker-Planck_equation" title="Fokker-Planck equation">Fokker-Planck equation</a> by introducing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_backward_equation" title="Kolmogorov backward equation">Kolmogorov backward equation</a> to population genetics, allowing the calculation of the probability of a gene to become <a href="/wiki/Fixation_(population_genetics)" title="Fixation (population genetics)">fixed</a> in a population. He received his PhD in 1956, before returning to Japan (where he would remain for the rest of his life, at the National Institute of Genetics).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kimura worked on a wide spectrum of theoretical population genetics problems, many of them in collaboration with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Takeo_Maruyama&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Takeo Maruyama (page does not exist)">Takeo Maruyama</a>. He introduced the "<a href="/wiki/Infinite_alleles_model" title="Infinite alleles model">infinite alleles</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Infinite_sites_model" title="Infinite sites model">infinite sites</a>", and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stepwise_Mutation_Model" title="Stepwise Mutation Model">stepwise mutation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>" models for the study of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a>, both of which would be used widely as the field of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_evolution" title="Molecular evolution">molecular evolution</a> grew alongside the number of available <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptide_sequence" title="Peptide sequence">peptide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_sequence" title="Genetic sequence">genetic sequences</a>. He also created the "ladder model" that could be applied to <a href="/wiki/Electrophoresis" title="Electrophoresis">electrophoresis</a> studies where <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> proteins differ by whole units of charge. An early statement of his approach was published in 1960, in his <i>An Introduction to Population Genetics</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also contributed an important review article on the ongoing controversy over <a href="/wiki/Genetic_load" title="Genetic load">genetic load</a> in 1961.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>1968 marked a turning point in Kimura's career. In that year he introduced the <a href="/wiki/Neutral_theory_of_molecular_evolution" title="Neutral theory of molecular evolution">neutral theory of molecular evolution</a>, the idea that, at the molecular level, the large majority of genetic change is neutral with respect to <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>—making <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a> a primary factor in <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimura83_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kimura83-9">[9]</a></sup> The field of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a> was expanding rapidly, and there was growing tension between advocates of the expanding reductionist field and scientists in organismal biology, the traditional domain of evolution. The neutral theory was immediately controversial, receiving support from many molecular biologists and attracting opposition from many evolutionary biologists.</p>
<p>Kimura spent the rest of his life developing and defending the neutral theory. As James Crow put it, "much of Kimura's early work turned out to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preadaptation" title="Preadaptation">pre-adapted</a> for use in the quantitative study of neutral evolution".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup> As new experimental techniques and genetic knowledge became available, Kimura expanded the scope of the neutral theory and created mathematical methods for testing it against the available evidence. Kimura produced a monograph on the neutral theory in 1983, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Neutral_Theory_of_Molecular_Evolution" title="The Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution">The neutral theory of molecular evolution</a></i>, and also worked to promote the theory through popular writings such as <i>My views on evolution</i>, a book that became a best-seller in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimura88_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kimura88-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though difficult to test against alternative selection-centered hypotheses, the neutral theory has become part of modern approaches to molecular evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nei87_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nei87-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crow96_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crow96-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, Kimura received the <a href="/wiki/Darwin_Medal" title="Darwin Medal">Darwin Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>, and the following year he was made a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society">Foreign Member of the Royal Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup> He was married to Hiroko Kimura. They had one child, a son, Akio, and a granddaughter, Hanako.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steen1996_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steen1996-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steen2014_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steen2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<li>1968 - <a href="/wiki/Japan_Academy_Prize_(academics)" title="Japan Academy Prize (academics)">Japan Academy Prize</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crow95_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Crow95-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
