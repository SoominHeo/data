<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Military order (monastic society)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>military order</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Militaris ordinis</i></span>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" title="Chivalric order">chivalric order</a> with <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> purpose originally established as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_church" title="Catholic church">Catholic</a> religious societies during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> for protection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> against violent <a href="/wiki/Persecution_of_Christians" title="Persecution of Christians">persecution</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_conquests" title="Islamic conquests">Islamic conquests</a> (623–) in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, as well as by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltic_paganism" title="Baltic paganism">Baltic paganism</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>.</p>
<p>Most members, often titled <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">Knights</a>, were and still are <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">laymen</a>, and not prelates, yet cooperating with the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, sometimes even taking <a href="/wiki/Religious_vows" title="Religious vows">religious vows</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty,_chastity,_and_obedience" title="Poverty, chastity, and obedience">poverty, chastity, and obedience</a>, according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monastic" title="Monastic">monastic</a> ideals. As such, it was in the military orders that the Medieval concept of <a href="/wiki/Chivalry" title="Chivalry">chivalry</a> reached its apogee in an exceptionate fusion under <a href="/wiki/Just_war_theory" title="Just war theory">Just war theory</a> of military discipline and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_virtues" title="Christian virtues">Christian virtues</a>.</p>
<p>Prominent examples include the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Outremer" title="Outremer">Outremer</a>, as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltics" title="Baltics">Baltics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orb_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-orb-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many military orders were suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> around the end of the Middle Ages, with few new recognised establishments afterwards. However, some persisted longer in its original functions, only later evolving into purely <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> and/or ceremonial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" title="Chivalric order">chivalric orders</a> with charitable aims in modern times, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Military_Order_of_Malta" title="Sovereign Military Order of Malta">Sovereign Military Order of Malta</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre" title="Order of the Holy Sepulchre">Order of the Holy Sepulchre</a>, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_orders_of_knighthood" title="Papal orders of knighthood">Papal orders of knighthood</a> conferred still today. Notably, the Teutonic Order became exclusively monastic except a limited associated confraternity of honorary Knights.</p>
<p>Today a Western <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural heritage</a> and legacy, besides those officially recognised by the Holy See, parallel honorific institutions of unilateral Catholic adherence exist in continuous or revived forms among a few current and former European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_house" title="Royal house">royal houses</a>.</p>
<p>These traditions in turn eventually influenced honorific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_(honour)" title="Order (honour)">orders</a> of modern states today, including that of secular <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Order_of_merit" title="Order of merit">orders of merit</a>, and other parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honour_system" title="Honour system">honour systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phaleristics" title="Phaleristics">phaleristics</a>.</p>
<p>Moreover, it inspired many subsequent modern era secular Western cultural phenonomens, such as <a href="/wiki/Fraternity" title="Fraternity">fraternities</a>, or brotherhoods, such as the <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasonry</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_order_(monastic_society)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Already in 1053, for the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Civitate" title="Battle of Civitate">Battle of Civitate</a> the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Knights_of_Saint_Peter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Knights of Saint Peter (page does not exist)">Knights of Saint Peter</a> (<i>Milites Sancti Petri</i>) was founded as a <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_IX" title="Pope Leo IX">Pope Leo IX</a> to counter the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The larger threat that would definitively establish the tradition, however, came from the east. In response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_conquests" title="Islamic conquests">Islamic conquests</a> of the former <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_Holy_Land" title="Christianity in the Holy Land">Christianity in the Holy Land</a>, numerous Catholic military orders were set up following the <a href="/wiki/First_Crusade" title="First Crusade">First Crusade</a>. The founding of such orders suited the Catholic church's plan of channelising the devotion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_nobility" title="European nobility">European nobility</a>, and also complemented the <a href="/wiki/Peace_and_Truce_of_God" title="Peace and Truce of God">Peace and Truce of God</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orb_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-orb-1">[1]</a></sup> The foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> in 1118 provided the first in a series of tightly organised military forces for the purpose of fighting invading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_conquests" title="Islamic conquests">Islamic conquests</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> — see the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> — as well as Islamic invaders and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagan" title="Pagan">pagan</a> tribes in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>.</p>
<p>The first secularised military order was the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_George_(Kingdom_of_Hungary)" title="Order of Saint George (Kingdom of Hungary)">Order of Saint George</a>, founded in 1326 by the King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Hungary" title="Charles I of Hungary">Charles I of Hungary</a>, on which he made all the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_nobility" title="Hungarian nobility">Hungarian nobility</a> swear loyalty to him. The next secular order which is known to appear was the Order of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Band" title="Knights of the Band">Knights of the Band</a>", founded in 1332 by the King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso XI of Castile">Alfonso XI of Castile</a>. Both orders existed only for about a century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_order_(monastic_society)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original features of the military orders were the combination of <a href="/wiki/Consecrated_life" title="Consecrated life">religious</a> and military ways of life. Some of them, like the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Saint_Thomas" title="Knights of Saint Thomas">Knights of Saint Thomas</a>, also had charitable purposes and cared for the sick and poor. However, they were not purely male institutions, as <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nuns</a> could attach themselves as <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convents</a> of the orders. One significant feature of the military orders is that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_hierarchy" title="Catholic Church hierarchy">clerical</a> brothers could be, and indeed often were, subordinate to non-ordained brethren.</p>
<p>In 1818, the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_studies" title="Oriental studies">orientalist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Hammer" title="Joseph von Hammer">Joseph von Hammer</a> compared the Catholic military orders, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a>, with certain Islamic models such as the <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shia Islamic</a> sect of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hashshashin" title="Hashshashin">Assassins</a>. In 1820, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Antonio_Conde" title="José Antonio Conde">José Antonio Conde</a> suggested they were modeled on the <a href="/wiki/Ribat" title="Ribat">ribat</a>, a fortified religious institution which brought together a religious or hospital way of life with fighting the enemies of Islam. However popular such views may have become, others have criticised this view, suggesting there were no such ribats around the <a href="/wiki/Outremer" title="Outremer">Outremer</a> until after the military orders had been founded.</p>
<p>Yet, the innovation of the role and function of the military orders has sometimes been obscured by the concentration on their military exploits in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prussia_(Baltic)" title="Prussia (Baltic)">Prussia</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltics" title="Baltics">Baltics</a>. In fact, they had extensive holdings and staff throughout <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>. The majority were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laymen" title="Laymen">laymen</a>. They provided a conduit for cultural and technical innovation, for example the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling</a> into <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a>, or the banking facilities of the Knights Templars.</p>
<p>Because of the necessity to have a <a href="/wiki/Standing_army" title="Standing army">standing army</a>, the military orders were founded, being adopted as the fourth <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious_vows" title="Religious vows">religious vows</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_military_orders">List of military orders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_order_(monastic_society)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: List of military orders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These are military orders listed chronologically according to their dates of foundation and extinction, sometimes approximate due to scarce sources, and/or repeated suppressions by Papal or royal authourities. Their militarisation may vary from case to case, the foundation of an order, its ecclesiastical approval, and occurring on different times and for different purposes. Presently active institutions are listed in consideration with their legitimacy according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Orders_of_Chivalry" title="International Commission on Orders of Chivalry">International Commission on Orders of Chivalry</a>.</p>
<p>They are divided into international and national according to their adherence, mission, and enrolment, disregarding the extent of eventual gradual geographical distribution outside of their region of concern.</p>

1700/<br/>
20th century</td>
<td>Historically both religious and chivalric. In 1692 in France, King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a> merged it with his own <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Our_Lady_of_Mount_Carmel" title="Order of Our Lady of Mount Carmel">Order of Our Lady of Mount Carmel</a>. The remaining organisation was <a href="/wiki/Edict" title="Edict">edicted</a> in 1700 as purely religious order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Offshoots of the order in France survived into the 20th century.</td>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_development">Modern development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_order_(monastic_society)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A few of the institutions survived into honorific and/or charitable organisations, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_orders_of_knighthood" title="Papal orders of knighthood">papal orders of knighthood</a>.</p>
<p>While other contemporary Catholic societies may share some military organisational features and ideology, such as the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Society of Jesus</a> (1540),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> they differ from the medieval military orders in absence of military purposes or potential.</p>
<p>As for several national, state and even dynastic military <a href="/wiki/Order_of_merit" title="Order of merit">orders of merit</a>, such as the Dutch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_Order_of_William" title="Military Order of William">Military Order of William</a> and the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Military_Order_of_Maria_Theresa" title="Military Order of Maria Theresa">Military Order of Maria Theresa</a>, they are not military orders other than nominally.</p>
<p>Echoing the medieval institutions, however, it is possible for modern orders to be founded explicitly as a military order, for instance the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Military_Order_of_Loyalty&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Military Order of Loyalty (page does not exist)">Military Order of Loyalty</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Orden Militar de la Constancia</i></span>), founded in 1946 by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_protectorate_in_Morocco" title="Spanish protectorate in Morocco">Spanish protectorate in Morocco</a>. Awarded to both Spanish and Moroccan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_officer" title="Military officer">military officers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldiers</a>, the single-class order was abolished in 1956.</p>
<p>In contrast, inspired by the legacy of the original military orders, besides legitimate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" title="Chivalric order">chivalric orders</a>, in addition, vast modern imitations flourish, referred to as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-styled_orders" title="Self-styled orders">self-styled orders</a>".</p>
