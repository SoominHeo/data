<p><i><b>The Giver</b></i> is a 1993 American <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">young adult</a> utopian/dystopian novel by <a href="/wiki/Lois_Lowry" title="Lois Lowry">Lois Lowry</a>. It is set in a society which at first appears to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utopian_society" title="Utopian society">utopian</a> but is revealed to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dystopian_fiction" title="Dystopian fiction">dystopian</a> as the story progresses. The novel follows a 12-year-old boy named Jonas. The society has eliminated pain and strife by converting to "Sameness", a plan that has also eradicated <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional</a> depth from their lives. Jonas is selected to inherit the position of Receiver of Memory, the person who stores all the past memories of the time before Sameness, as there may be times where one must draw upon the wisdom gained from history to aid the community's decision making. Jonas struggles with concepts of all the new emotions and things introduced to him: whether they are inherently good, evil, or in between, and whether it is even possible to have one without the other. The Community lacks any color, memory, climate, or <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a>, all in an effort to preserve structure, order, and a true sense of equality beyond personal individuality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Giver</i> won the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Medal</a> and has sold more than 10 million copies worldwide as of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In Australia, Canada, and the United States, it is on many <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">middle school</a> reading lists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but it is also frequently <a href="/wiki/Challenge_(literature)" title="Challenge (literature)">challenged</a> and it ranked number 11 on the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> list of the most challenged books of the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A 2012 survey based in the U.S. designated it the fourth-best children's novel of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLJChapter2012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SLJChapter2012-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, a <a href="/wiki/The_Giver_(film)" title="The Giver (film)">film adaptation</a> was released, starring <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges" title="Jeff Bridges">Jeff Bridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brenton_Thwaites" title="Brenton Thwaites">Brenton Thwaites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The novel forms a loose quartet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> with three other books set in the same future era, known as <a href="/wiki/The_Giver_Quartet" title="The Giver Quartet">The Giver Quartet</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Gathering_Blue" title="Gathering Blue">Gathering Blue</a></i> (2000), <i><a href="/wiki/Messenger_(novel)" title="Messenger (novel)">Messenger</a></i> (2004), and <i><a href="/wiki/Son_(novel)" title="Son (novel)">Son</a></i> (2012).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jonas, a 12-year-old boy, lives in a Community isolated from all except a few similar towns, where everyone from small infants to the Chief Elder has an assigned role. With the annual Ceremony of Twelve upcoming, he is nervous, for there he will be assigned his life's work. He seeks reassurance from his father, a Nurturer (who cares for the new babies, who are genetically engineered and so Jonas's parents are not related to him) and his mother, an official in the Department of Justice, and is told the Elders, who assign the children their careers, are always right.</p>
<p>The day finally arrives, and Jonas is assembled with his classmates in order of date of birth. All of the Community is present, and the Chief Elder presides. Jonas is stunned when his turn is passed by, and he is increasingly conspicuous and agonized until he is alone. The Chief Elder then explains that Jonas has not been given a normal assignment, but instead has been selected as the next Receiver of Memory, to be trained by the current one, who sits among the Elders, staring at Jonas, and who shares with the boy unusual pale eyes. The position of Receiver has high status, and Jonas quickly finds himself growing distant from his classmates, including his close friends Asher and Fiona. The rules Jonas receive further separate him, as they allow him no time to play with his friends. They also allow him to lie and withhold his feelings from his family, something generally not allowed in the regimented Community.</p>
<p>Once he begins it, Jonas's training makes clear his uniqueness, for the Receiver of Memory is just that—a person who bears the burden of the memories from all of history, and who is the only one allowed access to books beyond schoolbooks, and the rulebook issued to every household. The current Receiver, who asks Jonas to call him the Giver, begins the process of transferring those memories to Jonas, for the ordinary person in the Community knows nothing of the past. These memories, and being the only Community member allowed access to books about the past, give the Receiver perspective to advise the Council of Elders. The first memory is of sliding down a snow-covered hill on a sled, pleasantness made shocking by the fact that Jonas has never seen a sled, or snow, or a hill for even the memory of these things has been given up to assure security and conformity (called Sameness). Even color has been surrendered, and the Giver shows Jonas a rainbow. Less pleasantly, he gives Jonas memories of hunger and war, things alien to the boy. Hanging over Jonas's training is the fact that the Giver once before had an apprentice, named Rosemary, but the boy finds his parents and the Giver reluctant to discuss what happened to her.</p>
<p>Jonas's father is concerned about an infant at the Nurturing Center who is failing to thrive, and has received special permission to bring him home at night. The baby's name will be Gabriel if he grows strong enough to be assigned to a family. He has pale eyes, like Jonas and the Giver, and Jonas becomes attached to him, especially when Jonas finds that he is capable of being given memories. If Gabriel does not increase in strength, he will be "released from the Community," also in common speech termed being taken Elsewhere. This has happened to an off-course air pilot, to chronic rule breakers, to elderly people, and to the apprentice Rosemary. After Jonas casually speculates as to life in Elsewhere, the Giver educates him by showing the boy hidden-camera video of Jonas's father doing his job: as two identical community members cannot be allowed, Jonas's father releases the smaller of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identical_twin" title="Identical twin">identical twin</a> newborns by injecting the baby with poison before putting the body in a trash chute. There is no Elsewhere for those not wanted by the Community, those said to have been "released" have been killed.</p>
<p>Considering his father a murderer, Jonas refuses to return home, but the Giver convinces him that without the memories, the people of the Community cannot know that what they have been trained to do is wrong. Rosemary had been unable to endure the darker memories of the past and had chosen release, injecting the poison into her own body. Together, Jonas and the Giver come to the understanding that the time for change is now, that the Community has lost its way and must have its memories returned. The only way to make this happen is if Jonas leaves the Community, at which time the memories he has been given will flood back into the people, as did the relatively few memories Rosemary had been given. Jonas wants the Giver to escape with him, but the Giver insists that he will be needed to help the people manage the memories, or they will destroy themselves. Once the Community is re-established along new lines, the Giver plans to join his daughter, Rosemary, in death.</p>
<p>The Giver devises a plot in which Jonas will escape beyond the boundaries of the Communities. The Giver will make it appear as if Jonas drowned in the river so that the search for him will be limited. The plan is scuttled when Jonas learns that Gabriel will be "released" the following morning, and he feels he has no choice but to escape with the infant. Their escape is fraught with danger, and the two are near death from cold and starvation when they reach the border of what Jonas believes must be Elsewhere. Using his ability to "see beyond," a gift that he does not quite understand, he finds a sled waiting for him at the top of a snowy hill. He and Gabriel ride the sled down towards a house filled with colored lights and warmth and love and a Christmas tree, and for the first time he hears something he believes must be music. The ending is ambiguous, with Jonas depicted as experiencing symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>. This leaves his and Gabriel's future unresolved. However, their fate is revealed in <i><a href="/wiki/Gathering_Blue" title="Gathering Blue">Gathering Blue</a></i> and in <i><a href="/wiki/Messenger_(novel)" title="Messenger (novel)">Messenger</a></i>, companion novels written much later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_significance_and_criticism">Literary significance and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary significance and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While critical reception of <i>The Giver</i> has been mixed, the novel has found a home in "City Reads" programs, library-sponsored reading clubs on citywide or larger scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some reviewers have commented that the story lacks originality and is not likely to stand up to the sort of probing literary criticism used in "serious" circles, while others argue that books appealing to a young-adult audience are critical for building a developing reader's appetite for reading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Karen Ray, writing in <i>The New York Times</i>, detects "occasional logical lapses", but adds that the book "is sure to keep older children reading".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Young adult fiction author <a href="/wiki/Debra_Doyle" title="Debra Doyle">Debra Doyle</a> was more critical, stating that "Personal taste aside, <i>The Giver</i> fails the Plausibility Test", and that "Things are the way they are (in the novel) because The Author is Making A Point; things work out the way they do because The Author's Point Requires It".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natalie_Babbitt" title="Natalie Babbitt">Natalie Babbitt</a> of <i>The Washington Post</i> was more forgiving, calling Lowry's work "a warning in narrative form", saying:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The story has been told before in a variety of forms—<a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">Ray Bradbury</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_451" title="Fahrenheit 451">Fahrenheit 451</a></i> comes to mind—but not, to my knowledge, for children. It's well worth telling, especially by a writer of Lowry's great skill. If it is exceedingly fragile—if, in other words, some situations do not survive that well-known <a href="/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief" title="Suspension of disbelief">suspension of disbelief</a>—well, so be it. <i>The Giver</i> has things to say that cannot be said too often, and I hope there will be many, many young people who will be willing to listen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards.2C_nominations.2C_and_recognition">Awards, nominations, and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards, nominations, and recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Awards"></span><span id="Accolades"></span></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:24em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Now, through the memories, he had seen oceans and mountain lakes and streams that gurgled through woods; and now he saw the familiar wide river beside the path differently. He saw all of the light and color and history it contained and carried in its slow-moving water; and he knew that there was an Elsewhere from which it came, and an Elsewhere to which it was going.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;"><i>The Giver</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELowry131_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELowry131-16">[16]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Lowry won many awards for her work on <i>The Giver</i>, including the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>The 1994 <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Medal</a> – The John Newbery award (Medal) is given by the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Library_Service_to_Children" title="Association for Library Service to Children">Association for Library Service to Children</a>. The award is given for the most distinguished contribution to American literature for children.</li>
<li>The 1994 <a href="/wiki/Regina_Medal" title="Regina Medal">Regina Medal</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>The 1996 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Allen_White_Award" title="William Allen White Award">William Allen White Award</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> listings for "Best Book for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_adult_literature" title="Young adult literature">Young Adults</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALA_Notable_Children%27s_Book" title="ALA Notable Children's Book">ALA Notable Children's Book</a>", and "100 Most Frequently Challenged Books of 1990–2000."</li>
<li>A Boston Globe-Horn Book Honor Book</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Booklist" title="Booklist">Booklist</a> Editors' Choice</li>
<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">School Library Journal</a></i> Best Book of the Year</li>
</ul>
<p>A 2004 study found that <i>The Giver</i> was a common read-aloud book for sixth-graders in schools in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_County,_California" title="San Diego County, California">San Diego County, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Based on a 2007 online poll, the <a href="/wiki/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">National Education Association</a> named it one of "Teachers' Top 100 Books for Children".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEA2007_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEA2007-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2012 it was ranked number four among all-time children's novels in a survey published by <i><a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">School Library Journal</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLJChapter2012_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-SLJChapter2012-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oregon_Children%27s_Theatre" title="Oregon Children's Theatre">Oregon Children's Theatre</a> (<a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland</a>, Oregon) premiered a stage adaptation of <i>The Giver</i> by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Coble" title="Eric Coble">Eric Coble</a> in March 2006. Subsequent productions of Coble's one-hour script have been presented in several American theatres.</p>
<p>In the fall of 1994, actor <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cosby" title="Bill Cosby">Bill Cosby</a> and his ASIS Productions film company established an agreement with Lancit Media Productions to adapt <i>The Giver</i> to film. In the years following, members of the partnership changed and the production team grew in size, but little motion was seen toward making the film. At one point, screenwriter Ed Neumeier was signed to create the screenplay. Later, Neumeier was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Todd_Alcott" title="Todd Alcott">Todd Alcott</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Walden_Media" title="Walden Media">Walden Media</a> became the central production company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diana Basmajian adapted the novel to full-length play format, and <a href="/wiki/Prime_Stage_Theatre" title="Prime Stage Theatre">Prime Stage Theatre</a> produced in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Actor <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rifkin" title="Ron Rifkin">Ron Rifkin</a> reads the text for the <a href="/wiki/Audiobook" title="Audiobook">audiobook</a> edition.</p>
<p>A PC computer game based on the novel was planned by Edutainment Software company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8derbund" title="Brøderbund">Brøderbund</a> in 1995, however it was plagued by delays. Eventually the game was scrapped following the company's sale to <a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a>, however some audio clips of the game (the titular Giver was fully voice acted by American composer <a href="/wiki/Ry_Cooder" title="Ry Cooder">Ry Cooder</a>) have made their way into other media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Lyric Opera of Kansas City and the Minnesota Opera co-commissioned and premiered a new opera by Susan Kander based on the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It was presented in Kansas City in January and Minneapolis on 27–29 April 2012, and was webcast on 18 May 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Giver_(film)" title="The Giver (film)">The Giver (film)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/The_Giver_(film)" title="The Giver (film)">film adaptation</a> was given the <a href="/wiki/Green-light" title="Green-light">green light</a> in December 2012. Jeff Bridges had wanted to film the futuristic novel for several years, but encountered obstacles when Warner Bros. bought the rights in 2007. <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges" title="Jeff Bridges">Jeff Bridges</a> plays the title character<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Brenton_Thwaites" title="Brenton Thwaites">Brenton Thwaites</a> in the role of Jonas. <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katie_Holmes" title="Katie Holmes">Katie Holmes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odeya_Rush" title="Odeya Rush">Odeya Rush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Monaghan" title="Cameron Monaghan">Cameron Monaghan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Skarsg%C3%A5rd" title="Alexander Skarsgård">Alexander Skarsgård</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Swift" title="Taylor Swift">Taylor Swift</a> round out the rest of the main cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It was released in North America on August 15, 2014. Jeff Bridges has said he had wanted to make the film for nearly 20 years, but originally wanted to direct it with his father <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Bridges" title="Lloyd Bridges">Lloyd Bridges</a> in the title role. The elder Bridges's 1998 death cancelled that plan and the film languished in <a href="/wiki/Development_hell" title="Development hell">development hell</a> for another 15 years.</p>
