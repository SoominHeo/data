<p><b>Guillaume Henri Dufour</b> (15 September 1787 – 14 July 1875) was a <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> army officer, <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridge</a> <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographer</a>. He served under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a> and held the office of <a href="/wiki/General_(Switzerland)" title="General (Switzerland)">General</a> four times in career, with the first being in 1847 when he led the Swiss forces to victory against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonderbund" title="Sonderbund">Sonderbund</a>. Dufour presided over the <a href="/wiki/First_Geneva_Convention" title="First Geneva Convention">First Geneva Convention</a> which established the <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">International Red Cross</a>. He was founder and president of the <a href="/wiki/Swisstopo" title="Swisstopo">Swiss Federal Office of Topography</a> from 1838 to 1865.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dufourspitze" title="Dufourspitze">Dufourspitze</a> (the highest <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> in Switzerland) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monte_Rosa_Massif" title="Monte Rosa Massif">Monte Rosa Massif</a> is named after Dufour.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guillaume_Henri_Dufour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dufour was born in Konstanz,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-1">[1]</a></sup> where his parents were temporarily exiled from <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>. His father Bénédict was a Genevan watchmaker and farmer, who sent his son to school in Geneva, where he studied drawing and medicine. In 1807, Dufour travelled to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> to join the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique" title="École Polytechnique">École Polytechnique</a>, then a military academy. He studied descriptive geometry under <a href="/wiki/Jean_Nicolas_Pierre_Hachette" title="Jean Nicolas Pierre Hachette">Jean Nicolas Pierre Hachette</a>, and graduated fifth in his class in 1809, going on to study military engineering at the École d'Application. In 1810, he was sent to help defend <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a> against the British, and spent his time mapping the island's old fortifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1814, he had returned to France, and was awarded the Croix de la <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27Honneur" title="Légion d'Honneur">Légion d'Honneur</a> for his work repairing fortifications at <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyons</a>. In 1817, he returned to Geneva to become commander of the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Geneva" title="Canton of Geneva">Canton of Geneva</a>'s military engineers, as well as a professor of mathematics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Geneva" title="University of Geneva">University of Geneva</a>. His duties included preparing a map of the Canton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dufour remained a General in the army. Among the officers serving under him was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_III_of_France" title="Napoleon III of France">Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, nephew of the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Emperor</a>.</p>
<p>In 1847 the Catholic cantons of Switzerland attempted to form a separate alliance of their own, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonderbund" title="Sonderbund">Sonderbund</a>, effectively splitting from the rest of the country. The <a href="/wiki/Tagsatzung" title="Tagsatzung">Tagsatzung</a> appointed Dufour <a href="/wiki/General_(Switzerland)" title="General (Switzerland)">General</a> on 21 October 1847,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HLS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HLS-3">[3]</a></sup> and he led the federal army of 100,000 and defeated the Sonderbund under <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johann-Ulrich_von_Salis-Soglio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johann-Ulrich von Salis-Soglio (page does not exist)">Johann-Ulrich von Salis-Soglio</a> in a campaign that lasted only from November 3 to November 29, and claimed fewer than a hundred victims. He ordered his troops to spare the injured.</p>
<p>In addition to serving in the position of <a href="/wiki/General_(Switzerland)" title="General (Switzerland)">General</a> in 1847 due to the Sonderbund War,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HLS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HLS-3">[3]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Assembly_(Switzerland)" title="Federal Assembly (Switzerland)">Federal Assembly</a> appointed him to the same position in August 1849 due to the <a href="/wiki/Baden_Revolution" title="Baden Revolution">Baden Revolution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> on 27 December 1856 due to the <a href="/wiki/Neuch%C3%A2tel_Crisis" title="Neuchâtel Crisis">Neuchâtel Crisis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and in 1859 due to the <a href="/wiki/Second_Italian_War_of_Independence" title="Second Italian War of Independence">Second Italian War of Independence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1850 the mountaineer and topographer <a href="/wiki/Johann_Coaz" title="Johann Coaz">Johann Coaz</a> served as his private secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kev_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-kev-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1863 he was part of a committee which, under <a href="/wiki/Henry_Dunant" title="Henry Dunant">Henry Dunant</a> led to the foundation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross" title="International Red Cross">International Red Cross</a>.</p>
<p>On 16 July 1875, 60,000 persons participated at Dufour's burial at <a href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_des_Rois" title="Cimetière des Rois">Cimetière de Plainpalais</a> in Geneva.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Antoine_Bridge">Saint Antoine Bridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guillaume_Henri_Dufour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Saint Antoine Bridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="156" data-file-width="672" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b8/Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png/300px-Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b8/Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png/450px-Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b8/Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png/600px-Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saint-Antoine_Bridge.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saint Antoine Bridge as pictured by Drewry, 1832</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dufour acted as state engineer from 1817, although he was not officially appointed as such until 1828. His work included rebuilding a pumping station, quays and bridges, and he arranged the first steam boat on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">Lake Geneva</a> as well as the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas</a> streetlights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scientist <a href="/wiki/Marc-Auguste_Pictet" title="Marc-Auguste Pictet">Marc-Auguste Pictet</a> had visited <a href="/wiki/Marc_Seguin" title="Marc Seguin">Marc Seguin</a>'s temporary wire-cable <a href="/wiki/Simple_suspension_bridge" title="Simple suspension bridge">simple suspension bridge</a> at <a href="/wiki/Annonay" title="Annonay">Annonay</a> in 1822, the first wire-cable bridge in the world, and published details in Switzerland. He joined with others to promote a new bridge across the Genevan fortifications, consulting with Seguin on how it might be built, receiving back a series of sketches. Dufour developed the design in late 1822, proposing a two-span suspension bridge using wire cables - this would become the first permanent wire cable suspension bridge in the world. The design used three cables on each side of an iron and timber bridge deck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-1">[1]</a></sup> The cables stretched 131 feet between the towers, although the largest span was only 109 feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drewry_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drewry-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Memorials are at:</p>
<ul>
<li>Equestrian statue (1884) by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Lanz" title="Alfred Lanz">Alfred Lanz</a>, at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Place_Neuve&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Place Neuve (page does not exist)">Place Neuve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a></li>
<li>Two plaques on his birth house at Wessenbergstraße 14 (formerly Plattengasse), <a href="/wiki/Konstanz" title="Konstanz">Konstanz</a>, Germany <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Plaque at the building where he lived from 1826 to 1845 at Geneva<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Plaque at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Montrottier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Château de Montrottier (page does not exist)">Château de Montrottier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lovagny" title="Lovagny">Lovagny</a>, France <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Plaque at 22, rue Saint-Victor, Carouge, commemorating its use as office of topography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Bronze bust, made by Fonderie Leuba, B. Brasseur, succ. at Army Training Center, Lucerne</li>
</ul>
<p>His home from 1845-1875 at Rue de Contamines, Geneva, is preserved by a foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous cities and towns in Switzerland have streets named for him: <i>rue du Général-Dufour</i> in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Chaux-de-Fonds" title="La Chaux-de-Fonds">La Chaux-de-Fonds</a>; <i>via Gen. Henri Dufour</i> in <a href="/wiki/Chiasso" title="Chiasso">Chiasso</a>; <i>rue du Général- Dufour/General-Dufour-Strasse</i> in <a href="/wiki/Biel/Bienne" title="Biel/Bienne">Biel/Bienne</a>; <i>Dufourstrasse</i> in <a href="/wiki/Aarau" title="Aarau">Aarau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bern" title="Bern">Bern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biberist" title="Biberist">Biberist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lenzburg" title="Lenzburg">Lenzburg</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luzern" title="Luzern">Luzern</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rorschach,_St._Gallen" title="Rorschach, St. Gallen">Rorschach</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Gallen" title="St. Gallen">St. Gallen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thun" title="Thun">Thun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weinfelden" title="Weinfelden">Weinfelden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wettingen" title="Wettingen">Wettingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wil" title="Wil">Wil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zollikon" title="Zollikon">Zollikon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>; <i>via Dufour</i> in <a href="/wiki/Lugano" title="Lugano">Lugano</a>. There is also <i>Dufourplatz</i> in <a href="/wiki/Zollikon" title="Zollikon">Zollikon</a>.</p>
