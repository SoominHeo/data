<p><b>Vorbis</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source_software" title="Free and open-source software">free and open-source software</a> project headed by the <a href="/wiki/Xiph.Org_Foundation" title="Xiph.Org Foundation">Xiph.Org Foundation</a> (formerly Xiphophorus company). The project produces an <a href="/wiki/Audio_coding_format" title="Audio coding format">audio coding format</a> and software reference encoder/decoder (<a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codec</a>) for <a href="/wiki/Lossy_compression" title="Lossy compression">lossy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_compression_(data)" title="Audio compression (data)">audio compression</a>. Vorbis is most commonly used in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Ogg" title="Ogg">Ogg</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_container_format" title="Digital container format">container format</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and it is therefore often referred to as <b>Ogg Vorbis</b>.</p>
<p>Vorbis is a continuation of audio compression development started in 1993 by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Montgomery" title="Chris Montgomery">Chris Montgomery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-advogado_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-advogado-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xiphname_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-xiphname-9">[9]</a></sup> Intensive development began following a September 1998 letter from the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> announcing plans to charge licensing fees for the <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> audio format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Vorbis project started as part of the Xiphophorus company's Ogg project (also known as OggSquish multimedia project).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ogg-squish-dvorak_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ogg-squish-dvorak-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oggsquish-project_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-oggsquish-project-13">[13]</a></sup> Chris Montgomery began work on the project and was assisted by a growing number of other developers. They continued refining the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> until the Vorbis file format was frozen for 1.0 in May 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vorbis-freeze-monty_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-vorbis-freeze-monty-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xiph-vorbis-freeze_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-xiph-vorbis-freeze-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vorbis-freeze_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-vorbis-freeze-14">[14]</a></sup> Originally licensed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LGPL" title="LGPL">LGPL</a>, in 2001 the Vorbis license was changed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD_license" title="BSD license">BSD license</a> to encourage adoption with endorsement of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Stallman" title="Richard Stallman">Richard Stallman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A stable version (1.0) of the reference software was released on July 19, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Xiph.Org Foundation maintains a <a href="/wiki/Reference_implementation" title="Reference implementation">reference implementation</a>, <b>libvorbis</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-release-1.3.4_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-release-1.3.4-20">[20]</a></sup> There are also some fine-tuned <a href="/wiki/Fork_(software_development)" title="Fork (software development)">forks</a>, most notably <b>aoTuV</b>, that offer better audio quality, particularly at low bitrates. These improvements are periodically merged back into the reference codebase.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Vorbis" is named after a <i><a href="/wiki/Discworld" title="Discworld">Discworld</a></i> character, <a href="/wiki/Religions_of_the_Discworld#Vorbis" title="Religions of the Discworld">Exquisitor Vorbis</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Gods" title="Small Gods">Small Gods</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett" title="Terry Pratchett">Terry Pratchett</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xiphname_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-xiphname-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ogg" title="Ogg">Ogg</a> format, however, is <i>not</i> named after <a href="/wiki/Nanny_Ogg" title="Nanny Ogg">Nanny Ogg</a>, another Discworld character; the name is in fact derived from "ogging", <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargon</a> that arose in the computer game <a href="/wiki/Netrek" title="Netrek">Netrek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xiphname_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-xiphname-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Vorbis format has proven popular among supporters of <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> They argue that its higher fidelity and completely free nature, unencumbered by patents, make it a well-suited replacement for patented and restricted formats like <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a>.</p>

<p>Vorbis has different uses for consumer products. Many <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> titles store in-game audio as Vorbis, including <i><a href="/wiki/Amnesia:_The_Dark_Descent" title="Amnesia: The Dark Descent">Amnesia: The Dark Descent</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto:_San_Andreas" title="Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas">Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Halo:_Combat_Evolved" title="Halo: Combat Evolved">Halo: Combat Evolved</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Minecraft" title="Minecraft">Minecraft</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>, among others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Popular software players support Vorbis playback either natively or through an external <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_(computing)" title="Plug-in (computing)">plugin</a>. A number of websites, including <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, use it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playogg-sites_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-playogg-sites-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Others include <a href="/wiki/Jamendo" title="Jamendo">Jamendo</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mindawn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mindawn (page does not exist)">Mindawn</a>, as well as several national radio stations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playogg-sites_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-playogg-sites-22">[22]</a></sup> like JazzRadio, <a href="/wiki/Absolute_Radio" title="Absolute Radio">Absolute Radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_New_Zealand" title="Radio New Zealand">Radio New Zealand</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Deutschlandradio" title="Deutschlandradio">Deutschlandradio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a> audio streaming service uses Vorbis for its audio streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Also, the French music site Qobuz offers its customers the possibility to download their purchased songs in Vorbis format, as does the American music site <a href="/wiki/Bandcamp" title="Bandcamp">Bandcamp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality">Quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vorbis had been shown to perform significantly better than many other <a href="/wiki/List_of_codecs#Lossy_data_compression" title="List of codecs">lossy audio formats</a> in the past in that it produced smaller files at equivalent or higher quality while retaining computational complexity comparable to other <a href="/wiki/Modified_discrete_cosine_transform" title="Modified discrete cosine transform">MDCT</a> formats such as <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Audio_Coding" title="Advanced Audio Coding">AAC</a> or <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Audio" title="Windows Media Audio">Windows Media Audio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, by 2014, not many further significant tests had been made.</p>
<p>Listening tests have attempted to find the best quality lossy audio codecs at certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitrate" title="Bitrate">bitrates</a>. Some conclusions made by listening tests:</p>
<ul>
<li>Low bitrate (less than 64 kbit/s): the most recent (2007) public multiformat test at 48 kbit/s showed that aoTuV Vorbis had a better quality than <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Audio" title="Windows Media Audio">WMA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Audio_Coding" title="Advanced Audio Coding">LC-AAC</a>, the same quality as <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Audio" title="Windows Media Audio">WMA Professional</a>, and a lower quality than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HE-AAC" title="HE-AAC">HE-AAC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mf48_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mf48-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mid to low bitrates (less than 128 kbit/s down to 64 kbit/s): private tests in 2005 at 80 kbit/s and 96 kbit/s showed that aoTuV Vorbis had a better quality than other lossy audio formats (LC-AAC, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HE-AAC" title="HE-AAC">HE-AAC</a>, <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musepack" title="Musepack">MPC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Audio" title="Windows Media Audio">WMA</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydro80_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-hydro80-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardware_fr_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-hardware_fr-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>High bitrates (greater than 128 kbit/s): most people do not hear significant differences. However, trained listeners can often hear significant differences between codecs at identical bitrates, and aoTuV Vorbis performed better than LC-AAC, MP3, and MPC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydro180_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-hydro180-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Due to the ever-evolving nature of audio codecs, the results of many of these tests have become outdated.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Listening_tests">Listening tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Listening tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Codec_listening_test#Results" title="Codec listening test">Codec listening test § Results</a></div>
<p>Listening tests are normally carried out as <a href="/wiki/ABX_test" title="ABX test">ABX tests</a>, i.e., the listener has to identify an unknown sample X as being A or B, with A (the original) and B (the encoded version) available for reference. The outcome of a test must be statistically significant. This setup ensures that the listener is not biased by his/her expectations, and that the outcome is very unlikely to be the result of chance. If sample X can be identified reliably, the listener can assign a score as a subjective judgment of the quality. Otherwise, the encoded version is considered to be <i>transparent</i>. Below are links to several listening test results.</p>
<ul>
<li>2005, July comparison - AAC vs MP3 vs Vorbis vs WMA at 80 kbit/s. States that Vorbis aoTuV beta 4 is the best encoder for either classical or various music in this bitrate, and that its quality is comparable to the <a href="/wiki/LAME" title="LAME">LAME</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Average_bit_rate" title="Average bit rate">ABR</a> MP3 at 128 kbit/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydro80_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-hydro80-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>2005, August comparison - AAC vs MP3 vs Vorbis vs WMA at 96 kbit/s. States that Vorbis aoTuV beta 4 and AAC are tied as the best encoders for classical music in this bitrate, while aoTuV beta 4 is the best encoder for pop music, even better than LAME at 128 kbit/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardware_fr_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-hardware_fr-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>2005, August comparison - MPC vs Vorbis vs MP3 vs AAC at 180 kbit/s. An <a href="/wiki/Audiophile" title="Audiophile">audiophile</a> listening test, which states that, for classical music, Vorbis aoTuV beta 4 has 93% percent probability of being the best encoder, tied with MPC. MPC is tied with both Vorbis, in the first place, and LAME in the second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydro180_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-hydro180-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>2011, April comparison by Hydrogenaudio - Vorbis vs HE-AAC vs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opus_(codec)" title="Opus (codec)">Opus</a> at 64 kbit/s. Vorbis was on average between the LC-AAC low anchor and Nero HE-AAC, while the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Opus_(audio_format)" title="Opus (audio format)">Opus</a> (by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xiph" title="Xiph">Xiph</a>) was best.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristic_artifacts">Characteristic artifacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Characteristic artifacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with most modern formats, the most consistently cited problem with Vorbis is <a href="/wiki/Pre-echo" title="Pre-echo">pre-echo</a>, a faint copy of a sharp attack that occurs just before the actual sound (this artifact is most obvious when reproducing the sound of <a href="/wiki/Castanets" title="Castanets">castanets</a>).</p>
<p>When the bitrate is too low to encode the audio without perceptible loss, Vorbis exhibits an analog noise-like failure mode, which can be described as reverberations in a room or amphitheater. Vorbis's behavior is due to the <a href="/wiki/Noise_floor" title="Noise floor">noise floor</a> approach to encoding; see <a href="#Technical_details">technical details</a>.</p>

<p>Vorbis is intended for sample rates from 8 kHz telephony to 192 kHz digital masters and a range of channel representations (monaural, polyphonic, stereo, quadraphonic, 5.1, ambisonic, or up to 255 discrete channels).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vorbis-I_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-vorbis-I-37">[37]</a></sup> Given 44.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilohertz" title="Kilohertz">kHz</a> (standard <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CD</a> audio <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_frequency" title="Sampling frequency">sampling frequency</a>) stereo input, the encoder will produce output from roughly 45 to 500 kbit/s (32 to 500 kbit/s for aoTuV tunings) depending on the specified quality setting. Quality setting goes from −0.1 to 1.0 for the Xiph library and −0.2 to 1.0 for aoTuV. Encoding front-ends map these values to an integer-based quality setting that goes from −1 to 10 for the Xiph library and −2 to 10 for aoTuV. Files encoded with a given quality setting should have the same quality of sound in all versions of the encoder, but newer versions should be able to achieve that quality with a lower bitrate. The bit rates mentioned above are only approximate; Vorbis is inherently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_bit_rate" title="Variable bit rate">variable-bitrate</a> (VBR), so bitrate may vary considerably from sample to sample. (It is a free-form variable-bitrate codec and packets have no minimum size, maximum size, or fixed/expected size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vorbis-spec_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-vorbis-spec-3">[3]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>Vorbis aims to be more efficient than MP3, with <i><a href="/wiki/Transparency_(data_compression)" title="Transparency (data compression)">data compression transparency</a></i> being available at lower bitrates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outline_of_coder_algorithm">Outline of coder algorithm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Outline of coder algorithm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vorbis I is a forward-adaptive monolithic transform codec based on the <a href="/wiki/Modified_discrete_cosine_transform" title="Modified discrete cosine transform">modified discrete cosine transform</a> (MDCT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vorbis-mdct_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-vorbis-mdct-38">[38]</a></sup> Vorbis uses the modified discrete cosine transform for converting sound data from the <a href="/wiki/Time_domain" title="Time domain">time domain</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Frequency_domain" title="Frequency domain">frequency domain</a>. The resulting frequency-domain data is broken into <a href="/wiki/Noise_floor" title="Noise floor">noise floor</a> and residue components, and then <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(signal_processing)" title="Quantization (signal processing)">quantized</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entropy_coded" title="Entropy coded">entropy coded</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Codebook" title="Codebook">codebook</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Vector_quantization" title="Vector quantization">vector quantization</a> algorithm. The decompression algorithm reverses these stages. The noise floor approach gives Vorbis its characteristic analog noise-like failure mode when the bitrate is too low to encode the audio without perceptible loss. The sound of compression artifacts at low bitrates can be perhaps described as reverberations in an amphitheater or a room.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuned_versions">Tuned versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tuned versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various tuned versions of the encoder (Garf, aoTuV or MegaMix) attempt to provide better sound at a specified quality setting, usually by dealing with certain problematic waveforms by temporarily increasing the bitrate. Most of the tuned versions of Vorbis attempt to fix the <a href="/wiki/Pre-echo" title="Pre-echo">pre-echo</a> problem and to increase the sound quality of lower quality settings (-q-2 through -q4). Some tuning suggestions created by the Vorbis user community (especially the aoTuV beta 2 tunings) have been incorporated into the 1.1.0 release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bitrate_peeling">Bitrate peeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bitrate peeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Vorbis <i>format</i> supports <a href="/wiki/Bitrate_peeling" title="Bitrate peeling">bitrate peeling</a> for reducing the bitrate of already encoded files without re-encoding, and several experimental implementations exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peeling_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-peeling-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> However, the quality is "unusable",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peeling_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-peeling-40">[40]</a></sup> and markedly inferior to decoding and re-encoding.</p>
<p>The reason that peeling programs degrade quality so severely is that the format does not specify the relative importance of signal data, and existing encoders do not order the data in order of importance, hence peelers cannot easily determine if data is important or not.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Container_formats">Container formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Container formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Container_format_(digital)" title="Container format (digital)">Container format (digital)</a></div>
<p>Vorbis streams can be encapsulated in other media <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Container_format_(digital)" title="Container format (digital)">container formats</a> besides Ogg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A commonly used alternative is <a href="/wiki/Matroska" title="Matroska">Matroska</a>. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/WebM" title="WebM">WebM</a>, a container format based on a subset of Matroska.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xiph-webm_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-xiph-webm-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webm-blog_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-webm-blog-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faq_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-faq-45">[45]</a></sup> Vorbis streams can also be encapsulated in an <a href="/wiki/Real-time_Transport_Protocol" title="Real-time Transport Protocol">RTP</a> payload format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metadata">Metadata</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Metadata">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vorbis <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Vorbis_comment" title="Vorbis comment">Vorbis comments</a>, supports metadata tags similar to those implemented in the <a href="/wiki/ID3" title="ID3">ID3</a> standard for MP3. The metadata is stored in a vector of <a href="/wiki/8-bit_clean" title="8-bit clean">eight-bit-clean</a> strings of arbitrary length and size. The size of the vector and the size of each string in bytes is limited to 2<sup>32</sup>-1 (about 4.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a>, or any positive integer that can be expressed in 32 bits). This vector is stored in the second header packet that begins a Vorbis bitstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The strings are assumed to be encoded as <a href="/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a>. Music tags are typically implemented as strings of the form "[TAG]=[VALUE]", for instance, "ARTIST=The John Smith Band". The tags are case-insensitive, thus typing "ARTIST=The John Smith Band" would be the same as "artist=The John Smith Band". Like the current version of ID3, users and encoding software are free to use whichever tags are appropriate for the content. For example, an encoder could use localized tag labels, live music tracks might contain a "Venue=" tag or files could have multiple genre definitions. Most applications also support common de facto standards such as discnumber and <a href="/wiki/ReplayGain" title="ReplayGain">ReplayGain</a> information.</p>

<p>Knowledge of Vorbis' specifications is in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vorbis.com_FAQ_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vorbis.com_FAQ-5">[5]</a></sup> Concerning the specification itself, the Xiph.Org Foundation reserves the right to set the Vorbis specification and certify compliance. Its libraries are released under the revised 3-clause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD_License" title="BSD License">BSD license</a> and its tools are released under the <a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">GNU General Public License</a>. The libraries were originally released under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GNU_Lesser_General_Public_Licence" title="GNU Lesser General Public Licence">GNU Lesser General Public Licence</a>, but a BSD license was later chosen with the endorsement of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Stallman" title="Richard Stallman">Richard Stallman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Xiph.Org_Foundation" title="Xiph.Org Foundation">Xiph.Org Foundation</a> states that Vorbis, like all its developments, is completely free from the licensing or <a href="/wiki/Software_patent" title="Software patent">patent</a> issues raised by other <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a> formats such as <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a>. Although the Xiph.Org Foundation states it has conducted a patent search that supports its claims, outside parties (notably engineers working on rival formats) have expressed doubt that Vorbis is free of patented technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (August 2016)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Xiph.Org Foundation has not released an official statement on the patent status of Vorbis, pointing out that such a statement is technically impossible due to the number and scope of patents in existence and the questionable validity of many of them. Such issues can only be resolved by a court of law.</p>
<p>Vorbis is supported by several large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" title="Digital audio player">digital audio player</a> manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a>, <a href="/wiki/SanDisk" title="SanDisk">SanDisk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rio_(digital_audio_players)" title="Rio (digital audio players)">Rio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuros_Technology" title="Neuros Technology">Neuros Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cowon" title="Cowon">Cowon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iriver" title="Iriver">iriver</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Support">Support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Tremor_(software)" title="Tremor (software)">Tremor</a></i>, a version of the Vorbis decoder which uses <a href="/wiki/Fixed-point_arithmetic" title="Fixed-point arithmetic">fixed-point arithmetic</a> (rather than <a href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">floating point</a>), was made available to the public on September 2, 2002 (also under a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD_License" title="BSD License">BSD-style license</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Tremor, or platform specific versions based on it, is more suited to implementation on the limited facilities available in commercial portable players. A number of versions that make adjustments for specific platforms and include customized optimizations for given embedded microprocessors have been produced. Several hardware manufacturers have expressed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> intentions to produce Vorbis-compliant devices and new Vorbis devices seem to be appearing at a steady rate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sailfish_OS" title="Sailfish OS">Sailfish OS</a> devices</li>

<li>Digital audio players such as <a href="/wiki/Cowon" title="Cowon">Cowon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cowon_D2" title="Cowon D2">D2</a> and <a href="/wiki/IAUDIO#iAUDIO_X5" title="IAUDIO">iAudio X5</a> ship with Ogg Vorbis support.</li>

<p>Apple's <a href="/wiki/IPod" title="IPod">iPod</a> does not natively support Vorbis but through the use of <a href="/wiki/Rockbox" title="Rockbox">Rockbox</a>, an open-source firmware project, is capable of decoding Vorbis files on certain models. Similar statements apply to other devices capable of running Rockbox, as well. The Xiph.Org Foundation wiki has an up-to-date list of Vorbis-supporting hardware, such as portables, PDAs, and microchips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VorbisHardware_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-VorbisHardware-57">[57]</a></sup> Also see <a href="/wiki/Internet_radio_device" title="Internet radio device">Internet radio device</a> for an overview.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_software">Application software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Application software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Use_of_Ogg_formats_in_HTML5" title="Use of Ogg formats in HTML5">Use of Ogg formats in HTML5</a></div>
<p>Software supporting Vorbis exists for many platforms. The multi-platform open-source <a href="/wiki/VLC_media_player" title="VLC media player">VLC media player</a> and <a href="/wiki/MPlayer" title="MPlayer">MPlayer</a> can play Ogg Vorbis files, as can <a href="/wiki/Winamp" title="Winamp">Winamp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foobar2000" title="Foobar2000">foobar2000</a>. Although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple</a> <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> does not natively support Vorbis, the Xiph.Org Foundation provides a QuickTime component<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuickTime_Components_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-QuickTime_Components-58">[58]</a></sup> which can be used in iTunes and QuickTime on both <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a> (only before OS X 10.9's switch of Quicktime to 64-bit only). <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Player" title="Windows Media Player">Windows Media Player</a> also does not natively support Vorbis; however, DirectShow filters exist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illiminable_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-illiminable-59">[59]</a></sup> to decode Vorbis in Windows Media Player and other Windows multimedia players that support <a href="/wiki/DirectShow" title="DirectShow">DirectShow</a>. Vorbis is also supported in the multi-platform audio editing software <a href="/wiki/Audacity_(audio_editor)" title="Audacity (audio editor)">Audacity</a>, in the multi-platform <a href="/wiki/Multimedia_framework" title="Multimedia framework">multimedia frameworks</a> <a href="/wiki/FFmpeg" title="FFmpeg">FFmpeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/GStreamer" title="GStreamer">GStreamer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helix_(project)" title="Helix (project)">Helix DNA</a>. Vorbis is well-supported on the <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> platform in programs like <a href="/wiki/XMMS" title="XMMS">XMMS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xine" title="Xine">xine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amarok_(software)" title="Amarok (software)">Amarok</a> and many more. A list of Vorbis-supporting software can be found at the Xiph.Org Foundation wiki and Vorbis.com website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> For more information about support in software media players look at <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_audio_player_software#Audio_format_capability" title="Comparison of audio player software">comparison of media players</a>. Users can test these programs using the list of Vorbis audio streams available on the same wiki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="sb0"></span> Some newer <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a> games use Vorbis files renamed with the <a href="/wiki/Filename_extension" title="Filename extension">filename extension</a> <b>.sb0</b>. It can therefore be played using a compatible player, although sometimes one must force a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_rate" title="Sampling rate">sampling rate</a> to hear it correctly. A number of tools are available for extracting sound from <a href="/wiki/File_archiver" title="File archiver">archived files</a> such as the .m4b of <a href="/wiki/Myst_IV:_Revelation" title="Myst IV: Revelation">Myst IV: Revelation</a>.</p>
<p>As originally recommended by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HTML_5" title="HTML 5">HTML 5</a>, these <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browsers</a> natively support Vorbis audio (without a plug-in) using the <code>&lt;audio&gt;</code> element: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox_3.5" title="Mozilla Firefox 3.5">Mozilla Firefox 3.5</a> (and later versions),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gecko191_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-gecko191-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firefox35_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-firefox35-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a> (from version 3.0.182.2),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrome3_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-chrome3-65">[65]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SeaMonkey" title="SeaMonkey">SeaMonkey</a> (from version 2.0).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seamonkey2_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-seamonkey2-66">[66]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Opera_(web_browser)" title="Opera (web browser)">Opera</a> 9.5 experimental video builds released in 2007 and 2008 have only <code>&lt;video&gt;</code> support and play back Vorbis audio included in Ogg video files.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opera_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-opera-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opera-dev_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-opera-dev-68">[68]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opera_10" title="Opera 10">Opera 10.5</a> browser has support for Vorbis audio, <a href="/wiki/WAV" title="WAV">WAVE PCM</a> audio and <a href="/wiki/Theora" title="Theora">Theora</a> video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deployment">Deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorbis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vorbis faces competition from other audio formats, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mp3" title="Mp3">mp3</a>. Though Vorbis is technically superior, mp3 has a far higher public profile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Because Vorbis does not have financial support from large organisations, support for the format is not as widespread, though programs such as <a href="/wiki/Audacity_(audio_editor)" title="Audacity (audio editor)">Audacity</a> can convert to more popular formats,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and support in games has gradually improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
