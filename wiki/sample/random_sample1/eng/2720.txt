<p><b>Jizi</b> or <b>Qizi</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AE%95" title="wikt:箕">箕</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%90" title="wikt:子">子</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Chi-tzu</span></i>, <b>Gija</b> or <b>Kija</b> in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a semi-legendary <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> sage who is said to have ruled <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a> in the 11th century BCE. Early Chinese documents like the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Documents" title="Book of Documents">Book of Documents</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i> described him as a virtuous relative of the <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">last king</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> who was punished for remonstrating with the king. After Shang was overthrown by <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou</a> in the 1040s BCE, he allegedly gave political advice to <a href="/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou" title="King Wu of Zhou">King Wu</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the first Zhou king. Chinese texts from the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (206 BCE – 220 CE) onwards claimed that King Wu enfeoffed Jizi as ruler of Chaoxian (朝鮮, pronounced "Joseon" in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>). According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i> (1st century CE), Jizi brought <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">sericulture</a>, and many other facets of Chinese civilization to Joseon. Gija was <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a>.</p>
<p>Gija (the Korean pronunciation of "Jizi") may have been the object of a state cult in sixth-century <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, and a mausoleum to him was established in <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> in 1102, but the first extant Korean text to mention Gija was the <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i> (1145). Starting in the late thirteenth century, Gija was fully integrated into Korean history, being described as a successor to the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Dangun" title="Dangun">Dangun</a> in the state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Joseon" title="Old Joseon">Old Joseon</a>. Following the spread of <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a> in Korea in the fourteenth century, scholars of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> (est. 1392) promoted Gija as a <a href="/wiki/Culture_hero" title="Culture hero">culture hero</a> who had raised Korean civilization to the same level as China, and Gija became an integral part of Korean cultural identity.</p>
<p>Following the rise of <a href="/wiki/Korean_nationalism" title="Korean nationalism">Korean nationalism</a> in the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, however, Korean intellectuals lost their pride in association with Gija and started to doubt the traditional account of his enfeoffment as ruler of <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Shin_Chaeho" title="Shin Chaeho">Shin Chaeho</a> (1880–1936) questioned the extent of Gija's cultural contributions, in part because Gija, a Chinese, did not fit into Shin's view of Korean history as the history of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_ethnic_nationalism" title="Korean ethnic nationalism">Korean <i>minjok</i></a>, or "race-nation." Postwar Korean scholars in both <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> have also strongly criticized the story of Gija's migration to Korea in the eleventh century BCE.</p>
<p>His family name was Zi/Ja (子) and given name was Xuyu/Suyu (胥餘/서여 xūyú/seoyeo, or 須臾/수유 xūyú/suyu).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_ancient_Chinese_texts">In ancient Chinese texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In ancient Chinese texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Qin_sources">Pre-Qin sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-Qin sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest known mention of Jizi is in the "Mingyi" 明夷 hexagram of the <i><a href="/wiki/I_Ching" title="I Ching">Book of Change</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to other ancient Chinese texts like the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Documents" title="Book of Documents">Book of Documents</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Analects" title="Analects">Analects</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i>, Jizi was a relative of <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou</a>, the last ruler of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">Shang Dynasty</a>, and one of the three wise men of Shang, along with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Weizi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Weizi (page does not exist)">Weizi</a> (微子) and <a href="/wiki/Bi_Gan" title="Bi Gan">Bi Gan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_274_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_274-4">[4]</a></sup> Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> identify him as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Tutor" title="Grand Tutor">Grand Tutor</a> of the king. Jizi was either imprisoned or enslaved for remonstrating against King Zhou's misrule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_274_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_274-4">[4]</a></sup> (One later version states that he pretended to be mad after Bigan had been killed by King Zhou.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After Shang was overthrown by the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a> in the mid eleventh century BC, Jizi was released by <a href="/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou" title="King Wu of Zhou">King Wu</a>, to whom he gave advice on how to rule the new polity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_274_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_274-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>These texts mention neither Joseon nor Jizi's descendants; they simply describe Jizi as a virtuous man who was trusted by King Wu of <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou</a> after having been mistreated by the last <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang</a> king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_274_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_274-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Han_and_later_texts">Han and later texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Han and later texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first texts that make an explicit connection between Jizi and Joseon date from the second century BC, under the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_275_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_275-6">[6]</a></sup> The earliest known source stating that Jizi went to Joseon is the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shangshu_dazhuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shangshu dazhuan (page does not exist)">Shangshu dazhuan</a></i> (尚書大傳), a commentary on the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Documents" title="Book of Documents">Book of Documents</a></i> attributed to <a href="/wiki/Fu_Sheng_(scholar)" title="Fu Sheng (scholar)">Fu Sheng</a> of the second century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_274_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_274-4">[4]</a></sup> In that account, King Wu enfeoffed Jizi as the ruler of Joseon and Jizi became the subject of King Wu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In a similar story recorded in <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i> (or <i>Shiji</i>, compiled between 109 and 91 BC), Jizi was enfeoffed by King Wu but did not become his subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_275_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_275-6">[6]</a></sup> Sima Qian did not mention Jizi in his section on contemporary Joseon (i.e. northwestern Korea), where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wei_Man" title="Wei Man">Wei Man</a>'s kingdom had flourished since about 194 BC until it was conquered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> in 108 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_275_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_275-6">[6]</a></sup> Thus the location of <i>Joseon</i> as in these earlier sources is not clear. Among Han-Dynasty sources, the <i><a href="/wiki/Han_shi_waizhuan" title="Han shi waizhuan">Han shi waizhuan</a></i> mentions to Jizi but not to Jizi's migration to Joseon.</p>
<p>The "Monograph on Geography" (<i>Dili zhi</i> 地理志) of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i> (1st century AD) claims that Jizi had taught the people of Joseon <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">sericulture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a>, as well as proper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_(Confucian)" title="Li (Confucian)">ceremony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Jae-hoon Shim interprets the following sentence in that section of the <i>Hanshu</i> as claiming that Jizi also introduced the law of "Eight Prohibitions" (犯禁八條) in Joseon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Records_of_Three_Kingdoms" title="Records of Three Kingdoms">Records of Three Kingdoms</a></i> (first published in the early fifth century) claims that the descendants of Jizi reigned as kings of Joseon for forty generations until they were overthrown by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wei_Man" title="Wei Man">Wei Man</a>, a man from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Yan" title="State of Yan">state of Yan</a>, in 194 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to his commentary to the <i>Shiji</i>, <a href="/wiki/Du_Yu" title="Du Yu">Du Yu</a> (first half of the 3rd century) states that the tomb of Jizi was located in Meng Prefecture of the State of Liang (modern-day Henan). This suggests that the story of Jizi's association with Joseon was not necessarily prevailing although the narrative seen in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanshu" title="Hanshu">Hanshu</a></i> later became common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As historian Jae-hoon Shim concludes, only during the Han dynasty (206 BC – 220 AD) did Jizi begin to be associated with Joseon, and only after the Han were his descendants identified as the Joseon royal family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_275_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_275-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations_of_Gija_in_Korea">Interpretations of Gija in Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interpretations of Gija in Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_historiography">Traditional historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Traditional historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first extant Korean text to mention Gija (the Korean pronunciation of Jizi) was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Busik" title="Kim Busik">Kim Busik</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i> (completed in 1145), which claimed that Gija had been enfeoffed in Haedong (海東: Korea) by the Zhou court, but commented that this account was uncertain because of the brevity of the sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_276_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_276-11">[11]</a></sup> Only in the thirteenth century did Korean texts start to integrate Gija more fully into Korean history. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samguk_Yusa" title="Samguk Yusa">Samguk Yusa</a></i> (1281) explained that after being enfeoffed by <a href="/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou" title="King Wu of Zhou">King Wu of Zhou</a>, Gija replaced <a href="/wiki/Dangun" title="Dangun">Dangun</a>'s descendants as the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Joseon</a>, whereas <i><a href="/wiki/Jewang_Ungi" title="Jewang Ungi">Jewang Ungi</a></i> (1287) identified Dangun and Gija as the first rulers of former and latter Joseon respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_276_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_276-11">[11]</a></sup> Most premodern Korean historians after that accepted that Gija had replaced another indigenous power (represented by Dangun) in <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Old Joseon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_276_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_276-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1102, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goryeo_Dynasty" title="Goryeo Dynasty">Goryeo Dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sukjong_of_Goryeo" title="Sukjong of Goryeo">Emperor Sukjong</a> built a <a href="/wiki/Kija%27s_Tomb" title="Kija's Tomb">mausoleum</a> to Gija in a place near <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a> that had been identified as Gija's tomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The mausoleum was rebuilt in 1324 and repaired in 1355, but the cult of Gija spread most widely after the establishment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> in 1392. Because Joseon's state ideology was <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a> borrowed from China, Joseon intellectuals promoted Gija as a <a href="/wiki/Culture_hero" title="Culture hero">culture hero</a> who had raised Korean civilization to the same level as China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_277_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_277-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the second half of the sixteenth century to the eighteenth century, Joseon scholars published a number of books on Gija. In 1580, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yun_Dusu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yun Dusu (page does not exist)">Yun Dusu</a> (尹斗壽) collated all available material on him and published his research as the <i>Gijaji</i> (箕子志; "Record of Gija").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_277_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_277-13">[13]</a></sup> On the same year, eminent scholar <a href="/wiki/Yi_I" title="Yi I">Yi I</a> used Yun's book to compile the <i>Gija Silgi</i> (箕子實記), or <i>True Account of Gija</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_277_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_277-13">[13]</a></sup> Yi praised Gija for introducing agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">sericulture</a>, decorum, the <a href="/wiki/Well-field_system" title="Well-field system">well-field system</a>, and the Eight Prohibitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Though he emphasized Gija's independence from <a href="/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou" title="King Wu of Zhou">King Wu of Zhou</a>, Yi believed that Gija's teachings helped Korea to reach the same level of civilization as China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_278_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_278-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Korean scholars became more critical of Gija's role in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, this account of Gija as the "bearer of civilization from China" became widely accepted, so much that by the late Joseon, the worship of Gija "had become an integral part of Korean cultural identity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Some Korean clans proudly claimed descent from Gija.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">Twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the decline of China's prestige and the rise of <a href="/wiki/Korean_nationalism" title="Korean nationalism">Korean nationalism</a> in the early twentieth century, Korean intellectuals no longer took pride in their association with the Chinese Gija, and thus started to doubt the authenticity of his traditional story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_278_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_278-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shin_Chaeho" title="Shin Chaeho">Shin Chaeho</a> (1880–1936) was the first to question the extent of Gija's contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shim_278_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shim_278-15">[15]</a></sup> In an essay titled <i><a href="/wiki/Doksa_Sillon" title="Doksa Sillon">Doksa Sillon</a></i> ("New Reading of History"; 1908), he argued that Korean history was the history of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_ethnic_nationalism" title="Korean ethnic nationalism">Korean <i>minjok</i></a>, a race-nation descended from <a href="/wiki/Dangun" title="Dangun">Dangun</a>, the mythical founder of the state of <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Gija, a foreigner, did not belong in Shin's racially defined view of Korean history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Shin argued instead that Gija had become a vassal of the Kings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buyeo_kingdom" title="Buyeo kingdom">Buyeo</a> and was only given control of a small territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Going further than Shin, <a href="/wiki/Choe_Nam-seon" title="Choe Nam-seon">Choe Nam-seon</a> (1890–1957) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Byeong-do" title="Lee Byeong-do">Lee Byeong-do</a> (1896–1989) started to notice discrepancies between pre-<a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qin</a> Chinese records of Jizi/Gija, and later accounts (both Chinese and Korean) of his role in Joseon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1973, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeologist" title="Archeologist">archeologist</a> Kim Cheong-bae (金貞培) denied Chinese influence on Korea because no ancient Chinese bronzes had been found on the peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the end of <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese colonial rule</a> in 1945, nationalist historians in both <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> targeted Gija.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Ri Chirin, a leading North Korean historian of ancient Korea, argued that the Gija legend had been forged in Han times when the Chinese started to occupy part of Joseon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Most North Korean scholars have followed Ri in doubting the authenticity of Gija's migration to Joseon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, whether Gija and his state actually existed is a matter of controversy. Korean scholars deny its existence for various reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> These scholars point to the <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboo_Annals" title="Bamboo Annals">Bamboo Annals</a></i> and the Confucian <i><a href="/wiki/Analects" title="Analects">Analects</a></i>, which were among the first works to mention Gija, but do not mention his migration to Gojoseon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Detractors of the Gija Joseon theory also point out that the cultural artifacts found in the region do not appear to have Chinese origins. An example of such an artifact is found in a Gojoseon mandolin-shaped bronze dagger. Its shape and bronze composition are different from similar artifacts found in China.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The national-history <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbook</a> of high school education drove Gija's description out of the main text and he was marginally mentioned in a note.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The textbook of the latest 7th Curriculum completely eliminates Gija.</p>
<p>On the contrary, some South Korean historians believe that Gija Joseon coexisted with Dangun, and Gija Joseon was established at the west end of Gojoseon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_analysis">Further analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jizi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Further analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because it is confirmed that early Chinese history books reflect historical facts at least from the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">Shang Dynasty</a> onwards, the Jizi described in pre-Qin sources is generally considered a real character. Cultural material evidences suggest that a small city state in <a href="/wiki/Liaoning" title="Liaoning">Liaoning</a> was ruled by Jihou (㠱侯) or Lord Ji under the <a href="/wiki/Yan_(state)" title="Yan (state)">Yan</a> Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Jihou may be the model of Jizi.</p>
<p>As for post-Qin sources, the story in the Weilüe is controversial because it contains information that cannot be found in preceding materials. Japanese scholar Imanishi Ryū presumed that this story had spread to China because of Chinese direct rule of the northwestern part of the Korean peninsula during the Han dynasty and that the source of information would have been the influential Chinese Han clan of the <a href="/wiki/Lelang_Commandery" title="Lelang Commandery">Lelang Commandery</a>, who might have claimed themselves as descendants of Jizi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
