<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Kinetic theory of gases</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

The <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of an ideal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monatomic" title="Monatomic">monatomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> is proportional to the average <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of its atoms. The <a href="/wiki/Bohr_radius" title="Bohr radius">size</a> of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> atoms relative to their spacing is shown to scale under 1950 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmospheres</a> of pressure. The atoms have a certain, average speed, slowed down here two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000000_(number)" title="1000000000000 (number)">trillion</a> fold from room temperature.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>kinetic theory</b> describes a gas as a large number of submicroscopic particles (<a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>), all of which are in constant rapid <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a> that has <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">randomness</a> arising from their many collisions with each other and with the walls of the container.</p>
<p>Kinetic theory explains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> properties of gases, such as pressure, temperature, viscosity, thermal conductivity, and volume, by considering their molecular composition and motion. The theory posits that gas pressure is due to the impacts, on the walls of a container, of molecules or atoms moving at different velocities.</p>
<p>Kinetic theory defines temperature in its own way, not identical with the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">thermodynamic definition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C_.26_C_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-C_.26_C-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under a microscope, the molecules making up a liquid are too small to be visible, but the jittery motion of pollen grains or dust particles can be seen. Known as <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>, it results directly from collisions between the grains or particles and liquid molecules. As analyzed by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> in 1907, this experimental evidence for kinetic theory is generally seen as having confirmed the concrete material existence of atoms and molecules.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assumptions">Assumptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Assumptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The theory for ideal gases makes the following assumptions:</p>
<ul>
<li>The gas consists of very small particles known as molecules. This smallness of their size is such that the total <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> of the individual gas molecules added up is negligible compared to the volume of the smallest open ball containing all the molecules. This is equivalent to stating that the average distance separating the gas particles is large compared to their <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">size</a>.</li>
<li>These particles have the same <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>.</li>
<li>The number of molecules is so large that statistical treatment can be applied.</li>
<li>These molecules are in constant, <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">random</a>, and rapid motion.</li>
<li>The rapidly moving particles constantly collide among themselves and with the walls of the container. All these collisions are perfectly elastic. This means, the molecules are considered to be perfectly spherical in shape, and elastic in nature.</li>
<li>Except during collisions, the <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interactions</a> among molecules are negligible. (That is, they exert no <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> on one another.)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>This implies:
<dl>
<dd>1. <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">Relativistic</a> effects are negligible.</dd>
<dd>2. <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">Quantum-mechanical</a> effects are negligible. This means that the <a href="/wiki/Mean_inter-particle_distance" title="Mean inter-particle distance">inter-particle distance</a> is much larger than the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_de_Broglie_wavelength" title="Thermal de Broglie wavelength">thermal de Broglie wavelength</a> and the molecules are treated as <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical</a> <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">objects</a>.</dd>
<dd>3. Because of the above two, their dynamics can be treated classically. This means that the equations of motion of the molecules are time-reversible.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The average <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of the gas particles depends only on the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">absolute temperature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a>. The kinetic theory has its own definition of temperature, not identical with the thermodynamic definition.</li>
<li>The elapsed time of a collision between a molecule and the container's wall is negligible when compared to the time between successive collisions.</li>
<li>Because they have mass, the gas molecules will be affected by gravity.</li>
</ul>
<p>More modern developments relax these assumptions and are based on the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_equation" title="Boltzmann equation">Boltzmann equation</a>. These can accurately describe the properties of dense gases, because they include the volume of the molecules. The necessary assumptions are the absence of quantum effects, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_chaos" title="Molecular chaos">molecular chaos</a> and small gradients in bulk properties. Expansions to higher orders in the density are known as <a href="/wiki/Virial_expansion" title="Virial expansion">virial expansions</a>.</p>
<p>An important book on kinetic theory is that by <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Chapman_(mathematician)" title="Sydney Chapman (mathematician)">Chapman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cowling" title="Thomas Cowling">Cowling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C_.26_C_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-C_.26_C-1">[1]</a></sup> An important approach to the subject is called <a href="/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Enskog_theory" title="Chapman–Enskog theory">Chapman–Enskog theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There have been many modern developments and there is an alternative approach developed by Grad based on moment expansions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the other limit, for extremely rarefied gases, the gradients in bulk properties are not small compared to the mean free paths. This is known as the Knudsen regime and expansions can be performed in the <a href="/wiki/Knudsen_number" title="Knudsen number">Knudsen number</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_properties">Transport properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The kinetic theory of gases deals not only with gases in thermodynamic equilibrium, but also very importantly with gases not in thermodynamic equilibrium. This means considering what are known as 'transport properties', such a viscosity and thermal conductivity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In approximately 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_common_era" title="Before common era">BCE</a>, the Roman philosopher <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> proposed that apparently static macroscopic bodies were composed on a small scale of rapidly moving atoms all bouncing off each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicurean</a> atomistic point of view was rarely considered in the subsequent centuries, when <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotlean</a> ideas were dominant.</p>

<p>In 1738 <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">Daniel Bernoulli</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/Hydrodynamica" title="Hydrodynamica">Hydrodynamica</a></i>, which laid the basis for the kinetic theory of gases. In this work, Bernoulli posited the argument, still used to this day, that gases consist of great numbers of molecules moving in all directions, that their impact on a surface causes the gas pressure that we feel, and that what we experience as <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> is simply the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of their motion. The theory was not immediately accepted, in part because <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> had not yet been established, and it was not obvious to physicists how the collisions between molecules could be perfectly elastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PonomarevKurchatov1993_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PonomarevKurchatov1993-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36–37</sup></p>
<p>Other pioneers of the kinetic theory (which were neglected by their contemporaries) were <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Lomonosov" title="Mikhail Lomonosov">Mikhail Lomonosov</a> (1747),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Le_Sage" title="Georges-Louis Le Sage">Georges-Louis Le Sage</a> (ca. 1780, published 1818),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Herapath" title="John Herapath">John Herapath</a> (1816)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_James_Waterston" title="John James Waterston">John James Waterston</a> (1843),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> which connected their research with the development of <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_explanations_of_gravitation" title="Mechanical explanations of gravitation">mechanical explanations of gravitation</a>. In 1856 <a href="/wiki/August_Kr%C3%B6nig" title="August Krönig">August Krönig</a> (probably after reading a paper of Waterston) created a simple gas-kinetic model, which only considered the translational motion of the particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1857 <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius" title="Rudolf Clausius">Rudolf Clausius</a>, according to his own words independently of Krönig, developed a similar, but much more sophisticated version of the theory which included translational and contrary to Krönig also rotational and vibrational molecular motions. In this same work he introduced the concept of <a href="/wiki/Mean_free_path" title="Mean free path">mean free path</a> of a particle. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1859, after reading a paper by Clausius, <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> formulated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxwell_distribution" title="Maxwell distribution">Maxwell distribution</a> of molecular velocities, which gave the proportion of molecules having a certain velocity in a specific range. This was the first-ever statistical law in physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In his 1873 thirteen page article 'Molecules', Maxwell states: "we are told that an 'atom' is a material point, invested and surrounded by 'potential forces' and that when 'flying molecules' strike against a solid body in constant succession it causes what is called <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of air and other gases."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1871, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann" title="Ludwig Boltzmann">Ludwig Boltzmann</a> generalized Maxwell's achievement and formulated the <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution">Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution</a>. Also the <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithmic</a> connection between <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> was first stated by him.</p>
<p>In the beginning of the twentieth century, however, atoms were considered by many physicists to be purely hypothetical constructs, rather than real objects. An important turning point was <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>'s (1905)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Marian_Smoluchowski" title="Marian Smoluchowski">Marian Smoluchowski</a>'s (1906)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> papers on <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>, which succeeded in making certain accurate quantitative predictions based on the kinetic theory.</p>
