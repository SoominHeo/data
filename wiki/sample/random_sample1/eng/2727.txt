<p>A <b>cough</b> is a sudden and often repetitively occurring <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflex</a> which helps to clear the large breathing passages from fluids, irritants, foreign particles and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">microbes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cough_reflex" title="Cough reflex">cough reflex</a> consists of three phases: an <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inhalation</a>, a forced <a href="/wiki/Exhalation" title="Exhalation">exhalation</a> against a closed <a href="/wiki/Glottis" title="Glottis">glottis</a>, and a violent release of air from the lungs following opening of the glottis, usually accompanied by a distinctive sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet-causes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet-causes-1">[1]</a></sup> Coughing is either voluntary or involuntary.</p>
<p>Frequent coughing usually indicates the presence of a disease. Many <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> benefit evolutionarily by causing the <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a> to cough, which helps to spread the disease to new hosts. Most of the time, irregular coughing is caused by a <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract_infection" title="Respiratory tract infection">respiratory tract infection</a> but can also be triggered by <a href="/wiki/Choking" title="Choking">choking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smoking" title="Smoking">smoking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet-causes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet-causes-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">gastroesophageal reflux disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-nasal_drip" title="Post-nasal drip">post-nasal drip</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_bronchitis" title="Chronic bronchitis">chronic bronchitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lung_tumors" title="Lung tumors">lung tumors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a> and medications such as <a href="/wiki/ACE_inhibitor" title="ACE inhibitor">ACE inhibitors</a>.</p>
<p>Treatment should target the cause; for example, <a href="/wiki/Smoking_cessation" title="Smoking cessation">smoking cessation</a> or discontinuing ACE inhibitors. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cough_suppressant" title="Cough suppressant">Cough suppressants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dextromethorphan" title="Dextromethorphan">dextromethorphan</a> are frequently prescribed, but have been demonstrated to have little effect. Other treatment options may target airway <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> or may promote <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">mucus expectoration</a>. As it is a natural protective reflex, suppressing the cough reflex might have damaging effects, especially if the cough is productive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18424326_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18424326-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A cough can be classified by its duration, character, quality, and timing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> The duration can be either <a href="/wiki/Acute_(medicine)" title="Acute (medicine)">acute</a> (of sudden onset) if it is present less than three weeks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subacute" title="Subacute">subacute</a> if it is present between three or eight weeks, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medicine)" title="Chronic (medicine)">chronic</a> when lasting longer than eight weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> A cough can be non-productive (dry) or productive (when <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">sputum</a> is coughed up). It may occur only at night (then called <i>nocturnal cough</i>), during both night and day, or just during the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of characteristic coughs exist. While these have not been found to be diagnostically useful in adults, they are of use in <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> A barky cough is part of the common presentation of <a href="/wiki/Croup" title="Croup">croup</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> while a staccato cough has been classically described with <a href="/wiki/Chlamydia_trachomatis" title="Chlamydia trachomatis">chlamydia</a> <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_diagnosis">Differential diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Differential diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A cough in children may be either a normal physiological reflex or due to an underlying cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> In healthy children it may be normal in the absence of any disease to cough ten times a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> The most common cause of an acute or subacute cough is a viral <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract_infection" title="Respiratory tract infection">respiratory tract infection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> In adults with a chronic cough, i.e. a cough longer than 8 weeks, more than 90% of cases are due to <a href="/wiki/Post-nasal_drip" title="Post-nasal drip">post-nasal drip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eosinophilic_bronchitis" title="Eosinophilic bronchitis">eosinophilic bronchitis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">gastroesophageal reflux disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> The causes of chronic cough are similar in children with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bacterial bronchitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infections">Infections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Infections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A cough can be the result of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract_infections" title="Respiratory tract infections">respiratory tract infection</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">common cold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acute_bronchitis" title="Acute bronchitis">acute bronchitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pertussis" title="Pertussis">pertussis</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. In the vast majority of cases, acute coughs, i.e. coughs shorter than 3 weeks, are due to the common cold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20015366_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20015366-6">[6]</a></sup> In people with a normal chest X-ray, tuberculosis is a rare finding. Pertussis is increasingly being recognised as a cause of troublesome coughing in adults.</p>
<p>After a respiratory tract infection has cleared, the person may be left with a <a href="/wiki/Post-viral_cough" title="Post-viral cough">postinfectious cough</a>. This typically is a dry, non-productive cough that produces no <a href="/wiki/Phlegm" title="Phlegm">phlegm</a>. Symptoms may include a tightness in the chest, and a tickle in the throat. This cough may often persist for weeks after an illness. The cause of the cough may be inflammation similar to that observed in repetitive stress disorders such as <a href="/wiki/Carpal_tunnel_syndrome" title="Carpal tunnel syndrome">carpal tunnel syndrome</a>. The repetition of coughing produces inflammation which produces discomfort, which in turn produces more coughing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> Postinfectious cough typically does not respond to conventional cough treatments. Treatment consists of any anti-inflammatory medicine (such as ipratropium) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> to treat the inflammation, and a cough suppressant to reduce frequency of the cough until inflammation clears.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Inflammation may increase sensitivity to other existing issues such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergies" title="Allergies">allergies</a>, and treatment of other causes of coughs (such as use of an air purifier or allergy medicines) may help speed recovery. A bronchodilator, which helps open up the airways, may also help treat this type of cough.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactive_airway_disease">Reactive airway disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reactive airway disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When coughing is the only complaint of a person who meets the criteria for asthma (<a href="/wiki/Bronchial_hyperresponsiveness" title="Bronchial hyperresponsiveness">bronchial hyperresponsiveness</a> and reversibility), this is termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cough-variant_asthma" title="Cough-variant asthma">cough-variant asthma</a>. Two related conditions are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Atopic_cough&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Atopic cough (page does not exist)">atopic cough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eosinophilic_bronchitis" title="Eosinophilic bronchitis">eosinophilic bronchitis</a>. Atopic cough occurs in individuals with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_history" title="Family history">family history</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atopy" title="Atopy">atopy</a> (an allergic condition), abundant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eosinophils" title="Eosinophils">eosinophils</a> in the sputum, but with normal airway function and responsiveness. Eosinophilic bronchitis is also characterized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eosinophils" title="Eosinophils">eosinophils</a> in the sputum, without airway hyperresponsiveness or an atopic background. This condition responds to treatment with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">corticosteroids</a>. Cough can also worsen in an <a href="/wiki/Acute_exacerbation_of_chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Acute exacerbation of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">acute exacerbation of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a>.</p>
<p>Asthma is a common cause of chronic cough in adults and children. Coughing may be the only symptom the person has from their asthma, or asthma symptoms may also include wheezing, shortness of breath, and a tight feeling in their chest. Depending on how severe the asthma is, it can be treated with bronchodilators (medicine which causes the airways to open up) or inhaled steroids. Treatment of the asthma should make the cough go away.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_bronchitis" title="Chronic bronchitis">Chronic bronchitis</a> is defined clinically as a persistent cough that produces sputum (phlegm) and mucus, for at least three months in two consecutive years. Chronic bronchitis is often the cause of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smoker%27s_cough" title="Smoker's cough">smoker's cough</a>". The tobacco smoke causes inflammation, secretion of mucus into the airway, and difficulty clearing that mucus out of the airways. Coughing helps clear those secretions out. May be treated by quitting smoking. May also be caused by <a href="/wiki/Pneumoconiosis" title="Pneumoconiosis">pneumoconiosis</a> and long-term fume inhalation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastroesophageal_reflux">Gastroesophageal reflux</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gastroesophageal reflux">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In people with unexplained cough, <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">gastroesophageal reflux disease</a> should be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> This occurs when acidic contents of the stomach come back up into the esophagus. Symptoms usually associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GERD" title="GERD">GERD</a> include heartburn, sour taste in the mouth, or a feeling of acid reflux in the chest, although, more than half of the people with cough from GERD don’t have any other symptoms. An esophageal pH monitor can confirm the diagnosis of GERD. Sometimes GERD can complicate respiratory ailments related to cough, such as asthma or bronchitis. The treatment involves anti-acid medications and lifestyle changes with surgery indicated in cases not manageable with conservative measures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_pollution">Air pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Air pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coughing may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> including <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a>, particulate matter, irritant gases, and dampness in a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> The human health effects of poor air quality are far reaching, but principally affect the body's respiratory system and the cardiovascular system. Individual reactions to air pollutants depend on the type of pollutant a person is exposed to, the degree of exposure, the individual's health status and genetics. People who exercise outdoors on hot, smoggy days, for example, increase their exposure to pollutants in the air.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_body">Foreign body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreign body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Foreign_body" title="Foreign body">foreign body</a> can sometimes be suspected, for example if the cough started suddenly when the patient was eating. Rarely, sutures left behind inside the airway branches can cause coughing. A cough can be triggered by dryness from <a href="/wiki/Mouth_breathing" title="Mouth breathing">mouth breathing</a> or recurrent aspiration of food into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" title="Vertebrate trachea">windpipe</a> in people with <a href="/wiki/Dysphagia" title="Dysphagia">swallowing difficulties</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angiotensin-converting_enzyme_inhibitor">Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors are drugs used in diabetics, heart disease, and high blood pressure. In 10-25%<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of the people who take it, it can cause them to have a cough as a side effect. Cessation of ACE Inhibitor use is the only way to stop the cough. Such medicines for hypertension are very common in use such as <a href="/wiki/Ramipril" title="Ramipril">ramipril</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quinapril" title="Quinapril">quinapril</a>. There are cases of "cough of unknown origin" who had resolution with stopping the drug.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychogenic_cough">Psychogenic cough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Psychogenic cough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychogenic_cough" title="Psychogenic cough">psychogenic cough</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Habit_cough" title="Habit cough">habit cough</a>" or "tic cough" may be the cause in the absence of a physical problem. In these instances, emotional and psychological problems are suspected. However, other illnesses have to be ruled out before a firm diagnosis of psychogenic cough is made. Psychogenic coughing is different from habit coughing and tic coughing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16428707_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16428707-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Psychogenic cough is thought to be more common in children than in adults. A possible scenario: psychogenic cough develops in a child who has a chronically ill brother or sister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16428707_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16428707-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurogenic_cough">Neurogenic cough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Neurogenic cough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some cases of chronic cough may be attributed to a sensory neuropathic disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21524236_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21524236-10">[10]</a></sup> Treatment for neurogenic cough may include the use of certain neuralgia medications. Coughing may occur in <a href="/wiki/Tic_disorder" title="Tic disorder">tic disorders</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tourette_syndrome" title="Tourette syndrome">Tourette syndrome</a>, although it should be distinguished from throat-clearing in this disorder.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cough may also be caused by conditions affecting the lung tissue such as <a href="/wiki/Bronchiectasis" title="Bronchiectasis">bronchiectasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">cystic fibrosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_lung_disease" title="Interstitial lung disease">interstitial lung diseases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarcoidosis" title="Sarcoidosis">sarcoidosis</a>. Coughing can also be triggered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benign" title="Benign">benign</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">malignant lung tumors</a> or mediastinal masses. Through irritation of the nerve, diseases of the external auditory canal (wax, for example) can also cause cough. Cardiovascular diseases associated with cough are heart failure, pulmonary infarction and aortic aneurysm. Nocturnal cough is associated with heart failure, as the heart does not compensate for the increased volume shift to the pulmonary circulation, in turn causing <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a> and resultant cough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farzan_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farzan-11">[11]</a></sup> Other causes of nocturnal cough include <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-nasal_drip" title="Post-nasal drip">post-nasal drip</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">gastroesophageal reflux disease</a> (GERD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Another cause of cough occurring preferentially in <a href="/wiki/Supine_position" title="Supine position">supine position</a> is recurrent aspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farzan_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Farzan-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given its irritant nature to mammal tissues, <a href="/wiki/Capsaicin" title="Capsaicin">capsaicin</a> is widely used to determine the cough threshold and as a tussive stimulant in clinical research of cough suppressants. Capsaicin is what makes <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili peppers</a> spicy, and might explain why workers in factories with these vegetables can develop a cough.</p>
<p>Coughing may also be used for social reasons, such as coughing before giving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech-making" title="Speech-making">speech</a>. Coughing is not always involuntary, and can be used in social situations. Coughing can be used to attract attention, release internal psychological tension, or become a maladaptive displacement behavior. It is believed that the frequency of such coughing increases in environments vulnerable to psychological tension and social conflict. In such environments, coughing may become one of many <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(psychology)" title="Displacement (psychology)">displacement</a> behaviors or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_mechanism" title="Defense mechanism">defense mechanisms</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A cough is a protective <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflex</a> in healthy individuals which is influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological" title="Psychological">psychological</a> factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> The cough reflex is initiated by stimulation of two different classes of <a href="/wiki/Afferent_nerve" title="Afferent nerve">afferent nerves</a>, namely the <a href="/wiki/Myelin" title="Myelin">myelinated</a> rapidly adapting receptors, and nonmyelinated <a href="/wiki/Group_C_nerve_fiber" title="Group C nerve fiber">C-fibers</a> with endings in the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>. However it is not certain that the stimulation of nonmyelinated <a href="/wiki/Group_C_nerve_fiber" title="Group C nerve fiber">C-fibers</a> leads to cough with a <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflex</a> as it's meant in <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> (with its own five components): this stimulation may cause <a href="/wiki/Mast_cell" title="Mast cell">mast cells</a> <a href="/wiki/Degranulation" title="Degranulation">degranulation</a> (through an asso-assonic reflex) and <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a> which may work as a stimulus for rapidly adapting receptors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostic_approach">Diagnostic approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diagnostic approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The determination of the cause of a cough usually begins by determining if it is specific or nonspecific in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> A specific cough is one associated with other symptoms and further workup is dependent on these symptoms while a non specific cough occurs without other signs and symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-13"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> Further workup may include labs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">x-rays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spirometry" title="Spirometry">spirometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-14"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The treatment of a cough in children is based on the underlying cause. In children half of cases go away without treatment in 10 days and 90% in 25 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Pediatrics" title="American Academy of Pediatrics">American Academy of Pediatrics</a> the use of <a href="/wiki/Cough_medicine" title="Cough medicine">cough medicine</a> to relieve cough symptoms is supported by little evidence and thus not recommended for treating cough symptoms in children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-15"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup> There is tentative evidence that the use of honey is better than no treatment or <a href="/wiki/Diphenhydramine" title="Diphenhydramine">diphenhydramine</a> in decreasing coughing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:Oduwole_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:Oduwole-14">[14]</a></sup> It does not alleviate coughing to the same extent as <a href="/wiki/Dextromethorphan" title="Dextromethorphan">dextromethorphan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:Oduwole_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:Oduwole-14">[14]</a></sup> A trial of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhaled_corticosteroid" title="Inhaled corticosteroid">inhaled corticosteroids</a> may be tried in children with a chronic cough in an attempt to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protracted_bacterial_bronchitis" title="Protracted bacterial bronchitis">protracted bacterial bronchitis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-16"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The complications of coughing can be classified as either <a href="/wiki/Acute_(medicine)" title="Acute (medicine)">acute</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medical)" title="Chronic (medical)">chronic</a>. Acute complications include cough syncope (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fainting" title="Fainting">fainting</a> spells due to decreased blood flow to the brain when coughs are prolonged and forceful), <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">insomnia</a>, cough-induced <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, rupture of <a href="/wiki/Bleb_(medicine)" title="Bleb (medicine)">blebs</a> causing <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax#Spontaneous" title="Pneumothorax">spontaneous pneumothorax</a> (although this still remains to be proven), <a href="/wiki/Subconjunctival_hemorrhage" title="Subconjunctival hemorrhage">subconjunctival hemorrhage</a> or "<a href="/wiki/Red_eye_(medicine)" title="Red eye (medicine)">red eye</a>", coughing <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a> and in women with a <a href="/wiki/Prolapse" title="Prolapse">prolapsed</a> <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>, cough <a href="/wiki/Urination" title="Urination">urination</a>. Chronic complications are common and include abdominal or pelvic <a href="/wiki/Hernia" title="Hernia">hernias</a>, fatigue <a href="/wiki/Fracture" title="Fracture">fractures</a> of lower ribs and <a href="/wiki/Costochondritis" title="Costochondritis">costochondritis</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A cough is the most common reason for visiting a <a href="/wiki/Primary_care_physician" title="Primary care physician">primary care physician</a> in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review10_3-17"><a href="#cite_note-Review10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marine mammals such as dolphins cannot cough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
