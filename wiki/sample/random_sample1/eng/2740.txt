<p><b><i>Cis</i>–<i>trans</i> isomerism</b>, also known as <b>geometric isomerism</b> or <b>configurational isomerism,</b> is a term used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>. The terms “<i>cis</i>” and “<i>trans</i>” are from Latin. <i>Cis</i> means that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functional_groups" title="Functional groups">functional groups</a> are on the same side of the carbon chain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and <i>trans</i> means that functional groups are on the opposite side of the carbon chain. It is used to describe molecules that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoisomers" title="Stereoisomers">stereoisomers</a>, that is, pairs of molecules which have the same formula but whose functional groups are rotated into a different orientation in three-dimensional space. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/E-Z_notation" title="E-Z notation"><i>E</i>–<i>Z</i> isomerism</a>, which is an <i><a href="/wiki/Absolute_configuration" title="Absolute configuration">absolute</a></i> stereochemical description, and only to be used with alkenes. In general, stereoisomers contain <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a> that cannot rotate, or they may contain ring structures, where the rotation of bonds is restricted or prevented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reusch10_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reusch10-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Cis</i> and <i>trans</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomers" title="Isomers">isomers</a> occur both in organic molecules and in inorganic coordination complexes. <i>Cis</i> and <i>trans</i> descriptors are not used for cases of <a href="/wiki/Conformational_isomerism" title="Conformational isomerism">conformational isomerism</a> where the two geometric forms easily interconvert, such as most open-chain single-bonded structures; instead, the terms “<i>syn</i>” and “<i>anti</i>” would be used.</p>
<p>The term "geometric isomerism" is considered an obsolete synonym of "<i>cis</i>–<i>trans</i> isomerism" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_chemistry">Organic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent groups</a> are oriented in the same direction, the <a href="/wiki/Diastereomer" title="Diastereomer">diastereomer</a> is referred to as <i>cis</i>, whereas, when the substituents are oriented in opposing directions, the diastereomer is referred to as <i>trans</i>. An example of a small hydrocarbon displaying <i>cis</i>–<i>trans</i> isomerism is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-butene" title="2-butene">but-2-ene</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alicyclic_compound" title="Alicyclic compound">Alicyclic compounds</a> can also display <i>cis</i>–<i>trans</i> isomerism. As an example of a geometric isomer due to a ring structure, consider 1,2-dichlorocyclohexane:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_physical_properties">Comparison of physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison of physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>These differences can be very small, as in the case of the boiling point of straight-chain alkenes, such as <a href="/wiki/Pentene" title="Pentene">pent-2-ene</a>, which is 37 °C in the <i>cis</i> isomer and 36 °C in the <i>trans</i> isomer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The differences between <i>cis</i> and <i>trans</i> isomers can be larger if polar bonds are present, as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,2-dichloroethene" title="1,2-dichloroethene">1,2-dichloroethenes</a>. The <i>cis</i> isomer in this case has a boiling point of 60.3 °C, while the <i>trans</i> isomer has a boiling point of 47.5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the <i>cis</i> isomer the two polar C-Cl <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">bond dipole moments</a> combine to give an overall molecular dipole, so that there are intermolecular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermolecular_forces#Dipole-dipole_interactions" title="Intermolecular forces">dipole–dipole forces</a> (or Keesom forces), which add to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_dispersion_forces" title="London dispersion forces">London dispersion forces</a> and raise the boiling point. In the <i>trans</i> isomer on the other hand, this does not occur because the two C−Cl bond moments cancel and the molecule has a net zero dipole (it does however have a non-zero quadrupole).</p>
<p>The two isomers of butenedioic acid have such large differences in properties and reactivities that they were actually given completely different names. The <i>cis</i> isomer is called <a href="/wiki/Maleic_acid" title="Maleic acid">maleic acid</a> and the <i>trans</i> isomer <a href="/wiki/Fumaric_acid" title="Fumaric acid">fumaric acid</a>. Polarity is key in determining relative boiling point as it causes increased intermolecular forces, thereby raising the boiling point. In the same manner, symmetry is key in determining relative melting point as it allows for better packing in the solid state, even if it does not alter the polarity of the molecule. One example of this is the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elaidic_acid" title="Elaidic acid">elaidic acid</a>; oleic acid, the <i>cis</i> isomer, has a melting point of 13.4 °C, making it a liquid at room temperature, while the <i>trans</i> isomer, elaidic acid, has the much higher melting point of 43 °C, due to the straighter <i>trans</i> isomer being able to pack more tightly, and is solid at room temperature.</p>
<p>Thus, <i>trans</i> alkenes, which are less polar and more symmetrical, have lower boiling points and higher melting points, and <i>cis</i> alkenes, which are generally more polar and less symmetrical, have higher boiling points and lower melting points.</p>
<p>In the case of geometric isomers that are a consequence of double bonds, and, in particular, when both substituents are the same, some general trends usually hold. These trends can be attributed to the fact that the dipoles of the substituents in a <i>cis</i> isomer will add up to give an overall molecular dipole. In a <i>trans</i> isomer, the dipoles of the substituents will cancel out<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> due to being on opposite sides of the molecule. <i>Trans</i> isomers also tend to have lower densities than their <i>cis</i> counterparts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As a general trend, <i>trans</i> alkenes tend to have higher <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a> and lower <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in inert solvents, as <i>trans</i> alkenes, in general, are more symmetrical than <i>cis</i> alkenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-march_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-march-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vicinal_(chemistry)" title="Vicinal (chemistry)">Vicinal</a> <a href="/wiki/J-coupling" title="J-coupling">coupling constants</a> (<sup>3</sup><i>J</i><sub>HH</sub>), measured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>, are larger for <i>trans</i> (range: 12–18 Hz; typical: 15 Hz) than for <i>cis</i> (range: 0–12 Hz; typical: 8 Hz) isomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">Stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Usually for acyclic systems <i>trans</i> isomers are more stable than <i>cis</i> isomers. This is typically due to the increased unfavourable <a href="/wiki/Steric_effects" title="Steric effects">steric interaction</a> of the substituents in the <i>cis</i> isomer. Therefore, <i>trans</i> isomers have a less exothermic <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">heat of combustion</a>, indicating higher <a href="/wiki/Thermochemistry" title="Thermochemistry">thermochemical</a> stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-march_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-march-6">[6]</a></sup> In the Benson <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_formation_group_additivity" title="Heat of formation group additivity">heat of formation group additivity</a> dataset, <i>cis</i> isomers suffer a 1.10 kcal/mol stability penalty. Exceptions to this rule exist, such as 1,2-difluoroethylene, 1,2-difluorodiazene (FN=NF), and several other halogen- and oxygen-substituted ethylenes. In these cases, the <i>cis</i> isomer is more stable than the <i>trans</i> isomer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This phenomenon is called the <i>cis effect</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="E.2FZ_notation">E/Z notation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: E/Z notation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/E-Z_notation" title="E-Z notation">E-Z notation</a></div>
<p>The <i>cis</i>–<i>trans</i> system for naming alkene isomers should generally only be used when there are only two different substituents on the double bond, so there is no confusion about which substituents are being described relative to each other. For more complex cases, the cis/trans designation is generally based on the longest carbon chain as reflected in the root name of the molecule (i.e. an extension of standard organic nomenclature for the parent structure). The IUPAC standard designations <i>E</i>–<i>Z</i> are unambiguous in all cases, and therefore are especially useful for tri- and tetrasubstituted alkenes to avoid any confusion about which groups are being identified as <i>cis</i> or <i>trans</i> to each other.</p>
<p><i>Z</i> (from the German <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>zusammen</i></span>) means "together". <i>E</i> (from the German <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>entgegen</i></span>) means "opposed" in the sense of "opposite". That is, <i>Z</i> has the higher-priority groups <i>cis</i> to each other and <i>E</i> has the higher-priority groups <i>trans</i> to each other. Whether a molecular configuration is designated <i>E</i> or <i>Z</i> is determined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cahn-Ingold-Prelog_priority_rule" title="Cahn-Ingold-Prelog priority rule">Cahn-Ingold-Prelog priority rules</a>; higher atomic numbers are given higher priority. For each of the two atoms in the double bond, it is necessary to determine the priority of each substituent. If both the higher-priority substituents are on the same side, the arrangement is <i>Z</i>; if on opposite sides, the arrangement is <i>E</i>.</p>
<p>Because the cis/trans and <i>E</i>–<i>Z</i> systems compare different groups on the alkene, it is not strictly true that <i>Z</i> corresponds to <i>cis</i> and <i>E</i> corresponds to trans. For example, <i>trans</i>-2-chlorobut-2-ene (the two methyl groups, C1 and C4, on the <a href="/wiki/2-Butene" title="2-Butene">but-2-ene</a> backbone are <i>trans</i> to each other) is (<i>Z</i>)-2-chlorobut-2-ene (the chlorine and C4 are together because C1 and C4 are opposite).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inorganic_chemistry">Inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Cis</i>–<i>trans</i> isomerism can also occur in inorganic compounds, most notably in <a href="/wiki/Diazene" title="Diazene">diazenes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_compound" title="Coordination compound">coordination compounds</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diazenes">Diazenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diazenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diazene" title="Diazene">Diazenes</a> (and the related <a href="/wiki/Diphosphene" title="Diphosphene">diphosphenes</a>) can also exhibit cis/trans isomerism. As with organic compounds, the <i>cis</i> isomer is generally the more reactive of the two, being the only isomer that can reduce <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, but for a different reason: the <i>trans</i> isomer cannot line its hydrogens up suitably to reduce the alkene, but the <i>cis</i> isomer, being shaped differently, can.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_complexes">Coordination complexes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coordination complexes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In inorganic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_complexes" title="Coordination complexes">coordination complexes</a> with octahedral or square planar geometries, there are also <i>cis</i> isomers in which similar ligands are closer together and <i>trans</i> isomers in which they are further apart.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="124" data-file-width="275" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg/320px-Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg/480px-Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg/640px-Cisplatin_and_transplatin.svg.png 2x" width="320"/></a>

The two isomeric complexes, cisplatin and transplatin</div>

<p>For example, there are two isomers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_planar" title="Square planar">square planar</a> Pt(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>, as explained by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Werner" title="Alfred Werner">Alfred Werner</a> in 1893. The <i>cis</i> isomer, whose full name is <i>cis</i>-diamminedichloroplatinum(II), was shown in 1969 by <a href="/wiki/Barnett_Rosenberg" title="Barnett Rosenberg">Barnett Rosenberg</a> to have antitumor activity, and is now a chemotherapy drug known by the short name <a href="/wiki/Cisplatin" title="Cisplatin">cisplatin</a>. In contrast, the <i>trans</i> isomer (transplatin) has no useful anticancer activity. Each isomer can be synthesized using the <a href="/wiki/Trans_effect" title="Trans effect">trans effect</a> to control which isomer is produced.</p>

<p>For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octahedral_complex" title="Octahedral complex">octahedral complexes</a> of formula MX<sub>4</sub>Y<sub>2</sub>, two isomers also exist. (Here M is a metal atom, and X and Y are two different types of <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a>.) In the <i>cis</i> isomer, the two Y ligands are adjacent to each other at 90°, as is true for the two chlorine atoms shown in green in <i>cis</i>-[Co(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>]<sup>+</sup>, at left. In the <i>trans</i> isomer shown at right, the two Cl atoms are on opposite sides of the central Co atom.</p>
<p>A related type of isomerism in octahedral MX<sub>3</sub>Y<sub>3</sub> complexes is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fac%E2%80%93mer_isomerism" title="Fac–mer isomerism">facial–meridional</a> (or <i>fac</i>/<i>mer</i>) isomerism, in which different numbers of ligands are <i>cis</i> or <i>trans</i> to each other. Metal carbonyl compounds can be characterized as "<i>fac</i>" or "<i>mer</i>" using <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl#Infrared_spectra" title="Metal carbonyl">infrared spectroscopy</a>.</p>
