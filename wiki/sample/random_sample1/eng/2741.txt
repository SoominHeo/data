<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Teratology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Teratology</b> is the study of abnormalities of physiological development. It is often thought of as the study of human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_abnormalities" title="Congenital abnormalities">congenital abnormalities</a>, but it is broader than that, taking into account other non-birth developmental stages, including <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a>; and other non-human life forms, including plants. The related term <a href="/wiki/Developmental_toxicity" title="Developmental toxicity">developmental toxicity</a> includes all manifestations of abnormal development that are caused by environmental insult. These may include growth retardation, delayed mental development or other <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital disorders</a> without any structural malformations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Teratogens</b> are substances that may cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_defects" title="Birth defects">birth defects</a> via a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> effect on an embryo or fetus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teratogens_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Teratogens-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>teratology</i> stems from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">τέρας</span> <i>teras</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">genitive</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">τέρατος</span> <i>teratos</i>), meaning "monster" or "marvel", and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">λόγος</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">logos</a></i>, meaning "the word" or, more loosely, "the study of".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As early as the 17th century, <i>teratology</i> referred to a discourse on prodigies and marvels of anything so extraordinary as to seem abnormal. In the 19th century it acquired a meaning more closely related to biological deformities, mostly in the field of botany. Currently, its most instrumental meaning is that of the medical study of teratogenesis, <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital malformations</a> or individuals with significant malformations. Historically, people have used many pejorative terms to describe/label cases of significant physical malformations. In the 1960s <a href="/wiki/David_Weyhe_Smith" title="David Weyhe Smith">David W. Smith</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a> Medical School (one of the researchers who became known in 1973 for the discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_syndrome" title="Fetal alcohol syndrome">fetal alcohol syndrome</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jonesetal1973_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jonesetal1973-4">[4]</a></sup>), popularized the term <i>teratology</i>. With the growth of understanding of the origins of birth defects, the field of teratology as of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> overlaps with other fields of science, including <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">developmental biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>. Until the 1940s teratologists regarded birth defects as primarily hereditary. In 1941 the first well-documented cases of environmental agents being the cause of severe birth defects were reported<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammalia">Mammalia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mammalia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Teratogenesis">Teratogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Teratogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Wilson.27s_6_principles"></span> Along with this new awareness of the <i><a href="/wiki/In_utero" title="In utero">in utero</a></i> vulnerability of the developing <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a> came the development and refinement of <i>The Six Principles of Teratology</i> which are still applied today. These principles of teratology were put forth by <a href="/wiki/James_G._Wilson" title="James G. Wilson">Jim Wilson</a> in 1959 and in his monograph <i>Environment and Birth Defects</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-12-757750-5_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-12-757750-5-6">[6]</a></sup> These principles guide the study and understanding of teratogenic agents and their effects on developing organisms:</p>
<ol>
<li>Susceptibility to teratogenesis depends on the <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Conceptus" title="Conceptus">conceptus</a> and the manner in which this interacts with adverse environmental factors.</li>
<li>Susceptibility to teratogenesis varies with the developmental stage at the time of exposure to an adverse influence. There are critical periods of susceptibility to agents and organ systems affected by these agents.</li>
<li>Teratogenic agents act in specific ways on developing cells and tissues to initiate sequences of abnormal developmental events.</li>
<li>The access of adverse influences to developing tissues depends on the nature of the influence. Several factors affect the ability of a teratogen to contact a developing conceptus, such as the nature of the agent itself, route and degree of maternal exposure, rate of placental transfer and systemic absorption, and composition of the maternal and embryonic/fetal genotypes.</li>
<li>There are four manifestations of deviant development (Death, Malformation, Growth Retardation and Functional Defect).</li>
<li>Manifestations of deviant development increase in frequency and degree as dosage increases from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_Observable_Adverse_Effect_Level" title="No Observable Adverse Effect Level">No Observable Adverse Effect Level</a> (NOAEL) to a dose producing 100% Lethality (LD100).</li>
</ol>
<p>Studies designed to test the teratogenic potential of environmental agents use animal model systems (e.g., rat, mouse, rabbit, dog, and monkey). Early teratologists exposed pregnant animals to environmental agents and observed the fetuses for gross visceral and skeletal abnormalities. While this is still part of the teratological evaluation procedures today, the field of Teratology is moving to a more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular" title="Molecular">molecular</a> level, seeking the mechanism(s) of action by which these agents act. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetically_modified" title="Genetically modified">Genetically modified</a> mice are commonly used for this purpose. In addition, pregnancy registries are large, prospective studies that monitor exposures women receive during their pregnancies and record the outcome of their births. These studies provide information about possible risks of medications or other exposures in human pregnancies.</p>
<p>Understanding how a <b>teratogen</b> causes its effect is not only important in preventing congenital abnormalities but also has the potential for developing new therapeutic drugs safe for use with pregnant women.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans">Humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">Congenital disorder</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital disorders</a> resulted in about 510,000 deaths globally in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loz2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loz2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 3% of newborns have a "major physical anomaly", meaning a physical anomaly that has cosmetic or functional significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vaccinating_while_pregnant">Vaccinating while pregnant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vaccinating while pregnant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, vaccination has become readily available, and is important to the prevention of some diseases like polio, rubella, and smallpox, among others. There has been no association between congenital malformations and vaccination, as shown in Finland in which expecting mothers received the oral polio vaccine and saw no difference in infant outcomes than mothers who had not received the vaccine. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harj1993_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harj1993-9">[9]</a></sup> However, it is still not recommended to vaccinate for polio while pregnant due to possible complications for the mother. Another important implication of this includes the ability to get the influenza vaccine while pregnant. During the 1918-1957 influenza pandemic, mortality in pregnant women was 45%. However, even with prevention through vaccination, influenza vaccination in pregnant women remains low at 12%. Munoz et al demonstrated that there was no adverse outcomes observed in the new infants or mothers. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mun2005_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mun2005-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">Congenital disorder</a></div>
<p>Causes of teratogenesis can broadly be classified as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Toxic substances, such as, for humans, <a href="/wiki/Drugs_in_pregnancy" title="Drugs in pregnancy">drugs in pregnancy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_toxins_and_fetal_development" title="Environmental toxins and fetal development">environmental toxins in pregnancy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vertically_transmitted_infection" title="Vertically transmitted infection">Vertically transmitted infection</a></li>
<li>Lack of nutrients. For example, lack of folic acid in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrition_in_pregnancy" title="Nutrition in pregnancy">nutrition in pregnancy</a> for humans can result in <a href="/wiki/Spina_bifida" title="Spina bifida">spina bifida</a>.</li>
<li>Physical restraint. An example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potter_syndrome" title="Potter syndrome">Potter syndrome</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Oligohydramnios" title="Oligohydramnios">oligohydramnios</a> in humans.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">Genetic disorders</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Paleopathology" title="Paleopathology">Paleopathology</a></div>
<p>Evidence for congenital deformities found in the fossil record is studied by paleopathologists, specialists in ancient disease and injury. Fossils bearing evidence of congenital deformity are scientifically significant because they can help scientists infer the evolutionary history of life's developmental processes. For instance, because a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyrannosaurus_rex" title="Tyrannosaurus rex">Tyrannosaurus rex</a></i> specimen has been discovered with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_vertebra" title="Block vertebra">block vertebra</a>, it means that vertebrae have been developing the same basic way since at least the most recent common ancestor of dinosaurs and mammals. Other notable fossil deformities include a hatchling specimen of the bird-like dinosaur, <i><a href="/wiki/Troodon" title="Troodon">Troodon</a></i>, the tip of whose jaw was twisted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-molnar-pathology_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-molnar-pathology-11">[11]</a></sup> Another notably deformed fossil was a specimen of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choristodere" title="Choristodere">choristodere</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Hyphalosaurus" title="Hyphalosaurus">Hyphalosaurus</a></i>, which had two heads- the oldest known example of <a href="/wiki/Polycephaly" title="Polycephaly">polycephaly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plantae">Plantae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teratology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Plantae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, teratology investigates the theoretical implications of abnormal specimens. For example, the discovery of abnormal flowers—for example, flowers with leaves instead of petals, or flowers with staminoid pistils—furnished important evidence for the "<a href="/wiki/ABC_model_of_flower_development" title="ABC model of flower development">foliar theory</a>", the theory that all flower parts are highly specialised leaves.</p>
