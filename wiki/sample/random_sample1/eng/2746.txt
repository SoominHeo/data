<p>An <b>emergency communication system</b> (<b>ECS</b>) is any <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> (typically, computer-based) that is organized for the primary purpose of supporting one-way and two-way communication of emergency messages between both individuals and groups of individuals. These systems are commonly designed to integrate the cross-communication of messages between a variety of communication technologies, forming a <i>unified</i> communication system intended to optimize communications during emergencies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_communication_vs._notification">Emergency communication vs. notification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Emergency communication vs. notification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An emergency notification system refers to a collection of methods that facilitate the one-way <a href="/wiki/Dissemination" title="Dissemination">dissemination</a> or broadcast of messages to one or many groups of people with the details of an occurring or pending emergency situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Mass automated dialing services such as <a href="/wiki/Reverse_911" title="Reverse 911">Reverse 911</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Common_siren_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Common siren system (page does not exist)">common siren systems</a> that are used to alert for tornadoes, tsunami, air-raid, etc., are examples of <a href="/wiki/Emergency_notification_system" title="Emergency notification system">emergency notification systems</a>.</p>
<p>Emergency communication systems often provide or integrate those same notification services but will also include two-way communications typically to facilitate communications between emergency communications staff, affected people and first responders in the field. Another distinguishing attribute of the term "communication" may be that it implies the ability to provide detailed and meaningful information<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> about an evolving emergency and actions that might be taken; whereas "notification" denotes a relatively more simplistic one-time conveyance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> of the existence and general nature of an emergency (such as Emergency Rescue Location<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_and_related_terms">Alternate and related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternate and related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Being a collection of methods that are often incorporated in many diverse settings in varying ways, there are numerous words, terms, phrases, and jargon that are used interchangeably among the entities that utilize or have a need for emergency communication systems; but in most instances, these are all used to refer to the same or substantially similar concept. For example, use of the terms “emergency communications” and “disaster communications” definitively refer to the same concept, with the only potential difference lying in the connotation, or emotional meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Need">Need</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Need">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emergencies place demands on communication processes that are often significantly different from the demands of non-emergency circumstances. Emergencies often involve escalating and evolving events that demand high performance and flexibility from the systems that provide emergency communication services. Message prioritisation, automation of communication, fast message delivery, communication audit trails, and other capabilities are often required by each unique emergency situation. Inadequate emergency communications capabilities can have consequences that are inconvenient at best and disastrous at worst.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_studies.2C_failures_and_successes">Case studies, failures and successes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Case studies, failures and successes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_City_World_Trade_Center_attack">New York City World Trade Center attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New York City World Trade Center attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11_attack" title="September 11 attack">September 11 attack</a> in 2001, traditional telecommunications were stretched and overloaded. Phone networks along the entire <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> were congested into uselessness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> 911 operators were overwhelmed with calls and could do little more than offer encouragement because of the confusing information they were receiving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_radio_communications" title="September 11, 2001 radio communications">Communications</a> between emergency services personnel were limited by a lack of <a href="/wiki/Interoperability" title="Interoperability">interoperability</a> between departments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYFD" title="NYFD">fire-fighters</a> died when the towers collapsed because they couldn't receive the warning that the police officers received from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department" title="New York City Police Department">New York City Police Department</a> (NYPD) helicopters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">Amateur radio</a> played a large role in facilitating communications between the various emergency departments, which operated on different frequencies and protocols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="London_Underground_bombings">London Underground bombings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: London Underground bombings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the day of the <a href="/wiki/7_July_2005_London_bombings" title="7 July 2005 London bombings">7 July 2005 London bombings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">mobile phone networks</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Vodafone" title="Vodafone">Vodafone</a>, reached full capacity and were overloaded by 10:00 a.m., only an hour and ten minutes after the bombs went off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Because of an antiquated radio system, the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">damaged trains</a> were unable to communicate with the <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_London" title="Transport for London">Transport for London</a> control center or emergency personnel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> while senior emergency services managers, of the <a href="/wiki/London_Ambulance_Service" title="London Ambulance Service">London Ambulance Service</a> in particular, were forced to rely on the already overloaded mobile phone network because of the lack of <a href="/wiki/Digital_radio" title="Digital radio">digital radios</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/ACCOLC" title="ACCOLC">Access Overload Control</a>, implemented only in a 1 km area around <a href="/wiki/Aldgate_tube_station" title="Aldgate tube station">Aldgate Tube Station</a>, wasn't helpful because many officials didn't have ACCOLC-enabled mobile phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the aftermath, the <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a> determined the need for a digital radio <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Connect_Project" title="Connect Project">communications system</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> that can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airwave_(communications_network)" title="Airwave (communications network)">operate underground</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_Joplin.2C_Missouri_tornado">2011 Joplin, Missouri tornado</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2011 Joplin, Missouri tornado">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>161 people were killed and at least 990 injured when an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_Scale" title="Enhanced Fujita Scale">EF5</a> <a href="/wiki/Multiple-vortex_tornado" title="Multiple-vortex tornado">multiple-vortex tornado</a> hit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joplin_tornado" title="Joplin tornado">Joplin, Missouri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Because <a href="/wiki/Joplin,_Missouri" title="Joplin, Missouri">Joplin</a> is located in a <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">tornado-heavy area</a> of the country, many people considered the <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense_siren" title="Civil defense siren">tornado sirens</a> routine and ignored them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Instead, many residents waited until they received confirmation from another, non-routine, source, such as seeing the tornado, a radio or TV report, or hearing a second siren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In the aftermath, an assessment team recommended that emergency warnings take <a href="/wiki/Risk_perception" title="Risk perception">risk perception</a> into account to convey a sense of urgency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> Officials are considering ways to change the warning system to distinguish smaller tornadoes from more deadly ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hurricane Katrina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 5</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a>, hit <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, the emergency communications systems were completely destroyed, including power stations, internet servers, mobile phone towers, and 911 services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal</a> relief workers' satellite phones weren't <a href="/wiki/Interoperability" title="Interoperability">interoperable</a>, even when they did work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A few AM radio stations were able to continue broadcasting throughout the storm, notably <a href="/wiki/WWL_(AM)" title="WWL (AM)">WWL Radio</a>, which remained on the air by broadcasting from a closet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">Amateur radio</a> was instrumental in the rescue process and maintained signals when 911 communications were damaged or overloaded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia_Tech_Massacre">Virginia Tech Massacre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Virginia Tech Massacre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_Massacre" title="Virginia Tech Massacre">Virginia Tech Massacre</a>, which resulted in the death of 33 people, helped propel discourse for effective emergency communication systems in schools. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Polytechnic_Institute_and_State_University" title="Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University">Virginia Tech</a> had systems already in place, including <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">e-mail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Text_messaging" title="Text messaging">text notifications</a>, but lacked a cohesive plan for using them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> No warnings were sent out until after the event, two and a half hours after the initial shootings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Virginia Tech has since updated its emergency communications systems, especially public ones, since students can't always check e-mail in a timely fashion and professors often request that wireless devices be turned off in class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Because the massacre occurred within a 10-minute period, other schools have also implemented new, improved emergency communications systems with an emphasis on speed of communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_T.C5.8Dhoku_Earthquake">2011 Tōhoku Earthquake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2011 Tōhoku Earthquake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a>'s (JMA) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquake_Early_Warning" title="Earthquake Early Warning">early warning system</a>, which uses <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismometers</a>, was able to alert millions of people across <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> about the impending <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> via <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, mobile phone networks, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NTT_Docomo" title="NTT Docomo">Docomo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Au_(mobile_phone_company)" title="Au (mobile phone company)">AU</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SoftBank_Mobile" title="SoftBank Mobile">SoftBank</a>, and television, including both <a href="/wiki/NHK" title="NHK">NHK</a> channels and cable channels. The <a href="/wiki/Tsunami_warning_system" title="Tsunami warning system">tsunami warning system</a> alerted people shortly after, although the <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> was larger than expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In areas with <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> still intact, even though both landline and mobile phone lines were not functioning as might be expected, the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> was still accessible. In the hardest hit areas, particularly <a href="/wiki/Sendai" title="Sendai">Sendai</a> and other areas of <a href="/wiki/Miyagi_Prefecture" title="Miyagi Prefecture">Miyagi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iwate_Prefecture" title="Iwate Prefecture">Iwate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Prefecture" title="Fukushima Prefecture">Fukushima</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_Japan" title="Prefectures of Japan">Prefectures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">satellite phones</a> were often the only form of communication that functioned reliably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The following <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_meltdown" title="Nuclear meltdown">nuclear disaster</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant">Fukushima Daiichi Plant</a> was rife with communications problems. No communications plan was in place, internal communications were poor, external communications were slow, and the public quickly lost confidence in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Electric_Power_Company" title="Tokyo Electric Power Company">TEPCO</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The primary criticism was a failure of the government to release accurate information about the disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Calls were made for more transparency and promptness with future events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_Aurora_movie_theater_shooting">2012 Aurora movie theater shooting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2012 Aurora movie theater shooting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/2012_Aurora_shooting" title="2012 Aurora shooting">2012 Aurora shooting</a> on July 20, 2012, the gunman, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Eagan_Holmes" title="James Eagan Holmes">James Eagan Holmes</a>, released tear gas before opening fire during a showing of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_Rises" title="The Dark Knight Rises">The Dark Knight Rises</a></i>, causing many of the moviegoers to mistake the attack for part of the film's special effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> According to preliminary reports, the suspect initially went into the theater as a patron. He then exited and propped open an emergency exit, while he went to his car to get his bulletproof vest and weapons. He then attacked after reentry through the propped-open door.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In this type of situation, there could have been considerable warning. Technology exists that can warn about open doors (such as an emergency door that shouldn't normally be open), and can send clear warning and instructions to any number of devices, including a movie screen. There is also a significant opportunity to consider the use of the theater's <a href="/wiki/Fire_alarm_system" title="Fire alarm system">fire alarm system</a>. Shortly after the assailant began shooting, the building's fire alarms were sounded. The natural inclination, in response to a fire alarm, would be to evacuate the building, but in this case, the assailant was specifically targeting people who attempted to exit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hurricane Sandy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> hit <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_coast_of_the_united_states" title="East coast of the united states">surrounding area</a> on October 29, 2012, destroying thousands of houses and leaving millions without <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electricity</a> and thus without <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Landline" title="Landline">landline</a> communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> To prepare for the hurricane, many areas provided additional emergency help lines in case 911 wasn't available,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> has strengthened its ability to respond to a disaster since the communications problems during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and using <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> in its rescue efforts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> operators were on standby to provide emergency communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In the aftermath, up to 25% of mobile phone towers, network providers, and television stations were powerless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Communications have been steadily improving, however, although the hardest hit areas are still experiencing serious power outages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attributes">Attributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Attributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeliness_and_speed_of_delivery">Timeliness and speed of delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Timeliness and speed of delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Emergency" title="Emergency">emergency</a>, as defined by <a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam-Webster</a>, is an unforeseen combination of circumstances or the resulting state that calls for immediate action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> As such, it should be considered critically important that any communication about an emergency be timely and quick to disseminate, in order to mitigate damage or loss of life. For example, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_massacre" title="Virginia Tech massacre">Virginia Tech massacre</a>, about two hours had passed before the first communication (an <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a>) was sent to staff and students; and by that time, the gunman had already entered and secured a building in which he was shortly to begin his attack. In that case, it wasn't until about 20 minutes after the shootings began, that a <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeaker</a> announcement was made for people to take cover. In many cases, it's likely obvious that mere seconds and minutes are absolutely critical.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ease_of_use">Ease of use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ease of use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During a crisis, the people who use an ECS need to quickly and easily launch their notifications and they need to be able to do so in a way that securely provides them with confidence and an intuitive, familiar and easy-to-use interface that can be accessed from any location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> An emergency communication system that’s designed for non-technical users will ensure successful administration and usage; and during some life-threatening emergency situations, campus administrators must be able to react quickly and trigger the alert system swiftly. Yet emergency alert is probably among the least used and least familiar processes. Ease-of-use therefore is critical to the effectiveness of an emergency communication system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, in order to support a robust and capable emergency communication ability, this ease of use should not preclude the utilization of a complex, technologically advanced system. A sufficiently advanced system is required to coordinate multiple components to act in concert, to initiate and propagate emergency communications in any manner of ways. A distinguishing factor is in such a system bearing these advanced capabilities, while still being easy for the user to operate for both emergency and everyday communications <i>(so users can feel comfortable with it)</i> — not only for effective emergency communication, but also for an organization to realize the most <a href="/wiki/Return_on_investment" title="Return on investment">return on investment</a>, as well as the user being familiar enough with its operation as to effectively operate it under stressful emergency situations.such as in earthquake, tsunami, etc.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Affordability">Affordability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Affordability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Overall, the more affordable the cost of procuring, installing, and maintaining an emergency communication system is, the more prolific such systems may become; and the more prolific these systems are, the more likely it is that these systems will be available to aid in times of emergency in more locations. According to Federal Signal, beyond supporting emergency response, today’s mass notification systems have proven to be a valuable asset for everyday, non-emergency, intra- and inter-plant communications. This has become particularly evident in the deployment of interoperable, multi-device communications technology that not only enhances overall plant communications, but provides a host of useful software-based management and administrative tools. Additionally, many of the more traditional approaches to mass notification, i.e., public address and intercom systems, e-mail, and voice and text messaging, provide everyday functionality for plant communications and process control that represents attractive potential for return on investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Providing_instructions">Providing instructions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Providing instructions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A clearly needed attribute of any modern emergency communication system is the ability to not only provide notification of an emergency, but to also provide clear and actionable instructions for how to respond to an emergency. In a study by the Rehabilitation Engineering Research Center for Wireless Technologies, it was revealed that regardless of the initial form of notification, a secondary form was necessary before action would be taken. This supports the important observation that providing clear and concise instructions may reduce dependency on such secondary verification; and thus, providing instructions may save lives in an urgent emergency situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_audiences_or_recipients">Specific audiences or recipients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Specific audiences or recipients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Emergencies often require delivery of different versions of the same communication at the same time. For example, in an armed hostage-taking incident, occupants of a building may need to receive instructions to lock and barricade the door until further notice, while first responders to the incident need to be aware of the lockdown instructions and be provided more specific details of the hostage-taking event to inform their actions.</p>
<p>Using the hostage-taking example, some of the more modern emergency communication systems such as Siemens Sygnal or MessageNet Connections state the ability to deliver a single message that provides full details to first responders while filtering that same message to provide more limited instructions to lock doors to the general public. By utilizing a single message that segregates content between types of users fewer messages have to be created and sent, which may also save time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_communication_paths.2Fredundancy">Multiple communication paths/redundancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Multiple communication paths/redundancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There should be multiple means of delivering emergency information so that if one whould fail, others may get through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Also, according to the Partnership for Public Warning, research shows clearly that more than one channel of communication will be consulted by people at risk in order to confirm the need for action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The public expects to be contacted in a variety of ways. As evidenced by various historical and recent events, besides phone calls and emails, citizens also expect to be able to use and be reached via <a href="/wiki/Text_messaging" title="Text messaging">text messaging</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a>. In one exemplary incident, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oak_Creek_shooting" title="Oak Creek shooting">2012 Wisconsin Sikh temple shooting</a>, barricaded victims relied on sending text messages for help, in addition to traditional phone calls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In addition, the public may look to <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> as another vehicle to receive messages and check in on updates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, the Partnership for Public Warning states, “A single warning is frequently insufficient to move people to action, especially if it cannot be confirmed by direct observation. For most people the first warning received captures their attention and triggers a search for corroboration, but cannot be relied on to elicit the desired behavior. Scientific research supports the common-sense observation that people are disinclined to risk being fooled by a single alarm that might prove false or accidental. Effective warning requires the coordinated use of multiple channels of communication.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interoperability">Interoperability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Interoperability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RS-232.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/RS-232.jpeg/170px-RS-232.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/RS-232.jpeg/255px-RS-232.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/RS-232.jpeg/340px-RS-232.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RS-232.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A fire control system's RS-232 data connection which an emergency communication system may interface with</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>To support these attributes, a "unified" emergency communication system should be able to connect to and communicate with other related systems, hence the term. According to the Partnership for Public Warning, a fundamental problem is the lack of technical and procedural interoperability among warning originators, system providers, delivery systems, and warning recipients. Originators of warnings must undertake expensive, redundant tasks using multiple, dissimilar tools and techniques to take full advantage of today's warning systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, there are multiple ways that an emergency communication system might obtain an original warning. One example of this might be the case of a building’s fire control system dispatching a notice that a smoke detector has activated. A properly designed emergency communication system should be able to receive that notice and process it into a message that the building’s occupants can understand and take action on in order to save life and property. Another example might be of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> sending an <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Alert_System" title="Emergency Alert System">EAS</a> severe weather warning (e.g. via <a href="/wiki/RSS" title="RSS">RSS</a> feed or similar). In that case, the emergency communication system should be able to process and disseminate the warning in a similar way as the smoke detector example, and inform people of what actions to take.</p>
<p>The ability to interoperate should also consider the need to be both forward and backward compatible with older and yet-to-be-conceived technologies. Introduction of a system that cannot interoperate with previously deployed equipment creates potentially serious barriers to effective operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_versus_service">Product versus service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Product versus service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An emergency communication system may be composed of a product and its associated hardware and software, as owned by the entity using it (e.g., an on-site paging network), or as a service owned and provided by a third-party (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">cellular</a> carrier’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMS" title="SMS">SMS</a> network). Each have their own advantages and disadvantages; however, despite perception otherwise, services have some major inherent problems when it comes to effective emergency communications. For instance, using SMS as one example, due to the architecture of cellular networks, text messaging services would not be able to handle a large volume of communications in a short period of time, making this particular type of service a potentially ineffective emergency communication method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Premises_based_versus_non-premises_based">Premises based versus non-premises based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Premises based versus non-premises based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Premises based emergency communication systems are those which primarily or wholly exist in the same geographical or structural area as it serves, while non-premises based emergency communication systems are those which exist in a different geographical or structural area. There are advantages and disadvantages of each. Often, non-premises based systems are slower than those that are premises-based, because at the very least, the different locations need to be connected via (usually public) data networks, which may be susceptible to disruption or delay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG/110px-Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG/165px-Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG/220px-Civil_Defense_Siren.JPG 2x" width="110"/></a>

</div>
<p>With the growth of populations and the evolution of technology, the methods for communicating emergency situations have also changed, as has the definition for what might constitute an “emergency.” These methods would also very likely depend on a particular region’s culture and location, as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_systems">Early systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Early systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In America’s formative years, common means of emergency communications may have mostly consisted of church bells being rung or messengers on horseback. Later, as technology developed, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> became a nearly instant method of communicating. From there, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_communications" title="Radio communications">radio</a> communications, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telephones" title="Telephones">telephones</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siren_(noisemaker)" title="Siren (noisemaker)">sirens</a> became commonplace. After the surprise <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> in 1941, Civil Defense sirens became popular and resulted in their widespread use in military bases and towns across America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> A particular weakness of these systems is that they largely lack the ability to inform people what they should do.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fire_alarm_system" title="Fire alarm system">Fire alarm systems</a> were first developed around the late 1800s and other related life-safety detectors associated with those systems (e.g., duct detectors, <a href="/wiki/Heat_detector" title="Heat detector">heat detectors</a>, etc.) were developed around the early 1900s. These constitute the first automated systems used in public and private buildings that are in normal and widespread use today. These systems, although originally designed for fire, have also been used for many other types of emergencies, sometimes effectively and sometimes not. For instance, if a fire alarm has been activated (as a general warning device) during an <a href="/wiki/Active_shooter" title="Active shooter">active shooter</a> incident, the typical reaction to evacuate may not be the safest course of action; instead, a <a href="/wiki/Shelter_in_place" title="Shelter in place">shelter in place</a> action may be better.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> As an example, during the <a href="/wiki/2012_Aurora_shooting" title="2012 Aurora shooting">2012 Aurora shooting</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mass_shooting" title="Mass shooting">mass shooting</a> event that happened at the <a href="/wiki/Century_Theatres" title="Century Theatres">Century 16</a> movie theater in <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Colorado" title="Aurora, Colorado">Aurora, Colorado</a>, the assailant began targeting victims as they attempted to exit the theater; in which case, it would have been unwise to heed the fire alarm, that was sounding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and evacuate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_systems">Modern systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Modern systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, modern communication tools such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smartphones" title="Smartphones">smartphones</a>, flat-panel <a href="/wiki/Digital_signage" title="Digital signage">digital signage</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Text-to-speech" title="Text-to-speech">text-to-speech</a> (among many others) are changing the way in which people are notified about emergencies. Of added value, with these more modern tools, is the ability to provide more specific instructions; so that, instead of merely notifying people about an emergency, it is now possible to provide specific instructions on what to do in order to mitigate the effects of an emergency. Furthermore, those instructions might even be customized for those peoples' specific and unique circumstances. For example, smart-phones may have geo-location abilities that would allow a map to be shown of safe locations (and perhaps routing there-to), relative to those devices' specific users — all with a singular alert being sent as the source of those warnings.</p>

<p>Perhaps the oldest or most basic form of public communication is that which includes such staples as over-the-air television, sirens, radio, etc. More modern components (using the same concept) might include lights and Giant Voice systems. These all have one thing in common: they broadcast indiscriminately to anyone who has the means to receive the message; whether they are simply in the immediate area or require some sort of receiving device.</p>
<p>Broadcast technologies use <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point</a> communications methodology and may either require <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> or not. Examples of broadcast technologies requiring infrastructure might include such things as <a href="/wiki/Reverse_911" title="Reverse 911">Reverse 911</a> and <a href="/wiki/Network_affiliate" title="Network affiliate">broadcast-affiliate networks</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure-independent">Infrastructure-independent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Infrastructure-independent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Broadcast technologies that do not depend on man-made infrastructure to convey communication may be least susceptible to disruption during disasters and emergencies. Some examples of infrastructure-independent technologies are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Short-wave Radio</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Shortwave_radio" title="Shortwave radio">Short-wave (or Amateur) radio</a> is a relatively long range method of communicating using radio waves. Because of the nature of radio wave propagation, communications made via short-wave radio can be intercepted and heard by anyone with the proper equipment and knowledge. However, due to their reliance only on electrical power (which can be obtained via batteries, solar, and other alternative means) and Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, they are ideal for some of the worst case emergency scenarios. One disadvantage may be that one requires <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_license#Licensing" title="Amateur radio license">training and licensing</a> to transmit using a short-wave radio, in many countries.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Two-way Radio</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Two-way_radio" title="Two-way radio">Two-way radio</a> consists of at least two devices (typically portable and hand-held) that are capable of transmitting and receiving communications to each other, using radio waves. Similar to short-wave, this type of communication is also able to be intercepted and heard by anyone with the proper equipment. However, unlike short-wave, this communication equipment is restricted by range (usually several miles, at most), but is relatively simple to operate and the main power source is usually provided by batteries. Two-way radios are in-use by many emergency responders on a daily basis, so they require minimal concentration to operate in the stress of an emergency situation, since the person operating it is already familiar with it. Additionally, many non-professionals may use unlicensed two-way radios, as is common with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens%27_band_radio" title="Citizens' band radio">CB radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Family_Radio_Service" title="Family Radio Service">Family Radio Service</a> or <a href="/wiki/PMR446" title="PMR446">PMR446</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Weather Radio</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>A <a href="/wiki/Weather_radio" title="Weather radio">weather radio</a> is a device which receives normal and emergency weather broadcasts. It may automatically turn on whenever an emergency is eminent or occurring, providing an alarm as well as a description of the situation. Anyone with a weather radio device can receive these broadcasts.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Internet Based Communication</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>There is increasingly an opportunity to use infrastructure-independent network connection, sometimes called <i>Network-centric emergency notification</i>, for emergency communication. The emergence of initiatives such as Google's <a href="/wiki/Project_Loon" title="Project Loon">Project Loon</a> offer opportunities for networked communication when infrastructure-dependent communication is hindered in emergency situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Each device has its own <a class="external text" href="http://www.zipscanners.com/resources/emergency-communication-devices/" rel="nofollow">pros and cons</a> given the emergency situation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure-dependent">Infrastructure-dependent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Infrastructure-dependent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Horn_Speaker.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Horn_Speaker.jpg/170px-Horn_Speaker.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Horn_Speaker.jpg/255px-Horn_Speaker.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Horn_Speaker.jpg/340px-Horn_Speaker.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Horn_Speaker.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Audio public address speakers</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Broadcast technologies that depend on man-made infrastructure to convey communication are susceptible to disruption if any part of that infrastructure is overloaded, damaged or otherwise destroyed. Some examples of infrastructure-dependent technologies are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Audio Public Address Systems</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>A system which can provide audio (usually spoken language) messaging capability, usually consisting of microphone devices, wiring, and speakers installed in public areas of buildings. These systems are typically located indoors or in smaller outdoor areas with multiple speakers, due to their speakers' individual volume being too limited for large areas. Usually connected together by wiring requiring electricity, these systems may be vulnerable to electrical disruption or any other event which results in the wires being severed or disconnected.</dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LED_Sign.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="4808" height="65" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/LED_Sign.JPG/350px-LED_Sign.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/LED_Sign.JPG/525px-LED_Sign.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/LED_Sign.JPG/700px-LED_Sign.JPG 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LED_Sign.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
LED sign, in a wall-mounted orientation, depicting an emergency and the resulting action that should be taken to mitigate the emergency</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b>L.E.D. Electronic Signs</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>LED electronic signs use <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> to display messages when connected over a network to an emergency communication system. LED signs, when deployed in large numbers on a network, can be used as visual alternatives to traditional audio public address systems. LED Signs are able to communicate effectively in loud environments where audio PA systems can be ineffective. When deciding whether or not to include LED electronic signs as part of an emergency communication system, it is important to realize that a lack of hearing-impaired persons does not negate the need for visual signs. Explosions and other events can render many hearing-able people effectively deaf, necessitating the inclusion of a visual alternative to audio PA systems. LED electronic signs have multiple valuable attributes that enhance public communications:
<ul>
<li>Some emergency communication systems can deliver, share, and prioritize the display of messages on LED signs that have been sent by multiple people and systems.</li>
<li>LED signs, when connected to an emergency communication system, have the ability to project the communication in a specific direction while loud speakers generally radiate communication in many directions.</li>
<li>LED signs are manufactured in a large variety of shapes and sizes, with some models designed specifically for indoor use and very bright models designed for outdoor use.</li>
<li>Because LEDs consume very little electricity, <a href="/wiki/Power_over_Ethernet" title="Power over Ethernet">power over Ethernet</a> (PoE) and power over RS232 can be used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">UPS</a> power located in wiring closets to keep these devices functioning for a significant period of time during a general power outage in a building.</li>
<li>Like audio PA systems, visual PA systems composed of LED signs are network-attached appliances that, unlike computer screen popup messages <i>(more information about this technology, below)</i>, text messages, phone calls, etc., represent `Always On` technologies, which can be relied on to deliver the communication in real time without the risk that the device might be off, in use, or out of power.</li>
<li>LED signs are available that can display messages in a single color, red-green-yellow characters, or full color, which allows for color coding of emergency messages when desired. These types of signs can be relatively affordable compared to digital signs and are easy to install, making them a common choice for organizations wishing to use electronic forms of visual communication. Common means of connecting these devices include <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> or serial communication cabling.</li>
<li>In combination with audio PA systems, these devices can be used to achieve compliance with the <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act_of_1990" title="Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990">ADA</a> requirements for equal access to communications.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>Government, public and private entities may use LED electronic signs for any purpose ranging from advertising to emergency management. For emergency utilization of these types of signs, it is often necessary to electronically control them in a unified and coordinated manner, using one of or a mixture of specialized hardware and software. There exist at least a few commercial methods that advertise this capability, for example Siemens Sygnal, Inova Solutions, and MessageNet Connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Combination Audio/Visual Public Address Devices</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Any device which combines the audio capability of a PA system with the visual capability of an electronic sign (usually of the L.E.D. variety). These, too, rely on infrastructure to operate.</dd>
</dl>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="441" data-file-width="640" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg/170px-Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg/255px-Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg/340px-Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Public_Services_Centre_screen_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789387.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
LCD digital sign</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Digital Signage</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Throughout the first decade of the twenty-first century, <a href="/wiki/Plasma_display" title="Plasma display">plasma televisions</a> and other flat-panel <a href="/wiki/LCD_television" title="LCD television">LCD</a> televisions and <a href="/wiki/Computer_monitor" title="Computer monitor">monitors</a> have become commonplace in businesses, hospitals, schools, post-secondary institutions, government, airports, shopping malls, and financial institutions, etc.;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> with that prominence, they have begun to play a major role in emergency communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Similar to combination audio/vidual PA devices, yet much more advanced, <a href="/wiki/Digital_signage" title="Digital signage">digital signage</a> monitors are capable of displaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-resolution" title="High-resolution">high-resolution</a> videos, pictures, multimedia presentations, text and other <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">high-definition</a> media. Typically digital signage can provide video, audio, presentation graphics and web page content abilities that can support the communication of an emergency. In addition, the graphical multimedia capabilities of digital signage can enable emergency <a href="/wiki/Wayfinding" title="Wayfinding">wayfinding</a> or egress routing that can be customized to the event and location of the emergency and the affected people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Digital signage is often more expensive than relatively simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L.E.D." title="L.E.D.">L.E.D.</a> <a href="/wiki/Electronic_signage" title="Electronic signage">electronic signs</a>, which makes it more sensible to garner as much utility from it as possible for maximum <a href="/wiki/Return_on_investment" title="Return on investment">return on investment</a>. Over time, though, this technology has become more affordable, resulting in digital signage being used in more venues,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> usually for non-emergency purposes such as displaying news, weather, directions, etc. Other emerging uses of digital signage related to emergency communication systems include: displaying building <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floorplan" title="Floorplan">floorplans</a>, maps, <a href="/wiki/Emergency_evacuation#Small_scale_evacuations" title="Emergency evacuation">evacuation</a> routes, and <a href="/wiki/First_responder" title="First responder">first responder</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situational_awareness" title="Situational awareness">situational awareness</a> (such as showing a firefighter where a fire has been detected). Some commercial solutions have also begun taking advantage of the sensibility in combining emergency and non-emergency communications, such as <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> Sygnal, Exhibio, FourWinds, <a href="/wiki/MessageNet_systems" title="MessageNet systems">MessageNet</a> Connections and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scala,_Inc" title="Scala, Inc">Scala</a>. Currently, there do not appear to be any non-commercial methods to address this.</dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="960" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg/170px-MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg/255px-MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg/340px-MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MNEC_High_Power_Speaker_Array_-_Cropped.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Speakers used for Giant Voice systems</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Giant Voice Systems</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>A system focused on providing auditory messaging capability for large outdoor areas, being able to project voice and sounds over large distances, without the need for a large number of speakers. These systems are commonly used on military bases and chemical manufacturing plants, for example. These technologies allow specific instructions to be broadcast over a large area, however they may be subject to substantial echo and weather-related effects; and furthermore, environmental noise may interfere with their effectiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> This forces unnatural speech, on the part of the warning originator, and may render the communication difficult for the listener to comprehend. This fact has prompted some to primarily rely on tones and coded signals that the audience must be familiar with in advance. Even though these can be heard over a large area, the high-powered speakers require a connection to the communication originator via some sort of infrastructure, meaning they might have a certain level of vulnerability to disruption in the collection infrastructure. The alternative to the use of Giant Voice systems is to deploy large arrays of smaller lower power speakers, this approach also allows for greater specificity of message by location. When properly synchronized these speaker arrays can deliver more comprehensible speech, however these arrays will involve larger amounts of supporting infrastructure. Siemens and Federal Signal are two of the major commercial providers of Giant Voice systems being actively deployed at military bases, government facilities and industry, currently; with Federal Signal being one of the first providers of these types of systems, after the Pearl Harbor attack.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communication_devices">Communication devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Communication devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are primarily two major types of communication devices: those for individual people and those for groups of people. Public Communication Devices are the devices that are designed to deliver a communication to more than one person as a single process at the same time. Examples include a digital electronic sign, a loud speaker that is part of a PA system, or a large flat panel display on a wall. A private communication device is a device that is designed to deliver communication to one person at a time through a single process. Typically, a single person is in control of such a device which is usually not shared. Examples include a cell phone, a text message on the cell phone, an email, or a message over a 2 way radio.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_and_shared_devices">Public and shared devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Public and shared devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Public communication refers to the conveyance of messages to people, in such a way that anyone may receive the communication at nearly the same time as anyone else, typically using a common device. The most common way of facilitating public communications is by using devices that are incorporated into some public venue, such as public-address systems or digital signage. Using public devices for the purpose of public warning empowers people at risk to take actions to reduce losses from natural hazards, accidents, and acts of terrorism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_devices">Private devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Private devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Private" means the delivery of messages to a specific individual, in a private manner or in such a way that even those nearby may not get the message. Common ways of facilitating private communications involve devices such as telephones or electronic mail.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="640" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png/170px-Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png/255px-Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png/340px-Modern_Smart_Phone_Emergency_Text_Message.png 2x" width="170"/></a>

Example of an emergency SMS text message on a mobile phone. This exemplifies one potential weakness of using SMS for emergency communications, namely that messages can be forged.</div>
</div>
</div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_phones">Mobile phones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Mobile phones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>Mobiles phones may be considered a private communication device, because they are usually associated with or owned by a single individual. One possible limitation of using mobile phones for emergency communications, in a bomb-threat situation, for example, might include the potential of cellular networks being disabled for fear that a bomb might be detonated using a cellular phone.</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Line-based_phones">Line-based phones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Line-based phones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>These phones, in whole or in part (e.g. a cordless telephone with base station), are physically connected to and rely upon a wire (often called a landline) to operate. As with mobile phones, these are often associated with a single person, family or business. Regardless, this method may be considered private due to a single phone device’s inherent weakness in reaching large numbers of people at the same time.</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="SMS.2FText_messaging">SMS/Text messaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: SMS/Text messaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>SMS text messages should be considered a type of private communication because they are directed toward a specific mobile phone number; and, thus, they are designed to reach one person at a time without the general public knowing anything about the message. A limitation in using SMS messaging for emergencies might be that it doesn't meet the needs of emergency communication: that is, it must be highly reliable, be secure, have excellent access control, and high-speed delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_Media_-_Syndicated_Emergency_Communications">Social Media - Syndicated Emergency Communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Social Media - Syndicated Emergency Communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> is an online social networking and micro-blogging service utilizing SMS text messaging. While it wasn’t intended or designed for high performance communication, the idea that it could be used for emergency communication certainly was not lost on the originators, who knew that the service could have wide-reaching effects early on, when the San-Francisco, California-based company used it to communicate during earthquakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> may have potential for emergency communication, as it has a large involved user-base.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_mail">Electronic mail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Electronic mail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>Email should be considered a type of private communication because it is sent to a specific email address, which is associated with a person. Emails can be sent to multiple people, but even this results in multiple individual copies of the email that are ultimately sent to their individual recipients.</dd>
</dl>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency-oriented_instant_messengers_and_computer_screen_pop-ups">Emergency-oriented instant messengers and computer screen pop-ups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Emergency-oriented instant messengers and computer screen pop-ups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>Personal computer instant messengers have become popular and inexpensive technologies by which to deliver emergency communications to broad or specific audiences in a short period of time. With this technology, emergency communications will "pop up" on the personal computer screens as a new window that sits on top of any other window that may be open on the screen at the time. This technology utilizes LAN and/or WAN networks to deliver short messages, often less than about 120 characters in length, typically in real time. The speed of modern LAN and WAN technologies and the shortness of such messages makes it possible to deliver emergency messages to thousands of computer screens in less than a minute's time.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>In private communications, a computer pop-up can be targeted to a specific user, i.e. one that is logged into the computer or is associated with that computer in some way. The emergency communication system should be able to define the relationship between the user and his or her computer. A potential limitation of this is that if a user is not logged in, then the operating system may not allow any messages to display.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>In the case of public-oriented emergency communications, a computer pop-up ability might prove useful for <a href="/wiki/Public_computer" title="Public computer">public computers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interactive_kiosk" title="Interactive kiosk">interactive kiosks</a> where more than one person might use it, or for computers with displays that are in prominent locations. In this case, the pop-up might also need to be easily read from a distance, and be able to get attention — similar to how a digital sign may be used. These might be structured as a notification window with static non-moving text, or as a window with scrolling text.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Emergency communications may require certain attributes that are not commonly found in standard instant messengers; here is a list of attributes that may prove valuable to emergency-oriented instant messengers:</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li>Prevents the user from killing the program, which would prevent the delivery of emergency messages.</li>
<li>Delivers emergency messages in a large font that can be easily read a substantial distance from the screen.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li>Scrolls the message across the screen to attract attention and to ensure that the entire message can be displayed without any user effort. Scrolling the message may also allow for the use of a large font and readability from a distance.</li>

<li>Supports many priorities for messages, ensuring that emergency messages are not lost in the crowd of common messages that may appear in the computer screen.</li>

</li>

<dl>
<dd>Using instant messaging for emergency communications may also present some limitations:</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li>If the PC is not connected to the network, it will not display the message. This means that the network is another potential point of failure.</li>
<li>If the PC is logged off or if the screen saver is active and requires a password to access the screen, then conventional knowledge<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> posits that it will prevent the delivery of the emergency message.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>In addition to some well-known instant messengers, for example <a href="/wiki/ICQ" title="ICQ">ICQ</a> and <a href="/wiki/AOL_Instant_Messenger" title="AOL Instant Messenger">AIM</a>, a variety of specialized instant messengers exist that are intended for use in emergency communications. The commercially-available products sold by RedAlert and Desktop Alert are examples of specialized instant messengers that claim to provide a relatively narrow set of message delivery capabilities within a smaller subset of an overall ECS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Other commercial offerings <a href="/wiki/MessageNet_systems" title="MessageNet systems">MessageNet</a> Connections, React Systems, and <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> Sygnal also provide instant messengers that are sold to be used in conjunction with other communication equipment such as phones, and digital signage as part of an integrated and whole emergency communication system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_communication_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Depending on the location, time, and nature of the emergency, a large variety of limitations could present themselves when it comes to communicating details of an emergency and any resultant actions that may need to be taken to protect life and property. For example, an audio public address system might be rendered ineffective if the emergency happens to be an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosive</a> event which renders most or all of those affected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf" title="Deaf">deaf</a>. Another common example might be the limitation of a fire alarm’s siren component in a deaf school. Yet another example of a limitation could be the overloading of public services (such as cellular phone networks), resulting in the delay of vital SMS messages until they are too late. An effective emergency communication system should arguably be able to overcome as many of these potential limitations, as possible.</p>
