<p>The <b>Suzuka Line</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">鈴鹿線</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kintetsu Suzuka-sen</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a railway line of the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Japan#Major_private_railways" title="Rail transport in Japan">Japanese private railway</a> company <a href="/wiki/Kintetsu_Railway" title="Kintetsu Railway">Kintetsu Railway</a>, connecting <a href="/wiki/Ise-Wakamatsu_Station" title="Ise-Wakamatsu Station">Ise-Wakamatsu Station</a> (<a href="/wiki/Suzuka,_Mie" title="Suzuka, Mie">Suzuka, Mie</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Hiratach%C5%8D_Station" title="Hiratachō Station">Hiratachō Station</a> (Suzuka, Mie Prefecture) in Japan.</p>
<p>The line connects with the <a href="/wiki/Nagoya_Line_(Kintetsu)" title="Nagoya Line (Kintetsu)">Nagoya Line</a> at Ise-Wakamatsu Station.</p>
<p>Students and factory workers make up the majority of the line's ridership and trains are generally only crowded during <a href="/wiki/Rush_hour" title="Rush hour">rush hour</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suzuka_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kambe_Line">Kambe Line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suzuka_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kambe Line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Suzuka Line was originally built by <a href="/wiki/Ise_Electric_Railway" title="Ise Electric Railway">Ise Electric Railway</a> (<i>Iseden</i>) in the 1920s and was known as the <b>Iseden Kambe Line</b> (伊勢電神戸線 <i>Iseden Kambe-sen</i>) and for many years the track ended at Ise-Kambe Station (what is now <a href="/wiki/Suzukashi_Station" title="Suzukashi Station">Suzukashi Station</a>). <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">Steam locomotives</a> were used on the line for its first two years of operation but were soon replaced when the line was <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">electrified</a> in 1927. Though the line was built by Iseden, ownership of the Kambe Line was passed between various railway companies during the late 1930s and early 1940s due to many mergers occurring within the Japanese private railway industry at that time. It came under the ownership of Kintetsu in 1944.</p>
<p>Kintetsu made some improvements to the line in the late 1950s and 1960s. The <a href="/wiki/Track_gauge" title="Track gauge">track gauge</a> on the line, originally <a href="/wiki/3_ft_6_in_gauge_railways" title="3 ft 6 in gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">1,067 mm</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">3 ft 6 in</span>) was widened to <span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span>) so that it could connect directly with the Nagoya Line, also widened to <span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span> at that time. A few years later, the line was extended, two new stations were built, and <a href="/wiki/Hiratach%C5%8D_Station" title="Hiratachō Station">Hiratachō Station</a> became the new terminus. It was at this time that the line received its current name.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suzuka_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>December 20, 1925 - <a href="/wiki/Ise_Electric_Railway" title="Ise Electric Railway">Ise Railway</a> (<i>Iseden</i>) opens the line as the Iseden Kambe Line (Ise-Wakamatsu ~ Ise-Kambe).</li>
<li>December 26, 1927 - Electrification of the entire line completed.</li>
<li>September 15, 1936 - <a href="/wiki/Sang%C5%AB_Express_Electric_Railway" title="Sangū Express Electric Railway">Sangū Express Electric Railway</a> (<i>Sankyū</i>) acquires Iseden and all of its lines. Line is officially renamed Ise Kambe Line.</li>
<li>March 15, 1941 - <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Osaka_Electric_Railroad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Osaka Electric Railroad (page does not exist)">Osaka Electric Railroad</a> (<i>Daiki</i>) and Sankyū merge to form <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kansai_Express_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kansai Express Railway (page does not exist)">Kansai Express Railway</a> (<i>Kankyū</i>). Line officially renamed Kambe Line.</li>
<li>June 1, 1944 - Kankyū changes its name to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinki_Nippon_Railway" title="Kinki Nippon Railway">Kinki Nippon Railway</a> (<i>Kintetsu</i>). Line officially renamed Kintetsu Kambe Line.</li>
<li>November 23, 1959 - <a href="/wiki/Track_gauge" title="Track gauge">Gauge</a> along entire line widened from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrow_gauge_railway" title="Narrow gauge railway">1067 mm</a> to <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">1435 mm</a>.</li>
<li>April 8, 1963 - Ise-Kambe ~ Hiratachō extension opens. Ise-Kambe Station officially renamed Suzukashi Station. Line officially renamed Kintetsu Suzuka Line.</li>
<li>October 17, 1968 - <a href="/wiki/Automatic_train_stop" title="Automatic train stop">ATS system</a> activated on entire line.</li>
<li>March 16, 1995 - Maximum speed on the line raised from 65 km/h to 80 km/h; total ride time from end-to-end reduced by one minute.</li>
<li>June 13, 1998 - <i>One man</i> (conductor-less) train service begins.</li>
</ul>
