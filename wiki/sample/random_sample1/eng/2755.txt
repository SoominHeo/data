<p>The <b>long-tongued nectar bat</b> (<i>Macroglossus minimus</i>), also known as the <b>northern blossom bat</b>, <b>honey nectar bat</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>least blossom-bat</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>dagger-toothed long-nosed fruit bat</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> and <b>lesser long-tongued fruit bat</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Megabat" title="Megabat">megabat</a>. <i>M. minimus</i> is one of the smallest species in the family Pteropodidae, with an average length of 60–85 mm. It has a reddish-brown colouring with relatively long hair compared to the other species. The hair on the abdomen is a lighter colour, and a dark brown stripe runs bilaterally down the top of the head and back.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tongued_nectar_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>M. minimus</i> represents about 14% of the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruit_bat" title="Fruit bat">fruit bats</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its wide geographical range includes <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Malaysia" title="Peninsular Malaysia">Peninsular Malaysia</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. In Borneo, it had been recorded from <a href="/wiki/Kota_Kinabalu" title="Kota Kinabalu">Kota Kinabalu</a>, Sepilok, Sukau, and <a href="/wiki/Tawau" title="Tawau">Tawau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a>; Bandar Seri Begawan in <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>; Bario, Niah and <a href="/wiki/Bako_National_Park" title="Bako National Park">Bako</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a>; Gunung Kenepi, Kutai, and Sungai Tengah in <a href="/wiki/Kalimantan" title="Kalimantan">Kalimantan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payne85_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-payne85-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>M. minimus</i> has not been recorded in flocks, which suggest they live in small group or alone. It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, which it can obtain from <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banana_flower" title="Banana flower">banana flowers</a> in Malaysia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payne85_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-payne85-4">[4]</a></sup> Ecologically, the long-tongued nectar bat plays a major role as <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinator</a> of many trees, including the families <a href="/wiki/Bignoniaceae" title="Bignoniaceae">Bignoniaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bombacaceae" title="Bombacaceae">Bombacaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leguminosae" title="Leguminosae">Leguminosae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musaceae" title="Musaceae">Musaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">Myrtaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sonneratiaceae" title="Sonneratiaceae">Sonneratiaceae</a> in peninsular Malaysia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-start-marshall76_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-start-marshall76-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>M. minimus</i> has been recorded at elevations up to 1000 m near coastal mangroves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowak94_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nowak94-6">[6]</a></sup> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipterocarp" title="Dipterocarp">dipterocarp</a> forests, and in lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_forest" title="Montane forest">montane forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payne85_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-payne85-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tongued_nectar_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of total captures, males constituted 53% and females 47%. About 77% were adults.</p>
<p>Sexually active males have enlarged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testes" title="Testes">testes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyestrous" title="Polyestrous">polyestrous</a> females have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breeding_period" title="Breeding period">breeding period</a> of 140 to 160 days. Estimates for the <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> period for <i>M. minimus</i> is approximately 120  days (± 10 days), <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">lactation</a> occurs for 60 to 70 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-start74_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-start74-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negros_Island" title="Negros Island">Negros Island, Philippines</a>, females studies produced two or three young per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heideman87_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-heideman87-8">[8]</a></sup> The species reproduces <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aseasonal" title="wikt:aseasonal">aseasonally</a> (throughout the year) and synchronously in response to food abundance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-start74_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-start74-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heideman87_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-heideman87-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunnell96_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunnell96-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flannery90_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-flannery90-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External_measurements">External measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tongued_nectar_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: External measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For young bats, the forearm grows at 0.24 mm per day and weight is gained at gain 0.07 g per day. A free-flying immature bat has an forearm length 35.2 mm and weighs around 8.6 g.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunnell96_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-gunnell96-9">[9]</a></sup> The length of the head and body in adults is 60–85 mm (with the head being 26–28 mm in length), the length of the forearm is 40–43 mm, and the weight is 12–18 g.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medway78_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-medway78-11">[11]</a></sup> It is shorter and lighter than <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroglossus_sobrinus" title="Macroglossus sobrinus">Macroglossus sobrinus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowak94_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nowak94-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
