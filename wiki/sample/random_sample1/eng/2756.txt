<p><b>Gildas</b> (c. 500–570)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dates_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dates-5">[a]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-date2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-date2-6">[b]</a></sup> — also known as <b>Gildas the Wise</b> or <i><b>Gildas Sapiens</b></i> — was a 6th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" title="Britons (historic)">British</a> monk best known for his scathing religious <a href="/wiki/Polemic" title="Polemic">polemic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i>, which recounts the history of the Britons before and during <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">the coming of the Saxons</a>. He is one of the best-documented figures of the Christian church in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-Roman" title="Sub-Roman">sub-Roman</a> period, and was renowned for his Biblical knowledge and literary style. In his later life, he emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> where he founded a monastery known as <a href="/wiki/Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys" title="Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys">St. Gildas de Rhuys</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hagiography">Hagiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gildas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hagiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Differing versions of the <i>Life of Saint Gildas</i> exist, but both agree that he was born in what is now Scotland on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">River Clyde</a>, and that he was the son of a royal family. These works were written in the eleventh and twelfth centuries and are regarded by scholars as unhistorical. He is now thought to have his origins further south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> In his own work, he claims to have been born the same year as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mount_Badon" title="Battle of Mount Badon">Battle of Mount Badon</a>. He was educated at a monastic center, possibly <a href="/wiki/Cor_Tewdws" title="Cor Tewdws">Cor Tewdws</a> under <a href="/wiki/Illtud" title="Illtud">St. Illtud</a>, where he chose to forsake his royal heritage and embrace monasticism. He became a renowned teacher, converting many to Christianity and founding numerous churches and monasteries throughout Britain and Ireland. He is thought to have made a pilgrimage to Rome before emigrating to Brittany, where he took on the life of a <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a>. However, his life of solitude was short-lived, and pupils soon sought him out and begged him to teach them. He eventually founded a monastery for these students at Rhuys, where he wrote <i>De Excidio Britanniae,</i> criticising British rulers and exhorting them to put off their sins and embrace true Christian faith. He is thought to have died at Rhuys, and was buried there.</p>
<p>There are two different historical versions of the life of Gildas, the first written by an anonymous monk in the 9th century, and the other written by <a href="/wiki/Caradoc_of_Llancarfan" title="Caradoc of Llancarfan">Caradoc of Llancarfan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edmonds_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-edmonds-8">[6]</a></sup> in the middle of the 12th century. Some historians have attempted to explain the differences in the versions by saying that there were two saints named Gildas, but the more general opinion is that there was only one St. Gildas and that the discrepancies between the two versions can be accounted for by the fact that they were written several centuries apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edmonds_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-edmonds-8">[6]</a></sup> The 9th century <i>Rhuys Life</i> is generally accepted as being more accurate.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhuys_Life"><i>Rhuys Life</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gildas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rhuys Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The First <i>Life</i> of St. Gildas was written by an unnamed monk at the monastery which Gildas founded in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys" title="Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys">Rhuys</a>, Brittany in the 9th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Life_of_Gildas_by_the_Monk_of_Ruys_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Life_of_Gildas_by_the_Monk_of_Ruys-9">[7]</a></sup> According to this tradition, Gildas is the son of Caunus, king of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alt_Clut" title="Alt Clut">Alt Clut</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">Hen Ogledd</a></i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a>-speaking region of northern Britain. He had four brothers; his brother Cuillum ascended to the throne on the death of his father, but the rest became monks in their own right. Gildas was sent as a child to the College of Theodosius (<a href="/wiki/Cor_Tewdws" title="Cor Tewdws">Cor Tewdws</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Glamorgan" title="Glamorgan">Glamorgan</a>, under the care of <a href="/wiki/Illtud" title="Illtud">St. Illtud</a>, and was a companion of <a href="/wiki/Samson_of_Dol" title="Samson of Dol">St. Sampson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Aurelian" title="Paul Aurelian">St. Paul of Léon</a>. His master St. Illtud loved him tenderly and taught him with special zeal. He was supposed to be educated in liberal arts and divine scripture, but elected to study only holy doctrine, and to forsake his noble birth in favour of a religious life.</p>
<p>After completing his studies under St. Illtud, Gildas went to Ireland where he was ordained as a priest. He returned to his native lands in northern Britain where he acted as a missionary, preaching to the pagan people and converting many of them to Christianity. He was then asked by Ainmericus, high king of Ireland (<a href="/wiki/Ainmuire_mac_S%C3%A9tnai" title="Ainmuire mac Sétnai">Ainmuire mac Sétnai</a>, 566–569), to restore order to the church in Ireland, which had altogether lost the Christian faith. Gildas obeyed the king's summons and travelled all over the island, converting the inhabitants, building churches, and establishing monasteries. He then travelled to Rome and Ravenna where he performed many miracles, including slaying a dragon while in Rome. Intending to return to Britain, he instead settled on the Isle of <a href="/wiki/Houat" title="Houat">Houat</a> off <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> where he led a solitary, austere life. At around this time, he also preached to Nonnita, the mother of <a href="/wiki/Saint_David" title="Saint David">Saint David</a>, while she was pregnant with the saint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cna_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cna-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was eventually sought out by those who wished to study under him, and was entreated to establish a monastery in Brittany. He built an <a href="/wiki/Oratory_(worship)" title="Oratory (worship)">oratory</a> on the bank of the River Blavetum (<a href="/wiki/Blavet" title="Blavet">River Blavet</a>), today known as <a href="/wiki/Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys" title="Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys">St. Gildas de Rhuys</a>. Fragments of letters that he wrote reveal that he composed a <i>Rule</i> for monastic life that was somewhat less austere than the <i>Rule</i> written by Saint David. Ten years after leaving Britain, he wrote an epistolary book in which he reproved five of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" title="Britons (historical)">British</a> kings. He died at Rhuys on 29 January 570, and his body was placed on a boat and allowed to drift, according to his wishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> Three months later, on 11 May, men from Rhuys found the ship in a creek with the body of Gildas still intact. They took the body back to Rhuys and buried it there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Llancarfan_Life:_Gildas_and_King_Arthur"><i>Llancarfan Life</i>: Gildas and King Arthur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gildas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Llancarfan Life: Gildas and King Arthur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second "Life" of St. Gildas was written by <a href="/wiki/Caradoc_of_Llancarfan" title="Caradoc of Llancarfan">Caradoc of Llancarfan</a>, a friend of <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Norman</a> patrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Life_of_Gildas_by_Caradoc_of_Llancarfan_ca._1130-1150_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Life_of_Gildas_by_Caradoc_of_Llancarfan_ca._1130-1150-12">[10]</a></sup> However, Llancarfan's work is most probably historically inaccurate, as his hagiographies tend towards the fictitious, rather than the strictly historical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> Llancarfan's "Life" was written in the 12th century, and includes many elements of what have come to be known as mythical pseudo-histories, involving <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinevere" title="Guinevere">Guinevere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Abbey" title="Glastonbury Abbey">Glastonbury Abbey</a>, leading to the general opinion that this "life" is less historically accurate than the earlier version. For example, according to the dates in the <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Cambriae" title="Annales Cambriae">Annales Cambriae</a></i>, Gildas would have been a contemporary of King Arthur: however, Gildas' work never mentions Arthur by name, even though he gives a history of the Britons, and states that he was born in the same year as the Battle of Badon Hill, in which Arthur is supposed to have vanquished the Saxons.</p>
<p>In the <i>Llancarfan Life,</i> St. Gildas was the son of Nau, king of Scotia. Nau had 24 sons, all victorious warriors. Gildas studied literature as a youth, before leaving his homeland for Gaul, where he studied for seven years. When he returned, he brought back an extensive library with him, and was sought after as a master teacher. He became the most renowned teacher in all of the three kingdoms of Britain. Gildas was a subject of the mythical King Arthur, whom he loved and desired to obey. However, his 23 brothers were always rising up against their rightful king, and his eldest brother, <a href="/wiki/Hueil_mab_Caw" title="Hueil mab Caw">Hueil</a>, would submit to no rightful high king, not even Arthur. Hueil would often swoop down from Scotland to fight battles and carry off spoils, and during one of these raids, Hueil was pursued and killed by King Arthur. When news of his brother's murder reached Gildas in Ireland, he was greatly grieved, but was able to forgive Arthur, and pray for the salvation of his soul. Gildas then travelled to Britain, where he met Arthur face to face, and kissed him as he prayed for forgiveness, and Arthur accepted penance for murdering Gildas' brother.</p>
<p>After this, Gildas taught at the school of St. Cadoc, before retiring to a secret island for seven years. Pirates from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a> came and sacked his island, carrying off goods and his friends as slaves. In distress, he left the island, and came to Glastonbury, then ruled by <a href="/wiki/Maleagant" title="Maleagant">Melvas</a>, King of the 'Summer Country' (<i>Gwlad yr Haf</i>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>). Gildas intervened between <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and Melvas, who had abducted and raped Arthur's wife <a href="/wiki/Guinevere" title="Guinevere">Guinevere</a> and brought her to his stronghold at Glastonbury. Arthur soon arrived to besiege him, but, the peacemaking saint persuaded Melvas to release Guinevere and the two kings made peace. Then desiring to live a hermit's life, Gildas built a hermitage devoted to the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> on the banks of the river at Glastonbury. He died, and was buried at Glastonbury Abbey, in the floor of St. Mary's Church.</p>
<p>The <i>Llancarfan Life</i> contains the earliest surviving appearance of the abduction of Guinevere episode, common in later Arthurian literature. Huail's enmity with Arthur was also apparently a popular subject in medieval Britain: he is mentioned as an enemy of Arthur's in the Welsh prose tale <i><a href="/wiki/Culhwch_and_Olwen" title="Culhwch and Olwen">Culhwch and Olwen</a></i>, written around 1100. A strongly held tradition in <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a> places the beheading of Gildas' brother <a href="/wiki/Hueil_mab_Caw" title="Hueil mab Caw">Huail</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ruthin" title="Ruthin">Ruthin</a>, where what is believed to be the execution stone has been preserved in the town square. Another brother of Gildas, Celyn ap Caw, was based in the north-east corner of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae"><i>De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gildas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></div>
<p>Gildas is best known for his <a href="/wiki/Polemic" title="Polemic">polemic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i>, which recounts the sub-Roman history of Britain, and which is the only substantial source for history of this period written by a near-contemporary.</p>
<p>The work is a sermon in three parts condemning the acts of his contemporaries, both secular and religious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-butler-14">[12]</a></sup> The first part consists of Gildas' explanation for his work and a brief narrative of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> from its conquest under the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Principate</a> to Gildas' time. He describes the doings of the Romans and the <a href="/wiki/Groans_of_the_Britons" title="Groans of the Britons">Groans of the Britons</a>, in which the Britons make one last request for military aid from the departed Roman military. He excoriates his fellow Britons for their sins, while at the same time lauding heroes such as <a href="/wiki/Ambrosius_Aurelianus" title="Ambrosius Aurelianus">Ambrosius Aurelianus</a>, whom he is the first to describe as a leader of the resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Saxons#Saxons_in_Britain" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>. He mentions the victory at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mons_Badonicus" title="Battle of Mons Badonicus">Battle of Mons Badonicus</a>, a feat attributed to <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> in later texts, though Gildas is unclear as to who led the battle.</p>
<p>Part two consists of a condemnation of five British kings, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_(Briton)" title="Constantine (Briton)">Constantine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aurelius_Conanus" title="Aurelius Conanus">Aurelius Conanus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vortiporius" title="Vortiporius">Vortiporius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuneglas" title="Cuneglas">Cuneglas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maelgwn_Gwynedd" title="Maelgwn Gwynedd">Maelgwn</a>. As it is the only contemporary information about them, it is of particular interest to scholars of British history. Part three is a similar attack on the clergy of the time.</p>
<p>The works of Gildas, including the <i>Excidio</i>, can be found in volume 69 of the <i><a href="/wiki/Patrologia_Latina" title="Patrologia Latina">Patrologia Latina</a>.</i></p>
<p><i>De Excidio</i> is usually dated to the 540s, but the historian <a href="/wiki/Guy_Halsall" title="Guy Halsall">Guy Halsall</a> inclines to an "early Gildas" c. 490.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup> Cambridge historian Karen George offers a date range of c. 510–530 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneration">Veneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gildas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Veneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gildas' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relics" title="Relics">relics</a> were venerated in the abbey which he founded in Rhuys, until the 10th century, when they were removed to Berry. In the 18th century, they were said to be moved to the cathedral at Vannes and then hidden during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. The various relics survived the revolution and have all since been returned to <a href="/wiki/Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys" title="Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys">Saint-Gildas-de-Rhuys</a> where they are visible at various times of the year at a dedicated "treasury" in the village. The body of Saint Gildas (minus the pieces incorporated into various reliquaries) is buried behind the altar in the church of Saint Gildas de Rhuys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The gold and silver covered relics of Saint Gildas include:</p>
<ul>
<li>A reliquary head containing parts of the saints skull <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor-17">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>An arm reliquary containing bone pieces, topped with a blessing hand <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor-17">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>A reliquary femur and knee <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor-17">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The embroidered <a href="/wiki/Mitre" title="Mitre">mitre</a> supposedly worn by Gildas is also kept with these relics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-pourmenadenn-e-ruiz.fr_tresor-17">[15]</a></sup> Gildas is the patron saint of several churches and monasteries in Brittany, and his feast day is celebrated on 29 January.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_traditions">Further traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gildas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Further traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gildas is credited with a <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymn</a> called the <i>Lorica</i>, or <i>Breastplate</i>, a prayer for deliverance from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">evil</a>, which contains specimens of <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Latin" title="Hiberno-Latin">Hiberno-Latin</a>. A proverb is also attributed to <i>Gildas mab y Gaw</i> in the <i>Englynion y Clyweid</i> in Llanstephan MS. 27.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Bonedd_y_Saint" title="Bonedd y Saint">Bonedd y Saint</a></i>, Gildas is recorded as having three sons and a daughter. Gwynnog ap Gildas and Noethon ap Gildas are named in the earliest tracts, together with their sister Dolgar. Another son, Tydech, is named in a later document. <a href="/wiki/Iolo_Morganwg" title="Iolo Morganwg">Iolo Morganwg</a> adds Saint <a href="/wiki/Cenydd" title="Cenydd">Cenydd</a> to the list.</p>
<p>The scholar <a href="/wiki/David_Dumville" title="David Dumville">David Dumville</a> suggests that Gildas was the teacher of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnian_of_Moville" title="Finnian of Moville">Finnian of Moville</a>, who in turn was the teacher of <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">St. Columba</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a>.</p>
