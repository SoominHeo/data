<p><b>Tiger beetles</b> are a large group of <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> known for their aggressive predatory habits and running speed. The fastest species of tiger beetle, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cicindela_hudsoni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cicindela hudsoni (page does not exist)">Cicindela hudsoni</a></i>, can run at a speed of 9 km/h (5.6 mph), or about 125 body lengths per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cornell_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cornell-1">[1]</a></sup> As of 2005, about 2,600 species and subspecies were known, with the richest diversity in the Oriental (Indo-Malayan) region, followed by the Neotropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiger_beetle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tiger beetles often have large bulging eyes, long, slender legs and large curved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandible_(arthropod)" title="Mandible (arthropod)">mandibles</a>. All are predatory, both as adults and as larvae. The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cicindela" title="Cicindela">Cicindela</a></i> has a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan distribution</a>. Other well-known genera include <i><a href="/wiki/Tetracha" title="Tetracha">Tetracha</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Omus" title="Omus">Omus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Amblycheila" title="Amblycheila">Amblycheila</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Manticora_(genus)" title="Manticora (genus)">Manticora</a></i>. While members of the genus <i>Cicindela</i> are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" title="Diurnal animal">diurnal</a> and may be out on the hottest days, <i>Tetracha</i>, <i>Omus</i>, <i>Amblycheila</i> and <i>Manticora</i> are all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>. Both <i>Cicindela</i> and <i>Tetracha</i> are often brightly colored, while the other genera mentioned are usually uniform black in color. Tiger beetles in the genus <i>Manticora</i> are the largest in size of the subfamily. These live primarily in the dry regions of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern Africa</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of tiger beetles live in cylindrical burrows as much as a meter deep. They are large-headed, hump-backed <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">grubs</a> that flip backwards to capture prey insects that wander over the ground. The fast-moving adults run down their prey and are extremely fast on the wing, their reaction times being of the same order as that of common <a href="/wiki/Housefly" title="Housefly">houseflies</a>. Some tiger beetles in the tropics are arboreal, but most run on the surface of the ground. They live along sea and lake shores, on sand dunes, around playa lakebeds and on clay banks or woodland paths, being particularly fond of sandy surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tiger beetles are considered a good <a href="/wiki/Indicator_species" title="Indicator species">indicator species</a> and have been used in ecological studies on <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. Several species of wingless parasitic wasps in the genus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Methocha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Methocha (page does not exist)">Methocha</a></i> (family <a href="/wiki/Tiphiidae" title="Tiphiidae">Tiphiidae</a>), lay their eggs on larvae of various <i><a href="/wiki/Cicindela" title="Cicindela">Cicindela</a> spp.</i>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cicindela_dorsalis" title="Cicindela dorsalis">Cicindela dorsalis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiger_beetle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tiger beetles display an unusual form of pursuit in which they alternatively sprint quickly toward their prey, then stop and visually reorient. This may be because while running, the beetle is moving too fast for its visual system to accurately process images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cornell_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cornell-1">[1]</a></sup> To avoid obstacles while running they hold their antennae rigidly and directly in front of them to mechanically sense their environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tiger beetles were traditionally classified as the family <b>Cicindelidae</b> but most authorities now treat them as the subfamily <b>Cicindelinae</b> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carabidae" title="Carabidae">Carabidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ground_beetle" title="Ground beetle">ground beetles</a>). The most recent classifications, however, have relegated them to a monophyletic subgroup within the subfamily <a href="/wiki/Carabinae" title="Carabinae">Carabinae</a>, though this is not yet universally accepted. Accordingly, there is no consensus classification for this group, at any level from family down to subspecies, and it can be exceedingly difficult to decipher the taxonomic literature surrounding this group. Many genera are the result of the splitting of the large genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cicindela" title="Cicindela">Cicindela</a></i>, and many were described by the German entomologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walther_Hermann_Richard_Horn" title="Walther Hermann Richard Horn">Walther Horn</a>.</p>
<p>The genera of tiger beetles include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
<ul>
