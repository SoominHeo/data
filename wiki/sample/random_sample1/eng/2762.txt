<p>A <b>guide</b> is a person who leads <a href="/wiki/Travel" title="Travel">travelers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourists</a> through unknown or unfamiliar locations. The term can also be applied to a person who leads others to more abstract goals such as <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travel_and_recreation">Travel and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Travel and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Explorers in the past venturing into territory unknown by their own people invariably hired guides. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark" title="Lewis and Clark">Lewis and Clark</a> hired <a href="/wiki/Sacagawea" title="Sacagawea">Sacagawea</a> to help them explore the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_West" title="American West">American West</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Thesiger" title="Wilfred Thesiger">Wilfred Thesiger</a> hired guides in the deserts that he ventured into, such as Kuri on his journey to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibesti" title="Tibesti">Tibesti</a> Mountains in 1938.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tour_guide">Tour guide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tour guide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tour_guide" title="Tour guide">Tour guide</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tourguide2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Tourguide2.jpg/220px-Tourguide2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Tourguide2.jpg/330px-Tourguide2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Tourguide2.jpg/440px-Tourguide2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tourguide2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Tour guides lead visitors through <a href="/wiki/Tourist_attraction" title="Tourist attraction">tourist attractions</a> and give information about the attractions' natural and cultural significance. Often, they also act as interpreters for travelers who do not speak the local language. Automated systems like <a href="/wiki/Audio_tour" title="Audio tour">audio tours</a> are sometimes substituted for human tour guides. <a href="/wiki/Tour_operator" title="Tour operator">Tour operators</a> often hire guides to lead tourist groups.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_guide">Mountain guide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mountain guide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mountain_guide" title="Mountain guide">Mountain guide</a></div>
<p>Mountain guides are those employed in <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">mountaineering</a>; these are not merely to show the way but stand in the position of professional climbers with an expert knowledge of rock and snowcraft, which they impart to the amateur, at the same time assuring the safety of the climbing party. This professional class of guides arose in the middle of the 19th century when Alpine climbing became recognized as a <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a>.</p>
<p>In Switzerland, the central committee of the Swiss Alpine Club issues a guides‚Äô tariff which fixes the charges for guides and porters; there are three sections, for the <a href="/wiki/Valais" title="Valais">Valais</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">Vaudois</a> Alps, for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernese_Oberland" title="Bernese Oberland">Bernese Oberland</a>, and for central and eastern Switzerland.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chamonix" title="Chamonix">Chamonix</a> (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>) a statue has been raised to <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Balmat" title="Jacques Balmat">Jacques Balmat</a>, who was the first to climb <a href="/wiki/Mont_Blanc" title="Mont Blanc">Mont Blanc</a> in 1786. Other notable European guides are <a href="/wiki/Christian_Almer" title="Christian Almer">Christian Almer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Anderegg" title="Jakob Anderegg">Jakob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melchior_Anderegg" title="Melchior Anderegg">Melchior Anderegg</a>, Auguste Balmat, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Burgener" title="Alexander Burgener">Alexander Burgener</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armand_Charlet" title="Armand Charlet">Armand Charlet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Croz" title="Michel Croz">Michel Croz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Devouassoud" title="Fran√ßois Devouassoud">Fran√ßois Devouassoud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angelo_Dibona" title="Angelo Dibona">Angelo Dibona</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andreas_Heckmair" title="Andreas Heckmair">Andreas Heckmair</a>, the <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Innerkofler" title="de:Michael Innerkofler">Innerkofler</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Kain" title="Conrad Kain">Conrad Kain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Klucker" title="Christian Klucker">Christian Klucker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Zurbriggen" title="Matthias Zurbriggen">Matthias Zurbriggen</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wilderness_guide">Wilderness guide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wilderness guide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A wilderness guide leads others through <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> areas and works to ensure the safety of their clients.</p>
<p>Wilderness guides are expected to have a command of survival skills (such as making shelters, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Making_fire" title="Making fire">fire-making</a>, <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/First_aid" title="First aid">first aid</a>) and an understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> of the location where they guide. Other common skills among guides include traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handicrafts" title="Handicrafts">handicrafts</a> and cooking methods, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird_watching" title="Bird watching">bird watching</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_conservation" title="Nature conservation">nature conservation</a>.</p>
<p>Wilderness tours usually take place on foot (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skis" title="Skis">skis</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowshoes" title="Snowshoes">snowshoes</a> if there is snow) but may also involve other vehicles such as cars, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowmobiles" title="Snowmobiles">snowmobiles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canoes" title="Canoes">canoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kayaks" title="Kayaks">kayaks</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sledges" title="Sledges">sledges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunting_guide">Hunting guide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hunting guide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hunting guides are employed by those seeking to hunt wildlife, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_game_hunting" title="Big game hunting">big game</a> animals in the wild. European hunting guides working in Africa are sometimes called <a href="/wiki/White_hunter" title="White hunter">white hunters</a>, although the term is most commonly used in the context of the early <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th century</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safari_guide">Safari guide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Safari guide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Guides employed on <a href="/wiki/Safari" title="Safari">safari</a>, usually for "photographic safaris", although the term can also be a synonym of <i>hunting guide</i>. Safari guides who are self-employed, working on their own account with their own marketing and clientele (in contrast with those who work for an employer) sometimes refer to themselves as "professional safari guides". Safari Guides can be unqualified, but most should be qualified and be part of an Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Associations are typically linked to specific countries and are governed by that countries laws and policies. Associations such as <i>The Field Guides Association of Southern Africa (FGASA)</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <i>Uganda Safari Guides Association (USAGA)</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> play an important role in training and educating Safari guides to improve knowledge and safety.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing_guide">Fishing guide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fishing guide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Guides employed by those seeking to fish for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_game_fishing" title="Big game fishing">big game</a> fishing in Sea, lakes and also rivers. Fishing guides have been important in many areas of the world, including the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian coast</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_archipelago&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swedish archipelago (page does not exist)">Swedish archipelago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> coast, and north <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> rivers and lakes, etc. Fishing guides in the Gulf of Mexico are commonly referred to as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charter Guide (page does not exist)">Charter Guide</a>. There are also specialists such as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fly_fishing_Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fly fishing Guide (page does not exist)">Fly fishing Guide</a>. There are thousands of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_guides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fishing guides (page does not exist)">fishing guides</a> but some are better than others. Often when searching on search engines like <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">google</a>, captains that pay for ad-services appear higher in priority (not necessarily when they are the best).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_guides_and_Guides_regiments">Military guides and Guides regiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Military guides and Guides regiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In pre-modern times, the lack of detailed maps made local guides almost essential to the direction of military operations. In 18th century <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the stricter organization of military resources led in various countries to the special training of guide officers who had the primary duty of finding, and if necessary establishing, routes for other military units.</p>
<p>The genesis of the "Guides" regiments may be found in a short-lived Corps of Guides formed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a> in Italy in 1796, which appears to have been a personal escort or bodyguard composed of men who knew the country. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unification_of_Italy" title="Unification of Italy">unification of Italy</a> in 1870-71, the new national army included a regiment designated as Guides - the 19th Cavalleggieri (Light Horse).</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_Army" title="Belgian Army">Belgian Army</a> the two <a href="/wiki/Guides_Regiment" title="Guides Regiment">Guides regiments</a>, created respectively in 1833 and 1874, constituted part of the light cavalry and came to correspond to the Guard cavalry of other nations. Until the outbreak of World War I, they wore a distinctive uniform comprising a plumed <a href="/wiki/Busby" title="Busby">busby</a>, green <a href="/wiki/Dolman" title="Dolman">dolman</a> braided in yellow, and crimson breeches. Mechanised in October 1937, both regiments formed armored battalions in the post World War II Belgian Army. Following a series of amalgamations the Belgian Guides ceased to exist in 2011.</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Army" title="Swiss Army">Swiss army</a> prior to 1914, the squadrons of Guides acted as divisional cavalry. In this role these light cavalry units were called upon, on occasion, to lead columns and provide scouts.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corps_of_Guides_(British_India)" title="Corps of Guides (British India)">Corps of Guides</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">British Indian Army</a> consisted of a unique combination of infantry companies and cavalry squadrons. After World War I the infantry element was incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/12th_Frontier_Force_Regiment" title="12th Frontier Force Regiment">12th Frontier Force Regiment</a> and the Guides Cavalry formed a separate regiment - the <a href="/wiki/Guides_Cavalry" title="Guides Cavalry">10th Queen Victoria's Own Corps of Guides Cavalry (Frontier Force)</a>. This unit still exists as the 2nd (Guides) Battalion of the Frontier Force Regiment of the modern Army of Pakistan.</p>
<p>In drill, a "guide" is an officer or non-commissioned officer who regulates the direction and pace of movements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metaphysics">Metaphysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Metaphysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trip_sitter">Trip sitter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trip sitter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trip_sitter" title="Trip sitter">Trip sitter</a></div>
<p>A <b>psychedelic guide</b> is someone who guides a drug user's experiences as opposed to a sitter who merely remains present, ready to discourage <a href="/wiki/Bad_trip" title="Bad trip">bad trips</a> and handle emergencies but not otherwise getting involved. Guides are more common amongst spiritual users of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entheogens" title="Entheogens">entheogens</a>. Psychedelic guides were strongly encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Leary" title="Timothy Leary">Timothy Leary</a> and the other authors of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Psychedelic_Experience" title="The Psychedelic Experience">The Psychedelic Experience</a>: A Guide Based on the Tibetan Book of the Dead</i>. Trip sitters are also mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Responsible_drug_use" title="Responsible drug use">Responsible Drug User's Oath</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Islam">In Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Islam <i>ar-Rashid</i>, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/99_Names_of_God" title="99 Names of God">99 Names of God</a>, means <i>the Guide</i>. From this is derived the common Arabic name <a href="/wiki/Rashid_(name)" title="Rashid (name)">Rashid</a>.</p>
