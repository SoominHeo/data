<p>The <b>Battle of Guilford Court House</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Battle" title="Battle">battle</a> fought on March 15, 1781, at a site which is now in <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro</a>, the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guilford_County,_North_Carolina" title="Guilford County, North Carolina">Guilford County, North Carolina</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. A 2,100-man <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> force under the command of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a> defeated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a>'s 4,500 Americans. The <a href="/wiki/British_Army_during_the_American_War_of_Independence" title="British Army during the American War of Independence">British Army</a>, however, lost a considerable number of men during the battle with estimates as high as 27%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Such heavy British casualties resulted in a strategic victory for the Americans.</p>
<p>The battle was “the largest and most hotly contested action”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in the American Revolution’s southern campaign and led to the surrender of Cornwallis at Yorktown. Before the battle, the British appeared to have had great success in conquering much of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Georgia" title="Province of Georgia">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_South_Carolina" title="Province of South Carolina">South Carolina</a> with the aid of strong <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalist</a> factions, and thought that <a href="/wiki/Province_of_North_Carolina" title="Province of North Carolina">North Carolina</a> might be within their grasp. In fact, the British were in the process of heavy recruitment in North Carolina when this battle put an end to their recruiting drive. In the wake of the battle, Greene moved into South Carolina, while Cornwallis chose to march into <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and attempt to link up with roughly 3,500 men under British Major General Phillips and American turncoat <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a>. These decisions allowed Greene to unravel British control of the South, while leading Cornwallis to <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">Yorktown</a> and eventual surrender to General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> and Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Donatien_de_Vimeur,_comte_de_Rochambeau" title="Jean-Baptiste Donatien de Vimeur, comte de Rochambeau">Comte de Rochambeau</a>.</p>
<p>The battle is commemorated at <a href="/wiki/Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park" title="Guilford Courthouse National Military Park">Guilford Courthouse National Military Park</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Hoskins_House_Historic_District" title="Hoskins House Historic District">Hoskins House Historic District</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude">Prelude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prelude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War</a></div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowpens" title="Battle of Cowpens">Battle of Cowpens</a> in South Carolina, Lt. General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a> was determined to destroy Greene's army. But the loss of his light infantry at Cowpens led him to burn his supplies so that his army would be nimble enough for pursuit. He chased Greene in the "Race to the Dan", but Greene escaped across the flooded <a href="/wiki/Dan_River" title="Dan River">Dan River</a> to safety in Virginia. Cornwallis established camp at <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough,_North_Carolina" title="Hillsborough, North Carolina">Hillsborough</a>, foraged for supplies and recruited North Carolina Tories. However, the bedraggled state of his army and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyle%27s_massacre" title="Pyle's massacre">Pyle's massacre</a> deterred Loyalists from turning out. Due to the fighting, thousands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaves" title="Slaves">slaves</a> had escaped from plantations in South Carolina and other southern states, many joining the British to fight for their personal freedom. In the waning months of the war, the British evacuated more than 3,000 freedmen to <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, with others going to London and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. Northern slaves escaped to the British lines in occupied cities such as New York.</p>
<p>On March 14, 1781, while encamped in the forks of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_River_(North_Carolina)" title="Deep River (North Carolina)">Deep River</a>, Cornwallis was informed that Greene was encamped at the Guilford Court House. With him was a body of North Carolina <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>, plus reinforcements from <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, consisting of 3,000 Virginia militia, a Virginia State regiment, a corps of Virginian eighteen-month men and recruits for the <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Line" title="Maryland Line">Maryland Line</a>, totaling between 4,000-5,000 men. Cornwallis decided to give battle, though he had only 1,900 men at his disposal. He detached his baggage train, 100 infantry and 20 cavalry under the command of Lieutenant Colonel Hamilton, to Bell's Mills further down the Deep River. Before breakfast could be eaten, Cornwallis set off with his main force, arriving at Guilford at midday.</p>

<p>The advance guard met near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quaker</a> New Garden Meeting House. Dragoons from <a href="/wiki/Banastre_Tarleton" title="Banastre Tarleton">Banastre Tarleton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/British_Legion_(American_Revolution)" title="British Legion (American Revolution)">British Legion</a> were briefly engaged by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lee_III" title="Henry Lee III">Light Horse Harry Lee</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lee%27s_Legion" title="Lee's Legion">Dragoons</a> about 4 miles (6 km) from the Guilford Court House. The British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/23rd_Regiment_of_Foot" title="23rd Regiment of Foot">23rd Regiment of Foot</a> sent reinforcements forward and Lee withdrew, ordering a retreat to Greene's main body.</p>
<p>Cornwallis found the Americans in position on rising ground about one and a half miles (2.5 km) from the court house. He was unable to gain much information from his prisoners or the local residents as to the American disposition. To his front he saw a plantation with a large field straddling both sides of the road, with two more further over on the left separated by 200 yards or so of woodland. To his right beyond the fields the woodland extended for several miles. On the far side of the first field was a fenced wood, 1 mile (1.6 km) in depth, through which the road passed into an extensive cleared area around the court house. Along the edge of this woodland was a fence forming the American first line of defense and a six-pound <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a> on each side of the road.</p>
<p>Greene had prepared his defense in three lines. North Carolina <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> formed the first line, with backwoods riflemen on the left and right <a href="/wiki/Flanking_maneuver" title="Flanking maneuver">flanks</a> to snipe advancing British. In the second line, he placed the <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> militia. Two more six-pound cannons were sited in the center of the line. His third and strongest line consisting of his regulars, included the Virginian Regiment, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> infantry, and the 1st and 2nd <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> regiments were 400 yards further on, though placed at an angle to the west of the road. While superficially resembling the deployment successfully used by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Morgan" title="Daniel Morgan">Daniel Morgan</a> at Cowpens, the lines were hundreds of yards apart and could not support one another.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="792" data-file-width="477" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png/170px-Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png/255px-Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png/340px-Map_Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Since the east side of the road was mostly open, Cornwallis opted to attack up the west side and, following a short barrage of cannon shot on the cannon positions of the first line, at 1:30 p.m., Cornwallis moved his men forward. When they were about 150 yards short of the fence, a volley was fired from the Americans, but the British continued until they were within musket shot then fired their own volley in return. On a command from Webster, they then charged forward, coming to a halt 50 paces from the American lines because the North Carolina militia, as noted by Sergeant Lamb of the 23rd Regiment "had their arms presented and resting on the picket fence...they were taking aim with nice precision". Urged onwards by Lieutenant Colonel <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Webster_(officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Webster (officer) (page does not exist)">James Webster</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33rd_Regiment_of_Foot" title="33rd Regiment of Foot">33rd Regiment of Foot</a>, the British continued to advance. The North Carolina Militia, to the west of the road, fired their muskets then turned and fled back through the woods, some reported to have discarded their personal equipment as they ran. Before they retreated, the militia did inflict heavy casualties on the British. One officer of the 71st Regiment claimed that "one half of the Highlanders dropped on that spot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The British advanced on the second line. Heavy resistance was shown, but Webster pushed around the flank and on to the American 3rd line. The woodland was too dense to allow practical use of the <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonets</a>. The British army forced its way through the first two lines with significant losses.</p>
<p>The 71st Regiment, grenadiers and 2nd Guards moved up the center, following the musket shots from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33rd_Regiment_of_Foot" title="33rd Regiment of Foot">33rd</a> and 23rd regiments to their left. To the right, the 1st Guards and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hessian_(soldiers)" title="Hessian (soldiers)">Hessians</a> were being harried by Lee's Legion. The British guns and Tarleton’s light dragoons moved forward along the road, keeping pace. The 2nd Guards in the center found themselves coming out into open ground around the court house to the left of the Salisbury road. They spotted a large force of Continental infantry, and immediately attacked them and captured two six-pounders. They then pursued the Continentals into the wood and were repulsed by Lt. Col. <a href="/wiki/William_Washington" title="William Washington">William Washington</a>'s <a href="/wiki/3rd_Continental_Light_Dragoons" title="3rd Continental Light Dragoons">light dragoons</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/1st_Maryland_Regiment" title="1st Maryland Regiment">1st Maryland Regiment</a>, abandoning the two guns they had just captured. Lieutenant John Macleod, in command of two British three-pounders, had just arrived and was directed by Cornwallis to fire on the Dragoons and the British alike.</p>
<p>While many British soldiers were killed from <a href="/wiki/Friendly_fire" title="Friendly fire">friendly fire</a>, the Americans broke off and retreated from the field. Cornwallis ordered the 23rd and 71st regiments, with part of the cavalry, to pursue the Americans, though not for any great distance. Tarleton and the remainder of the dragoons were sent off to the right flank to join Bose and put an end to the action from Washington.</p>
<p>During the battle, Cornwallis had a horse shot from under him. American colonel <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Williams" title="Benjamin Williams">Benjamin Williams</a> was later decorated for his personal bravery at Guilford Courthouse.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="1000" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg/220px-Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg/330px-Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg/440px-Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guilford_Courthouse_Flag.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Guilford_Courthouse_flag" title="Guilford Courthouse flag">Non-standard American flag</a> believed to have been carried in battle, although its validity is questioned.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The battle lasted only ninety minutes. The British were outnumbered more than two to one, yet defeated the American force; however, in doing so they lost over a quarter of their men.</p>
<p>The British, by taking ground with their accustomed tenacity when engaged with superior numbers, were tactically victorious. However, Cornwallis took unsustainable casualties and ultimately withdrew, leaving the Americans with the strategic victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MtVernon_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MtVernon-1">[1]</a></sup> Seeing this as a classic <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhic_victory" title="Pyrrhic victory">Pyrrhic victory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Whig_Party" title="British Whig Party">British Whig Party</a> leader and war critic <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_Fox" title="Charles James Fox">Charles James Fox</a> echoed <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>'s famous words by saying, "Another such victory would ruin the British Army!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a letter to <a href="/wiki/George_Germain,_1st_Viscount_Sackville" title="George Germain, 1st Viscount Sackville">Lord George Germain</a> delivered by his aide-de-camp Captain Broderick, Cornwallis commented: "From our observation, and the best accounts we could procure, we did not doubt but the strength of the enemy exceeded 7,000 men...I cannot ascertain the loss of the enemy, but it must have been considerable; between 200 and 300 dead were left on the field of battle...many of their wounded escaped...Our forage parties have reported to me that houses in a circle six to eight miles around us are full of others...We took few prisoners".</p>
<p>He went on to comment further on the British force: "The conduct and actions of the officers and soldiers that composed this little army will do more justice to their merit than I can by words. Their persevering intrepidity in action, their invincible patience in the hardships and fatigues of a march of above 600 miles, in which they have forded several large rivers and numberless creeks, many of which would be reckoned large rivers in any other country in the world, without tents or covering against the climate, and often without provisions, will sufficiently manifest their ardent zeal for the honour and interests of their Sovereign and their country."</p>
<p>After the battle, the British were spread across a large expanse of woodland without food and shelter, and during the night torrential rains started. Fifty of the wounded died before sunrise. Had the British followed the retreating Americans they might have come across their baggage and supply wagons which had been left where the Americans had camped on the west of the Salisbury road prior to the battle.</p>
<p>On March 17, two days after the battle, Cornwallis reported his casualties as 5 officers and 88 of other ranks killed, and 24 officers and 389 of other ranks wounded, with a further 26 men missing in action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Webster was wounded during the battle and he died a fortnight later. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Banastre_Tarleton,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Banastre Tarleton, 1st Baronet">Lieutenant-Colonel Tarleton</a>, commander of the loyalist provincial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Legion_(1778)" title="British Legion (1778)">British Legion</a> was another notable wounded officer, losing two fingers after taking a bullet in his right hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cheaney_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cheaney-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greene reported his casualties as 57 killed, 111 wounded and 161 missing for the Continental troops and 22 killed, 74 wounded and 885 missing for the militia, a total of 79 killed, 185 wounded and 1,046 missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps.gov_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-5">[5]</a></sup> Of those reported missing, 75 were wounded men who were captured by the British.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> When Cornwallis resumed his march, these 75 wounded prisoners were left behind at Cross Creek,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conrad_page_441_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conrad_page_441-6">[6]</a></sup> Cornwallis having earlier left 70 of his own most severely wounded men at the Quaker settlement of New Garden<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_Camp" title="Snow Camp">Snow Camp</a>.</p>
<p>To avoid another <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Camden" title="Battle of Camden">Camden</a>, Greene retreated with his forces intact. With his small army, less than 2,000 strong, Cornwallis declined to follow Greene into the back country. Retiring to Hillsborough, he raised the royal standard, offered protection to the inhabitants, and for the moment appeared to be master of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> and the two Carolinas. In a few weeks, however, he abandoned the heart of the state and marched to the coast at <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>, to recruit and refit his command.</p>
<p>At Wilmington the British general faced a serious problem. Instead of remaining in North Carolina, he determined to march into <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, justifying the move on the ground that until Virginia was reduced, he could not firmly hold the more southerly states he had just overrun. General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Clinton</a> sharply criticized the decision as unmilitary, and as having been made contrary to his instructions. To Cornwallis he wrote in May: "Had you intimated the probability of your intention, I should certainly have endeavoured to stop you, as I did then as well as now consider such a move likely to be dangerous to our interests in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Colonies" title="Southern Colonies">Southern Colonies</a>." For three months, Cornwallis raided every farm or plantation he came across, from which he took hundreds of horses for his Dragoons. He converted another 700 infantry to mounted duties. During these raids, he freed thousands of slaves, of whom 12,000 joined his own force.</p>
<p>General Greene boldly pushed down towards <a href="/wiki/Camden,_South_Carolina" title="Camden, South Carolina">Camden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, with a view to drawing his antagonist after him to the points where he was the year before, as well as to driving back <a href="/wiki/Francis_Rawdon-Hastings,_1st_Marquess_of_Hastings" title="Francis Rawdon-Hastings, 1st Marquess of Hastings">Lord Rawdon</a>, whom Cornwallis had left in that field. In his main object—the recovery of the southern states—Greene succeeded by the close of the year, but not without hard fighting and repeated reverses. "We fight, get beat, rise, and fight again," were his words.</p>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="330" data-file-width="647" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG/250px-Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG/375px-Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG/500px-Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guilford_re-enactment20.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Smoke fills the air at an annual re-enactment</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Every year, on or about March 15, re-enactors in period uniforms present a tactical demonstration of Revolutionary War fighting techniques on or near the battle site, major portions of which are preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park" title="Guilford Courthouse National Military Park">Guilford Courthouse National Military Park</a>, established in 1917. Recent research has shown that the battlefield extended into the area now within the boundaries of the adjacent Greensboro Country Park to the east.</p>
<p>Three current Army National Guard units (116th IN,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> 175th IN<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and 198th SIG<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>) are derived from American units that participated in the Battle of Guilford Courthouse. There are only thirty <a href="/wiki/Army_National_Guard_and_Active_Regular_Army_Units_with_Colonial_Roots" title="Army National Guard and Active Regular Army Units with Colonial Roots">Army National Guard and active Regular Army units with lineages that go back to the colonial era</a>.</p>
<p>On Sunday, March 13, 2016, a Crown Forces Monument was opened at the <a href="/wiki/Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park" title="Guilford Courthouse National Military Park">Guilford Courthouse National Military Park</a> in honor of the officers and men of Cornwallis's army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
