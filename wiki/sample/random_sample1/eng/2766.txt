<p><b>Kim Kyung-Moon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: 김경문, <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: 金卿文) (born November 1, 1958) is the <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> of the <a href="/wiki/NC_Dinos" title="NC Dinos">NC Dinos</a>, and a former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catcher_(baseball)" title="Catcher (baseball)">catcher</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Baseball_Organization" title="Korea Baseball Organization">Korea Baseball Organization</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Kyung-moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kim, a former <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a> who threw and batted right-handed, attended <a href="/wiki/Korea_University" title="Korea University">Korea University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. After graduation, he was signed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OB_Bears" title="OB Bears">OB Bears</a>, which later became Doosan, and directly took the starting catcher position on the team as a rookie in <a href="/wiki/Korea_Baseball_Organization" title="Korea Baseball Organization">Korean professional baseball</a>'s first year in 1982. Kim eventually led his team to the first <a href="/wiki/Korean_Series" title="Korean Series">Korean Series</a> Championship in the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Baseball_Organization" title="Korea Baseball Organization">KBO</a> history, along with his battery mate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Park_Chul-Soon" title="Park Chul-Soon">Park Chul-Soon</a> who won 22 consecutive games in the regular season.</p>
<p>In his 10-year major league career in KBO, Kim <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batted</a> .220 with 6 <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a>, 126 <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">runs batted in</a>, 119 <a href="/wiki/Run_(baseball)" title="Run (baseball)">runs</a>, 329 <a href="/wiki/Hit_(baseball)" title="Hit (baseball)">hits</a>, 60 <a href="/wiki/Double_(baseball)" title="Double (baseball)">doubles</a>, 1 <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triple</a>, and 17 <a href="/wiki/Stolen_base" title="Stolen base">stolen bases</a> in 700 games. Kim was not a very good hitter, batting around the <a href="/wiki/Mendoza_Line" title="Mendoza Line">Mendoza Line</a> during his ten seasons, but is considered one of the best defensive catchers of all time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Managing_career">Managing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Kyung-moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Managing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After retirement as a player in 1991, Kim went to <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> to study coaching under <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Cox" title="Bobby Cox">Bobby Cox</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> until <a href="/wiki/1994_in_baseball" title="1994 in baseball">1994</a> when he was hired as a coach for the <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Lions" title="Samsung Lions">Samsung Lions</a>. He was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullpen_coach" title="Bullpen coach">bullpen coach</a> for the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Lions" title="Samsung Lions">Samsung Lions</a> and remained there until the 1996 season. In 1998, Kim returned to the Bears and worked as bullpen and bench coach until the 2003 season.</p>
<p>On October 10, <a href="/wiki/2003_in_baseball" title="2003 in baseball">2003</a>, Kim was named manager of the Bears, replacing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_In-Sik" title="Kim In-Sik">Kim In-Sik</a>, who had won two <a href="/wiki/Korean_Series" title="Korean Series">Korean Series</a> titles with the Bears. He led the Bears to their two-year consecutive postseason appearance (2004 and 2005).</p>
<p>On October 30, <a href="/wiki/2005_in_baseball" title="2005 in baseball">2005</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Doosan_Bears" title="Doosan Bears">Doosan Bears</a> extended Kim's contract by three years, through <a href="/wiki/2008_in_baseball" title="2008 in baseball">2008</a>. In the 2006 season, Kim failed to make the 3rd consecutive postseason appearance for the Bears, but in the 2007 season, he led his team to the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Series" title="Korean Series">Korean Series</a> once again since 2005.</p>
<p>After the 2007 season Kim was named the manager of for the <a href="/wiki/South_Korea_national_baseball_team" title="South Korea national baseball team">South Korea national baseball team</a>, and in August he participated in the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics" title="Baseball at the 2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Olympic Games</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, China. There Kim led the South Koreans to the gold medal, beating heavily favored <a href="/wiki/Cuba_national_baseball_team" title="Cuba national baseball team">Cuba</a>, which had won the gold medals at the three previous Olympics.</p>
<p>On June 13, <a href="/wiki/2011_in_baseball" title="2011 in baseball">2011</a>, Kim was quit manager of the Bears.</p>
<p>On August 31, <a href="/wiki/2011_in_baseball" title="2011 in baseball">2011</a>, Kim was named 1st manager of <a href="/wiki/NC_Dinos" title="NC Dinos">NC Dinos</a></p>
