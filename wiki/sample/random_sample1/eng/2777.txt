<p><b>Kim Dae-seung</b> (born June 18, 1967) is a South Korean <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">film director</a> and <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Dae-seung&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Chung-Ang_University" title="Chung-Ang University">Chung-Ang University</a> with a degree in Film Studies, Kim Dae-seung first honed his filmmaking skills as a protege of legendary Korean director <a href="/wiki/Im_Kwon-taek" title="Im Kwon-taek">Im Kwon-taek</a>, working for almost ten years as Im's <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">assistant director</a> on major films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Seopyeonje" title="Seopyeonje">Seopyeonje</a></i> (1993), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Taebaek_Mountains" title="The Taebaek Mountains">The Taebaek Mountains</a></i> (1994), and <i><a href="/wiki/Chunhyang_(2000_film)" title="Chunhyang (2000 film)">Chunhyang</a></i> (2000).</p>
<p>Kim made his directorial debut with <i><a href="/wiki/Bungee_Jumping_of_Their_Own" title="Bungee Jumping of Their Own">Bungee Jumping of Their Own</a></i> (2001), a melodrama about <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnation</a>. Despite its <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a> subject, the film was received well by audiences and critics due to Kim's sensitive direction and the acting by leads <a href="/wiki/Lee_Byung-hun" title="Lee Byung-hun">Lee Byung-hun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Eun-ju" title="Lee Eun-ju">Lee Eun-ju</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kofic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kofic-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He subsequently directed <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_Rain_(film)" title="Blood Rain (film)">Blood Rain</a></i> (2005), a mystery thriller set in the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The surprise casting of <a href="/wiki/Cha_Seung-won" title="Cha Seung-won">Cha Seung-won</a> (then-known for comedic roles)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and newcomer <a href="/wiki/Park_Yong-woo" title="Park Yong-woo">Park Yong-woo</a> paid off, resulting in critical acclaim and an unexpectedly robust box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Blood Rain</i> received multiple acting and technical nominations from local award-giving bodies, and won Best Film at the <a href="/wiki/Chunsa_Film_Art_Awards" title="Chunsa Film Art Awards">Chunsa Film Art Awards</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baeksang_Arts_Awards" title="Baeksang Arts Awards">Baeksang Arts Awards</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Yubari_International_Fantastic_Film_Festival" title="Yubari International Fantastic Film Festival">Yubari International Fantastic Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>Kim's third film was based on the true story of the <a href="/wiki/Sampoong_Department_Store_collapse" title="Sampoong Department Store collapse">1995 collapse of Sampoong Department Store</a> which claimed 502 lives and wounded 937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Traces_of_Love" title="Traces of Love">Traces of Love</a></i> (2006) explores the painful aftermath of trauma and loss, guilt and memory, against the backdrop of beautiful autumn scenery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The melodrama starred <a href="/wiki/Yoo_Ji-tae" title="Yoo Ji-tae">Yoo Ji-tae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Ji-soo_(actress)" title="Kim Ji-soo (actress)">Kim Ji-soo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uhm_Ji-won" title="Uhm Ji-won">Uhm Ji-won</a>, and was the opening film of the 11th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pusan_International_Film_Festival" title="Pusan International Film Festival">Pusan International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Kim directed <i>Q&amp;A</i> as part of <i>If You Were Me 5</i>, an <a href="/wiki/Anthology_film" title="Anthology film">omnibus</a> commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/National_Human_Rights_Commission_of_Korea" title="National Human Rights Commission of Korea">National Human Rights Commission of Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Starring television star <a href="/wiki/Kim_Hyun-joo" title="Kim Hyun-joo">Kim Hyun-joo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short film</a> denounced the collection and control of personal information that leads to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_harassment" title="Sexual harassment">sexual harassment</a> in the Korean male-dominated workplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kofic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kofic-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kim returned to feature filmmaking in 2012 with the erotic period drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Concubine_(film)" title="The Concubine (film)">The Concubine</a></i> starring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jo_Yeo-jeong" title="Jo Yeo-jeong">Jo Yeo-jeong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Dong-wook" title="Kim Dong-wook">Kim Dong-wook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Min-joon" title="Kim Min-joon">Kim Min-joon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The film, which delves into sexual politics and its role in a power struggle within the royal court, was a hit following its early summer release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kofic_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-kofic-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
