<p><b>Kim Man-il</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">김만일</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani" xml:lang="ko-Hani">金萬一</span>; 1944–1947/8) was the second son and child of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a> and his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-suk" title="Kim Jong-suk">Kim Jong-suk</a>.</p>
<p>Soviet records show that he was born <b>Alexander Irsenovich Kim</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Александр Ирсенович Ким</span>) in 1944 in the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> village of <a href="/wiki/Vyatskoye,_Khabarovsk_Krai" title="Vyatskoye, Khabarovsk Krai">Vyatskoye</a>. Inside his family, he was nicknamed <i>Shura</i>. Official North Korean biographies state that Shura and his older brother <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">Kim Jong-il</a> got along very well and played together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Korean based sources indicate that in the summer of 1947 or 1948, Shura and his brother were playing in a pond in the city of <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a>, when Shura mysteriously drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Russian sources indicate that he drowned in a well in Vyatskoye, prior to the family moving back to Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Official records state that Kim Jong-il was devastated and could never get over the trauma of losing his younger brother. In 1949, his mother, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-suk" title="Kim Jong-suk">Kim Jong-suk</a> died while giving birth to a stillborn girl.</p>
