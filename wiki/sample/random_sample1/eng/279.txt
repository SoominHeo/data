<p><b>MIDI</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; short for <b>Musical Instrument Digital Interface</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">technical standard</a> that describes a <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" title="Communications protocol">protocol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_electronics" title="Digital electronics">digital interface</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_connector" title="Electrical connector">connectors</a> and allows a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_musical_instrument" title="Electronic musical instrument">electronic musical instruments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> and other related devices to connect and communicate with one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A single MIDI link can carry up to sixteen channels of information, each of which can be routed to a separate device.</p>
<p>MIDI carries event messages that specify <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">notation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">velocity</a>, control signals for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parameter_(music)" title="Parameter (music)">parameters</a> such as volume, <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panning_(audio)" title="Panning (audio)">audio panning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cue_(theatrical)" title="Cue (theatrical)">cues</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIDI_clock" title="MIDI clock">clock signals</a> that set and synchronize <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> between multiple devices. These messages are sent via a MIDI cable to other devices where they control sound generation and other features. A simple example of a MIDI setup is the use of a MIDI controller such as an electronic <a href="/wiki/Musical_keyboard" title="Musical keyboard">musical keyboard</a> to trigger sounds created by a <a href="/wiki/Sound_module" title="Sound module">sound module</a>. This MIDI data can also be recorded into a hardware or software device called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_sequencers" title="Music sequencers">sequencer</a>, which can be used to edit the data and to play it back at a later time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup> Advantages of MIDI include compactness (an entire song can be coded in a few hundred lines, i.e. in a few <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">kilobytes</a>), ease of modification and manipulation and a wide choice of electronic instruments and synthesizer or digitally-sampled sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>MIDI technology was standardized in 1983 by a panel of music industry representatives, and is maintained by the <a href="/wiki/MIDI_Manufacturers_Association" title="MIDI Manufacturers Association">MIDI Manufacturers Association</a> (MMA). All official MIDI standards are jointly developed and published by the MMA in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, US, and for Japan, the MIDI Committee of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Musical_Electronics_Industry" title="Association of Musical Electronics Industry">Association of Musical Electronics Industry</a> (AMEI) in Tokyo. In 2016, the MMA established The MIDI Association (TMA) to support a global community of people who work, play, or create with MIDI, establishing the www.MIDI.org website as the central repository of information about anything related to MIDI technology, from early MIDI technology to future developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 1981, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Ikutaro_Kakehashi" title="Ikutaro Kakehashi">Ikutaro Kakehashi</a> proposed the idea of standardization to <a href="/wiki/Oberheim_Electronics" title="Oberheim Electronics">Oberheim Electronics</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Tom_Oberheim" title="Tom Oberheim">Tom Oberheim</a>, who then talked it over with <a href="/wiki/Sequential_Circuits" title="Sequential Circuits">Sequential Circuits</a> president <a href="/wiki/Dave_Smith_(engineer)" title="Dave Smith (engineer)">Dave Smith</a>. In October 1981, Kakehashi, Oberheim and Smith discussed the idea with representatives from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha" title="Yamaha">Yamaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korg" title="Korg">Korg</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kawai_(company)" title="Kawai (company)">Kawai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chadab5100_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-chadab5100-5">[5]</a></sup> Sequential Circuits engineers and synthesizer designers Dave Smith and Chet Wood devised a universal synthesizer interface, which would allow direct communication between equipment from different manufacturers. Smith proposed this standard at the <a href="/wiki/Audio_Engineering_Society" title="Audio Engineering Society">Audio Engineering Society</a> show in November 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup> Over the next two years, the standard was discussed and modified by representatives of companies such as Roland, Yamaha, Korg, Kawai, Oberheim, and Sequential Circuits,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:22</sup> and was renamed Musical Instrument Digital Interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup> MIDI's development was announced to the public by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moog" title="Robert Moog">Robert Moog</a>, in the October 1982 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Keyboard_(magazine)" title="Keyboard (magazine)">Keyboard magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:276</sup></p>
<p>By the time of the January 1983 Winter <a href="/wiki/NAMM_Show" title="NAMM Show">NAMM Show</a>, Smith was able to demonstrate a MIDI connection between his <a href="/wiki/Sequential_Circuits_Prophet-5" title="Sequential Circuits Prophet-5">Prophet 600</a> analog synthesizer and a <a href="/wiki/Roland_Jupiter-6" title="Roland Jupiter-6">Roland JP-6</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_MIDI_1.0_Protocol" title="The MIDI 1.0 Protocol">MIDI Specification</a> was published in August 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chadab5100_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-chadab5100-5">[5]</a></sup> The MIDI standard was unveiled by Ikutaro Kakehashi and Dave Smith, who both later received <a href="/wiki/Technical_Grammy_Award" title="Technical Grammy Award">Technical Grammy Awards</a> in 2013 for their key roles in the development of MIDI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_the_music_industry">Impact on the music industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact on the music industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MIDI's appeal was originally limited to professional musicians and <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">record producers</a> who wanted to use electronic instruments in the production of popular music. The standard allowed different instruments to "speak" with each other and with computers, and this spurred a rapid expansion of the sales and production of electronic instruments and music software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:21</sup> This interoperability allowed one device to be controlled from another, which reduced the amount of hardware musicians needed to own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> MIDI's introduction coincided with the <a href="/wiki/History_of_personal_computers" title="History of personal computers">dawn of the personal computer era</a> and the introductions of <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">samplers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_synthesizer" title="Digital synthesizer">digital synthesizers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The creative possibilities brought about by MIDI technology have been credited as having helped to revive the music industry in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>MIDI introduced many capabilities which transformed the way musicians work. <a href="/wiki/Music_sequencer" title="Music sequencer">MIDI sequencing</a> made it possible for a user with no notation skills to build complex arrangements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A musical act with as few as one or two members, each operating multiple MIDI-enabled devices, could deliver a performance which sounds similar to that of a much larger group of musicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The expense of hiring outside musicians for a project could be reduced or eliminated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup> and complex productions could be realized on a system as small as a synthesizer with integrated keyboard and sequencer. MIDI helped establish <a href="/wiki/Home_recording" title="Home recording">home recording</a>. By performing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preproduction" title="Preproduction">preproduction</a> in a home environment, an artist can reduce recording costs by arriving at a recording studio with a song that is already partially completed and worked out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7–8</sup> <a href="/wiki/Educational_technology" title="Educational technology">Educational technology</a> enabled by MIDI has transformed <a href="/wiki/Music_education" title="Music education">music education</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_digital_audio">Comparison with digital audio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparison with digital audio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Those new to the subject of MIDI might confuse it with <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio" title="Digital audio">digital audio</a>. While it may appear that MIDI and digital audio equipment do the same task, recording of multiple channels of music using digital equipment, this is done differently by MIDI and digital audio systems. MIDI symbolically represents a note (e.g., when the synth player performs a note, the note "A4" is recorded), whereas digital audio represents the sound <i>produced</i> by the note.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrument_control">Instrument control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Instrument control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MIDI was invented so that musical instruments could communicate with each other and so that one instrument can control another. Analog synthesizers that have no digital component and were built prior to MIDI's development can be retrofit with kits that convert MIDI messages into analog control voltages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:277</sup> When a note is played on a MIDI instrument, it generates a digital signal that can be used to trigger a note on another instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:20</sup> The capability for remote control allows full-sized instruments to be replaced with smaller sound modules, and allows musicians to combine instruments to achieve a fuller sound, or to create combinations such as acoustic piano and strings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Why_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Why-17">[17]</a></sup> MIDI also enables other instrument parameters to be controlled remotely.</p>
<p>Synthesizers and samplers contain various tools for shaping a sound. <a href="/wiki/Filter_(signal_processing)" title="Filter (signal processing)">Filters</a> adjust <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>, and envelopes automate the way a sound evolves over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The frequency of a filter and the envelope attack, or the time it takes for a sound to reach its maximum level, are examples of synthesizer <a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">parameters</a>, and can be controlled remotely through MIDI. Effects devices have different parameters, such as delay feedback or reverb time. When a MIDI continuous controller number is assigned to one of these parameters, the device will respond to any messages it receives that are identified by that number. Controls such as knobs, switches, and pedals can be used to send these messages. A set of adjusted parameters can be saved to a device's internal memory as a "patch", and these patches can be remotely selected by MIDI program changes. The MIDI standard allows selection of 128 different programs, but devices can provide more by arranging their patches into banks of 128 programs each, and combining a program change message with a bank select message.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>MIDI events can be sequenced with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_MIDI_editors_and_sequencers" title="List of MIDI editors and sequencers">computer software</a>, or in specialized hardware <a href="/wiki/Music_workstation" title="Music workstation">music workstations</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation" title="Digital audio workstation">digital audio workstations</a> (DAWs) are specifically designed to work with MIDI as an integral component. MIDI <a href="/wiki/Piano_roll" title="Piano roll">piano rolls</a> have been developed in many DAWs so that the recorded MIDI messages can be extensively modified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (August 2012)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These tools allow composers to audition and edit their work much more quickly and efficiently than did older solutions, such as <a href="/wiki/Multitrack_recording" title="Multitrack recording">multitrack recording</a>. They improve the efficiency of composers who lack strong pianistic abilities, and allow untrained individuals the opportunity to create polished arrangements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muse_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muse-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:67–8,72</sup></p>
<p>Because MIDI is a set of commands that create sound, MIDI sequences can be manipulated in ways that prerecorded audio cannot. It is possible to change the key, instrumentation or tempo of a MIDI arrangement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewster_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brewster-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:227</sup> and to reorder its individual sections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The ability to compose ideas and quickly hear them played back enables composers to experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muse_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Muse-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:175</sup> <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_composition" title="Algorithmic composition">Algorithmic composition</a> programs provide computer-generated performances that can be used as song ideas or accompaniment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:122</sup></p>
<p>Some composers may take advantage of <a href="/wiki/MIDI_1.0" title="MIDI 1.0">MIDI 1.0</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_MIDI" title="General MIDI">General MIDI (GM)</a> technology to allow musical data files to be shared among various electronic instruments by using a standard, portable set of commands and parameters. The data composed via the sequenced MIDI recordings can be saved as a Standard MIDI File (SMF), digitally distributed, and reproduced by any computer or electronic instrument that also adheres to the same MIDI, GM, and SMF standards. MIDI data files are much smaller than recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_file" title="Audio file">audio files</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_with_computers">Use with computers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use with computers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the time of MIDI's introduction, the computing industry was mainly devoted to <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe computers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a> were not commonly owned. The personal computer market stabilized at the same time that MIDI appeared, and computers became a viable option for music production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:324</sup> In the years immediately after the 1983 ratification of the MIDI specification, MIDI features were adapted to several early computer platforms, including <a href="/wiki/Apple_II_Plus" title="Apple II Plus">Apple II Plus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIe" title="Apple IIe">IIe</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" title="Apple Macintosh">Macintosh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commodore_Amiga" title="Commodore Amiga">Amiga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acorn_Archimedes" title="Acorn Archimedes">Acorn Archimedes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_DOS" title="PC DOS">PC DOS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:325–7</sup> The Macintosh was the favorite among US musicians, as it was marketed at a competitive price, and would be several years before PC systems would catch up to its efficiency and <a href="/wiki/WIMP_(computing)" title="WIMP (computing)">graphical interface</a>.</p>
<p>The Atari ST was favored in Europe, where Macintoshes were more expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:324–5, 331</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIGS" title="Apple IIGS">Apple IIGS</a> used a digital sound chip designed for the <a href="/wiki/Ensoniq_Mirage" title="Ensoniq Mirage">Ensoniq Mirage</a> synthesizer, and later models used a custom sound system and upgraded processors, which drove other companies to improve their own offerings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:326,328</sup> The Atari ST was favored for its MIDI ports that were built directly into the computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:329</sup> Most music software in MIDI's first decade was published for either the Apple or the Atari.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:335</sup> By the time of <a href="/wiki/Windows_3.0" title="Windows 3.0">Windows 3.0</a>'s 1990 release, PCs had caught up in processing power and had acquired a graphical interface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:325</sup> and software titles began to see release on multiple platforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:335</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_files">Standard files</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Standard files">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Standard MIDI File (SMF) is a <a href="/wiki/File_format" title="File format">file format</a> that provides a standardized way for music sequences to be saved, transported, and opened in other systems. The compact size of these files led to their widespread use in computers, mobile phone <a href="/wiki/Ringtone" title="Ringtone">ringtones</a>, webpage authoring and musical greeting cards. These files are intended for universal use, and include such information as note values, timing and track names. Lyrics may be included as <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>, and can be displayed by <a href="/wiki/Karaoke" title="Karaoke">karaoke</a> machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The SMF specification was developed and is maintained by the MMA.</p>
<p>SMFs are created as an export format of software sequencers or hardware workstations. They organize MIDI messages into one or more parallel <a href="/wiki/Multitrack_recording" title="Multitrack recording">tracks</a>, and timestamp the events so that they can be played back in sequence. A <a href="/wiki/Header_(computing)" title="Header (computing)">header</a> contains the arrangement's track count, tempo and which of three SMF formats the file is in. A type 0 file contains the entire performance, merged onto a single track, while type 1 files may contain any number of tracks that are performed in synchrony. Type 2 files are rarely used<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and store multiple arrangements, with each arrangement having its own track and intended to be played in sequence. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> bundles SMFs together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downloadable_Sounds" title="Downloadable Sounds">Downloadable Sounds</a> (DLS) in a <a href="/wiki/Resource_Interchange_File_Format" title="Resource Interchange File Format">Resource Interchange File Format</a> (RIFF) wrapper, as RMID files with a <code>.rmi</code> extension. RIFF-RMID has been <a href="/wiki/Deprecation" title="Deprecation">deprecated</a> in favor of Extensible Music Files (<a href="/wiki/XMF" title="XMF">XMF</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="File_sharing">File sharing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: File sharing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A MIDI file is not a recording of actual audio. Rather, it is a set of instructions, and can use a thousand times less disk space than the equivalent recorded audio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-27">[27]</a></sup> This made MIDI file arrangements an attractive way to share music, before the advent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadband_internet_access" title="Broadband internet access">broadband internet access</a> and multi-gigabyte hard drives. Licensed MIDI files on <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disks</a> were commonly available in stores in Europe and Japan during the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The major drawback to this is the wide variation in quality of users' audio cards, and in the actual audio contained as samples or synthesized sound in the card that the MIDI data only refers to symbolically. Even a sound card that contains high-quality sampled sounds can have inconsistent quality from one instrument to another,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-27">[27]</a></sup> while different model cards have no guarantee of consistent sound of the same instrument. Early budget cards, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AdLib" title="AdLib">AdLib</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sound_Blaster" title="Sound Blaster">Sound Blaster</a> and its compatibles, used a stripped-down version of Yamaha's <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation_synthesis" title="Frequency modulation synthesis">frequency modulation synthesis</a> (FM synthesis) technology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiffenFM_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-WiffenFM-29">[29]</a></sup> played back through low-quality digital-to-analog converters. The low-fidelity reproduction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-27">[27]</a></sup> of these ubiquitous<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiffenFM_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-WiffenFM-29">[29]</a></sup> cards was often assumed to somehow be a property of MIDI itself. This created a perception of MIDI as low-quality audio, while in reality MIDI itself contains no sound,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Battino_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Battino-30">[30]</a></sup> and the quality of its playback depends entirely on the quality of the sound-producing device (and of samples in the device).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewster_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brewster-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:227</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_MIDI_editors_and_sequencers" title="Comparison of MIDI editors and sequencers">Comparison of MIDI editors and sequencers</a></div>
<p>The main advantage of the personal computer in a MIDI system is that it can serve a number of different purposes, depending on the software that is loaded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:55</sup> <a href="/wiki/Computer_multitasking" title="Computer multitasking">Multitasking</a> allows simultaneous operation of programs that may be able to share data with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:65</sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequencers">Sequencers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sequencers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music_sequencer" title="Music sequencer">Music sequencer</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Audio_sequencer" title="Audio sequencer">Audio sequencer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation" title="Digital audio workstation">Digital audio workstation</a></div>
<p>Sequencing software provides a number of benefits to a composer or arranger. It allows recorded MIDI to be manipulated using standard computer editing features such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cut,_copy_and_paste" title="Cut, copy and paste">cut, copy and paste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drag_and_drop" title="Drag and drop">drag and drop</a>. <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_shortcut" title="Keyboard shortcut">Keyboard shortcuts</a> can be used to streamline workflow, and editing functions are often selectable via MIDI commands. The sequencer allows each channel to be set to play a different sound, and gives a graphical overview of the arrangement. A variety of editing tools are made available, including a notation display that can be used to create printed parts for musicians. Tools such as <a href="/wiki/Loop_(music)" title="Loop (music)">looping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(music)" title="Quantization (music)">quantization</a>, randomization, and <a href="/wiki/Transposition_(music)" title="Transposition (music)">transposition</a> simplify the arranging process.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beats_(music)" title="Beats (music)">Beat</a> creation is simplified, and <a href="/wiki/Groove_(music)" title="Groove (music)">groove</a> templates can be used to duplicate another track's rhythmic feel. Realistic expression can be added through the manipulation of real-time controllers. Mixing can be performed, and MIDI can be synchronized with recorded audio and video tracks. Work can be saved, and transported between different computers or studios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desmond_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Desmond-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:164–6</sup></p>
<p>Sequencers may take alternate forms, such as drum pattern editors that allow users to create beats by clicking on pattern grids,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:118</sup> and loop sequencers such as <a href="/wiki/ACID_Pro" title="ACID Pro">ACID Pro</a>, which allow MIDI to be combined with prerecorded audio loops whose tempos and keys are matched to each other. Cue list sequencing is used to trigger dialogue, sound effect, and music cues in stage and broadcast production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:121</sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Notation.2Fscoring_software">Notation/scoring software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notation/scoring software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scorewriter" title="Scorewriter">Scorewriter</a></div>
<p>With MIDI, notes played on a keyboard can automatically be transcribed to <a href="/wiki/Sheet_music" title="Sheet music">sheet music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:213</sup> <a href="/wiki/Scorewriter" title="Scorewriter">Scorewriting</a> software typically lacks advanced sequencing tools, and is optimized for the creation of a neat, professional printout designed for live instrumentalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desmond_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Desmond-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:157</sup> These programs provide support for dynamics and expression markings, chord and lyric display, and complex score styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desmond_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Desmond-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:167</sup> Software is available that can print scores in <a href="/wiki/Braille" title="Braille">braille</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scorebutt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scorebutt (page does not exist)">ScoreButt</a> is software that can transcribe from MIDI to scores in real time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SmartScore" title="SmartScore">SmartScore</a> software performs the reverse process and can produce MIDI files from <a href="/wiki/Image_scanner" title="Image scanner">scanned</a> sheet music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Other notation programs include <a href="/wiki/Finale_(software)" title="Finale (software)">Finale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Encore_(software)" title="Encore (software)">Encore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sibelius_(software)" title="Sibelius (software)">Sibelius</a>.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Editor.2Flibrarians">Editor/librarians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Editor/librarians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Patch editors allow users to program their equipment through the computer interface. These became essential with the appearance of complex synthesizers such as the <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_FS1R" title="Yamaha FS1R">Yamaha FS1R</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> which contained several thousand programmable parameters, but had an interface that consisted of fifteen tiny buttons, four knobs and a small LCD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Digital instruments typically discourage users from experimentation, due to their lack of the feedback and direct control that switches and knobs would provide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibbs_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:393</sup> but patch editors give owners of hardware instruments and effects devices the same editing functionality that is available to users of software synthesizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Some editors are designed for a specific instrument or effects device, while other, "universal" editors support a variety of equipment, and ideally can control the parameters of every device in a setup through the use of System Exclusive commands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:129</sup></p>
<p>Patch librarians have the specialized function of organizing the sounds in a collection of equipment, and allow transmission of entire banks of sounds between an instrument and a computer. This allows the user to augment the device's limited patch storage with a computer's much greater disk capacity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:133</sup> and to share custom patches with other owners of the same instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cakewalk_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cakewalk-40">[40]</a></sup> Universal editor/librarians that combine the two functions were once common, and included Opcode Systems' Galaxy and <a href="/wiki/Emagic" title="Emagic">eMagic</a>'s SoundDiver. These programs have been largely abandoned with the trend toward computer-based synthesis, although <a href="/wiki/Mark_of_the_Unicorn" title="Mark of the Unicorn">Mark of the Unicorn</a>'s (MOTU)'s Unisyn and Sound Quest's Midi Quest remain available. <a href="/wiki/Native_Instruments" title="Native Instruments">Native Instruments</a>' Kore was an effort to bring the editor/librarian concept into the age of software instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Auto-accompaniment_programs">Auto-accompaniment programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Auto-accompaniment programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Programs that can dynamically generate accompaniment tracks are called "auto-accompaniment" programs. These create a full band arrangement in a style that the user selects, and send the result to a MIDI sound generating device for playback. The generated tracks can be used as educational or practice tools, as accompaniment for live performances, or as a songwriting aid. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Band-in-a-Box" title="Band-in-a-Box">Band-in-a-Box</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bozeman_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bozeman-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:42</sup> which originated on the Atari platform in the 1980s, One Man Band,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Busker,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> MiBAC Jazz, SoundTrek JAMMER,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> DigiBand<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and "Juzisound" Real Time Arranger(freeware).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_and_sampling">Synthesis and sampling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Synthesis and sampling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Software_synthesizer" title="Software synthesizer">Software synthesizer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_sampler" title="Software sampler">Software sampler</a></div>
<p>Computers can use software to generate sounds, which are then passed through a <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">digital-to-analog converter</a> (DAC) to a loudspeaker system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:213</sup> The number of sounds that can be played simultaneously (the polyphony) is dependent on the power of the computer's <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a>, as are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_rate" title="Sample rate">sample rate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Audio_bit_depth" title="Audio bit depth">bit depth</a> of playback, which directly affect the quality of the sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Synthesizers implemented in software are subject to <a href="/wiki/Jitter" title="Jitter">timing issues</a> that are not present with hardware instruments, whose dedicated operating systems are not subject to interruption from background tasks as desktop <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a> are. These timing issues can cause synchronization problems, and clicks and pops when sample playback is interrupted. Software synthesizers also exhibit a noticeable <a href="/wiki/Latency_(audio)" title="Latency (audio)">delay</a> known as latency in their sound generation, because computers use an <a href="/wiki/Data_buffer" title="Data buffer">audio buffer</a> that delays playback and disrupts MIDI timing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkerTime_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-WalkerTime-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Software synthesis' roots go back as far as the 1950s, when <a href="/wiki/Max_Mathews" title="Max Mathews">Max Mathews</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> wrote the <a href="/wiki/MUSIC-N" title="MUSIC-N">MUSIC-N</a> programming language, which was capable of non-real-time sound generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMM1997_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMM1997-51">[51]</a></sup> The first synthesizer to run directly on a host computer's CPU<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> was Reality, by Dave Smith's <a href="/wiki/Seer_Systems" title="Seer Systems">Seer Systems</a>, which achieved a low latency through tight driver integration, and therefore could run only on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Labs" title="Creative Labs">Creative Labs</a> soundcards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Some systems use dedicated hardware to reduce the load on the host CPU, as with <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_Sound_Corporation" title="Symbolic Sound Corporation">Symbolic Sound Corporation</a>'s Kyma System,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMM1997_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-DMM1997-51">[51]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Creamware_(software_company)" title="Creamware (software company)">Creamware</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sonic_Core" title="Sonic Core">Sonic Core</a> Pulsar/SCOPE systems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> which power an entire studio's worth of instruments, effects, and mixers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ability to construct full MIDI arrangements entirely in computer software allows a composer to render a finalized result directly as an audio file.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Why_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Why-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_music">Game music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Game music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Early PC games were distributed on floppy disks, and the small size of MIDI files made them a viable means of providing soundtracks. Games of the <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a> and early Windows eras typically required compatibility with either <a href="/wiki/Ad_Lib,_Inc." title="Ad Lib, Inc.">Ad Lib</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sound_Blaster" title="Sound Blaster">Sound Blaster</a> audio cards. These cards used <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation_synthesis" title="Frequency modulation synthesis">FM synthesis</a>, which generates sound through <a href="/wiki/Modulation" title="Modulation">modulation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine waves</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Chowning" title="John Chowning">John Chowning</a>, the technique's pioneer, theorized that the technology would be capable of accurate recreation of any sound if <a href="/wiki/Additive_synthesis" title="Additive synthesis">enough sine waves were used</a>, but budget computer audio cards performed FM synthesis with only two sine waves. Combined with the cards' 8-bit audio, this resulted in a sound described as "artificial"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and "primitive".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wavetable <a href="/wiki/Daughterboard" title="Daughterboard">daughterboards</a> that were later available provided audio samples that could be used in place of the FM sound. These were expensive, but often used the sounds from respected MIDI instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/E-mu_Proteus" title="E-mu Proteus">E-mu Proteus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-57">[57]</a></sup> The computer industry moved in the mid-1990s toward wavetable-based soundcards with 16-bit playback, but standardized on a 2MB ROM, a space too small in which to fit good-quality samples of 128 instruments plus drum kits. Some manufacturers used 12-bit samples, and padded those to 16 bits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">Other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MIDI has been adopted as a control protocol in a number of non-musical applications. <a href="/wiki/MIDI_Show_Control" title="MIDI Show Control">MIDI Show Control</a> uses MIDI commands to direct stage lighting systems and to trigger cued events in theatrical productions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VJ_(video_performance_artist)" title="VJ (video performance artist)">VJs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turntablism" title="Turntablism">turntablists</a> use it to cue clips, and to synchronize equipment, and recording systems use it for synchronization and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Console_automation" title="Console automation">automation</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Motion" title="Apple Motion">Apple Motion</a> allows control of animation parameters through MIDI. The 1987 <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/MIDI_Maze" title="MIDI Maze">MIDI Maze</a></i> and the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_puzzle_game" title="Computer puzzle game">computer puzzle game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Oxyd" title="Oxyd">Oxyd</a></i> used MIDI to network computers together, and kits are available that allow MIDI control over home lighting and appliances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite its association with music devices, MIDI can control any device that can read and process a MIDI command. It is therefore possible to send a spacecraft from earth to another destination in space, control home lighting, heating and air conditioning and even sequence traffic light signals all through MIDI commands. The receiving device or object would require a General MIDI processor, however in this instance, the program changes would trigger a function on that device rather than notes from MIDI instrument. Each function can be set to a timer (also controlled by MIDI) or other condition determined by the device's creator.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devices">Devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Connectors">Connectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Connectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg"><img alt="MIDI connectors and a MIDI cable" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="534" data-file-width="780" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg/220px-Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg/330px-Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg/440px-Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Midi_ports_and_cable.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
MIDI connectors and a MIDI cable.</div>
</div>

<p>The cables terminate in a <a href="/wiki/DIN_connector" title="DIN connector">180° five-pin DIN connector</a>. Standard applications use only three of the five conductors: a <a href="/wiki/Ground_(electricity)" title="Ground (electricity)">ground</a> wire, and a <a href="/wiki/Balanced_line" title="Balanced line">balanced pair</a> of conductors that carry a +5 volt signal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bozeman_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bozeman-42">[42]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:41</sup> This connector configuration can only carry messages in one direction, so a second cable is necessary for two-way communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> Some proprietary applications, such as <a href="/wiki/Phantom_power" title="Phantom power">phantom-powered</a> footswitch controllers, use the spare pins for <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> (DC) power transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Opto-isolator" title="Opto-isolator">Opto-isolators</a> keep MIDI devices electrically separated from their connectors, which prevents the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Ground_loop_(electricity)" title="Ground loop (electricity)">ground loops</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:63</sup> and protects equipment from voltage spikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:277</sup> There is no <a href="/wiki/Error_detection_and_correction" title="Error detection and correction">error detection</a> capability in MIDI, so the maximum cable length is set at 15 meters (50 feet) in order to limit <a href="/wiki/Interference_(communication)" title="Interference (communication)">interference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MIDI_connector2.svg"><img alt="Diagram of a MIDI connector" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="177" data-file-width="177" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/MIDI_connector2.svg/110px-MIDI_connector2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/MIDI_connector2.svg/165px-MIDI_connector2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/MIDI_connector2.svg/220px-MIDI_connector2.svg.png 2x" width="110"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MIDI_connector2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A MIDI connector, showing the pins as numbered.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most devices do not copy messages from their input to their output port. A third type of port, the "thru" port, emits a copy of everything received at the input port, allowing data to be forwarded to another instrument<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:278</sup> in a <a href="/wiki/Daisy_chain_(electrical_engineering)" title="Daisy chain (electrical engineering)">"daisy chain"</a> arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiana.edu_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-indiana.edu-63">[63]</a></sup> Not all devices contain thru ports, and devices that lack the ability to generate MIDI data, such as effects units and sound modules, may not include out ports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibbs_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:384</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_devices">Management devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Management devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Each device in a daisy chain adds delay to the system. This is avoided with a MIDI thru box, which contains several outputs that provide an exact copy of the box's input signal. A MIDI merger is able to combine the input from multiple devices into a single stream, and allows multiple controllers to be connected to a single device. A MIDI switcher allows switching between multiple devices, and eliminates the need to physically repatch cables. MIDI <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patch_bay" title="Patch bay">patch bays</a> combine all of these functions. They contain multiple inputs and outputs, and allow any combination of input channels to be routed to any combination of output channels. Routing setups can be created using computer software, stored in memory, and selected by MIDI program change commands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:47–50</sup> This enables the devices to function as standalone MIDI routers in situations where no computer is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62–3</sup> MIDI patch bays also clean up any skewing of MIDI data bits that occurs at the input stage.</p>
<p>MIDI data processors are used for utility tasks and special effects. These include MIDI filters, which remove unwanted MIDI data from the stream, and MIDI delays, effects which send a repeated copy of the input data at a set time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:51</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interfaces">Interfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Interfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A computer MIDI interface's main function is to match clock speeds between the MIDI device and the computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiana.edu_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-indiana.edu-63">[63]</a></sup> Some computer sound cards include a standard MIDI connector, whereas others connect by any of various means that include the <a href="/wiki/D-subminiature" title="D-subminiature">D-subminiature</a> DA-15 <a href="/wiki/Game_port" title="Game port">game port</a>, <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USB</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FireWire" title="FireWire">FireWire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> or a proprietary connection. The increasing use of <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USB</a> connectors in the 2000s has led to the availability of MIDI-to-USB data interfaces that can transfer MIDI channels to USB-equipped computers. Some MIDI keyboard controllers are equipped with USB jacks, and can be plugged into computers that run music software.</p>
<p>MIDI's serial transmission leads to timing problems. Experienced musicians can detect time differences of as small as 1/3 of a millisecond (ms)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (which is how long it takes sound to travel 4 inches), and a three-byte MIDI message requires nearly 1ms for transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Because MIDI is serial, it can only send one event at a time. If an event is sent on two channels at once, the event on the higher-numbered channel cannot transmit until the first one is finished, and so is delayed by 1ms. If an event is sent on all channels at the same time, the highest-numbered channel's transmission will be delayed by as much as 16ms. This contributed to the rise of MIDI interfaces with multiple in- and out-ports, because timing improves when events are spread between multiple ports as opposed to multiple channels on the same port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkerTime_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-WalkerTime-50">[50]</a></sup> The term "MIDI slop" refers to audible timing errors that result when MIDI transmission is delayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controllers">Controllers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Controllers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Remote_25.jpg"><img alt="A Novation Remote 25 two-octave MIDI controller" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="511" data-file-width="800" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Remote_25.jpg/220px-Remote_25.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Remote_25.jpg/330px-Remote_25.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Remote_25.jpg/440px-Remote_25.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Remote_25.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two-octave MIDI controllers are popular for use with laptop computers, due to their portability. This unit provides a variety of real-time controllers, which can manipulate various sound design parameters of computer-based or standalone hardware instruments, effects, mixers and recording devices.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MIDI_controller" title="MIDI controller">MIDI controller</a></div>
<p>There are two types of MIDI controllers: performance controllers that generate notes and are used to perform music,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and controllers which may not send notes, but transmit other types of real-time events. Many devices are some combination of the two types.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/MIDI_keyboard" title="MIDI keyboard">Keyboards</a> are by far the most common type of MIDI controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cakewalk_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cakewalk-40">[40]</a></sup> MIDI was designed with keyboards in mind, and any controller that is not a keyboard is considered an "alternative" controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> This was seen as a limitation by composers who were not interested in keyboard-based music, but the standard proved flexible, and MIDI compatibility was introduced to other types of controllers, including guitars, stringed and wind instruments, drums and specialized and experimental controllers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:23</sup> Other controllers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drum_controller" title="Drum controller">drum controllers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_controller" title="Wind controller">wind controllers</a>, which can emulate the playing of <a href="/wiki/Drum_kit" title="Drum kit">drum kit</a> and wind instruments, respectively.</p>
<p>Software synthesizers offer great power and versatility, but some players feel that division of attention between a MIDI keyboard and a computer keyboard and mouse robs some of the immediacy from the playing experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Devices dedicated to real-time MIDI control provide an ergonomic benefit, and can provide a greater sense of connection with the instrument than an interface that is accessed through a mouse or a push-button digital menu. Controllers may be general-purpose devices that are designed to work with a variety of equipment, or they may be designed to work with a specific piece of software. Examples of the latter include Akai's APC40 controller for <a href="/wiki/Ableton_Live" title="Ableton Live">Ableton Live</a>, and Korg's MS-20ic controller that is a reproduction of their <a href="/wiki/Korg_MS-20" title="Korg MS-20">MS-20</a> analog synthesizer. The MS-20ic controller includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patch_cables" title="Patch cables">patch cables</a> that can be used to control signal routing in their virtual reproduction of the MS-20 synthesizer, and can also control third-party devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korg_05RW_front.jpg"><img alt="A General MIDI sound module." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Korg_05RW_front.jpg/220px-Korg_05RW_front.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Korg_05RW_front.jpg/330px-Korg_05RW_front.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Korg_05RW_front.jpg/440px-Korg_05RW_front.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korg_05RW_front.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sound module, which requires an external controller (e.g., a MIDI keyboard) to trigger its sounds. These devices are highly portable, but their limited programming interface requires computer-based tools for comfortable access to their sound parameters.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A MIDI instrument contains ports to send and receive MIDI signals, a CPU to process those signals, an interface that allows user programming, audio circuitry to generate sound, and controllers. The operating system and factory sounds are often stored in a <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">Read-only memory</a> (ROM) unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:67–70</sup></p>
<p>A MIDI instrument can also be a stand-alone module (without a piano style keyboard) consisting of a General MIDI soundboard (GM, GS and/XG), onboard editing, including transposing/pitch changes, MIDI instrument changes and adjusting volume, pan, reverb levels and other MIDI controllers. Typically, the MIDI Module will include a large screen, enabling the user to view information depending on the function selected at that time. Features can include scrolling lyrics, usually embedded in a MIDI File or Karaoke MIDI, playlists, song library and editing screens. Some MIDI Modules include a Harmonizer and the ability to playback and transpose MP3 audio files.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesizers">Synthesizers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Synthesizers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">Synthesizer</a></div>
<p>Synthesizers may employ any of a variety of sound generation techniques. They may include an integrated keyboard, or may exist as "sound modules" or "expanders" that generate sounds when triggered by an external controller, such as a MIDI keyboard. Sound modules are typically designed to be mounted in a <a href="/wiki/19-inch_rack" title="19-inch rack">19-inch rack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70–72</sup> Manufacturers commonly produce a synthesizer in both standalone and rack-mounted versions, and often offer the keyboard version in a variety of sizes.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Samplers">Samplers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Samplers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">Sampler (musical instrument)</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">sampler</a> can record and digitize audio, store it in <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">random-access memory</a> (RAM), and play it back. Samplers typically allow a user to edit a <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(signal_processing)" title="Sampling (signal processing)">sample</a> and save it to a hard disk, apply effects to it, and shape it with the same tools that synthesizers use. They also may be available in either keyboard or rack-mounted form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-19"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:74–8</sup> Instruments that generate sounds through sample playback, but have no recording capabilities, are known as "<a href="/wiki/Rompler" title="Rompler">ROMplers</a>".</p>
<p>Samplers did not become established as viable MIDI instruments as quickly as synthesizers did, due to the expense of memory and processing power at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:295</sup> The first low-cost MIDI sampler was the <a href="/wiki/Ensoniq" title="Ensoniq">Ensoniq</a> Mirage, introduced in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:304</sup> MIDI samplers are typically limited by displays that are too small to use to edit sampled waveforms, although some can be connected to a computer monitor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:305</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Drum_machines">Drum machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Drum machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drum_machine" title="Drum machine">Drum machine</a></div>
<p>Drum machines typically are sample playback devices that specialize in drum and percussion sounds. They commonly contain a sequencer that allows the creation of drum patterns, and allows them to be arranged into a song. There often are multiple audio outputs, so that each sound or group of sounds can be routed to a separate output. The individual drum voices may be playable from another MIDI instrument, or from a sequencer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-20"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:84</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Workstations_and_hardware_sequencers">Workstations and hardware sequencers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Workstations and hardware sequencers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Music_workstation" title="Music workstation">Music workstation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_sequencer" title="Music sequencer">Music sequencer</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tenori-on.jpg"><img alt="A button matrix MIDI controller" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="330" data-file-width="330" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Tenori-on.jpg/220px-Tenori-on.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/70/Tenori-on.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tenori-on.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Yamaha's <a href="/wiki/Tenori-on" title="Tenori-on">Tenori-on</a> controller allows arrangements to be built by "drawing" on its array of lighted buttons. The resulting arrangements can be played back using its internal sounds or external sound sources, or recorded in a computer-based sequencer.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sequencer technology predates MIDI. <a href="/wiki/Analog_sequencer" title="Analog sequencer">Analog sequencers</a> use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CV/Gate" title="CV/Gate">CV/Gate</a> signals to control pre-MIDI analog synthesizers. MIDI sequencers typically are operated by transport features modeled after those of <a href="/wiki/Tape_recorder" title="Tape recorder">tape decks</a>. They are capable of recording MIDI performances, and arranging them into individual tracks along a <a href="/wiki/Multitrack_recording" title="Multitrack recording">multitrack recording</a> concept. Music workstations combine controller keyboards with an internal sound generator and a sequencer. These can be used to build complete arrangements and play them back using their own internal sounds, and function as self-contained music production studios. They commonly include file storage and transfer capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-21"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:103–4</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_devices">Effects devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Effects devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_unit" title="Effects unit">Effects unit</a></div>
<p>Audio effects units that are frequently used in stage and recording, such as <a href="/wiki/Reverberation" title="Reverberation">reverbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delay_(audio_effect)" title="Delay (audio effect)">delays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chorus_effect#Electronic_effect" title="Chorus effect">choruses</a>, can be remotely adjusted via MIDI signals. Some units allow only a limited number of parameters to be controlled this way, but most will respond to program change messages. The <a href="/wiki/Eventide,_Inc" title="Eventide, Inc">Eventide</a> H3000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmonizer" title="Harmonizer">Ultra-harmonizer</a> is an example of a unit that allows such extensive MIDI control that it is playable as a synthesizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:322</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_specifications">Technical specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Technical specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MIDI messages are made up of 8-bit <i>word</i>s (commonly called <i><a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">bytes</a></i>) that are transmitted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_communications" title="Serial communications">serially</a> at 31.25 <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">kbit/s</a>. This rate was chosen because it is an exact division of 1 MHz, the speed at which many early <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a> operated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-16"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:286</sup> The first bit of each word identifies whether the word is a status byte or a data byte, and is followed by seven bits of information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-22"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13–14</sup> A start bit and a stop bit are added to each byte for <a href="/wiki/Frame_synchronization" title="Frame synchronization">framing</a> purposes, so a MIDI byte requires ten bits for transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-17"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:286</sup></p>
<p>A MIDI link can carry sixteen independent channels of information. The channels are numbered 1–16, but their actual corresponding <a href="/wiki/Binary_code" title="Binary code">binary</a> encoding is 0–15. A device can be configured to only listen to specific channels and to ignore the messages sent on other channels ("Omni Off" mode), or it can listen to all channels, effectively ignoring the channel address ("Omni On"). An individual device may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophonic" title="Monophonic">monophonic</a> (the start of a new "note-on" MIDI command implies the termination of the previous note), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphonic" title="Polyphonic">polyphonic</a> (multiple notes may be sounding at once, until the polyphony limit of the instrument is reached, or the notes reach the end of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADSR_envelope#ADSR_envelope" title="ADSR envelope">decay envelope</a>, or explicit "note-off" MIDI commands are received). Receiving devices can typically be set to all four combinations of "omni off/on" versus "mono/poly" modes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-23"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:14–18</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Messages">Messages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Messages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A MIDI message is an instruction that controls some aspect of the receiving device. A MIDI message consists of a status byte, which indicates the type of the message, followed by up to two data bytes that contain the parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewster_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brewster-22">[22]</a></sup> MIDI messages can be "channel messages", which are sent on only one of the 16 channels and can be heard only by devices receiving on that channel, or "system messages", which are heard by all devices. Any data not relevant to a receiving device is ignored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibbs_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:384</sup> There are five types of message: Channel Voice, Channel Mode, System Common, System Real-Time, and System Exclusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Channel Voice messages transmit real-time performance data over a single channel. Examples include "note-on" messages which contain a MIDI note number that specifies the note's pitch, a velocity value that indicates how forcefully the note was played, and the channel number; "note-off" messages that end a note; program change messages that change a device's patch; and control changes that allow adjustment of an instrument's parameters. Channel Mode messages include the Omni/mono/poly mode on and off messages, as well as messages to reset all controllers to their default state or to send "note-off" messages for all notes. System messages do not include channel numbers, and are received by every device in the MIDI chain. MIDI time code is an example of a System Common message. System Real-Time messages provide for synchronization, and include MIDI clock and Active Sensing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-24"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18–35</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="System_Exclusive_messages">System Exclusive messages <span id="SysEx"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: System Exclusive messages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>System Exclusive (SysEx) messages are a major reason for the flexibility and longevity of the MIDI standard. They allow manufacturers to create proprietary messages which provide control over their equipment in a way that is more thorough than is provided for by standard MIDI messages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-18"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:287</sup> SysEx messages are addressed to a specific device in a system. Each manufacturer has a unique identifier that is included in its SysEx messages, which helps ensure that the messages will be heard only by the targeted device, and ignored by all others. Many instruments also include a SysEx ID setting, which allows two devices of the same model to be addressed independently while connected to the same system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> SysEx messages may include functionality beyond what the MIDI standard provides. They are targeted at a specific instrument, and are ignored by all other devices on the system.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation_chart">Implementation chart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Implementation chart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Devices typically do not respond to every type of message defined by the MIDI specification. The MIDI implementation chart was standardized by the MMA as a way for users to see what specific capabilities an instrument has, and how it responds to messages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-25"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:231</sup> A specific MIDI Implementation Chart is usually published for each MIDI device within the device documentation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_specifications">Electrical specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Electrical specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg"><img alt="MIDI interconnection schematic" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="229" data-file-width="694" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg/440px-MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg/660px-MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg/880px-MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg.png 2x" width="440"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MIDI_IN_OUT_schematic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An electrical schematic of the MIDI electrical/optical interconnection.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The MIDI specification for the electrical interface is based on a fully isolated <a href="/wiki/Current_loop" title="Current loop">current loop</a>. The MIDI out port nominally sources a +5 volt source<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[a]</a></sup> through a 220 ohm resistor out through pin 4 on the MIDI out DIN connector, in on pin 4 of the receiving device's MIDI in DIN connector, through a 220 ohm protection resistor and the LED of an opto-isolator. The current then returns via pin 5 on the MIDI in port to the originating device's MIDI out port pin 5, again with a 220 ohm resistor in the path, giving a nominal current of about 5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliampere" title="Milliampere">milliamperes</a>. Despite the cable's appearance, there is no conductive path between the two MIDI devices, only an optically isolated one. Properly designed MIDI devices are relatively immune to ground loops and similar interference. The data rate on this system is 31,250 bits per second, logic 0 being current on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MIDI_specification_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-MIDI_specification-73">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MIDI specification provides for a ground "wire" and a braid or foil shield, connected on pin 2, protecting the two signal-carrying conductors on pins 4 and 5. Although the MIDI cable is supposed to connect pin 2 and the braid or foil shield to chassis ground, it should do so only at the MIDI out port; the MIDI in port should leave pin 2 unconnected and isolated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MIDI_specification_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-MIDI_specification-73">[72]</a></sup> Some large manufacturers of MIDI devices use modified MIDI in-only DIN 5-pin sockets with the metallic conductors intentionally omitted at pin positions 1, 2, and 3 so that the maximum voltage isolation is obtained.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensions">Extensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Extensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg"><img alt="GM Standard Drum Map on the keyboard" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="693" data-file-width="570" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg/220px-GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg/330px-GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg/440px-GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GM_Standard_Drum_Map_on_the_keyboard.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The GM Standard Drum Map, which specifies the percussion sound that a given note will trigger.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>MIDI's flexibility and widespread adoption have led to many refinements of the standard, and have enabled its application to purposes beyond those for which it was originally intended.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_MIDI">General MIDI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: General MIDI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/General_MIDI" title="General MIDI">General MIDI</a></div>
<p>MIDI allows selection of an instrument's sounds through program change messages, but there is no guarantee that any two instruments have the same sound at a given program location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bello_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bello-74">[73]</a></sup> Program #0 may be a piano on one instrument, or a flute on another. The <a href="/wiki/General_MIDI" title="General MIDI">General MIDI</a> (GM) standard was established in 1991, and provides a standardized sound bank that allows a Standard MIDI File created on one device to sound similar when played back on another. GM specifies a bank of 128 sounds arranged into 16 families of eight related instruments, and assigns a specific program number to each instrument. Percussion instruments are placed on channel 10, and a specific MIDI note value is mapped to each percussion sound. GM-compliant devices must offer 24-note polyphony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup> Any given program change will select the same instrument sound on any GM-compatible instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The GM standard eliminates variation in note mapping. Some manufacturers had disagreed over what note number should represent middle C, but GM specifies that note number 69 plays <a href="/wiki/A440_(pitch_standard)" title="A440 (pitch standard)">A440</a>, which in turn fixes middle C as note number 60. GM-compatible devices are required to respond to velocity, aftertouch, and pitch bend, to be set to specified default values at startup, and to support certain controller numbers such as for <a href="/wiki/Sustain_pedal" title="Sustain pedal">sustain pedal</a>, and Registered Parameter Numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup> A simplified version of GM, called "GM Lite", is used in mobile phones and other devices with limited processing power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bello_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bello-74">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GS.2C_XG.2C_and_GM2">GS, XG, and GM2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: GS, XG, and GM2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/General_MIDI_Level_2" title="General MIDI Level 2">General MIDI Level 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roland_GS" title="Roland GS">Roland GS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_XG" title="Yamaha XG">Yamaha XG</a></div>
<p>A general opinion quickly formed that the GM's 128-instrument sound set was not large enough. Roland's General Standard, or <a href="/wiki/Roland_GS" title="Roland GS">GS</a>, system included additional sounds, drumkits and effects, provided a "bank select" command that could be used to access them, and used MIDI Non-Registered Parameter Numbers (NRPNs) to access its new features. Yamaha's Extended General MIDI, or <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_XG" title="Yamaha XG">XG</a>, followed in 1994. XG similarly offered extra sounds, drumkits and effects, but used standard controllers instead of NRPNs for editing, and increased polyphony to 32 voices. Both standards feature backward compatibility with the GM specification, but are not compatible with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[77]</a></sup> Neither standard has been adopted beyond its creator, but both are commonly supported by music software titles.</p>
<p>Member companies of Japan's <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Musical_Electronics_Industry" title="Association of Musical Electronics Industry">AMEI</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/General_MIDI_Level_2" title="General MIDI Level 2">General MIDI Level 2</a> specification in 1999. GM2 maintains backward compatibility with GM, but increases polyphony to 32 voices, standardizes several controller numbers such as for <a href="/wiki/Sostenuto" title="Sostenuto">sostenuto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soft_pedal" title="Soft pedal">soft pedal</a> (<i>una corda</i>), RPNs and Universal System Exclusive Messages, and incorporates the MIDI Tuning Standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[78]</a></sup> GM2 is the basis of the instrument selection mechanism in Scalable Polyphony MIDI (SP-MIDI), a MIDI variant for low power devices that allows the device's polyphony to scale according to its processing power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bello_74-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bello-74">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuning_standard">Tuning standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Tuning standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIDI_Tuning_Standard" title="MIDI Tuning Standard">MIDI Tuning Standard</a></div>
<p>Most MIDI synthesizers use <a href="/wiki/Equal_temperament" title="Equal temperament">equal temperament</a> tuning. The <a href="/wiki/MIDI_tuning_standard" title="MIDI tuning standard">MIDI tuning standard</a> (MTS), ratified in 1992, allows alternate tunings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTS_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTS-80">[79]</a></sup> MTS allows <a href="/wiki/Microtonal_music" title="Microtonal music">microtunings</a> that can be loaded from a bank of up to 128 patches, and allows real-time adjustment of note pitches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[80]</a></sup> Manufacturers are not required to support the standard. Those who do are not required to implement all of its features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTS_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTS-80">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_code">Time code</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Time code">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MIDI_timecode" title="MIDI timecode">MIDI timecode</a></div>
<p>A sequencer can drive a MIDI system with its internal clock, but when a system contains multiple sequencers, they must synchronize to a common clock. MIDI Time Code (MTC), developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digidesign" title="Digidesign">Digidesign</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[81]</a></sup> implements SysEx messages<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup> that have been developed specifically for timing purposes, and is able to translate to and from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMPTE_time_code" title="SMPTE time code">SMPTE time code</a> standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-19"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:288</sup> MIDI Clock is based on tempo, but SMPTE time code is based on <a href="/wiki/Frame_(video)" title="Frame (video)">frames</a> per second, and is independent of tempo. MTC, like SMPTE code, includes position information, and can adjust itself if a timing pulse is lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup> MIDI interfaces such as Mark of the Unicorn's MIDI Timepiece can convert SMPTE code to MTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Machine_control">Machine control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Machine control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MIDI_Machine_Control" title="MIDI Machine Control">MIDI Machine Control</a></div>
<p>MIDI Machine Control (MMC) consists of a set of SysEx commands<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[85]</a></sup> that operate the transport controls of hardware recording devices. MMC allows a sequencer to send "Start", "Stop", and "Record" commands to a connected tape deck or hard disk recording system, and to fast-forward or rewind the device so that it starts playback at the same point as the sequencer. No synchronization data is involved, although the devices may synchronize through MTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Show_control">Show control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Show control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waterworld_Plane.jpg"><img alt="A theatrical event operated by MIDI Show Control" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1932" data-file-width="2576" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Waterworld_Plane.jpg/220px-Waterworld_Plane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Waterworld_Plane.jpg/330px-Waterworld_Plane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Waterworld_Plane.jpg/440px-Waterworld_Plane.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

MIDI Show Control is used to cue and synchronize lighting and effects for theatrical events, such as the <a href="/wiki/Waterworld:_A_Live_Sea_War_Spectacular" title="Waterworld: A Live Sea War Spectacular">Waterworld</a> attraction at <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios_Hollywood" title="Universal Studios Hollywood">Universal Studios Hollywood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richmondsounddesign.com_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-richmondsounddesign.com-88">[87]</a></sup></div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MIDI_Show_Control" title="MIDI Show Control">MIDI Show Control</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/MIDI_Show_Control" title="MIDI Show Control">MIDI Show Control</a> (MSC) is a set of SysEx commands which allows sequencing and remote <a href="/wiki/Cue_(theatrical)" title="Cue (theatrical)">cueing</a> of show control devices such as lighting, music and sound playback, and <a href="/wiki/Motion_control" title="Motion control">motion control</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[88]</a></sup> Applications include stage productions, museum exhibits, recording studio control systems, and <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement park</a> attractions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richmondsounddesign.com_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-richmondsounddesign.com-88">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timestamping">Timestamping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Timestamping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One solution to MIDI timing problems is to mark MIDI events with the times they are to be played, and store them in a buffer in the MIDI interface ahead of time. Sending data beforehand reduces the likelihood that a busy passage will send a large amount of information that will overwhelm the transmission link. Once stored in the interface, the information is no longer subject to timing issues associated with USB jitter and computer operating system interrupts, and can be transmitted with a high degree of accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[89]</a></sup> MIDI timestamping only works when both the hardware and software support it. MOTU's MTS, eMagic's AMT, and Steinberg's Midex 8 were implementations that were incompatible with each other, and required users to own software and hardware manufactured by the same company in order to gain its benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkerTime_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-WalkerTime-50">[50]</a></sup> Timestamping is built into FireWire MIDI interfaces,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkerLate_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-WalkerLate-91">[90]</a></sup> Mac OS X <a href="/wiki/Core_Audio" title="Core Audio">Core Audio</a>, and Linux ALSA Sequencer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_dump_standard">Sample dump standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Sample dump standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An unforeseen capability of SysEx messages was their use for transporting audio samples between instruments. This led to the development of the sample dump standard (SDS), which established a new SysEx format for sample transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_7-20"><a href="#cite_note-Manning-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:287</sup> The SDS was later augmented with a pair of commands that allow the transmission of information about sample loop points, without requiring that the entire sample be transmitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Downloadable_sounds">Downloadable sounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Downloadable sounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DLS_format" title="DLS format">DLS format</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downloadable_Sounds" title="Downloadable Sounds">Downloadable Sounds</a> (DLS) specification, ratified in 1997, allows mobile devices and computer <a href="/wiki/Sound_card" title="Sound card">sound cards</a> to expand their wave tables with downloadable sound sets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[92]</a></sup> The DLS Level 2 Specification followed in 2006, and defined a standardized synthesizer architecture. The Mobile DLS standard calls for DLS banks to be combined with SP-MIDI, as self-contained Mobile XMF files.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_hardware_transports">Alternative hardware transports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Alternative hardware transports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to the original 31.25 kbit/s current-loop transported on <a href="/wiki/DIN_connector" title="DIN connector">5-pin DIN</a>, other connectors have been used for the same electrical data, and transmission of MIDI streams in different forms over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus">USB</a>, IEEE 1394 a.k.a. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FireWire" title="FireWire">FireWire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> is now common. Some samplers and hard drive recorders can also pass MIDI data between each other over SCSI.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USB_and_FireWire">USB and FireWire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: USB and FireWire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Members of the USB-IF in 1999 developed a standard for MIDI over USB, the "Universal Serial Bus Device Class Definition for MIDI Devices"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[94]</a></sup> MIDI over USB has become increasingly common as other interfaces that had been used for MIDI connections (serial, joystick, etc.) disappeared from personal computers. Linux, Microsoft Windows, Macintosh OS X, and Apple iOS operating systems include standard class drivers to support devices that use the "Universal Serial Bus Device Class Definition for MIDI Devices". Some manufacturers choose to implement a MIDI interface over USB that is designed to operate differently from the class specification, using custom drivers.</p>
<p>Apple Computer developed the FireWire interface during the 1990s. It began to appear on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_video_camera" title="Digital video camera">digital video cameras</a> toward the end of the decade, and on G3 Macintosh models in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiffenFW1_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-WiffenFW1-96">[95]</a></sup> It was created for use with multimedia applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkerLate_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-WalkerLate-91">[90]</a></sup> Unlike USB, FireWire uses intelligent controllers that can manage their own transmission without attention from the main CPU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiffenFW2_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-WiffenFW2-97">[96]</a></sup> As with standard MIDI devices, FireWire devices can communicate with each other with no computer present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cables_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cables-98">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="XLR_connectors">XLR connectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: XLR connectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Octave-Plateau <a href="/wiki/Voyetra-8" title="Voyetra-8">Voyetra-8</a> synthesizer was an early MIDI implementation using <a href="/wiki/XLR_connector#XLR3_connectors" title="XLR connector">XLR3 connectors</a> in place of the <a href="/wiki/DIN_connector" title="DIN connector">5-pin DIN</a>. It was released in the pre-MIDI years and later retrofitted with a MIDI interface but keeping its XLR connector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[98]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Serial_parallel.2C_and_joystick_port">Serial parallel, and joystick port</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Serial parallel, and joystick port">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As computer-based studio setups became common, MIDI devices that could connect directly to a computer became available. These typically used the <a href="/wiki/Mini-DIN_connector#8-pin" title="Mini-DIN connector">8-pin mini-DIN</a> connector that was used by Apple for <a href="/wiki/Serial_port" title="Serial port">serial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printer_port" title="Printer port">printer ports</a> prior to the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Power_Macintosh_G3_(Blue_%26_White)" title="Power Macintosh G3 (Blue &amp; White)">Blue &amp; White G3</a> models. MIDI interfaces intended for use as the centerpiece of a studio, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mark_of_the_Unicorn" title="Mark of the Unicorn">Mark of the Unicorn</a> MIDI Time Piece, were made possible by a "fast" transmission mode that could take advantage of these serial ports' ability to operate at 20 times the standard MIDI speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-26"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62–3</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cables_98-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cables-98">[97]</a></sup> Mini-DIN ports were built into some late-1990s MIDI instruments, and enabled such devices to be connected directly to a computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[99]</a></sup> Some devices connected via PCs' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DB-25" title="DB-25">DB-25</a> <a href="/wiki/Parallel_port" title="Parallel port">parallel port</a>, or through the joystick port found in many PC sound cards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cables_98-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cables-98">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="mLAN">mLAN</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: mLAN">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MLAN" title="MLAN">mLAN</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Corporation" title="Yamaha Corporation">Yamaha</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/MLAN" title="MLAN">mLAN</a> protocol in 1999. It was conceived as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_Area_Network" title="Local Area Network">Local Area Network</a> for musical instruments using FireWire as the transport, and was designed to carry multiple MIDI channels together with multichannel digital audio, data file transfers, and time code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiffenFW1_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-WiffenFW1-96">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiffenFW2_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-WiffenFW2-97">[96]</a></sup> mLan was used in a number of Yamaha products, notably <a href="/wiki/Digital_mixing_console" title="Digital mixing console">digital mixing consoles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motif" title="Yamaha Motif">Motif</a> synthesizer, and in third-party products such as the PreSonus FIREstation and the <a href="/wiki/Korg_Triton#Studio" title="Korg Triton">Korg Triton Studio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[100]</a></sup> No new mLan products have been released since 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethernet">Ethernet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: Ethernet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer network</a> implementation of MIDI provides network routing capabilities, and provides the high-bandwidth channel that earlier alternatives to MIDI, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZIPI" title="ZIPI">ZIPI</a>, were intended to bring. Proprietary implementations have existed since the 1980s, some of which use <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">fiber optic</a> cables for transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-27"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:53–4</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RTP_MIDI" title="RTP MIDI">RTP MIDI</a> open specification is gaining industry support, as proprietary MIDI/IP protocols require expensive licensing fees, or provide no advantage, apart from speed, over the original MIDI protocol. Apple has supported this protocol from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a> 10.4 onwards, and a <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> driver based on Apple's implementation exists for Windows XP and newer versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[101]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wireless">Wireless</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title="Edit section: Wireless">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Systems for wireless MIDI transmission have been available since the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber_1991_2-28"><a href="#cite_note-Huber_1991-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44</sup> Several commercially available transmitters allow wireless transmission of MIDI and OSC signals over <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[102]</a></sup> iOS devices are able to function as MIDI control surfaces, using Wi-Fi and OSC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[103]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/XBee" title="XBee">XBee</a> radio can be used to build a wireless MIDI transceiver as a do-it-yourself project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[104]</a></sup> Android devices are able to function as full MIDI control surfaces using several different protocols over <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[105]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3.5mm_audio_jack">3.5mm audio jack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: 3.5mm audio jack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some devices use <a href="/wiki/Phone_connector_(audio)" title="Phone connector (audio)">standard TRS audio minijack connectors</a> for MIDI data, including the Korg Electribe 2 and the Arturia Beatstep Pro. Both come with adaptors that break out to standard 5-pin DIN connectors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_developments">New developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MIDI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: New developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A new version of MIDI tentatively called "HD Protocol" or "High-Definition Protocol" has been under discussion since 2005, when it was announced as "HD-MIDI".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Battino_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Battino-30">[30]</a></sup> This new standard offers full <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backward compatibility</a> with MIDI 1.0 and is intended to support higher-speed transports, allow plug-and-play device discovery and enumeration, and provide greater data range and resolution. The numbers of channels and controllers are to be increased and messages are to be simplified. Entirely new kinds of events will be supported, such as a Note Update message and Direct Pitch in the Note message which are aimed at guitar controllers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMA_HD_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMA_HD-107">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMA_HD_2012_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMA_HD_2012-108">[107]</a></sup> Proposed <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a> transports include <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>-based protocols such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RTP_MIDI" title="RTP MIDI">RTP MIDI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Audio_Video_Bridging" title="Audio Video Bridging">Audio Video Bridging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cables_98-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cables-98">[97]</a></sup> The HD Protocol and a <a href="/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol" title="User Datagram Protocol">User Datagram Protocol</a> (UDP)-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_Layer" title="Transport Layer">transport</a> are under review by MMA's High-Definition Protocol Working Group (HDWG), which includes representatives from all sizes and types of companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMA_HD_2012_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-MMA_HD_2012-108">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prototype devices based on the draft standard have been shown privately at NAMM using wired and wireless connections,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMA_HD_107-1"><a href="#cite_note-MMA_HD-107">[106]</a></sup> however it is uncertain if and when the new protocol will be picked up by the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAMM2013_future_of_MIDI_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAMM2013_future_of_MIDI-109">[108]</a></sup> As of 2015, the HD Protocol specifications are nearing completion and MMA develops the policies on licensing and product certification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMA_AGM_2015_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMA_AGM_2015-110">[109]</a></sup> MIDI connectivity and a software synthesizer is still included in Linux, Windows, OS X, iOS. USB MIDI interfaces are supported by Android 6.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another development of the MIDI specification gaining wide adoption is MPE (Multidimensional Polyphonic Expression). This is a method of using MIDI that enables devices with several dimensions of continuous per-note control to interact with MPE-compatible synthesizers and software. Instruments like the <a href="/wiki/Continuum_Fingerboard" title="Continuum Fingerboard">Continuum Fingerboard</a>, Linnstrument, and Seaboard let users control pitch, timbre, and other nuances for individual notes within chords. A growing number of soft synths are now compatible with MPE, such as Strobe 2, Equator, and UVI Falcon. MPE works by assigning each note to its own MIDI channel so that particular messages can be applied to each note individually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[111]</a></sup></p>
