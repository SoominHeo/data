<p><b>Tachihara Masaaki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">立原 正秋</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 6 January 1926 – 12 August 1980)</span> was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pen-name" title="Pen-name">pen-name</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> novelist, essayist, poet and literary critic <a href="/wiki/Koreans_in_Japan" title="Koreans in Japan">of Korean descent</a>, active during the <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_period" title="Shōwa period">Shōwa period</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masaaki_Tachihara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tachihara was born in <a href="/wiki/Andong" title="Andong">Andong</a> city, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeongsangbuk-do" title="Gyeongsangbuk-do">Gyeongsangbuk-do</a> province, <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Korea</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_people" title="Korean people">Korean</a> parents. His father was a member of the former Korean aristocracy and a military officer serving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>, who became a <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen</a> priest after the Japanese annexation of Korea, and subsequently committed suicide when Tachihara was age five.</p>
<p>Four years after his father's death in 1931, Tachihara moved with his mother to <a href="/wiki/Yokosuka,_Kanagawa" title="Yokosuka, Kanagawa">Yokosuka</a> city, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanagawa_prefecture" title="Kanagawa prefecture">Kanagawa prefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. His <a href="/wiki/Korean_name" title="Korean name">Korean name</a> was <b>Kim Yun Kyu</b> (金胤奎: 김윤규), but he changed his name to <b>Nomura Shintarō <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">野村 震太郎</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span></b> when his mother married a Japanese man. Later, he registered his name as <b>Kanai Masaaki</b> under the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soushi-kaimei" title="Soushi-kaimei">soushi-kaimei</a></i> policy, but after his own marriage (to a Japanese woman), he once again changed his name to <b>Yonemoto Masaaki <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">米本 正秋</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span></b>. He has been naturalized in Japan in 1947.</p>
<p>He was a student at <a href="/wiki/Waseda_University" title="Waseda University">Waseda University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, and was initially enrolled in the Law Department. However, he gradually shifted over the Literature Department, drawn by his interest in the novels of <a href="/wiki/Yasunari_Kawabata" title="Yasunari Kawabata">Yasunari Kawabata</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">literary criticism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hideo_Kobayashi" title="Hideo Kobayashi">Hideo Kobayashi</a>.</p>
<p>Tachihara was strongly attracted to medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_culture" title="Japanese culture">Japanese culture</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Noh" title="Noh">Noh</a> drama, and traditional <a href="/wiki/Japanese_garden" title="Japanese garden">Japanese gardens</a>, and his novels are patterned after the aesthetics in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeami" title="Zeami">Zeami</a>'s Noh treatise <i>Fushi Kaiden</i>. Tachihara's interests included the collection of ceramics, especially many Korean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Yi Dynasty</a> works. It was not until after his death that his Korean ethnic background became widely known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">Literary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masaaki_Tachihara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tachihara's first novel, <i>Bakushū</i> (“Autumn Wheat”) was published in the <a href="/wiki/Literary_magazine" title="Literary magazine">literary magazine</a> <i>Bungei kenkyūkai</i>. It was well received by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_critic" title="Literary critic">literary critics</a>, which led to his decision to become a professional writer. In 1958, he published <i>Tanin no jiyū</i> (“Other People's Freedom”) in the magazine <i>Gunzō</i>, followed by <i>Takigi nō</i> (“Firelight Noh”), <i>Tsurugigasaki</i> (“Cape Tsurugi”) and <i>Urushi no hana</i> (“Lacquer Flower”).</p>
<p>He won the 55th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naoki_Award" title="Naoki Award">Naoki Award</a> for his novel <i>Shiroi keshi</i> (“White Poppy”, 1965). He also declined the <a href="/wiki/Akutagawa_Prize" title="Akutagawa Prize">Akutagawa Prize</a> twice, as he felt that the reward would damage his reputation as an author of serious literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of his books, <i>Wind and Stone</i>, has been translated into <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> by Stephen W. Kohl.</p>
<p>Tachihara lived in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamakura,_Kanagawa" title="Kamakura, Kanagawa">Kamakura</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanagawa_prefecture" title="Kanagawa prefecture">Kanagawa prefecture</a> from 1950 until his death of <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_cancer" title="Esophageal cancer">esophageal cancer</a>. Before he died, he officially changed his name to <b>Tachihara Masaaki</b>. His grave is at the temple of <a href="/wiki/Zuisen-ji" title="Zuisen-ji">Zuisen-ji</a> in Kamakura.</p>
