<p><b>Kim Chong-kon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">김종곤</span>; born 21 November 1930) is a former <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">admiral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diplomat" title="Diplomat">diplomat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a>, serving as Chief of Naval Operations, Ambassadors to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> (ROC) in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and member of <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(South_Korea)" title="National Assembly (South Korea)">National Assembly</a> before retirement. Started his military career in 1951, Kim participated several maritime operations during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, assigned to positions like warship commander, flotilla commander, naval station commander, fleet commander and senior staff in the Navy Headquarters. Kim also gained himself several medals from South Korea and Taiwan for his efforts on military and foreign affairs.</p>

<p>Belonging to the Gimhae Kim clan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E9.87.91.E8.90.AC.E6.96.B0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E9.87.91.E8.90.AC.E6.96.B0-1">[1]</a></sup> Kim Chong-kon was born in <a href="/wiki/Miryang" title="Miryang">Miryang</a>, Keishō-nandō of the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese Korea</a> (now <a href="/wiki/South_Gyeongsang_Province" title="South Gyeongsang Province">South Gyeongsang Province</a>, South Korea) on November 21, 1930, and spent years studying at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jinhae" title="Jinhae">Jinhae</a> High School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.BA.BA.E5.90.8D.E8.BE.AD.E5.85.B898_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.BA.BA.E5.90.8D.E8.BE.AD.E5.85.B898-2">[2]</a></sup> On September 1, 1948, Kim attend the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Naval_Academy" title="Korea Naval Academy">Korea Naval Academy</a>, and was commissioned as an <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">Ensign</a> after graduating with the 4th class on August 31, 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup> In his beginning career as naval officer, Kim was an active chief for <a href="/wiki/PT_boat" title="PT boat">PT boat</a> repair during the Korean War,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E9.A6.96.E7.88.BE.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E9.A6.96.E7.88.BE.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E-4">[4]</a></sup> and saw operations with different scale in places like offshore <a href="/wiki/Wonsan" title="Wonsan">Wonsan</a>, north of the <a href="/wiki/38th_parallel_north" title="38th parallel north">38th parallel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1953, he went to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for a six-month study, learning maintenance knowledge at a naval yard in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E9.A6.96.E7.88.BE.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E9.A6.96.E7.88.BE.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E-4">[4]</a></sup> He was also awarded the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Military_Merit_(South_Korea)" title="Order of Military Merit (South Korea)">Chungmu's Order of Military Merit with silver star</a> in 1953 for participating the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954, Kim Chong-kon was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(navy)" title="Lieutenant (navy)">Lieutenant</a> and a PT boat leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E9.A6.96.E7.88.BE.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-.E9.A6.96.E7.88.BE.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E-4">[4]</a></sup> During his years as <a href="/wiki/Field_officer" title="Field officer">field officer</a>, he assumed command of vessels like LSM-613 <i>Ulleung</i> (<span lang="ko">울릉</span>; from August 1, 1956 to November 7, 1957) and PCEC-53 <i>Hansan</i> (<span lang="ko">한산</span>; from July 5, 1962 to August 3, 1963),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup> later a professor at the Joint Forces Staff College (JFCS) in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.BA.BA.E5.90.8D.E8.BE.AD.E5.85.B898_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.BA.BA.E5.90.8D.E8.BE.AD.E5.85.B898-2">[2]</a></sup> On April 6, 1966, he became captain of the <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a> DD-91 <i>Chungmu</i> (<span lang="ko">충무</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup> and saw combat against a <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Spy_ship" title="Spy ship">spy ship</a> at night on October 10. Kim, commanding <i>Chungmu</i> on a security mission at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Sea" title="Japan Sea">Japan Sea</a>, encountered the North Korean vessel on territorial waters between the 38th parallel and <a href="/wiki/Ulleungdo" title="Ulleungdo">Ulleungdo</a>, and sank it with bombardment after a three-hour chase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.9D.B1.E4.BA.9E.E8.AB.9C.E8.88.B9_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.9D.B1.E4.BA.9E.E8.AB.9C.E8.88.B9-6">[6]</a></sup> For the victory of <i>Chungmu</i>, Kim received the Hwarang's Order of Military Merit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 31, 1967, Kim ended his tenure as the captain of <i>Chungmu</i>, and was assigned to a naval station (<span lang="ko">해군통제부</span>) as its chief of staff in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup> On 1970, he officially gained the rank of <a href="/wiki/Commodore_(rank)" title="Commodore (rank)">Rear Admiral lower half</a>, becoming commander of South Korean Navy's 2nd Flotilla. Other major assignments included the commander for naval security in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inchon" title="Inchon">Inchon</a> (1971), Deputy Chief of Staff for G3-Operations, commander of the naval station (1973), commander of the Korean Fleet (1975) and Vice Chief of Naval Operations (1977). On April 16, 1979, he was promoted to full Admiral and was appointed as the 12th Chief of Naval Operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.BA.AC.E9.84.89.E6.B5.B7.E5.8F.83.E7.B8.BD.E9.95.B7_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.BA.AC.E9.84.89.E6.B5.B7.E5.8F.83.E7.B8.BD.E9.95.B7-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1980 he also worked as a member of the Special Committee for National Security Measures (SCNSM), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_junta" title="Military junta">junta</a>-like committee establish by <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_of_December_Twelfth" title="Coup d'état of December Twelfth">coup d'état</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Chun_Doo-hwan" title="Chun Doo-hwan">Chun Doo-hwan</a> (who was seizing state power as then-<a href="/wiki/Defense_Security_Command" title="Defense Security Command">Defense Security Commander</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.95.8F.E9.A1.8C.E8.88.87.E7.A0.94.E7.A9.B6_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.95.8F.E9.A1.8C.E8.88.87.E7.A0.94.E7.A9.B6-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomatic_and_political_career">Diplomatic and political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Chong-kon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diplomatic and political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kim Chong-kon retired from the post as Chief of Naval Operations on May 14, 1981,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup> and was later appointed as the country's Ambassador to the ROC on July 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E7.B6.93.E7.B7.AF_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E7.B6.93.E7.B7.AF-10">[10]</a></sup> He arrived at <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a> on August 18 to assume his new office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> handing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Letter_of_credence" title="Letter of credence">Letter of credence</a> on August 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During his term as the foremost Korean representative to the ROC, Kim saw the hijacking of <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Administration_of_China" title="Civil Aviation Administration of China">CAAC</a> Flight 296, which led to a diplomatic dispute between Seoul and Taipei because the six hijackers defecting from <a href="/wiki/Shenyang" title="Shenyang">Shenyang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC) was in custody after landing on South Korean soil. Taipei, however, regarded them as anti-communist patriots, and ask the Koreans to release them to Taiwan. Kim took the role of communicator for this incident for nearly one year, passing the Taiwanese public opinion to Seoul, and suggest the latter to carry out administrative remedies as solution. The six was finally allowed to depart from Korea and headed for Taiwan in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.B1.9F.E5.81.89.E7.A2.A9_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.B1.9F.E5.81.89.E7.A2.A9-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 11, 1985, the South Korean government announced the replacement of its six ambassadors abroad, with Kim Chong-kon's post succeeded by Kim Sang-tae, a former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Korea" title="Republic of Korea">Air Force</a> Chief of Staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The ROC government awarded Kim Chong-kon with <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Brilliant_Star" title="Order of Brilliant Star">Order of Brilliant Star (2nd rank)</a> for his contribution in enhancing Korea-Taiwan relation and cooperation as ambassador on May 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> After returning to Korea, Kim remained as an <a href="/wiki/Ambassador-at-Large" title="Ambassador-at-Large">Ambassador-at-Large</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(South_Korea)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (South Korea)">Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a> until April 8, 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.B8.AD.E6.99.821986_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.B8.AD.E6.99.821986-16">[16]</a></sup> He later launched into politics and was elected in 1988 as a member of the 13th National Assembly from <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Justice_Party" title="Democratic Justice Party">Democratic Justice Party</a> (DJP), then ruling party of South Korea which merged with the Unification Democratic Party (UDP) and New Democratic Republican Party (RP) to form the new Democratic Liberal Party (later <a href="/wiki/New_Korea_Party" title="New Korea Party">New Korea Party</a>) in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.BA.BA.E5.90.8D.E8.BE.AD.E5.85.B898_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.BA.BA.E5.90.8D.E8.BE.AD.E5.85.B898-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_awards">Personal life and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Chong-kon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kim Chong-kon married Kim Sang-jo (<span lang="ko">김상조</span>), with whom he has three sons:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80-5">[5]</a></sup> Myung-joon (<span lang="ko">명준</span>), Hee-joon (<span lang="ko">희준</span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.B5.B7.E4.BA.8B.E7.B6.93.E6.BF.9F.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.B5.B7.E4.BA.8B.E7.B6.93.E6.BF.9F.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E-17">[17]</a></sup> and Seong-joon (<span lang="ko">성준</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.BD.99.E6.80.9D.E5.AE.99_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.BD.99.E6.80.9D.E5.AE.99-18">[18]</a></sup> The former two also joined the navy, graduated as Officer cadets 70th class and 71st class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.B5.B7.E4.BA.8B.E7.B6.93.E6.BF.9F.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.B5.B7.E4.BA.8B.E7.B6.93.E6.BF.9F.E6.96.B0.E8.81.9E-17">[17]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.B7.A8.E8.BB.80-5">[5]</a></sup> Kim's hobbies included reading classical novels/historical stories and playing <a href="/wiki/Ball_game" title="Ball game">ball games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E4.BD.99.E6.80.9D.E5.AE.99_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E4.BD.99.E6.80.9D.E5.AE.99-18">[18]</a></sup> After retirement, he joined an advisory council of reserve admirals (<span lang="ko">해군 예비역장성 자문회의</span>) hosted by Admiral An Pyong-tae, then-Chief of Naval Operations, in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.9D.B1.E4.BA.9E1996_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.9D.B1.E4.BA.9E1996-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1998 he was also elected as the 5th Vice President of the Korean Retired Generals and Admirals Association (<span lang="ko">성우회</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.98.9F.E5.8F.8B.E6.9C.83_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.98.9F.E5.8F.8B.E6.9C.83-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides medals like the Chungmu's Order of Military Merit with silver star in 1953, the Hwarang's Order of Military Merit in 1966 and the Taiwanese Order of Brilliant Star 2nd rank in 1985, Kim was also awarded the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_National_Security_Merit" title="Order of National Security Merit">Order of National Security Merit</a> 4th rank (1968), Cheon-Su Medal (1971), Tong-il Medal (1979) and the Eulji's Order of Military Merit (1980).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milkr_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-milkr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
