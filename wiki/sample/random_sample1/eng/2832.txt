<p><i><b>Kimchibuchimgae</b></i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">김치부침개</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[kim.tɕʰi.bu.tɕʰim.ɡɛ]</a></span>), sometimes referred to as <i><b>kimchijeon</b></i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">김치전</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[kim.tɕʰi.dʑʌn]</a></span>), is a variety of <i><a href="/wiki/Buchimgae" title="Buchimgae">buchimgae</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Buchimgae" title="Buchimgae">Korean pancake</a>. It is primarily made with sliced <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>, flour <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">batter</a> and sometimes other vegetables. However, <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> (ground pork) is also often added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Kimchi, spicy pickled vegetables seasoned with chili pepper and <a href="/wiki/Jeotgal" title="Jeotgal">jeotgal</a>, is a staple in <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>. The dish is good for using up ripened kimchi. Kimchibuchimgae is often recognized in Korean culture as a folk dish of low profile that anyone could make easily at home with no extra budget.</p>
<p>It is usually served as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appetizer" title="Appetizer">appetizer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snack" title="Snack">snack</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/Banchan" title="Banchan">banchan</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Side_dish" title="Side dish">side dish</a>).</p>
<p>When preparing kimchijeon, <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> from kimchi is often added, especially that of <i>baechu kimchi</i>, made from <a href="/wiki/Napa_cabbage" title="Napa cabbage">Napa cabbage</a>. The brine lends its red color to the batter but is not spicy itself. Along with kimchi, it is served as <a href="/wiki/Anju_(food)" title="Anju (food)">anju</a> with <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcoholic</a> beverages such as <i><a href="/wiki/Makgeolli" title="Makgeolli">makgeolli</a></i> or <i>dongdongju</i>.</p>
