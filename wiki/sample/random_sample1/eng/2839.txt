<p><b>Kim Han-gil</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">김한길</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani" xml:lang="ko-Hani">金한길</span>; born 17 September 1954) is a South Korean politician in the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(South_Korea)" title="People's Party (South Korea)">People's Party</a>, presently serving as a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(South_Korea)" title="National Assembly (South Korea)">National Assembly</a> for <a href="/wiki/Gwangjin_District" title="Gwangjin District">Gwangjin District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>. He was previously chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(South_Korea,_2011)" title="Democratic Party (South Korea, 2011)">Democratic Party</a> from 2013 to 2014, and of its successor the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Politics_Alliance_for_Democracy" title="New Politics Alliance for Democracy">New Politics Alliance for Democracy</a> from March to July 2014.</p>
<p>Having studied political science and journalism at <a href="/wiki/Konkuk_University" title="Konkuk University">Konkuk University</a>, Kim worked as a journalist in the United States, writing for the <i><a href="/wiki/Hankook_Ilbo" title="Hankook Ilbo">Hankook Ilbo</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/JoongAng_Ilbo" title="JoongAng Ilbo">JoongAng Ilbo</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoare_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoare-1">[1]</a></sup> He became active in the <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_for_New_Politics" title="National Congress for New Politics">National Congress for New Politics</a> in 1996, and remained a member of the main liberal party under its various names until 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoare_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-hoare-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KimLeaves_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-KimLeaves-2">[2]</a></sup> He served as <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Culture_(South_Korea)" title="Minister of Culture (South Korea)">Minister of Culture and Tourism</a> from 2000 to 2001, undertaking a highly publicized trip to North Korea on 10 March 2001, ostensibly to discuss tourism and culture arrangements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Subsequent to his trip, Kim announced an agreement to form a joint North–South team to compete in the <a href="/wiki/2001_World_Table_Tennis_Championships" title="2001 World Table Tennis Championships">2001 World Table Tennis Championships</a>, but North Korea rejected the arrangement later in March, citing "incomplete agreement and preparation problems".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kim became a member of the National Assembly in 1996, but lost his seat in 2008. He returned to the Assembly in the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_legislative_election,_2012" title="South Korean legislative election, 2012">2012 elections</a>, and became chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(South_Korea,_2011)" title="Democratic Party (South Korea, 2011)">Democratic Party</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> When the Democratic Party merged with <a href="/wiki/Ahn_Cheol-soo" title="Ahn Cheol-soo">Ahn Cheol-soo</a>'s New Political Vision Party to form the New Politics Alliance for Democracy in March 2014, Kim became co-chairman of the new party alongside Ahn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Kim and Ahn both resigned as co-chairmen three months later, however, following the party's disappointing performance in the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_by-elections,_2014" title="South Korean by-elections, 2014">2014 by-elections</a>, which had seen a conservative candidate win a constituency in <a href="/wiki/South_Jeolla_Province" title="South Jeolla Province">South Jeolla Province</a> for the first time in 26 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 January 2016, after Ahn had left the NPAD's successor, the Minjoo Party of Korea, Kim announced that he was leaving the Minjoo Party to join Ahn as a founding member of the new People's Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KimLeaves_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-KimLeaves-2">[2]</a></sup> In March, in the lead-up to the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_legislative_election,_2016" title="South Korean legislative election, 2016">2016 legislative elections</a>, Kim fell into dispute with Ahn over the need for an electoral alliance with the Minjoo Party. Kim resigned his position as co-chairman of the party's election campaign committee on March 11 over the dispute,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and with Ahn refusing to pursue such a pact and co-chairman <a href="/wiki/Chun_Jung-bae" title="Chun Jung-bae">Chun Jung-bae</a> backing down from the prospect, on March 17 he withdrew his candidacy for the upcoming elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Kim nonetheless welcomed the results of the election, which were positive for the People's Party, stating that the day for a <a href="/wiki/Regime_change" title="Regime change">regime change</a> was not far away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kim is known for his "acerbic" remarks: in 2007 he labelled English teachers in Korea "<a href="/wiki/White_trash" title="White trash">white trash</a>", and he criticized President <a href="/wiki/Park_Geun-hye" title="Park Geun-hye">Park Geun-hye</a> for giving speeches in foreign languages overseas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoare_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-hoare-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
