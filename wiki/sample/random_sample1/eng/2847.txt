<p>The <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">concept</a> of <b>flagship species</b> has its genesis in the field of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation biology</a>. The flagship species concept holds that by raising the profile of a particular species, it can successfully leverage more support for biodiversity conservation at large in a particular context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charisma_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Charisma-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several definitions have been advanced for the flagship species concept and for some time been there has been confusion even in the academic literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup> Most of the latest definitions focus on the strategic and socio-economical character of the concept, with a recent publication establishing a clear link with the marketing field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>“a species used as the focus of a broader conservation marketing campaign based on its possession of one or more traits that appeal to the target audience.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>species that have the ability to capture the imagination of the public and induce people to support conservation action and/or to donate funds</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>popular, charismatic species that serve as symbols and rallying points to stimulate conservation awareness and action</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The term flagship is linked to the metaphor of representation. In its popular usage, flagships are viewed as ambassadors or icons for a conservation project or movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Metaphor_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Metaphor-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, more recently, work in the field of microbiology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foissner_2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foissner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foissner_2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foissner_2006-7">[7]</a></sup> has started to use the concept of flagship species in a distinct way. This work relates to the biogeography of micro-organisms and uses particular species because "eyecatching “flagships” with conspicuous size and/or morphology are the best distribution indicators".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foissner_2005_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foissner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examples of flagship species include the <a href="/wiki/Bengal_tiger" title="Bengal tiger">Bengal tiger</a> (<i>Panthera tigris</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Giant_panda" title="Giant panda">giant panda</a> (<i>Ailuropoda melanoleuca</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Golden_lion_tamarin" title="Golden lion tamarin">Golden lion tamarin</a> (<i>Leontopithecus rosalia</i>), the <a href="/wiki/African_elephant" title="African elephant">African elephant</a> (<i>Loxodonta sp.</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Asian_elephant" title="Asian elephant">Asian elephant</a> (<i>Elephas maximus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Mutiny_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Mutiny-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flagship species can represent an environmental feature (e.g. a species or ecosystem), cause (e.g. climate change or ocean acidification), organization (e.g. NGO or government department) or geographic region (e.g. state or protected area).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Ambio_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Ambio-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The flagship species concept appears to have become popular around the mid 1980s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> within the debate on how to prioritise species for conservation. The first widely available references to use the flagship concept applied it to both neotropical primates<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and African elephants and rhinos,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> in the typical mammal centric approach that still dominates how the concept is used today<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Mutiny_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Mutiny-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Ambio_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Ambio-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leader-Williams_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leader-Williams-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of the concept has been largely dominated by large bodied species,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Seychelles_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Seychelles-15">[15]</a></sup> especially mammals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leader-Williams_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leader-Williams-14">[14]</a></sup> although species from other taxonomic groups have occasionally been used<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection">Selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flagship species can be selected according to many different characteristics depending on what is valued by the audience they try to target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Ambio_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Ambio-10">[10]</a></sup> This is best illustrated by the differences in recommendations made for flagship species selection targeting different target audiences such as local communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen-Jones_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen-Jones-17">[17]</a></sup> and tourists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Seychelles_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Seychelles-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several limitations have been recognized concerning the use of flagship species:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charisma_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Charisma-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The use of flagship species can skew the management and conservation priorities in their favour and to the detriment of more threatened species<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simberloff_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simberloff-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The managements of different flagships can conflict<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simberloff_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Simberloff-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The disappearance of the flagship can have negative impacts on the attitudes of the conservation stakeholders<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simberloff_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Simberloff-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flagships_and_conflict">Flagships and conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flagships and conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A major challenge for the deployment of several flagship species in non-Western contexts is that they may come into conflict with local communities, thereby jeopardizing well-intended conservation actions. This has been termed 'flagship mutiny' and is exemplified by the Asian elephant in countries where there is human-elephant conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barua_Mutiny_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barua_Mutiny-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_conservation_flagships">Other types of conservation flagships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagship_species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other types of conservation flagships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conservation flagships can also appear at broader levels, for example as ecosystems (such as coral reefs or rainforests or protected areas (Serengeti or Yellowstone).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup> A number of recent initiatives has developed new conservation flagships based on conservation values of particular areas or species. Examples of these are the EDGE project run by the <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_London" title="Zoological Society of London">Zoological Society of London</a> and the Hotspots run by <a href="/wiki/Conservation_International" title="Conservation International">Conservation International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ver.C3.ADssimo_Framework-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
