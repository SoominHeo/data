<p><b>Aluctoidea</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">superfamily</a> of <b>many-plumed and false plume moths</b>. These small <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a> are most easily recognized by their <a href="/wiki/Insect_wing" title="Insect wing">wings</a>. These each consist of many (typically more than 3) narrow strips of membrane around the major <a href="/wiki/Insect_wing#Veins" title="Insect wing">veins</a>, instead of a continuous sheet of membrane between the veins. In living moths in the wild, this is often hard to see however. When they are at rest, the "plumes" partly overlap, appearing as solid wings. But even then, they can be recognized by the wings having a marked lengthwise pattern and uneven edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>They contain two <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a> at most:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alucitidae" title="Alucitidae">Alucitidae</a> – many-plumed moths</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tineodidae" title="Tineodidae">Tineodidae</a> – false plume moths</li>
</ul>
<p>Sometimes, only one family is accepted, Tineodidae being merged into Alucitidae with the Alucitoidea thus becoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>. Most of the roughly 160 described <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the superfamily belong to the many-plumed moths; these include a few rather widespread <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genera" title="Genera">genera</a>. The false plume moths consist of numerous small and well-distinct lineages; none of their genera have managed to become as successful as the Alucitidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minet1991_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-minet1991-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alucitoidea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systematics and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Even though they are "<a href="/wiki/Microlepidoptera" title="Microlepidoptera">micromoths</a>", the Aluctoidea are not especially primitive <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a>; the sizable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenter_moth" title="Carpenter moth">carpenter moths</a> (Cossidae) as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butterflies" title="Butterflies">butterflies</a> are not particularly distant relatives. The closest living relatives of the Aluctoidea, however, seem to be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plume_moth" title="Plume moth">plume moths</a> (Pterophoroidea), which like the many-plumed moths have wings consisting each of several narrow straps (though less strikingly so than in the Aluctoidea). However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomic" title="Taxonomic">taxonomic</a> treatment of the many-plumed moths among the Ditrysia is disputed, mostly because of their unclear relationship to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruitworm_moth" title="Fruitworm moth">fruitworm moths</a> (Copromorphoidea).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the arrangement used here, the Copromorphoidea are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obtectomera" title="Obtectomera">obtectomeran</a> Ditrysia, significantly more advanced than the Aluctoidea (which certainly belong to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basal_(evolution)" title="Basal (evolution)">basal</a> lineages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoditrysia" title="Apoditrysia">Apoditrysia</a>). Some authors disagree and instead assume the Copromorphoidea to be closer relatives of the Alucitidae than even the Tineodidae and Pterophoroidea. This splits the many-plumed moths into two lineages, the Alucitidae (as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fringe-tufted_moth" title="Fringe-tufted moth">fringe-tufted moths</a>, Epermeniidae) being included in an expanded Copromorphoidea, and the Tineodidae affiliated with the plume moth instead. The subfamily Alucitoidea is thus abandoned in this approach. The rationale for doing so is the marked similarity of Alucitidae <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysalis" title="Chrysalis">chrysalises</a> to those of Copromorphoidea. But this may simply be <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symplesiomorph" title="Symplesiomorph">symplesiomorphies</a>, considering that the Copromorphoidea otherwise appear to possess the characteristic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synapomorph" title="Synapomorph">synapomorphies</a> of the Obtectomera, which are absent in Aluctoidea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minet1991_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-minet1991-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
