<p>The <b>magpie goose</b> (<i>Anseranas semipalmata</i>) is the sole living representative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterbird" title="Waterbird">waterbird</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Anseranatidae" title="Anseranatidae">Anseranatidae</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>. It is a resident breeder in northern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and a vagrant to southern <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. The species was once also widespread in southern Australia, but disappeared from there largely due to the drainage of the wetlands where the birds once bred.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magpie_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magpie geese are unmistakable birds with their black and white plumage and yellowish legs. The feet are only partially webbed, and the magpie goose feeds on vegetable matter in the water, as well as on land. Males are larger than females. Unlike true <a href="/wiki/Anserinae" title="Anserinae">geese</a>, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moult" title="Moult">moult</a> is gradual, so no flightless period results. Their voice is a loud honking.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_evolution">Systematics and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magpie_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systematics and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is placed in the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Anseriformes" title="Anseriformes">Anseriformes</a>, having the characteristic bill structure, but is considered to be distinct from the other species in this <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>. The related and extant families, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhimidae" title="Anhimidae">Anhimidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Screamer" title="Screamer">screamers</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">geese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a>), contain all the other taxa. The magpie goose is contained in the genus <i>Anseranas</i> and family Anseranatidae, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg"><img alt="Magpie Goose taking off.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1280" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg/220px-Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg/330px-Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg/440px-Magpie_Goose_taking_off.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>A cladistic study of the morphology of waterfowl found that the magpie goose was an early and distinctive offshoot, diverging after screamers and before all other ducks, geese, and swans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auk_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-auk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>This family is quite old, a <a href="/wiki/Living_fossil" title="Living fossil">living fossil</a>, having apparently diverged before the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a> — the relative <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegavis_iaai" title="Vegavis iaai">Vegavis iaai</a></i> lived some 68-67 million years ago. The <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record is limited, nonetheless. The enigmatic genus <i><a href="/wiki/Anatalavis" title="Anatalavis">Anatalavis</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hornerstown_Formation" title="Hornerstown Formation">Hornerstown</a> <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Paleocene" title="Early Paleocene">Early Paleocene</a> of New Jersey, USA - <a href="/wiki/London_Clay" title="London Clay">London Clay</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Eocene" title="Early Eocene">Early Eocene</a> of <a href="/wiki/Walton-on-the-Naze" title="Walton-on-the-Naze">Walton-on-the-Naze</a>, England) is sometimes considered to be the earliest known anseranatid. Other <a href="/wiki/Paleogene" title="Paleogene">Paleogene</a> birds sometimes considered magpie-geese are the genera <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Geranopsis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Geranopsis (page does not exist)">Geranopsis</a></i> from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hordwell_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hordwell Formation (page does not exist)">Hordwell Formation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Eocene" title="Late Eocene">Late Eocene</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Oligocene" title="Early Oligocene">Early Oligocene</a> of England and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anserpica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anserpica (page does not exist)">Anserpica</a></i> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Oligocene" title="Late Oligocene">Late Oligocene</a> of <a href="/wiki/Billy,_Allier" title="Billy, Allier">Billy</a>-<a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A9chy" title="Créchy">Créchy</a> (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hugueney_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hugueney-4">[4]</a></sup> The earliest known member of the group in Australia is an unnamed species represented by fossils found in the late Oligocene Carl Creek Limestone of Queensland. Additional fossils from North America and Europe suggest that the family was spread across the globe during the late Paleogene period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worthy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Worthy-5">[5]</a></sup> The Australian distribution of the living species ties in well with the presumed <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwanan</a> origin of <a href="/wiki/Anseriformes" title="Anseriformes">Anseriformes</a>, but Northern Hemisphere fossils are puzzling. Perhaps the magpie geese were one of the dominant groups of Paleogene waterfowl, only to become largely extinct later.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (June 2015)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_status">Ecology and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magpie_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg/220px-Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg/330px-Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg/440px-Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magpie_Geese_June09_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Magpie goose colony at <a href="/wiki/Serendip_Sanctuary" title="Serendip Sanctuary">Serendip Sanctuary</a></div>

<p>The magpie goose is found in a variety of open <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> areas such as <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>. It is fairly sedentary apart from some movement during the dry season. They are colonial breeders and are gregarious outside of the breeding season when they can form large and noisy flocks of up to a few thousand individuals. Its nest is on the ground, and a typical <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is 5-14 eggs. Some males mate with two females, all of which raise the young, unlike some other polygamous birds. This may be beneficial when predation of young is high as chicks raised by trios are more likely to survive.</p>
<p>This species is plentiful across its range, although this is significantly reduced in comparison to the range at time of European settlement. The range once extended as far south as the Coorong and the wetlands of the southeast of South Australia and Western Victoria. For Australia as a whole, it is not threatened and has a controlled hunting season when numbers are large. However, most of the southern populations were extirpated in the mid-20th century by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overhunting" title="Overhunting">overhunting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>. The species has been subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reintroduction" title="Reintroduction">reintroduction</a> projects such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bool_Lagoon" title="Bool Lagoon">Bool Lagoon</a> between Penola and Naracoorte. Populations in more northern areas have again reached a level where it can be regularly utilized by hunters, although not in the example provided. In <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, the magpie goose was listed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_threatened" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a> on the 2007 advisory list of threatened vertebrate fauna in Victoria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VDSE2007a_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-VDSE2007a-6">[6]</a></sup> In the December 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_and_Fauna_Guarantee_Act" title="Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act">Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act</a> list of threatened fauna, it is also listed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VDSE2007c_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-VDSE2007c-8">[8]</a></sup> As of early 2008, an Action Statement for the recovery and future management of this species had not been prepared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VDSE2007b_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-VDSE2007b-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
