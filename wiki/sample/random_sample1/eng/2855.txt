<p><i><b>Babe</b></i> is a 1995 Australian-American <a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">comedy-drama</a> film directed by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Noonan" title="Chris Noonan">Chris Noonan</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/George_Miller_(director)" title="George Miller (director)">George Miller</a>, and written by both. It is an adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Dick_King-Smith" title="Dick King-Smith">Dick King-Smith</a>'s 1983 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sheep-Pig" title="The Sheep-Pig">The Sheep-Pig</a></i>, also known as <i>Babe: The Gallant Pig</i> in the US, which tells the story of a <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">pig</a> who wants to be a <a href="/wiki/Herding_dog" title="Herding dog">sheepdog</a>. The main animal characters are played by a combination of real and <a href="/wiki/Audio-Animatronics" title="Audio-Animatronics">animatronic</a> pigs and <a href="/wiki/Border_Collie" title="Border Collie">Border Collies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After seven years of development,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Babe</i> was filmed in <a href="/wiki/Robertson,_New_South_Wales" title="Robertson, New South Wales">Robertson</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The talking-animal <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">visual effects</a> were done by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhythm_%26_Hues_Studios" title="Rhythm &amp; Hues Studios">Rhythm &amp; Hues Studios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Henson%27s_Creature_Shop" title="Jim Henson's Creature Shop">Jim Henson's Creature Shop</a>.</p>
<p>The film was a box office success and grossed $36,776,544 at the box office in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has received considerable acclaim from critics: it was nominated for seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a> including <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a> and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Adapted_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay">Best Adapted Screenplay</a>, winning <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Best Visual Effects</a>. It also won the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Motion_Picture_%E2%80%93_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Musical or Comedy">Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Musical or Comedy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Fantasy_Film" title="Saturn Award for Best Fantasy Film">Saturn Award for Best Fantasy Film</a>.</p>
<p>In 1998, Miller directed a <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Babe:_Pig_in_the_City" title="Babe: Pig in the City">Babe: Pig in the City</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span></h2>
<p>Babe, an orphaned piglet, is chosen for a "guess the weight" contest at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_fair" title="County fair">county fair</a>. The winning farmer, Arthur Hoggett, brings him home and allows him to stay with a <a href="/wiki/Border_Collie" title="Border Collie">Border Collie</a> named Fly, her mate Rex and their puppies, in the barn.</p>
<p>A duck named Ferdinand, who poses as a rooster to spare himself from being eaten, persuades Babe to help him destroy the alarm clock that threatens his mission. Despite succeeding in this, they wake Duchess, the Hoggetts' cat, and in the confusion accidentally destroy the living room. Rex sternly instructs Babe to stay away from Ferdinand (now a fugitive) and the house. Sometime later, when Fly's puppies are put up for sale, Babe asks if he can call her "Mom".</p>
<p>Christmas brings a visit from the Hoggetts' relatives. Babe is almost chosen for <a href="/wiki/Christmas_dinner" title="Christmas dinner">Christmas dinner</a> but a duck is picked instead after Hoggett remarks to his wife Esme that Babe may bring a prize for ham at the next county fair. On Christmas Day, Babe justifies his existence by alerting Hoggett to sheep rustlers stealing sheep from one of the fields. The next day, Hoggett sees Babe sort the hens, separating the brown from the white ones. Impressed, he takes him to the fields and allows him to try and herd the sheep. Encouraged by an elder ewe named Maa, the sheep cooperate, but Rex sees Babe's actions as an insult to sheepdogs and confronts Fly in a vicious fight for encouraging Babe. He injures her leg and accidentally bites Hoggett's hand when he tries to intervene. Rex is then chained to the dog house, muzzled and sedated, leaving the sheep herding job to Babe.</p>
<p>One morning, Babe is awakened by the sheep's cries and sees three dogs attacking them. Though he manages to scare them off, Maa is mortally injured and dies as a result. Hoggett arrives and, thinking that Babe killed her, prepares to shoot him. Fly is so anxious to find out whether he is guilty or innocent that, instead of barking orders at the sheep, she talks to them to find out what happened. Learning the truth, she barks to distract Hoggett, delaying him until Esme mentions that the police say <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feral_dogs" title="Feral dogs">feral dogs</a> have been killing sheep on neighboring farms and asks him why he has taken his shotgun out.</p>
<p>When Esme leaves on a trip, Hoggett signs Babe up for a local sheepherding competition. As it is raining the night before, Hoggett lets him and Fly into the house. However, Duchess scratches him when he tries to speak to her, so Hoggett immediately confines her outside. When she is let back in later, she gets revenge on Babe by revealing that humans eat pigs. Horrified, he runs out to the barn and learns from Fly that this is true. The next morning, Fly discovers that Babe has run away. She and Rex alert Hoggett and they all search for him. Rex finds him in a cemetery and Hoggett brings him home. However, he is still demoralized and refuses to eat. Hoggett gives him a drink from a baby bottle, sings to him "<a href="/wiki/If_I_Had_Words" title="If I Had Words">If I Had Words</a>" and dances a <a href="/wiki/Jig" title="Jig">jig</a> for him. This restores Babe’s faith in Hoggett's affection and he begins eating again.</p>
<p>At the competition, Babe meets the sheep that he will be herding, but they ignore his attempts to speak to them. As Hoggett is criticized by the bemused judges and ridiculed by the public for using a pig instead of a dog, Rex runs back to the farm to ask the sheep what to do. They give him a secret password, first extracting a promise that he will treat them better from now on. He returns in time to convey the password to Babe, and the sheep now follow his instructions flawlessly. Amid the crowd’s acclamation, he is unanimously given the highest score. While he sits down next to the farmer, Hoggett praises him by saying, "That'll do, Pig. That'll do."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<p>According to actor Cromwell, there was tension on the set between producer Miller and director Noonan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Noonan later complained, "I don't want to make a lifelong enemy of George Miller but I thought that he tried to take credit for <i>Babe</i>, tried to exclude me from any credit, and it made me very insecure... It was like your guru has told you that you are no good and that is really disconcerting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Miller shot back, “Chris said something that is defamatory: that I took his name off the credits on internet sites, which is just absolutely untrue. You know, I’m sorry but I really have a lot more to do with my life than worry about that... when it comes to <i>Babe</i>, the vision was handed to Chris on a plate.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">musical score</a> for <i>Babe</i> was composed by <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Westlake" title="Nigel Westlake">Nigel Westlake</a> and performed by the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Melbourne Symphony Orchestra">Melbourne Symphony Orchestra</a>. Classical orchestral music by 19th-century French composers is used throughout the film, but is disguised in a variety of ways and often integrated by Westlake into his score. The theme song "<a href="/wiki/If_I_Had_Words" title="If I Had Words">If I Had Words</a>" (lyrics by Jonathan Hodge), sung by Hoggett near the film's conclusion, is an adaptation of the <i>Maestoso</i> final movement of the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Saint-Sa%C3%ABns)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Saint-Saëns)"><i>Organ Symphony</i></a> by <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>, and was originally performed in 1977 by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Fitzgerald_(singer)" title="Scott Fitzgerald (singer)">Scott Fitzgerald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yvonne_Keeley" title="Yvonne Keeley">Yvonne Keeley</a>. This tune also recurs throughout the film's score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also brief quotations within the score from <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Grieg" title="Edvard Grieg">Edvard Grieg</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peer_Gynt_Suite" title="Peer Gynt Suite">Peer Gynt Suite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDU_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDU-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other music featured is by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_Delibes" title="Leo Delibes">Leo Delibes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rodgers" title="Richard Rodgers">Richard Rodgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9" title="Gabriel Fauré">Gabriel Fauré</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bizet" title="Georges Bizet">Georges Bizet</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span></h2>
<p><i>Babe</i> received widespread critical acclaim; it currently holds a 97% "Certified Fresh" approval rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was also a box office success, grossing $254,134,910 worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was nominated for seven <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It won the award for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Best Visual Effects</a>, defeating <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">Apollo 13</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> named <i>Babe</i> #80 on its list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Cheers" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Cheers">America's Most Inspiring Movies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cheers_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cheers-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of its subject being a piglet, <i>Babe</i> was initially banned from <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> in order to avoid upsetting or annoying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a>, who view pigs as <a href="/wiki/Haram" title="Haram">haram</a>, although the ruling was overturned almost a year later and the film was released <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">direct-to-video</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <i>Babe</i> was released in the USA, it is reported that "activists around the country staked out movie theatres with flyers documenting the real life abuses of pigs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The film had a marked effect on the growth of <a href="/wiki/Vegetarianism" title="Vegetarianism">vegetarianism</a>, particularly among the young. It also promoted a more sympathetic view of the intellectual, emotional and social capacities of animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> James Cromwell became an <a href="/wiki/Veganism#Philosophy:_ethical_and_environmental_perspectives" title="Veganism">ethical vegan</a> as a result of starring as Farmer Hoggett, saying, "I decided that to be able to talk about this [movie] with conviction, I needed to become a vegetarian."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1996 he went on to organize a vegetarian dinner for the Los Angeles homeless at a “Compassionate Christmas” event<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> in order to reverse the barnyard view that "Christmas is carnage".</p>
