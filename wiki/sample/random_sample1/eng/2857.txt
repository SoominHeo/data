<p><b>Pollen</b> is a fine to coarse powdery substance comprising pollen grains which are male <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">microgametophytes</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spermatophyta" title="Spermatophyta">seed plants</a>, which produce male <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gametes</a> (sperm cells). Pollen grains have a hard coat made of <a href="/wiki/Sporopollenin" title="Sporopollenin">sporopollenin</a> that protects the gametophytes during the process of their movement from the <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium" title="Gynoecium">pistil</a> of flowering plants or from the male <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">cone</a> to the female cone of <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">coniferous plants</a>. If pollen lands on a compatible pistil or female cone, it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germinate" title="Germinate">germinates</a>, producing a <a href="/wiki/Pollen_tube" title="Pollen tube">pollen tube</a> that transfers the <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovule</a> containing the female gametophyte. Individual pollen grains are small enough to require magnification to see detail. The study of pollen is called <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a> and is highly useful in paleoecology, <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">forensics</a>.</p>
<p>Pollen in plants is used for transferring haploid male genetic material from the <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">anther</a> of a single flower to the stigma of another in cross-pollination. In a case of self-pollination, this process takes place from the anther of a flower to the stigma of the same flower.</p>

<p>Pollen itself is not the male gamete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_and_practice_for_a_level_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-facts_and_practice_for_a_level-1">[1]</a></sup> Each pollen grain contains vegetative (non-reproductive) cells (only a single cell in most flowering plants but several in other seed plants) and a generative (reproductive) cell. In flowering plants the vegetative tube cell produces the <a href="/wiki/Pollen_tube" title="Pollen tube">pollen tube</a>, and the generative cell divides to form the two sperm cells.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pollen is produced in the 'microsporangium' (contained in the anther of an <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">angiosperm</a> <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flower</a>, male cone of a coniferous plant, or male cone of other seed plants). Pollen grains come in a wide variety of shapes (most often spherical), sizes, and surface markings characteristic of the species (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_micrograph" title="Electron micrograph">electron micrograph</a>, right). Pollen grains of <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">firs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruces</a> are winged. The smallest pollen grain, that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forget-me-not" title="Forget-me-not">forget-me-not</a> (<i>Myosotis</i> spp.), is around 6 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a> (0.006 mm) in diameter. Wind-borne pollen grains can be as large as about 90–100 µm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In angiosperms, during flower development the anther is composed of a mass of cells that appear undifferentiated, except for a partially differentiated dermis. As the flower develops, four groups of sporogenous cells form within the anther. The fertile sporogenous cells are surrounded by layers of sterile cells that grow into the wall of the pollen sac. Some of the cells grow into nutritive cells that supply nutrition for the microspores that form by meiotic division from the sporogenous cells.</p>
<p>In a process called <b>microsporogenesis</b>, four haploid <a href="/wiki/Microspore" title="Microspore">microspores</a> are produced from each diploid sporogenous cell (microsporocyte, pollen mother cell or <a href="/wiki/Meiocyte" title="Meiocyte">meiocyte</a>), after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiotic_division" title="Meiotic division">meiotic division</a>. After the formation of the four microspores, which are contained by callose walls, the development of the pollen grain walls begins. The callose wall is broken down by an enzyme called callase and the freed pollen grains grow in size and develop their characteristic shape and form a resistant outer wall called the exine and an inner wall called the intine. The exine is what is preserved in the fossil record. Two basic types of microsporogenesis are recognised, simultaneous and successive. In simultaneous microsporogenesis meiotic steps I and II are completed prior to <a href="/wiki/Cytokinesis" title="Cytokinesis">cytokinesis</a>, whereas in successive microsporogenesis cytokinesis follows. While there may be a continuum with intermediate forms, the type of microsporogenesis has systematic significance. The predominant form amongst the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocots" title="Monocots">monocots</a> is successive, but there are important exceptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furness2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Furness2001-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the microgametogenesis, the unicellular microspores undergoes mitosis and develops into mature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgametophyte" title="Microgametophyte">microgametophytes</a> containing the gametes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In some flowering plants, <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a> of the pollen grain often begins before it leaves the microsporangium, with the generative cell forming the two sperm cells.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Except in the case of some submerged aquatic plants, the mature pollen grain has a double wall. The vegetative and generative cells are surrounded by a thin delicate wall of unaltered <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> called the <b>endospore</b> or <b>intine</b>, and a tough resistant outer cuticularized wall composed largely of <a href="/wiki/Sporopollenin" title="Sporopollenin">sporopollenin</a> called the <b>exospore</b> or <b>exine</b>. The exine often bears spines or warts, or is variously sculptured, and the character of the markings is often of value for identifying genus, species, or even cultivar or individual. The spines may be less than a micron in length (spinulus, plural spinuli) referred to as <b>spinulose</b> (scabrate), or longer than a micron (echina, echinae) referred to as <b>echinate</b>. Various terms also describe the sculpturing such as <b>reticulate</b>, a net like appearance consisting of elements (murus, muri) separated from each other by a lumen (plural lumina).</p>
<p>The pollen wall protects the sperm while the pollen grain is moving from the anther to the stigma; it protects the vital genetic material from drying out and solar radiation. The pollen grain surface is covered with waxes and proteins, which are held in place by structures called sculpture elements on the surface of the grain. The outer pollen wall, which prevents the pollen grain from shrinking and crushing the genetic material during desiccation, is composed of two layers. These two layers are the tectum and the foot layer, which is just above the intine. The tectum and foot layer are separated by a region called the columella, which is composed of strengthening rods. The outer wall is constructed with a resistant biopolymer called sporopollenin.</p>
<p>Pollen apertures are regions of the pollen wall that may involve exine thinning or a significant reduction in exine thickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> They allow shrinking and swelling of the grain caused by changes in moisture content. Elongated apertures or furrows in the pollen grain are called colpi (singular: colpus) or sulci (singular: <a href="/wiki/Sulcus_(morphology)" title="Sulcus (morphology)">sulcus</a>). Apertures that are more circular are called pores. Colpi, sulci and pores are major features in the identification of classes of pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Pollen may be referred to as <b>inaperturate</b> (apertures absent) or <b>aperturate</b> (apertures present). The aperture may have a lid (operculum), hence is described as <b>operculate</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However the term inaperturate covers a wide range of morphological types, such as functionally inaperturate (cryptoaperturate) and omniaperturate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furness2001_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Furness2001-3">[3]</a></sup> Inaperaturate pollen grains often have thin walls, which facilitates pollen tube germination at any position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The orientation of furrows (relative to the original tetrad of microspores) classifies the pollen as <b>sulcate</b> or <b>colpate</b>. Sulcate pollen has a furrow across the middle of what was the outer face when the pollen grain was in its tetrad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spor72_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spor72-8">[8]</a></sup> If the pollen has only a single sulcus, it is described as <b>monosulcate</b>, has two sulci, as <b>bisulcate</b>, or more, as <b>polysulcate</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simp11_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simp11-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh1-10">[10]</a></sup> Colpate pollen has furrows other than across the middle of the outer faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spor72_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spor72-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eudicots" title="Eudicots">Eudicots</a> have pollen with three colpi (<b><a href="/wiki/Tricolpate" title="Tricolpate">tricolpate</a></b>) or with shapes that are evolutionarily derived from tricolpate pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The evolutionary trend in plants has been from monosulcate to polycolpate or polyporate pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spor72_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spor72-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The transfer of pollen grains to the female reproductive structure (<i><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpel" title="Carpel">pistil</a></b></i> in angiosperms) is called <b><a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a></b>. This transfer can be mediated by the wind, in which case the plant is described as <b><a href="/wiki/Anemophily" title="Anemophily">anemophilous</a></b> (literally wind-loving). Anemophilous plants typically produce great quantities of very lightweight pollen grains, sometimes with air-sacs. Non-flowering seed plants (e.g. pine trees) are characteristically anemophilous. Anemophilous flowering plants generally have inconspicuous flowers. <b><a href="/wiki/Entomophily" title="Entomophily">Entomophilous</a></b> (literally insect-loving) plants produce pollen that is relatively heavy, sticky and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>-rich, for dispersal by <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinators</a> attracted to their flowers. Many insects and some <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a> are specialized to feed on pollen, and are called <a href="/wiki/Palynivore" title="Palynivore">palynivores</a>.</p>
<p>In non-flowering seed plants, pollen germinates in the pollen chamber, located beneath the <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">micropyle</a>, underneath the integuments of the ovule. A pollen tube is produced, which grows into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucellus" title="Nucellus">nucellus</a> to provide nutrients for the developing sperm cells. Sperm cells of <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">Pinophyta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gnetophyta" title="Gnetophyta">Gnetophyta</a> are without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flagella" title="Flagella">flagella</a>, and are carried by the pollen tube, while those of <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">Cycadophyta</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ginkgophyta" title="Ginkgophyta">Ginkgophyta</a> have many flagella.</p>
<p>When placed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpel" title="Carpel">stigma</a> of a flowering plant, under favorable circumstances, a pollen grain puts forth a pollen tube, which grows down the tissue of the style to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovary_(plants)" title="Ovary (plants)">ovary</a>, and makes its way along the <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">placenta</a>, guided by projections or hairs, to the micropyle of an <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovule</a>. The nucleus of the tube cell has meanwhile passed into the tube, as does also the generative nucleus, which divides (if it hasn't already) to form two sperm cells. The sperm cells are carried to their destination in the tip of the pollen tube.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollen_in_the_fossil_record">Pollen in the fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pollen in the fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Palynology</a></div>
<p>Pollen's sporopollenin outer sheath affords it some resistance to the rigours of the fossilisation process that destroy weaker objects; it is also produced in huge quantities. There is an extensive fossil record of pollen grains, often disassociated from their parent plant. The discipline of palynology is devoted to the study of pollen, which can be used both for biostratigraphy and to gain information about the abundance and variety of plants alive — which can itself yield important information about paleoclimates. Pollen is first found in the fossil record in the late <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> period<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (July 2007)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and increases in abundance until the present day.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Allergic_rhinitis" title="Allergic rhinitis">Nasal allergy</a> to pollen is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollinosis" title="Pollinosis">pollinosis</a>, and allergy specifically to grass pollen is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hay_fever" title="Hay fever">hay fever</a>. Generally, pollens that cause allergies are those of anemophilous plants (pollen is dispersed by air currents.) Such plants produce large quantities of lightweight pollen (because wind dispersal is random and the likelihood of one pollen grain landing on another flower is small), which can be carried for great distances and are easily inhaled, bringing it into contact with the sensitive nasal passages.</p>
<p>In the US, people often mistakenly blame the conspicuous <a href="/wiki/Goldenrod" title="Goldenrod">goldenrod</a> flower for allergies. Since this plant is entomophilous (its pollen is dispersed by animals), its heavy, sticky pollen does not become independently airborne. Most late summer and fall pollen allergies are probably caused by <a href="/wiki/Ragweed" title="Ragweed">ragweed</a>, a widespread anemophilous plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> was once regarded as a haven for people with pollen allergies, although several ragweed species grow in the desert. However, as suburbs grew and people began establishing <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigated lawns and gardens</a>, more irritating species of ragweed gained a foothold and Arizona lost its claim of freedom from hay fever.</p>
<p>Anemophilous spring blooming plants such as <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hickory" title="Hickory">hickory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pecan" title="Pecan">pecan</a>, and early summer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grasses</a> may also induce pollen allergies. Most cultivated plants with showy flowers are entomophilous and do not cause pollen allergies.</p>
<p>The number of people in the United States affected by hay fever is between 20 and 40 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and such allergy has proven to be the most frequent <a href="/wiki/Allergic_response" title="Allergic response">allergic response</a> in the nation. There are certain evidential suggestions pointing out hay fever and similar allergies to be of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary_disease" title="Hereditary disease">hereditary origin</a>. Individuals who suffer from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema">eczema</a> or are <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthmatic</a> tend to be more susceptible to developing long-term hay fever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, decades of rising temperatures cause pollen to appear earlier and in greater numbers, as well as introduction of new species such as ragweed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most efficient way to handle a pollen allergy is by preventing contact with the material. Individuals carrying the ailment may at first believe that they have a simple summer cold, but hay fever becomes more evident when the apparent cold does not disappear. The confirmation of hay fever can be obtained after examination by a <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general physician</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Allergic_rhinitis#treatment" title="Allergic rhinitis">Allergic rhinitis § treatment</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihistamines" title="Antihistamines">Antihistamines</a> are effective at treating mild cases of pollinosis, this type of non-prescribed drugs includes <a href="/wiki/Loratadine" title="Loratadine">loratadine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cetirizine" title="Cetirizine">cetirizine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorphenamine" title="Chlorphenamine">chlorphenamine</a>. They do not prevent the discharge of <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a>, but it has been proven that they do prevent a part of the chain reaction activated by this <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_amine" title="Biogenic amine">biogenic amine</a>, which considerably lowers hay fever symptoms.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decongestants" title="Decongestants">Decongestants</a> can be administered in different ways such as tablets and <a href="/wiki/Nasal_spray" title="Nasal spray">nasal sprays</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allergen_immunotherapy" title="Allergen immunotherapy">Allergy immunotherapy</a> (AIT) treatment involves administering doses of allergens to accustom the body to pollen, thereby inducing specific long-term tolerance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Allergy immunotherapy can be administered orally (as sublingual tablets or sublingual drops), or by injections under the skin (subcutaneous). Discovered by Leonard Noon and John Freeman in 1911, allergy immunotherapy represents the only causative treatment for respiratory allergies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most major classes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predatory" title="Predatory">predatory</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasitic" title="Parasitic">parasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> contain species that eat pollen, despite the common perception that <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a> are the primary pollen-consuming arthropod group. Many other <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> other than bees consume pollen as adults, though only a small number feed on pollen as <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> (including some <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> larvae). <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">Spiders</a> are normally considered <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a> but pollen is an important source of food for several species, particularly for <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiderlings</a>, which catch pollen on their <a href="/wiki/Spider_web" title="Spider web">webs</a>. It is not clear how spiderlings manage to eat pollen however, since their mouths are not large enough to consume pollen grains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Acari" title="Acari">predatory mites</a> also feed on pollen, with some species being able to subsist solely on pollen, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euseius_tularensis" title="Euseius tularensis">Euseius tularensis</a></i>, which feeds on the pollen of dozens of plant species. Members of some beetle families such as <a href="/wiki/Mordellidae" title="Mordellidae">Mordellidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melyridae" title="Melyridae">Melyridae</a> feed almost exclusively on pollen as adults, while various lineages within larger families such as <a href="/wiki/Curculionidae" title="Curculionidae">Curculionidae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysomelidae" title="Chrysomelidae">Chrysomelidae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerambycidae" title="Cerambycidae">Cerambycidae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scarabaeidae" title="Scarabaeidae">Scarabaeidae</a> are pollen specialists even though most members of their families are not (e.g., only 36 of 40000 species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carabidae" title="Carabidae">ground beetles</a>, which are typically predatory, have been shown to eat pollen—but this is thought to be a severe underestimate as the feeding habits are only known for 1000 species). Similarly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladybird" title="Ladybird">Ladybird</a> beetles mainly eat insects, but many species also eat pollen, as either part or all of their diet. <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">Hemiptera</a> are mostly <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> or <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a> but pollen feeding is known (and has only been well studied in the <a href="/wiki/Anthocoridae" title="Anthocoridae">Anthocoridae</a>). Many adult flies, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syrphidae" title="Syrphidae">Syrphidae</a>, feed on pollen, and three UK syrphid species feed strictly on pollen (syrphids, like all <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, cannot eat pollen directly due to the structure of their mouthparts, but can consume pollen contents that are dissolved in a fluid).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Some species of fungus, including <i><a href="/wiki/Fomes_fomentarius" title="Fomes fomentarius">Fomes fomentarius</a></i>, are able to break down grains of pollen as a secondary nutrition source that is particularly high in nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Pollen may be valuable diet supplement for <a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">detritivores</a>, providing them with nutrients needed for growth, development and maturation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">[20]</a></sup> It was suggested that obtaining nutrients from pollen, deposited on the forest floor during periods of pollen rains, allows fungi to decompose nutritionally scarce litter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some species of <i><a href="/wiki/Heliconius" title="Heliconius">Heliconius</a></i> butterflies consume pollen as adults, which appears to be a valuable nutrient source, and these species are more distasteful to predators than the non-pollen consuming species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hummingbird" title="Hummingbird">hummingbirds</a> are not pollen eaters <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_se_(terminology)" title="Per se (terminology)">per se</a></i>, their consumption of <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectar</a> in flowers is an important aspect of the <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> process.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_humans">In humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A variety of producers have started selling <a href="/wiki/Bee_pollen" title="Bee pollen">bee pollen</a> for human consumption, often marketed as a <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> (rather than a <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">dietary supplement</a>). The largest constituent is <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, with protein content ranging from 7 to 35 percent depending on the plant species collected by bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">Honey</a> produced by bees from natural sources contains pollen derived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-coumaric_acid" title="P-coumaric acid">p-coumaric acid</a>, an antioxidant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berenbaum_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berenbaum-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) has not found any harmful effects of bee pollen consumption, except from the usual allergies. However, FDA does not allow bee pollen marketers in the United States to make health claims about their produce, as no scientific basis for these has ever been proven. Furthermore, there are possible dangers not only from allergic reactions but also from contaminants such as pesticides and from fungi and bacteria growth related to poor storage procedures. A manufacturers's claim that pollen collecting helps the bee colonies is also controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pine pollen (<span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">송화가루</span>; <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Songhwa Garu</span></i>) is traditionally consumed in Korea as an ingredient in sweets and beverages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The growing industries in pollen harvesting for human and bee consumption rely on harvesting pollen baskets from honey bees as they return to their hives using a <i>pollen trap</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> When this pollen has been tested for parasites, it has been found that a multitude of pollinator viruses and eukaryotic parasites are present in the pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graystock2013_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graystock2013-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh2010_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh2010-28">[28]</a></sup> It is currently unclear if the parasites are introduced by the bee that collected the pollen or if it is from contamination to the flower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh2010_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singh2010-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ParaBloom_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ParaBloom-29">[29]</a></sup> Though this is not likely to pose a risk to humans, it is a major issue for the bumblebee rearing industry that relies on thousands of tonnes of honey bee collected pollen per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Several sterilization methods have been employed, though no method has been 100% effective at steralizing, without reducing the nutritional value, of the pollen <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steralization_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-steralization-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forensic_palynology">Forensic palynology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Forensic palynology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Forensic_palynology" title="Forensic palynology">Forensic palynology</a></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Forensic_biology" title="Forensic biology">forensic biology</a>, pollen can tell a lot about where a person or object has been, because regions of the world, or even more particular locations such a certain set of bushes, will have a distinctive collection of pollen species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bryant_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-bryant-32">[32]</a></sup> Pollen evidence can also reveal the season in which a particular object picked up the pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Pollen has been used to trace activity at mass graves in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnia" title="Bosnia">Bosnia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> catch a burglar who brushed against a <i><a href="/wiki/Hypericum" title="Hypericum">Hypericum</a></i> bush during a crime,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and has even been proposed as an additive for bullets to enable tracking them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
