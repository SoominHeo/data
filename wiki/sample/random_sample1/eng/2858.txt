<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Flower bouquet</h1>

<p>A <b>flower bouquet</b> is a collection of <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> in a creative arrangement. Flower bouquets can be arranged for the decor of homes or public buildings, or may be handheld. Handheld bouquets are classified by several different popular shapes and styles, including <a href="/wiki/Nosegay" title="Nosegay">nosegay</a>, crescent, and cascading bouquets. Flower bouquets are often given for special occasions such as <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">birthdays</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anniversary" title="Anniversary">anniversaries</a>. They are also used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Wedding" title="Wedding">weddings</a>. Bouquets arranged in vases or planters for home decor can be arranged in either traditional or modern styles. Symbolism may be attached to the types of flowers used, according to the culture.</p>

<p>The arrangement of flowers for home or building decor has a long history throughout the world. The oldest evidence of formal arranging of bouquets in vases comes from Egypt, and depictions of flower arrangements date back to 2500 BCE. The sacred lotus was often used, as were herbs, palms, irises, anemones, and narcissus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belcher_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belcher-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some cultures, ancient practises still survive today, for example in <a href="/wiki/Ikebana" title="Ikebana">ikebana</a>, the art of flower-arranging that comes from Japan. The oldest known book on flower-arranging is Japanese and dates from 1445. Simplicity and linear form are core features of ikebana, which has had a great influence on Western flower arranging since the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belcher_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Belcher-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flower-arranging as an art form was brought to Japan by Buddhist monks, who learned it while in China. In ancient China, flower-arranging developed into a highly refined art form, based on the principle that life is sacred, including the life of plants, therefore cut flowers were used sparingly in carefully planned arrangements. Flowers were a traditional ritual offering among Buddhists, however, and remain so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belcher_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Belcher-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Europe, flower arranging as a formal art was first documented among the Dutch, who "in particular, painted wonderful informal arrangements of flowers [...] In the 18th century, arrangements were used to decorate the houses of the wealthy families and the aristocracy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flower symbolism is common in many cultures, and can be complex. In China, certain flowers symbolize seasons: white plum blossoms represent winter, peach and cherry blossoms represent spring, lotus represents summer, and chrysanthemums the fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belcher_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Belcher-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nosegay">Nosegay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flower_bouquet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nosegay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Nosegay" title="Nosegay">Nosegay</a></div>
<p>The term "tussie-mussie" is sometimes used interchangeably with <a href="/wiki/Nosegay" title="Nosegay">nosegay</a>. A nosegay was also known as a "talking bouquet" or "flower poesy" during the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, when they became a popular gift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Bride" title="Bride">brides</a> will also carry a small nosegay. Tussie mussies were introduced to England in the early 18th century, and were a fashionable accessory for young women by the early 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A tussie mussie is a small circular bouquet like a nosegay, but carries symbolic meaning based upon the <a href="/wiki/Language_of_flowers" title="Language of flowers">language of flowers</a>, where particular flowers represent specific sentiments. They were commonly exchanged by lovers, who sent messages to one another based upon the flowers used in the bouquet. Traditionally, tussie mussies are arranged in a cone- or cornucopia-shaped container, made of tin or silver, with a chain attached for carrying the bouquet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_of_flowers">Language of flowers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flower_bouquet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language of flowers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Language_of_flowers" title="Language of flowers">Language of flowers</a></div>
<p>Flower symbolism originated in Asia and the Middle East, where certain flowers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nelumbo_nucifera" title="Nelumbo nucifera">lotus</a>, were considered sacred, or at least to be associated with spiritual themes. This was often reflected in artwork, for example the use of <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> in Chinese art to represent longivity and eternity. The <a href="/wiki/Language_of_flowers" title="Language of flowers">language of flowers</a> was introduced to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in the early 18th century by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Wortley" title="Mary Wortley">Mary Wortley</a>, Lady Montague, whose husband was Ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. By the Victorian era, almost every flower had a specific meaning attached to it. Small <a href="/wiki/Nosegay" title="Nosegay">nosegay</a> or "tussie mussie" bouquets might include <a href="/wiki/Chamomile" title="Chamomile">chamomile</a> flowers, which a woman might send to a romantic interest to tell him "Patience"; <a href="/wiki/Goldenrod" title="Goldenrod">goldenrod</a> represented indecision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wedding_bouquets">Wedding bouquets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flower_bouquet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wedding bouquets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally the <a href="/wiki/Bride" title="Bride">bride</a> will hold the bouquet, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maid_of_honor" title="Maid of honor">maid of honor</a> will hold it during the ceremony. After the wedding the bride will toss it over her shoulder, and it is believed that whoever catches the bouquet is the next in line to be married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This practice may be related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Apple_of_Discord" title="Golden Apple of Discord">Golden Apple of Discord</a> myth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
