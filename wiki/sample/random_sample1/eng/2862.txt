<p><i><b>Physalis alkekengi</b></i> (<b>bladder cherry</b>, <b>Chinese lantern</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>Japanese-lantern</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBI07_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBI07-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>strawberry groundcherry</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or <b>winter cherry</b>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-2">[2]</a></sup>) is a relative of <i><a href="/wiki/Physalis_peruviana" title="Physalis peruviana">P. peruviana</a></i> (Cape gooseberry). It is easily identifiable by the large, bright orange to red papery covering over its fruit, which resembles <a href="/wiki/Paper_lantern" title="Paper lantern">paper lanterns</a>. It grows naturally in the regions covering <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a> to <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plant</a> growing to 40â60Â cm tall, with spirally arranged <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> 6â12Â cm long and 4â9Â cm broad. The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are white, with a five-lobed corolla 10â15Â mm across, with an inflated basal calyx which matures into the papery orange fruit covering, 4â5Â cm long and broad.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physalis_alkekengi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg/220px-Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg/330px-Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg/440px-Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Physalis_alkekengi_Dried_and_Plucked_on_a_plastic_background.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Physalis alkekengi</i>, or the Chinese lantern, dries during spring. Once it is dried, the bright red fruit is seen.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a popular <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a>, though it can be <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a> with its wide-spreading <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root</a> system sending up new shoots some distance from where it was originally planted. In various places around the world, it has escaped cultivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-china_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-china-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_uses">Traditional uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physalis_alkekengi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traditional uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dried fruit of <i>P. alkekengi</i> is called the golden flower in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unani" title="Unani">Unani</a> system of medicine, and used as a diuretic, antiseptic, liver corrective, and sedative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_constituents">Chemical constituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physalis_alkekengi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical constituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like a number of other species in the genus <i>Physalis</i>, it contains a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Physalin" title="Physalin">physalins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When isolated from the plant, these have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibacterial</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Leishmaniasis" title="Leishmaniasis">leishmanicidal</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> activities <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i>.</p>
<p>It also contains <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_caffeate" title="Ethyl caffeate">caffeic acid ethyl ester</a>, 25,27-dehydro-physalin L, physalin D, and cuneataside E.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physalis_alkekengi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2136" data-file-width="3216" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg/220px-Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg/330px-Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg/440px-Sensouji_hoozukiichi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In Japan, its seeds are used as part of the <a href="/wiki/Bon_Festival" title="Bon Festival">Bon Festival</a> as offerings to guide the souls of the deceased. Also, an annual market is dedicated to the flower called <i>hÅzuki-ichi</i>, which occurs in <a href="/wiki/Asakusa" title="Asakusa">Asakusa</a> around <a href="/wiki/Sens%C5%8D-ji" title="SensÅ-ji">SensÅ-ji</a> every year on July 9 and 10.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physalis_alkekengi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
