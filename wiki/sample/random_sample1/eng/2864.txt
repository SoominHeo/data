<p>The <b>Phasianidae</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of heavy, groundliving <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> which includes <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Junglefowl" title="Junglefowl">junglefowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_World_quail" title="Old World quail">Old World quail</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peafowl" title="Peafowl">peafowl</a>. The family includes many of the most popular <a href="/wiki/Game_(hunting)" title="Game (hunting)">gamebirds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGowan1994_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGowan1994-1">[1]</a></sup> The family is a large one, and is occasionally broken up into two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subfamily_(biology)" title="Subfamily (biology)">subfamilies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Phasianinae" title="Phasianinae">Phasianinae</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Perdicinae" title="Perdicinae">Perdicinae</a>. Sometimes, additional families and birds are treated as part of this family. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="American Ornithologists' Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a> includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraonidae" title="Tetraonidae">Tetraonidae</a> (grouse), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numididae" title="Numididae">Numididae</a> (guineafowl), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meleagrididae" title="Meleagrididae">Meleagrididae</a> (turkeys) as subfamilies in Phasianidae.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_evolution">Systematics and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phasianidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systematics and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Galliformes" title="List of Galliformes">List of Galliformes</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> Phasianidae is the largest of the branch <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">Galliformes</a>, comprising more than 150 species. This group includes the pheasants and partridges, junglefowl chickens, quail and peafowl. Turkeys and grouse have also been recognized as having their origins in the pheasant- and partridge-like birds.</p>
<p>Until the early 1990s, this family was broken up into two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subfamily_(biology)" title="Subfamily (biology)">subfamilies</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Phasianinae" title="Phasianinae">Phasianinae</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tragopan" title="Tragopan">tragopans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Junglefowl" title="Junglefowl">junglefowls</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peafowl" title="Peafowl">peafowls</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnsgard1986_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard1986-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Perdicinae" title="Perdicinae">Perdicinae</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_World_quail" title="Old World quail">Old World quails</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Francolin" title="Francolin">francolins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnsgard1988_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard1988-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics" title="Molecular phylogenetics">Molecular phylogenies</a> have shown that these two subfamilies are not each <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a> but actually constitute only one lineage with one common ancestor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimball1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kimball1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimball2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kimball2014-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, some <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a> (genus <i><a href="/wiki/Perdix" title="Perdix">Perdix</a></i>) are more closely affiliated to <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasants</a>, whereas <a href="/wiki/Old_World_quail" title="Old World quail">Old World quails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a> from the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Alectoris" title="Alectoris">Alectoris</a></i> are closer to <a href="/wiki/Junglefowl" title="Junglefowl">junglefowls</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimball1999_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kimball1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimball2014_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kimball2014-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest fossil records of phasianids date to the late Oligocene epoch, about 30 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayr2006_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr2006-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phasianidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phasianids are terrestrial. They range in weight from 43 g (1.5 oz) in the case of the <a href="/wiki/King_quail" title="King quail">king quail</a> to 6 kg (13 lb) in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_peafowl" title="Indian peafowl">Indian peafowl</a>. If turkeys are included, rather than classified as a separate family, then the considerably heavier <a href="/wiki/Wild_turkey" title="Wild turkey">wild turkey</a> reaches a maximum weight of more than 17 kg (37 lb). Length in this taxonomic family can vary from 12.5 cm (4.9 in) in the king quail up to 300 cm (120 in) (including elongated tail streamers) in <a href="/wiki/Green_peafowl" title="Green peafowl">green peafowl</a>, thus they beat even the <a href="/wiki/True_parrot" title="True parrot">true parrots</a> in length diversity within a family of birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGowan1994_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-McGowan1994-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There is generally <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in size, with males tending to be larger than females. They are generally plump, with broad relatively short <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> and strong legs. Many have a spur on their <a href="/wiki/Leg" title="Leg">legs</a>, a feature shared only with guineafowl and turkeys. The bill is short and generally strong, particularly in species that dig for food. Males of the larger <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> often have brightly coloured <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> as well as facial ornaments such as <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crest_(feathers)" title="Crest (feathers)">crests</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phasianidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phasianidae is mostly an Old World family, with a distribution that includes most of Europe and Asia (except the far north), all of Africa except the driest deserts and down into much of eastern Australia and (formerly) New Zealand. <a href="/wiki/Meleagridinae" title="Meleagridinae">Meleagridinae</a> (turkeys) are native to the New World, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraoninae" title="Tetraoninae">Tetraoninae</a> (grouse) are circumpolar. The greatest diversity of species is in Southeast Asia and Africa. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo_peacock" title="Congo peacock">Congo peacock</a> is specific to the African Congo. The subfamily <a href="/wiki/Perdicinae" title="Perdicinae">Perdicinae</a> has a much more widespread distribution. Within their range they occupy almost every available habitat except for the boreal forests and tundra.</p>
<p>The family is generally <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sedentary" title="wikt:sedentary">sedentary</a> and resident, although some quails undertake long <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrations</a>. Several species in the family have been widely <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> around the world, particularly pheasants which have been introduced to Europe, Australia and the Americas, specifically for hunting purposes. Captive populations of peacocks and chickens have also escaped (or been released) and become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phasianidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pheasants and partridges have a varied diet, with foods taken ranging from purely vegetarian diets of seeds, leaves, fruits, tubers and roots, to small animals including insects, insect grubs and even small reptiles. Most species either specialise in feeding on plant matter or are predatory, although the chicks of most species are insectivorous.</p>
<p>In addition to the variation in diet there is a considerable amount of variation in breeding strategies among the Phasianidae. Compared to birds in general there is a large number of species that do not engage in <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamy</a> (the typical breeding system of most birds). The francolins of Africa and some partridges are reportedly monogamous, but polygamy has been reported in the pheasants and junglefowl, some quail, and the breeding displays of peacocks have been compared to those of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lek_(biology)" title="Lek (biology)">lek</a>. Nesting usually occurs on the ground; only the <a href="/wiki/Tragopan" title="Tragopan">tragopans</a> nest higher up in stumps of bushes. Nests can vary from mounds of vegetation to slight <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">scrapes</a> in the ground. As many as 18 eggs can be laid in the nest, although 7-12 is the more usual number, with smaller numbers in tropical species. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation">Incubation</a> is almost always performed by the female only, and lasts from 14–30 days depending on the species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phasianidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several species of pheasant and partridge are extremely important to humans. The <a href="/wiki/Red_junglefowl" title="Red junglefowl">red junglefowl</a> of Southeast Asia is the wild ancestor of the domesticated <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a>, the most important bird in agriculture. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring-necked_pheasant" title="Ring-necked pheasant">Ring-necked pheasants</a>, several partridge and quail species and some francolins have been widely introduced and managed as game birds for hunting. Several species are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threatened" title="Threatened">threatened</a> by human activities.</p>
