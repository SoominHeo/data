<p><i><b>Drosera</b></i>, commonly known as the <b>sundews</b>, is one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous_plant" title="Carnivorous plant">carnivorous plants</a>, with at least 194 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-habitats_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-habitats-1">[1]</a></sup> These members of the family <a href="/wiki/Droseraceae" title="Droseraceae">Droseraceae</a> lure, capture, and digest insects using stalked <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilaginous</a> glands covering their leaf surfaces. The insects are used to supplement the poor mineral nutrition of the soil in which the plants grow. Various species, which vary greatly in size and form, are native to every continent except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McPherson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-McPherson-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both the botanical name (from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> δρόσος: <i>drosos</i> = "dew, dewdrops") and the English common name (sundew, derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ros solis</i>, meaning "dew of the sun") refer to the glistening drops of <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a> at the tip of each <a href="/wiki/Tentacle" title="Tentacle">tentacle</a> that resemble drops of morning <a href="/wiki/Dew" title="Dew">dew</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DroseraZonariaTuber2-.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="287" data-file-width="230" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/DroseraZonariaTuber2-.jpg/220px-DroseraZonariaTuber2-.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/DroseraZonariaTuber2-.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DroseraZonariaTuber2-.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A tuber of <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_zonaria" title="Drosera zonaria">D. zonaria</a></i>, a tuberous sundew, beginning its winter growth</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sundews are <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> (or rarely <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual</a>) <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plants</a>, forming prostrate or upright rosettes between 1 and 100 cm (0.39 and 39.37 in) in height, depending on the species. Climbing species form scrambling stems which can reach much longer lengths, up to 3 m (9.8 ft) in the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_erythrogyne" title="Drosera erythrogyne">D. erythrogyne</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Sundews have been shown to be able to achieve a lifespan of 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The genus is so specialized for <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> uptake through its carnivorous behavior, the pygmy sundew is missing the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nitrate_reductase" title="Nitrate reductase">nitrate reductase</a>, in particular)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that plants normally use for the uptake of earth-bound nitrates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habit">Habit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The genus can be divided into several <a href="/wiki/Habit_(biology)" title="Habit (biology)">habits</a>, or growth forms:</p>
<ul>
<li>Temperate sundews: These species form a tight cluster of unfurled leaves called a <a href="/wiki/Hibernaculum_(botany)" title="Hibernaculum (botany)">hibernaculum</a> in a winter dormancy period (= <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemicryptophyte" title="Hemicryptophyte">Hemicryptophyte</a>). All of the North American and European species belong to this group. <i>Drosera arcturi</i> from Australia (including Tasmania) and New Zealand is another temperate species that dies back to a horn-shaped hibernaculum.</li>
<li>Subtropical sundews: These species maintain vegetative growth year-round under uniform or nearly uniform climatic conditions.</li>
<li>Pygmy sundews: A group of roughly 40 Australian species, they are distinguished by miniature growth, the formation of <a href="/wiki/Gemma_(botany)" title="Gemma (botany)">gemmae</a> for <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a>, and dense formation of hairs in the crown center. These hairs serve to protect the plants from Australia's intense summer sun. Pygmy sundews form the subgenus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera_subg._Bryastrum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drosera subg. Bryastrum (page does not exist)">Bryastrum</a></i>.</li>
<li>Tuberous sundews: These nearly 50 Australian species form an underground <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tuber</a> to survive the extremely dry summers of their habitat, re-emerging in the autumn. These so-called tuberous sundews can be further divided into two groups, those that form rosettes and those that form climbing or scrambling stems. Tuberous sundews comprise the subgenus <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_subg._Ergaleium" title="Drosera subg. Ergaleium">Ergaleium</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1060" data-file-width="1363" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG/220px-Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG/330px-Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG/440px-Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_derbyensis_ne1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_derbyensis" title="Drosera derbyensis">D. derbyensis</a></i>, from the petiolaris complex</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><i>Petiolaris</i> complex: A group of tropical Australian species, they live in constantly warm but sometimes wet conditions. Several of the 14 species that comprise this group have developed special strategies to cope with the alternately drier conditions. Many species, for example, have <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petioles</a> densely covered in <a href="/wiki/Trichome" title="Trichome">trichomes</a>, which maintain a sufficiently humid environment and serve as an increased <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> surface for morning dew. The <i>Petiolaris</i> complex comprises the subgenus <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_subg._Lasiocephala" title="Drosera subg. Lasiocephala">Lasiocephala</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Although they do not form a single strictly defined growth form, a number of species are often put together in a further group:</p>
<ul>
<li>Queensland sundews: A small group of three species (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_adelae" title="Drosera adelae">D. adelae</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_schizandra" title="Drosera schizandra">D. schizandra</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_prolifera" title="Drosera prolifera">D. prolifera</a></i>), all are native to highly humid habitats in the dim understories of the Australian rainforest.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaves_and_carnivory">Leaves and carnivory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leaves and carnivory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="786" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG/220px-Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG/330px-Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG/440px-Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_capensis_bend.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Leaf and tentacle movement on <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_capensis" title="Drosera capensis">D. capensis</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sundews are characterised by the glandular tentacles, topped with sticky secretions, that cover their <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">laminae</a>. The trapping and digestion mechanism usually employs two types of glands: stalked glands that secrete sweet mucilage to attract and ensnare insects and enzymes to digest them, and <a href="/wiki/Sessility_(botany)" title="Sessility (botany)">sessile</a> <a href="/wiki/Gland" title="Gland">glands</a> that absorb the resulting nutrient soup (the latter glands are missing in some species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_erythrorhiza" title="Drosera erythrorhiza">D. erythrorhiza</a></i>). Small prey, mainly consisting of insects, are attracted by the sweet secretions of the peduncular glands. Upon touching these, the prey become entrapped by sticky mucilage which prevents their progress or escape. Eventually, the prey either succumb to death through exhaustion or through asphyxiation as the mucilage envelops them and clogs their <a href="/wiki/Spiracle" title="Spiracle">spiracles</a>. Death usually occurs within 15 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darwin1875_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Darwin1875-6">[6]</a></sup> The plant meanwhile secretes <a href="/wiki/Esterase" title="Esterase">esterase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peroxidase" title="Peroxidase">peroxidase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphatase" title="Phosphatase">phosphatase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">protease</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These enzymes dissolve the insect and free the nutrients contained within it. This nutrient mixture is then absorbed through the leaf surfaces to be used by the rest of the plant.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1216" data-file-width="1428" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg/220px-Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg/330px-Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg/440px-Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_Glandular_Hair.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Drosera Glandular Hair</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All species of sundew are able to move their tentacles in response to contact with edible prey. The tentacles are extremely sensitive and will bend toward the center of the leaf to bring the insect into contact with as many stalked glands as possible. According to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, the contact of the legs of a small gnat with a single tentacle is enough to induce this response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darwin1875_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Darwin1875-6">[6]</a></sup> This response to touch is known as <a href="/wiki/Thigmonasty" title="Thigmonasty">thigmonasty</a>, and is quite rapid in some species. The outer tentacles (recently coined as "snap-tentacles") of <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_burmannii" title="Drosera burmannii">D. burmannii</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_sessilifolia" title="Drosera sessilifolia">D. sessilifolia</a></i> can bend inwards toward prey in a matter of seconds after contact, while <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_glanduligera" title="Drosera glanduligera">D. glanduligera</a></i> is known to bend these tentacles in toward prey in tenths of a second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In addition to tentacle movement, some species are able to bend their laminae to various degrees to maximize contact with the prey. Of these, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_capensis" title="Drosera capensis">D. capensis</a></i> exhibits what is probably the most dramatic movement, curling its leaf completely around prey in 30 minutes. Some species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_filiformis" title="Drosera filiformis">D. filiformis</a></i>, are unable to bend their leaves in response to prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAmato1998_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAmato1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG/220px-DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG/330px-DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG/440px-DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DroseraIndicaEmergences.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emergences of an Australian <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_indica" title="Drosera indica">D. indica</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A further type of (mostly strong red and yellow) emergence has recently been discovered in a few Australian species (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_hartmeyerorum" title="Drosera hartmeyerorum">D. hartmeyerorum</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_indica" title="Drosera indica">D. indica</a></i>). Their function is not known yet, although they may help in attracting prey.</p>
<p>The leaf morphology of the species within the genus is extremely varied, ranging from the sessile <a href="/wiki/File:Leaf_morphology_no_title.png" title="File:Leaf morphology no title.png">ovate</a> leaves of <i>D. erythrorhiza</i> to the bipinnately divided <a href="/wiki/File:Leaf_morphology_no_title.png" title="File:Leaf morphology no title.png">acicular</a> leaves of <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_binata" title="Drosera binata">D. binata</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flowers_and_fruit">Flowers and fruit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flowers and fruit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="834" data-file-width="867" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg/220px-DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg/330px-DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg/440px-DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DroseraKenneallyiFlora.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Flower of <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_kenneallyi" title="Drosera kenneallyi">D. kenneallyi</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The flowers of sundews, as with nearly all carnivorous plants, are held far above the leaves by a long stem.This physical isolation of the flower from the traps was originally thought to be an adaptation meant to avoid trapping potential <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinators</a>; a recent study, however, indicated <i>Drosera</i> species attract distinct types of insects as pollinators and prey, with little overlap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Instead, the tall flower stalks probably help raise the flowers to a height where they are noticeable to pollinators. The mostly unforked <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescences</a> are <a href="/wiki/Raceme" title="Raceme">spikes</a>, whose flowers open one at a time and usually only remain open for a short period. Flowers open in response to light intensity (often opening only in direct sunlight), and the entire inflorescence is also <a href="/wiki/Heliotropism" title="Heliotropism">helitropic</a>, moving in response to the sun's position in the sky.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_(biology)#Radial_symmetry" title="Symmetry (biology)">radially symmetrical</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actinomorphic" title="Actinomorphic">actinomorphic</a>) flowers are always <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_flower" title="Perfect flower">perfect</a> and have five parts (the exceptions to this rule are the four-petaled <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_pygmaea" title="Drosera pygmaea">D. pygmaea</a></i> and the eight to 12-petaled <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_heterophylla" title="Drosera heterophylla">D. heterophylla</a></i>). Most of the species have small flowers (&lt;1.5 cm or 0.6 in). A few species, however, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_regia" title="Drosera regia">D. regia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_cistiflora" title="Drosera cistiflora">D. cistiflora</a></i>, have flowers 4 cm (1.6 in) or more in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAmato1998_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-DAmato1998-9">[9]</a></sup> In general, the flowers are white or pink. Australian species display a wider range of colors, including orange (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_callistos" title="Drosera callistos">D. callistos</a></i>), red (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_adelae" title="Drosera adelae">D. adelae</a></i>), yellow (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_zigzagia" title="Drosera zigzagia">D. zigzagia</a></i>) or metallic violet (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_microphylla" title="Drosera microphylla">D. microphylla</a></i>).</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovary_(plants)" title="Ovary (plants)">ovary</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovary_(plants)" title="Ovary (plants)">superior</a> and develops into a <a href="/wiki/Dehiscence_(botany)#Fruit_dehiscence" title="Dehiscence (botany)">dehiscent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsule_(fruit)" title="Capsule (fruit)">seed capsule</a> bearing numerous tiny seeds. The <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> grain type is compound, which means four <a href="/wiki/Microspore" title="Microspore">microspores</a> (pollen grains) are stuck together with a protein called <a href="/wiki/Callose" title="Callose">callose</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roots">Roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg/220px-Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg/330px-Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg/440px-Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_anglica_ne2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Drosera anglica</i> with prey</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The root systems of most <i>Drosera</i> are often only weakly developed. Serving mainly to absorb water and to anchor the plant to the ground, the roots are relatively useless for nutrient uptake. A few South African species use their roots for water and food storage. Some species have wiry root systems that remain during frosts if the stem dies. Some species, such as <i>D. adelae</i> and <i>D. hamiltonii</i>, use their roots for asexual propagation, by sprouting plantlets along their length. Some Australian species form underground <a href="/wiki/Corm" title="Corm">corms</a> for this purpose, which also serve to allow the plants to survive dry summers. The roots of pygmy sundews are often extremely long in proportion to their size, with a 1-cm (0.4-in) plant extending roots over 15 cm (5.9 in) beneath the soil surface. Some pygmy sundews, such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera_lasiantha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drosera lasiantha (page does not exist)">D. lasiantha</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_scorpioides" title="Drosera scorpioides">D. scorpioides</a></i>, also form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adventitious_root" title="Adventitious root">adventitious roots</a> as supports. <i>D. intermedia</i> and <i>D. rotundifolia</i> have been reported to form <a href="/wiki/Arbuscular_mycorrhiza" title="Arbuscular mycorrhiza">arbuscular mycorrhizae</a>, which penetrate the plant's tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The unrooted <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a> to the right shows the relationship between various subgenera and classes as defined by the analysis of Rivadavia <i>et al.</i> in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rivadavia2003_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rivadavia2003-12">[12]</a></sup> The monotypic section <i>Meristocaulis</i> was not included in the study, so its place in this system is unclear. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many species of sundews are self-fertile; their flowers will often self-pollinate upon closing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAmato1998_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-DAmato1998-9">[9]</a></sup> Often, numerous seeds are produced. The tiny black seeds germinate in response to moisture and light, while seeds of temperate species also require cold, damp, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratification_(botany)" title="Stratification (botany)">stratification</a> to germinate. Seeds of the tuberous species require a hot, dry summer period followed by a cool, moist winter to germinate.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">Vegetative reproduction</a> occurs naturally in some species that produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stolons" title="Stolons">stolons</a> or when roots come close to the surface of the soil. Older leaves that touch the ground may sprout plantlets. Pygmy sundews reproduce asexually using specialized scale-like leaves called <a href="/wiki/Gemma_(botany)" title="Gemma (botany)">gemmae</a>. Tuberous sundews can produce offsets from their corms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAmato1998_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-DAmato1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In culture, sundews can often be propagated through leaf, crown, or root cuttings, as well as through seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAmato1998_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-DAmato1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous_plants_of_Australia" title="Carnivorous plants of Australia">Carnivorous plants of Australia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a> of the sundew genus stretches from Alaska in the north to New Zealand in the south. The centers of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">diversity</a> are Australia, with roughly 50% of all known species, and South America and southern Africa, each with more than 20 species. A few species are also found in large parts of Eurasia and North America. These areas, however, can be considered to form the outskirts of the generic range, as the ranges of sundews do not typically approach temperate or Arctic areas. Contrary to previous supposition, the evolutionary <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> of this genus is no longer thought to have occurred with the breakup of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> through <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a>. Rather, speciation is now thought to have occurred as a result of a subsequent wide dispersal of its range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rivadavia2003_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rivadavia2003-12">[12]</a></sup> The origins of the genus are thought to have been in Africa or Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rivadavia2003_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rivadavia2003-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Europe is home to only three species: <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_intermedia" title="Drosera intermedia">D. intermedia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_anglica" title="Drosera anglica">D. anglica</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_rotundifolia" title="Drosera rotundifolia">D. rotundifolia</a></i>. Where the ranges of the two latter species overlap, they sometimes hybridize to form the sterile <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera_%C3%97_obovata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drosera × obovata (page does not exist)">D. × obovata</a></i>. In addition to the three species and the hybrid native to Europe, North America is also home to four additional species; <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_brevifolia" title="Drosera brevifolia">D. brevifolia</a></i> is a small <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual</a> native to coastal states from <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, while <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_capillaris" title="Drosera capillaris">D. capillaris</a></i>, a slightly larger plant with a similar range, is also found in areas of the Caribbean. The third species, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_linearis" title="Drosera linearis">D. linearis</a></i>, is native to the northern United States and southern Canada. <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_filiformis" title="Drosera filiformis">D. filiformis</a></i> has two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> native to the East Coast of North America, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_panhandle" title="Florida panhandle">Florida panhandle</a>.</p>
<p>This genus is often described as <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan</a>, meaning it has worldwide distribution. The botanist <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Diels" title="Ludwig Diels">Ludwig Diels</a>, author of the only <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monograph</a> of the family to date, called this description an "arrant misjudgment of this genus' highly unusual distributional circumstances (<i>arge Verkennung ihrer höchst eigentümlichen Verbreitungsverhältnisse</i>)", while admitting sundew species do "occupy a significant part of the Earth's surface (<i>einen beträchtlichen Teil der Erdoberfläche besetzt</i>)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diels1906_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diels1906-14">[14]</a></sup> He particularly pointed to the absence of <i>Drosera</i> species from almost all <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> climate zones, countless <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, the American Pacific Coast, <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a>, the Mediterranean region, and North Africa, as well as the scarcity of species diversity in temperate zones, such as Europe and North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diels1906_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Diels1906-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg/220px-Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg/330px-Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg/440px-Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_rotundifolia_habitat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Sundews generally grow in seasonally moist or more rarely constantly wet habitats with acidic soils and high levels of sunlight. Common habitats include <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tepui" title="Tepui">tepuis</a> of Venezuela, the <a href="/wiki/Wallum" title="Wallum">wallums</a> of coastal Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Fynbos" title="Fynbos">fynbos</a> of South Africa, and moist streambanks. Many species grow in association with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphagnum_moss" title="Sphagnum moss">sphagnum moss</a>, which absorbs much of the soil's nutrient supply and also acidifies the soil, making nutrients less available to plant life. This allows sundews, which do not rely on soil-bound nutrients, to flourish where more dominating vegetation would usually outcompete them.</p>
<p>The genus, though, is very variable in terms of habitat. Individual sundew species have adapted to a wide variety of environments, including atypical habitats, such as rainforests, deserts (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_burmannii" title="Drosera burmannii">D. burmannii</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_indica" title="Drosera indica">D. indica</a></i>), and even highly shaded environments (Queensland sundews). The temperate species, which form <a href="/wiki/Hibernaculum_(botany)" title="Hibernaculum (botany)">hibernacula</a> in the winter, are examples of such adaptation to habitats; in general, sundews tend to inhabit warm climates, and are only moderately frost-resistant.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg/220px-Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg/330px-Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg/440px-Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosera_rotundifolia_leaf1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Leaf of the common sundew, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_rotundifolia" title="Drosera rotundifolia">Drosera rotundifolia</a></i></div>

<p>Although none of the <i>Drosera</i> species in the United States are federally protected, all are listed as <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened</a> or <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> in some states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Additionally, many of the remaining native populations lie on protected land, such as <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a> or wildlife preserves. <i>Drosera</i> species are protected by law in many European countries, such as Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup> Austria, Switzerland, the Czech Republic, Finland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup> and Bulgaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup> Currently, the largest threat in Europe and North America is habitat destruction for development projects, as well as the draining of bogs for agricultural uses and peat harvesting. In many regions, this has led to the extirpation of some species from parts of their former range. Reintroduction of plants into such habitats is usually difficult or impossible, as the ecological needs of certain populations is closely tied to their geographical location. Through increased legal protection of bogs and moors, as well as a concentrated effort to renaturalize such habitats, the threat to these plants' survival might be curbed, although most species would remain endangered. The relatively unimpressive image of these plants, as well as their small, low growth, makes them difficult to protect.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As part of the landscape, sundews are often overlooked or not recognized at all.</p>
<p>In South Africa and Australia, two of the three centers of <a href="/wiki/Species_diversity" title="Species diversity">species diversity</a>, the natural habitats of these plants are undergoing a high degree of pressure from human activities. Expanding population centers (such as <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>) threaten many such habitats, as does the draining of moist areas for agriculture and forestry in rural areas. The droughts that have been sweeping Australia over the last 10 years also pose a threat to many species by drying up previously moist areas.</p>
<p>Those species endemic to a very limited area are often most threatened by the collection of plants from the wild. <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_madagascariensis" title="Drosera madagascariensis">D. madagascariensis</a></i> is considered endangered in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> because of the large-scale removal of plants from the wild for exportation; 10 - 200 million plants are harvested for commercial medicinal use annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_medicinal_plant">As a medicinal plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: As a medicinal plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sundews were used as medicinal herbs as early as the 12th century, when an Italian doctor from the School of Salerno, <a href="/wiki/Matthaeus_Platearius" title="Matthaeus Platearius">Matthaeus Platearius</a>, described the plant as an herbal remedy for <a href="/wiki/Cough" title="Cough">coughs</a> under the name <i>herba sole</i>. It has been used commonly in cough preparations in Germany and elsewhere in Europe. Sundew tea was especially recommended by herbalists for dry coughs, <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whooping_cough" title="Whooping cough">whooping cough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> and "bronchial cramps".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A modern study has shown that <i>Drosera</i> exhibits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antitussive" title="Antitussive">antitussive</a> properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Culbreth's 1927 <i>Materia Medica</i> listed <i>D. rotundifolia</i>, <i>D. anglica</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_linearis" title="Drosera linearis">D.linearis</a></i> as being used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stimulants" title="Stimulants">stimulants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expectorant" title="Expectorant">expectorants</a>, and "of doubtful efficacy" for treating <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whooping_cough" title="Whooping cough">whooping cough</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Sundews have also been used as an <a href="/wiki/Aphrodisiac" title="Aphrodisiac">aphrodisiac</a> and to strengthen the heart, as well as to treat sunburn, toothache,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and prevent freckles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Drosera#Dubious" title="Talk:Drosera">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> They are still used today in some 200-300 registered medications, usually in combination with other herbal ingredients.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Today, <i>Drosera</i> is usually used to treat ailments such as <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, coughs, lung infections, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomach_ulcer" title="Stomach ulcer">stomach ulcers</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (September 2012)">medical citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Medicinal preparations are primarily made using the roots, flowers, and fruit-like capsules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Since all native sundews species are protected in many parts of Europe and North America, extracts are usually prepared using cultivated fast-growing sundews (specifically <i>D. rotundifolia</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_intermedia" title="Drosera intermedia">D. intermedia</a></i>, <i>D. anglica</i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera_ramentacea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drosera ramentacea (page does not exist)">D. ramentacea</a></i> and <i>D. madagascariensis</i>) or from plants collected and imported from Madagascar, Spain, France, Finland and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltics" title="Baltics">Baltics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFG_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-WWFG-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_ornamental_plants">As ornamental plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: As ornamental plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of their carnivorous nature and the beauty of their glistening traps, sundews have become favorite <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plants</a>; however, the environmental requirements of most species are relatively stringent and can be difficult to meet in cultivation. As a result, most species are unavailable commercially. A few of the hardiest varieties, however, have made their way into the mainstream nursery business and can often be found for sale next to <a href="/wiki/Venus_flytrap" title="Venus flytrap">Venus flytraps</a>. These most often include <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_capensis" title="Drosera capensis">D. capensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_aliciae" title="Drosera aliciae">D. aliciae</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_spatulata" title="Drosera spatulata">D. spatulata</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rice_2006_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rice_2006-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cultivation requirements vary greatly by species. In general, though, sundews require high environmental moisture content, usually in the form of a constantly moist or wet soil substrate. Most species also require this water to be pure, as nutrients, salts, or minerals in their soil can stunt their growth or even kill them. Commonly, plants are grown in a soil substrate containing some combination of dead or live <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphagnum_moss" title="Sphagnum moss">sphagnum moss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">sphagnum</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_moss" title="Peat moss">peat moss</a>, sand, and/or <a href="/wiki/Perlite" title="Perlite">perlite</a>, and are watered with <a href="/wiki/Distilled_water" title="Distilled water">distilled</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a>, or rain water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAmato1998_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-DAmato1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanobiotechnology">Nanobiotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nanobiotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mucilage produced by <i>Drosera</i> has remarkable elastic properties and has made this genus a very attractive subject in biomaterials research. In one recent study, the adhesive mucilages of three species (<i>D. binata</i>, <i>D. capensis</i>, and <i>D. spatulata</i>) were analyzed for <a href="/wiki/Nanofiber" title="Nanofiber">nanofiber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanoparticle" title="Nanoparticle">nanoparticle</a> content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-zhang-23">[23]</a></sup> Using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_force_microscopy" title="Atomic force microscopy">atomic force microscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">transmission electron microscopy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Energy-dispersive_X-ray_spectroscopy" title="Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy">energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy</a>, researchers were able to observe networks of nanofibers and nanoparticles of various sizes within the mucilage residues. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> – key components of biological salts - were identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-zhang-23">[23]</a></sup> These nanoparticles are theorized to increase the viscosity and stickiness of the mucilage, in turn increasing the effectiveness of the trap. More importantly for biomaterials research, however, is the fact that, when dried, the mucin provides a suitable substrate for the attachment of living cells. This has important implications for tissue engineering, especially because of the elastic qualities of the adhesive. Essentially, a coating of <i>Drosera</i> mucilage on a surgical implant, such as a replacement hip or an organ transplant, could drastically improve the rate of recovery and decrease the potential for rejection, because living tissue can effectively attach and grow on it. The authors also suggest a wide variety of applications for <i>Drosera</i> mucin, including wound treatment, regenerative medicine, or enhancing synthetic adhesives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-zhang-23">[23]</a></sup> Also of note, because this mucilage can stretch to nearly a million times its original size and is readily available for use, it can be an extremely cost-efficient source of biomaterial.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Corm" title="Corm">corms</a> of the tuberous sundews native to Australia are considered a delicacy by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigines" title="Australian Aborigines">Australian Aborigines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Some of these corms were also used to dye textiles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plantarara2001_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plantarara2001-25">[25]</a></sup> while another purple or yellow dye was <a href="/wiki/Traditional_dyes_of_the_Scottish_Highlands" title="Traditional dyes of the Scottish Highlands">traditionally prepared</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a> using <i>D. rotundifolia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A sundew <a href="/wiki/Liqueur" title="Liqueur">liqueur</a> is also still produced using a recipe from the 14th century. It is made using fresh leaves from mainly <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_capensis" title="Drosera capensis">D. capensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosera_spatulata" title="Drosera spatulata">D. spatulata</a></i>, and <i>D. rotundifolia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plantarara2001_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Plantarara2001-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_constituents">Chemical constituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Chemical constituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several chemical compounds with potential biological activities are found in sundews, including <a href="/wiki/Flavonoid" title="Flavonoid">flavonoids</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kaempferol" title="Kaempferol">kaempferol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myricetin" title="Myricetin">myricetin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quercetin" title="Quercetin">quercetin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyperoside" title="Hyperoside">hyperoside</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quinones" title="Quinones">quinones</a> (<a href="/wiki/Plumbagin" title="Plumbagin">plumbagin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydroplumbagin_glucoside&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydroplumbagin glucoside (page does not exist)">hydroplumbagin glucoside</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rossoliside&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rossoliside (page does not exist)">rossoliside</a> (7–methyl–hydrojuglone–4–glucoside)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>), and other constituents such as <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a>, plant acids (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallic_acid" title="Gallic acid">gallic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a>), <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tannins" title="Tannins">tannins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a> (vitamin C).</p>
