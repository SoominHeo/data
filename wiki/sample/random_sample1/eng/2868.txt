<p><i><b>I, Daniel Blake</b></i> is a 2016 British-French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Loach" title="Ken Loach">Ken Loach</a> and written by Loach's frequent collaborator <a href="/wiki/Paul_Laverty" title="Paul Laverty">Paul Laverty</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Dave_Johns" title="Dave Johns">Dave Johns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hayley_Squires" title="Hayley Squires">Hayley Squires</a>, Dylan McKiernan, and Briana Shann.</p>
<p>It won the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2016_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2016 Cannes Film Festival">2016 Cannes Film Festival</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prix_du_public" title="Prix du public">Prix du public</a> at the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Locarno_International_Film_Festival" title="Locarno International Film Festival">Locarno International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deadline2016_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deadline2016-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Locarno_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Locarno-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I,_Daniel_Blake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Daniel Blake is a 59-year-old <a href="/wiki/Joiner" title="Joiner">joiner</a> living in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>, a city in North East England. Daniel has had a heart attack at work. His recovery is incomplete and his cardiologist is concerned that Daniel's heart might begin to beat abnormally, putting him at risk of developing a life-threatening <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhythmia" title="Arrhythmia">arrhythmia</a>. She tells him that he is not ready to go back to work.</p>
<p>Daniel applies for the sickness benefit called '<a href="/wiki/Employment_and_Support_Allowance" title="Employment and Support Allowance">Employment and Support Allowance</a>'. He gets it at first, but when an eligibility test is carried out, his points tally is slightly below the threshold needed to keep receiving payments. As a result, he is deemed to be able to work. Daniel had assumed that the unspecified healthcare professional who carried out his <a href="/wiki/Work_Capability_Assessment" title="Work Capability Assessment">Work Capability Assessment</a>, a simplistic box-ticking exercise, had contacted his doctor for information on his condition, but she had not. Consequently, the test's criterion for people who are at risk – which would have qualified Daniel for sickness benefits by itself – is not applied by a government "decision-maker". The supervisor at the job centre says Daniel's only option is to claim <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jobseekers_Allowance" title="Jobseekers Allowance">Jobseekers Allowance</a>. As a condition of receiving Jobseekers Allowance, he must actively look for work. Daniel launches a legal appeal against being found fit for work but he finds it difficult because he is not computer literate.</p>
<p>Daniel gets to know single mother Katie and her two children, Dylan and Daisy, who have left a homeless persons' hostel in London and, with no other affordable accommodation being available in the capital, have moved to Daniel's home town, a place unfamiliar to Katie and her children. On her first visit to the Job Centre Katie was "sanctioned" — her benefits were stopped because she briefly got lost on the way there — and she can no longer feed all her family nor heat their flat.</p>
<p>Widower Daniel, single-parent Katie and her children try to deal with the problems they face together. They visit a <a href="/wiki/Food_bank" title="Food bank">food bank</a> and Katie is overcome by hunger. She is slowly drawn into the black economy, while Daniel seeks work on the local industrial estate and is offered a job working in a scrapyard. He reluctantly turns it down, with his doctor's advice in mind. Daniel's "work coach" feels he is not making enough effort to get a job. Daniel becomes disillusioned and sprays "I, Daniel Blake demand my appeal date before I starve" on the job centre's outside wall. The bystanders sympathize with him, but he's taken away by the police and given a formal warning. Daniel sells most of his belongings and becomes very withdrawn.</p>
<p>Finally, Daniel's appeal date arrives. He goes to the appeal court, with Katie to support him, where he meets his welfare rights adviser. The adviser has obtained copies of the medical records and he advises Daniel that his case looks sound. On glimpsing the judge and doctor who will decide on his appeal, Daniel becomes anxious. He goes to the washroom to cool down. Shortly afterwards, someone shouts for an ambulance. Daniel has collapsed. The tribunal doctor is called but finds that Daniel has no pulse. She begins <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">CPR</a> — unsuccessfully.</p>
<p>At his "<a href="/wiki/Pauper%27s_funeral" title="Pauper's funeral">pauper's funeral</a>", Katie reads the <a href="/wiki/Eulogy" title="Eulogy">eulogy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I,_Daniel_Blake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Principal photography began in October 2015 in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> and the surrounding area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The film was produced by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rebecca_O%27Brien&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rebecca O'Brien (page does not exist)">Rebecca O'Brien</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> for Sixteen Films, <a href="/wiki/Why_Not_Productions" title="Why Not Productions">Why Not Productions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wild_Bunch_(company)" title="Wild Bunch (company)">Wild Bunch</a> with the support of the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Films" title="BBC Films">BBC Films</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I,_Daniel_Blake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I,_Daniel_Blake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a> website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film has an approval rating of 92%, based on 60 reviews, with an average rating of 7.8/10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-I.2C_Daniel_Blake_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-I.2C_Daniel_Blake-9">[9]</a></sup> The site's consensus reads: "I, Daniel Blake marks yet another well-told chapter in director Ken Loach's powerfully populist filmography."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-I.2C_Daniel_Blake_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-I.2C_Daniel_Blake-9">[9]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> the film has a score of 78 out of 100 score, based on 12 critics, indicating "generally favourable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I,_Daniel_Blake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>I, Daniel Blake</i> became Ken Loach's biggest success at the UK box office, especially as the film sparked debate in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_response">Political response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I,_Daniel_Blake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Political response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been a wide variety of both praise and criticism of the film from politicians.</p>
<p>Former Work and Pensions Secretary <a href="/wiki/Iain_Duncan_Smith" title="Iain Duncan Smith">Iain Duncan Smith</a> referred to the film as unfair, aiming particular criticism at its portrayal of Jobcentre staff, saying: "This idea that everybody is out to crunch you, I think it has really hurt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jobcentre" title="Jobcentre">jobcentre</a> staff who don’t see themselves as that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Producer Rebecca O'Brien responded by stating that Duncan Smith "is living in cloud cuckoo land."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, on an episode of BBC's topical debate programme <i><a href="/wiki/Question_Time_(TV_series)" title="Question Time (TV series)">Question Time</a></i> broadcast on 27 October 2016, which featured Ken Loach as a panellist, Business Secretary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregory_David_Clark" title="Gregory David Clark">Greg Clark</a> described the film as "a fictional film",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph.co.uk_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph.co.uk-15">[15]</a></sup> saying "It’s a difficult job administering a benefits system...<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Work_and_Pensions" title="Department of Work and Pensions">Department of Work and Pensions</a> staff have to make incredibly difficult decisions and I think they should have our support in making those decisions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph.co.uk_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph.co.uk-15">[15]</a></sup> Loach responded to this by criticising the pressure that DWP staff are placed under.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph.co.uk_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph.co.uk-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn" title="Jeremy Corbyn">Jeremy Corbyn</a> offered glowing praise towards the film - he appeared at the film's London premiere with the director, then subsequently praised the film on his Facebook page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In a session of <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister%27s_Questions" title="Prime Minister's Questions">Prime Minister's Questions</a> on 2 November 2016, he advised <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a> to watch the film, as he criticised the fairness of the welfare system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
