His birth is celebrated world-wide as <a href="/wiki/Guru_Nanak_Gurpurab" title="Guru Nanak Gurpurab">Guru Nanak Gurpurab</a> on Kartik Pooranmashi, the full-moon day in the month of <a href="/wiki/Katak" title="Katak">Katak</a>, October–November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbans_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harbans-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guru Nanak has been called "one of the greatest religious innovators of all time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuruNanak_BBC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuruNanak_BBC-3">[3]</a></sup> He travelled far and wide teaching people the message of one God who dwells in every one of His creations and constitutes the eternal Truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He set up a unique spiritual, social, and political platform based on equality, fraternal love, goodness, and virtue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guru Nanak's words are registered in the form of 974 poetic hymns in the holy text of Sikhism, the <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a>, with some of the major prayers being the <a href="/wiki/Japji_Sahib" title="Japji Sahib">Japji Sahib</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Asa_di_Var" title="Asa di Var">Asa di Var</a> and the Sidh-Ghost. It is part of Sikh religious belief that the spirit of Guru Nanak's sanctity, divinity and religious authority descended upon each of the nine subsequent Gurus when the Guruship was devolved on to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_early_life">Family and early life</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="712" data-file-width="513" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg/220px-Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg/330px-Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg/440px-Baba_Nanak_goes_to_school.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Baba Nanak goes to school</div>

</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nankana_Sahib.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1330" data-file-width="2848" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Nankana_Sahib.JPG/200px-Nankana_Sahib.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Nankana_Sahib.JPG/300px-Nankana_Sahib.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Nankana_Sahib.JPG/400px-Nankana_Sahib.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nankana_Sahib.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Gurdwara_Janam_Asthan" title="Gurdwara Janam Asthan">Gurdwara Janam Asthan</a></div>

<p>Nanak was born on 15 April 1469 at Rāi Bhoi Kī Talvaṇḍī (present day <a href="/wiki/Nankana_Sahib" title="Nankana Sahib">Nankana Sahib</a>, Punjab, Pakistan) near <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macauliffe_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macauliffe-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His parents were Kalyan Chand Das Bedi, popularly shortened to <a href="/wiki/Mehta_Kalu" title="Mehta Kalu">Mehta Kalu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mata_Tripta" title="Mata Tripta">Mata Tripta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His father was the local <a href="/wiki/Village_accountant" title="Village accountant">patwari</a> (accountant) for crop revenue in the village of Talwandi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His parents were both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindus" title="Hindus">Hindus</a> and belonged to the merchant caste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>He had one sister, <a href="/wiki/Bebe_Nanaki" title="Bebe Nanaki">Bebe Nanaki</a>, who was five years older than he was. In 1475 she married and moved to <a href="/wiki/Sultanpur_Lodhi" title="Sultanpur Lodhi">Sultanpur</a>. Nanak was attached to his sister and followed her to Sultanpur to live with her and her husband, Jai Ram. At the age of around 16 years, Nanak started working under <a href="/wiki/Daulat_Khan_Lodi" title="Daulat Khan Lodi">Daulat Khan Lodi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbans_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harbans-2">[2]</a></sup> employer of Nanaki's husband. This was a formative time for Nanak, as the Puratan (traditional) <a href="/wiki/Janamsakhis" title="Janamsakhis">Janam Sakhi</a> suggests, and in his numerous allusions to governmental structure in his hymns, most likely gained at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated9_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated9-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Sikh traditions, the birth and early years of Guru Nanak's life were marked with many events that demonstrated that Nanak had been marked by divine grace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuruNanak_BBC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-GuruNanak_BBC-3">[3]</a></sup> Commentaries on his life give details of his blossoming awareness from a young age. At the age of five, Nanak is said to have voiced interest in divine subjects. At age seven, his father enrolled him at the village school as was the custom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macauliffe_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macauliffe-9">[9]</a></sup> Notable lore recounts that as a child Nanak astonished his teacher by describing the implicit symbolism of the <a href="/wiki/Aleph" title="Aleph">first letter of the alphabet</a>, resembling the mathematical version of one, as denoting the unity or oneness of God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Other childhood accounts refer to strange and miraculous events about Nanak, such as one witnessed by Rai Bular, in which the sleeping child's head was shaded from the harsh sunlight, in one account, by the stationary shadow of a tree<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> or, in another, by a venomous cobra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kartar_singh_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-kartar_singh-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 September 1487 Nanak married Mata Sulakkhani, daughter of Mūl Chand and Chando Rāṇī, in the town of <a href="/wiki/Batala" title="Batala">Batala</a>. The couple had two sons, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Chand" title="Sri Chand">Sri Chand</a> (8 September 1494 – 13 January 1629)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and Lakhmi Chand (12 February 1497 – 9 April 1555). Sri Chand received enlightenment from Guru Nanak's teachings and went on to become the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Udasi" title="Udasi">Udasi</a> sect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biographies">Biographies</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="642" data-file-width="450" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg/220px-Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg/330px-Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg/440px-Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bhai_Mani_Singh%27s_Janamsakhi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bhai_Mani_Singh" title="Bhai Mani Singh">Bhai Mani Singh</a>'s Janamsakhi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest biographical sources on Nanak's life recognised today are the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Janams%C4%81kh%C4%ABs" title="Janamsākhīs">Janamsākhīs</a></i> (life accounts). <a href="/wiki/Bhai_Gurdas" title="Bhai Gurdas">Bhai Gurdas</a>, a scribe of the <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Gurū Granth Sahib</a>, also wrote about Nanak's life in his <i>vārs</i>. Although these too were compiled some time after Nanak's time, they are less detailed than the Janamsākhīs. The Janamsākhīs recount in minute detail the circumstances of the birth of the guru.</p>
<p>Gyan-ratanavali is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Bhai_Mani_Singh" title="Bhai Mani Singh">Bhai Mani Singh</a> who wrote it with the express intention of correcting heretical accounts of Guru Nanak. Bhai Mani Singh was a Sikh of <a href="/wiki/Guru_Gobind_Singh" title="Guru Gobind Singh">Guru Gobind Singh</a> who was approached by some Sikhs with a request that he should prepare an authentic account of Guru Nanak’s life. Bhai Mani Singh writes : Just as swimmers fix reeds in the river so that those who do not know the way may also cross, so I shall take Bhai Gurdas’s var as my basis and in accordance with it, and with the accounts that I have heard at the court of the tenth Master, I shall relate to you whatever commentary issues from my humble mind. At the end of the Janam-sakhi there is an epilogue in which it is stated that the completed work was taken to Guru Gobind Singh for his seal of approval. Guru Sahib duly signed it and commended it as a means of acquiring knowledge of Sikh belief.</p>
<p>One popular Janamsākhī was allegedly written by a close companion of the Guru, <a href="/wiki/Bhai_Bala" title="Bhai Bala">Bhai Bala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> However, the writing style and language employed have left scholars, such as <a href="/wiki/Max_Arthur_Macauliffe" title="Max Arthur Macauliffe">Max Arthur Macauliffe</a>, certain that they were composed after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macauliffe_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Macauliffe-9">[9]</a></sup> According to the scholars, there are good reasons to doubt the claim that the author was a close companion of Guru Nanak and accompanied him on many of his travels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sikhism">Sikhism</span></h2>
<p>Rai Bular, the local landlord and Nanak's sister Bibi Nanaki were the first people who recognised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine" title="Divine">divine</a> qualities in the boy. They encouraged and supported him to study and travel. Sikh tradition states that at around 1499, at the age of 30, he had a vision. After he failed to return from his <a href="/wiki/Ritual_purification" title="Ritual purification">ablutions</a>, his clothes were found on the bank of a local stream called the Kali Bein. The townspeople assumed he had drowned in the river; Daulat Khan had the river dragged, but no body was found. Three days after disappearing, Nanak reappeared, staying silent.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2775" data-file-width="3645" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg/220px-Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg/330px-Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg/440px-Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sikhs_paying_homage_to_Guru_Nanak_Wellcome_V0045987.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sikhs paying homage to Guru Nanak</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The next day, he spoke to pronounce:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"There is neither Hindu nor Mussulman (Muslim), but only man. So whose path shall I follow? I shall follow God's path. God is neither Hindu nor Mussulman and the path which I follow is God's."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated9_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated9-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Nanak said that he had been taken to God's court. There, he was offered a cup filled with <a href="/wiki/Amrita" title="Amrita">amrita</a> (nectar) and given the command,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"This is the cup of the adoration of God's name. Drink it. I am with you. I bless you and raise you up. Whoever remembers you will enjoy my favour. Go, rejoice of my name and teach others to do so. I have bestowed the gift of my name upon you. Let this be your calling."</dd>
</dl>
<p>From this point onwards, Nanak is described in accounts as a <a href="/wiki/Guru" title="Guru">Guru</a> (teacher), and Sikhism was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main basic belief of Sikhism is to spread the message of kindness, and peace, instead of revenge and spite.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sikhism is one of the most recently formed religions in the world. Sikhs follow the teaching of the <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a>, the holy book which comprises the teaching of six of the ten gurus of Sikhism and some saints and men of devotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eos_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-eos-23">[23]</a></sup> The Guru Granth Sahib is worshipped as the Supreme Authority of Sikhism and is considered the eleventh and final guru of Sikhism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As the first guru of Sikhism, Guru Nanak contributed a total of 974 hymns to the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teachings">Teachings</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GuruNanakFresco-Goindwal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="318" data-file-width="318" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GuruNanakFresco-Goindwal.jpg/220px-GuruNanakFresco-Goindwal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/GuruNanakFresco-Goindwal.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GuruNanakFresco-Goindwal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fresco of Guru Nanak</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nanak’s teachings can be found in the Sikh scripture <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a>, as a collection of verses recorded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gurmukhi" title="Gurmukhi">Gurmukhi</a>.</p>
<p>There are two competing theories on Guru Nanak's teachings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arvindmandair131_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-arvindmandair131-26">[26]</a></sup> One, according to Cole and Sambhi, is based on hagiographical <a href="/wiki/Janamsakhis" title="Janamsakhis">Janamsakhis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and states that Nanak's teachings and Sikhism were a revelation from God, and not a social protest movement nor any attempt to reconcile Hinduism and Islam in the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_Sambhi_Dic_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole_Sambhi_Dic-28">[28]</a></sup> The other states, Nanak was a <a href="/wiki/Guru" title="Guru">Guru</a>. According to Singha, "Sikhism does not subscribe to the theory of incarnation or the concept of prophethood. But it has a pivotal concept of Guru. He is not an incarnation of God, not even a prophet. He is an illumined soul."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hagiographical <i>Janamsakhis</i> were not written by Nanak, but by later followers without regard for historical accuracy, and contain numerous legends and myths created to show respect for Nanak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The term revelation, clarify Cole and Sambhi, in Sikhism is not limited to the teachings of Nanak, they include all Sikh Gurus, as well as the words of past, present and future men and women, who possess divine knowledge intuitively through meditation. The Sikh revelations include the words of non-Sikh <a href="/wiki/Bhagat" title="Bhagat">bhagats</a>, some who lived and died before the birth of Nanak, and whose teachings are part of the Sikh scriptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Adi Granth and successive Sikh Gurus repeatedly emphasised, states Mandair, that Sikhism is "not about hearing voices from God, but it is about changing the nature of the human mind, and anyone can achieve direct experience and spiritual perfection at any time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arvindmandair131_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-arvindmandair131-26">[26]</a></sup> Guru Nanak emphasised that all human beings can have direct access to God without rituals or priests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuruNanak_BBC_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-GuruNanak_BBC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept of man as elaborated by Guru Nanak, states Arvind-pal Singh Mandair, refines and negates the "monotheistic concept of self/God", and "monotheism becomes almost redundant in the movement and crossings of love".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandair1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mandair1-32">[32]</a></sup> The goal of man, taught the Sikh Gurus, is to end all dualities of "self and other, I and not-I", attain the "attendant balance of separation-fusion, self-other, action-inaction, attachment-detachment, in the course of daily life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandair1_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mandair1-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guru Nanak, and other Sikh Gurus emphasised Bhakti, and taught that the spiritual life and secular householder life are intertwined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kamala1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kamala1-33">[33]</a></sup> In Sikh worldview, the everyday world is part of the Infinite Reality, increased spiritual awareness leads to increased and vibrant participation in the everyday world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nikky1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nikky1-34">[34]</a></sup> Guru Nanak, states Sonali Marwaha, described living an "active, creative, and practical life" of "truthfulness, fidelity, self-control and purity" as being higher than the <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marwha1_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marwha1-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through popular tradition, Nanak’s teaching is understood to be practised in three ways:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Va%E1%B9%87%E1%B8%8D_Chakk%C5%8D" title="Vaṇḍ Chakkō">Va<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">ṇḍ</span> Chakkō</a></i>: Sharing with others, helping those with less who are in need</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirat_Kar%C5%8D" title="Kirat Karō">Kirat Karō</a></i>: Earning/making a living honestly, without exploitation or fraud</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naam_Japna" title="Naam Japna">Naam Japna</a></i>: Meditating on God's name to control the <a href="/wiki/Five_Thieves" title="Five Thieves">five weaknesses</a> of the human personality.</li>
</ul>
<p>Guru Nanak emphasised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naam_Japna" title="Naam Japna">Nam Japna</a> (or Naam Simran), that is repetition of God's name and attributes, as a means to feel God's presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span></h2>
<p>Nanak was raised in a Hindu family and belonged to the Bhakti Sant tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Lorenzen_1995_pages_1-2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_Lorenzen_1995_pages_1-2-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louis_Fenech_2014_page_35_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Louis_Fenech_2014_page_35-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclobritannicasikh_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclobritannicasikh-39">[39]</a></sup> Scholars state that in its origins, Guru Nanak and Sikhism were influenced by the <i>nirguni</i> (formless God) tradition of <a href="/wiki/Bhakti_movement" title="Bhakti movement">Bhakti movement</a> in medieval India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Lorenzen_1995_pages_1-2_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-David_Lorenzen_1995_pages_1-2-37">[37]</a></sup> However, Sikhism was not simply an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Bhakti_movement" title="Bhakti movement">Bhakti movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grewal_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grewal-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singha_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singha-41">[41]</a></sup> Sikhism, for instance, disagreed with some views of Bhakti saints <a href="/wiki/Kabir" title="Kabir">Kabir</a> and Ravidas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singha_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singha-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pruthi_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pruthi-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The roots of the Sikh tradition are, states Louis Fenech, perhaps in the <a href="/wiki/Sant_(religion)" title="Sant (religion)">Sant</a>-tradition of India whose ideology grew to become the Bhakti tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louis_Fenech_2014_page_35_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Louis_Fenech_2014_page_35-38">[38]</a></sup> Furthermore, adds Fenech, "Indic mythology permeates the Sikh sacred canon, the <i>Guru Granth Sahib</i> and the secondary canon, the <i>Dasam Granth</i> and adds delicate nuance and substance to the sacred symbolic universe of the Sikhs of today and of their past ancestors".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journeys_.28Udasis.29">Journeys (Udasis)</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="506" data-file-width="800" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg/220px-SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg/330px-SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg/440px-SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SriGuruNanak%27sTravels.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 4 Udasis and other locations visited by Guru Nanak</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Guru Nanak travelled extensively during his lifetime. Some modern accounts state that he visited Tibet, most of South Asia and Arabia starting in 1496, at age 27, when he left his family for a thirty-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuruNanak_BBC_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-GuruNanak_BBC-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> These claims include Guru Nanak visiting the Mount Sumeru of Indian mythology, as well as Mecca, Baghdad, Achal Batala and Multan, in places he debated religious ideas with competing groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CallewaertSnell1994p26_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-CallewaertSnell1994p26-46">[46]</a></sup> These stories became widely popular in the 19th and 20th century, and exist in many versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CallewaertSnell1994p26_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-CallewaertSnell1994p26-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lorenzen1995p41_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lorenzen1995p41-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiographic</a> details is a subject of dispute, with modern scholarship questioning the details and authenticity of many claims. For example, Callewaert and Snell state that early Sikh texts do not contain these stories, and after these travel stories first appear in hagiographic accounts of Guru Nanak centuries after his death, they continue to become more sophisticated over time, with the late phase <i>Puratan</i> version describing four missionary journeys (<i>udasis</i>), which however differs from the <i>Miharban</i> version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CallewaertSnell1994p26_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-CallewaertSnell1994p26-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lorenzen1995p41_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lorenzen1995p41-47">[47]</a></sup> Some of the stories about Guru Nanak's extensive travels first appear in the 19th-century versions of <i>janam-sakhi</i> in the Puratan version. Further, stories about Guru Nanak's travel to Baghdad is absent from even the early 19th-century <i>Puratan</i> version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CallewaertSnell1994p26_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-CallewaertSnell1994p26-46">[46]</a></sup> These embellishments and insertion of new stories, according to Callewaert and Snell, closely parallel claims of miracles by Islamic <i>pirs</i> found in Sufi tazkiras of the same era, and these legends may have been written in a competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CallewaertSnell1994p26_46-4"><a href="#cite_note-CallewaertSnell1994p26-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another source of dispute has been the Baghdad stone inscription in a Turkish script, which some interpret saying <i>Baba Nanak Fakir</i> was there in 1511-1512, other interpret it stating 1521-1522 (and that he lived in the Middle East for 11 years away from his family), while others particularly Western scholars stating that the stone inscription is from the 19th century and the stone is not a reliable evidence that Guru Nanak visited Baghdad in early 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Further, beyond the stone, no evidence or mention of Guru Nanak's journey in the Middle East has been found in any other Middle Eastern textual or epigraphical records. Claims have been asserted of additional inscriptions, but no one has been able to locate and verify them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Baghdad inscription remains the basis of writing by Indian scholars that Guru Nanak journeyed in the Middle East, with some claiming he visited <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Novel claims about his travels, as well as claims such as Guru Nanak's body vanishing after his death, are also found in later versions and these are similar to the miracle stories in Sufi literature about their <i>pirs</i>. Other direct and indirect borrowings in the Sikh <i>janam-sakhis</i> relating to legends around Guru Nanak's journeys are from Hindu epics and Puranas and Buddhist Jataka stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lorenzen1995p41_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lorenzen1995p41-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CallewaertSnell1994p28_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-CallewaertSnell1994p28-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession">Succession</span></h2>
<p>Guru Nanak appointed Bhai Lehna as the successor <a href="/wiki/Guru" title="Guru">Guru</a>, renaming him as <a href="/wiki/Guru_Angad" title="Guru Angad">Guru Angad</a>, meaning "one’s very own" or "part of you". Shortly after proclaiming Bhai Lehna as his successor, Guru Nanak died on 22 September 1539 in <a href="/wiki/Kartarpur,_Pakistan" title="Kartarpur, Pakistan">Kartarpur</a>, at the age of 70.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
