<p><b>Nadar</b> was the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> of <b>Gaspard-Félix Tournachon</b> (6 April 1820 – 23 March 1910),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a French <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caricaturist" title="Caricaturist">caricaturist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, novelist, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">balloonist</a> (or, more accurately, proponent of manned flight).</p>
<p>Photographic portraits by Nadar are held by many of the great national collections of photographs.</p>

<p>Nadar was born in April 1820 in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> (though some sources state <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>). His father, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Tournachon" title="Victor Tournachon">Victor Tournachon</a>, was a printer and bookseller. After his father's death, Nadar decided to quit his medical studies for economic reasons. Nadar started working as a caricaturist and novelist for various newspapers. He fell in with the Parisian bohemian group of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Nerval" title="Gérard de Nerval">Gérard de Nerval</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Baudelaire" title="Charles Baudelaire">Charles Baudelaire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_de_Banville" title="Théodore de Banville">Théodore de Banville</a>. His friends picked a nickname for him: Tournadar, which later became Nadar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archivesdefrance_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-archivesdefrance-2">[2]</a></sup> His work was published in <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Charivari" title="Le Charivari">Le Charivari</a></i> for the first time in 1848. In 1849, he founded the <i>Revue comique</i> and the <i>Petit journal pour rire</i>.</p>
<p>From work as a caricaturist, he moved on to photography, particularly portraits. He opened his photography studio in rue Saint Lazare in 1854 and moved to 35 Boulevard des Capucines in 1860. Nadar photographed a wide range of personalities: politicians (Guizot, Proudhon), stage actors (Sarah Bernhardt), writers (Hugo, Baudelaire, Sand, Nerval, Gautier, Dumas), painters (Corot, Delacroix, Millet), and musicians (Liszt, Rossini, Offenbach, Verdi, Berlioz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archivesdefrance_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-archivesdefrance-2">[2]</a></sup> Portrait photography was going through a period of native industrialization and Nadar refused to use the traditional sumptuous decors, preferred natural daylight and despised useless accessories. In 1886, with his son Paul, he did what may be the first photo-report: an interview with the great scientist Chevreul (who was then 100 years old). It was published in le <i>Journal Illustré</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archivesdefrance_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-archivesdefrance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He took his first photographs in 1853 and in 1855 opened a photographic studio at 25 <a href="/wiki/Boulevard_des_Capucines" title="Boulevard des Capucines">Boulevard des Capucines</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and in 1858 he became the first person to take <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_photograph" title="Aerial photograph">aerial photographs</a>. This was done using the <a href="/wiki/Collodion_process" title="Collodion process">wet plate collodion process</a>, and since the plates had to be prepared and developed while the balloon was aloft Nadar experienced problems caused by the chemical action on the plates of gas escaping from the balloon, eventually overcome by using a gas-proof cotton cover over the balloon basket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He also pioneered the use of artificial lighting in photography, working in the catacombs of Paris. He was the first person to photograph above ground with his balloons, as well as the first to photograph below ground, in the Parisian catacombs.</p>

<p>In 1863, Nadar commissioned the prominent balloonist <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Godard" title="Eugène Godard">Eugène Godard</a> to construct an enormous balloon, 60 metres (196 ft) high and with a capacity of 6,000 m<sup>3</sup> (210,000 cu ft), and named <i>Le Géant</i> (The Giant),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> thereby inspiring <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Five_Weeks_in_a_Balloon" title="Five Weeks in a Balloon">Five Weeks in a Balloon</a></i>. <i>Le Géant</i> was badly damaged at the end of its second flight, leading Nadar to the conviction that <a href="/wiki/Aircraft#Heavier-than-air_.E2.80.93_aerodynes" title="Aircraft">heavier-than-air machines</a> would be more successful. Later, "The Society for the Encouragement of Aerial Locomotion by Means of Heavier than Air Machines" was established, with Nadar as president and Verne as secretary. Nadar was the inspiration for the character of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michel_Ardan" title="Michel Ardan">Michael Ardan</a> in Verne's <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nadar_autoportrait_tournant.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Circa 1865: <i>"Revolving" self-portrait</i> by Nadar</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On his visit to <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> with <i>Le Géant</i>, on 26 September 1864, Nadar erected mobile barriers to keep the crowd at a safe distance. <a href="/wiki/Crowd_control_barrier" title="Crowd control barrier">Crowd control barriers</a> are still known in Belgium as <i>Nadar barriers</i>.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Paris_(1870-1871)" title="Siege of Paris (1870-1871)">Siege of Paris</a> in 1870–71 Nadar was instrumental in organising balloon flights carrying mail to reconnect the besieged Parisians with the rest of the world, thus establishing the world's first airmail service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 1874, he lent his photo studio to a group of painters to present the first exhibition of the <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionists</a>. He photographed <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a> on his death-bed in 1885. He is credited with having published (in 1886) the first <i>photo-interview</i> (of famous chemist <a href="/wiki/Michel_Eug%C3%A8ne_Chevreul" title="Michel Eugène Chevreul">Michel Eugène Chevreul</a>, then a centenarian), and also took <a href="/wiki/Erotic_photography" title="Erotic photography">erotic photographs</a>. From 1895 until his return to Paris in 1909, the Nadar photo studio was in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marseilles" title="Marseilles">Marseilles</a> (France).</p>
<p>Nadar died in 1910, aged 89. He was buried in <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_Lachaise_Cemetery" title="Père Lachaise Cemetery">Père Lachaise Cemetery</a> in Paris. The studio continued under the direction of his son and long-term collaborator, <b>Paul Nadar</b> (1856 - 1939).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nadar_(photographer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Towards the end of his life, Nadar published <i>Quand j’étais photographe,</i> which was translated into English and published by MIT press in 2015. The book is full of both anecdotes and samples of his photography, including many portraits of recognizable names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
