<p><b>Nara</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchu" title="Manchu">Manchu</a>: <span style="display:inline-block; font-weight:normal; font-family:'Menk Hawang Tig', 'Menk Qagan Tig', 'Menk Garqag Tig', 'Menk Har_a Tig', 'Menk Scnin Tig', 'Oyun Gurban Ulus Tig', 'Oyun Qagan Tig', 'Oyun Garqag Tig', 'Oyun Har_a Tig', 'Oyun Scnin Tig', 'Mongolian BT', 'Mongolian Baiti', 'Noto Sans Mongolian', 'Mongol Usug', 'Mongolian White', 'MongolianScript', 'Code2000', 'Menksoft Qagan'; font-size: 1.5em; line-height: 1em; writing-mode: vertical-lr; -webkit-writing-mode: vertical-lr; -o-writing-mode: vertical-lr; -ms-writing-mode: tb-lr; writing-mode: tb-lr; vertical-align:text-top;">ᠨᠠᡵᠠ ᡥᠠᠯᠠ</span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wade-Giles" title="Wade-Giles">Wade-Giles</a>: Nara hala, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: 納喇氏, 納蘭氏 or 那拉氏, also Nala, Nalan) is a clan name shared by a number of royal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchu" title="Manchu">Manchu</a> clans. The four tribes of the <a href="/wiki/Hulun_(alliance)" title="Hulun (alliance)">Hūlun confederation</a> (扈倫四部) -- Hada (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: 哈達, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Hādá), Ula (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: 烏拉, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Wūlā), Hoifa (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: 輝發, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Huīfā) and Yehe (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: 葉赫, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Yèhè) -- were all ruled by clans bearing this name.</p>
<p>The head of each clan held the princely title of "beile" (貝勒, Manchu: "chief, lord").</p>
<p><i>Nara</i> is the Mongolian word for 'sun'. In Mongolia, the sun is associated to <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a> as the nara <a href="/wiki/Tamga" title="Tamga">tamga</a> is the main tamga attributed to him.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan_Nara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Naras lived in the <a href="/wiki/Haixi_Jurchens" title="Haixi Jurchens">Haixi</a> area, which encompasses parts of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heilongjiang" title="Heilongjiang">Heilongjiang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liaoning" title="Liaoning">Liaoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>. The Hada Naras and Ula Naras are native to Manchuria and shared an ancestor. The Yehe Naras were founded by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%BCmed" title="Tümed">Tümed</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> who conquered the local Nara tribe and assumed their name, establishing his rule over the banks of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yehe_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yehe river (page does not exist)">Yehe river</a>. The Hoifa Naras, on the other hand, came from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ikderi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ikderi (page does not exist)">Ikderi</a> clan.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Nurhaci" title="Nurhaci">Nurhaci</a>'s efforts to unite the <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_people" title="Jurchen people">Jurchen people</a>, the Naras resisted because they had always been rather well-treated by the Ming government. Instead they tried to appease Nurhachi by offering him a daughter from each of the tribal rulers, the most famous of which were <a href="/wiki/Lady_Abahai" title="Lady Abahai">Lady Abahai</a> (阿巴亥) of the Ula tribe and <a href="/wiki/Empress_Xiaocigao_(Qing_dynasty)" title="Empress Xiaocigao (Qing dynasty)">Monggo</a> (孟古) of the Yehe tribe. Nonetheless, Nurhaci eventually began his assault against the Naras, and the Hada, Ula and Hoifa tribes soon fell. The Yehe Naras were able to resist the longest as they were the largest and strongest of the tribes, but even they soon had to enlist the help of the Ming empire.</p>
<p>Using how well-treated the Yehe Naras were by the Ming government as an excuse, Nurhaci began to wage war against the Ming forces as well. Both the Ming soldiers and the Yehe Naras were defeated in subsequent battles, including the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sarhu" title="Battle of Sarhu">Battle of Sarhu</a>, and the Yehe Nara prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jintaiji" title="Jintaiji">Jintaiji</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%87%91%E5%8F%B0%E5%90%89" title="zh:金台吉">金台吉</a>) was either forced to kill himself or hanged, but not before he allegedly cursed Nurhaci that as long as one of Jintaiji's descendants lived, even a female one, he or she would remember the clan's vendetta and bring down the <a href="/wiki/Aisin_Gioro" title="Aisin Gioro">Aisin Gioros</a>. The last prince of the Ula tribe <a href="/wiki/Bujantai" title="Bujantai">Bujantai</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%B8%83%E5%8D%A0%E6%B3%B0" title="zh:布占泰">布占泰</a>), who was fighting alongside the Yehe Naras, was captured as well and later killed by Nurhaci's son, <a href="/wiki/Cuyen" title="Cuyen">Cuyen</a>.</p>
<p>The Hada and Hoifa clans fell from prominence after Nurhaci's Manchurian conquest, whereas Ula and Yehe survived the defeat and successfully integrated into Qing's Banner aristocracy. They continued to be powerful clans in the Qing court, often named among the <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%BB%A1%E6%97%8F%E5%85%AB%E5%A4%A7%E5%A7%93" title="zh:满族八大姓">eight great Manchu houses</a>. Modern day Nara descendants mostly hail from these two clans.</p>
<p>Present-day descendants of the Nara clan generally adopted "Na" (那) and "Zhao" (趙) as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_name" title="Family name">family name</a>, so that it would be similar to the usually monosyllabic Han family names. Others, less commonly, took "Na" (納), "Nan" (南), "Liu" (劉), "Su" (蘇). Those descended from Hada Nara took "Wang" (王). Descendants of Yehe Nara primarily chose "Ye" (葉) or "He" (赫 or 何), others took after their <a href="/wiki/Borjigin" title="Borjigin">Borjigin</a> forebears and used "Bao" (鮑 or 包) or "Bo" (博).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wanyan_Nara">Wanyan Nara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan_Nara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wanyan Nara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nacibulu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nacibulu (page does not exist)">Nacibulu</a> (ca. 14th century) was the first to adapt the clan name Nara. A Jurchen clan native to Manchuria, they descended from the imperial <a href="/wiki/Wanyan" title="Wanyan">Wanyan</a> clan of the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(1115%E2%80%931234)" title="Jin dynasty (1115–1234)">Jin dynasty</a>.</p>
<p>The Nara gradually grew to become the dominant clan in the Haixi region, culminating in the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Hulun_(alliance)" title="Hulun (alliance)">Hūlun confederation</a> in the 16th century, with Nara princes at its core. At the same time, this Nara clan split into two branches: the senior Hada line founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kesina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kesina (page does not exist)">Kesina</a>, leader of the Hūlun confederation, and the junior Ula line founded by Kesina's younger brother 古對硃顏.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hada_Nara">Hada Nara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan_Nara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hada Nara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hada Nara (哈達那拉氏) ruled the Hada state, based around the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hada_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hada river (page does not exist)">Hada river</a> in southwestern Manchuria. It is native to Manchuria and kin to the Ula Nara.</p>
<p>Under the prince (beile) <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wang_Tai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wang Tai (page does not exist)">Wang Tai</a> (王台), the Hada Nara gained ascendency over the <a href="/wiki/Haixi_Jurchens" title="Haixi Jurchens">Haixi Jurchens</a>. He assumed the title Wan Khan (萬汗), and held hegemony in the <a href="/wiki/Hulun_(alliance)" title="Hulun (alliance)">Hūlun confederation</a>.</p>
<p>In 1574, Wang Tai captured the rebellious <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jianzhou_Jurchen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jianzhou Jurchen (page does not exist)">Jianzhou Jurchen</a> leader <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wanggao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wanggao (page does not exist)">Wanggao</a> (王杲), and was rewarded by the Ming court with the titles Right Pillar of State (右柱國, highest honorary civil title) and Dragon-Tiger General (龍虎將軍, highest honorary martial generaliship), further acknowledging the Hada supremecy in Haixi.</p>
<p>Upon Wangtai's death (1582), a struggle for succession ensued, sapping Hada of its strength and allowing the Yehe Nara and later Nurhaci to eclipse its power. In 1599, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Narimbulu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Narimbulu (page does not exist)">Narimbulu</a> (納林布祿) of Yehe invaded Hada. Weakened, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Menggebulu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Menggebulu (page does not exist)">Menggebulu</a> (孟格布祿, younger son of Wangtai and beile of Hada) requests aid from Nurhaci. Nurhaci sent two thousand troops led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fiongdon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fiongdon (page does not exist)">Fiongdon</a> (費英東) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gagai" title="Gagai">Gagai</a> (噶蓋). Fearing the rise of the Jianzhou Jurchens, Narimbulu in turn offered to ally with Menggebulu to defeat Nurhaci. Menggebulu accepted the offer, but the plot was leaked and Nurhaci attacked Hada instead.</p>
<p>Nurhaci's general <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yangguli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yangguli (page does not exist)">Yangguli</a> (揚古利) captured the Hada Castle and the ruling Hada Nara clan. Nurhaci spared Menggebulu and offered him an alliance, but Menggebulu again plotted to assassinate Nurhaci. This plot was also discovered, leading to his execution.</p>
<p>In 1601, Nurhaci married his daughter to Urgudai (吳爾古代, son of Menggebulu), who succeeded Menggebulu. The Ming court accused Nurhaci of attempting to annex Hada. In response, Nurhaci released Urgudai from Jianzhou and allowed him to return to rule Hada. Learning this, Narimbulu of Yehe again started raiding Hada. Severely weakened and defenseless, Urgudai eventually capitulated and submitted to Nurhaci's rule, becoming the last Beile of Hada.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ula_Nara">Ula Nara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan_Nara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ula Nara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ula Nara (烏拉那拉氏) ruled the Ula state, based around <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hulan_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hulan river (page does not exist)">Hulan river</a> in northeastern Manchuria. They shared <a href="/wiki/Wanyan" title="Wanyan">Wanyan</a> descent with the Hada Nara.</p>
<p>Of the four tribes, Ula was the economic and cultural powerhouse of Manchuria. The Ula tribe were mostly traders, buying <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a> from the steppe <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> and selling them at the Jianzhou (建州) plateau on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liao_river" title="Liao river">Liao river</a> basin, the economic center and farmland of the Manchu region. They in turn buy <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grains</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> at Jianzhou and sell them to the Mongols. The Ula Naras, for a large part, controlled trade between Manchuria and Mongolia by controlling the mountain pass at modern day <a href="/wiki/Baicheng" title="Baicheng">Baicheng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a>, where the only passage between the two areas was located.</p>
<p>The Nara chief Buyan (布顔) built the Ula Castle by the Hulan river and founded the Ula state. (Ula means "riverside" in Manchu.)</p>
<p>Ula and Jianzhou had numerous conflicts, culminating in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Mount_Gele&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battle of Mount Gele (page does not exist)">Battle of Mount Gele</a>. Defeated at Mount Gele, Mantai fled back to Ula but was killed by his subordinates 3 years later in 1596.</p>
<p>On the other hand, Mantai's younger brother, the Second Beile <a href="/wiki/Bujantai" title="Bujantai">Bujantai</a> (布占泰) was captured at Mount Gele. Bujantai submitted to Nurhaci and married both Nurhaci's and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgaci" title="Surgaci">Surgaci</a>'s daughters. Upon Mantai's death, Nurhaci aided Bujantai in defeating other Ula Nara pretenders to regain the Ula throne. The following year, he married his younger sister to Surgaci to formalise the alliance. Two years later, he again married Mantai's daughter <a href="/wiki/Lady_Abahai" title="Lady Abahai">Lady Abahai</a> to Nurhaci, who later became his primary consort.</p>
<p>The alliance between Ula and Jianzhou did not last, however. A <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Donghai_Jurchen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Donghai Jurchen (page does not exist)">Donghai Jurchen</a> tribe Warka, after repeated harassment by Bujantai, sought to submit to Nurhaci. Nurhaci sent troops to annex Warka, which Ula responded by intercepting Nurhaci's troops. The alliance broken, the two states resumed their conflicts. Eventually, Nurhaci captured Ula Castle and annexed the Ula state. Bujantai alone fled to Yehe, and spent the rest of his life under the protection of the Yehe Nara.</p>
<p>The descendents of the last Nara prince of Ula were mostly incorporated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plain_White_Banner" title="Plain White Banner">Plain White Banner</a>. They supplied numerous high officials and imperial consorts to the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing court</a> and are among the most prominent Manchu noble houses.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoifa_Nara">Hoifa Nara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan_Nara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hoifa Nara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hoifa Nara (輝發那拉氏) ruled the Hoifa state, based around the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hoifa_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hoifa river (page does not exist)">Hoifa river</a> in southeastern Manchuria. The Hoifa Nara descended from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ikderi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ikderi (page does not exist)">Ikderi</a> clan (益克得里氏). The Ikderi was a powerful native Jurchen clan. For two generations prior to assuming the Hoifa <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beile" title="Beile">beile</a> princeship, they had been appointed tributary military commanders (都督) loyal to the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming</a> court.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wangginu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wangginu (page does not exist)">Wangginu</a> (王機褚) was the first of the clan to assume the beile title. He built his castle on the Hurki Mountain, which provided him a secure power base. He established Hoifa as a major force in the Haixi region, and even withstood assaults by the Chahar Mongols.</p>
<p>Upon Wangginu's death, his grandson <a href="/wiki/Baindari" title="Baindari">Baindari</a> (拜音達里) seized the throne, killing seven of his uncles in the process. Hoifa was a major member of the coalition was defeated by Nurhaci at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Mount_Gele&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battle of Mount Gele (page does not exist)">Battle of Mount Gele</a> (古勒山之戰). Severely weakened and stuck between the ascendant Jianzhou and Yehe states, Baindari tried to play both sides against each other, and relying on the defensible Hoifa Castle for security. This policy further isolated Hoifa, and Hoifa Castle eventually fell to Nurhaci in 1607. Baindari and his sons were killed in the battle, ending the princely Hoifa Nara main line.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Yehe_Nara">Yehe Nara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clan_Nara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Yehe Nara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Yehe Nara (葉赫那拉氏) ruled the Yehe state, based around the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yehe_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yehe river (page does not exist)">Yehe river</a>. This area was originally called Zhang (張), occupied by the Hulun (扈倫) tribe. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Singgen_Darhan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Singgen Darhan (page does not exist)">Singgen Darhan</a> (星根達爾漢), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genghisid" title="Genghisid">Genghisid</a> prince of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%BCmed" title="Tümed">Tümed Mongol</a>, conquered the area and assumed the Nara name.</p>
<p>Initially, Yehe was relatively weak and was frequently raided by Hada. Conflict between Yehe and Hada continued until the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cinggiyanu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cinggiyanu (page does not exist)">Cinggiyanu</a> (清佳砮) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yangginu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yangginu (page does not exist)">Yangginu</a> (楊吉砮), who were sons of a younger brother of the beile <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Taicu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Taicu (page does not exist)">Taicu</a> (台杵). They expanded Yehe's territory through conquest of smaller neighbouring states, consolidated Yehe's powerbase with the construction of two castles, and made peace with Hada; Cinggiyanu married a daughter of Wangtai and Wangtai married Cinggiyanu's younger sister. With the support of Hada, Cinggiyanu and Yangginu successfully defeated the sons of Taicu and gained the throne of Yehe themselves.</p>
<p>From the rule of Cinggiyanu and Yangginu, the Yehe Nara had a unique system of co-princeship. Cinggiyanu and Yangginu built two castles on strategic locations only several <i><a href="/wiki/Li_(unit)" title="Li (unit)">li</a></i> apart. These were the West Yehe Castle and the East Yehe Castle, held by Cinggiyanu's and Yangginu's families respectively. The two co-princes were both equal beiles, ruled Yehe jointly, and acted in unity until the fall of Yehe.</p>
<p>Upon Wangtai's death, Yehe, along with Hoifa and Ula, broke away from Hada's hegemony. They allied to attack Hada, only to be defeated by the reinforcement from Ming. Cinggiyanu and Yangginu both died in this battle, and were succeeded by their sons <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bujai" title="Bujai">Bujai</a> (布寨) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Narimbulu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Narimbulu (page does not exist)">Narimbulu</a> (納林布祿) respectively.</p>
<p>Narimbulu allied with their Mongol Tümed and Khorchin kins to attack Hada again. This time Narimbulu managed to defeat the Ming reinforcement and destroyed Hada as a major power, becoming the most powerful of the Haixi tribes and the new leader of the Hulun confederation.</p>
<p>Seeing Nurhaci's rise, Yehe initially sought to make peace by marrying Narimbulu's sister Monggo Jeje (孟古哲哲) to Nurhaci (later <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Empress_Xiaocigao" title="Empress Xiaocigao">Empress Xiaocigao</a>). She would later give birth to <a href="/wiki/Hong_Taiji" title="Hong Taiji">Hong Taiji</a>, who would succeed Nurhaci and found the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>. This peace was short-lived, however, and Yehe soon entered a long struggle against Nurhaci's domination. Princes Bujai and Narimbulu lead the nine-tribe coalition against Jianzhou at the Battle of Mount Gele, which ended in decisive defeat. The Yehe state continued to resist the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Jin dynasty</a> until the fall of the East Yehe Castle, the last bastion to stand against Jin's Manchurian conquest.</p>
<p>The Yehe Nara is the most legendary of the Nara clans today, due in part to its status as the last Jurchen clan to challenge Nurhaci's hegemony, in part to the imperial favourites they issued, but most importantly to the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Dowager_Cixi" title="Empress Dowager Cixi">Empress Dowager Cixi</a>, who descended from a cadet branch of the East Yehe Nara belonging to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bordered_Blue_Banner" title="Bordered Blue Banner">Bordered Blue Banner</a>. The descendents of the princes of East Yehe were mostly incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Plain_Yellow_Banner" title="Plain Yellow Banner">Plain Yellow Banner</a>, cadet branch descending from Asi Darhan (阿什達爾漢, brother of Gin Taisi) were incorporated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plain_White_Banner" title="Plain White Banner">Plain White Banner</a>, while those of West Yehe were mostly incorporated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plain_Red_Banner" title="Plain Red Banner">Plain Red Banner</a>. They supplied numerous high officials and imperial consorts to the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing court</a> and are a fixture among the <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%BB%A1%E6%97%8F%E5%85%AB%E5%A4%A7%E5%A7%93" title="zh:满族八大姓">eight great Manchu houses</a>. In common usage, the <i>Nara clan</i> most often refers to the Yehe Nara.</p>
<p>A popular legend says that <a href="/wiki/Gintaisi" title="Gintaisi">Gin Taisi</a>, the last prince of East Yehe, upon defeat by Nurhaci, cursed that the Yehe Nara will be the downfall of the <a href="/wiki/Aisin_Gioro" title="Aisin Gioro">Aisin Gioro</a> clan, even if there's Yehe Nara daughters left. This curse was supposedly fulfilled with <a href="/wiki/Empress_Dowager_Cixi" title="Empress Dowager Cixi">Empress Dowager Cixi</a>'s disastrous reign that lead to the end of the Manchu dynasty.</p>
