<p><b>Narmer</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">king</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Dynastic_Period_of_Egypt" title="Early Dynastic Period of Egypt">Early Dynastic Period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkinson199967_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkinson199967-1">[1]</a></sup> Probably the successor to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protodynastic_Period_of_Egypt" title="Protodynastic Period of Egypt">Protodynastic</a> kings <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Scorpion" title="King Scorpion">Scorpion</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Ka_(pharaoh)" title="Ka (pharaoh)">Ka</a>, some consider him the unifier of Egypt and founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="First dynasty of Egypt">First Dynasty</a>, and in turn the first king of a unified Egypt.</p>
<p>Narmer's identity is the subject of ongoing debate, although mainstream Egyptological consensus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lloyd_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lloyd-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerv_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cerv-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeagy201459.E2.80.9392_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeagy201459.E2.80.9392-5">[5]</a></sup> identifies Narmer with the <a href="/wiki/First_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="First Dynasty of Egypt">First Dynasty</a> pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Menes" title="Menes">Menes</a>, who is also sometimes credited with the unification of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a>, as the first pharaoh. This conclusion is based on the Narmer Palette (which shows Narmer as the unifier of Egypt) and the two <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> seals from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umm_el-Qa%27ab" title="Umm el-Qa'ab">necropolis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> that show him as the first king of the First Dynasty.</p>
<p>The date commonly given for the beginning of his reign is c. 3100 BC. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHayes1970174_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHayes1970174-6">[6]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEQuirkeSpence1992223_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEQuirkeSpence1992223-7">[7]</a></sup> Other mainstream estimates using both the Historical Method and <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> are in the range 3273 - 2987 BC. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup></p>

<p>The famous <a href="/wiki/Narmer_Palette" title="Narmer Palette">Narmer Palette</a>, discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Quibell" title="James E. Quibell">James E. Quibell</a> in 1898 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierakonpolis" title="Hierakonpolis">Hierakonpolis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEQuibell189881.E2.80.9384.2C_pl._XII.E2.80.93XIII_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEQuibell189881.E2.80.9384.2C_pl._XII.E2.80.93XIII-9">[8]</a></sup> shows Narmer displaying the insignia of both <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>, giving rise to the theory that he unified the two kingdoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardiner1961403.E2.80.93404_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardiner1961403.E2.80.93404-10">[9]</a></sup> Since its discovery, it has been debated whether the Narmer Palette represents a historic event<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardiner1961403.E2.80.93404_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardiner1961403.E2.80.93404-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmery196141.E2.80.9343_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmery196141.E2.80.9343-11">[10]</a></sup> or was purely symbolic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaines1995117_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaines1995117-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEO.27Connor2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO.27Connor2011-13">[12]</a></sup> In 1993, however, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Dreyer" title="GÃ¼nter Dreyer">GÃ¼nter Dreyer</a> discovered in Abydos a year label of Narmer depicting the same event as that on the Narmer Palette which clearly shows that the Narmer Palette depicts an actual historic event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDreyer20006.E2.80.937_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDreyer20006.E2.80.937-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mainstream Egyptological consensus identifying Narmer with Menes is by no means universal. This has ramifications for the agreed <a href="/wiki/History_of_ancient_Egypt" title="History of ancient Egypt">history of ancient Egypt</a>. Some Egyptologists hold that Menes is the same person as <a href="/wiki/Hor-Aha" title="Hor-Aha">Hor-Aha</a> and that he inherited an already-unified Egypt from Narmer;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMidant-Reynes2000243.E2.80.9350_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMidant-Reynes2000243.E2.80.9350-15">[14]</a></sup> others hold that Narmer began the process of unification but either did not succeed or succeeded only partially, leaving it to Menes to complete. Arguments have been made that Narmer is <a href="/wiki/Menes" title="Menes">Menes</a> because of his appearance on a mud seal impression found in <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> in conjunction with the gameboard hieroglyph for "mn", which appears to be a contemporary record of the otherwise unattested king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardiner1961404.E2.80.935_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardiner1961404.E2.80.935-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another possible theory is that Narmer was an immediate successor to the king who did manage to unify Egypt (perhaps the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Scorpion" title="King Scorpion">King Scorpion</a> whose name was found on a macehead also discovered in Hierakonpolis), but he adopted symbols of unification that had already been in use for perhaps a generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchulman1991.E2.80.939285_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchulman1991.E2.80.939285-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two necropolis mud sealings listing kings recently found in the tombs of <a href="/wiki/Den_(pharaoh)" title="Den (pharaoh)">Den</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qa%27a" title="Qa'a">Qa'a</a> (both in Abydos) show Narmer as the founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Dynasty" title="First Dynasty">First Dynasty</a>, who was then followed by Hor-Aha. The Qa'a sealing shows all eight kings of the First Dynasty in the correct sequence beginning with Narmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECervell.C3.B3-Autuori200531.E2.80.9337_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECervell.C3.B3-Autuori200531.E2.80.9337-18">[17]</a></sup> Menes is not mentioned on either list of kings because at that time the name generally used on the monuments was the <a href="/wiki/Horus_name" title="Horus name">Horus name</a>, while Menes was a personal name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeidlmayer201025_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeidlmayer201025-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until recently, Narmer's wife was thought to have been <a href="/wiki/Neithhotep" title="Neithhotep">Neithhotep</a>, whose name means "<a href="/wiki/Neith" title="Neith">Neith</a> is satisfied". In this theory, she would have been a princess of Lower Egypt. Inscriptions bearing her name were found in tombs belonging to Narmer's immediate successors Hor-Aha as well as <a href="/wiki/Djer" title="Djer">Djer</a>, which Egyptologists took to suggest that she was the mother of Hor-Aha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETyldesley200626.E2.80.9329_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETyldesley200626.E2.80.9329-20">[19]</a></sup> However, the discovery in 2012 of rock inscriptions in Sinai during an expedition under the authority of Pierre Tallet raise questions about this theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[b]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomb_and_artifacts">Tomb and artifacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tomb and artifacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

Chambers B17 and B18 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umm_el-Qa%27ab" title="Umm el-Qa'ab">Umm el-Qa'ab</a> constitute the tomb of Narmer.</div>

<p>Narmer's tomb is composed of two joined chambers (B17 and B18) found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umm_el-Qa%27ab" title="Umm el-Qa'ab">Umm el-Qa'ab</a> locality, near <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> in Upper Egypt. It is located next to the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Ka_(pharaoh)" title="Ka (pharaoh)">Ka</a>, who likely ruled <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a> just before him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDreyer1999110.E2.80.9311_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDreyer1999110.E2.80.9311-22">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Narmer is well attested throughout Egypt, southern <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>, and Sinai: altogether 94 inscriptions at 26 sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[c]</a></sup> At every site except Coptos, it appears in a <i><a href="/wiki/Serekh" title="Serekh">serekh</a></i>. In Egypt his name has been found at 17 sites: four in Upper Egypt (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierakonpolis" title="Hierakonpolis">Hierakonpolis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEQuibell189881.E2.80.9384.2C_pl._XII.E2.80.93XIII_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEQuibell189881.E2.80.9384.2C_pl._XII.E2.80.93XIII-9">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Naqada" title="Naqada">Naqada</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpencer198064.28454.29.2C_pl._47.454.2C_pl.64.454_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpencer198064.28454.29.2C_pl._47.454.2C_pl.64.454-24">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetrie1900_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetrie1900-25">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetrie1901_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetrie1901-26">[23]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coptos" title="Coptos">Coptos</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams198835-50.2C_fig._3a_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams198835-50.2C_fig._3a-27">[24]</a></sup>), ten in Lower Egypt (<a href="/wiki/Tarkhan_(Egypt)" title="Tarkhan (Egypt)">Tarkhan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetrieWainwrightGardiner1913_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetrieWainwrightGardiner1913-28">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetrie1914_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetrie1914-29">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Helwan_(cemetery)" title="Helwan (cemetery)">Helwan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaad194726-27_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaad194726-27-30">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zawyet_el%27Aryan" title="Zawyet el'Aryan">Zawyet el'Aryan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDunham197825-26pl._16A_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDunham197825-26pl._16A-31">[28]</a></sup> Tell Ibrahim Awad,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_den_Brink199252-53_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_den_Brink199252-53-32">[29]</a></sup> Ezbet el-Tell,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBakr198850-51pl._1b_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBakr198850-51pl._1b-33">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Minshat_Abu_Omar" title="Minshat Abu Omar">Minshat Abu Omar</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWildung198135-37_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWildung198135-37-34">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saqqara" title="Saqqara">Saqqara</a> ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELacauLauer19591-2.2C_pl._1.1_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELacauLauer19591-2.2C_pl._1.1-35">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Buto" title="Buto">Buto</a> ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvon_der_Way1989285-286n.76.2C_fig._11.7_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvon_der_Way1989285-286n.76.2C_fig._11.7-36">[33]</a></sup> Tell el-Farkhan <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJucha2008132-133.2C_fig_47.2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJucha2008132-133.2C_fig_47.2-37">[34]</a></sup> and Kafr Hassan Dawood <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHassan200039_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHassan200039-38">[35]</a></sup>), and one in the Eastern Desert (Wadi el-Qaash<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWinkler193810.2C25pl.11.1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWinkler193810.2C25pl.11.1-39">[36]</a></sup>), and two in the Western Desert (<a href="/wiki/Kharga_Oasis" title="Kharga Oasis">Kharga Oasis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIkramRossi2004211-215fig_1_.26_2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIkramRossi2004211-215fig_1_.26_2-40">[37]</a></sup> and Gebel Tjauti<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDarnellDarnell199771-72fig._10_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDarnellDarnell199771-72fig._10-41">[38]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>During Narmer's reign, Egypt had an active economic presence in southern Canaan. Pottery <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">sherds</a> have been discovered at several sites, both from pots made in Egypt and imported to Canaan and others made in the Egyptian style out of local materials. The latter discovery has led to the conclusion that Egypt's presence in Canaan was in the form of a colony rather than just the result of trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorat1986.E2.80.9387119_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorat1986.E2.80.9387119-42">[39]</a></sup> While Egypt's presence in Canaan has been explained as the result of a military invasion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYadin19551-16_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYadin19551-16-43">[40]</a></sup> this view is not generally accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampagno2008695.E2.80.9396_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampagno2008695.E2.80.9396-44">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorat1986.E2.80.9387109_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorat1986.E2.80.9387109-45">[42]</a></sup> Fortifications at Tel es-Sakan dating to this period and almost entirely Egyptian in construction suggest a military presence, if not a military invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_Miroschedji20082028.E2.80.9329_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Miroschedji20082028.E2.80.9329-46">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The extent of Egyptian activity in southern Canaan is shown by the discovery of 33 <i>serekhs</i> on pottery sherds at sites in Canaan dating from the <a href="/wiki/Naqada_III" title="Naqada III">Protodynastic Period</a> to the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Dynasty" title="First Dynasty">First Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87201131_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87201131-47">[44]</a></sup> Fifteen of these belong to Narmer, and came from eight different sites: <a href="/wiki/Tel_Arad" title="Tel Arad">Tel Arad</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAmiran19744-12.2C_fig._20.2C_pl.1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAmiran19744-12.2C_fig._20.2C_pl.1-48">[45]</a></sup> En Besor (<a href="/wiki/Ein_HaBesor" title="Ein HaBesor">Ein HaBesor</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchulman197625-26_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchulman197625-26-49">[46]</a></sup> Tel es-Sakan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_MiroschedjiSadeq2000136-137fig._9_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_MiroschedjiSadeq2000136-137fig._9-50">[47]</a></sup> Nahal Tillah (Halif Terrace),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevyvan_den_BrinkGorenAlon199731-33_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevyvan_den_BrinkGorenAlon199731-33-51">[48]</a></sup> Tel Erani (Tel Gat),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYeivin1960193-203fig._2.2C_pl._24a_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYeivin1960193-203fig._2.2C_pl._24a-52">[49]</a></sup> Small Tel Malhata,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAmiranIlanAron198375-83fig.7c_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAmiranIlanAron198375-83fig.7c-53">[50]</a></sup> Tel Ma'ahaz, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchulmanGophna1981_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchulmanGophna1981-54">[51]</a></sup> and Tel <a href="/wiki/Lod" title="Lod">Lod</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_den_BrinkBraun2002167-192_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_den_BrinkBraun2002167-192-55">[52]</a></sup> An additional <i>serekh</i> from Tel Lod is attributed to Narmer's probable predecessor, Ka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJim.C3.A9nez-Serrano2007370.2C_Table_8_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJim.C3.A9nez-Serrano2007370.2C_Table_8-56">[53]</a></sup> Significantly only one is attributable to Narmer's successors, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hor_Aha" title="Hor Aha">Hor Aha</a>, his immediate successor. The remainder of the <i>serekhs</i> either have no name on them or have a name not attributable to any known pharaoh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87201131_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87201131-47">[44]</a></sup> Narmer's <i>serekh</i>, along with those of other Predynastic and Early Dynastic kings, has been found at the Wadi 'Ameyra in the southern Sinai, where inscriptions commemorate Egyptian mining expeditions to the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETalletLaisney2012383.E2.80.93389_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETalletLaisney2012383.E2.80.93389-57">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the summer of 1994, excavators from the Nahal Tillah expedition, in southern <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, discovered an incised ceramic <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">sherd</a> with the <i>serekh</i> sign of Narmer. The sherd was found on a large circular platform, possibly the foundations of a storage silo on the Halif Terrace. Dated to c. 3000 BCE, mineralogical studies of the sherd conclude that it is a fragment of a wine jar which had been imported from the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> valley to <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevyvan_den_BrinkGorenAlon199526.E2.80.9335_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevyvan_den_BrinkGorenAlon199526.E2.80.9335-58">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>After about 200 years of active presence in Canaan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87199572_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87199572-59">[56]</a></sup> Egyptian presence peaked during Narmer's reign and quickly declined after that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87201131_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAn.C4.91elkovi.C4.87201131-47">[44]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Establishing absolute dating for Ancient Egypt relies on two different methods, each of which is problematic. Using the Historical Method, an astronomical event that is recorded in Ancient Egyptian texts is used as a starting point, and then the date of the king in question is determined by âdead reckoningâ â adding or subtracting the length of each kingâs reign (based primarily on <a href="/wiki/Manetho" title="Manetho">Manetho</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Turin_King_List" title="Turin King List">Turin King List</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Palermo_Stone" title="Palermo Stone">Palermo Stone</a>) until you get to the reign of the king in question. Of course, there is uncertainty about the length of reigns, especially in the Intermediate Periods and the <a href="/wiki/Early_Dynastic_Period_(Egypt)" title="Early Dynastic Period (Egypt)">Archaic Period</a>. Two astrological events are available to anchor the estimates, one in the <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">NewKingdom</a>, and one in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a>. ( for a discussion of the problems in establishing absolute dates for Ancient Egypt, see <a href="#CITEREFShaw2000">Shaw 2000</a>, pp.Â 1-16). Two estimates based on this method are: <a href="#CITEREFHayes1970">Hayes 1970</a>, p.Â 174, which gives the beginning of the reign of Narmer/Menes as 3114 BC which he rounds to 3100 BC; and, <a href="#CITEREFKraussWarburton2006">Krauss &amp; Warburton 2006</a>, p.Â 487 which gives that date as 2950 BC. Several estimates of the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/First_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="First Dynasty of Egypt">First Dynasty</a> assume that it began with <a href="/wiki/Hor-Aha" title="Hor-Aha">Hor-Aha</a>. Setting aside the question of whether the First Dynasty began with Narmer or Hor-Aha, these calculations, must be adjusted by the length of Narmerâs reign in order to produce an estimate of the date of the beginning of Narmerâs reign. Unfortunately, we have no reliable estimate of the length of Narmerâs reign. The best we can do is use Manethoâs estimate of the length of the reign of <a href="#CITEREFManetho">Manetho</a>, p.Â 29 (Africanus) of 62 years based on the assumption that Narmer and Menes are the same person. The date for the beginning of Narmerâs reign is, thus, 62 years earlier than the date given by the authors who assume the beginning of the First Dynasty is the start of Hor-Ahaâs reign. Estimates of the beginning of Narmerâs reign calculated in this way include <a href="#CITEREFvon_Beckerath1997">von Beckerath 1997</a>, p.Â 179 (3094-3044 BC); <a href="#CITEREFHelck1986">Helck 1986</a>, p.Â 28 (2987 BC); <a href="#CITEREFKitchen2000">Kitchen 2000</a>, p.Â 48 (3092 BC), and <a href="#CITEREFShaw2000">Shaw 2000</a>, p.Â 480 (3062). Considering all six estimates gives us a range of 3114 â 2987 BC based on the Historical Method. The exception to the mainstream consensus, is <a href="#CITEREFMellaart1979">Mellaart 1979</a>, pp.Â 9-10 who estimates the beginning of the First Dynasty at 3400 BC. However, he reached this conclusion by disregarding the Middle Kingdom astronomical date. His conclusion is not widely accepted. Radiocarbon dating sounds scientific, but also has its problems. According to <a href="#CITEREFHendrickx2006">Hendrickx 2006</a>, p.Â 90, âFurthermore the calibration curves for the (second half) of the 4th millennium BC show important fluctuations with long possible data ranges as a consequence. It is generally considered a âbad periodâ for Radiocarbon dating.â Using a statistical approach, including all available carbon 14 dates for the Archaic Period, reduces, but does not eliminate, the inherent problems. <a href="#CITEREFDeeet_al.">Dee &amp; et al.</a>, 2013,Table 1, use such an approach and derive a 65Â % confidence interval estimate for the beginning of the First Dynasty of 3211 â 3045 BC. However, they define the beginning of the First Dynasty as the beginning of the reign of Hor-Aha. There are no Radiocarbon dates for Narmer, so to translate this to the beginning of Narmerâs reign we must again adjust for the length of Narmerâs reign of 62 years, to get to the beginning of Narmerâs reign, which gives us a range of 3273-3107 BC. This is reassuringly close to the range of mainstream Egyptologists using the Historical Method of 3114 - 2987 BC. Combining the results of two different methodologies, we get a range for the accession on Narmer of 3273 - 2987 BC.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In 2012, Pierre Tallet discovered an important new series of rock carvings in Wadi âAmeyro in the Sinai.
