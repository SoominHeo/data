<p><b>Narva</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Нарва</span>) is the third largest city in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>. It is located at the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_Estonia" title="Extreme points of Estonia">eastern extreme point of Estonia</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> border, on the <a href="/wiki/Narva_River" title="Narva River">Narva River</a> which drains <a href="/wiki/Lake_Peipus" title="Lake Peipus">Lake Peipus</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People settled in the area from the 5th to 4th millennium BC, as witnessed by the archeological traces of the <a href="/wiki/Narva_culture" title="Narva culture">Narva culture</a>, named after the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The fortified settlement at Narva Joaoru is the oldest known in Estonia, dated to around 1000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The earliest written reference of Narva is in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Novgorod_Chronicle" title="First Novgorod Chronicle">First Novgorod Chronicle</a>, which in the year 1172 describes a district in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a> called <i>Nerevsky</i> or <i>Narovsky konets</i> (yard). According to historians, this name derives from the name of Narva or Narva River and indicates that a frequently used trade route went through Narva, although there is no evidence of the existence of a trading settlement at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The favourable location at the crossing of trade routes and the <a href="/wiki/Narva_River" title="Narva River">Narva River</a> was behind the founding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narva_castle" title="Narva castle">Narva castle</a> and the development of an urban settlement around it. The castle was founded during the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Estonia" title="Danish Estonia">Danish rule of northern Estonia</a> during the second half of the 13th century, the earliest written record of the castle is from 1277.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kivimae_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kivimae-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Narvia</i> village is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Census_Book" title="Danish Census Book">Danish Census Book</a> already in 1241. A town developed around the stronghold and in 1345 obtained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_City_Rights" title="Lübeck City Rights">Lübeck City Rights</a> from Danish king <a href="/wiki/Valdemar_IV_of_Denmark" title="Valdemar IV of Denmark">Valdemar IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-museum-5">[5]</a></sup> The castle and surrounding town of Narva became a possession of the <a href="/wiki/Livonian_Order" title="Livonian Order">Livonian Order</a> in 1346, after the Danish king sold its lands in Northern Estonia. In 1492 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivangorod_fortress" title="Ivangorod fortress">Ivangorod fortress</a> across the Narva River was established by <a href="/wiki/Ivan_III_of_Russia" title="Ivan III of Russia">Ivan III</a> of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Moscow" title="Grand Duchy of Moscow">Moscow</a>.</p>
<p>Trade, particularly <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic</a> long distance trade remained Narva's <i>raison d'être</i> throughout the Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kivimae_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-kivimae-4">[4]</a></sup> However, due to opposition from <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>, Narva itself never became part of the Hanseatic League and also remained a very small town – its population in 1530 is estimated at 600–750 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kivimae_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-kivimae-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swedish_and_Russian_rule">Swedish and Russian rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Swedish and Russian rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Captured by the Russians during the <a href="/wiki/Livonian_War" title="Livonian War">Livonian War</a> in 1558, for a short period Narva became an important port and trading city for <a href="/wiki/Tsardom_of_Russia" title="Tsardom of Russia">Russia</a>, trans-shipping goods from <a href="/wiki/Pskov" title="Pskov">Pskov</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a>. Russian rule ended in 1581 when the Swedes under the command of <a href="/wiki/Pontus_De_la_Gardie" title="Pontus De la Gardie">Pontus De la Gardie</a> conquered the city and it became part of <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Sweden</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Swedish_War_(1590%E2%80%931595)" title="Russo-Swedish War (1590–1595)">Russo-Swedish War (1590–1595)</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Arvid_St%C3%A5larm_the_Younger" title="Arvid Stålarm the Younger">Arvid Stålarm</a> was governor, Russian forces attempted to re-gain the city without success (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Teusina" title="Treaty of Teusina">Treaty of Teusina</a>, May 1595).</p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Estonia_under_Swedish_rule" title="Estonia under Swedish rule">Swedish rule</a> the Old Town of Narva was built. Following a big fire in 1659, which almost completely destroyed the town, only stone buildings were allowed to be built in the central part of the town. Incomes from flourishing trade allowed rebuilding of the town center in two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-museum-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">baroque</a> Old Town underwent practically no changes until World War II and became in later centuries quite famous all over Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Towards the end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Estonia" title="Swedish Estonia">Swedish rule</a> the defence structures of Narva were greatly improved – beginning in 1680s, an outstanding system of <a href="/wiki/Bastion" title="Bastion">bastions</a>, planned by the renowned Swedish military engineer <a href="/wiki/Erik_Dahlbergh" title="Erik Dahlbergh">Erik Dahlbergh</a>, was built around the town. The new defence structures were among the most powerful in Northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-museum-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a> of 1700-1721, Narva became the setting for <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Narva_(1700)" title="Battle of Narva (1700)">the first great battle</a> between the forces of King <a href="/wiki/Charles_XII_of_Sweden" title="Charles XII of Sweden">Charles XII of Sweden</a> and Tsar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Russia" title="Peter I of Russia">Peter I of Russia</a> (November 1700). Although outnumbered four to one, the Swedish forces routed their 40,000-strong opponent. <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> subsequently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Narva_(1704)" title="Battle of Narva (1704)">conquered</a> the city in 1704.</p>
<p>After the war the bastions were renovated. Narva remained in the list of Russian fortifications until 1863, though there was no real military need for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-museum-5">[5]</a></sup> During Russian rule Narva formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_Governorate" title="Saint Petersburg Governorate">Saint Petersburg Governorate</a>.</p>

<p>In the middle of the 19th century, Narva started to develop into a major industrial town. <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Knoop" title="Ludwig Knoop">Ludwig Knoop</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Krenholm_Manufacturing_Company" title="Krenholm Manufacturing Company">Krenholm Manufacturing Company</a> in 1857. The factory could use the cheap energy of the powerful Narva waterfalls and at the end of the century became, with about 10,000 workers, one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Cotton_mill" title="Cotton mill">cotton mills</a> in Europe and the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1872, Krenholm Manufacturing became the site of the first <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strike</a> in Estonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raun_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-raun-7">[7]</a></sup> At the end of the 19th century, Narva was the leading industrial town in Estonia – 41% of industrial workers in Estonia worked in Narva, compared to 33% in <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raun_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-raun-7">[7]</a></sup> The first railway in Estonia, completed in 1870, connected Narva to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> and to Tallinn.</p>
<p>In August 1890 Narva was the site of a key meeting between German Kaiser <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II" title="Wilhelm II">Wilhelm II</a> and Russian Tsar <a href="/wiki/Alexander_III_of_Russia" title="Alexander III of Russia">Alexander III</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The status of Narva was finally resolved in a July 1917 referendum, when the district population, now roughly equally divided between ethnic Russians and Estonians, voted to attach itself to the newly autonomous and soon to be independent republic of Estonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batt_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-batt-8">[8]</a></sup> Narva became part of an independent Estonia in 1918, at the end of World War I. The town saw fighting during the <a href="/wiki/Estonian_War_of_Independence" title="Estonian War of Independence">Estonian War of Independence</a>. The war started in Narva on 28 November 1918, on the next day the city was captured by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>. Russia retained control of the city until 19 January 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Heavy battles occurred in and around Narva in World War II. The city was damaged in the German invasion of 1941 and by smaller air raids throughout the war, but remained relatively intact until February 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kattago_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-kattago-10">[10]</a></sup> However, as the focus of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Narva_(1944)" title="Battle of Narva (1944)">Battle of Narva</a>, the city was destroyed by Soviet bombardment and fires and explosions set by retreating German troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The most devastating action was the bombing raids of 6th and 7 March 1944 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Force" title="Soviet Air Force">Soviet Air Force</a>, which destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> old town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-museum-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kattago_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-kattago-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of July 98% of Narva had been destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kattago_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-kattago-10">[10]</a></sup> After the war, most of the buildings could have been restored as the walls of the houses still existed, but in early 1950s the Soviet authorities decided to demolish the ruins to make room for apartment buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kattago_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-kattago-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faure_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-faure-12">[12]</a></sup> Only three buildings remain of the old town, including the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a>-style Town Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The civilian casualties of the bombing were low as the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">German forces</a> had evacuated the city in January 1944.</p>
<p>The original native inhabitants were not allowed to return to Narva after the war and immigrant Russian-speaking workers from other parts of the USSR were brought in to populate the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kattago_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-kattago-10">[10]</a></sup> The main reason behind this was a plan to build a secret <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> processing plant in the city, which would turn Narva into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_town" title="Closed town">closed town</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faure_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-faure-12">[12]</a></sup> Although already in 1947 nearby <a href="/wiki/Sillam%C3%A4e" title="Sillamäe">Sillamäe</a> was selected as the location of the factory instead of Narva, the existence of such a plan was decisive for the development of Narva in the first post-war years and thus also shaped its later evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vseviov_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-vseviov-14">[14]</a></sup> The planned uranium factory and other large-scale industrial developments, like the restoring of Kreenholm Manufacture, were the driving force behind the influx of internal migrants from other parts of the Soviet Union, mainly Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vseviov_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-vseviov-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1945 <a href="/wiki/Ivangorod" title="Ivangorod">Ivangorod</a>, a town across the river which was founded in 1492 by Tsar <a href="/wiki/Ivan_III_of_Russia" title="Ivan III of Russia">Ivan III of Russia</a>, was separated from Narva and was made part of the <a href="/wiki/Leningrad_Oblast" title="Leningrad Oblast">Leningrad Oblast</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_SFSR" title="Russian SFSR">Russian SFSR</a>. Ivangorod received the official status of town in 1954.</p>

<p>When Estonia <a href="/wiki/History_of_Estonia#Regaining_independence" title="History of Estonia">regained its independence</a> in 1991, Narva again became a border city. Local leaders, holdovers from the Soviet era, wanted autonomy, and contended that the notion of a breakaway 'Transnarovan SSR' republic in <a href="/wiki/Ida-Viru_County" title="Ida-Viru County">northeastern Estonia</a> was becoming increasingly popular, but this was contradicted by polls showing 87% of the region's population opposed secession from Estonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batt_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-batt-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1993, dissatisfaction with newly enacted <a href="/wiki/Estonian_nationality_law" title="Estonian nationality law">citizenship</a> and election laws (non-citizens were not allowed to hold office) culminated in the Narva <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> of 16–17 July 1993, which proposed autonomy for Narva and <a href="/wiki/Sillam%C3%A4e" title="Sillamäe">Sillamäe</a>, a nearby town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batt_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-batt-8">[8]</a></sup> 97% voted in favor of the referendum, though turnout in Narva was 55% and there were credible charges of vote rigging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batt_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-batt-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1991 there have also been disputes about the Estonian-Russian border in the Narva area, as the new <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Estonia" title="Constitution of Estonia">constitution of Estonia</a> (adopted in 1992) recognizes the 1920 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tartu_(Russian%E2%80%93Estonian)" title="Treaty of Tartu (Russian–Estonian)">Treaty of Tartu</a> border to be currently legal. The Russian Federation, however, considers Estonia to be a successor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estonian_SSR" title="Estonian SSR">Estonian SSR</a> and recognizes the 1945 border between two former national republics. Officially, Estonia has no territorial claims in the area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> which was also reflected in the new Estonian-Russian border treaty signed in Moscow in May 18, 2005. Russia didn't ratify it because together with the ratification Estonian parliament approved a communiqué which reminded of Soviet Occupation. In February 18, 2014 a new border treaty was signed by both countries. The treaty must still be ratified by the parliaments of both Russia and Estonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 1 January 2013 Narva's population was 62,078, down from 64,667 inhabitants two years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-narva2013_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-narva2013-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nf07_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-nf07-20">[20]</a></sup> The population was 83,000 in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nf07_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-nf07-20">[20]</a></sup> 93.85% of the current population of Narva are Russian-speakers, and 82% are ethnic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estonian_Russians" title="Estonian Russians">Russians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nf07_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-nf07-20">[20]</a></sup> Most non-Estonians are ethnically Russian, Belarusian or Ukrainian immigrants or the children of immigrants, though 69% of Narva residents in the early 1990s had been born in Narva or had lived there for more than 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batt_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-batt-8">[8]</a></sup> Ethnic <a href="/wiki/Estonians" title="Estonians">Estonians</a> accounted for 3.86% of total population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nf07_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-nf07-20">[20]</a></sup> Much of the city was destroyed during World War II and for several years during the following reconstruction the Soviet authorities largely prohibited return of Narva's pre-war residents (among whom ethnic Estonians had been the majority, forming 64.8% of the town's population of 23,512 according to the 1934 census<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>), thus radically altering the city's ethnic composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raun_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-raun-7">[7]</a></sup> Nevertheless, ethnic Russians had already formed a significant minority: 29.7% of the city's population were Russian in the census of 1934.</p>
<p>46.7% of the city's inhabitants are Estonian citizens, 36.3% are citizens of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, while 15.3% of the population has <a href="/wiki/Estonian_nationality_law#Undefined_citizenship" title="Estonian nationality law">undefined citizenship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-narva2013_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-narva2013-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>A concern in Narva is the spread of HIV, which infected 1.2% of Estonia's population in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Between 2001 and 2008, more than 1600 cases of HIV were registered in Narva, making it one of the worst areas in Estonia, alongside <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a> and the rest of <a href="/wiki/Ida-Viru_County" title="Ida-Viru County">Ida-Viru County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The HIV infection rate in Estonia declined in 2014, with 59 new cases in Narva.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Narva is situated in the eastern extreme point of Estonia, 200 km (124 mi) to the east from the Estonian capital Tallinn and 130 km (81 mi) southwest from Saint Petersburg. The capital of Ida-Viru County, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B5hvi" title="Jõhvi">Jõhvi</a>, lies 50 km (31 mi) to the west. The eastern border of the city along the <a href="/wiki/Narva_River" title="Narva River">Narva River</a> coincides with the Estonian-Russian border. The Estonian part of the <a href="/wiki/Narva_Reservoir" title="Narva Reservoir">Narva Reservoir</a> lies mostly within the territory of Narva, to the southwest of city center. The mouth of the Narva River to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a> is about 13 km (8 mi) downstream from the city.</p>
<p>The territory of Narva is 84.54 square kilometres (32.64 sq mi). The city proper has an area of 62 square kilometres (24 sq mi) (excluding the reservoir), while two separate districts surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Vaivara_Parish" title="Vaivara Parish">Vaivara Parish</a>, Kudruküla and Olgina, cover 5.6 km<sup>2</sup> (2.16 sq mi) and 0.58 km<sup>2</sup> (0.22 sq mi), respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Kudruküla is the largest of Narva's <a href="/wiki/Dacha" title="Dacha">dacha</a> regions, located 6 km (4 mi) to northwest from the main city, near <a href="/wiki/Narva-J%C3%B5esuu" title="Narva-Jõesuu">Narva-Jõesuu</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Castle" title="Hermann Castle">Hermann Castle</a></div>
<p>Narva is dominated by the 15th-century castle, with the 51-metre-high <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Hermann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Long Hermann (page does not exist)">Long Hermann</a> tower as its most prominent landmark. The sprawling complex of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kreenholm_Manufacture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kreenholm Manufacture (page does not exist)">Kreenholm Manufacture</a>, located in the proximity of scenic waterfalls, is one of the largest textile mills of 19th-century Northern Europe. Other notable buildings include Swedish mansions of the 17th century, a Baroque town hall (1668–71), and remains of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erik_Dahlberg" title="Erik Dahlberg">Erik Dahlberg</a>'s fortifications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Across the Narva River is the Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivangorod_fortress" title="Ivangorod fortress">Ivangorod fortress</a>, founded by Grand Prince <a href="/wiki/Ivan_III_of_Russia" title="Ivan III of Russia">Ivan III of Muscovy</a> in 1492 and known in Western sources as <i>Counter-Narva</i>. During the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> times Narva and <a href="/wiki/Ivangorod" title="Ivangorod">Ivangorod</a> were <a href="/wiki/Twin_cities" title="Twin cities">twin cities</a>, despite belonging to different republics. Before World War II, <a href="/wiki/Ivangorod" title="Ivangorod">Ivangorod</a> (<a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>: <span lang="et" xml:lang="et"><i>Jaanilinn</i></span>) was administrated as part of Narva.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Narva_Kreenholmi_Stadium" title="Narva Kreenholmi Stadium">Narva Kreenholmi Stadium</a> is home to <a href="/wiki/Meistriliiga" title="Meistriliiga">Meistriliiga</a> football team, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FC_Narva_Trans" title="FC Narva Trans">FC Narva Trans</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Narva is on the international railway line between Estonia and Russia. There is one daily international passenger train linking the two countries; the overnight train between Moscow via St Petersburg to Tallinn calls at Narva.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There are also three daily domestic trains between Narva and Tallinn operated by <a href="/wiki/Elron_(rail_transit)" title="Elron (rail transit)">Elron</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Close to the city there is a general aviation grass airfield (ICAO: EENA)</p>
