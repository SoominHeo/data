<p>The <b>Namib</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_desert" title="Coastal desert">coastal desert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern Africa</a>. The name <i>Namib</i> is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nama_language" title="Nama language">Nama</a> origin and means "vast place". According to the broadest definition, the Namib stretches for more than 2,000 kilometres (1,200 mi) along the Atlantic coasts of <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, extending southward from the Carunjamba River in Angola, through Namibia and to the <a href="/wiki/Olifants_River_(Western_Cape)" title="Olifants River (Western Cape)">Olifants River</a> in Western Cape, South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gates-Appiah_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-Appiah-2">[2]</a></sup> The Namib's northernmost portion, which extends 450 kilometres (280 mi) from the Angola-Namibia border, is known as <a href="/wiki/Mo%C3%A7%C3%A2medes_Desert" title="Moçâmedes Desert">Moçâmedes Desert</a>, while its southern portion approaches the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Kalahari_Desert" title="Kalahari Desert">Kalahari Desert</a>. From the Atlantic coast eastward, the Namib gradually ascends in elevation, reaching up to 200 kilometres (120 mi) inland to the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Escarpment,_Southern_Africa" title="Great Escarpment, Southern Africa">Great Escarpment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup> Annual precipitation ranges from 2 millimetres (0.079 in) in the most arid regions to 200 millimetres (7.9 in) at the escarpment, making the Namib the only true desert in southern Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gates-Appiah_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-Appiah-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spriggs_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spriggs-3">[3]</a></sup> Having endured <a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">arid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid</a> conditions for roughly 55–80 million years, the Namib may be the oldest desert in the world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spriggs_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spriggs-3">[3]</a></sup> and contains some of the world's driest regions.</p>
<p>The desert geology consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_sea" title="Sand sea">sand seas</a> near the coast, while gravel plains and scattered mountain outcrops occur further inland. The sand dunes, some of which are 300 metres (980 ft) high and span 32 kilometres (20 mi) long, are the second largest in the world after the <a href="/wiki/Badain_Jaran_Desert" title="Badain Jaran Desert">Badain Jaran Desert</a> dunes in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup> Temperatures along the coast are stable and generally range between 9–20 °C (48–68 °F) annually, while temperatures further inland are variable—summer daytime temperatures can exceed 45 °C (113 °F) while nights can be freezing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson2011-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">Fogs</a> that originate offshore from the collision of the cold <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a> and warm air from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Cell" title="Hadley Cell">Hadley Cell</a> create a fog belt that frequently envelops parts of the desert. Coastal regions can experience more than 180 days of thick fog a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spriggs_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spriggs-3">[3]</a></sup> While this has proved a major hazard to ships—more than a thousand wrecks litter the <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_Coast" title="Skeleton Coast">Skeleton Coast</a>—it is a vital source of moisture for desert life.</p>
<p>The Namib is almost completely uninhabited by humans except for several small settlements and indigenous pastoral groups, including the <a href="/wiki/Himba_people" title="Himba people">Ovahimba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herero_people" title="Herero people">Obatjimba Herero</a> in the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Nama_people" title="Nama people">Topnaar Nama</a> in the central region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gates-Appiah_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-Appiah-2">[2]</a></sup> Owing to its antiquity, the Namib may be home to more <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic species</a> than any other desert in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson2011_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson2011-4">[4]</a></sup> Most of the desert wildlife is <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and other small animals that live on little water, although larger animals inhabit the northern regions. Near the coast, the cold ocean water is rich in fishery resources and supports populations of <a href="/wiki/Brown_fur_seal" title="Brown fur seal">brown fur seals</a> and shorebirds, which serve as prey for the Skeleton Coast's <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson2011_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson2011-4">[4]</a></sup> Further inland, the <a href="/wiki/Namib-Naukluft_National_Park" title="Namib-Naukluft National Park">Namib-Naukluft National Park</a>, the largest game park in Africa, supports populations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Bush_Elephant" title="African Bush Elephant">African Bush Elephants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_Zebra" title="Mountain Zebra">Mountain Zebras</a>, and other large mammals. Although the outer Namib is largely barren of vegetation, <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Succulent_plant" title="Succulent plant">succulents</a> are found in coastal areas, while grasses, shrubs, and <a href="/wiki/Ephemeral_plant" title="Ephemeral plant">ephemeral plants</a> thrive near the <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a>. A few types of trees are also able to survive the extremely arid climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson2011_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson2011-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Namib Desert is one of the 500 distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_Geography" title="Physical Geography">physiographic</a> provinces of the South African <a href="/wiki/Platform_(geology)" title="Platform (geology)">Platform</a> physiographic division. It occupies an area of around 80,950 km²<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (31,200 square miles), stretching from the[Usiab River (north) to the town of <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCderitz" title="Lüderitz">Lüderitz</a> (south) and from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> (west) to the Namib Escarpment (east). It is about 1,000 miles (1,600 km) long from north to south and its east-west width varies from 30 to 100 miles (48 to 161 kilometres). To the north, the desert leads into the <a href="/wiki/Kaokoveld" title="Kaokoveld">Kaokoveld</a>; the dividing line between these two regions is roughly at the <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> of the city of <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Bay" title="Walvis Bay">Walvis Bay</a>, and it consists in a narrow strip of land (about 50 km wide) that is the driest place in Southern Africa. To the south, the Namib borders on the <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a> semi-desert.</p>
<p>Southern Namib (between Lüderitz and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuiseb" title="Kuiseb">Kuiseb</a> River) comprises a vast dune sea with some of the tallest and most spectacular dunes of the world, ranging in color from pink to vivid orange. In the Sossusvlei area, several dunes exceed 300 meters (980 feet) in height. The complexity and regularity of dune patterns in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dune_sea" title="Dune sea">dune sea</a> have attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a> for decades, but it remains poorly understood.</p>
<p>Moving north from Sossusvlei, the sand gradually gives way to a rocky desert that extends all the way from Sossusvlei to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swakop" title="Swakop">Swakop</a> river. This area is traversed by the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a> and is mostly flat, although some scenic canyons and elevations are found in some areas, for example in the Moon Valley system. While most of the soil is rocky, sand dunes are still occasionally found in this region; for example, sand dunes occupy much of the coastline between Walvis Bay and Swakopmund.</p>
<p>The Namib desert is an important location for the mining of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamonds</a>.</p>
<p>Several rivers and streams run through the Namib, although all of the rivers south of the <a href="/wiki/Cunene_River" title="Cunene River">Cunene River</a> and north of the <a href="/wiki/Orange_River" title="Orange River">Orange River</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephemeral" title="Ephemeral">ephemeral</a> and rarely or never reach the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup> These rivers arise in the interior mountains of Namibia and flow after summer rain storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namib_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Namib's aridity is caused by the descent of dry air of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Cell" title="Hadley Cell">Hadley Cell</a>, cooled by the cold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benguela_current" title="Benguela current">Benguela current</a> along the coast. It has less than 10 mm (0.39 in) of rain annually and is almost completely barren. Besides rain being scarce, it is also hardly predictable. The western Namib gets less rain (5 mm) than the eastern Namib (85 mm). This is due to several factors. Winds coming from the Indian Ocean lose part of their humidity when passing the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg</a> mountains, and are essentially dry when they reach the Namib Escarpment at the eastern end of the desert. Winds coming from the Atlantic Ocean, on the other hand, are pressed down by hot air from the east; their humidity thus forms clouds and fog. Morning fogs coming from the ocean and pushing inwards into the desert are a regular phenomenon along the coast, and much of the life cycle of animals and plants in the Namib relies on these fogs as the main source of water.</p>
<p>The dry climate of the Namib reflects the almost complete lack of bodies of water on the surface. Most rivers flow underground and/or are dry for most of the year, and even when they are not, they usually drain into <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic</a> basins, without reaching the sea. The Swakop and the <a href="/wiki/Omaruru_River" title="Omaruru River">Omaruru</a> are the only rivers that occasionally drain into the ocean.</p>
<p>All along the coast, but mostly in the northernmost part of it, interaction between the water-laden air coming from the sea via southerly <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>, some of the strongest of any coastal desert, and the dry air of the desert causes immense <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fogs</a> and strong currents. It causes sailors to lose their way; this is testified by the remnants of a number of <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a> that can be found along the <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_Coast" title="Skeleton Coast">Skeleton Coast</a>, in northern Namib. Some of these wrecked ships can be found as much as 50 metres (55 yards) inland, as the desert slowly moves westwards into the sea, reclaiming land over a period of many years. Benguela's El Niño (similar to the Pacific event in its environmental change in the seas) spreads from the Kunene estuary southward to, on occasion, south of Luderitz. Warm waters with depth and associated water flows from the northwest were first fully catalogued by Sea Fisheries researchers, Cape Town (L V Shannon <i>et al.</i>). The research noted the positive effect of Benguela's El Niño on the rainfall of the interior. Rainfall records also show positive values variously across the Namib, Desert Research Station, Gobabeb for instance. This event recurs approximately mid-decade (1974, 1986, 1994, 1995 and 2006 are recent examples)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals_and_plants">Animals and plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namib_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Animals and plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of unusual <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> are found in this desert, many of which are <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> and highly adapted to the specific climate of the area. One of the most well-known endemic plants of the Namib is the bizarre <i><a href="/wiki/Welwitschia" title="Welwitschia">Welwitschia</a> mirabilis</i>; a shrub-like plant, it grows two long strap-shaped leaves continuously throughout its lifetime. These leaves may be several meters long, gnarled and twisted from the desert winds. The <a href="/wiki/Taproot" title="Taproot">taproot</a> of the plant develops into a flat, concave disc in age. <i>Welwitschia</i> is notable for its survival in the extremely arid conditions in the Namib, mostly deriving moisture from the coastal sea fogs. An area where <i>Welwitschias</i> are a common sight is found in the surroundings of the Moon Valley, including the eponymous Welwitschia Plains.</p>
<p>The Namib fauna mostly comprises <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and other small animals that can live on little water, but a few species of bigger animals are also found, including <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelopes</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Oryx" title="Oryx">oryxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Springbok" title="Springbok">springboks</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostriches</a>, and in some areas even <a href="/wiki/Desert_elephant" title="Desert elephant">desert elephants</a>. All these species have developed techniques to survive in the Namib environment. A number of endemic <a href="/wiki/Darkling_beetle" title="Darkling beetle">darkling beetles</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> — such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Namib_Desert_beetle" title="Namib Desert beetle">Namib Desert beetle</a> — have bumpy <a href="/wiki/Elytron" title="Elytron">elytrons</a> with a pattern of hydrophilic bumps and hydrophobic troughs. These cause humidity from the morning fogs to condense into droplets, which roll down the beetle's back to its mouth; they are collectively known as "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fog_beetle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fog beetle (page does not exist)">fog beetles</a>". Another beetle, the <i>Lepidochora discoidalis</i>, builds "water-capturing" webs. <a href="/wiki/Black-backed_jackal" title="Black-backed jackal">Black-backed jackals</a> lick humidity from stones. <a href="/wiki/Gemsbok" title="Gemsbok">Gemsboks</a> (also known as Oryxes) can raise the temperature of their bodies to 40 °C in the hottest hours of the day. The desert is also home to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meerkats" title="Meerkats">meerkats</a> and several species of lizards.</p>
<p>In 1934 it was observed that African lions were common in the coastal regions, mountains, and ephemeral rivers, between the lower Kuiseb River and the Kunene River.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namib_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the desert is largely unpopulated and inaccessible, there are year-round settlements at <a href="/wiki/Sesriem" title="Sesriem">Sesriem</a>, close to the famous <a href="/wiki/Sossusvlei" title="Sossusvlei">Sossusvlei</a> area, and other small outposts in other locations. <a href="/wiki/Namibe" title="Namibe">Namibe</a> in Angola, and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCderitz" title="Lüderitz">Lüderitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Bay" title="Walvis Bay">Walvis Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swakopmund" title="Swakopmund">Swakopmund</a> in Namibia, bordering on the desert, are the main settlements in the area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Namib-Naukluft_National_Park">Namib-Naukluft National Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namib_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Namib-Naukluft National Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Namib-Naukluft_National_Park" title="Namib-Naukluft National Park">Namib-Naukluft National Park</a></div>
<p>The Namib-Naukluft National Park, that extends over a large part of the Namib Desert, is the largest game reserve in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and one of the largest of the world. While most of the park is hardly accessible, several well-known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visitor_attraction" title="Visitor attraction">visitor attractions</a> are found in the desert. The prominent attraction is the famous <a href="/wiki/Sossusvlei" title="Sossusvlei">Sossusvlei</a> area, where high orange sand dunes surround vivid white salt pans, creating a fascinating landscape.</p>
<p>Access to the park is either by <a href="/wiki/Gravel_road" title="Gravel road">gravel roads</a> or dust roads (except for 60 km of <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_concrete" title="Asphalt concrete">concrete road</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sesriem" title="Sesriem">Sesriem</a> gate to Sossusvlei) or by light aircraft from <a href="/wiki/Windhoek" title="Windhoek">Windhoek</a> (the capital of Namibia, about 480 km (300 mi) north-east of the centre of the desert), or <a href="/wiki/Swakopmund" title="Swakopmund">Swakopmund</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Bay" title="Walvis Bay">Walvis Bay</a> at the north end of the desert.</p>
