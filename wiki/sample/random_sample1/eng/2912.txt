<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nabis</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Nabis</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Νάβις</span>) was ruler of <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a> from 207 BC to 192 BC, during the years of the <a href="/wiki/First_Macedonian_War" title="First Macedonian War">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Macedonian_War" title="Second Macedonian War">Second Macedonian Wars</a> and the eponymous "<a href="/wiki/War_against_Nabis" title="War against Nabis">War against Nabis</a>", i.e. against him. After taking the throne by executing two claimants, he began rebuilding Sparta's power. During the Second Macedonian War, he sided with King <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Macedon" title="Philip V of Macedon">Philip V of Macedon</a> and in return he received the city of <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a>. However, when the war began to turn against the Macedonians, he defected to Rome. After the war, the Romans urged by the <a href="/wiki/Achaean_League" title="Achaean League">Achaean League</a> attacked Nabis and defeated him in the War against Nabis. He was assassinated in 192 BC by the <a href="/wiki/Aetolian_League" title="Aetolian League">Aetolian League</a> and was Sparta's last independent ruler. He represented the last phase of Sparta's reformist period.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruler_of_Sparta">Ruler of Sparta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ruler of Sparta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the years following the defeat of the reformist king <a href="/wiki/Cleomenes_III" title="Cleomenes III">Cleomenes III</a> of Sparta at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sellasia" title="Battle of Sellasia">Battle of Sellasia</a> (222 BC), Sparta experienced a power vacuum that eventually led to the Spartan kingship being bestowed on a child, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelops_(Sparta)" title="Pelops (Sparta)">Pelops</a>, for whom first <a href="/wiki/Machanidas" title="Machanidas">Machanidas</a> (d. 207 BC) and then Nabis acted as regents. Nabis, however, soon overthrew Pelops, claiming to be a descendent of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurypontid" title="Eurypontid">Eurypontid</a> king <a href="/wiki/Demaratus" title="Demaratus">Demaratus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green302_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green302-2">[2]</a></sup> Although Nabis styled himself as king and is called <a href="/wiki/Basileus" title="Basileus">basileus</a> on his coins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a> refer to him as a <a href="/wiki/Tyrant" title="Tyrant">tyrant</a>.</p>
<p>Nabis was committed to the reformist program of <a href="/wiki/Cleomenes_III" title="Cleomenes III">Cleomenes III</a> and took it to extreme lengths, exiling the wealthy and dividing up their estates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_13.6-8_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_13.6-8-4">[4]</a></sup> He freed many slaves and made them citizens, but left the institution of <a href="/wiki/Helots" title="Helots">helotry</a> in place as part of the Lycurgan system which he was claiming to restore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.27_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.27-5">[5]</a></sup> The increased citizen body, however, meant that Nabis had more citizen troops for his army, which also included numerous mercenaries. <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a>, who was deeply hostile to Nabis' revolutionary program, described his supporters as <i>"a crowd of murderers, burglars, cutpurses and highwaymen" (ἀνδροφόνοι καὶ παρασχίσται, λωποδύται, τοιχωρύχοι)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_13.6_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_13.6-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nabis executed the last descendants of the two Spartan royal dynasties; and the ancient sources, especially <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a>, depict him as a bloodthirsty ruler who held power through armed force and shocking brutality. Polybius (13.6-7) claims that he would frequently exile the leading citizens of conquered communities and marry their wives to the brigands and freed slaves under his command. Polybius (13.6) in an account that either demonstrates the extent of Nabis's tyranny or Polybius's bias, tells how wealthy landowners were often summoned into his presence and forced to pay him large sums of money - those who refused were tortured, supposedly through use of a machine that resembled an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_maiden_(torture)" title="Iron maiden (torture)">Iron Maiden</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> known as the <a href="/wiki/Apega_of_Nabis" title="Apega of Nabis">Apega of Nabis</a>, made after his own wife Queen Apega.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In foreign policy, Nabis pursued much the same policy as his predecessors: opposing the <a href="/wiki/Achaean_League" title="Achaean League">Achaean League</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(Greece)" title="Macedonia (Greece)">Macedonia</a> by allying himself with the <a href="/wiki/Aetolian_League" title="Aetolian League">Aetolians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elis" title="Elis">Elis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Messene" title="Messene">Messene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_16.13_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_16.13-8">[8]</a></sup> This led him into an alliance with Rome during the <a href="/wiki/First_Macedonian_War" title="First Macedonian War">First Macedonian War</a>, signing a peace treaty with Rome in 205 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_of_Phoenice" title="Peace of Phoenice">Peace of Phoenice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_29.12_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_29.12-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.31_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.31-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the following years, Nabis expanded Spartan power, reconquering much of <a href="/wiki/Laconia" title="Laconia">Laconia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messene" title="Messene">Messene</a>. In 204 BC, he unsuccessfully attacked <a href="/wiki/Megalopolis,_Greece" title="Megalopolis, Greece">Megalopolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_13.8.3-7_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_13.8.3-7-11">[11]</a></sup> He also re-established a fleet (apparently with the help of Cretan allies, whom Polybius describes as "pirates",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_13.8_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_13.8-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.35.9_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.35.9-13">[13]</a></sup> and used it to re-establish control of the Laconian coastline. He also fortified the city of Sparta for the first time. (Previously, the Spartans had viewed their city's defence as depending on the bravery of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoplites" title="Hoplites">hoplites</a>.)</p>
<p>In 201 BC he invaded the territory of <a href="/wiki/Messene" title="Messene">Messene</a>, which had been an ally of Sparta in the previous decades,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_16.13_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_16.13-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.32_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.32-14">[14]</a></sup> apparently an attempt to re-establish the control which Sparta had had over the region until the mid 4th century BC. Messene fell to Nabis, but the Spartans were forced to retreat when the army of <a href="/wiki/Philopoemen" title="Philopoemen">Philopoemen</a> intervened. Nabis' forces were decisively defeated at <a href="/wiki/Tegea" title="Tegea">Tegea</a> and he was forced to check his expansionist ambitions for the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_16.13_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_16.13-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nabis' territorial ambitions brought him into conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Achaean_League" title="Achaean League">Achaean League</a>, which controlled the northern half of the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>. Although repeatedly defeated by the gifted Achaean strategos <a href="/wiki/Philopoemen" title="Philopoemen">Philopoemen</a>, he nevertheless remained a serious threat to the Achaeans, who were considerably less successful against him when led by less competent generals. In 200 BC, alarmed at the ease with which he was ravaging their territory, the Achaeans asked the Macedonian <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Macedon" title="Philip V of Macedon">King Philip V</a> for help, which he did not provide. In the following years, Nabis was able to skillfully exploit the conflict between Philip and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, gaining control of the important city of <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a> as the price of his alliance with the Macedonians, and then defecting to the soon to be victorious Romans so that he might hold on to his conquest.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_with_Rome">War with Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: War with Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 195 BC, however, the Roman <a href="/wiki/Proconsul" title="Proconsul">Proconsul</a> <a href="/wiki/Titus_Quinctius_Flamininus" title="Titus Quinctius Flamininus">Titus Quinctius Flamininus</a> was persuaded by the Achaeans that the power of Nabis in the Peloponnese needed to be checked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleaux190_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleaux190-16">[16]</a></sup> Flamininus ordered Nabis to give Argos back to the Achaeans, or face war with Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.25_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.25-17">[17]</a></sup> When Nabis refused, citing the Roman acceptance of his friendship at a time when he had already been in possession of the city as justification, Flamininus invaded Laconia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.25_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.25-17">[17]</a></sup> After an inconclusive campaign the Spartans were defeated, and Nabis was forced to surrender both Argos and the port of <a href="/wiki/Gytheio" title="Gytheio">Gytheum</a>, which gave him access to the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleaux191_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleaux191-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_34.41_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_34.41-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery_and_death">Recovery and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recovery and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the territory under his control now consisted only of the city of Sparta and its immediate environs, Nabis still hoped to regain his former power. In 192 BC, seeing that the Romans and their Achaean allies were distracted by the imminent war with King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochus_III" title="Antiochus III">Antiochus III</a> of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aetolian_League" title="Aetolian League">Aetolian League</a>, Nabis attempted to recapture Gytheum and the Laconian coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_Smith_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-William_Smith-20">[20]</a></sup> Initially, he was successful, capturing the <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> and defeating the <a href="/wiki/Achaean_League" title="Achaean League">Achaean League</a> in a minor naval battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_Smith_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-William_Smith-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after, however, his <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">army</a> was routed by Philopoemen and shut up within the walls of <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a>. After ravaging the surrounding countryside, Philopoemen returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_Smith_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-William_Smith-20">[20]</a></sup> Within a few months, Nabis appealed to the Aetolian League to send troops so that he might protect his territory against the Romans and the Achaean League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_Smith_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-William_Smith-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Aetolians responded by sending an army of 1,000 infantry and 300 cavalry to Sparta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy35.35_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy35.35-21">[21]</a></sup> Once there, however, the Aetolians betrayed the tyrant, assassinating him while he was drilling his army outside the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy35.35_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Livy35.35-21">[21]</a></sup> The Aetolians then attempted to take control of the city, but were prevented from doing so by an uprising of the citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy35.35_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Livy35.35-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Achaeans, seeking to take advantage of the ensuing chaos, dispatched Philopoemen to Sparta with a large army. Once there, he compelled the Spartans to join the Achaean League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cartledge_and_Spawforth77_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cartledge_and_Spawforth77-22">[22]</a></sup> Nabis had thus been the last leader of an independent Sparta, and the last ruler under whom the Spartans had been a major power in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.</p>
