<p><i><b>Les Nabis</b></i> (pronounced <i>nah-<small>BEE</small></i>) were a group of <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">Post-Impressionist</a> <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> artists who set the pace for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_arts" title="Fine arts">fine arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphic_arts" title="Graphic arts">graphic arts</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in the 1890s. Initially a group of friends interested in contemporary art and literature, most of them studied at the private art school of <a href="/wiki/Rodolphe_Julian" title="Rodolphe Julian">Rodolphe Julian</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Julian" title="Académie Julian">Académie Julian</a></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in the late 1880s.</p>
<p>In 1890, they began to participate successfully in public exhibitions, while most of their artistic output remained in private hands or in the possession of the artists themselves. By 1896, the unity of the group had already begun to break: The <i><a href="/wiki/Homage_to_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Homage to Cézanne">Homage to Cézanne</a></i>, painted by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Denis" title="Maurice Denis">Maurice Denis</a> in 1900, recollects memories of a time already gone, before even the term <i>Nabis</i> had been revealed to the public. Meanwhile, most members of the group—Maurice Denis, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">Pierre Bonnard</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Vuillard" title="Édouard Vuillard">Édouard Vuillard</a>—could stand, artistically, on their own. Only Paul Sérusier had problems to overcome—though it was his <i>Talisman</i>, painted at the advice of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a>, that had revealed to them the way to go.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_term">Origin of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1787" data-file-width="2412" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg/220px-Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg/330px-Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg/440px-Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maurice_Denis_Homage_to_Cezanne_1900.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Homage to Cézanne</i>, Maurice Denis, 1900. <i>Musée d'Orsay</i>, Paris.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Nabi</i> means <i><a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in Hebrew and in Arabic.</p>
<p>Les Nabis originated as a rebellious group of young student artists who banded together at the Académie Julian. <a href="/wiki/Paul_S%C3%A9rusier" title="Paul Sérusier">Paul Sérusier</a> galvanized Les Nabis, and provided the name and disseminated the example of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a> among them. <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">Pierre Bonnard</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Vuillard" title="Édouard Vuillard">Édouard Vuillard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Denis" title="Maurice Denis">Maurice Denis</a> became the best known of the group; at the time, however, they were somewhat peripheral to the core group.</p>
<p>The term was coined by the linguist Auguste Cazalis who drew a parallel between the way these painters aimed to revitalize painting (as <i>prophets of <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a></i>) and the way the ancient prophets had rejuvenated Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Possibly the nickname arose because "most of them wore beards, some were Jews and all were desperately earnest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Les Nabis regarded themselves as initiates, and used a private vocabulary. They called a studio <i>ergasterium</i>, and ended their letters with the initials <i>E.T.P.M.V. et M.P.</i>, meaning "En ta paume, mon verbe et ma pensée" ("In the palm of your hand, my word and my thoughts.")</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Meeting at <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Julian" title="Académie Julian">Académie Julian</a>, and then at the apartment of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ranson" title="Paul Ranson">Paul Ranson</a>, they preached that a work of art is the end product and the visual expression of an artist's synthesis of nature in personal aesthetic metaphors and <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">symbols</a>. They paved the way for the early 20th-century development of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract</a> and non-representational art, and they had in common with most progressive artists of the time the goal of integrating art and daily life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Nabis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="786" data-file-width="479" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg/170px-Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg/255px-Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg/340px-Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Les_Parisiennes_cph.3g10009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">Pierre Bonnard</a>, <i>Les Parisiennes</i> 1893, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithograph" title="Lithograph">lithograph</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Les Nabis artists worked in a variety of media, using oils on both canvas and cardboard, <a href="/wiki/Distemper_(paint)" title="Distemper (paint)">distemper</a> on canvas and wall decoration, and also produced posters, prints, book illustrations, textiles and furniture. Considered to be on the cutting edge of modern art during their early period, their subject matter was representational (though often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolist" title="Symbolist">Symbolist</a> in inspiration), but was design oriented along the lines of the Japanese prints they so admired, and <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a>. Unlike those types, however, the artists of this circle were highly influenced by the paintings of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impressionists" title="Impressionists">Impressionists</a>, and thus while sharing the flatness, page layout and negative space of art nouveau and other decorative modes, much of Les Nabis art has a painterly, non-realistic look, with color palettes often reminding one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A9zanne" title="Cézanne">Cézanne</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauguin" title="Gauguin">Gauguin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">Bonnard</a>'s posters and lithographs are more firmly in the Art Nouveau, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Toulouse-Lautrec">Toulouse-Lautrec</a> manner. After the turn of the century, as modern art moved towards <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">Abstraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>, etc., Les Nabis were viewed as conservatives and, indeed, were among the last group of artists to stick to the roots and artistic ambitions of the Impressionists, pursuing these ends almost into the middle of the 20th century. In their later years, these painters also largely abandoned their earlier interests in Decorative and Applied Arts.</p>

<p>Among the artists who considered themselves Nabis was <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Denis" title="Maurice Denis">Maurice Denis</a>, whose journalism put the aims of the group in the eye of a progressive audience, and whose definition of painting – "a flat surface covered with colors assembled in a certain order" – expressed the Nabis approach. His <i>Théories</i> (1920; 1922) summed up the Nabis' aims long after they had been superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Fauvism" title="Fauvism">fauve</a> painters and by <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">cubism</a>.</p>
<p>Other Nabis were <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">Pierre Bonnard</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Vuillard" title="Édouard Vuillard">Édouard Vuillard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ker-Xavier_Roussel" title="Ker-Xavier Roussel">Ker-Xavier Roussel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ranson" title="Paul Ranson">Paul Ranson</a> and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Vallotton" title="Félix Vallotton">Félix Vallotton</a>. The sculptor <a href="/wiki/Aristide_Maillol" title="Aristide Maillol">Aristide Maillol</a> was associated for a time with the group. The Scot, James Pitcairn-Knowles and the Hungarian, Jozsef Rippl-Ronai, lived together in Paris during the first decade of the 20th century and also turned to glass blowing and wood carving. The <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">post-Impressionist</a> styles they embraced skirted some aspects of contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_nouveau" title="Art nouveau">art nouveau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolism</a>. The influence of the English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_Movement" title="Arts and Crafts Movement">Arts and Crafts Movement</a> set them to work in media that involved crafts beyond painting: printmaking, book illustration and poster design, textiles and set design.</p>

<p>Other members of the group included the playwright Pierre Veber, musician Pierre Hermant and linguist Auguste Cazalis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> called (by Ranson) <i>le nabi Ben Kallyre</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
