<p><b>Nawab</b> or <b>nawaab</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi">नवाब</span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urdu_language" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ur" xml:lang="ur">نوّاب</span>‎, <i>Nawaab</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> title ratified and bestowed by the reigning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mughal_emperor" title="Mughal emperor">Mughal emperor</a> to semi-autonomous <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> rulers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princely_states" title="Princely states">princely states</a> in South Asia. "Nawab" usually refers to males; the female equivalent is "<a href="/wiki/Begum" title="Begum">begum</a>" or "nawab begum". The primary duty of a nawab was to uphold the sovereignty of the Mughal emperor along with the administration of a certain province.</p>
<p>The title of "nawab" was also awarded as a personal distinction by the paramount power, similar to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_peerage" title="British peerage">British peerage</a>, to persons and families who ruled a princely state, for various services to the Government of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_India" title="British India">British India</a>. In some cases, these titles were also accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Jagir" title="Jagir">jagir</a> grants, either in cash revenues and allowances or land-holdings. During the British Raj, some of the chiefs or <a href="/wiki/Sardar" title="Sardar">Sardars</a> of large or important tribes were also given the title, in addition to traditional titles already held by virtue of chieftainship.</p>
<p>The term "nawab" was originally used for the <i><a href="/wiki/Subahdar" title="Subahdar">subahdar</a></i> (provincial governor) or viceroy of a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subah_(province)" title="Subah (province)">subah</a></i> (province) or region of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mughal_empire" title="Mughal empire">Mughal empire</a>.</p>

<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> term, used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urdu_language" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> and many other North-Indian languages, borrowed via <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, being the honorific plural of <i>naib</i>, or "deputy." In some areas, especially <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a>, the term is pronounced <i>nobab</i>. This later variation has also entered English and other foreign languages (see <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nabob" title="wikt:nabob">Wiktionary</a>).</p>

<p>The term "nawbab" is often used to refer to any Muslim ruler in north or south India while the term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nizam" title="Nizam">nizam</a>" is preferred for a senior official—it literally means "governor of region". The Nizam of Hyderabad had several nawabs under him: Nawabs of Cuddapah, Sira, Rajahmundry, Kurnool, Chicacole, et al. "Nizam" was his personal title, awarded by the Mughal Government and based on the term "Nazim" as meaning "senior officer". "Nazim" is still used for a district collector in many parts of India. The term "nawab" is still technically imprecise, as the title was also awarded to Hindus and <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikhs</a>, as well, and large <a href="/wiki/Zamindar" title="Zamindar">zamindars</a> and not necessarily to all Muslim rulers. With the decline of that empire, the title, and the powers that went with it, became hereditary in the ruling families in the various provinces.</p>
<p>Under later British rule, nawabs continued to rule various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princely_states" title="Princely states">princely states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Awadh" title="Awadh">Awadh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amb_(princely_state)" title="Amb (princely state)">Amb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahawalpur" title="Bahawalpur">Bahawalpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balasinor" title="Balasinor">Balasinor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baoni" title="Baoni">Baoni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banganapalle" title="Banganapalle">Banganapalle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambay" title="Cambay">Cambay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaora" title="Jaora">Jaora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Junagadh" title="Junagadh">Junagadh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurnool" title="Kurnool">Kurnool</a> (the main city of Deccan), <a href="/wiki/Kurwai" title="Kurwai">Kurwai</a>, Mamdot, <a href="/wiki/Multan" title="Multan">Multan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palanpur" title="Palanpur">Palanpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pataudi" title="Pataudi">Pataudi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radhanpur" title="Radhanpur">Radhanpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rampur,_Uttar_Pradesh" title="Rampur, Uttar Pradesh">Rampur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malerkotla" title="Malerkotla">Malerkotla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sachin,_Gujarat" title="Sachin, Gujarat">Sachin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajoli" title="Rajoli">Rajoli</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonk_(princely_state)" title="Tonk (princely state)">Tonk</a>. Other former rulers bearing the title, such as the nawabs of <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oudh" title="Oudh">Oudh</a>, had been dispossessed by the British or others by the time the Mughal dynasty finally ended in 1857.</p>
<p>Some princes became Nawab by promotion, e.g. the ruler of Palanpur was "diwan" until 1910, then "nawab sahib". Other nawabs were promoted are restyled to another princely style, or to and back, e.g. in <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rajgarh" title="Rajgarh">Rajgarh</a> a single <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rawat" title="Rawat">rawat</a> (rajah) went by nawab.</p>
<p>The style for a nawbab's queen is <i>begum</i>. Most of the nawbab dynasties were male <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogenitures</a>, although several ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Begum_of_Bhopal" title="Begum of Bhopal">Begums of Bhopal</a> were a notable exception.</p>
<p>Before the incorporation of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent#The_Subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Subcontinent</a> into the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, nawabs ruled the kingdoms of Awadh (or Oudh, encouraged by the British to shed the Mughal suzerainty and assume the imperial style of Badshah), Bengal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcot" title="Arcot">Arcot</a> and Bhopal.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous_nawabs">Miscellaneous nawabs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous nawabs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_nawabs">Personal nawabs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal nawabs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The title <i>nawab</i> was also awarded as a personal distinction by the paramount power, similarly to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_peerage" title="British peerage">British peerage</a>, to persons and families who never ruled a princely state. For the Muslim elite various Mughal-type titles were introduced, including nawab. Among the noted British creations of this type were Nawab <a href="/wiki/Hashim_Ali_Khan" title="Hashim Ali Khan">Hashim Ali Khan</a> (1858–1940), Nawab <a href="/wiki/Khwaja_Abdul_Ghani" title="Khwaja Abdul Ghani">Khwaja Abdul Ghani</a> (1813–1896), Nawab <a href="/wiki/Nawab_Abdul_Latif" title="Nawab Abdul Latif">Abdul Latif</a> (1828–1893), Nawab <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faizunnesa_Choudhurani" title="Faizunnesa Choudhurani">Faizunnesa Choudhurani</a> (1834–1904), Nawab <a href="/wiki/Syed_Nawab_Ali_Chowdhury" title="Syed Nawab Ali Chowdhury">Ali Chowdhury</a> (1863–1929), <a href="/wiki/Nawaab_Syed_Shamsul_Huda" title="Nawaab Syed Shamsul Huda">Nawaab Syed Shamsul Huda</a> (1862–1922), Nawab Sirajul Islam (1848–1923), <a href="/wiki/Nawab_Alam_yar_jung_Bahadur" title="Nawab Alam yar jung Bahadur">Nawab Alam yar jung Bahadur</a>, M.A, Madras, B.A., B.C.L., Barr-At-Law (1890–1974). There also were the Nawabs of Dhanbari, Nawabs of Ratanpur, Nawabs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroda" title="Baroda">Baroda</a> and such others.</p>

A powerful <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nawbab_of_Oudh" title="Nawbab of Oudh">Nawbab of Oudh</a></div>

</div>
<p><i>Nawbab</i> was also the rank title—again not an office—of a much lower class of <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> nobles—in fact retainers—at the court of the <a href="/wiki/Nizam_of_Hyderabad" title="Nizam of Hyderabad">Nizam of Hyderabad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berar_Province" title="Berar Province">Berar</a> State, ranking only above <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan Bahadur</a> and Khan, but under (in ascending order) <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Jang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al-Dawla" title="Al-Dawla">Daula</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mulk" title="Mulk">Mulk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umara" title="Umara">Umara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jah" title="Jah">Jah</a>; the equivalent for Hindu courtiers was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raja_Bahadur" title="Raja Bahadur">Raja Bahadur</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derived_titles">Derived titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Derived titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nawabzada">Nawabzada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nawabzada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="558" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg/220px-The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg/330px-The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg/440px-The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Navab%27s_arrival_before_Clive%27s_position.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nawab_of_Bengal" title="Nawab of Bengal">Nawab of Bengal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mir_Qasim" title="Mir Qasim">Mir Qasim</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This style, adding the Persian suffix <i>-zada</i> which means son (or other male descendants; see other cases in prince), etymologically fits a nawbab's sons, but in actual practice various dynasties established other customs.</p>
<p>For example, in <a href="/wiki/Bahawalpur_(princely_state)" title="Bahawalpur (princely state)">Bahawalpur</a> only the nawbab's <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> used <i>nawabzada</i> before his personal name, then <i>Khan Abassi</i>, finally <i>Wali Ahad Bahadur</i> (an enhancement of Wali Ehed), while the other sons of the ruling nawab used the style <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahibzada" title="Sahibzada">sahibzada</a> before the personal name and only Khan Abassi behind. "Nawabzadi" implies daughters of the reigning nawbab.</p>
<p>Elsewhere, rulers who were not styled nawbab yet awarded a title nawabzada.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nabob">Nabob</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nabob">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Nabob_(disambiguation)" title="Nabob (disambiguation)">Nabob (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>In colloquial usage in English (since 1612),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> adopted in other Western languages, the <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliteration</a> "nabob" refers to commoners: a merchant-leader of high social status and wealth. "Nabob" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> pronunciation of "nawab": <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>: <span lang="bn" xml:lang="bn">নবাব</span> <i>nôbab</i>. During the 18th century in particular, it was widely used as a disparaging term for British merchants or administrators who, having made a fortune in India, returned to Britain and aspired to be recognised as having the higher social status that their new wealth would enable them to maintain. <a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(novel)#Jos_Sedley" title="Vanity Fair (novel)">Jos Sedley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(novel)" title="Vanity Fair (novel)">Thackeray's <i>Vanity Fair</i></a> is probably the best known example in fiction. From this specific usage it came to be sometimes used for ostentatiously rich businesspeople in general. It can also be used metaphorically for people who have a grandiose sense of their own importance, as in the famous alliterative dismissal of the news media as <i>"nattering nabobs of negativism"</i> in a speech that was delivered by <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">Spiro Agnew</a> and written by <a href="/wiki/William_Safire" title="William Safire">William Safire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naib">Naib</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Naib">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word <i>naib</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">نائب</span>‎‎) has been historically used to refer to any local leader in some parts of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, successive early modern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iranian</a> kingdoms (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safavids" title="Safavids">Safavids</a>, etc.), and in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> (e.g. during <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_Imamate" title="Caucasian Imamate">Caucasian Imamate</a>).</p>
<p>Today, the word is used to refer to directly-elected legislators in lower houses of parliament in many Arabic-speaking areas to contrast them against officers of upper houses (or <a href="/wiki/Shura" title="Shura">Shura</a>). The term <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Majlis_al-Nuwwab_(disambiguation)" title="Majlis al-Nuwwab (disambiguation)">Majlis al-Nuwwab</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مجلس النواب</span>‎‎, literally <i>council of deputies</i>) has been adopted as the name of several legislative lower houses and unicameral legislatures.</p>
