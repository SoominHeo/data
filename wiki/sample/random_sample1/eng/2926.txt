<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Niobium-tin</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Niobium-tin</b> (<b>Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn</b>) or triniobium-tin is a <a href="/wiki/Intermetallic" title="Intermetallic">metallic chemical compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a> (Nb) and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> (Sn), used industrially as a <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">type II superconductor</a>. This intermetallic compound is an <a href="/wiki/A15_phases" title="A15 phases">A15 phase</a> superconductor. It is more expensive than <a href="/wiki/Niobium-titanium" title="Niobium-titanium">niobium-titanium</a> (NbTi), but remains superconducting up to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux_density" title="Magnetic flux density">magnetic flux density</a> of 30 <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">teslas</a> [T] (300,000 G),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> compared to a limit of roughly 15 T for NbTi.</p>
<p>Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn was discovered to be a superconductor in 1954. The material's ability to support high currents and magnetic fields was discovered in 1961 and started the era of large-scale applications of superconductivity.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_temperature#Superconductivity" title="Critical temperature">critical temperature</a> is 18.3 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a> (−254.8 °C; −426.7 °F). Application temperatures are commonly around 4.2 K, the boiling point of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquid helium</a> at atmospheric pressure.</p>
<p>In April 2008 a record non-copper <a href="/wiki/Current_density" title="Current density">current density</a> was claimed of 2,643 A/mm² at 12 T and 4.2 K (−268.95 °C; −452.11 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EAS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_use">Practical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium-tin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Practical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mechanically, Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn is extremely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a> and thus can not be easily drawn into a wire, which is necessary for winding <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_magnet" title="Superconducting magnet">superconducting magnets</a>. To overcome this, wire manufacturers typically draw down composite wires containing ductile precursors. The "internal tin" process includes separate alloys of Nb, Cu and Sn. The "bronze" process contains Nb in a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>-tin <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> matrix. With both processes the strand is typically drawn to final size and coiled into a solenoid or cable <i>before</i> heat treatment. It is only during the heat treatment that the Sn reacts with the Nb to form the brittle, superconducting niobium-tin compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_wire#Powder-in-tube" title="Superconducting wire">powder-in-tube</a> process is also used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EAS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The high field section of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR" title="NMR">NMR</a> magnets are composed of niobium-tin wire.</p>
<p>Some niobium-tin wires can be wound <i>after</i> heat treatment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium-tin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn was discovered to be a superconductor in 1954, one year after the discovery of V<sub>3</sub>Si, the first example of an A<sub>3</sub>B superconductor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1961 it was discovered that niobium-tin still exhibits superconductivity at large currents and strong magnetic fields, thus becoming the first known material to support the high currents and fields necessary for making useful high-power magnets and electric power <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinery" title="Machinery">machinery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geballe_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-geballe-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_uses">Notable uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium-tin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The central <a href="/wiki/Solenoid" title="Solenoid">solenoid</a> and toroidal field <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_magnet" title="Superconducting magnet">superconducting magnets</a> for the planned experimental <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> reactor use niobium-tin as a superconductor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The central solenoid coil will produce a field of 13.5 teslas (135,000 G). The toroidal field coils will operate at a maximum field of 11.8 T. Estimated use is 600 metric tons (590 long tons) of Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn strands and 250 metric tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Niobium-titanium" title="Niobium-titanium">NbTi</a> strands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alstrom_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-alstrom-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986 it had been proposed for the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> to use niobium-tin superconducting magnets instead of <a href="/wiki/Niobium-titanium" title="Niobium-titanium">niobium-titanium</a>, and thus avoid the requirement to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenically</a>-cool the collider below the 4.22K limit with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfluid_helium" title="Superfluid helium">superfluid helium</a>, but this option was not pursued in order to avoid delays while competing with the then-planned US-led <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_Super_Collider" title="Superconducting Super Collider">Superconducting Super Collider</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Super Large Hadron Collider">planned future upgrade</a> on LHC will eventually use niobium-tin, superconducting magnets.</p>
