<p><b>Nitroxyl</b> (common name) or <b>azanone</b> (IUPAC name)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the chemical compound HNO. It is well known in the gas phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In aqueous solution it acts as an acid with the conjugate base NO<sup>−</sup>, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a> = 11.4).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg.26Holleman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg.26Holleman-3">[3]</a></sup> NO<sup>−</sup> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redox_reaction" title="Redox reaction">reduced form</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO) and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxygen" title="Dioxygen">dioxygen</a>. Nitroxyl can be formed as a reaction intermediate. Dichloramine reacts with hydroxyl ion, which can be present in water or comes from water molecules, to yield <b>nitroxyl</b> radical and the chloride ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_Chlorine_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-White_Chlorine-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nitroxyl is very reactive towards nucleophiles (especially <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a>) and quickly dimerizes to <a href="/wiki/Hyponitrous_acid" title="Hyponitrous acid">hyponitrous acid</a>, H<sub>2</sub>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which is then dehydrated to <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> N<sub>2</sub>O. Therefore, HNO is generally prepared <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i> for example with the compounds such as <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Angeli%E2%80%99s_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Angeli’s salt (page does not exist)">Angeli’s salt</a></b> (Na<sub>2</sub>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) and <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Piloty%E2%80%99s_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Piloty’s acid (page does not exist)">Piloty’s acid</a></b> (PhSO<sub>2</sub>NHOH) when it is needed.</p>
<p>Nitroxyl donors, known as <a href="/wiki/Nitroso" title="Nitroso">nitroso</a> compounds, show potential in the treatment of heart failure and ongoing research is focused on finding new molecules for this task. In one study <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> such donor is prepared by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_oxidation" title="Organic oxidation">organic oxidation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxime" title="Oxime">cyclohexanone oxime</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_tetraacetate" title="Lead tetraacetate">lead tetraacetate</a> to form 1-nitrosocyclohexyl acetate:</p>
<dl>
<dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nitrosocyclohexyl_acetate.png" title="Nitrosocyclohexyl acetate"><img alt="Nitrosocyclohexyl acetate" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="687" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Nitrosocyclohexyl_acetate.png/400px-Nitrosocyclohexyl_acetate.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Nitrosocyclohexyl_acetate.png/600px-Nitrosocyclohexyl_acetate.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/Nitrosocyclohexyl_acetate.png 2x" width="400"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>This compound can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrolyzed" title="Hydrolyzed">hydrolyzed</a> under <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> conditions in a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">phosphate buffer</a> to HNO, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanone" title="Cyclohexanone">cyclohexanone</a>.</p>
<p>Other studies that have been conducted on HNO precursors include those from Nagasawa et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in which Piloty's acid is derivatized and produces HNO upon thermal decomposition. Other notable studies on the production of HNO come from Toscano <i>et al.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in which cycloadducts of acyl nitroso species (which are known to decompose via hydrolysis to HNO and acyl acid) are synthesized. Upon photolysis these compounds release the acyl nitroso species which then further decomposes.</p>
<p>More information can be found in the book <i>The Chemistry and Biology of Nitroxyl</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
