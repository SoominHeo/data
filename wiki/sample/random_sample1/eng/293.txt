<p>The <b>Men's 100 Meters</b> at the <a href="/wiki/1988_Summer_Olympics" title="1988 Summer Olympics">1988 Summer Olympics</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seoul,_South_Korea" title="Seoul, South Korea">Seoul, South Korea</a> – frequently dubbed "the dirtiest race in history"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardianmackay_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardianmackay-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montague_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montague-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mehaffey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mehaffey-4">[4]</a></sup> – ended in controversy after Canada's <a href="/wiki/Ben_Johnson_(sprinter)" title="Ben Johnson (sprinter)">Ben Johnson</a> defeated defending champion <a href="/wiki/Carl_Lewis" title="Carl Lewis">Carl Lewis</a> from the United States with a world record time of 9.79s, topping his own record of 9.83s that he set at the <a href="/wiki/1987_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="1987 World Championships in Athletics">1987 World Championships in Athletics</a> in Rome. Two days later, Johnson was stripped of his gold medal by the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC) when his post-race <a href="/wiki/Doping_in_sport" title="Doping in sport">drug test</a> indicated <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroid</a> use. His record time of 9.79 seconds was also erased. The gold medal for the 100 meters was awarded to Lewis, who ran 9.92s. Later, the world record Johnson held prior to the Olympics was also rescinded and Lewis' time in this race became the official world record, breaking the 9.93 mark that <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Smith" title="Calvin Smith">Calvin Smith</a> set in 1983 and Lewis had tied twice since. Smith had participated in this race as well and finished fourth, but was elevated to third place and given the bronze medal behind Lewis and <a href="/wiki/Linford_Christie" title="Linford Christie">Linford Christie</a> of the United Kingdom, the original bronze medal winner that moved up to silver. The other participants in this race, in order of finish, were <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Mitchell" title="Dennis Mitchell">Dennis Mitchell</a> of the United States, who would go on to win the bronze medal in this event in <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1992_Summer_Olympics" title="Athletics at the 1992 Summer Olympics">Barcelona</a>; <a href="/wiki/Robson_da_Silva" title="Robson da Silva">Robson da Silva</a> of Brazil, who won the bronze in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1988_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_200_meters" title="Athletics at the 1988 Summer Olympics – Men's 200 meters">200 meters</a> in Seoul; Johnson's teammate <a href="/wiki/Desai_Williams" title="Desai Williams">Desai Williams</a>, a bronze medalist in the 4 x 100 meter relay in <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1984_Summer_Olympics" title="Athletics at the 1984 Summer Olympics">Los Angeles</a> four years earlier; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray_Stewart_(athlete)" title="Ray Stewart (athlete)">Ray Stewart</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, who won a silver medal in the same relay at the Los Angeles Olympics.</p>
<p>It would take another eleven years for a runner to run a clean 9.79 in the 100 meters, a feat <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Greene_(athlete)" title="Maurice Greene (athlete)">Maurice Greene</a> accomplished at the world championships in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> in 1999.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Athletics_at_the_1988_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_100_metres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Johnson was not the only participant whose success was questioned. Lewis had tested positive at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Trials_(track_and_field)" title="United States Olympic Trials (track and field)">US Olympic Trials</a> for <a href="/wiki/Pseudoephedrine" title="Pseudoephedrine">pseudoephedrine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ephedrine" title="Ephedrine">ephedrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenylpropanolamine" title="Phenylpropanolamine">phenylpropanolamine</a>, medications available at the time in common cold remedies, but the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Committee" title="United States Olympic Committee">United States Olympic Committee</a> (USOC) cleared Lewis to participate at the games in Seoul. Christie was found to have <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolites</a> of pseudoephedrine in his urine after a 200m heat at the same Olympics but was later cleared of any wrongdoing. <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Mitchell" title="Dennis Mitchell">Dennis Mitchell</a> tested positive ten years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardianmackay_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardianmackay-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Of the top five competitors in the race, only former world record holder and eventual bronze medalist Smith never failed a drug test during his career. Smith later said: "I should have been the gold medalist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Johnson had demanded that Lewis be stripped of his gold medal, but the IOC had no intention of redressing the issue, stating they operate under a three-year statute of limitations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> documentary <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9.79*" title="9.79*">9.79*</a></i>, eventual silver medallist Christie states, and footage of the race shows, that Lewis "ran out of his lane... two or three times" during the race, which could have resulted in Lewis' disqualification had he impeded other competitors. The numerous athletes using performance-enhancing drugs at the time understood how long before a race, and possible drug test, they should stop using the drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian-8">[8]</a></sup> Johnson has stated that André Jackson, a mutual friend of Johnson and Lewis, entered the drug testing area in Seoul to deposit <a href="/wiki/Stanozolol" title="Stanozolol">stanozolol</a> in the beer Johnson consumed to produce a urine sample.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The CBC radio documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/Rewind_(radio_program)" title="Rewind (radio program)">Rewind</a></i>, "Ben Johnson: A Hero Disgraced" broadcast on September 19, 2013, for the 25th anniversary of the race, stated 20 athletes tested positive for drugs but were cleared by the IOC at this 1988 Seoul Olympics, and an IOC official stated that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endocrine" title="Endocrine">endocrine</a> profiles done at those games indicated that 80 percent of the track and field athletes tested showed evidence of long-term steroid use although not all were banned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In addition, CBC Radio was told by its sources that NBC had threatened to withhold its second rights payment to the IOC due on completion of the 1988 Seoul Olympics games stating, "if these games collapse in scandal, we're out and that money's gone".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
