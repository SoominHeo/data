<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nazar (amulet)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <b>nazar</b> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>nazar boncuğu</i></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Turkic" title="Old Turkic">Old Turkic</a>: <i>gökçe munçuk</i>, meaning "blue bead") is an eye-shaped <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">amulet</a> believed to protect against the <a href="/wiki/Evil_eye" title="Evil eye">evil eye</a>. “Evil eye”, from <i>boncuğu</i> (derivative of <i>boncuk</i>, which means “bead” in Turkish) and <i>nazar</i> (from the Arabic نظر, “sight” or “seeing”).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iranian_folklore#Beliefs" title="Iranian folklore">Persian/Afghan folklore</a>, it is called a <i>cheshm nazar</i> (چشم نظر) or <i>nazar ghorboni</i> (نظرقربونی).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They believe that if a person is complimented a lot, they will be jinxed and often sick the next day unless a verse of the Quran is recited.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>, it is also called "nazar" (نظر).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amulet">Amulet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazar_(amulet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Amulet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A typical nazar is made of handmade glass featuring concentric circles or teardrop shapes in dark blue, white, light blue and black, occasionally with a yellow/gold edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LonelyPlanet_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LonelyPlanet-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a legacy of the Turkish <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, it is a common sight in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> where the nazar is often hung in homes, offices, cars, children's clothing, or incorporated in jewellery and ornaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LonelyPlanet_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LonelyPlanet-2">[2]</a></sup> They are a popular choice of souvenir with tourists.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salafi_movement" title="Salafi movement">Salafist</a> Muslim scholars, however, have rejected the use of such traditional amulets, including the evil-eye, stating that it is a form of <i>Shirk</i> - meaning associating with God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The reason they forbid this is because one now believes protection is coming from the object rather than from God, or that it intercedes on behalf of an individual before God when it has no power to do such. The Standing Committee for Research and Fatwas of Saudi Arabia stated that these amulets are prohibited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Similar was also mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Wahhabism" title="Wahhabism">Wahhabi</a> Shaykh Al-Albaanee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The evidence used for the prohibition is the narration in <i>Musnad Ahmed</i> from Muhammad which states: "Whoever hangs an amulet, then he has committed <i>Shirk</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eye_bead">Eye bead</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazar_(amulet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eye bead">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari,_Batumi_museum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2318" data-file-width="920" height="554" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari%2C_Batumi_museum.jpg/220px-Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari%2C_Batumi_museum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari%2C_Batumi_museum.jpg/330px-Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari%2C_Batumi_museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari%2C_Batumi_museum.jpg/440px-Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari%2C_Batumi_museum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eyebeads_from_Pichvnari,_Batumi_museum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
eye beads</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Turkish <i>boncuk</i> (sometimes called a <i>göz boncuğu</i> 'eye bead') is a glass bead characterized by a blue glass field with a blue or black dot superimposed on a white or yellow center. Historically old, the blue bead has gained importance as an item of popular culture in Modern Turkey. The bead probably originated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and is associated with the development of glass making. Written documents and extant beads date as early as the 16th century BC. Glass beads were made and widely used throughout the ancient world: from <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicia</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>, and throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_imperial_period" title="Roman imperial period">Roman imperial period</a>.</p>
<p>The <b>eye bead</b> is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Glass_art" title="Glass art">glass art</a> based on nazar in Turkey. This art has changed very little for thousands of years. The 3,000-year-old antique Mediterranean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_bead" title="Glass bead">glass art</a> lives in these eye bead furnaces with its every detail.</p>
<p>The roots of the very few glass evil eye bead masters that still practice this tradition go back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia">Arabian</a> artisans who settled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izmir" title="Izmir">Izmir</a> and its towns during the decline of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> by the end of the 19th century. The glass art that had lost its glamour in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, combined with the eye sign, was enlivened. The masters who practised their arts at Araphan and <a href="/wiki/Kemeralt%C4%B1" title="Kemeraltı">Kemeraltı</a> districts of Izmir were exiled due to the disturbance of the smoke from their furnace and risk of fire in the neighbourhood.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_and_popular_culture">Other uses and popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazar_(amulet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses and popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="660" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg/110px-Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg/165px-Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg/220px-Nazar_boncu%C4%9Fu_tailfin.jpg 2x" width="110"/></a>

The <i>nazar boncuğu</i> symbol on a <a href="/wiki/Fly_Air" title="Fly Air">Fly Air</a> airplane, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabiha_G%C3%B6k%C3%A7en_Airport" title="Sabiha Gökçen Airport">Sabiha Gökçen Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul, Turkey</a>.</div>

<p>The nazar image was used as a <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> on the tailfins of aeroplanes belonging to the private Turkish airline <a href="/wiki/Fly_Air" title="Fly Air">Fly Air</a>.</p>
<p>It is used in the logo for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CryEngine_3" title="CryEngine 3">CryEngine 3</a>, a game engine designed by <a href="/wiki/Crytek" title="Crytek">Crytek</a>, a video game company founded by three Turkish brothers (<a href="/wiki/Cevat_Yerli" title="Cevat Yerli">Cevat</a>, Avni and Faruk Yerli).</p>
<p>It was also used in the logo of the <a href="/wiki/2013_FIFA_U-20_World_Cup" title="2013 FIFA U-20 World Cup">2013 FIFA U-20 World Cup</a> events.</p>
<p>The video game <a href="/wiki/Terraria" title="Terraria">Terraria</a> (2011) has an accessory item called "Nazar" which grants immunity to the "Cursed debuff".</p>
