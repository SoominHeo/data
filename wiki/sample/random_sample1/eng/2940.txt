<p><b>Najran</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">نجران</span>‎‎ <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">Najrān</span></i>), is a city in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> near the border with <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>. It is the capital of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Najran_Province" title="Najran Province">Najran Province</a>. Designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_town" title="New town">new town</a>, Najran is one of the fastest-growing cities in the kingdom; its population has risen from 47,500 in 1974 and 90,983 in 1992 to 246,880 in 2004. The population mostly originates from the ancient tribe of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Banu_Makram&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Banu Makram (page does not exist)">Mákram</a><a href="/wiki/Banu_Yam" title="Banu Yam">Yām</a> <a href="/wiki/Hamdan" title="Hamdan">Hamdan</a>.</p>
<p>Najranis are Muslims, with <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shia</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismailism" title="Ismailism">Ismaili</a> forming the plurality of the religious adherents. <a href="/wiki/Hanbali" title="Hanbali">Hanbali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shafi%27i" title="Shafi'i">Shafi'i</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunnis</a> form the second largest religious group in the city, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaydi" title="Zaydi">Zaydi</a> Shia form the smallest religious group.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> term <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">Najrān</span></i> has at least two meanings. It means both the wooden frame on which a door opens and also 'thirsty'. Local tradition also has it that the land derived its name from the first man to settle in the area, Najran ibn Zaydan ibn Saba ibn Yahjub ibn Yarub ibn Qahtan.</p>
<p>Najran was the Yemeni centre of cloth making and originally, the <i>kiswah</i> or the cloth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ka%27aba" title="Ka'aba">Ka'aba</a> was made there (the clothing of the Kaba first started by the Yemeni kings of Saba). There used to be a Jewish community at Najran, renowned for the garments they manufactured. According to <a href="/wiki/Yemenite_Jews" title="Yemenite Jews">Yemenite Jewish</a> tradition, the Jews of Najran traced their origin to the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Lost_Tribes" title="Ten Lost Tribes">Ten Tribes</a>. Najran was also an important stopping place on the <a href="/wiki/Incense_Route" title="Incense Route">Incense Route</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Najran_museum_entrance.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Najran_museum_entrance.jpg/220px-Najran_museum_entrance.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Najran_museum_entrance.jpg/330px-Najran_museum_entrance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Najran_museum_entrance.jpg/440px-Najran_museum_entrance.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Najran_museum_entrance.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Najran Museum Entrance</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The history of Najrān can be traced back to 4,000 years ago. It was once occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>; in fact it was the first <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemeni</a> city to fall to the Romans on their way to the Yemeni kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Sheba" title="Sheba">Saba'</a>. Najrān's most prosperous trading time was during the 1st and 2nd centuries BC. In ancient times it was known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Ukhd%C5%ABd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Al-Ukhdūd (page does not exist)">Al-Ukhdūd</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similarly to other ancient place names in Arabia, Najrān may have originally been the name of the whole oasis including all towns and villages. The old name of the ruins now known as "al-Ukhdūd", which may have been the central town, was probably Ragmat.</p>
<p>Najrān was a focal point of the <a href="/wiki/Incense_Route" title="Incense Route">Incense Route</a>. All routes that left ancient Yemen to the north or west had to meet at Najrān, where the routes branched into two general directions, the ones leading north through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hijaz" title="Hijaz">Ḥijāz</a> towards Egypt and the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and those leading to the northeast towards <a href="/wiki/Gerrha" title="Gerrha">Gerrha</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Najrān was conquered around 685 BC by the <a href="/wiki/Sabaeans" title="Sabaeans">Sabean</a> <a href="/wiki/Mukarrib" title="Mukarrib">Mukarrib</a> (King) Karib'il Watar I King of Yemen. The later Sabean king Yithi'amar Bayin destroyed RagHmat around 510 BC. Najrān seems to have been under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minaean" title="Minaean">Minaean</a> or Sabean rule at different times during the next centuries and after that it was part of Yemen till 1937.</p>
<p>The Roman prefect of Egypt <a href="/wiki/Aelius_Gallus" title="Aelius Gallus">Aelius Gallus</a> led an costly, arduous and ultimately usuccessful expedition to conquer <a href="/wiki/Arabia_Felix" title="Arabia Felix">Arabia Felix</a> and won a battle near Najrān in 25 BC. He occupied the city and used it as a base from which to attack the Sabaean capital at <a href="/wiki/Ma%27rib" title="Ma'rib">Ma'rib</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Najrān was at this time the northernmost city of the realm of Saba'.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Himyarite_Kingdom" title="Himyarite Kingdom">Ḥimyarites</a> conquered the Sabeans in AD 280 they probably also took control of Najrān. Some time during the 3rd century the people of Najrān sided with the Abyssinians who sent a governor named Sqlmqlm" in inscriptions. The Ḥimyar King Ilsharah Yahdib crushed this rebellion.</p>
<p>The north Arabian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lakhmid" title="Lakhmid">Lakhmid</a> king Imru’ al-Qays ibn 'Amqu attacked Najrān in AD 328. Under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Axum" title="Axum">Axum</a>, the Christians in Najrān thrived and started an alliance with Aksum again at the beginning of the 6th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Christian_community">Early Christian community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early Christian community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christian_community_of_Najran" title="Christian community of Najran">Christian community of Najran</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> must have been introduced into Najrān, as in the rest of South Arabia, in the 5th century AD or perhaps a century earlier. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_people" title="Arab people">Arab</a> <a href="/wiki/Historiography_of_early_Islam" title="Historiography of early Islam">Muslim historian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Ishaq" title="Ibn Ishaq">Ibn Isḥāq</a>, Najran was the first place where Christianity took root in South Arabia. According to the contemporary sources, after seizing the throne of the Ḥimyarites, in ca. 518 or 523 <a href="/wiki/Dhu_Nuwas" title="Dhu Nuwas">Dhū Nuwās</a>, a Jewish king,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> attacked the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum" title="Kingdom of Aksum">Aksumite</a> (mainly Christian) garrison at <a href="/wiki/Zafar,_Yemen" title="Zafar, Yemen">Zafar</a>, capturing them and burning their churches. He then moved against Najrān, a <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> and Aksumite stronghold. After accepting the city's capitulation, he massacred those inhabitants who would not renounce Christianity. Estimates of the death toll from this event range up to 20,000 in some sources; a surviving letter (where he is called <i>Dimnon</i>) written by Simon, the bishop of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beth_Arsham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beth Arsham (page does not exist)">Beth Arsham</a> in 524 AD, recounts Dhū Nuwās's persecution in Najrān (modern al-Ukhdūd in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The persecution is apparently described and condemned in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Buruj" title="Al-Buruj">al-Buruj</a>:4).</p>
<p>Under the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Caliph ‘Umar</a>, the Christian community of Najrān was deported to Mesopotamia, on the grounds that no non-Muslims were to live in the Arabian Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town of Najrān was already an important centre of arms manufacture during the lifetime of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>. However, it was more famous for <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_Jewish_community">Former Jewish community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Former Jewish community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Saudi_Arabia" title="History of the Jews in Saudi Arabia">History of the Jews in Saudi Arabia</a></div>
<p>Najrān had a Jewish community dating back to pre-Islamic times, historically affiliated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bnei_Chorath" title="Bnei Chorath">Bnei Chorath</a> who were <a href="/wiki/Yemenite_Jews" title="Yemenite Jews">Yemenite Jews</a> that had conquered the city and ruled until the Christian invasion of Yemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> With the Saudi conquest of Najrān in 1934, persecution increased, and some 200 Jews of Najrān fled south to <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> between September and October 1949. The Saudi King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Saud_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Ibn Saud of Saudi Arabia">ʻAbd al-ʻAziz</a> demanded their return, but the Yemeni king <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Yahya" title="Ahmad bin Yahya">Aḥmad bin Yaḥyá</a> refused, because these refugees were Yemenite Jews. After settling in the Ḥashid Camp (also called Mahane Geula) they were airlifted to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> as part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Operation_Magic_Carpet_(Yemen)" title="Operation Magic Carpet (Yemen)">Operation Magic Carpet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Najran_as_part_of_Saudi_Arabia">Najran as part of Saudi Arabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Najran as part of Saudi Arabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Najrān joined the newly announced Kingdom of Saudi Arabia in 1934 as a result of the efforts and struggles of Sheikh Jābir Abū Sāq, the leader of one large clan of the Yam tribe. Starting in 1924, the forces of the former Yemeni king launched several unsuccessful raids to annex Najrān to the Yemeni Kingdom. The king of Yemen performed some new maneuvers to strengthen his tie with some of the Najrān tribal leaders to counter the strong relations of the people of Najrān with Bin Saʻūd. Then in 1932 the forces of Imam Yaḥyá of Yemen attacked Najrān with more than 50,000 troops, with all kinds of new weapons. Yām, as the dominant tribe in Najrān, along with some other loyalist Najranis, started strong resistance against the occupation forces. However, a strong segment of the tribal leaders in Najrān sided with the occupying power and some became passive, waiting to take a side at the end of the crisis. Sheikh Jābir Abū Sāq, the head of a clan of Yām at the time, managed to get quick support from King ʻAbd al-ʻAzīz Bin Saʻūd and was able to lead the Yām tribe and all of the Najrānī resistance fighting the Yemeni forces in all parts of Najrān and Bilād Yām. Later, in the spring of 1934, the army of Bin Saʻud under the command of Prince Saʻūd son of ʻAbd al-ʻAzīz carried out a massive campaign, surrounding Najrān from the north and northwest, and defeated the Yemeni army. Najrān became part of Saudi Arabia. In short, Najran has always been independent and ruled by its people (Yam) but they choose to be annexed to New Kingdom. Indeed, there was a strong treaty between King ʻAbd al-ʻAzīz and the people from Najrān indicating conditions to be respected by both sides.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests">Protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismailism" title="Ismailism">Ismailis</a>, a religious and ethnic minority with historic roots in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Najran_Province" title="Najran Province">Najrān Province</a> of southwestern Saudi Arabia, face increasing threats to their identity as a result of official discrimination. With the arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mishaal_bin_Saud" title="Mishaal bin Saud">Mishʻal bin Suʻūd</a> as the governor of Najrān in 1996, tension between local authorities and the Ismaili population increased, culminating in a confrontation between armed Ismāʻīlī demonstrators and police and army units outside the Holiday Inn hotel in Najrān city on April 2000. Official discrimination in Saudi Arabia against Ismāʻīlīs encompasses government employment, religious practices, and the justice system. Government officials exclude Ismāʻīlīs from decision making, and publicly disparage their faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The confrontation at the Holiday Inn in Najrān city on April 23, 2000, marked a watershed in Ismāʻīlī relations with the central government. Three months earlier, police had closed all Ismāʻīlī <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a> on a religious holiday. On April 23, after security forces and religious morality police arrested an Ismāʻīlī cleric, a large demonstration took place outside the Holiday Inn, where governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mishaal_bin_Saud" title="Mishaal bin Saud">Mishʻal</a> resided. After the governor refused for hours to meet the petitioners, an exchange of fire between security forces and armed demonstrators left two Ismāʻīlīs dead and, according to some government accounts, killed one policeman as well. Believing their religious identity to be under attack, Ismāʻili men erected defenses around <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Khushaywah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Khushaywah (page does not exist)">Khushaywah</a>, the seat of the Ismāʻīlī religious leader, <a href="/wiki/Da%27i_al-Mutlaq" title="Da'i al-Mutlaq">al-Dāʼiʻ al-Muṭlaq</a> (Absolute Guide), and the spiritual capital of Sulaymānī Ismāʻīlīs, a community with followers in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> as well as Saudi Arabia and Yemen. Khushaywah, which is an area of Najrān city, includes the Manṣūrah Mosque complex. The army surrounded the Ismāʻīlī positions and placed the city under its control. The standoff ended later the same day without further bloodshed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Execution">Execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 19, 2012, Murīʻ bin ʻAlī bin ʻIsá al-ʻAsīrī (مريع بن علي بن عيسى العسيري) was executed by beheading on charges of sorcery and witchcraft, according to <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Press_Agency" title="Saudi Press Agency">Saudi Press Agency</a> (SPA), the state news agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A statement was issued by the Interior Ministry, which said he was found in possession of books and talismans, and had also admitted adultery with two women. After an investigation, security authorities referred the case to the General Court, which sentenced him to death. The sentence was upheld by the Court of Cassation and the Supreme Judicial Council permanent panel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_ruins_and_artifacts">Archaeological ruins and artifacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Archaeological ruins and artifacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Najrān city is famous for its archeological significance. Old Najrān was surrounded by a circular wall, 220 by 230 meters, built of square stone with defensive balconies. It contained several unique buildings. There is also a cemetery south of the external wall. Excavations of this site have uncovered glass, metals, pottery, and bronze artifacts. Square and rectangular buildings have also been found. At Al-Ukhdūd which is south of Najrān city, carvings from those days and human bones can be seen. A museum displays, among other items, a bronze lion head. Najrān's landmarks include the "Rass" stone, a 2-meter-high granite stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Najran enjoys three different geographic landscapes, and they are: the oases, mountains, and desert at its eastside.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Najran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The climate for Najran is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_desert_climate" title="Hot desert climate">hot desert climate</a> type, typical of the Arabian Peninsula. The Rainfall is very sporadic in occurrence and light individual rainfalls. The yearly average high temperatures are lower than <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, despite being further south, due to its elevation.</p>
