<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of Nazi concentration camps</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>This article presents a partial list of the most prominent <b><a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Germany concentration camps</a> set up across Europe</b> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and during <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a>. A more complete list drawn up in 1967 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Ministry_of_Justice" title="German Ministry of Justice">German Ministry of Justice</a> names about 1,200 camps and subcamps in <a href="/wiki/German-occupied_Europe" title="German-occupied Europe">countries occupied by Nazi Germany</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bundesrecht_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bundesrecht-2">[2]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Virtual_Library" title="Jewish Virtual Library">Jewish Virtual Library</a> writes: "It is estimated that the Nazis established 15,000 camps in the occupied countries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishvirtuallibrary1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-jewishvirtuallibrary1-3">[3]</a></sup> The concentration camps are not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Extermination_camp" title="Extermination camp">extermination camps</a> which were designed and built exclusively to kill prisoners on a massive scale immediately upon arrival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEn-1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HEn-1-4">[4]</a></sup> The extermination camps of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Reinhard" title="Operation Reinhard">Operation Reinhard</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Be%C5%82%C5%BCec_extermination_camp" title="Bełżec extermination camp">Belzec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sobib%C3%B3r_extermination_camp" title="Sobibór extermination camp">Sobibor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treblinka_extermination_camp" title="Treblinka extermination camp">Treblinka</a> served as "death factories" in which German SS and police murdered nearly 2,700,000 Jews either by asphyxiation with poison gas or by shooting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEn-1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-HEn-1-4">[4]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the concentration camps listed herein served primarily as detention and <a href="/wiki/Forced_labour_under_German_rule_during_World_War_II" title="Forced labour under German rule during World War II">slave labor exploitation centers</a>. Most of them were destroyed by the Nazis in an attempt to hide the evidence of war crimes and crimes against humanity; nevertheless tens of thousands of prisoners sent on <a href="/wiki/Death_march" title="Death march">death marches</a> were liberated by the Allies afterward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ushmm.org_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ushmm.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concentration camps held large groups of prisoners without trial or judicial process. In modern historiography, the term refers to a place of systemic mistreatment, starvation, forced labour and murder. In 1933-1939, before the onset of war, most prisoners consisted of German Communists, Socialists, Social Democrats, Roma, Jehovah's Witnesses, homosexuals, and persons accused of 'asocial' or socially 'deviant' behavior by the Nazis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEn-2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HEn-2-6">[6]</a></sup> They have not been utilized to sustain the German war effort, unlike the prisoners of 42,500 camps and ghettos in which an estimated 15 to 20 million people were imprisoned and often pressed into slavery during the subsequent years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Helman-7">[7]</a></sup> according to research by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Holocaust_Memorial_Museum" title="United States Holocaust Memorial Museum">United States Holocaust Memorial Museum</a> conducted more recently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helman_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Helman-7">[7]</a></sup> Though the term 'concentration camp' is often used as a general term for all Nazi camps, there were in fact several types of concentration camps in the Nazi camp system during World War II. Holocaust scholars make a clear distinction between death camps and concentration camps which served a number of war related purposes including prison facilities, labor camps, <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> camps, and transit camps among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V-L_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-V-L-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The system of about 20,000 camps in Germany and Nazi-Occupied Europe played a pivotal role in sustaining the Nazi reign of terror economically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEn-2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HEn-2-6">[6]</a></sup> Some of the data presented in this table originates from the monograph titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_Against_the_Jews" title="The War Against the Jews">The War Against the Jews</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Dawidowicz" title="Lucy Dawidowicz">Lucy Dawidowicz</a> among similar others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ushmm.2Fsearch_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ushmm.2Fsearch-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
