<p><b>Yuji Naka</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">中 裕司</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Naka Yūji</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born September 17, 1965)</span> is a Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_programmer" title="Video game programmer">video game programmer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_designer" title="Video game designer">designer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Video_game_producer" title="Video game producer">producer</a> best known as the former head of <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Team" title="Sonic Team">Sonic Team</a>, where he was the lead programmer of the original <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(series)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (series)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i> series of games on the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a>. Since 2006, he has been the head of <a href="/wiki/Prope" title="Prope">Prope</a>, a game company he founded after leaving Sonic Team that same year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuji_Naka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Naka learned how to program by replicating and debugging video game code printed in magazines. The experience prompted him to study <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assembler_(computer_programming)" title="Assembler (computer programming)">assemblers</a> and practice writing code during his school classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG-35_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RG-35-1">[1]</a></sup> After graduating high school, Naka decided to skip <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> and stay in his home town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1983, Naka saw that Sega was looking for programming assistants and decided to apply. After a brief interview, he was hired and his first project was a game called <i><a href="/wiki/Girl%27s_Garden" title="Girl's Garden">Girl's Garden</a></i>, which he and <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Kawaguchi_(composer)" title="Hiroshi Kawaguchi (composer)">Hiroshi Kawaguchi</a> created together as part of their training process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Their boss was impressed and decided to publish the game, and it earned them notice among their peers and Japanese gamers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG-35_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-RG-35-1">[1]</a></sup> Naka's abilities as a programmer were further demonstrated in 1987 for his work on <i><a href="/wiki/Phantasy_Star" title="Phantasy Star">Phantasy Star</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Master_System" title="Master System">Master System</a>, where he was responsible for the impressive pseudo-3D animation effects present in the game's first-person dungeons.</p>
<p>His true breakthrough, however, came in 1991 when he programmed the original <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(1991_video_game)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (1991 video game)"><i>Sonic the Hedgehog</i></a> game for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Genesis</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Naoto_Ohshima" title="Naoto Ohshima">Naoto Ohshima</a> designing the characters and <a href="/wiki/Hirokazu_Yasuhara" title="Hirokazu Yasuhara">Hirokazu Yasuhara</a> creating the stages. The origins of <i>Sonic</i> can be traced farther back to a tech demo created by Naka, who had developed an algorithm that allowed a sprite to move smoothly on a curve by determining its position with a <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix" title="Dot matrix">dot matrix</a>. Naka's original prototype was a platform game that involved a fast-moving character rolling in a ball through a long winding tube, and this concept was subsequently fleshed out with Oshima's character design and levels conceived by Yasuhara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yasuhara_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuhara-4">[4]</a></sup> Following <i>Sonic The Hedgehog's</i> release, Naka moved to Sega's U.S. branch, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Technical_Institute" title="Sega Technical Institute">Sega Technical Institute</a>, where he worked with famed American designer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cerny" title="Mark Cerny">Mark Cerny</a> on the follow-up in conjunction with the original team back in Japan, now known as "<a href="/wiki/Sonic_Team" title="Sonic Team">Sonic Team</a>". This partnership between the Eastern and Western teams continued through the development of <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_3" title="Sonic the Hedgehog 3">Sonic the Hedgehog 3</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_%26_Knuckles" title="Sonic &amp; Knuckles">Sonic &amp; Knuckles</a></i>, though the bulk of the development duties shifted back to <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Team" title="Sonic Team">Sonic Team</a> in Japan for those titles, which Naka had also returned to by that time.</p>
<p>After the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_%26_Knuckles" title="Sonic &amp; Knuckles">Sonic &amp; Knuckles</a></i>, Naka was moved up to the role of producer at Sega Enterprises in Japan. During his tenure in that position, he oversaw titles including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nights_into_Dreams..." title="Nights into Dreams...">Nights into Dreams...</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Burning_Rangers" title="Burning Rangers">Burning Rangers</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Sega_Saturn" title="Sega Saturn">Sega Saturn</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Adventure" title="Sonic Adventure">Sonic Adventure</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Phantasy_Star_Online" title="Phantasy Star Online">Phantasy Star Online</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Dreamcast</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Billy_Hatcher_and_the_Giant_Egg" title="Billy Hatcher and the Giant Egg">Billy Hatcher and the Giant Egg</a></i> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube" title="Nintendo GameCube">Nintendo GameCube</a>; and the "<a href="/wiki/EyeToy" title="EyeToy">EyeToy</a>" game <i><a href="/wiki/Sega_Superstars" title="Sega Superstars">Sega Superstars</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>.</p>
<p>On March 16, 2006, Naka announced that he intended to create his own game studio, <a href="/wiki/Prope" title="Prope">Prope</a>, and that he would be leaving Sega to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Naka stated that he considered it a benefit to be able to create games other than <i>Sonic the Hedgehog</i> titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Following <a href="/wiki/Naoto_Ohshima" title="Naoto Ohshima">Naoto Ohshima</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Hirokazu_Yasuhara" title="Hirokazu Yasuhara">Hirokazu Yasuhara</a>'s departure by 2002, Naka was the final member of the original creative core that created <i>Sonic the Hedgehog</i> to leave <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>.</p>
