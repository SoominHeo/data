<p>The <b>Nakajima G5N <i>Shinzan</i></b> (深山"Deep Mountain") was a four-engined long-range <a href="/wiki/Heavy_bomber" title="Heavy bomber">heavy bomber</a> designed and built for the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a> prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The Navy designation was "<b>Experimental 13-Shi Attack Bomber</b>"; the Allied code name was "<b>Liz</b>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakajima_G5N&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Project_Z" title="Project Z">Project Z</a></div>
<p>The Nakajima G5N <i>Shinzan</i> originated due to the Imperial Japanese Navy's interest in developing a long-range attack bomber capable of carrying heavy loads of bombs or torpedoes a minimum distance of 3,000 nmi (5,600 km; 3,500 mi). To meet this requirement, it became apparent a four-engine lay-out would be necessary. As Japanese aircraft manufacturers lacked experience in building such large complex aircraft, the Navy was forced to search for a suitable existing foreign-made model upon which to base the new design. It settled on the American <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4E" title="Douglas DC-4E">Douglas DC-4E</a> <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a>. In 1939 the sole prototype of this airliner (previously rejected by American airline companies) was purchased by Nippon Koku K.K. (Japan Airlines Co) and clandestinely handed over to the <a href="/wiki/Nakajima_Aircraft_Company" title="Nakajima Aircraft Company">Nakajima Aircraft Company</a> for dismantling and inspection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francillon1979p423_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francillon1979p423-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design that emerged from this study was for an all-metal mid-wing <a href="/wiki/Monoplane" title="Monoplane">monoplane</a> with fabric-covered control surfaces and powered by four 1,870 hp Nakajima <a href="/wiki/Nakajima_Mamoru" title="Nakajima Mamoru">NK7A Mamori</a> 11 air-cooled radial engines driving four-bladed propellers. Notable features included a long ventral bomb-bay, glazed nose and twin tailfins replacing the DC-4E's distinctive triple rudder. The DC-4E's retractable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tricycle_gear" title="Tricycle gear">tricycle undercarriage</a> was retained, as well as the original wing form and powerplant arrangement. Defensive armament comprised one <a href="/wiki/Type_99_cannon" title="Type 99 cannon">20mm Type 99 Model 1 cannon</a> each in a power-operated dorsal and tail turret plus single-mount hand-operated 7.7mm <a href="/wiki/Type_92_machine_gun" title="Type 92 machine gun">Type 92 machine guns</a> in the nose, ventral and waist positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francillon1979p425_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francillon1979p425-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first prototype G5N1 made its maiden flight on 14:35 8 April 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAW_1984_11_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAW_1984_11-1">[1]</a></sup> Overall performance proved disappointingly poor however, due to a combination of excessive weight, the unreliability of the Mamori engines and the complexity of the design. Only three more prototypes were completed. In an attempt to salvage the project, two additional airframes were fitted with 1,530 hp <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Kasei" title="Mitsubishi Kasei">Mitsubishi MK4B 12 "Kasei"</a> engines and redesignated G5N2s. Although the Mitsubishi engines were more reliable than the original Mamori 11s, the aircraft was now even more hopelessly underpowered and further development of the type was halted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francillon1979p425_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Francillon1979p425-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakajima_G5N&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of the six completed <i>Shinzans</i>, four of them were relegated for use as long-range Navy transports under the designation <b>G5N2-L Shinzan-Kai Transport</b>. The Allies allocated the code-name "<b>Liz</b>" to the aircraft, in the expectation it would be used as a bomber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francillon1979p425_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Francillon1979p425-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAW_1984_11_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAW_1984_11-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAW_1984_32_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAW_1984_32-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>G5N1 <i>Experimental Type 13 Land-based Attack Bomber "Shinzan"</i> (13試陸上攻撃機 深山 <i>13-Shi Rikujō Kōgekiki "Shinzan"</i>)</dt>
<dd>Prototype, two built. Initial named <i>Experimental Type 13 Large-size land-based Attack Bomber</i> (13試大型陸上攻撃機 <i>13-Shi Ōgata Rikujō Kōgekiki</i>). Four-engined <a href="/wiki/Heavy_bomber" title="Heavy bomber">heavy bomber</a>/<a href="/wiki/Torpedo_bomber" title="Torpedo bomber">torpedo bomber</a>. Three-blade propeller, mounted four <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Kasei" title="Mitsubishi Kasei">Mitsubishi MK4B Kasei 12</a> (1,530 hp) radial engines.</dd>
<dt>G5N2 <i>Test production "Shinzan Kai"</i> (試製深山改 <i>Shisei "Shinzan Kai"</i>)</dt>
<dd>Supplementary prototype, four built. Four-blade propeller, mounted four <a href="/wiki/Nakajima_Mamoru" title="Nakajima Mamoru">Nakajima NK7A Mamori 11</a> (1,870 hp) radial engines. All G5N2s were rebuilt to G5N2-L in 1943.</dd>
<dt>G5N2-L <i>"Shinzan Kai" Freighter</i> (深山改輸送機 <i>"Sinzan Kai" Yusōki</i>)</dt>
<dd>Long-range Navy transport conversion. All G5N2-Ls were deployed to 1021st <i>Kōkutai</i>, Katori Air Base.</dd>
<dt>Nakajima Ki-68</dt>
<dd>Proposed Army bomber prototype version of the G5N1. Engines were planned Mitsubishi Ha-101, Ha-104, Ha-107, Nakajima Ha-39 or Ha-103 engines. Discontinued in 1941.</dd>
<dt>Kawasaki Ki-85</dt>
<dd>Proposed Army bomber version of the G5N1. Four Mitsubishi Ha-111M engines. Full-scale mock-up was built in 1942, discontinued in May 1943.</dd>
</dl>
