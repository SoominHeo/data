<p>The <a href="/wiki/Epipaleolithic" title="Epipaleolithic">Epipaleolithic</a> <b>Natufian culture</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/tj/ 't' in 'tune'">tj</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> existed from around 12,500 to 9,500 BC in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, a region in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean" title="Eastern Mediterranean">Eastern Mediterranean</a>. It was unusual in that it supported a <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> or semi-sedentary population before the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. The Natufian communities may be the ancestors of the builders of the first <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> settlements of the region, which may have been the earliest in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some evidence suggests deliberate cultivation of <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, by the Natufian culture, at <a href="/wiki/Tell_Abu_Hureyra" title="Tell Abu Hureyra">Tell Abu Hureyra</a>, the site of earliest evidence of agriculture in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore2000_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore2000-1">[1]</a></sup> Generally, though, Natufians exploited wild cereals. Animals hunted included <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">gazelles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kottak2005_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kottak2005-2">[2]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Christy_G._Turner_II" title="Christy G. Turner II">Christy G. Turner II</a>, there is archaeological and physical anthropological evidence for a relationship between the modern <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">Semitic</a>-speaking populations of the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and the Natufians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Garrod" title="Dorothy Garrod">Dorothy Garrod</a> coined the term Natufian based on her excavations at <a href="/wiki/Shuqba_cave" title="Shuqba cave">Shuqba cave</a> in Wadi an-Natuf, in the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judean_Mountains" title="Judean Mountains">Judean Mountains</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Garrod_1928.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="830" data-file-width="645" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Garrod_1928.jpg/220px-Garrod_1928.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Garrod_1928.jpg/330px-Garrod_1928.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Garrod_1928.jpg/440px-Garrod_1928.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Garrod_1928.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Garrod" title="Dorothy Garrod">Dorothy Garrod</a> (centre) discovered the Natufian culture in 1928</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Natufian culture was discovered by British archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Garrod" title="Dorothy Garrod">Dorothy Garrod</a> during her excavations of <a href="/wiki/Shuqba_cave" title="Shuqba cave">Shuqba cave</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judean_Hills" title="Judean Hills">Judaean Hills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar-Yosef_159_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bar-Yosef_159-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Prior to the 1930s, the majority of archaeological work taking place in <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> was <a href="/wiki/Biblical_archaeology" title="Biblical archaeology">biblical archaeology</a> focused on historic periods, and little was known about the region's prehistory. In 1928, Garrod was invited by the the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_School_of_Archaeology_in_Jerusalem" title="British School of Archaeology in Jerusalem">British School of Archaeology in Jerusalem</a> (BSAJ) to excavate Shuqba cave, where prehistoric stone tools had been discovered by a French priest named Alexis Mallon four years earlier. She discovered a layer sandwiched between the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Palaeolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> deposits characterised by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Microlith" title="Microlith">microliths</a>. She identified this with the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a>, a transitional period between the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Palaeolithic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> which was well represented in Europe but had not yet been found in the Near East. A year later, when she discovered similar material at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=El-Wad_Terrace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="El-Wad Terrace (page does not exist)">el-Wad Terrace</a>, Garrod suggested the name the Natufian culture, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wadi_an-Natuf" title="Wadi an-Natuf">Wadi an-Natuf</a> that ran close to Shuqba. Over the next two decades Garrod found Natufian material at several of her pioneering excavations in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a> region, including el-Wad, <a href="/wiki/Kebara_Cave" title="Kebara Cave">Kebara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tabun_Cave" title="Tabun Cave">Tabun</a>, as did the French archaeologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Neuville&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="René Neuville (page does not exist)">René Neuville</a>, firmly establishing the Natufian in the regional prehistoric chronology. As early as 1931, both Garrod and Neuville drew attention to the presence of stone <a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">sickles</a> in Natufian assemblages and the possibility that this represented very early agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dating">Dating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> places this culture from the terminal <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> to the very beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>, from 12,500 to 9,500 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">BC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munro2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Munro2003-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The period is commonly split into two subperiods: Early Natufian (12,500–10,800 BC) and Late Natufian (10,800–9,500 BC). The Late Natufian most likely occurred in tandem with the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> (10,800 to 9,500 BC). The <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> hosts more than a hundred kinds of cereals, fruits, nuts, and other edible parts of plants, and the flora of the Levant during the Natufian period was not the dry, barren, and thorny landscape of today, but rather <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar-Yosef_159_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bar-Yosef_159-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors_and_associated_cultures">Precursors and associated cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Precursors and associated cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Natufian developed in the same region as the earlier <a href="/wiki/Kebaran" title="Kebaran">Kebaran</a> industry. It is generally seen as a successor, which evolved out of elements within that preceding culture. There were also other industries in the region, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mushabian_culture" title="Mushabian culture">Mushabian culture</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai">Sinai</a>, which are sometimes distinguished from the Kebaran or believed to have been involved in the evolution of the Natufian.</p>
<p>More generally there has been discussion of the similarities of these cultures with those found in coastal North Africa. Graeme Barker notes there are: "similarities in the respective archaeological records of the Natufian culture of the Levant and of contemporary foragers in coastal North Africa across the late Pleistocene and early Holocene boundary".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ofer_Bar-Yosef" title="Ofer Bar-Yosef">Ofer Bar-Yosef</a> has argued that there are signs of influences coming from North Africa to the Levant, citing the <a href="/wiki/Microburin" title="Microburin">microburin</a> technique and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microlithic" title="Microlithic">microlithic</a> forms such as arched backed bladelets and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_Mouillah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La Mouillah (page does not exist)">La Mouillah</a> points."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar-Yosef_29_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bar-Yosef_29-8">[8]</a></sup> But recent research has shown that the presence of arched backed bladelets, La Mouillah points, and the use of the microburin technique was already apparent in the Nebekian industry of the Eastern Levant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> And Maher et al. state that, "Many technological nuances that have often been always highlighted as signiﬁcant during the Natuﬁan were already present during the Early and Middle EP [Epipalaeolithic] and do not, in most cases, represent a radical departure in knowledge, tradition, or behavior."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Authors such as <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Ehret" title="Christopher Ehret">Christopher Ehret</a> have built upon the little evidence available to develop scenarios of intensive usage of plants having built up first in North Africa, as a precursor to the development of true farming in the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a>, but such suggestions are considered highly speculative until more North African archaeological evidence can be gathered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehret_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehret-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bellwood_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bellwood-12">[12]</a></sup> In fact, Weiss et al. have shown that the earliest known intensive usage of plants was in the Levant 23,000 years ago at the Ohalo II site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/C._Loring_Brace" title="C. Loring Brace">C. Loring Brace</a> (1993) cross-analysed the craniometric traits of Natufian specimens with those of various ancient and modern groups from the Near East, Africa and Europe. The Late Pleistocene Epipalaeolithic Natufian sample was described as problematic due to its small size (consisting of only three males and one female), as well as the lack of a comparative sample from the Natufians' putative descendants in the Neolithic Near East. Brace observed that the Natufian fossils lay between those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niger-Congo_languages" title="Niger-Congo languages">Niger-Congo</a>-speaking populations and the other samples, which he suggested may point to a Sub-Saharan influence in their constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Subsequent <a href="/wiki/Ancient_DNA" title="Ancient DNA">ancient DNA</a> analysis of Natufian skeletal remains by Lazaridis et al. (2016) found that the specimens instead mainly belonged to a Basal Eurasian ancestral component (see <a href="/wiki/Natufian_culture#Genetics" title="Natufian culture">genetics</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lazaridis2016_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lazaridis2016-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Bar-Yosef and Belfer-Cohen, "It seems that certain preadaptive traits, developed already by the Kebaran and Geometric Kebaran populations within the Mediterranean park forest, played an important role in the emergence of the new socioeconomic system known as the Natufian culture."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements">Settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg/220px-Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg/330px-Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg/440px-Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Natufian-SupportingWall-Elwad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Remains of a wall of a Natufian house</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Settlements occur in the <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> belt where <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pistacia" title="Pistacia">Pistacia</a></i> species dominated. The underbrush of this open woodland was grass with high frequencies of grain. The high mountains of <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Lebanon" title="Anti-Lebanon">Anti-Lebanon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> areas of the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a> desert in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai">Sinai</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syro-Arabian_desert" title="Syro-Arabian desert">Syro-Arabian desert</a> in the east were much less favoured for Natufian settlement, presumably due to both their lower carrying capacity and the company of other groups of foragers who exploited this region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar-Yosef_114_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bar-Yosef_114-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The habitations of the Natufian are semi-subterranean, often with a dry-stone foundation. The superstructure was probably made of brushwood. No traces of <a href="/wiki/Mudbrick" title="Mudbrick">mudbrick</a> have been found, which became common in the following <a href="/wiki/Pre-Pottery_Neolithic_A" title="Pre-Pottery Neolithic A">Pre-Pottery Neolithic A</a> (PPNA). The round houses have a diameter between three and six meters, and they contain a central round or subrectangular fireplace. In <a href="/wiki/Ain_Mallaha" title="Ain Mallaha">Ain Mallaha</a> traces of <a href="/wiki/Posthole" title="Posthole">postholes</a> have been identified. "Villages" can cover over 1,000 square meters. Smaller settlements have been interpreted by some researchers as camps. Traces of rebuilding in almost all excavated settlements seem to point to a frequent relocation, indicating a temporary abandonment of the settlement. Settlements have been estimated to house 100–150 people, but there are three categories: small, medium, and large, ranging from 15 sq. m to 1,000 sq. m. There are no definite indications of storage facilities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Material_culture">Material culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Material culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithics">Lithics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lithics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Natufian had a <a href="/wiki/Microlith" title="Microlith">microlithic</a> industry centered on short <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">blades</a> and bladelets. The <a href="/wiki/Microburin_technique" title="Microburin technique">microburin technique</a> was used. Geometric microliths include <a href="/wiki/Lunate" title="Lunate">lunates</a>, trapezes, and triangles. There are backed blades as well. A special type of <a href="/wiki/Retouch_(lithics)" title="Retouch (lithics)">retouch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Helwan_retouch" title="Helwan retouch">Helwan retouch</a>) is characteristic for the early Natufian. In the late Natufian, the Harif-point, a typical <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead" title="Arrowhead">arrowhead</a> made from a regular blade, became common in the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a>. Some scholars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2012)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> use it to define a separate culture, the <a href="/wiki/Harifian" title="Harifian">Harifian</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">Sickle</a> blades also appear for the first time in the Natufian lithic industry. The characteristic <a href="/wiki/Sickle-gloss" title="Sickle-gloss">sickle-gloss</a> shows that they were used to cut the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>-rich stems of cereals, indirectly suggesting the existence of incipient agriculture. Shaft straighteners made of <a href="/wiki/Ground_stone" title="Ground stone">ground stone</a> indicate the practice of <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archery</a>. There are heavy ground-stone bowl <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortar_(bowl)" title="Mortar (bowl)">mortars</a> as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <i>Ain Sakhri lovers</i>, a carved stone object held at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, is the oldest known depiction of a couple having sex. It was found in the Ain Sakhri cave in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judean_desert" title="Judean desert">Judean desert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_AinSak_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_AinSak-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burials">Burials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Burials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Burials made of shell, teeth (of <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a>), bones, and stone. There are pendants, bracelets, necklaces, earrings, and belt-ornaments as well.</p>
<p>In 2008, the 12,000-years-old grave of an apparently significant Natufian female was discovered in a ceremonial pit located in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hilazon_Tachtit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hilazon Tachtit (page does not exist)">Hilazon Tachtit</a> cave in northern Israel. Media reports referred to this person as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaman" title="Shaman">shaman</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeo2008_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeo2008-20">[20]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Witch_doctor" title="Witch doctor">witch doctor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mail_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mail-21">[21]</a></sup> The burial contained the remains of at least at least three <a href="/wiki/Aurochs" title="Aurochs">aurochs</a> and 70 tortoises, all of which are thought to have been brought to the site during a funeral feast. The body was surrounded by tortoise shells, the pelvis of a leopard, forearm of a wild boar, wingtip of a <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a>, and skull of a <a href="/wiki/Beech_marten" title="Beech marten">stone marten</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burston_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burston-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_distance_exchange">Long distance exchange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Long distance exchange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At Ain Mallaha (in Northern Israel), Anatolian <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a> and shellfish from the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> valley have been found. The source of <a href="/wiki/Malachite" title="Malachite">malachite</a> beads is still unknown.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_finds">Other finds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other finds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There was a rich <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_industry" title="Bone industry">bone industry</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Harpoon" title="Harpoon">harpoons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_hook" title="Fish hook">fish hooks</a>. Stone and bone were worked into pendants and other ornaments. There are a few human figurines made of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> (El-Wad, Ain Mallaha, Ain Sakhri), but the favourite subject of representative art seems to have been animals. Ostrich-shell containers have been found in the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsistence">Subsistence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Subsistence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Natufian people lived by hunting and gathering. The preservation of plant remains is poor because of the soil conditions, but wild cereals, <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pistachio" title="Pistachio">pistachios</a> may have been collected. Animal bones show that <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">gazelle</a> (<i>Gazella gazella</i> and <i>Gazella subgutturosa</i>) were the main prey. Additionally <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aurochs" title="Aurochs">aurochs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a> were hunted in the <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> zone, as well as <a href="/wiki/Onager" title="Onager">onagers</a> and caprids (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibex" title="Ibex">ibex</a>). Water fowl and freshwater fish formed part of the diet in the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a> valley. Animal bones from Salibiya I (12,300 – 10,800 BP) have been interpreted as evidence for communal hunts with nets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_agriculture">Development of agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Development of agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to one theory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeo2008_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeo2008-20">[20]</a></sup> it was a sudden change in <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> event (ca. 10,800 to 9500 BCE), that inspired the development of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. The Younger Dryas was a 1,000-year-long interruption in the higher temperatures prevailing since the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, which produced a sudden drought in the Levant. This would have endangered the wild cereals, which could no longer compete with dryland scrub, but upon which the population had become dependent to sustain a relatively large sedentary population. By artificially clearing scrub and planting seeds obtained from elsewhere, they began to practice agriculture. However, this theory of the origin of agriculture is controversial in the scientific community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domesticated_dog">Domesticated dog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Domesticated dog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_domestic_dog#Archaeological_evidence" title="Origin of the domestic dog">Origin of the domestic dog § Archaeological evidence</a></div>
<p>It is at Natufian sites that some of the earliest archaeological evidence for the <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domestication</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> is found. At the Natufian site of Ain Mallaha in Israel, dated to 12,000 BCE, the remains of an elderly human and a four-to-five-month-old puppy were found buried together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clutton-Brock1995_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clutton-Brock1995-25">[25]</a></sup> At another Natufian site at the cave of Hayonim, humans were found buried with two canids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clutton-Brock1995_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clutton-Brock1995-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">Genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to ancient DNA analyses conducted by Lazaridis et al. (2016) on six Natufian skeletal remains from present-day northern Israel, the Natufians carried the Y-DNA haplogroup <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_E-Z827#E-Z830_.28E1b1b1b2.29" title="Haplogroup E-Z827">E-Z830</a> or E1b1b1b2, whose ancestral paternal clade is <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_E-M123" title="Haplogroup E-M123">E1b1b-M123</a>. One Natufian individual was also found to belong to the <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_N_(mtDNA)" title="Haplogroup N (mtDNA)">N1b</a> mtDNA haplogroup. In terms of autosomal DNA, these Natufians carried high frequencies of the Basal Eurasian component, but were slightly distinct from the northern Anatolian populations that contributed to the peopling of Europe. The scientists suggest that the Levantine early farmers may instead have spread southward into East Africa, bringing along a West Eurasian ancestral component separate from that which would arrive later in North Africa. Elsewhere on the continent, the Natufians were found to share no significant genetic affinities with Sub-Saharan African populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lazaridis2016_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lazaridis2016-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natufian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the period involved makes it difficult to speculate on any language associated with the Natufian culture, linguists who believe it is possible to speculate this far back in time have written on this subject. As with other Natufian subjects, opinions tend to either emphasize North African connections or Eurasian connections. The view that the Natufians spoke an Afro-Asiatic language is accepted by <a href="/wiki/Vitaly_Shevoroshkin" title="Vitaly Shevoroshkin">Vitaly Shevoroshkin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Militarev" title="Alexander Militarev">Alexander Militarev</a> and others have argued that the Natufian may represent the culture which spoke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Afroasiatic" title="Proto-Afroasiatic">Proto-Afroasiatic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> which he in turn believes has a <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> origin associated with the concept of <a href="/wiki/Nostratic_languages" title="Nostratic languages">Nostratic languages</a>. The possibility of Natufians speaking proto-Afro-Asiatic, and that the language was introduced into Africa from the Levant, is approved by <a href="/wiki/Colin_Renfrew,_Baron_Renfrew_of_Kaimsthorn" title="Colin Renfrew, Baron Renfrew of Kaimsthorn">Colin Renfrew</a> with caution, as a possible hypothesis for proto-Afro-Asiatic dispersal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some scholars, for example <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Ehret" title="Christopher Ehret">Christopher Ehret</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Blench" title="Roger Blench">Roger Blench</a> and others, contend that the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_Urheimat" title="Afroasiatic Urheimat">Afroasiatic Urheimat</a> is to be found in North or North East Africa, probably in the area of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blench2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-blench2006-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehret2004_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehret2004-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bernal1987_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-bernal1987-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bender1997_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-bender1997-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militarev2005_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-militarev2005-33">[33]</a></sup> Within this group, Ehret, who like Militarev believes Afroasiatic may already have been in existence in the Natufian period, would associate Natufians only with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Eastern" title="Near Eastern">Near Eastern</a> pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Semitic" title="Proto-Semitic">Proto-Semitic</a> branch of Afroasiatic.</p>
