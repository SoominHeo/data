<p>The <b>Nakh languages</b> are a small family of languages spoken chiefly by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nakh_peoples" title="Nakh peoples">Nakh peoples</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ingushetia" title="Ingushetia">Ingushetia</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Chechen_diaspora" title="Chechen diaspora">Chechen diaspora</a> (mainly in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>).</p>
<p>The Nakh languages were historically classified as an independent <b>North-Central Caucasian</b> family, but are now recognized as a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">Northeast Caucasian</a> family. They are believed to have split off some 5,000–6,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vainakh_languages" title="Vainakh languages">Vainakh languages</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> with two literary languages:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chechen_language" title="Chechen language">Chechen</a> – approximately 1,330,000 speakers (2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EthChe_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EthChe-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ingush_language" title="Ingush language">Ingush</a> – approximately 413,000 speakers (2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EthInh_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EthInh-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bats_language" title="Bats language">Bats</a> or Batsbi – approximately 3,420 (2000),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EthBbl_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EthBbl-5">[5]</a></sup> spoken mostly in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zemo-Alvani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zemo-Alvani (page does not exist)">Zemo-Alvani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>. Not mutually intelligible with Chechen or Ingush.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_voicing_of_ejective_consonants">The voicing of ejective consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The voicing of ejective consonants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nakh languages are relevant to the <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_theory" title="Glottalic theory">glottalic theory</a> of Indo-European, because the Veinakh branch has undergone the voicing of ejectives that has been postulated but widely derided as improbable in that family. In initial position, Bats ejectives correspond to Vainakh ejectives, but in non-initial position to Vainakh voiced consonants. (The exception is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*qʼ</span>, which remains an ejective in Vainakh.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinct_Nakh_languages">Extinct Nakh languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extinct Nakh languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many obscure ancient languages or peoples have been postulated by scholars of the Caucasus as Nakh, many in the South Caucasus. None of these have been confirmed; most are classified as Nakh on the basis of placenames.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".C3.88rsh">Èrsh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Èrsh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/%C3%88rsh_language" title="Èrsh language">Èrsh language</a>, language of the Èrs who inhabited Northern Armenia, and then, (possibly) later, mainly <a href="/wiki/Hereti" title="Hereti">Hereti</a> in Southeast Georgia and Northwest <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>. This is considered to be more or less confirmed as Nakh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaimoukha_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jaimoukha-7">[7]</a></sup> They were assimilated eventually, and their language was replaced by Georgian or Azeri.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malkh">Malkh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Malkh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The language of the <a href="/wiki/Malkh" title="Malkh">Malkhs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaimoukha_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jaimoukha-7">[7]</a></sup> (whose name, malkh, refers to the sun) in the North Caucasus, who lived in modern day <a href="/wiki/Kabardino-Balkaria" title="Kabardino-Balkaria">Kabardino-Balkaria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karachay%E2%80%93Cherkessia" title="Karachay–Cherkessia">Karachay–Cherkessia</a>, and once briefly conquered <a href="/wiki/Ubykhia" title="Ubykhia">Ubykhia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abkhazia" title="Abkhazia">Abkhazia</a>. They were conquered first by Scythian-speaking Alan tribes and then by Turkic tribes, and seem to have largely abandoned their homeland and found shelter among the <a href="/wiki/Chechens" title="Chechens">Chechens</a>, leading to the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Teip" title="Teip">teip</a> named after them. Those who stayed behind were either wiped out or assimilated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kakh">Kakh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kakh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The language of the Kakh, old inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Kakheti" title="Kakheti">Kakheti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tusheti" title="Tusheti">Tusheti</a> in Eastern Georgia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaimoukha_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jaimoukha-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Kakh apparently called themselves Kabatsas and their territory Kakh-Batsa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They may or may not be ancestral to the modern Bats, and they may or may not be closely related to them. They were assimilated (completely or almost completely, depending on if one believes the theory that the Bats are descended from them) by the Kartlians to speak Georgian.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gligvic">Gligvic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gligvic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gligvs, a mysterious people in the North Caucasus attributed by Georgian historians to be a Nakh people. They may be ancestral to the Ingush, but the term used by Georgians consistently for the Ingush is "Kist", causing large amounts of confusion (as the Nakh people in Georgia who speak Chechen are also called "Kists").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tsanar">Tsanar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tsanar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The language of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsanars" title="Tsanars">Tsanars</a> in historical Georgia is thought by many historians to be Nakh, based on <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">place names</a>, geographic location, and other such information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaimoukha_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jaimoukha-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dval">Dval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The language of the <a href="/wiki/Dvals" title="Dvals">Dvals</a> is thought to be Nakh by many historians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaimoukha_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jaimoukha-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melikishvilli_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Melikishvilli-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuznetsov_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuznetsov-14">[14]</a></sup> though there is a rivaling camp arguing for its status as a close relative of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ossetic_language" title="Ossetic language">Ossetic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuznetsov_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kuznetsov-14">[14]</a></sup> Various backing for the Nakh theory (different scholars use different arguments) includes the presence of Nakh placenames in former Dval territory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuznetsov_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kuznetsov-14">[14]</a></sup> evidence of Nakh–Svan contact which probably would've required the Nakh nature of the Dvals or people there before them,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaimoukha_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jaimoukha-7">[7]</a></sup> and the presence of a foreign-origin Dval clan among the Chechens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melikishvilli_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Melikishvilli-13">[13]</a></sup> seemingly implying that the Dvals found shelter (like the Malkhs are known to have done) among the Chechens from the conquest of their land by foreign invaders (presumably Ossetes). The Dvals were assimilated by the Georgians (and possibly the Ossetes as well) and conquered by the Ossetes in the north. It is thought that Dval did not go fully extinct until the 18th century, making the Dvals the most recent Nakh people known to have died out (if they were Nakh).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tsov">Tsov</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nakh_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tsov">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Georgian scholars I.A. Djavashvili and <a href="/wiki/Giorgi_Melikishvili" title="Giorgi Melikishvili">Giorgi Melikishvili</a> the <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">Urartuan</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Sophene" title="Sophene">Supani</a> was occupied by the ancient Nakh tribe Tzov, whose state is called Tsobena in ancient Georgian historiography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Tzov/Tsov language was the dominant language spoken by its people, and was thought by these Georgian historians (as well as a number of others) to be Nakh. Tsov and its relatives in the area may have contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Hurro-Urartian_languages" title="Hurro-Urartian languages">Hurro-Urartian</a> substratum in the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian language</a>.</p>
