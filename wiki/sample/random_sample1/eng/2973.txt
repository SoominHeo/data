<p><b>Naksansa</b> or <b>Naksan Temple</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Korea" title="Buddhism in Korea">Korean Buddhist</a> temple complex in the <a href="/wiki/Jogye_Order" title="Jogye Order">Jogye order of Korean Buddhism</a> that stands on the slopes of Naksan Mountain (also called "Obongsan Mountain"). It is located about midway between <a href="/wiki/Sokcho" title="Sokcho">Sokcho</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangyang" title="Yangyang">Yangyang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangwon-do_(South_Korea)" title="Gangwon-do (South Korea)">Gangwon Province</a>, eastern region of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. Naksansa is one of the few temples in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> to overlook the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a> (East Sea).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddhapia_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buddhapia-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Naksan is an abbreviated term of <i>Botarakgasan</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%A3%9C" title="wikt:補">補</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%99%80" title="wikt:陀">陀</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B4%9B" title="wikt:洛">洛</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BC%BD" title="wikt:伽">伽</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B1%B1" title="wikt:山">山</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a> that refers to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Potalaka" title="Mount Potalaka">Mount Potalaka</a>, the place where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalokitesvara" title="Avalokitesvara">Avalokitesvara</a> <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">Bodhisattva</a> is believed to live. Thus, Naksan Mountain is also known as Korea's Mount Potalaka. The Bodhisattva is said to live on a sea-bound island along with guardian dragons. Naksansa is regarded as one of the great eight scenic areas in the Eastern region of Korea, known as "Gwandong Palgyeong" (관동팔경).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddhapia_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buddhapia-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KBS_World_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-KBS_World-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naksansa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Naksansa was founded in 671, by the <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhu" title="Bhikkhu">Buddhist monk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uisang" title="Uisang">Uisang</a> Daesa (Grand Master Uisang), an ambassador of the <a href="/wiki/Munmu_of_Silla" title="Munmu of Silla">30th King Munmu</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> Period (BC 57-AD 935)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddhapia_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Buddhapia-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup> after he had returned from studying abroad during the Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a>, who, while <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditating</a> near the cave in which the Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva (Gwaneumbosal) was believed to have lived, was told by the Bodhisattva to build the temple there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gonseekorea_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gonseekorea-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, the <a href="/wiki/Octagon" title="Octagon">octagonal</a> Uisangdae <a href="/wiki/Chinese_pavilion" title="Chinese pavilion">Pavilion</a> sits on the spot where Uisang once meditated, the current pavilion having been built in 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KBPF_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-KBPF-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treasures">Treasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naksansa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Treasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG/220px-Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG/330px-Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG/440px-Korea-_Naksansa_2159-07_Bodhisttva.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Haesugwaneumsang (Bodhisattva of Mercy), known as the goddess Gwanseeum-Bosal, gazes out to sea</div>

<p>Mount Naksan is crowned by the Buddhist statue of Haesugwaneumsang (해수관음입상, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodhisattva_of_Mercy" title="Bodhisattva of Mercy">Bodhisattva of Mercy</a>), known as the goddess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guan_Yin" title="Guan Yin">Gwanseum-Bosal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gonseekorea_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-gonseekorea-4">[4]</a></sup> facing southeast standing 15 meters (49 feet) high on a 2.8-meter (9.2 ft) high pedestal. The white granite statue stands in a clearing at the top of the hill, eyes and fingers closed in peaceful meditation as it gazes out to sea. The massive statue is the work of <a href="/wiki/Busan" title="Busan">Busan</a> sculptor Gwon Jeong-hwan (권정환) and was dedicated in 1977. 700 tons of granite and six months were required to complete the sculpture. It is the largest Buddhist statue of its kind in the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddapia_Naksansa_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buddapia_Naksansa-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hongreanam was built by <a href="/wiki/Uisang" title="Uisang">Uisang</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">Hermitage</a> above a stone cave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KBPF_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-KBPF-5">[5]</a></sup> In the sanctuary floor there is a 10-centimeter (4 in) opening through which you can see the sea below.</p>
<p>Naksansa's seven storied stone <a href="/wiki/Pagoda" title="Pagoda">pagoda</a>, Naksansa Chilcheung Seoktap (Seven storied stone pagoda in Naksansa Temple) built in 1467, the 13th year of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Sejo_of_Joseon" title="Sejo of Joseon">King Sejo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddhapia_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Buddhapia-1">[1]</a></sup> is Treasure No.499.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddapia_Naksansa_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buddapia_Naksansa-6">[6]</a></sup> During a repair, it is said that a Buddhist rosary and a magic bead were sealed inside the pagoda. The seven storied stone pagoda is an example of the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> style pagoda having a single square foundation. The square foundation on this pagoda is carved with 24 lotus petals. Of special note is a stone prop in each story, which is larger and thicker than its body stone. The pagoda is formed with thin eaves, a straight slope, and upward edges. On the <a href="/wiki/Finial" title="Finial">finial</a>, around the long thin, bronze rod are ornaments similar to those in <a href="/wiki/Lama" title="Lama">Lama</a> pagoda of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a> (1279-1368) in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Mostly it follows the rather simple style of the Goryeo pagodas though.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_fire_and_reconstruction">2005 fire and reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naksansa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2005 fire and reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="930" data-file-width="1160" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG/185px-Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG/278px-Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG/370px-Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG 2x" width="185"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Naksansa_2217-07_fire_picture.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A picture of the 2005 fire posted on a signboard on the grounds of Naksansa</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most of Naksansa was destroyed by a fire that started in the surrounding forest on April 5, 2005. The temple bell, a national treasure dated 15th century, was also melted and destroyed in the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KBS_World_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-KBS_World-3">[3]</a></sup> The museum displays a violin and cello built from wood found on the temple grounds that survived the fire.</p>
<p>The temple was earlier destroyed by fire during the Mongolian invasion of the 13th century. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> (1392-1910) the temple was repeatedly reconstructed and expanded by royal order in 1467, 1469, 1631 and 1643. The pre-2005 fire facilities were constructed in 1953 after the buildings were again destroyed during the 1950-53 <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CHA-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Present reconstruction is well under way<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KBS_World_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-KBS_World-3">[3]</a></sup> as can be seen in the gallery below. The new construction projects are expected to be completed and the temple fully restored by 2010.</p>
