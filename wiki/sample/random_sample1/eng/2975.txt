<p>The <b>Nan River</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">แม่น้ำน่าน</span>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><i>Maenam Nan</i></span>, <small>IPA:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Thai_and_Lao" title="Help:IPA for Thai and Lao">[mɛ̂ːnáːm nâːn]</a></span>) is a river in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. It is one of the most important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tributaries" title="Tributaries">tributaries</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River" title="Chao Phraya River">Chao Phraya River</a>.</p>

<p>The Nan River originates in the <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang_Range" title="Luang Prabang Range">Luang Prabang Range</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nan_Province" title="Nan Province">Nan Province</a>. The provinces along the river after Nan Province are <a href="/wiki/Uttaradit_Province" title="Uttaradit Province">Uttaradit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phitsanulok_Province" title="Phitsanulok Province">Phitsanulok</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phichit_Province" title="Phichit Province">Phichit</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Yom_River" title="Yom River">Yom River</a> joins the Nan River at <a href="/wiki/Chum_Saeng_District" title="Chum Saeng District">Chum Saeng District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakhon_Sawan_Province" title="Nakhon Sawan Province">Nakhon Sawan Province</a>. When the Nan river joins together with the <a href="/wiki/Ping_River" title="Ping River">Ping River</a> at Pak Nam Pho within the town <a href="/wiki/Nakhon_Sawan" title="Nakhon Sawan">Nakhon Sawan</a> it becomes the <a href="/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River" title="Chao Phraya River">Chao Phraya River</a>. The Nan river runs about 630 kilometres (390 mi) south.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributaries">Tributaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tributaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tributaries_of_the_Chao_Phraya_River" title="Tributaries of the Chao Phraya River">Tributaries of the Chao Phraya River</a></div>
<p>The chief tributary of the Nan is the Yom River, which joins the Nan within Chum Saeng District in Nakhon Sawan Province. Other direct tributaries include the <a href="/wiki/Butsabong_River" title="Butsabong River">Butsabong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Pong_River" title="Wang Pong River">Wang Pong</a> rivers of the lower Nan Basin, the <a href="/wiki/Wat_Ta_Yom_River" title="Wat Ta Yom River">Wat Ta Yom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Thong_River" title="Wang Thong River">Wang Thong</a> Rivers which join the Nan within Phichit Province, the <a href="/wiki/Khwae_Noi_River,_Phitsanulok" title="Khwae Noi River, Phitsanulok">Khwae Noi River</a> which joins the Nan within Phitsanulok Province, the <a href="/wiki/Tron_River" title="Tron River">Tron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pat_River" title="Pat River">Pat</a> Rivers, which join the Nan within Uttaradit Province, the <a href="/wiki/Wa_River" title="Wa River">Wa</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Haet_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Haet River (page does not exist)">Haet</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pua_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pua River (page does not exist)">Pua</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yao_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yao River (page does not exist)">Yao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hao_River" title="Hao River">Hao</a> Rivers, which join the Nan within Nan Province.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drainage">Drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_Nan_Basin">Greater Nan Basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Greater Nan Basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The expanse of the Nan River's tributaries, i.e. the Nan River System, together with the land upon which falling rain drains into these bodies of water, form the Greater Nan Drainage Basin, which is part of the Chao Phraya watershed]].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chao_Phraya_Watershed_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chao_Phraya_Watershed-3">[3]</a></sup> The total area drained by the Nan and its tributaries is 57,947 square kilometres (22,373 sq mi). Much of the lower basin has perfect soil for farming.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nan_Basin">Nan Basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nan Basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most drainage analyses, however, divide the Greater Nan Basin into the Nan Basin and the Yom River Basin. Using this convention, and subtracting the 23,616 square kilometres (9,118 sq mi) of land drained by the Yom and its tributaries, the Nan basin drains a total of 34,331 square kilometres (13,255 sq mi) of land in the provinces of Phitsanulok, Phichit, Nan and Uttaradit.</p>

<p>Ancient civilizations lived in the fertile land along the Nan River and its tributaries, as is evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> tools unearthed near <a href="/wiki/Phitsanulok" title="Phitsanulok">Phitsanulok</a>. However, these early hunter-gatherers are not likely to be ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Tai_peoples" title="Tai peoples">Tai</a> who presently inhabit the Nan basin. The human population around the Chao Phraya River and its tributaries, such as the Nan, remained sparse until late in the region’s history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> The settlement of the indigenous populations of the region began around the advent of rice agriculture during the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, and continued through the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> Archaeologists suspect that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mon%E2%80%93Khmer" title="Mon–Khmer">Mon–Khmer</a> speaking tribes spread through the region, bringing rice agriculture, metalworking, domestic animals. The main course of migration during the metal ages probably ran along the coast of Thailand, but migrants also travelled inland along the Chao Phraya to the Nan Basin and other areas, where it was relatively easier to establish settlements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> The next major wave of migration into the Nan Basin came not from the coast along the Chao Phraya, but rather from the mountainous areas of northern Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> These migrants were the Tai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> Their course of immigration probably began south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangzi_River" title="Yangzi River">Yangzi River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> spread south of the Yangzi around the sixth century BC, the ancestors of the Thai retreated into the high valleys and, over many centuries, migrated west along an arc from the <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_Valley" title="Brahmaputra Valley">Brahmaputra Valley</a>. The Thai brought rice-farming expertise to the mountainous areas of Northern Thailand, and eventually to the Nan Basin and the other lowland regions of Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> Some of the Nan River Mon-Khmer retreated into the hills as the Thai expansion continued, while others generally adopted dialects of the <a href="/wiki/Tai_languages" title="Tai languages">Tai language</a> and blended into the culture of the new settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> The Tai language spoken in the area was heavily influenced by the Khmer culture as well, and evolved into the language we now call <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>, which is considerably different from other Tai dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> Even after this Thai migration, the population in the Nan Basin, other than along the banks of the Nan River and its major tributaries, was sparse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> predator animal species, as well as <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, tropical temperatures and other hardships, kept the population from expanding far from the river, despite the region's extremely fertile soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> As the population grew during these ancient times, settlements along the Nan eventually became more urban, as populations of larger scale were better able to survive the hardships of the heavily forested region. The earliest urban developments along the Nan were modelled after the Mon-Khmer capital at <a href="/wiki/Angkor" title="Angkor">Angkor</a> in Cambodia, which was already quite advanced by the time the Nan Basin had significant population to support urban settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> Along with the positive effects of urbanization (e.g., development of art and specialization of labor), however, came slavery, war and other societal problems associated with urban culture in the forecoming city-state period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> Even in spite of urbanization along the river banks, most of the Nan Basin (aside from the river-side settlements) consisted of virgin forests until around the turn of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_Thailand_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_Thailand-4">[4]</a></sup> One of the early river-side urban areas of the Nan emerging during the era of the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Empire" title="Khmer Empire">Khmer Empire</a> was <i>Song Khwae</i>, which over the years developed into the modern city of Phitsanulok.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Houseboats">Houseboats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Houseboats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Phitsanulok is the only place in Thailand where houseboats are legal, as they have been an important aspect of regional culture since long before the drafting of modern municipal law. People of Phitsanulok still gather on houseboats and raft houses along both sides of the Nan River in the city, and adhere to a traditional way of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phitsanulok_on_ThaiWebsites.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phitsanulok_on_ThaiWebsites.com-5">[5]</a></sup> There is even a floating houseboat museum along the Nan in Phitsanulok, which allows tourists to experience the Nan houseboat culture first-hand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naresuan_Dam">Naresuan Dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Naresuan Dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Naresuan Dam, named after King <a href="/wiki/Naresuan" title="Naresuan">Naresuan</a>, was constructed throughout the span of 1976 through 1985 on the Nan River in Phitsanulok Province, north of the city of Phitsanulok, as part of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phitsanulok_Irrigation_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phitsanulok Irrigation Project (page does not exist)">Phitsanulok Irrigation Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phitsanulok_Irrigation_Project_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phitsanulok_Irrigation_Project-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water quality in the Nan River is deteriorating from heavy <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial contamination</a> attributed primarily to the rapid increase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_development" title="Urban development">urban development</a> in the provinces of Phitsanulok, Phichit, Nan and Uttaradit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chao_Phraya_Watershed_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chao_Phraya_Watershed-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
