<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namling_County&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote>
<p>Namling is the current administrative name given to the valleys of Oyuk, Tobgyet, and Shang ... [associated]... with both Buddhism and <a href="/wiki/Bon" title="Bon">Bon</a>. Through these valleys respectively flow the '<i>Nang-gung-chu'mang ra chu</i> Tobpu-chu, and Shang-chu rivers, with their various tributaries, which rise amid the southern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Nyenchen_Tanglha_Mountains" title="Nyenchen Tanglha Mountains">Nyenchen Tanglha range</a> to the north, and flow southwards to converge with the <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_River" title="Brahmaputra River">Brahmaputra</a> [called the Yarlung Tsangpo River in Tibet].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibetmaster_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tibetmaster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The lower reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Yarlung_Tsangpo_River" title="Yarlung Tsangpo River">Yarlung Tsangpo River</a> make "a sharp U-turn around <a href="/wiki/Namcha_Barwa" title="Namcha Barwa">Namjagbarwa Peak</a> in Pai, Namling County." This is regarded as the starting point of the <a href="/wiki/Yarlung_Tsangpo_Grand_Canyon" title="Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon">Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyingchi_Prefecture" title="Nyingchi Prefecture">Nyingchi Prefecture</a>, which stretches 496 kilometres (308 mi) and averages over 5,000 metres (3.1 mi) in depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The county has three geysers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_efforts">Development efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namling_County&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Namling County Schools Project was founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tashi_Tsering_(educator)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tashi Tsering (educator) (page does not exist)">Tashi Tsering</a> in 1991, and has been sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder</a>-<a href="/wiki/Lhasa" title="Lhasa">Lhasa</a> Sister City Project (BLSCP) since 1994. As of 2013, it supports 53 rural elementary schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2006, 15 out of the county's 17 towns had <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">cell phone</a> service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2001, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potatoes" title="Potatoes">potatoes</a> have been introduced in Aimagang (Emagang) as a crop for herdsmen in the county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Potatoes and vegetables were shipped to markets in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xigaz%C3%AA" title="Xigazê">Xigazê</a>, and production of peas and wheat decreased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2009 <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> outreach efforts in rural areas of the county appear to have had a positive impact in comparison to a similar non-intervention area of <a href="/wiki/Tingri_County" title="Tingri County">Tingri County</a>, according to <a href="/wiki/Medicus_Mundi_International" title="Medicus Mundi International">Medicus Mundi Switzerland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, protests of environmental damage by <a href="/wiki/Gold_mining" title="Gold mining">gold mining</a> resulted in arrests and at least one fatality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Qin Weiqiang, secretary of the Namling County Party Committee, said that after the "<a href="/wiki/Lhasa%E2%80%93Xigaz%C3%AA_Railway" title="Lhasa–Xigazê Railway">Lhasa–Xigazê Railway</a> is put into operation", the number of tourists in Namling County was expected to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xianba_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-xianba-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namling_County&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"The famous local specialities are silver utensils, Tibetan sword and so on. Namling Town is the office place of Namling with a population 5,000, where are many monasteries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditional Tibetan opera is popular in Namling County, and government funding efforts to preserve the tradition were started in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> "Xiangba Tibetan play in Namling County of Tibet has a history of more than 700 years. It is one of the four major schools of the blue-mask Tibetan play, and was born in the late 18th century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Local residents preserve "some traditional Tibetan operas such as "Princess Wencheng", "Chimei Gongdan", "Langsa Wenbo" and so on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> "Tongdong Gyaibo (1365-1455), the founder of Tibet Opera, was born in Ngamring County. Legend has it that the iron chain bridge over the Xiongqoi River in Namling County was built with funds collected by Tongdong Gyaibo through performances."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tongdong_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-tongdong-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Namling County and the Xiangqu River Basin were a cultural center of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supi" title="Supi">Supi</a> tribe, known as "The Kingdom of Women", and "one of the earliest tribes in Tibet." After "the end of the sixth century, the Supi tribe moved its political center to the Lhasa River Basin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xianba_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-xianba-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 1999, the county was home to the <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a> <a href="/wiki/Yellow-headed_box_turtle" title="Yellow-headed box turtle">Golden-headed box turtle</a> (Cuora aurocapitata).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monasteries">Monasteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namling_County&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Monasteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yungdrungling_Monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yungdrungling Monastery (page does not exist)">Yungdrungling Monastery</a> "is located at the foot of the Yulha Jiesam mountain in the Numa village in Namling County about 90 km. from Xigazê. The monastery is surrounded by dense wood ... it is one of the four major monasteries of Bon religion in Tibet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Danag Monastery, over 800 years old, is located in Danag Village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remains of the Dingma Monastery, an 11th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kadampa" title="Kadampa">Kadampa</a> monastery, are located on a hill in the Oyuk Valley. It was founded by Ram Dingma Deshek Jungne, a student of Geshe Potowa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibetmaster_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tibetmaster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gongon Lhakhang temple, in Oyuk Township, was said to be built by <a href="/wiki/Songtsen_Gampo" title="Songtsen Gampo">Songtsen Gampo</a>, and frequented by <a href="/wiki/Padmasambhava" title="Padmasambhava">Padmasambhava</a> and his students <a href="/wiki/Namkhai_Nyingpo" title="Namkhai Nyingpo">Namkhai Nyingpo</a> and King <a href="/wiki/Trisong_Detsen" title="Trisong Detsen">Trisong Detsen</a> in the 8th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibetmaster_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tibetmaster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oyuk Chigong or Oyuk Jara Gon is a hermitage located at Jarasa in lower Oyuk. It is "the abode or castle of the protector deity Dorje Lekpa: guardian of the <a href="/wiki/Dzogchen" title="Dzogchen">Dzogchen</a> teachings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibetmaster_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tibetmaster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gelukpa monasteries of Drungzhi and Sogchen are located in the Tobgyel Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibetmaster_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tibetmaster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gadan Chiokorling Monastery, located 1 km from the county center, originally called Gegon Songrapling Monastery, became part of the Gelgupa Sect during the reign of the <a href="/wiki/5th_Dalai_Lama" title="5th Dalai Lama">5th Dalai Lama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namling_County&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mokchokpa_Rinchen_Tsondr%C3%BC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mokchokpa Rinchen Tsondrü (page does not exist)">Mokchokpa Rinchen Tsondrü</a> (1110-1170?) of the <a href="/wiki/Shangpa_Kagyu" title="Shangpa Kagyu">Shangpa Kagyu</a> was born in the Lhabu grasslands of the county's Shang Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lobsang_Yeshe,_Fifth_Panchen_Lama" title="Lobsang Yeshe, Fifth Panchen Lama">Lobsang Yeshe, Fifth Panchen Lama</a> (1663–1737) was born in Namling County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tenpai_Wangchuk,_8th_Panchen_Lama" title="Tenpai Wangchuk, 8th Panchen Lama">Tenpai Wangchuk, 8th Panchen Lama</a> (1855–1882) was born in Namling County.</li>
<li>Guisang Sang (b. appx. 1958), who joined the Tibet Mountaineering Team in 1982, was the first woman in Asia to reach the top of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> twice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tashi_Tsering_(educator)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tashi Tsering (educator) (page does not exist)">Tashi Tsering (educator)</a> (1929-2014)</li>
</ul>
