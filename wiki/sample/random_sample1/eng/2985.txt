<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Tolkien%27s_legendarium" title="Tolkien's legendarium">fantasy</a> of <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>, the <b>Dwarves</b> are a race inhabiting <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a>, the central continent of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in an imagined mythological past. They are based on the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(mythology)" title="Dwarf (mythology)">dwarfs of Germanic myths</a>: small humanoids that dwell in mountains, and are associated with mining, metal-craft and jewelry.</p>
<p>They appear in his books <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> (1937), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> (1954–55), the posthumously published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">The Silmarillion</a></i> (1977), <i><a href="/wiki/Unfinished_Tales" title="Unfinished Tales">Unfinished Tales</a></i> (1980), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_Middle-earth" title="The History of Middle-earth">The History of Middle-earth</a></i> series (1983–96), the last three edited by his son and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_executor" title="Literary executor">literary executor</a> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Tolkien" title="Christopher Tolkien">Christopher Tolkien</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Book_of_Lost_Tales"><i>The Book of Lost Tales</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Book of Lost Tales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Lost_Tales" title="The Book of Lost Tales">The Book of Lost Tales</a></i> the very few Dwarves who appear are portrayed as evil beings, employers of <a href="/wiki/Orc_(Middle-earth)" title="Orc (Middle-earth)">Orc</a> mercenaries and in conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Elf_(Middle-earth)" title="Elf (Middle-earth)">Elves</a>—who are the imagined "authors" of the myths, and are therefore biased against Dwarves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Tolkien was inspired by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_dwarves" title="Norse dwarves">dwarves of Norse myths</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chance_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-chance-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Germanic_mythology)" title="Dwarf (Germanic mythology)">dwarves of Germanic folklore</a> (such as those of the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>), from whom his Dwarves take their characteristic affinity with mining, metalworking, crafting and avarice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chance_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-chance-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hobbit"><i>The Hobbit</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Hobbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The representation of Dwarves as evil changed dramatically with <i>The Hobbit</i>. Here the Dwarves became occasionally comedic and bumbling, but largely seen as honourable, serious-minded, but still portraying some negative characteristics such as being gold-hungry, extremely proud and occasionally officious. Tolkien was now influenced by his own selective reading of medieval texts regarding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> people and their history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotH_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HotH-5">[5]</a></sup> The dwarves' characteristics of being dispossessed of their homeland (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erebor" title="Erebor">Lonely Mountain</a>, their ancestral home, is the goal the exiled Dwarves seek to reclaim), and living among other groups whilst retaining their own culture are all derived from the medieval image of Jews,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotH_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-HotH-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-6">[6]</a></sup> whilst their warlike nature stems from accounts in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotH_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-HotH-5">[5]</a></sup> Medieval views of Jews also saw them as having a propensity for making well-crafted and beautiful things,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotH_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-HotH-5">[5]</a></sup> a trait shared with Norse dwarves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chance_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-chance-4">[4]</a></sup> For <i>The Hobbit</i> almost all dwarf-names are taken from the <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">Dvergatal</a></i> or "Catalogue of the Dwarves", found in the <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-names_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-names-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, more than just supplying names, the "Catalogue of the Dwarves" appears to have inspired Tolkien to supply meaning and context to the list of names—that they travelled together, and this in turn became the quest told of in <i>The Hobbit</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Dwarves' written language is represented on maps and in illustrations by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runic_alphabet" title="Runic alphabet">Anglo-Saxon Runes</a>. The Dwarf calendar invented for <i>The Hobbit</i> reflects the Jewish calendar in beginning in late autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotH_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-HotH-5">[5]</a></sup> The dwarves taking Bilbo out of his complacent existence has been seen as an eloquent metaphor for the "impoverishment of Western society without Jews."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lord_of_the_Rings"><i>The Lord of the Rings</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Lord of the Rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>When writing <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> Tolkien continued many of the themes he had set up in <i>The Hobbit</i>. When giving Dwarves their own language (<a href="/wiki/Khuzdul" title="Khuzdul">Khuzdul</a>) Tolkien decided to create an analogue of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semitic_language" title="Semitic language">Semitic language</a> influenced by <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a>. Like medieval Jewish groups, the Dwarves use their own language only amongst themselves, and adopted the languages of those they live amongst for the most part, for example taking public names from the cultures they lived within, whilst keeping their "true-names" and true language a secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Along with a few words in Khuzdul, Tolkien also developed runes of his own invention (the <a href="/wiki/Cirth" title="Cirth">Cirth</a>), said to have been invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elves_(Middle-earth)" title="Elves (Middle-earth)">Elves</a> and later adopted by the Dwarves. Tolkien further underlines the <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> of the Dwarves with the lost stronghold of the Mines of <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Moria</a>. In <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, Tolkien uses the main dwarf character <a href="/wiki/Gimli_(Middle-earth)" title="Gimli (Middle-earth)">Gimli</a> to finally reconcile the conflict between Elves and Dwarves through showing great courtesy to <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a> and forming a deep friendship with <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a>, which has been seen as Tolkien's reply toward "Gentile anti-Semitism and Jewish exclusiveness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tolkien also elaborated on Jewish influence on his Dwarves in a letter: "I do think of the 'Dwarves' like Jews: at once native and alien in their habitations, speaking the languages of the country, but with an accent due to their own private tongue..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22Quenta_Silmarillion.22">The "Quenta Silmarillion"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: The "Quenta Silmarillion"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After preparing <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, Tolkien returned again to the matter of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silmarillion" title="Silmarillion">Silmarillion</a></i>, in which he gave the Dwarves a <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation myth</a>. The most Dwarf-centric story from <i>The Book of Lost Tales</i>, "The Nauglafring", was not redrafted to fit with the later positive portrayal of the dwarves from <i>The Hobbit</i> and <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, nor other events in the <i>Silmarillion</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> leading Christopher Tolkien significantly to rewrite it with input from <a href="/wiki/Guy_Gavriel_Kay" title="Guy Gavriel Kay">Guy Gavriel Kay</a> in preparation for publication.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Later_Silmarillion_and_last_writings">The Later <i>Silmarillion</i> and last writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Later Silmarillion and last writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometime before 1969 Tolkien wrote the essay <i>Of Dwarves and Men</i>, in which detailed consideration was given to the Dwarves' use of language, that the names given in the stories were of Northern Mannish origin, and Khuzdul being their own secret tongue and the naming of the Seven Houses of the Dwarves. The essay represents the last of Tolkien's writing regarding the Dwarves and was published in volume 12 of <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_Middle-earth" title="The History of Middle-earth">The History of Middle-earth</a></i> in 1996.</p>
<p>In the last interview before his death, Tolkien, after discussing the nature of Elves, briefly says of his Dwarves: "The dwarves of course are quite obviously, wouldn't you say that in many ways they remind you of the Jews? Their words are Semitic, obviously, constructed to be Semitic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling_.22Dwarves.22">Spelling "Dwarves"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: Spelling "Dwarves"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original editor of <i>The Hobbit</i> "corrected" Tolkien's plural <i>dwarves</i> to <i>dwarfs</i>, as did the editor of the Puffin paperback edition of <i>The Hobbit</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to Tolkien, the "real 'historical<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>" plural of <i>dwarf</i> is <i>dwarrows</i> or <i>dwerrows</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He referred to <i>dwarves</i> as "a piece of private bad grammar".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In Appendix F of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> it is explained that if we still spoke of <i>dwarves</i> regularly, English might have retained a special plural for the word <i>dwarf</i> as with <i>goose</i>—<i>geese</i>. Despite Tolkien's fondness for it, the form <i>dwarrow</i> only appears in his writing as <i>Dwarrowdelf</i>, a name for Moria.</p>
<p>Tolkien used <i>Dwarves</i>, instead, which corresponds with <i>Elf</i> and <i>Elves</i>. In this matter, one has to consider the fact that the etymological development of the term <i>dwarf</i> differs from the similar-sounding word <i>scarf</i> (plural <i>scarves</i>). The English word is related to old <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">Norse</a> <i>dvergr</i>, which, in the other case, would have had the form <i>dvorgr</i>. But this word was never recorded, and the f/g-emendation (English/Norse) dates further back in language history.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives">Adjectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adjectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Tolkien used <i>dwarvish</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <i>dwarf(-)</i> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Power#The_Verse_of_the_Rings" title="Rings of Power">Dwarf-lords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth_roads#Old_Forest_Road" title="Middle-earth roads">Old Dwarf Road</a>) as adjectives for the people he created, rarely <i>dwarven</i> if ever.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wicked_Dwarves">Wicked Dwarves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wicked Dwarves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Of the people of Middle-earth, Dwarves are the most resistant to corruption and influence of Morgoth and later Sauron. The seven rings of Power of the dwarves did not turn them to evil, but it did amplify their greed and lust for gold. It is said that very few wilfully served the side of darkness. Of those who did very little was written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Of the seven houses few fought on either side during The Last Alliance at the end of the Second Age, and it's known that none from the House of Durin ever fought on the side of evil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During the early parts of the Third Age (or at least in legends of the previous), it is known that in some places wicked dwarves had made alliances with orcs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is suggested by Tolkien in some of his notes that of the dwarves that turned to wickedness they most likely came from the Dwarves of the far eastern mansions (and perhaps some of the nearer ones) came under the Shadow of Morgoth and turned to evil. It is however unclear if these refer to Dwarves beyond Iron Hills (the most eastern known stronghold of the Dwarves).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Because Dwarves are not evil by nature, few ever served the Enemy of their own free will (though rumours of Men suggest the total was greater).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the dwarves accused of fighting on the side of darkness may have been in conflict due to enmity between the races due to misunderstandings (which may also apply to the Petty-dwarves' distrust and hatred of the elves), or enmity between the races caused by greed and envy (men lusting after the dwarves' wealth and their handiwork, and possibly between the Dwarven clans themselves), or by races becoming estranged from each other (the rise of the enmity and distrust between dwarves and elves after the fall of Khazad-dûm). What may have been fought in self-defence may be seen as an evil action by the opposing force. But in these cases those involved were never allied with the Enemy or his ambassadors (though the wars between the Free Peoples may have worked to his advantage). The trust and interaction between elves and dwarves was later restored through the friendship of Legolas and Gimli.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dwarf.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="939" data-file-width="641" height="322" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dwarf.jpg/220px-Dwarf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dwarf.jpg/330px-Dwarf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dwarf.jpg/440px-Dwarf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dwarf.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<center>An illustration of a Middle-earth dwarf.</center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <i>The Silmarillion</i>, the Dwarves are described as shorter and stockier than Elves and Men, able to withstand both heat and cold. Though they are mortal, Dwarves have an average lifespan of 250 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> Tolkien writes that they breed slowly, for no more than a third of them are female, and not all marry; also, female Dwarves look and sound (and dress, if journeying—which is rare) so alike to Dwarf-males that other folk cannot distinguish them, and thus others wrongly believe Dwarves grow out of stone. Tolkien names only one female, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%ADs_(Middle-earth)" title="Dís (Middle-earth)">Dís</a>. In <i>The War of the Jewels</i> Tolkien says both males and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bearded_woman" title="Bearded woman">females have beards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tolkien's Dwarves, much like their mythical forebears, are great metalworkers, smiths and stoneworkers. Fierce in battle, their main weapons are <a href="/wiki/Battle_axe" title="Battle axe">axes</a> (referenced in many subsequent fantasy works), but they also use <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_arrow" title="Bow and arrow">bows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">swords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mattock" title="Mattock">mattocks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Unlike other fantasy dwarves, Tolkien does not explicitly have them use <a href="/wiki/War_hammer" title="War hammer">war hammers</a>.</p>
<p>Since they lived underground, Dwarves did not grow their own food supplies if they could help it, and usually obtained food through trade with Elves and Men. In the essay "Of Dwarves and Men" in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Peoples_of_Middle-earth" title="The Peoples of Middle-earth">The Peoples of Middle-earth</a></i> it is written that Dwarf and human communities often formed trade relations where the Men were the prime suppliers of food, farmers and herdsmen, while the Dwarves supplied tools and weapons, road-building and construction work.</p>
<p>Unlike Elves and Men, created by the supreme god <a href="/wiki/Eru_Il%C3%BAvatar" title="Eru Ilúvatar">Ilúvatar</a>, Dwarves were created by the <a href="/wiki/Vala_(Middle-earth)" title="Vala (Middle-earth)">Vala</a> <a href="/wiki/Aul%C3%AB" title="Aulë">Aulë</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout the First Age and most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Age" title="Second Age">Second Age</a>, the Dwarves maintain mostly friendly trading relations with Men and Elves (the treachery of the Dwarves of Nogrod towards <a href="/wiki/Thingol" title="Thingol">Thingol</a> of <a href="/wiki/Doriath" title="Doriath">Doriath</a> being an exception). However, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Age" title="Third Age">Third Age</a>, particularly after the closure of <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Moria</a>, they grow mistrustful of Elves, though in later times cordial relations are established with the Elves of Mirkwood and the Men of Dale. They also maintain somewhat ambivalent relations with <a href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">Hobbits</a> for most of the Third Age, although after the mission to retake the Lonely Mountain <a href="/wiki/Bilbo_Baggins" title="Bilbo Baggins">Bilbo Baggins</a> is held in great esteem there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Khuzdul" title="Khuzdul">Khuzdul</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cirth.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="467" height="424" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cirth.png/220px-Cirth.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cirth.png/330px-Cirth.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cirth.png/440px-Cirth.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cirth.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Cirth runes used to write Khuzdul.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From their creation, the Dwarves spoke <a href="/wiki/Khuzdul" title="Khuzdul">Khuzdul</a>, a constructed language made for them by Aulë. Because it was a constructed (though living) language, it was not descended from any form of Elvish, as most of the languages of Men were, although it is suggested that the language may have had influence on the early languages of Men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Khuzdul was for the most part a closely guarded tongue (one of the few recorded outsiders to have a knowledge of it was <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B6l" title="Eöl">Eöl</a>), however, and the Dwarves never revealed their Khuzdul names to outsiders, going so far as to omit them from even their tombs. Khuzdul was written in <a href="/wiki/Cirth" title="Cirth">Cirth</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes">runic</a> alphabet developed by the Elves. There is no extant corpus for the Khuzdul language, whether in Tolkien's novels or in his private works, other than the battle cry: Baruk Khazâd! Khazâd ai-mênu! (meaning "Axes of the Dwarves! The Dwarves are upon you!") and the inscription on <a href="/wiki/Balin_(Middle-earth)" title="Balin (Middle-earth)">Balin</a>'s tombstone, reading: <small>BALIN FUNDINUL UZBAD KHAZAD-DÛMU</small>, or Balin son of Fundin Lord of Moria. The remainder of the corpus of Khuzdul consists of single words and names, such as the mountains Barazinbar (Redhorn) and Zirakzigil (Silvertine). Tolkien himself does not seem to have had a firm idea of the meaning of these words when he created them; his notes on Zirakzigil originally suggested that "zirak" means "silver" and "zigil" means "spike", but he later reversed this and proposed that "zirak" is "spike" and "zigil" is "silver". Of another Khuzdul name "Kibil-nâla" he wrote "the meaning of <i>nâla</i> is not known."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Grey-elvish or <a href="/wiki/Sindarin" title="Sindarin">Sindarin</a> the Dwarves were called <i>Naugrim</i> ("Stunted People"), <i>Gonnhirrim</i> ("Stone-lords"), and <i>Dornhoth</i> ("Thrawn Folk"), and also <i>Hadhodrim</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Quenya" title="Quenya">Quenya</a> they were the <i>Casári</i>. The Dwarves called themselves <i>Khazâd</i> in their own language, <a href="/wiki/Khuzdul" title="Khuzdul">Khuzdul</a>.</p>
<p>Tolkien took the names of twelve of the thirteen dwarves he used in <i>The Hobbit</i> (and Gandalf's name as well) from <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">Völuspá</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i>The Silmarillion</i>, it is stated that the Dwarves were originally divided into seven <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clans</a> or "Houses". The three who enter Tolkien's histories are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Longbeards</b> (Khuzdul: <i><b>Sigin-tarâg</b></i>) (<b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durin%27s_folk" title="Durin's folk">Durin's folk</a></b>), who founded the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khazad-d%C3%BBm" title="Khazad-dûm">Khazad-dûm</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Misty_Mountains" title="Misty Mountains">Misty Mountains</a> (they later founded realms in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ered_Mithrin" title="Ered Mithrin">Grey Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lonely_Mountain" title="Lonely Mountain">Lonely Mountain</a>);</li>
<li><b>Firebeards</b>, who founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nogrod" title="Nogrod">Nogrod</a> in the Blue Mountains;</li>
<li><b>Broadbeams</b>, who founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belegost" title="Belegost">Belegost</a> in the Blue Mountains.</li>
</ul>
<p>After the end of the First Age, the Dwarves spoken of are almost exclusively of <a href="/wiki/Durin" title="Durin">Durin</a>'s line. Almost nothing is known of the histories of the other four Houses, except that they each sent a contingent west to fight in The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Dwarves_and_Orcs" title="War of the Dwarves and Orcs">War of the Dwarves and Orcs</a> in the late Third Age, and their names:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Ironfists</b></li>
<li><b>Stiffbeards</b></li>
<li><b>Blacklocks</b></li>
<li><b>Stonefoots</b></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Petty-dwarves">Petty-dwarves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Petty-dwarves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Petty-dwarves were Dwarves of several <a href="/wiki/Fathers_of_the_Dwarves" title="Fathers of the Dwarves">houses</a>, which had been exiled in very ancient times for reasons unknown. They were the first to cross the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ered_Luin" title="Ered Luin">Ered Luin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/First_Age" title="First Age">First Age</a>, and established strongholds in <a href="/wiki/Beleriand" title="Beleriand">Beleriand</a> before the building of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nogrod" title="Nogrod">Nogrod</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belegost" title="Belegost">Belegost</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ered_Luin" title="Ered Luin">Blue Mountains</a>, and before the <a href="/wiki/Elf_(Middle-earth)" title="Elf (Middle-earth)">Elves</a> arrived. These very ancient settlements were at <a href="/wiki/Nargothrond" title="Nargothrond">Nargothrond</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amon_R%C3%BBdh" title="Amon Rûdh">Amon Rûdh</a>.</p>
<p>The usual <a href="/wiki/Sindarin" title="Sindarin">Sindarin</a> name was <i>Noegyth Nibin</i>; others include <i>Nibin-Nogrim</i> and <i>Noegoethig</i>, formed of <i>nibin</i> ("petty") and one of the Elvish names for the true Dwarves. In <a href="/wiki/Quenya" title="Quenya">Quenya</a> they were called <i>Pitya-naukor</i>. The Petty-dwarves of West <a href="/wiki/Beleriand" title="Beleriand">Beleriand</a> dwindled to a single family, and then at last became extinct.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sindar" title="Sindar">Sindar</a>, not acquainted with Dwarves yet, saw the Petty-dwarves as little more than bothersome animals, calling them <i>(Levain) Tad-dail</i> ("two-legged (animals)"), and hunted them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (October 2009)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Not until the Dwarves of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ered_Luin" title="Ered Luin">Ered Luin</a> established contact with the Sindar did they realize what the Petty-dwarves were. Afterwards they were mostly left alone, but not before the Petty-dwarves came to hate all Elves with a passion.</p>
<p>Petty-dwarves differed from normal Dwarves in various ways: they were smaller, far more unsociable, and they freely gave away their names: other Dwarves kept their <a href="/wiki/Khuzdul" title="Khuzdul">Khuzdul</a> names and language a secret. In reality, Tolkien's original conception was that all Dwarves were greedy, treacherous and unable to create works of beauty without aid. This was so different from their characteristics that evolved in later stories that he felt obliged to describe the Dwarves in these earlier stories as a separate race, the Petty-dwarves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Jewels" title="War of the Jewels">War of the Jewels</a>, after the return of the <a href="/wiki/Noldor" title="Noldor">Noldor</a>, the Petty-dwarves had nearly died out. The last remnant of their people were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%AEm" title="Mîm">Mîm</a> and his two sons, Ibûn and Khîm, who lived at Amon Rûdh. They gave shelter to <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BArin_Turambar" title="Túrin Turambar">Túrin Turambar</a> and his band at their home of Amon Rûdh. Mîm was later captured by a band of Orcs and saved his own life by betraying Túrin, though his sons were killed. Mîm later became the possessor of a treasure-hoard abandoned by the dragon <a href="/wiki/Glaurung" title="Glaurung">Glaurung</a>, but was later killed by <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BArin" title="Húrin">Húrin</a>, Túrin's father.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dwarves are portrayed as a very ancient people who awoke, like the Elves, at the start of the First Age, before the existence of the Sun and Moon (the Elves, however, awakened first).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Tolkien's works, the Dwarves (in the form of seven patriarchs) were created during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Years_of_the_Trees" title="Years of the Trees">Years of the Trees</a> (also known as the Ages of Darkness), when all of Middle-earth was controlled by the forces of <a href="/wiki/Morgoth" title="Morgoth">Melkor</a>. They were created by the Vala Aulë, in secret from the other Valar, intended to be his children to whom he could teach his crafts. Ilúvatar, however, knew of their creation, despite Aulë's efforts. When confronted by Ilúvatar, Aulë confessed his deed and raised his hammer to destroy his creations. However, Ilúvatar stayed Aulë's hand and sanctified the dwarves, though he did not allow them to "awake" before the Elves (whom he had designated as "The Firstborn"). Aulë sealed the seven <a href="/wiki/Fathers_of_the_Dwarves" title="Fathers of the Dwarves">Fathers of the Dwarves</a> in stone chambers in far-flung regions of Middle-earth to await their awakening.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Age">First Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: First Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some time after the Elves had awakened at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuivi%C3%A9nen" title="Cuiviénen">Cuiviénen</a>, the Seven Fathers of the Dwarves were released from their stone chambers. The eldest of them, called Durin, wandered until he founded the city of <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Khazad-dûm</a> in the natural caves beneath three peaks: <a href="/wiki/Caradhras" title="Caradhras">Caradhras</a>, Celebdil, and Fanuidhol (known in Khuzdul as Baranzinbar, Zirakzigil, and Bundushathûr, respectively). The city, populated by the Longbeards or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durin%27s_folk" title="Durin's folk">Durin's folk</a>, grew and prospered continuously through Durin's life (which was so long that he was called Durin the Deathless, also a reference to the belief by his people that he would be reincarnated seven times). It was the only one of the Dwarf mansions to survive the First Age.</p>
<p>Far to the west of Khazad-dûm, the great dwarven cities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belegost" title="Belegost">Belegost</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nogrod" title="Nogrod">Nogrod</a> were founded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ered_Luin" title="Ered Luin">Ered Luin</a> (The Blue Mountains) during the First Age, before the arrival of the Elves in <a href="/wiki/Beleriand" title="Beleriand">Beleriand</a>. The Dwarves of Belegost were the first to forge mail of linked rings, and they also traded weaponry with the Sindar and carved the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menegroth" title="Menegroth">Thousand Caves of Menegroth</a> for the Elf king <a href="/wiki/Thingol" title="Thingol">Thingol</a>. In Nogrod, the smith <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telchar" title="Telchar">Telchar</a> forged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Middle-earth_weapons_and_armour#Narsil" title="List of Middle-earth weapons and armour">Narsil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angrist" title="Angrist">Angrist</a>, two of the most fateful weapons in the history of Arda, as well as the famed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon-helm" title="Dragon-helm">Dragon-helm</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dor-L%C3%B3min" title="Dor-Lómin">Dor-Lómin</a>.</p>
<p>The dwarves of Beleriand fought against the forces of Melkor during the first age, and the dwarves of Belegost were the only people able to withstand dragon-fire in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Unnumbered_Tears" title="Battle of Unnumbered Tears">Battle of Unnumbered Tears</a>, when King Azaghâl, who died in the battle, stabbed Glaurung, the first dragon.</p>
<p>The dwarves of Nogrod fought against Melkor as well. However, they slew <a href="/wiki/Thingol" title="Thingol">Thingol</a> out of greed and stole the <a href="/wiki/Silmaril" title="Silmaril">Silmaril</a> they had been charged to set into the necklace called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauglam%C3%ADr" title="Nauglamír">Nauglamír</a>. A number of retaliatory actions ensued, and the Nogrod army was destroyed by a force of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laiquendi" title="Laiquendi">Laiquendi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ent" title="Ent">Ents</a>. Both dwarf kingdoms would eventually be destroyed, along with nearly all of Beleriand, after the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Wrath" title="War of Wrath">War of Wrath</a>, with the dwarven refugees mainly resettling in Khazad-dûm.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Age">Second Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Second Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Refugees from Belegost and Nogrod added to the population of Khazad-dûm, and its wealth was also enriched with the discovery of <i><a href="/wiki/Mithril" title="Mithril">mithril</a></i>, a magical and extremely valuable metal found only in its mines. During this time the Dwarves continued to trade with neighbouring Men and the Elves of <a href="/wiki/Eregion" title="Eregion">Eregion</a>. Seven of the <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Power" title="Rings of Power">Rings of Power</a> forged by the Elven-Smiths were later given by Sauron, who had seized them, to the heads of the seven Dwarf clans (although the Longbeards maintained that Durin's Ring was given to him by <a href="/wiki/Celebrimbor" title="Celebrimbor">Celebrimbor</a>). The Dwarves of Moria at first fought in the War of Sauron and the Elves, but in the year 1697 of the Second Age, the doors of Khazad-dûm were shut and its inhabitants no longer ventured forth into the world. Thereafter it was known by the elven name of Moria, meaning "dark chasm".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_Age">Third Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Third Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the Third Age the Dwarves of Moria continued to prosper until the year 1980, when, in pursuing a vein of <i>mithril</i>, they broke open a chamber containing the last <a href="/wiki/Balrog" title="Balrog">balrog</a> known in the histories of Middle-earth, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durin%27s_Bane" title="Durin's Bane">Durin's Bane</a>. They battled against the demon for one year, and after the death of two kings, the Dwarves who had not been killed fled from the Misty Mountains. For almost two decades they had no kingdom, but in the year 1999, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thr%C3%A1in_I" title="Thráin I">Thráin I</a> founded a kingdom at the <a href="/wiki/Lonely_Mountain" title="Lonely Mountain">Lonely Mountain</a>. This kingdom prospered for a time, and the great jewel known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arkenstone" title="Arkenstone">Arkenstone</a> was discovered.</p>
<p>In 2210 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorin_I" title="Thorin I">Thorin I</a> founded a kingdom in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ered_Mithrin" title="Ered Mithrin">Grey Mountains</a> to the north of <a href="/wiki/Mirkwood" title="Mirkwood">Mirkwood</a>. Both of these realms would eventually be consumed by dragons—the Grey Mountains in 2590 by a horde and The Lonely Mountain in 2770 by the dragon <a href="/wiki/Smaug" title="Smaug">Smaug</a>. The refugees from the Grey Mountains who did not return to The Lonely Mountain colonized the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Hills" title="Iron Hills">Iron Hills</a>, one of the only Dwarf kingdoms never to be abandoned or taken. The main body of the Dwarves became a wandering people, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thr%C3%B3r" title="Thrór">Thrór</a>, who had been king of the Lonely Mountain when it was captured, was slain by Orcs in the year 2790 and his body mutilated. This led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Dwarves_and_Orcs" title="War of the Dwarves and Orcs">War of the Dwarves and Orcs</a>, in which nearly all of the Orc hordes of the Misty Mountains were exterminated but half of the Dwarf host were killed, a blow that took the Dwarves several hundred years to recover from.</p>
<p>For a time an exile kingdom was founded in the northern Blue Mountains, but <a href="/wiki/Thr%C3%A1in_II" title="Thráin II">Thráin II</a> was driven to wandering the wilderness by his Ring, the last of the Dwarven Rings not yet taken by <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a> or consumed by dragons. He was soon captured by Sauron, then reigning as the Necromancer in <a href="/wiki/Dol_Guldur" title="Dol Guldur">Dol Guldur</a>. Thráin was tortured, his Ring of Power taken, and finally died. In 2941 Thorin II Oakenshield, son of Thráin II and grandson of Thrór, recolonized the Lonely Mountain after Smaug the dragon was slain by Bard, the future King of Dale. After the ensuing Battle of the Five Armies, in which the Eagles, the Elves of Mirkwood, the Men of Dale, and the Dwarves of the Iron Hills (as well as Thorin's band) defeated an invading horde of Goblins and in which Thorin was killed, his cousin <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1in_II_Ironfoot" title="Dáin II Ironfoot">Dáin II Ironfoot</a>, already lord of the Iron Hills, became King Under the Mountain, and the Lonely Mountain was not abandoned again.</p>
<p>Dwarves did not figure prominently in the major battles of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Ring" title="War of the Ring">War of the Ring</a> although the Lonely Mountain was besieged for a time and Dáin killed in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dale" title="Battle of Dale">Battle of Dale</a>. One dwarf, however—<a href="/wiki/Gimli_(Middle-earth)" title="Gimli (Middle-earth)">Gimli</a>—joined the Fellowship of the Ring and was a companion of the Ringbearer for a great part of his journey, and also fought at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Hornburg" title="Battle of the Hornburg">Battle of the Hornburg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Pelennor_Fields" title="Battle of the Pelennor Fields">Battle of the Pelennor Fields</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Morannon" title="Battle of the Morannon">Battle of the Morannon</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourth_Age">Fourth Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_(Middle-earth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Fourth Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the start of the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Age" title="Fourth Age">Fourth Age</a>, Gimli led a group of colonists from the Lonely Mountain to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glittering_Caves" title="Glittering Caves">Glittering Caves</a>, beneath Hornburg in <a href="/wiki/Rohan_(Middle-earth)" title="Rohan (Middle-earth)">Rohan</a>, where he established another Dwarf kingdom and ruled there for more than a century. After the death of <a href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a> in the year 120 of the Fourth Age, he sailed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undying_Lands" title="Undying Lands">Undying Lands</a> with <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a>.</p>
<p>It is said that the Dwarf population began to dwindle because most male Dwarves did not desire wives, or could not find one that they desired. It does not help that Dwarf women are less than a third of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROTK__Appendix_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ROTK_Appendix-31">[31]</a></sup> Yet much is still left a mystery in Dwarven history; the Dwarves' true fate is left unknown.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iron_Crown_Enterprises" title="Iron Crown Enterprises">Iron Crown Enterprises</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Middle-earth_Role_Playing" title="Middle-earth Role Playing">Middle-earth Role Playing</a></i> (1986) Dwarven player-characters receive statistical bonuses to Strength and Constitution, and subtractions from Presence, Agility and Intelligence. Seven "Dwarven Kindreds", named after each of the founding fathers – Durin, Bávor, Dwálin, Thrár, Druin, Thelór and Bárin – are given in <i>The Lords of Middle-earth—Volume III</i> (1989).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> adaptation of <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)"><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> film trilogy</a>, Gimli's character is occasionally used as comic relief and several of his appearances are intended to emphasize the difference between the Dwarves and Elves.</p>
<p>As explained in the DVD commentary, the races in the Fellowship come in three height ranges: regular sized Men and Elves, Dwarves who average four feet six inches (1.37 m), and Hobbits who average three feet six inches (1.06 m). The adaptation had to digitally composite certain wide shots in which all of them appear, as well as make use of stunt doubles and various camera perspective tricks. However, while the different races come in three size ranges, the adaptations only used <i>two</i> different size scales, by putting both Dwarves and Hobbits into the same size scale. This was achieved by simply casting tall actors to play Dwarves, who thus seem proportionately taller than the average-height Hobbit actors, without the need for camera tricks or digital compositing. Thus Gimli rarely interacts with Men and Elves within the same camera frame, or when he does, Gimli is actually played by a shorter stunt double seen from behind. In contrast, because Dwarves and Hobbits are in the same size scale, Gimli interacts freely with the Hobbit characters within the same camera frame. A good example is when the Fellowship enters <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="Lothlórien">Lothlórien</a>, and Gimli walks up to Frodo and puts his hand on his shoulder, warning him to stay close. Actor John Rhys-Davies is actually 6'1'' (1.85 m) (two inches (5 cm) taller than Viggo Mortensen, who plays Aragorn), much taller than Elijah Wood (Frodo) who is 5'6'' (1.67 m), thus keeping the proportion that Dwarves are taller than Hobbits.</p>
<p>In Peter Jackson's adaption of The Hobbit, a wider range of dwarves is seen: the 13 in Thorin's Company and others seen early on in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit:_An_Unexpected_Journey" title="The Hobbit: An Unexpected Journey">The Hobbit: An Unexpected Journey</a></i>. Much more of the Dwarves' history and culture is seen throughout the main story, as with the 13 Dwarves of Thorin's Company. Examples of Khuzdul are seen, such as on <a href="/wiki/Thorin_Oakenshield" title="Thorin Oakenshield">Thorin Oakenshield</a>'s map, as well as the Dwarves' history, with the main story and the flashbacks.</p>
<p>Other dwarves appear in passing in two scenes of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring">The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring</a></i> movie: the "prologue" introduces the seven dwarf-lords that received the Rings of Power, and four more are present at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Elrond" title="Council of Elrond">Council of Elrond</a> (in contrast to the book, where only Gimli and his father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gl%C3%B3in,_son_of_Gr%C3%B3in" title="Glóin, son of Gróin">Glóin</a> are described). Peter Jackson's Dwarves wear scale armour.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decipher_Inc." title="Decipher Inc.">Decipher Inc.</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_Roleplaying_Game" title="The Lord of the Rings Roleplaying Game">The Lord of the Rings Roleplaying Game</a></i> (2001), based on the Jackson films, Dwarven player-characters get bonuses to Vitality and Strength attributes and must be given craft skills. In the <i>Dwarves of Middle-earth</i> (2003) supplement, the seven Dwarf Lords and their houses are named as Durin, Sindri, Linnar, Var, Uri, Thulin and Vigdis.</p>
<p>In the real-time strategy game <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Battle_for_Middle-earth_II" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Battle for Middle-earth II">The Lord of the Rings: The Battle for Middle-earth II</a></i>, and its <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Battle_for_Middle-earth_II:_The_Rise_of_the_Witch-king" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Battle for Middle-earth II: The Rise of the Witch-king">expansion</a>, both based on the Jackson films, Dwarves are heavily influenced by classical military practice, and use throwing axes, war hammers, spears, and circular or Roman-style shields. One dwarven unit is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phalanx_formation" title="Phalanx formation">"Phalanx"</a> and is very similar to its <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> counterpart.</p>
