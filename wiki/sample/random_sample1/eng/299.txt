<p><b>!Kung</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ʊ/ short 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span></span>/</a></span></span> (<b>!Xuun</b>), also known as <b>Ju</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> (language complex) spoken in <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> by the <a href="/wiki/%C7%83Kung_people" title="ǃKung people">ǃKung people</a>. Together with the <a href="/wiki/%C7%82%E2%80%99Amkoe_language" title="ǂ’Amkoe language">ǂHoan language</a>, it forms the proposed <a href="/wiki/Kx%27a_languages" title="Kx'a languages">Kx'a</a> <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a>. !Kung constituted one of the branches of the putative <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan</a> language family, and was called <b>Northern Khoisan</b> in that scenario, but the unity of Khoisan has never been demonstrated and is suspected to be spurious. Nonetheless, the term "Khoisan" has been retained as an umbrella term for click languages in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>!Kung is famous for having a large number of <a href="/wiki/Click_consonant" title="Click consonant">clicks</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_clicks" title="Alveolar clicks">ǃ</a> in its name, and has some of the most complex inventories of both <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a> in the world. It also has <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tone</a>. For a description, see <a href="/wiki/Ju%C7%80%27hoan_dialect" title="Juǀ'hoan dialect">Juǀʼhoansi</a>. To pronounce <i>!Xuun</i> (pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[!͡χũː˦˥]</a></span> in Western !Kung/!Xuun) one makes a <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_clicks" title="Alveolar clicks">click sound</a> before the <i>x</i> sound (which is like a Scottish or German <i>ch</i>), followed by a long <a href="/wiki/Nasal_vowel" title="Nasal vowel">nasal <i>u</i> vowel</a> with a high rising tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers">Speakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=!Kung_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Speakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If the !Kung languages are counted together, they would make the third-most-populous click language after <a href="/wiki/Khoekhoe_language" title="Khoekhoe language">Khoekhoe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandawe_language" title="Sandawe language">Sandawe</a>. The most populous !Kung language, Juǀʼhoan, is perhaps tied for third place with <a href="/wiki/Naro_language" title="Naro language">Naro</a>.</p>
<p>Estimates vary, but there are perhaps 15,000 speakers, though counting is difficult because speakers are scattered on farms, interspersed with speakers of other languages. Brenzinger (2011)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brenzinger_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brenzinger-1">[1]</a></sup> counts 9,000 in Namibia, 2,000 in Botswana, 3,700 in South Africa, and 1,000 in Angola. Botswana hosts a minority of Juǀʼhoan speakers. Most of these figures are preliminary estimates, especially in Angola, where no demographic or linguistic surveys have been conducted since the civil war.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Until the mid–late twentieth century, the ǃʼOǃKung and Maligo dialects were widespread in southern and central Angola. However, most !Kung fled the <a href="/wiki/Angolan_Civil_War" title="Angolan Civil War">Angolan Civil War</a> to Namibia (primarily to the <a href="/wiki/Caprivi_Strip" title="Caprivi Strip">Caprivi Strip</a>), where they were recruited into the South African Defence Force special forces against the Angolan Army and <a href="/wiki/SWAPO" title="SWAPO">SWAPO</a>. At the end of the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Border_War" title="South African Border War">Border War</a>, more than one thousand fighters and their families were relocated to <a href="/wiki/Schmidtsdrift" title="Schmidtsdrift">Schmidtsdrift</a> in South Africa amid uncertainty over their future in Namibia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After more than a decade living in precarious conditions, the post-Apartheid government bought and donated land for a permanent settlement at Platfontein, near Schmidtsdrift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=!Kung_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The better-known !Kung dialects are <a href="/wiki/Ju%C7%80%27hoan_dialect" title="Juǀ'hoan dialect">Tsumkwe Juǀʼhoan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ekoka_!Kung" title="Ekoka !Kung">Ekoka !Kung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sekele_language" title="Sekele language">ǃʼOǃKung</a>, and <a href="/wiki/%C7%82Kx%27ao-%C7%81%27ae" title="ǂKx'ao-ǁ'ae">ǂKxʼauǁʼein</a>. Scholars distinguish between eleven and fifteen dialects, which may not be mutually intelligible when not adjacent, but there are no clear-cut distinctions between them at our present state of knowledge.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protolanguage">Protolanguage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=!Kung_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protolanguage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ancestral language, Proto-Juu or Proto-!Xuun, had five places of click articulation: <a href="/wiki/Dental_clicks" title="Dental clicks">Dental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_clicks" title="Alveolar clicks">alveolar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palatal_clicks" title="Palatal clicks">palatal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lateral_clicks" title="Lateral clicks">alveolar lateral</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_clicks" title="Retroflex clicks">retroflex</a> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*ǃ˞</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*‼</span>). The retroflex clicks have dropped out of Southeastern dialects such as Juǀʼhoan, but remain in Central !Kung. In ǀʼAkhwe (Ekoka), the palatal click has become a <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_clicks#.22Fricated.22_alveolar_clicks" title="Alveolar clicks">fricated alveolar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
