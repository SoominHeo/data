<p>The <b>albacore</b> (<i>Thunnus alalunga</i>), known also as the <b>bonito</b> or <b>longfin tuna</b>, is a species of <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a> of the order <a href="/wiki/Perciformes" title="Perciformes">Perciformes</a>. It is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> waters across the globe in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epipelagic" title="Epipelagic">epipelagic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopelagic" title="Mesopelagic">mesopelagic</a> zones. There are six distinct stocks known globally in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a> oceans, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. The albacore has an elongate, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fusiform" title="Fusiform">fusiform</a> body with a conical snout, large eyes, and remarkably long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fins" title="Pectoral fins">pectoral fins</a>. Its body is a deep blue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsally" title="Dorsally">dorsally</a> and shades of silvery white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">ventrally</a>. Individuals can reach up to 1.4 m (4.6 ft) in length.</p>
<p>Hard-hitting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Predators" title="Predators">predators</a>, albacore eat a variety of foods including <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>. They are unique among tuna in that their primary food source is cephalopods, with fish making up a much smaller portion of their diet. Reproduction usually occurs from November to February and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a>. An adult female can release over 2 million eggs in a single cycle. Fry generally stay near where they were spawned for about a year before moving on. Albacore form schools based on their stage in the life cycle, but also combine with other tuna like the <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">yellowfin tuna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bluefin_tuna" title="Bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a>. Once grown, schools are highly migratory.</p>
<p>The albacore is a very economically important fish and is a target of <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational</a> fisheries. It was originally the basis for the United States tuna-canning industry and is no less important today, making up significant percentages of the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic products</a> of various Pacific nations. It is listed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature">International Union for the Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) because of the threat of overfishing. Several stocks are in significant decline and the species' overall population trend is decreasing.</p>

<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_description" title="Scientific description">scientific description</a> of the albacore was authored in 1788 by Pierre Joseph Bonnaterre in the illustrated encyclopedia <i>Tableau encyclopédique et methodique des trois règnes de la nature</i>. He originally placed it in the <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">mackerel</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Scomber" title="Scomber">Scomber</a></i>. It was assigned to the genus <i>Thunnus</i> by ichthyologists Bruce B. Collette and Cornelia E. Nauen in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-5">[5]</a></sup> It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thunnus_(subgenus)" title="Thunnus (subgenus)">Thunnus subgenus</a>, also known as the bluefin group. Populations of albacore differ genetically by region, with Atlantic, Pacific, and Mediterranean groups each showing differences in <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondial</a> and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">nuclear DNA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vinas_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-vinas-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_(10438834583).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1928" data-file-width="3200" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_%2810438834583%29.jpg/220px-Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_%2810438834583%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_%2810438834583%29.jpg/330px-Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_%2810438834583%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_%2810438834583%29.jpg/440px-Histoire_naturelle_des_poissons_%2810438834583%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The albacore has an streamlined, fusiform body with a conical snout, large mouth, and big eyes. Its body is dark blue dorsally, shades of silvery white ventrally, and covered by small scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fao-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fins" title="Pectoral fins">pectoral fins</a> begin slightly before the first <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> and extend well beyond the front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a>, usually as far as the second dorsal finlet, often as long as 30% of the fish's total length. Like the fish's body, the fins are dark blue on top, but change to a medium yellow color on the underside. They are markedly shorter in fish under 0.5 m (1.6 ft) in length, often resulting in confusion with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunnus_obesus" title="Thunnus obesus">T. obesus</a></i> juveniles, which also have long pectoral fins, though these are rounded at the tips where the albacore's taper to a point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-5">[5]</a></sup> The first dorsal fin is a deep yellow and the second, which is smaller than the first, is a light yellow, as is the anal fin. It has 7-9 dorsal finlets and 7-8 anal finlets, dark blue and silvery white in color respectively, matching the part of the fish's body they are on. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a> is also silvery white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wash_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-wash-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-fao-7">[7]</a></sup> At 1.4 m (4.6 ft) maximum length, the albacore is the smallest of the <a href="/wiki/Thunnus_(subgenus)" title="Thunnus (subgenus)">bluefin tuna</a>. It reaches sexual maturity at 0.9 m (3.0 ft) and its common length is only slightly larger at 1.0 m (3.3 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-5">[5]</a></sup> Males and females exhibit no <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ramon_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ramon-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The albacore has a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan distribution</a> in tropical and temperate waters across the globe and in every ocean as well as the Mediterranean Sea. A highly migratory species, schools of albacore travel great distances, though Atlantic and Pacific populations do not appear to mix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-fao-7">[7]</a></sup> North Pacific albacore migrate to two regions of the Northeast Pacific: one off the northern part of <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>, Mexico, and the other off the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-childers_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-childers-10">[10]</a></sup> Every summer, North Atlantic albacore head to the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> off of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, but now arrive about 8 days earlier than they did 40 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dufour_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dufour-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its latitudinal range extends from <a href="/wiki/59th_parallel_north" title="59th parallel north">59°N</a> to <a href="/wiki/46th_parallel_south" title="46th parallel south">46°S</a>. Its temperature range is 10–25 °C (50–77 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-5">[5]</a></sup> Its depth range is 0–600 metres (0–1,969 ft) in the epipelagic and mesopelagic zones. It is most often found in surface waters 15.6–19.4 °C (60.1–66.9 °F) in temperature, though larger individuals can be found in deeper waters 13.5–25.2 °C (56.3–77.4 °F). It can survive at temperatures as low as 9.5 °C (49.1 °F) for short periods of time. It favors areas where warm and cool water mix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are 6 globally managed stocks of albacore worldwide, one in the North Pacific, one in the South Pacific, another in the Indian Ocean, two for the North and South Atlantic, and one in the Mediterranean Sea. Both Pacific stocks are stable, while both Atlantic stocks are likely overfished. The status of the Indian and Mediterranean stocks is less clear, but preliminary reports suggest that these are also declining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_ecology">Biology and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg"><img alt="Hooked albacore tuna.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="600" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg/220px-Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg/330px-Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg/440px-Hooked_albacore_tuna.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The albacore is a powerful, hard-hitting predator<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-5">[5]</a></sup> that forms mixed schools with <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">yellowfin tuna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bluefin_tuna" title="Bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a>, sometimes around floating objects like <a href="/wiki/Sargassum" title="Sargassum">sargassum</a> weeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-fao-7">[7]</a></sup> Schools of albacore are highly migratory within bodies of water and segregated by maturity, with older fish tending to form more compact groups. Of those caught by humans, immature albacore have a 1:1 sex ratio while older albacore are mostly male. In the Atlantic Ocean, older fish are found in cooler waters. The opposite is true for the Pacific Ocean, where fish are found more abundantly along thermal discontinuities. Depth range also varies by location: Atlantic fish dive as deep as 600 m (2,000 ft) where Pacific fish reach only 380 m (1,250 ft) in depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-fao-7">[7]</a></sup> In the northeast Atlantic, feeding migrations to productive areas occur during the summer. Due to climate changes over the last 40 years, the timing and spatial distribution of the albacore have also changed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dufour_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-dufour-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Albacore show a broad range of behavioral differences by region. In <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>, albacore make frequent dives to depths exceeding 200 m (660 ft) during the day and stay near the surface at night, while off the coast of Washington and Oregon they stay near the surface the entire day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-childers_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-childers-10">[10]</a></sup> Albacore never really rest; their need for oxygen means they must always be on the move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dufour_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-dufour-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albacore tuna are pelagic predators - open-sea hunters. Their diets vary very little from season to season. Unlike other tuna that eat primarily fish, for example the <a href="/wiki/Bigeye_tuna" title="Bigeye tuna">bigeye</a> and yellowfin tuna, the albacore's main source of food is <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>. The most abundant cephalopod in its diet is <i><a href="/wiki/Heteroteuthis_dispar" title="Heteroteuthis dispar">Heteroteuthis dispar</a></i>, a tiny deep-water squid found in the Mediterranean Sea and Atlantic Ocean. Other food sources of the albacore include fish, crustaceans, and gelatinous organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bertrand_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bertrand-13">[13]</a></sup> Not much is known about the food pattern of the albacore, however, mostly because it dives over 400 m underwater when searching for food, and tagging and tracking has thus far been unsuccessful thus far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bertrand_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-bertrand-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_history">Life history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The albacore's reproduction is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a> and a 20 kg (44 lb) female can produce between 2-3 million eggs per spawning,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-fao-7">[7]</a></sup> which usually takes place between November and February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ramon_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ramon-9">[9]</a></sup> Eggs mature outside of the female's body and hatch in 1-2 days, after which <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fry" title="Fry">fry</a> begin to grow quickly. For the first year of their lives, juveniles remain close to the place where they were hatched. They begin to migrate after their first year. Albacore have a lifespan of 11-12 years, but they reach reproductive maturity at around 5-6 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thisfish_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-thisfish-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A large majority of albacore have larger right testes or ovaries, depending on sex. Albacore have asynchronous <a href="/wiki/Oocyte" title="Oocyte">oocyte</a> development, that is their immature egg cells do not develop at regular intervals. The creation of <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Ova" title="Ova">ova</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Oogenesis" title="Oogenesis">oogenesis</a>, begins with the rapid production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oogonia" title="Oogonia">oogonia</a> (undifferentiated germ cells that give rise to oocytes) by mitotic separations in the oogonial nests of female tuna. The resulting oocytes are cast <i>en masse</i> into the sea, where full development and later fertilization take place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_fishing">Commercial fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Commercial fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albacore is a prized food, and the albacore <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> is economically significant. Methods of fishing include <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">pole and line</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-line_fishing" title="Long-line fishing">long-line fishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troll_(angling)" title="Troll (angling)">trolling</a>, and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encircling_net" title="Encircling net">purse seining</a>. It is also sought after by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_fishing" title="Sport fishing">sport fishers</a>.</p>
<p>The harvest of albacore tuna for commercial use began at the start of the 20th century. The migratory patterns of the fish brought droves of albacore schools near the coastline of southern California, which sparked the start of commercial albacore fishing. In 1903, 700 cases of albacore were used as an experimental pack which ultimately lead to the development of the U.S. tuna-canning industry. The experiment was a huge success, and the commercial fishery expanded rapidly due to the high level of demand for canned tuna. By the 1920s, the industry expanded further and three other species of tuna, bluefin, yellowfin, and skipjack, were also being canned. Albacore tuna is the only species that can be marketed as "white meat tuna". The canning industry uses this label as a way to differentiate canned albacore from other types of tuna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific island</a> countries and territories (PICTs) heavily rely on oceanic fisheries for economic development and food security. The albacore is one of the main four species of tuna that support oceanic fisheries along with the skipjack, yellowfin, and the bigeye tunas. Domestic tuna fleets and local fish processing operations contribute from 3-20% of the gross domestic product in four PICTs. License fees from foreign ships provide an average of 3-40% of government revenue for seven different PICTs. Processing facilities and tuna fishing vessels provide more than 12,000 jobs for workers in the Pacific islands. Fish provide 50-90% of dietary animal protein in rural areas of PICTs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bell_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bell-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albacore are managed by four tuna Regional Fisheries Management Organizations, (RFMO's) include the <a href="/wiki/Western_and_Central_Pacific_Fisheries_Commission" title="Western and Central Pacific Fisheries Commission">Western and Central Pacific Fisheries Commission</a> (WCPFC), the <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Tropical_Tuna_Commission" title="Inter-American Tropical Tuna Commission">Inter-American Tropical Tuna Commission</a> (IATTC), the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_for_the_Conservation_of_Atlantic_Tunas" title="International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas">International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas</a> (ICCAT), and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean_Tuna_Commission" title="Indian Ocean Tuna Commission">Indian Ocean Tuna Commission</a> (IOTC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-allen-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>ICCAT has established catch quotas in the North and South Atlantic.There is substantial uncertainty on current stock status, since different models and assumptions provide a wide range of estimates However, most of them agreed on the view that spawning stock biomass decreased since the 1930s and started to recover since the mid-1990s Most of the model formulations, as well as the base case,concluded that currently the stock is not undergoing <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> but the spawning stock biomass is overfished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> IOTC judges albacore in the Indian Ocean are not overfished, but maintaining or increasing effort in the core albacore fishing grounds is likely to result in further declines in albacore biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The WCPFC has assessed the South Pacific Albacore are not over fished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In the 2014 assessment,the International Scientific Committee for Tuna and Tuna-like Species in North Pacific Ocean (ISC), Albacore Working Group (ALBWG), found estimates of total stock biomass(age-1 and older)show a long term decline from the early 1970s to 1990 followed by a recovery through the 1990s and subsequent fluctuations without trend in the 2000s. The ALBWG concludes that the stock is likely not in an overfished condition at present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> All of the tuna Regional Fisheries Management Organizations noted that there is uncertainty surrounding the life history and biology of Tunas and tuna like species including age and growth, maturity, and natural mortality rates; uncertainty about the quality and completeness of available data; and uncertainty about recruitment.</p>
<p>In the North Pacific, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> Fisheries, Southwest Fisheries Science Center (SWFSC). Since the 1970s the SWFSC has collaborated with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Fishermen%27s_Research_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Fishermen's Research Foundation (page does not exist)">American Fishermen's Research Foundation</a> (AFRF) in tagging studies of albacore. Through these studies we have learned that juvenile albacore (2 to years of age) make trans-Pacific migrations in their younger years between Japan and the West coast of North America. To date over 24,000 albacore have been tagged with conventional dart tags and 1,245 of these have been recovered. In Spring of 2001 AFRF and the SWFSC began a pilot project to learn more about the migration habits of North Pacific albacore, Thunnus alalunga in an effort to allow the incorporation of detailed migration movements into stock assessment models. Archival tags are a recent technical innovation that are being used to collect daily locations (through light level data recorded by the tag), internal temperature of the fish's abdomen, ambient water temperature, and depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of programs have been developed to help consumers identify and support responsible and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_fisheries" title="Sustainable fisheries">sustainable fisheries</a>. Perhaps the most widely accepted of these programs is that of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Stewardship_Council" title="Marine Stewardship Council">Marine Stewardship Council</a> (MSC). Several albacore fisheries have been certified as sustainable according to MSC standards, including the U.S. North and South Pacific albacore <a class="external text" href="http://www.fao.org/fishery/fishtech/30/en" rel="nofollow">pole and line</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.fao.org/fishery/fishtech/1015/en" rel="nofollow">troll/jig</a> fisheries ("pole and troll"), Canadian North Pacific troll fishery, and the New Zealand South Pacific troll fishery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSC_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSC-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/SeaChoice" title="SeaChoice">SeaChoice</a> ranks albacore as a "best choice" for consumers, although notes some "moderate concerns" regarding the management effectiveness (in particular, no definitive assessment of the albacore stock of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> fishery has taken place), and "moderate concern" over the fishing stock, especially regarding the North Atlantic albacore population, which the <a href="/wiki/National_Marine_Fisheries_Service" title="National Marine Fisheries Service">National Marine Fisheries Service</a> (NMFS) considers overfished with <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> still occurring. The southern Atlantic stock is also considered (in 2007) overfished but not currently experiencing overfishing. North Pacific albacore stocks are not overfished and are not experiencing overfishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> South Pacific albacore stocks have recently (2007 to 2015) shown a 40% reduction in stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury_levels">Mercury levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mercury levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">Mercury poisoning</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mercury_in_fish" title="Mercury in fish">Mercury in fish</a></div>
<p>Like other fish, albacore accumulate <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a> in body tissue over time. Methylmercury is removed from the body naturally, but it may take over a year for the levels to drop significantly. Thus, it may remain in a woman from before she becomes pregnant. Ranging from as low as .027 ppm (parts per million) to .26 ppm, the average total mercury content of albacore is .14 ± .05 ppm. Larger fish tend to <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulate</a> higher methylmercury levels. For the most part, there is positive correlation between an albacore's methylmercury measurement and its weight and length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrissey_2008_41.E2.80.9352_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morrissey_2008_41.E2.80.9352-29">[29]</a></sup> Albacore caught by the American albacore fishing fleet off the coasts of Washington, Oregon, and California have far lower mercury levels than in previous years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Albacore caught in this region also show methylmercury levels well below the 1.0 ppm mercury standard set by The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrissey_2008_41.E2.80.9352_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morrissey_2008_41.E2.80.9352-29">[29]</a></sup> Nevertheless, since mercury does take time to be removed from the body, albacore tuna should be eaten in moderation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_species_called_albacore">Other species called albacore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albacore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other species called albacore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some parts of the world, other species may be called "albacore":</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blackfin_tuna" title="Blackfin tuna">Blackfin tuna</a> <i>Thunnus atlanticus</i> (albacore)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">Yellowfin tuna</a> <i>Thunnus albacares</i> (albacore, autumn albacore, yellowfinned albacore)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yellowtail_amberjack" title="Yellowtail amberjack">Yellowtail amberjack</a> <i>Seriola lalandi</i> (albacore)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kawakawa_(fish)" title="Kawakawa (fish)">Kawakawa</a> <i>Euthynnus affinis</i> (false albacore)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Little_tunny" title="Little tunny">Little tunny</a> <i>Euthynnus alletteratus</i> (false albacore)</li>
</ul>
