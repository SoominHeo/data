<p><i><b>Deinosuchus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/juː/ long 'u' in 'cute'">juː</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">DY</span></span></b>-nə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">SEW</span></span></b>-kəs</i></a></span>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Alligator" title="Alligator">alligator</a> that lived 80 to 73 million years ago (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annum" title="Annum">Ma</a>), during the late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale#Terminology" title="Geologic time scale">period</a>. The name translates as "terrible crocodile" and is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>deinos</i> (δεινός), "terrible", and <i>soukhos</i> (σοῦχος), "crocodile". The first remains were discovered in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> (United States) in the 1850s; the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> was named and described in 1909. Additional fragments were discovered in the 1940s and were later incorporated into an influential, though inaccurate, skull reconstruction at the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a>. Knowledge of <i>Deinosuchus</i> remains incomplete, but better <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">cranial</a> material found in recent years has expanded scientific understanding of this massive predator.</p>
<p>Although <i>Deinosuchus</i> was far larger than any modern crocodile or alligator, with the largest adults measuring 10.6 m (35 ft) in total length, its overall appearance was fairly similar to its smaller relatives. It had large, robust teeth built for crushing, and its back was covered with thick hemispherical <a href="/wiki/Osteoderm" title="Osteoderm">osteoderms</a>. One study indicated <i>Deinosuchus</i> may have lived for up to 50 years, growing at a rate similar to that of modern crocodilians, but maintaining this growth over a much longer time.</p>
<p><i>Deinosuchus</i> fossils have been found in 10 US states, including Texas, Montana, and many along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>. Fossils have also been found in northern Mexico. It lived on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a>, and was an opportunistic <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predator</a> in the coastal regions of eastern North America. <i>Deinosuchus</i> reached its largest size in its western habitat, but the eastern populations were far more abundant. Opinion remains divided as to whether these two populations represent separate <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. <i>Deinosuchus</i> was probably capable of killing and eating large <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>. It may have also fed upon <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, fish, and other aquatic and terrestrial prey.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="645" data-file-width="576" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG/170px-Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG/255px-Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG/340px-Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus_hatcheri.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Life restoration of <i>D. rugosus</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Despite its large size, the overall appearance of <i>Deinosuchus</i> was not considerably different from that of modern crocodilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-lifetimes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-lifetimes-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> had an <a href="/wiki/Alligator" title="Alligator">alligator</a>-like, broad snout, with a slightly bulbous tip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-lifetimes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-lifetimes-1">[1]</a></sup> Each <a href="/wiki/Premaxilla" title="Premaxilla">premaxilla</a> contained four teeth, with the pair nearest to the tip of the snout being significantly smaller than the other two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-species_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-species-2">[2]</a></sup> Each <a href="/wiki/Maxilla" title="Maxilla">maxilla</a> (the main tooth-bearing bone in the upper jaw) contained 21 or 22 teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup> The tooth count for each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dentary" title="Dentary">dentary</a> (tooth-bearing bone in the lower jaw) was at least 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-species_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-species-2">[2]</a></sup> All the teeth were very thick and robust; those close to the rear of the <a href="/wiki/Jaw" title="Jaw">jaws</a> were short, rounded, and blunt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-prey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-prey-4">[4]</a></sup> They appear to have been adapted for crushing, rather than piercing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-prey_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-prey-4">[4]</a></sup> When the mouth was closed, only the fourth tooth of the lower jaw would have been visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-species_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-species-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern saltwater crocodiles (<a href="/wiki/Saltwater_crocodile" title="Saltwater crocodile"><i>Crocodylus porosus</i></a>) have the strongest recorded bite of any living animal, with a maximum force of 16,414 N (3,690 lbf).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-erickson2012-5">[5]</a></sup> The bite force of <i>Deinosuchus</i> has been estimated to be 18,000 N (4,000 lbf)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-lifetimes_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-lifetimes-1">[1]</a></sup> to 100,000 N (22,000 lbf).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson2012_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-erickson2012-5">[5]</a></sup> It has been argued that even the largest and strongest <a href="/wiki/Theropoda" title="Theropoda">theropod</a> dinosaurs, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tyrannosaurus" title="Tyrannosaurus">Tyrannosaurus</a></i>, probably had bite forces inferior to that of <i>Deinosuchus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson-et-al_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-erickson-et-al-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Deinosuchus</i> had a secondary bony palate, which would have permitted it to breathe through its nostrils while the rest of the head remained submerged underwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cloudsley-thompson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cloudsley-thompson-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a> were articulated in a procoelous manner, meaning they had a concave hollow on the front end and a convex bulge on the rear; these would have fit together to produce a <a href="/wiki/Ball_and_socket_joint" title="Ball and socket joint">ball and socket joint</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-foster-9">[9]</a></sup> The secondary palate and procoelous vertebrae are advanced features also found in modern <a href="/wiki/Eusuchia" title="Eusuchia">eusuchian</a> crocodilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cloudsley-thompson_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-cloudsley-thompson-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rolleston-jackson_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-rolleston-jackson-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Osteoderm" title="Osteoderm">osteoderms</a> (scutes) covering the back of <i>Deinosuchus</i> were unusually large, heavy, and deeply pitted; some were of a roughly hemispherical shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup> Deep pits and grooves on these osteoderms served as attachment points for connective tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup> Together, the osteoderms and connective tissue would have served as load-bearing reinforcement to support the massive body of <i>Deinosuchus</i> out of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup> These deeply pitted osteoderms have been used to suggest that, despite its bulk, <i>Deinosuchus</i> could probably have walked on land much like modern-day crocodiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cloudsley-thompson_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-cloudsley-thompson-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Large_crocodyliformes.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1150" data-file-width="1900" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Large_crocodyliformes.svg/220px-Large_crocodyliformes.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Large_crocodyliformes.svg/330px-Large_crocodyliformes.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Large_crocodyliformes.svg/440px-Large_crocodyliformes.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Large_crocodyliformes.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The maximum size reached by <i>Deinosuchus</i> (yellow) is currently estimated at 10.6 m (35 ft). In contrast, the largest modern <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_crocodile" title="Saltwater crocodile">saltwater crocodiles</a> rarely reach more than 6 m (20 ft) in length.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The large size of <i>Deinosuchus</i> has generally been recognized despite the fragmentary nature of the fossils assigned to it. However, estimates of how large it really was have varied considerably over the years. The original estimate from 1954 for the type specimen of the then-named <i>"Phobosuchus riograndensis"</i> were based on a skull of 1.5 m (4.9 ft) and a lower jaw of 1.8 m (5.9 ft) long, reconstructed with similar proportions to the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_crocodile" title="Cuban crocodile">Cuban crocodile</a> giving a total estimated length of 15 m (49 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> However, this reconstruction is currently considered to be inaccurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup> Using more complete remains, it was estimated in 1999 that the size attained by specimens of <i>Deinosuchus</i> varied from 8 to 10 m (26 to 33 ft) with weights from 2.5 to 5 t (2.8 to 5.5 short tons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson-brochu_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-erickson-brochu-13">[13]</a></sup> This was later corroborated when it was noted that most known specimens of <i>D. rugosus</i> usually had skulls of about 1 m (3.3 ft) with estimated total lengths of 8 m (26 ft) and weights of 2.3 t (2.5 short tons). A reasonably well-preserved skull specimen discovered in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> indicated the animal's head measured about 1.31 m (4.3 ft), and its body length was estimated at 9.8 m (32 ft). However, the largest fragmentary remains of <i>D. riograndensis</i> were 1.5 times the size of those of the average <i>D. rugosus</i> and it was determined that the largest individuals of this species may have been up to 12 m (39 ft) in length and perhaps weighed as much as 8.5 t (9.4 short tons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A particularly large mandibular fragment from a <i>D. riograndensis</i> specimen was estimated to have come from an individual with a skull length of about 147.5 cm (4.84 ft). This length was used in conjunction with a regression equation relating skull length to total length in the <a href="/wiki/American_alligator" title="American alligator">American alligator</a> to estimate a total length of 10.6 metres (35 ft) for this particularly specimen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farlowetal2005_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farlowetal2005-14">[14]</a></sup> This is only slightly lower than previous estimates for the species. <i>Deinosuchus</i> has often been described as the largest <a href="/wiki/Crocodyliformes" title="Crocodyliformes">crocodyliform</a> of all time. However other crocodyliformes such as <i><a href="/wiki/Purussaurus" title="Purussaurus">Purussaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rhamphosuchus" title="Rhamphosuchus">Rhamphosuchus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sarcosuchus" title="Sarcosuchus">Sarcosuchus</a></i> may have equaled or exceeded it in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochu-memoirs_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-brochu-memoirs-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="2048" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Deinosuchus.jpg/220px-Deinosuchus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Deinosuchus.jpg/330px-Deinosuchus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Deinosuchus.jpg/440px-Deinosuchus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <i>Deinosuchus</i> jaw fragment, exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Museum_of_Natural_Sciences" title="North Carolina Museum of Natural Sciences">North Carolina Museum of Natural Sciences</a>: Fossils of this large alligatoroid have been discovered in 10 US states and northern Mexico.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Deinosuchus</i> was present on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-localities_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-localities-16">[16]</a></sup> Specimens have been found in 10 U.S. states: Utah, Montana, Wyoming, New Mexico, New Jersey, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Texas, and North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titus-et-al_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-titus-et-al-17">[17]</a></sup> A <i>Deinosuchus</i> osteoderm from the <a href="/wiki/San_Carlos_Formation" title="San Carlos Formation">San Carlos Formation</a> was also reported in 2006, so the giant crocodilian's range may have included parts of northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westgate-et-al_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-westgate-et-al-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> fossils are most abundant in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coastal_Plain" title="Gulf Coastal Plain">Gulf Coastal Plain</a> region of Georgia, near the Alabama border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-localities_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-localities-16">[16]</a></sup> All known specimens of <i>Deinosuchus</i> were found in rocks dated to the <a href="/wiki/Campanian" title="Campanian">Campanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Stage_(stratigraphy)" title="Stage (stratigraphy)">stage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale#Terminology" title="Geologic time scale">period</a>. The oldest examples of this genus lived approximately 80 Ma, and the youngest lived around 73 Ma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lucas_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lucas-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The distribution of <i>Deinosuchus</i> specimens indicates these giant crocodilians may have preferred <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuarine</a> environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-localities_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-localities-16">[16]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Aguja_Formation" title="Aguja Formation">Aguja Formation</a> of Texas, where some of the largest specimens of <i>Deinosuchus</i> have been found, these massive predators probably inhabited <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish-water</a> <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bays</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anglen-lehman_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-anglen-lehman-20">[20]</a></sup> Although some specimens have also been found in <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">marine</a> deposits, it is not clear whether <i>Deinosuchus</i> ventured out into the ocean (like modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltwater_Crocodile" title="Saltwater Crocodile">saltwater crocodiles</a>); these remains might have been displaced after the animals died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-localities_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-localities-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> has been described as a "conspicuous" component of a purportedly distinct biome occupying the southern half of Late Cretaceous North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lehman_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-lehman-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kritosaurus_BW.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="800" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Kritosaurus_BW.jpg/220px-Kritosaurus_BW.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Kritosaurus_BW.jpg/330px-Kritosaurus_BW.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Kritosaurus_BW.jpg/440px-Kritosaurus_BW.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kritosaurus_BW.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Deinosuchus</i> may have preyed upon large <a href="/wiki/Ornithopod" title="Ornithopod">ornithopods</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Kritosaurus" title="Kritosaurus">Kritosaurus</a></i> lived alongside the giant crocodilian in the <a href="/wiki/Aguja_Formation" title="Aguja Formation">Aguja Formation</a> ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sankey_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-sankey-22">[22]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1954, Edwin H. Colbert and Roland T. Bird speculated that <i>Deinosuchus</i> "may very well have hunted and devoured some of the dinosaurs with which it was contemporaneous".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> Colbert restated this hypothesis more confidently in 1961: "Certainly this crocodile must have been a predator of dinosaurs; otherwise why would it have been so overwhelmingly gigantic? It hunted in the water where the giant theropods could not go."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-dinoworld_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-dinoworld-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-debus_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-debus-24">[24]</a></sup> David R. Schwimmer proposed in 2002 that several <a href="/wiki/Hadrosaurid" title="Hadrosaurid">hadrosaurid</a> tail vertebrae found near <a href="/wiki/Big_Bend_National_Park" title="Big Bend National Park">Big Bend National Park</a> show evidence of <i>Deinosuchus</i> tooth marks, strengthening the hypothesis that <i>Deinosuchus</i> fed on dinosaurs in at least some instances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-prey_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-prey-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2003, Christopher A. Brochu agreed that <i>Deinosuchus</i> "probably dined on <a href="/wiki/Ornithopod" title="Ornithopod">ornithopods</a> from time to time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochu-review_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-brochu-review-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> is generally thought to have employed hunting tactics similar to those of modern crocodilians, ambushing dinosaurs and other terrestrial animals at the water's edge and then submerging them until they drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lifehistory_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-lifehistory-26">[26]</a></sup> A 2014 study suggested that it would have been able to perform a "<a href="/wiki/Crocodilia#Feeding" title="Crocodilia">death roll</a>", like modern crocodiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schwimmer and G. Dent Williams proposed in 1996 that <i>Deinosuchus</i> may have preyed on <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">marine turtles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-williams_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-williams-28">[28]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> would probably have used the robust, flat teeth near the back of its jaws to crush the turtle shells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-prey_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-prey-4">[4]</a></sup> The "<a href="/wiki/Pleurodira" title="Pleurodira">side-necked</a>" sea turtle <i><a href="/wiki/Bothremydidae" title="Bothremydidae">Bothremys</a></i> was especially common in the eastern habitat of <i>Deinosuchus</i>, and several of its shells have been found with bite marks that were most likely inflicted by the giant crocodilian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-prey_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-prey-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-williams_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-williams-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schwimmer concluded in 2002 that the feeding patterns of <i>Deinosuchus</i> most likely varied by geographic location; the smaller <i>Deinosuchus</i> specimens of eastern North America would have been opportunistic feeders in an ecological niche similar to that of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Alligator" title="American Alligator">American alligator</a>. They would have consumed marine turtles, large fish, and smaller dinosaurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup> The bigger, but less common, <i>Deinosuchus</i> that lived in Texas and Montana might have been more specialized hunters, capturing and eating large dinosaurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup> Schwimmer noted no theropod dinosaurs in <i>Deinosuchus'</i>s eastern range approached its size, indicating the massive crocodilian could have been the region's <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-prey_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-prey-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, Mark Witton took a rigorous look at the diet of <i>Deinosuchus</i>. His results show that, although <i>Deinosuchus</i> did eat dinosaurs, its diet was mostly made up of sea turtles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_rates">Growth rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Growth rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="2800" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png/220px-Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png/330px-Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png/440px-Deinosuchus_scutes_by_Hatcher.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

The osteoderms of <i>Deinosuchus</i>, as illustrated by W.J. Holland. They are proportionately much thicker than those of modern crocodilians</div>

</div>
<p>A 1999 study by Gregory M. Erickson and Christopher A. Brochu suggested the growth rate of <i>Deinosuchus</i> was comparable to that of modern crocodilians, but was maintained over a far longer time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson-brochu_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-erickson-brochu-13">[13]</a></sup> Their estimates, based on growth rings in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsum_(biology)" title="Dorsum (biology)">dorsal</a> osteoderms of various specimens, indicated each <i>Deinosuchus</i> might have taken over 35 years to reach full adult size, and the oldest individuals may have lived for more than 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson-brochu_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-erickson-brochu-13">[13]</a></sup> This was a completely different growth strategy than that of large dinosaurs, which reached adult size much more quickly and had shorter lifespans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erickson-brochu_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-erickson-brochu-13">[13]</a></sup> According to Erickson, a full-grown <i>Deinosuchus</i> "must have seen several generations of dinosaurs come and go".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-connor_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-connor-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schwimmer noted in 2002 that Erickson and Brochu's assumptions about growth rates are only valid if the osteodermal rings reflect annual periods, as they do in modern crocodilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup> According to Schwimmer, the growth ring patterns observed could have been affected by a variety of factors, including "migrations of their prey, wet-dry seasonal climate variations, or oceanic circulation and nutrient cycles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup> If the ring cycle were biannual rather than annual, this might indicate <i>Deinosuchus</i> grew faster than modern crocodilians, and had a similar maximum lifespan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-size_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-size-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="1920" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png/220px-Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png/330px-Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png/440px-Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polyptychodon_tooth_by_Emmons.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ebenezer Emmons illustrated two fossil teeth in 1858. Most likely, they belonged to the crocodilian that would later be named <i>Deinosuchus</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1858, <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ebenezer_Emmons" title="Ebenezer Emmons">Ebenezer Emmons</a> described two large fossil teeth found in <a href="/wiki/Bladen_County,_North_Carolina" title="Bladen County, North Carolina">Bladen County, North Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmons_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-emmons-31">[31]</a></sup> Emmons assigned these teeth to <i><a href="/wiki/Polyptychodon" title="Polyptychodon">Polyptychodon</a></i>, which he then believed to be "a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of crocodilian reptiles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmons_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-emmons-31">[31]</a></sup> Later discoveries showed that <i>Polyptychodon</i> was actually a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pliosaur" title="Pliosaur">pliosaur</a>, a type of marine reptile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup> The teeth described by Emmons were thick, slightly curved, and covered with vertically grooved enamel; he assigned them a new <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> name, <i>P. rugosus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmons_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-emmons-31">[31]</a></sup> Although not initially recognized as such, these teeth were probably the first <i>Deinosuchus</i> remains to be scientifically described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup> Another large tooth that likely came from <i>Deinosuchus</i>, discovered in neighboring <a href="/wiki/Sampson_County,_North_Carolina" title="Sampson County, North Carolina">Sampson County</a>, was named <i>Polydectes biturgidus</i> by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">Edward Drinker Cope</a> in 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1903, at <a href="/wiki/Willow_Creek,_Montana" title="Willow Creek, Montana">Willow Creek, Montana</a>, several fossil osteoderms were discovered "lying upon the surface of the soil" by <a href="/wiki/John_Bell_Hatcher" title="John Bell Hatcher">John Bell Hatcher</a> and T.W. Stanton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup> These osteoderms were initially attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Ankylosauridae" title="Ankylosauridae">ankylosaurid</a> dinosaur <i><a href="/wiki/Euoplocephalus" title="Euoplocephalus">Euoplocephalus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup> Excavation at the site, carried out by W.H. Utterback, yielded further fossils, including additional osteoderms, as well as vertebrae, <a href="/wiki/Rib" title="Rib">ribs</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Pubis_(bone)" title="Pubis (bone)">pubis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup> When these specimens were examined, it became clear that they belonged to a large crocodilian and not a dinosaur; upon learning this, Hatcher "immediately lost interest" in the material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup> After Hatcher died in 1904, his colleague <a href="/wiki/William_Jacob_Holland" title="William Jacob Holland">W.J. Holland</a> studied and described the fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup> Holland assigned these specimens to a new genus and species, <i>Deinosuchus hatcheri</i>, in 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> δεινός/<i>deinos</i>, meaning "terrible", and σοῦχος/<i>suchos</i>, meaning "crocodile".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holland_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-holland-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1545" data-file-width="2352" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png/220px-1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png/330px-1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png/440px-1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1954-Colbert-Bird-Phobosuchus.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This skull reconstruction, exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a> for nearly a half-century, is probably the best known of all <i>Deinosuchus</i> fossils. The darker-shaded portions are actual fossil bone, while the light portions are plaster.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A 1940 expedition by the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a> yielded more fossils of giant crocodilians, this time from <a href="/wiki/Big_Bend_National_Park" title="Big Bend National Park">Big Bend National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> These specimens were described by Edwin H. Colbert and Roland T. Bird in 1954, under the name <i>Phobosuchus riograndensis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> Donald Baird and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Horner_(paleontologist)" title="Jack Horner (paleontologist)">Jack Horner</a> later assigned the Big Bend remains to <i>Deinosuchus</i>, which has been accepted by most modern authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baird-horner_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-baird-horner-32">[32]</a></sup> The genus name <i>Phobosuchus</i>, which was initially created by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Nopcsa_von_Fels%C5%91-Szilv%C3%A1s" title="Franz Nopcsa von Felső-Szilvás">Baron Franz Nopcsa</a> in 1924, has since been discarded because it contained a variety of different crocodilian species that turned out to not be closely related to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American Museum of Natural History incorporated the skull and jaw fragments into a plaster restoration, modeled after the present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuban_Crocodile" title="Cuban Crocodile">Cuban crocodile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> Colbert and Bird stated this was a "conservative" reconstruction, since an even greater length could have been obtained if a long-skulled modern species, such as the saltwater crocodile had been used as the template.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> Because it was not then known that <i>Deinosuchus</i> had a broad snout, Colbert and Bird miscalculated the proportions of the skull, and the reconstruction greatly exaggerated its overall width and length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup> Despite its inaccuracies, the reconstructed skull became the best-known specimen of <i>Deinosuchus</i>, and brought public attention to this giant crocodilian for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous additional specimens of <i>Deinosuchus</i> were discovered over the next several decades. Most were quite fragmentary, but they expanded knowledge of the giant predator's geographic range. As noted by Chris Brochu, the osteoderms are distinctive enough that even "bone granola" can adequately confirm the presence of <i>Deinosuchus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-early_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-early-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochu-occurrences_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-brochu-occurrences-33">[33]</a></sup> Better cranial material was also found; by 2002, David R. Schwimmer was able to create a composite computer reconstruction of 90% of the skull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-lifetimes_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-lifetimes-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-localities_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-localities-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_and_species">Classification and species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinosuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classification and species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2349" data-file-width="2288" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg/220px-Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg/330px-Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg/440px-Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deinosuchus_at_the_CMNH.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Deinosuchus</i> scutes and vertebra, <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Carnegie Museum of Natural History">Carnegie Museum of Natural History</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Since the discovery of the earliest fragmentary remains that will come to be known as <i>Deinosuchus</i>, it was found that it was a relative of <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, going as far as placing it in the same <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (<a href="/wiki/Crocodylidae" title="Crocodylidae">crocodylidae</a>) in 1954, this assignment was mostly supported by dental features<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colbert-bird_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-colbert-bird-8">[8]</a></sup> but was overturned in 1999 when the finding of new specimens from <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_(US_state)" title="Georgia (US state)">Georgia</a> helped place <i>Deinosuchus</i> in a phylogenetic analysis, finding it in a basal position within the clade <a href="/wiki/Alligatoroidea" title="Alligatoroidea">Alligatoroidea</a> along with <i><a href="/wiki/Leidyosuchus" title="Leidyosuchus">Leidyosuchus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochu-memoirs_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-brochu-memoirs-15">[15]</a></sup> This classification was bolstered in 2005 by the discovery of a well-preserved <i>Deinosuchus</i> <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">brain case</a> from the Blufftown Formation of Alabama, which shows some features reminiscent of those in the modern American alligator,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knight_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-knight-34">[34]</a></sup> <i>Deinosuchus</i> however, was not a direct ancestor of modern alligators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-genealogy_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-genealogy-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species pertaining to <i>Deinosuchus</i> since the resurrection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genus_(biology)" title="Genus (biology)">generic</a> name in 1979 have been traditionally recognized as <i>D. rugosus</i> from the eastern side of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a> and the larger <i>D. riograndensis</i> from the western side, characterized by differences of the shape of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osteoderms" title="Osteoderms">osteoderms</a> and teeth, this view was not always favored by all researchers and in 2002 it was proposed that there was only one species, <i>Deinosuchus rugosus</i>, with the differences found between the two species explained as the result of the larger size of the western morph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwimmer-species_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwimmer-species-2">[2]</a></sup> This proposal faced support<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lucas_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-lucas-19">[19]</a></sup> and criticism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westgate-et-al_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-westgate-et-al-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anglen-lehman_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-anglen-lehman-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochu-review_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-brochu-review-25">[25]</a></sup> and was dropped in 2010 when new fossils of specimens from both sides of the interior seaway showed that the differences between the two morphs were not just size related, teeth from large eastern specimens retained the common characteristics of other specimens of the region and osteoderms from small specimens of the western morph were no different from those of large ones, supporting the specific separation of the two morphs, now formalized as <i>D. rugosus</i> and <i>D. riograndensis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwimmer2010_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwimmer2010-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
