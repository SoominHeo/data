<p>The domain name <b>com</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> (TLD) in the <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. Its name is derived from the word <i>commercial</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> indicating its original intended purpose for domains registered by commercial organizations. Later, the domain opened for general purposes.</p>
<p>The domain was originally administered by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a>, but is today operated by <a href="/wiki/Verisign" title="Verisign">Verisign</a>, and remains under ultimate jurisdiction of U.S. law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Verisign Registrations in the .com domain are processed via <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registrar" title="Domain name registrar">registrars</a> accredited by <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>. The registry accepts <a href="/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">internationalized domain names</a>.</p>
<p>The domain was one of the original <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domains</a> (TLDs) in the Internet when the Domain Name System was implemented in January 1985, the others being <a href="/wiki/.edu" title=".edu">edu</a>, <a href="/wiki/.gov" title=".gov">gov</a>, <a href="/wiki/.mil" title=".mil">mil</a>, <a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">net</a>, <a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">org</a>, and <a href="/wiki/.arpa" title=".arpa">arpa</a>. It has grown into the largest top-level domain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vreport_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-vreport-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The domain com was one of the first set of top-level domains when the Domain Name System was first implemented for use on the Internet in January 1985. The domain was administered by the U.S. Department of Defense (DoD), however the department contracted the domain maintenance to <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>. SRI created DDN-NIC, also known as SRI-NIC, or simply <i>the NIC</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_Information_Center" title="Network Information Center">Network Information Center</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> then accessible online with the domain name nic.ddn.mil. Beginning October 1, 1991, an operations contract was awarded to Government Systems Inc. (GSI), which sub-contracted it to <a href="/wiki/Network_Solutions" title="Network Solutions">Network Solutions</a> Inc. (NSI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 1, 1993, the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> assumed responsibility of maintenance, as com was primarily being used for non-defense interests. The NSF contracted operation to <a href="/wiki/Network_Solutions" title="Network Solutions">Network Solutions</a> (NSI). In 1995, the NSF authorized NSI to begin charging registrants an annual fee, for the first-time since the domain's inception. Initially the fee was US$50 per year, with US$35 going to NSI, and US$15 going to a government fund. New registrations had to pay for the first two years, making the new-domain registration fee US$100. In 1997 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">United States Department of Commerce</a> assumed authority over all generic TLDs. It is currently operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VeriSign" title="VeriSign">VeriSign</a>, which had acquired Network Solutions. VeriSign later spun off Network Solutions' non-registry functions into a separate company which continues as a registrar. In the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> the domain is often spelled with a leading period and commonly pronounced as <i>dot-com</i>, and has entered common parlance this way.</p>
<p>Although com domains were originally intended to designate commercial entities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (others such as government agencies or educational institutions have different top-level domains assigned to them), there has been no restriction on who can register com domains since the mid-1990s. With the commercialization and popularization of the Internet, the com domain was opened to the public and quickly became the most common top-level domain for <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">websites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a>, and networking. Many companies that flourished in the period from 1997 to 2001, (the time known as the "<a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>") incorporated the com suffix into company names; these became known as dot-coms or <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot-com companies</a>. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/.biz" title=".biz">biz</a> in 2001, which is restricted to businesses, has had no impact on the popularity of com.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although companies anywhere in the world can register com domains, many countries have a second-level domain with a similar purpose under their own country code top-level domain (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLD</a>), such as Australia (com.au), Sri Lanka (com.lk), Greece (com.gr), Mexico (com.mx), South Korea (co.kr), India (co.in), Indonesia (co.id), China (com.cn), Japan (co.jp), Vietnam (.com.vn) or (.vn) and the United Kingdom (co.uk) are all examples.</p>
<p>Many non-commercial sites and networks use com names to benefit from the perceived recognizability of a com domain. However, the registration statistics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vreport_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-vreport-6">[6]</a></sup> show varying popularity over the years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As of December 2011, VeriSign reported that approximately 100 million com domains were registered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-201112_Monthly_Transaction_Report_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-201112_Monthly_Transaction_Report-10">[10]</a></sup> As of March 2009, VeriSign reported that the domain is served by 926 accredited registrars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-201112_Monthly_Transaction_Report_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-201112_Monthly_Transaction_Report-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Department of Commerce on November 29, 2012, approved renewal of the .com Registry Agreement between Verisign, Inc., and <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>. Through this agreement, Verisign will manage the registry for the .com top level domain until November 30, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
