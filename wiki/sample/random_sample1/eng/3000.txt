<p>The <b>cod icefishes</b> or <b>nothothens</b> are the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Nototheniidae</b> of <a href="/wiki/Acanthopterygii" title="Acanthopterygii">acanthopterygian</a> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fishes</a>. They are traditionally placed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perciform" title="Perciform">perciform</a> assemblage together with their relatives, but like every lineage in the "Perciformes", their actual relationships are not yet determined with certainty.</p>
<p>They are largely found in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. As the dominant Antarctic fish taxa, they occupy both sea-bottom and water-column ecological niches. Although lacking a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_bladder" title="Gas bladder">gas bladder</a>, they have undergone a depth-related diversification, such as increased fatty tissues and reduced mineralization of the bones, resulting in a body density approaching neutral, to fill a variety of niches. The spleen may be used to remove ice crystals from circulating blood. As the chilly subantarctic waters averages –1 to 4°C, most Antarctic species have <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze_protein" title="Antifreeze protein">antifreeze glycoproteins</a> in their blood and other body fluids.</p>
<p>Some species exhibit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" title="Morph (zoology)">polymorphism</a>, for example, the circum-Antarctic <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trematomus_newnesi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trematomus newnesi (page does not exist)">Trematomus newnesi</a></i> exists as two morphs in the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Sea" title="Ross Sea">Ross Sea</a>, the typical morph and a large-mouthed/broad-headed morph.</p>
<p>As the major fish resource in the Southern Ocean, notothens are under increasing pressure from commercial fishing.</p>
