<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gender Development Index</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Gender Development Index</b> (<b>GDI</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Index_(statistics)" title="Index (statistics)">index</a> designed to <a href="/wiki/Measures_of_gender_equality" title="Measures of gender equality">measure of gender equality</a>.</p>
<p>GDI together with the <a href="/wiki/Gender_Empowerment_Measure" title="Gender Empowerment Measure">Gender Empowerment Measure</a> (GEM) were introduced in 1995 in the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Report" title="Human Development Report">Human Development Report</a> written by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Program" title="United Nations Development Program">United Nations Development Program</a>. The aim of these measurements was to add a gender-sensitive dimension to the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI). The first measurement that they created as a result was the Gender-related Development Index (GDI). The GDI is defined as a “distribution-sensitive measure that accounts for the human development impact of existing gender gaps in the three components of the HDI” (Klasen 243). Distribution sensitive means that the GDI takes into account not only the average or general level of well-being and wealth within a given country, but focuses also on how this wealth and well-being is distributed between different groups within society. The <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">HDI</a> and the GDI (as well as the <a href="/wiki/Gender_Empowerment_Measure" title="Gender Empowerment Measure">GEM</a>) were created to rival the more traditional general income-based measures of development such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">Gross Domestic Product</a> (GDP) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_National_Product" title="Gross National Product">Gross National Product</a> (GNP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gender_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gender-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_calculation">Definition and calculation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gender_Development_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and calculation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GDI is often considered a “gender-sensitive extension of the HDI” (Klasen 245). It addresses gender-gaps in life expectancy, education, and incomes. It uses an “inequality aversion” penalty, which creates a development score penalty for gender gaps in any of the categories of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> which include life expectancy, adult <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">literacy</a>, school enrollment, and logarithmic transformations of per-capita income. In terms of life expectancy, the GDI assumes that women will live an average of five years longer than men. Additionally, in terms of income, the GDI considers income-gaps in terms of actual earned income.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gender_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gender-1">[1]</a></sup> The GDI cannot be used independently from the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI) score and so, it cannot be used on its own as an indicator of gender-gaps. Only the gap between the HDI and the GDI can actually be accurately considered; the GDI on its own is not an independent measure of gender-gaps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reforming_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reforming-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gender_Development_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_debates">General debates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gender_Development_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General debates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the years since its creation in 1995, much debate has arisen surrounding the reliability, and usefulness of the Gender Development Index (GDI) in making adequate comparisons between different countries and in promoting gender-sensitive development. The GDI is particularly criticized for being often mistakenly interpreted as an independent measure of gender-gaps when it is not, in fact, intended to be interpreted in that way, because it can only be used in combination with the scores from the Human Development Index, but not on its own. Additionally, the data that is needed in order to calculate the GDI is not always readily available in many countries, making the measure very hard to calculate uniformly and internationally. There is also worry that the combination of so many different developmental influences in one measurement could result in muddled results and that perhaps the GDI (and the GEM) actually hide more than they reveal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gender_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gender-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Debates_surrounding_the_life_expectancy_adjustment">Debates surrounding the life expectancy adjustment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gender_Development_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Debates surrounding the life expectancy adjustment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More specifically, there has been a lot of debate over the life-expectancy component of the Gender-related Development Index (GDI). As was mentioned previously, the GDI life expectancy section is adjusted to assume that women will live, normally, five years longer than men. This provision has been debated, and it has been argued that if the GDI was really looking to promote true equality, it would strive to attain the same life-expectancy for women and men, despite what might be considered a biological advantage or not. However, this may seem paradoxical in terms of policy implications, because, theoretically, this could only be achieved through providing preferential treatment to males, effectively discriminating against females. Furthermore, it has been argued that the GDI doesn’t account for <a href="/wiki/Sex-selective_abortion" title="Sex-selective abortion">sex-selective abortion</a>, meaning that the penalty levied against a country for gender inequality is less because it affects less of the population (see Sen, Missing Women).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gender_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gender-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Debates_surrounding_income_gaps">Debates surrounding income gaps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gender_Development_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Debates surrounding income gaps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another area of debate surrounding the Gender-related Development Index (GDI) is in the area of income gaps. The GDI considers income-gaps in terms of actual earned income. This has been said to be problematic because often, men may make more money than women, but their income is shared. Additionally, the GDI has been criticized because it does not consider the value of care work as well as other work performed in the informal sector (such as cleaning, cooking, housework, and childcare). Another criticism of the GDI is that it only takes gender into account as a factor for inequality, it does not, however, consider inequality among class, region or race, which could be very significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gender_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Gender-1">[1]</a></sup> Another criticism with the income-gap portion of the GDI is that it is heavily dependent on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">Gross Domestic Product</a> (GDP) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_National_Product" title="Gross National Product">Gross National Product</a> (GNP). For most countries, the earned-income gap accounts for more than 90% of the gender penalty.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suggested_alternatives">Suggested alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gender_Development_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Suggested alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As was suggested by Halis Akder in 1994, one alternative to the Gender-related Development Index would be the calculation of a separate male and female <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI). Another suggested alternative is the Gender Gap Measure which could be interpreted directly as a measure of gender inequality, instead of having to be compared to the Human Development Index (HDI) as the GDI is. It would average the female-male gaps in human development and use a gender-gap in labor force participation instead of earned income. In the 2010 Human Development Report, another alternative to the Gender-related Development Index (GDI), namely, the <a href="/wiki/Gender_Inequality_Index" title="Gender Inequality Index">Gender Inequality Index</a> (GII) was proposed in order to address some of the shortcomings of the GDI. This new experimental measure contains three dimensions: Reproductive Health, Empowerment, and Labor Market Participation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reforming_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reforming-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
