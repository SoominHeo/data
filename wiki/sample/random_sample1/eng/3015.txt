<p><b>Southwest Finland</b>, also known in English as <b>Finland Proper</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <span lang="fi" xml:lang="fi"><i>Varsinais-Suomi</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv"><i>Egentliga Finland</i></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Finland" title="Regions of Finland">region</a> in south-western <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> that borders the regions of <a href="/wiki/Satakunta" title="Satakunta">Satakunta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tavastia_Proper" title="Tavastia Proper">Tavastia Proper</a>. Its capital and biggest city is <a href="/wiki/Turku" title="Turku">Turku</a> with 182,000 inhabitants, which was also the capital of Finland from its establishment around the 13th century until 1812.</p>
<p>The area comprising the southwest is largely the same as the historical province of <a href="/wiki/Finland_Proper_(historical_province)" title="Finland Proper (historical province)">Finland Proper</a>, so named because it is the original home of the tribe known as the <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a>. 5.7% of population of the region speaks Swedish natively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_name">Origin of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of Finland Proper has a historial function. In historic times, in the area of the present southern Finland lived three tribes, which were the <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tavastians" title="Tavastians">Tavastians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Karelians" title="Karelians">Karelians</a>. The southwestern part of the country, the province where the Finns lived, was called simply Finland (Finnish: <i>Suomi</i>). In the 17th century the name began to be used to refer to the whole land and a specified name for the lesser Finland was required. The first notes <i>Fennigia specialiter dicta</i> and <i>Fennigia presse dicta</i> were recorded in Latin in the 1650s and the Swedish <i>Finland för sig sielft</i> and <i>Egenteliga Finland</i> later in the 18th century the modern form <i>Egentliga Finland</i> being in official use at the end of the century. The Finnish term <i>Varsinais-Suomi</i> became established only around the 1850s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
