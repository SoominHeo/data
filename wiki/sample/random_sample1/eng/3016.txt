<p><b>Masculinity</b> (also called <b>boyhood</b>, <b>manliness</b>, or <b>manhood</b>) is a set of attributes, behaviors and roles generally associated with boys and men. Masculinity is socially constructed, but made up of both socially-defined and biologically-created factors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wijngaard_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wijngaard-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_and_Finn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_and_Finn-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunphy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sex_and_gender_distinction" title="Sex and gender distinction">distinct</a> from the definition of the <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> biological <a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">sex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferrante_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrante-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Both males and females can exhibit masculine traits and behavior. Those exhibiting both masculine and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminine" title="Feminine">feminine</a> characteristics are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Androgynous" title="Androgynous">androgynous</a>, and feminist philosophers have argued that gender ambiguity may blur gender classification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Masculine traits include <a href="/wiki/Courage" title="Courage">courage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assertiveness" title="Assertiveness">assertiveness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vetterling-Braggin_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vetterling-Braggin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worell_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Worell-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R._Murray_Thomas_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-R._Murray_Thomas-10">[10]</a></sup> These traits vary by location and context, and are influenced by social and cultural factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> An overemphasis on masculinity and power, often associated with a disregard for consequences and responsibility, is known as <a href="/wiki/Machismo" title="Machismo">machismo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Masculine qualities, characteristics or roles are considered typical of, or appropriate for, a boy or man. They have degrees of comparison: "more masculine" and "most masculine", and the opposite may be expressed by "unmanly" or "<a href="/wiki/Epicene" title="Epicene">epicene</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roget_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roget-13">[13]</a></sup> Similar to masculinity is <a href="/wiki/Virility" title="Virility">virility</a> (from the Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://la.wikipedia.org/wiki/vir" title="la:vir">vir</a></i>, "man"). The concept of masculinity varies historically and culturally; although the <a href="/wiki/Dandy" title="Dandy">dandy</a> was seen as a 19th-century ideal of masculinity, he is considered <a href="/wiki/Effeminacy" title="Effeminacy">effeminate</a> by modern standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Masculine norms, as described in Ronald F. Levant's <i>Masculinity Reconstructed</i>, are "avoidance of <a href="/wiki/Femininity" title="Femininity">femininity</a>; restricted emotions; sex disconnected from intimacy; pursuit of achievement and status; self-reliance; <a href="/wiki/Physical_strength" title="Physical strength">strength</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aggression" title="Aggression">aggression</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobia</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> These norms reinforce <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> by associating attributes and characteristics with one gender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The academic study of masculinity received increased attention during the late 1980s and early 1990s, with the number of courses on the subject in the United States rising from 30 to over 300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This has sparked investigation of the intersection of masculinity with other axes of social discrimination and concepts from other fields, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_construction_of_gender_difference" title="Social construction of gender difference">social construction of gender difference</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flood_2007_Viii_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flood_2007_Viii-18">[18]</a></sup> (prevalent in a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophical" title="Philosophical">philosophical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociological" title="Sociological">sociological</a> theories).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In many cultures, displaying characteristics not typical of one's gender may be a social problem. In <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, this labeling is known as <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender assumptions</a> and is part of <a href="/wiki/Socialization" title="Socialization">socialization</a> to meet the <a href="/wiki/Mores" title="Mores">mores</a> of a society. Non-standard behavior may be considered indicative of <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>, despite the fact that gender expression, <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a> are widely accepted as distinct concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nacua_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nacua-19">[19]</a></sup> When sexuality is defined in terms of object choice (as in early <a href="/wiki/Sexology" title="Sexology">sexology</a> studies), male homosexuality is interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Effeminacy" title="Effeminacy">effeminacy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrysler_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-chrysler-20">[20]</a></sup> Social disapproval of excessive masculinity may be expressed as "machismo"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-12">[12]</a></sup> or by <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologisms</a> such as "<a href="/wiki/Testosterone_poisoning" title="Testosterone poisoning">testosterone poisoning</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The relative importance of socialization and genetics in the development of masculinity is debated. Although <a href="/wiki/Social_conditioning" title="Social conditioning">social conditioning</a> is believed to play a role, <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoanalysts" title="Psychoanalysts">psychoanalysts</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a> believed that aspects of "feminine" and "masculine" identity are <a href="/wiki/Subconscious" title="Subconscious">subconsciously</a> present in all human males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[a]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historical development of gender roles is addressed by <a href="/wiki/Behavioural_genetics" title="Behavioural genetics">behavioural genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_ecology" title="Human ecology">human ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and sociology. All human cultures seem to encourage gender roles in literature, costume and song; examples may include the epics of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hengist_and_Horsa" title="Hengist and Horsa">Hengist and Horsa</a> tales and the <a href="/wiki/Norm_(philosophy)" title="Norm (philosophy)">normative</a> commentaries of <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> More specialized treatments of masculinity may be found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">Bhagavad Gita</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Bushido" title="Bushido">bushidō</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Hagakure" title="Hagakure">Hagakure</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_versus_nurture">Nature versus nurture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nature versus nurture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The extent to which masculinity is <a href="/wiki/Nature_versus_nurture" title="Nature versus nurture">inborn or conditioned</a> is debated. <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">Genome</a> research has yielded information about the development of masculine characteristics and the process of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_differentiation" title="Sexual differentiation">sexual differentiation</a> specific to the human reproductive system. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testis_determining_factor" title="Testis determining factor">testis determining factor</a> (also known as SRY protein) on the <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a>, critical for male sexual development, activates the <a href="/wiki/SOX9" title="SOX9">SOX9</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moniot_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moniot-23">[22]</a></sup> SOX9 works with the <a href="/wiki/SF1_(gene)" title="SF1 (gene)">SF1 protein</a> to increase the level of <a href="/wiki/Anti-M%C3%BCllerian_hormone" title="Anti-Müllerian hormone">anti-Müllerian hormone</a>, repressing female development while activating and forming a <a href="/wiki/Feed_forward_(control)" title="Feed forward (control)">feedforward</a> loop with the <a href="/wiki/FGF9" title="FGF9">FGF9</a> protein; this creates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testis_cords" title="Testis cords">testis cords</a> and is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Sertoli_cell" title="Sertoli cell">sertoli cells</a>, which aid in <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> The activation of SRY halts the process of creating a female, beginning a chain of events leading to <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testicle</a> formation, <a href="/wiki/Androgen" title="Androgen">androgen</a> production and a number of pre- and post-natal hormonal effects.</p>
<p>How a child develops <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a> is also debated. Some believe that masculinity is linked to the male body; in this view, masculinity is associated with <a href="/wiki/Human_penis" title="Human penis">male genitalia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeser_2010_3_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reeser_2010_3-25">[24]</a></sup> Others have suggested that although masculinity may be influenced by biology, it is also a cultural construct. Recent research has been done on one's self concept of masculinity and its relation to testosterone; the results have shown that masculinity not only differs in different cultures, but the levels of testosterone do not predict how masculine or feminine one feels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pletzer_2015_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pletzer_2015-26">[25]</a></sup> Proponents of this view argue that women can become men <a href="/wiki/Hormone_replacement_therapy_(female-to-male)" title="Hormone replacement therapy (female-to-male)">hormonally</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">physically</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeser_2010_3_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reeser_2010_3-25">[24]</a></sup> and many aspects of masculinity assumed to be natural are linguistically and culturally driven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> On the nurture side of the debate, it is argued that masculinity does not have a single source. Although the military has a vested interest in constructing and promoting a specific form of masculinity, it does not create it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Facial hair is linked to masculinity through language, in stories about boys becoming men when they begin to shave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeser_2010_30.E2.80.9331_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reeser_2010_30.E2.80.9331-29">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>Traditional avenues for men to gain honor were providing for their families and exercising leadership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Raewyn_Connell" title="Raewyn Connell">Raewyn Connell</a> has labeled traditional male roles and privileges <a href="/wiki/Hegemonic_masculinity" title="Hegemonic masculinity">hegemonic masculinity</a>, encouraged in men and discouraged in women: "Hegemonic masculinity can be defined as the configuration of gender practice which embodies the currently accepted answer to the problem of the legitimacy of <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchy</a>, which guarantees the dominant position of men and the subordination of women".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precarious_manhood">Precarious manhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Precarious manhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Researchers have argued that the "precariousness" of manhood contributes to traditionally-masculine behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> "Precarious" means that manhood is not inborn, but must be achieved. In many cultures, boys endure painful initiation rituals to become men. Manhood may also be lost, as when a man is derided for not "being a man." Researchers have found that men respond to threats to their manhood by engaging in stereotypically-masculine behaviors and beliefs, such as supporting hierarchy, espousing homophobic beliefs, supporting aggression and choosing physical tasks over intellectual ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Winegard and Geary wrote that the precariousness of manhood involves social status (prestige or dominance), and manhood may be more (or less) precarious due to the avenues men have for achieving status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Men who identify with creative pursuits, such as poetry or painting, may not experience manhood as precarious but may respond to threats to their intelligence or creativity. However, men who identify with traditionally-masculine pursuits (such as football or the military) may see masculinity as precarious. According to Winegard, Winegard, and Geary, this is functional; poetry and painting do not require traditionally-masculine traits, and attacks on those traits should not induce anxiety.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (August 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Masculinity#Dubious" title="Talk:Masculinity">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> Football and the military require traditionally-masculine traits, such as pain tolerance, endurance, muscularity and courage, and attacks on those traits induce anxiety and may trigger retaliatory impulses and behavior. This suggests that nature-versus-nurture debates about masculinity may be simplistic. Although men evolved to pursue prestige and dominance (status), how they pursue status depends on their talents, traits and available possibilities. In modern societies, more avenues to status may exist than in traditional societies and this may mitigate the precariousness of manhood (or of traditional manhood); however, it will probably not mitigate the intensity of male-male competition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_women">In women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although often ignored in discussions of masculinity, women can also express masculine traits and behaviors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> In Western culture, female masculinity has been codified into identities such as "<a href="/wiki/Tomboy" title="Tomboy">tomboy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Butch_and_femme" title="Butch and femme">butch</a>". Although female masculinity is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbianism</a>, expressing masculinity is not necessarily related to a woman's sexuality. In <a href="/wiki/Feminist_philosophy" title="Feminist philosophy">feminist philosophy</a>, female masculinity is often characterized as a type of gender performance which challenges traditional masculinity and <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">male dominance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Masculine women are often subject to social stigma and harassment, although the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">feminist movement</a> has led to greater acceptance of women expressing masculinity in recent decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_care">Health care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Health care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence points to the negative impact of hegemonic masculinity on men's health-related behavior, with American men making 134.5 million fewer physician visits per year than women. Men make 40.8 percent of all physician visits, including women's obstetric and gynecological visits. Twenty-five percent of men aged 45 to 60 do not have a personal physician, increasing their risk of death from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>. Men between 25 and 65 are four times more likely to die from <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a> than women, and are more likely to be diagnosed with a <a href="/wiki/Terminal_illness" title="Terminal illness">terminal illness</a> because of their reluctance to see a doctor. Reasons cited for not seeing a physician include fear, denial, embarrassment, a dislike of situations out of their control and the belief that visiting a doctor is not worth the time or cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2004, Arran Stibbe published an analysis of a well-known men's-health magazine in 2000. According to Stibbe, although the magazine ostensibly focused on health it also promoted traditional masculine behaviors such as excessive consumption of convenience foods and meat, alcohol consumption and unsafe sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research on beer-commercial content by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lance_Strate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lance Strate (page does not exist)">Lance Strate</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> yielded results relevant to a study of masculinity. In beer commercials, masculine behavior (especially risk-taking) is encouraged. Commercials often focus on situations in which a man overcomes an obstacle in a group, working or playing hard (construction or farm workers or <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboys</a>). Those involving play have central themes of mastery (of nature or each other), risk and adventure: fishing, camping, playing sports or socializing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">bars</a>. There is usually an element of danger and a focus on movement and speed (watching fast cars or driving fast). The bar is a setting for the measurement of masculinity in skills such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billiards" title="Billiards">billiards</a>, strength and drinking ability. Despite the beer industry's encouragement of risk-taking, alcohol consumption has declined in all age groups,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> and no particular paradigm exists that suggests males take more risk consuming liquor than females,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> and many males world-wide do not consume alcohol.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since what constitutes masculinity has varied by time and place, according to <a href="/wiki/Raewyn_Connell" title="Raewyn Connell">Raewyn Connell</a>, it is more appropriate to discuss "masculinities" than a single overarching concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> Study of the history of masculinity emerged during the 1980s, aided by the fields of women’s and (later) gender history. Before women’s history was examined, there was a "strict gendering of the public/private divide"; regarding masculinity, this meant little study of how men related to the household, domesticity and family life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-44">[43]</a></sup> Although women’s historical role was negated, despite the writing of history by (and primarily about) men a significant portion of the male experience was missing. This void was questioned during the late 1970s, when women’s history began to analyze gender and women to deepen the female experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> Joan Scott’s seminal article, calling for gender studies as an analytical concept to explore society, power and discourse, laid the foundation for this field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> According to Scott gender should be used in two ways: productive and produced. Productive gender examined its role in creating power relationships, and produced gender explored the use and change of gender throughout history. This has influenced the field of masculinity, as seen in Pierre Bourdieu’s definition of masculinity: produced by society and culture, and reproduced in daily life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> A flurry of work in women’s history led to a call for study of the male role (initially influenced by psychoanalysis) in society and emotional and interpersonal life. Connell wrote that these initial works were marked by a "high level of generality" in "broad surveys of cultural norms". The scholarship was aware of contemporary societal changes aiming to understand and evolve (or liberate) the male role in response to feminism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> John Tosh calls for a return to this aim for the history of masculinity to be useful, academically and in the public sphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity"><span id="Ancient"></span>Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Ancient literature dates back to about 3000 BC, with explicit expectations for men in the form of laws and implied masculine ideals in myths of gods and heroes. In the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> of 1000 BC, King David of Israel told his son to "be strong, and be a man" after David's death. Throughout history, men have met exacting cultural standards. Kate Cooper wrote about ancient concepts of femininity, "Wherever a woman is mentioned a man's character is being judged – and along with it what he stands for."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi" title="Code of Hammurabi">Code of Hammurabi</a></i> (about 1750 BC):</p>
<ul>
<li>Rule 3: "If any one bring an accusation of any crime before the elders, and does not prove what he has charged, he shall, if it be a capital offense charged, be put to death."</li>
<li>Rule 128: "If a man takes a woman to wife, but has no intercourse with her, this woman is no wife to him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Scholars cite <a href="/wiki/Integrity" title="Integrity">integrity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">equality</a> as masculine values in male-male relationships<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> and virility in male-female relationships. Legends of ancient heroes include the <i><a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i>. The stories demonstrate qualities in the hero which inspire <a href="/wiki/Respect" title="Respect">respect</a>, such as wisdom and courage: knowing things other men do not know and taking risks other men would not dare.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_and_Victorian_eras">Medieval and Victorian eras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medieval and Victorian eras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Richards" title="Jeffrey Richards">Jeffrey Richards</a> describes a European "medieval masculinity which was essentially Christian and chivalric."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> Courage, respect for women of all classes and generosity characterize the portrayal of men in literary history. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxons</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hengest" title="Hengest">Hengest</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horsa" title="Horsa">Horsa</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a> are examples of medieval masculine ideals. According to David Rosen, the traditional view of scholars (such as <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>) that <i>Beowulf</i> is a tale of medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heroism" title="Heroism">heroism</a> overlooks the similarities between Beowulf and the monster Grendel. The masculinity exemplified by Beowulf "cut[s] men off from women, other men, passion and the household".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Victorian era, <a href="/wiki/Victorian_masculinity" title="Victorian masculinity">masculinity</a> underwent a transformation from traditional heroism. Scottish philosopher <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Thomas Carlyle</a> wrote in 1831: "The old ideal of Manhood has grown obsolete, and the new is still invisible to us, and we grope after it in darkness, one clutching this phantom, another that; <a href="/wiki/The_Sorrows_of_Young_Werther" title="The Sorrows of Young Werther">Werterism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byron" title="Byron">Byronism</a>, even <a href="/wiki/Beau_Brummell" title="Beau Brummell">Brummelism</a>, each has its day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day"><span id="Modern_times"></span>Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the beginning of the twentieth century, a traditional family consisted of the father as <a href="/wiki/Breadwinner_model" title="Breadwinner model">breadwinner</a> and the mother as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homemaker" title="Homemaker">homemaker</a>. Characteristic of present-day masculinity is men's willingness to counter stereotypes. Regardless of age or nationality, men more frequently rank good health, a harmonious family life and a good relationship with their spouse or partner as important to their quality of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effeminacy">Effeminacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effeminacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Effeminacy#Effeminacy_and_gay_men" title="Effeminacy">Effeminacy § Effeminacy and gay men</a></div>
<p>Gay men are considered by some to "deviate from the masculine norm" and are <a href="/wiki/Benevolent_prejudice" title="Benevolent prejudice">benevolently stereotyped</a> as "gentle and refined", even by other gay men. According to gay human-rights campaigner <a href="/wiki/Peter_Tatchell" title="Peter Tatchell">Peter Tatchell</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Contrary to the well-intentioned claim that gays are "just the same" as straights, there is a difference. What is more, the distinctive style of gay masculinity is of great social benefit. Wouldn't life be dull without the flair and imagination of <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a> fashion designers and interior decorators? How could the NHS cope with no gay nurses, or the education system with no gay teachers? Society should thank its lucky stars that not all men turn out straight, macho and insensitive. The different hetero and homo modes of maleness are not, of course, biologically fixed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatchell_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tatchell-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Psychologist Joseph Pleck argues that a hierarchy of masculinity exists largely as a <a href="/wiki/Dichotomy" title="Dichotomy">dichotomy</a> of homosexual and heterosexual males: "Our society uses the male heterosexual-homosexual dichotomy as a central symbol for all the rankings of masculinity, for the division on any grounds between males who are "real men" and have power, and males who are not".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomas.org_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-nomas.org-58">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kimmel" title="Michael Kimmel">Michael Kimmel</a> adds that the <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">trope</a> "You're so gay" indicates a lack of masculinity, rather than homosexual orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> According to Pleck, to avoid male oppression of women, themselves and other men, <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchal</a> structures, institutions and discourse must be eliminated from Western society.</p>
<p>In the documentary <i>The Butch Factor</i>, gay men (one of them <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a>) were asked about their views of masculinity. Masculine traits were generally seen as an advantage in and out of the <a href="/wiki/Closeted" title="Closeted">closet</a>, allowing "butch" gay men to conceal their <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a> longer while engaged in masculine activities such as sports. <a href="/wiki/Effeminacy" title="Effeminacy">Effeminacy</a> is inaccurately<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nacua_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-nacua-19">[19]</a></sup> associated with <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrysler_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-chrysler-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> and some gay men doubted their sexual orientation; they did not see themselves as effeminate, and felt little connection to <a href="/wiki/LGBT_culture" title="LGBT culture">gay culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifbutscoconuts_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifbutscoconuts-61">[60]</a></sup> Some effeminate gay men in <i>The Butch Factor</i> felt uncomfortable about their femininity (despite being comfortable with their sexuality),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feminine_Gay_Man_Strength_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feminine_Gay_Man_Strength-62">[61]</a></sup> and feminine gay men may be derided by stereotypically-masculine gays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Feminine-looking men tended to <a href="/wiki/Coming_out" title="Coming out">come out</a> earlier after being labeled gay by their peers. More likely to face bullying and harassment throughout their lives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifbutscoconuts_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-ifbutscoconuts-61">[60]</a></sup> they are taunted by derogatory words (such as "<a href="/wiki/Sissy" title="Sissy">sissy</a>") implying feminine qualities. Effeminate, "<a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">campy</a>" gay men sometimes use what John R. Ballew called "camp humor", such as referring to one another by <a href="/wiki/She" title="She">female pronouns</a> (according to Ballew, "a funny way of defusing hate directed toward us [gay men]"); however, such humor "can cause us [gay men] to become confused in relation to how we feel about being men."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gay_men_and_masculinity_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gay_men_and_masculinity-65">[64]</a></sup> He further stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[Heterosexual] men are sometimes advised to get in touch with their "inner feminine." Maybe gay men need to get in touch with their "inner masculine" instead. Identifying those aspects of being a man we most value and then cultivate those parts of our selves can lead to a healthier and less distorted sense of our own masculinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gay_men_and_masculinity_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gay_men_and_masculinity-65">[64]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A study by the Center for Theoretical Study at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_University_in_Prague" title="Charles University in Prague">Charles University in Prague</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_Czech_Republic" title="Academy of Sciences of the Czech Republic">Academy of Sciences of the Czech Republic</a> found "significant" differences in shape among the faces of heterosexual and gay men, with gay men having "masculine" features ("undermin[ing] stereotypical notions of gay men as more feminine looking.")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gay men have been presented in the media as feminine and open to ridicule, although films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Brokeback_Mountain" title="Brokeback Mountain">Brokeback Mountain</a></i> are countering the stereotype.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gay_men_and_masculinity_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gay_men_and_masculinity-65">[64]</a></sup> A recent development is the portrayal of gay men in the <a href="/wiki/LGBT_community" title="LGBT community">LGBT community</a> as "<a href="/wiki/Bear_(gay_culture)" title="Bear (gay culture)">bears</a>", a <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subculture</a> of gay men celebrating rugged masculinity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mazzei_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mazzei-68">[67]</a></sup> and "secondary sexual characteristics of the male: <a href="/wiki/Facial_hair" title="Facial hair">facial hair</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Androgenic_hair" title="Androgenic hair">body hair</a>, proportional size, <a href="/wiki/Hair_loss" title="Hair loss">baldness</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Second-wave_feminism" title="Second-wave feminism">Second-wave</a> <a href="/wiki/Pro-feminism" title="Pro-feminism">pro-feminism</a> paid greater attention to issues of sexuality, particularly the relationship between homosexual men and <a href="/wiki/Hegemonic_masculinity" title="Hegemonic masculinity">hegemonic masculinity</a>. This shift led to increased cooperation between the <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_liberation_movement" title="Men's liberation movement">men's liberation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gay_liberation" title="Gay liberation">gay liberation</a> movements developing, in part, because masculinity was understood as a <a href="/wiki/Social_constructionism" title="Social constructionism">social construct</a> and in response to the universalization of "men" in previous men's movements. Men's-rights activists worked to stop second-wave feminists from influencing the gay-rights movement, promoting <a href="/wiki/Hypermasculinity" title="Hypermasculinity">hypermasculinity</a> as inherent to gay sexuality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Masculinity has played an important role in <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> culture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup> although lesbians vary widely in the degree to which they express masculinity and femininity. In <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> cultures, masculine women are often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Butch_and_femme" title="Butch and femme">butch</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two concerns over the study of the history of masculinity are that it would stabilize the historical process (rather than change it) and that a cultural overemphasis on the approach to masculinity lacks the reality of actual experience. According to John Tosh, masculinity has become a conceptual framework used by historians to enhance their cultural explorations instead of a specialty in its own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-75">[74]</a></sup> This draws attention from reality to representation and meaning, not only in the realm of masculinity; culture was becoming "the bottom line, the real historical reality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">[48]</a></sup> Tosh critiques Martin Francis' work of in this light because popular culture, rather than the experience of family life, is the basis for Francis’ argument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-76">[75]</a></sup> Francis uses contemporary literature and film to demonstrate that masculinity was restless, shying away from domesticity and commitment, during the late 1940s and 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-76">[75]</a></sup> Francis wrote that this flight from commitment was "most likely to take place at the level of fantasy (individual and collective)." In focusing on culture, it is difficult to gauge the degree to which films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Scott_of_the_Antarctic_(film)" title="Scott of the Antarctic (film)">Scott of the Antarctic</a></i> represented the era’s masculine fantasies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_76-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-76">[75]</a></sup> Michael Roper’s call to focus on the subjectivity of masculinity addresses this cultural bias, because broad understanding is set aside for an examination "of what the relationship of the codes of masculinity is to actual men, to existential matters, to persons and to their psychic make-up" (Tosh's human experience).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Tosh, the culture of masculinity has outlived its usefulness because it cannot fulfill the initial aim of this history (to discover how manhood was conditioned and experienced) and he urged "questions of behaviour and agency".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-75">[74]</a></sup> His work on Victorian masculinity uses individual experience in letters and sketches to illustrate broader cultural and social customs, such as birthing or Christmas traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stefan Dudink believes that the methodological approach (trying to categorize masculinity as a phenomenon) undermined its <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiographic</a> development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-78">[77]</a></sup> Abigail Solomou-Godeau’s work on post-revolutionary French art addresses a strong, constant patriarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tosh’s overall assessment is that a shift is needed in conceptualizing the topic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_75-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-75">[74]</a></sup> back to the history of masculinity as a speciality aiming to reach a broader audience, rather than as an analytical tool of cultural and social history. The importance he places on public history hearkens back to the initial aims of gender history, which sought to use history to enlighten and change the present. Tosh appeals to historians to live up to the "social expectation" of their work,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_75-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-75">[74]</a></sup> which would also require a greater focus on subjectivity and masculinity. This view is contrary to Dudink’s; the latter called for an "outflanking movement" towards the history of masculinity, in response to the errors he perceived in the study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-78">[77]</a></sup> This would do the opposite of what Tosh called for, deconstructing masculinity by not placing it at the center of historical exploration and using discourse and culture as indirect avenues towards a more-representational approach. In a study of the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, Dudink proposes moving beyond the history of masculinity by embedding analysis into the exploration of nation and nationalism (making masculinity a lens through which to view conflict and nation-building).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[79]</a></sup> Martin Francis' work on domesticity through a cultural lens moves beyond the history of masculinity because "men constantly travelled back and forward across the frontier of domesticity, if only in the realm of the imagination"; normative codes of behavior do not fully encompass the male experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_76-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-76">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Media images of boys and young men may lead to the persistence of harmful concepts of masculinity. According to men's-rights activists, the media does not address men's-rights issues and men are often portrayed negatively in advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[80]</a></sup> Peter Jackson called hegemonic masculinity "economically exploitative" and "socially oppressive": "The form of oppression varies from patriarchal controls over women's bodies and reproductive rights, through ideologies of domesticity, femininity and compulsory heterosexuality, to social definitions of the value of work, the nature of skill and the differential remuneration of 'productive' and 'reproductive' labor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_research">Psychological research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Psychological research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a paper submitted by Tracy Tylka to the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a>, "Instead of seeing a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Objectification" title="Objectification">objectification</a> of women in society, there has just been an increase in the objectification of both sexes. And you can see that in the media today." Men and women restrict food intake in an effort to achieve what they consider an attractively-thin body; in extreme cases, this leads to <a href="/wiki/Eating_disorder" title="Eating disorder">eating disorders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup> Psychiatrist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Holbrook" title="Thomas Holbrook">Thomas Holbrook</a> cited a recent Canadian study indicating that as many as one in six people with eating disorders are men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research in the United Kingdom found, "Younger men and women who read fitness and fashion magazines could be psychologically harmed by the images of perfect female and male physiques." Young women and men exercise excessively in an effort to achieve what they consider an attractively-fit and muscular body, which may lead to <a href="/wiki/Body_dysmorphic_disorder" title="Body dysmorphic disorder">body dysmorphic disorder</a> or <a href="/wiki/Muscle_dysmorphia" title="Muscle dysmorphia">muscle dysmorphia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[86]</a></sup> Although the stereotypes may have remained constant, the value attached to masculine stereotypes has changed; it has been argued that masculinity is an unstable phenomenon, never ultimately achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeser_2010_30.E2.80.9331_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reeser_2010_30.E2.80.9331-29">[28]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender-role_stress"><span id="Masculine_gender_role_stress"></span>Gender-role stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Gender-role stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1987 Eisler and Skidmore studied masculinity, creating the idea of "masculine stress" and finding three elements of masculinity which often result in emotional stress:</p>
<ul>
<li>The emphasis on prevailing in situations requiring body and fitness</li>
<li>Being perceived as emotional</li>
<li>The need for adequacy in sexual matters and financial status</li>
</ul>
<p>Because of social norms and pressures associated with masculinity, men with spinal-cord injuries must adapt their self-identity to the losses associated with such injuries; this may "lead to feelings of decreased physical and sexual prowess with lowered self-esteem and a loss of male identity. Feelings of guilt and overall loss of control are also experienced."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[87]</a></sup> Research also suggests that men feel social pressure to endorse traditional masculine male models in advertising. Brett Martin and Juergen Gnoth (2009) found that although feminine men privately preferred feminine models, they expressed a preference for traditional masculine models in public; according to the authors, this reflected social pressure on men to endorse traditional masculine norms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>In their book <i>Raising Cain: Protecting The Emotional Life of Boys</i>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Kindlon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dan Kindlon (page does not exist)">Dan Kindlon</a> and Michael Thompson wrote that although all boys are born loving and empathic, exposure to gender socialization (the tough male ideal and <a href="/wiki/Hypermasculinity" title="Hypermasculinity">hypermasculinity</a>) limits their ability to function as emotionally-healthy adults. According to Kindlon and Thompson, boys lack the ability to understand and express emotions productively because of the stress imposed by masculine gender roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the article "Sexual Ethics, Masculinity and Mutual Vulnerability," <a href="/wiki/Rob_Cover" title="Rob Cover">Rob Cover</a> works to unpack Judith Butler's study of masculinity. Cover goes over issues such as sexual assault and how it can be partially explained by a hypermasculinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Masculinity_in_crisis.22"><span id=".22Notion"></span>"Masculinity in crisis"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title='Edit section: "Masculinity in crisis"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A theory of "masculinity in crisis" has emerged;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[92]</a></sup> Australian archeologist Peter McAllister said, "I have a strong feeling that masculinity is in crisis. Men are really searching for a role in modern society; the things we used to do aren't in much demand anymore".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[93]</a></sup> Others see the changing labor market as a source of stress. <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">Deindustrialization</a> and the replacement of <a href="/wiki/Smokestack_industry" title="Smokestack industry">smokestack industries</a> by technology have allowed more women to enter the labor force, reducing its emphasis on physical strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crisis has also been attributed to <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> and its questioning of male dominance and rights granted to men solely on the basis of sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[95]</a></sup> British sociologist John MacInnes wrote that "masculinity has always been in one crisis or another", suggesting that the crises arise from the "fundamental incompatibility between the core principle of modernity that all human beings are essentially equal (regardless of their sex) and the core tenet of patriarchy that men are naturally superior to women and thus destined to rule over them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>John Beynon examined the discussion of masculinity in crisis, finding that masculinity and men are often conflated and it is unclear whether masculinity, men or both are allegedly in crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[97]</a></sup> According to Beynon, the "crisis" is not a recent phenomenon; he illustrated several periods of masculine crisis throughout history (some predating the women's movement and <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial society</a>), suggesting that due to masculinity's fluid nature "crisis is constitutive of masculinity itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[98]</a></sup> Film scholar Leon Hunt agreed: "Whenever masculinity's 'crisis' actually started, it certainly seems to have been in place by the 1970s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Herbivore_men">Herbivore men</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masculinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Herbivore men">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Herbivore_men" title="Herbivore men">Herbivore men</a></div>
<p>In 2008, the word "herbivore men" became popular in Japan and was reported worldwide. Herbivore men refers to young Japanese men who naturally detach themselves from masculinity. <a href="/wiki/Masahiro_Morioka" title="Masahiro Morioka">Masahiro Morioka</a> characterizes them as men 1) having gentle nature, 2) not bound by manliness, 3) not aggressive when it comes to romance, 4) viewing women as equals, and 5) hating emotional pain. Herbivore men was severely criticized by men who love masculinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[100]</a></sup></p>
