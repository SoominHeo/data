<p>The <b>South American foxes</b> (<i>Lycalopex</i>), commonly called "<b>raposas</b>", in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, or "<b>zorros</b>", in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, are a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">Canidae</a> family from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Despite their name, they are not <a href="/wiki/Vulpes" title="Vulpes">true foxes</a> but are a unique canid genus, which some somewhat resemble foxes and are named after them. The <a href="/wiki/South_American_gray_fox" title="South American gray fox">South American gray fox</a> <i>Lycalopex griseus</i>, is the most common species, and is known for its large ears and a highly marketable, russet-fringed <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">pelt</a>.</p>
<p>The oldest known fossils belonging to the genus were discovered in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, and date from 2.0 to 2.5 million years ago, in the mid to late <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucherini2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lucherini2008-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_American_fox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common English words "zorro" and "raposa" are loan words from <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, respectively, with both words originally meaning "fox". Current usage lists <i>Pseudalopex</i> (literally: "false fox") as synonymous with <i>Lycalopex</i> ("wolf fox"), with the latter taking precedence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup> The IUCN, for instance, retains the use of <i>Pseudalopex</i> while also acknowledging <i>Lycalopex</i> as a legitimate alternative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCNCulpeo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCNCulpeo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_American_fox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Species currently included in this genus include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Culpeo" title="Culpeo">Culpeo</a> or Andean fox, <i>Lycalopex culpaeus</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Darwin%27s_fox" title="Darwin's fox">Darwin's fox</a>, <i>Lycalopex fulvipes</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_American_gray_fox" title="South American gray fox">South American gray fox</a>, <i>Lycalopex griseus</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pampas_fox" title="Pampas fox">Pampas fox</a>, <i>Lycalopex gymnocercus</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sechuran_fox" title="Sechuran fox">Sechuran fox</a>, <i>Lycalopex sechurae</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hoary_fox" title="Hoary fox">Hoary fox</a>, <i>Lycalopex vetulus</i></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1914, Oldfield Thomas established the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Dusicyon" title="Dusicyon">Dusicyon</a></i>, in which he included these zorros. They were later reclassified to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lycalopex" title="Lycalopex">Lycalopex</a></i> (via <i>Pseudalopex</i>) by Langguth in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_American_fox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The raposas are hunted in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> for their durable, soft pelt. They are also often labelled 'lamb-killers'.</p>
