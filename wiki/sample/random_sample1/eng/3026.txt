<p>The <b>Southern Alps</b> (<a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a>: Kā Tiritiri o te Moana <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) are a <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a> extending along much of the length of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>'s <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a>, reaching its greatest elevations near the island's western side. The term "Southern Alps" generally refers to the entire range, although separate names are given to many of the smaller ranges that form part of it.</p>
<p>The range includes the South Island's <b>Main Divide</b>, which separates the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">water catchments</a> of the more heavily populated eastern side of the island from those on the west coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Politically, the Main Divide forms the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Canterbury,_New_Zealand" title="Canterbury, New Zealand">Canterbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Coast,_New_Zealand" title="West Coast, New Zealand">West Coast</a> <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_New_Zealand" title="Regions of New Zealand">Regions</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_description">Location and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Alps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Southern Alps run 500 km<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> north to south. Their tallest peak is <a href="/wiki/Aoraki_/_Mount_Cook" title="Aoraki / Mount Cook">Aoraki / Mount Cook</a>, the highest point in New Zealand at 3,724 metres (12,218 ft) and there are sixteen other points in the range that exceed 3,000 metres (9,800 ft) in height. The mountains are cut through with glacial valleys and lakes. According to an inventory conducted in the late 1970s, the Southern Alps contained over 3,000 <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> larger than a hectare,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chinn_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chinn-4">[4]</a></sup> the longest of which – the <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Glacier" title="Tasman Glacier">Tasman Glacier</a> – is 29 kilometres in length down towards <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pukaki" title="Lake Pukaki">Lake Pukaki</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glaciers_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-glaciers-5">[5]</a></sup> A chain of glacial lakes are found on the eastern side of the ridge from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Coleridge" title="Lake Coleridge">Lake Coleridge</a> in the north to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Wakatipu" title="Lake Wakatipu">Lake Wakatipu</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otago_Region" title="Otago Region">Otago</a> in the south.</p>
<p>Settlements include Maruia Springs, a spa near <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Pass" title="Lewis Pass">Lewis Pass</a>, the town of <a href="/wiki/Arthur%27s_Pass" title="Arthur's Pass">Arthur's Pass</a> and some lakeside resorts.</p>
<p>The Southern Alps were named by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Cook" title="Captain Cook">Captain Cook</a> on 23 March 1770, who described their "prodigious height".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naming_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naming-6">[6]</a></sup> They had previously been noted by <a href="/wiki/Abel_Tasman" title="Abel Tasman">Abel Tasman</a> in 1642, whose description of the South Island's west coast is often translated as "a land uplifted high".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tasman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tasman-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The climate is cold with snow and ice year-round at the highest points.</p>
<p>Because of its orientation perpendicular to the prevailing westerly winds, the range creates excellent wave soaring conditions for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glider_aircraft" title="Glider aircraft">glider</a> pilots. The town of <a href="/wiki/Omarama" title="Omarama">Omarama</a>, in the lee of the mountains, has gained an international reputation for its gliding conditions. The prevailing westerlies also create a weather pattern known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Nor%27west_arch" title="Nor'west arch">Nor'west arch</a></i>, in which moist air is pushed up over the mountains, forming an arch of cloud in an otherwise blue sky. This weather pattern is frequently visible in summer across <a href="/wiki/Canterbury,_New_Zealand" title="Canterbury, New Zealand">Canterbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Otago" title="North Otago">North Otago</a>. The 'Nor'wester' is a <a href="/wiki/Foehn_wind" title="Foehn wind">foehn wind</a> similar to the <a href="/wiki/Chinook_wind" title="Chinook wind">Chinook</a> of Canada, where mountain ranges in the path of prevailing moisture laden winds force air upwards, thus cooling the air and condensing the moisture to rain, producing hot dry winds in the descending air lee of the mountains.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Alps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Southern Alps lie along a geological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_boundary" title="Plate boundary">plate boundary</a>, part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Ring_of_Fire" title="Pacific Ring of Fire">Pacific Ring of Fire</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> to the southeast pushing westward and colliding with the northward-moving <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian Plate</a> to the northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Over the last 45 million years, the collision has pushed up a 20 km thickness of rocks on the Pacific Plate to form the Alps, although much of this has been eroded away. Uplift has been most rapid during the last 5 million years, and the mountains continue to be raised today by <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic</a> pressure, causing earthquakes on the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Fault" title="Alpine Fault">Alpine Fault</a> and other nearby faults. Despite the substantial uplift, most of the relative motion along the Alpine Fault is <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Strike-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">transverse</a>, not <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Dip-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">vertical</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, significant dip-slip occurs on the plate boundary to the north and east of the North Island, in the <a href="/wiki/Hikurangi_Trench" title="Hikurangi Trench">Hikurangi Trench</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Trench" title="Kermadec Trench">Kermadec Trench</a>. The transfer of motion from strike-slip on the Alpine Fault to dip-slip motion at these subduction zones to the north creates the <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_Fault_System" title="Marlborough Fault System">Marlborough Fault System</a>, which has resulted in significant uplift in the region.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Alps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The mountains are rich in flora with about 25% of the country's plant species being found above the treeline in <a href="/wiki/Alpine_plant" title="Alpine plant">alpine plant</a> habitats and grassland with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_beech" title="Mountain beech">mountain beech</a> forest at lower elevations (of the eastern side but not in <a href="/wiki/Westland_District" title="Westland District">Westland</a>). The cold windswept slopes above the treeline are covered with areas of <a href="/wiki/Fellfield" title="Fellfield">fellfield</a>. To the east, the Alps descend to the <a href="/wiki/Canterbury-Otago_tussock_grasslands" title="Canterbury-Otago tussock grasslands">high grasslands of Canterbury and Otago</a>. Plants adapted to the alpine conditions include woody shrubs like <i><a href="/wiki/Hebe_(plant)" title="Hebe (plant)">Hebe</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dracophyllum" title="Dracophyllum">Dracophyllum</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Coprosma" title="Coprosma">Coprosma</a></i>, the conifer <a href="/wiki/Podocarpus_nivalis" title="Podocarpus nivalis">snow totara</a> <i>(Podocarpus nivalis)</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Carex" title="Carex">Carex</a></i> sedge grasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Alps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wildlife of the mountains includes the endemic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_rockwren" title="New Zealand rockwren">rock wren</a> <i>(Xenicus gilviventris)</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Kea" title="Kea">kea</a>, a large parrot that was once hunted as a pest. There are also a number of endemic insects adapted to these high altitudes especially flies, moths, beetles, and bees. The beech forests of the lower elevations are important habitat for two birds; the <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_kiwi" title="Great spotted kiwi">great spotted kiwi</a> <i>(Apteryx haastii)</i> and the South Island <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_kaka" title="New Zealand kaka">kaka</a> <i>(Nestor meridionalis meridionalis)</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_preservation">Threats and preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Alps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Threats and preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mountains are inaccessible and retain their natural vegetation. A large proportion of the range is well protected as part of various national parks, notably the <a href="/wiki/Westland_Tai_Poutini_National_Park" title="Westland Tai Poutini National Park">Westland Tai Poutini National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Aspiring_National_Park" title="Mount Aspiring National Park">Mount Aspiring National Park</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aoraki/Mount_Cook_National_Park" title="Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park">Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park</a> or <a href="/wiki/Protected_areas_of_New_Zealand" title="Protected areas of New Zealand">protected areas</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Sumner_Forest_Park" title="Lake Sumner Forest Park">Lake Sumner Forest Park</a>. Indigenous plant life is affected by introduced animals such as <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> (Cervus elaphus), <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">chamois</a> (Rupicapra rupicapra), and <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_tahr" title="Himalayan tahr">Himalayan tahr</a> (Hemitragus jemlahicus) all of which have at times been targeted for culling, while the birds and reptiles are vulnerable to introduced predators.</p>
