<p>The <b>dinar</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">دينار</span>‎‎) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مؤسسة النقد للجنوب العربي</span>‎‎) was the currency of <a href="/wiki/South_Arabia" title="South Arabia">South Arabia</a> and then <a href="/wiki/South_Yemen" title="South Yemen">South Yemen</a> between 1965 and 1990. It was subdivided into 1000 <i>fils</i> (فلس). After Yemen's monetary unification on 1 July 1990, it was one of the two official currencies used in Yemen Republic until 11 June 1996.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Yemeni_dinar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dinar was introduced in 1965 as the South Arabian Dinar, replacing the <a href="/wiki/East_African_shilling" title="East African shilling">East African shilling</a> at a rate of 1 dinar = 20 shillings, thus setting the dinar initially equal to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_pound" title="British pound">British pound</a>. It was renamed the <b>South Yemeni dinar</b> after the <a href="/wiki/Aden_Protectorate" title="Aden Protectorate">Aden Protectorate</a> became independent in 1967 as the <a href="/wiki/South_Yemen" title="South Yemen">South Yemen</a>. The <b>South Yemeni dinar</b> was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Yemeni_rial" title="Yemeni rial">rial</a> following unification with <a href="/wiki/North_Yemen" title="North Yemen">North Yemen</a>. The exchange rate was 1 dinar = 26 rial. Dinar banknotes remained legal tender until 1996.</p>
<p>For a wider history surrounding currency in the region, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_History_of_British_Currency_in_the_Middle_East" title="The History of British Currency in the Middle East">The History of British Currency in the Middle East</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coins">Coins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Yemeni_dinar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1965, coins (dated 1964) were introduced for <a href="/wiki/South_Arabia" title="South Arabia">South Arabia</a> in denominations of 1, 5, 25 and 50 fils. The 1 fils was struck in aluminium, the 5 fils in bronze and the higher two denominations in cupro-nickel. In 1971, coins were issued in the name of "Democratic Yemen", changing to the "People's democratic Republic of Yemen" in 1973. That year, aluminium 2½ fils were introduced, followed by aluminium 10 fils and cupro-nickel 100 and 250 fils in 1981. The 10 fils was scalloped shaped whilst the 100 fils was <a href="/wiki/Octagon" title="Octagon">octagonal</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Banknotes">Banknotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Yemeni_dinar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Banknotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 1 April 1965, the South Arabian Currency Authority introduced notes in denominations of 250 and 500 fils, as well as 1 and 5 dinars. A 10-dinar note was issued on 1 July 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1984, the Bank of Yemen introduced 500 fils as well as 1, 5 and 10 dinar notes that are like the preceding issues of South Arabia, except the English text and printer’s imprint have been removed from the front, the name of the issuer has changed and now appears on the back, along with the name of the capital (ADEN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
