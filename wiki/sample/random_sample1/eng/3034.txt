<p>The superorder <b>Peracarida</b> is a large group of <a href="/wiki/Malacostraca" title="Malacostraca">malacostracan</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, having members in marine, freshwater, and terrestrial habitats. They are chiefly defined by the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Brood_pouch_(Peracarida)" title="Brood pouch (Peracarida)">brood pouch</a>, or <i>marsupium</i>, formed from thin flattened plates (<a href="/wiki/Oostegite" title="Oostegite">oostegites</a>) borne on the basalmost segments of the <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">legs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Peracarida is one of the largest crustacean taxa and includes about 12,000 species. Most members are less than 2 cm (0.8 in) in length<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-3">[3]</a></sup> but the largest is probably the <a href="/wiki/Giant_isopod" title="Giant isopod">giant isopod</a> (<i>Bathynomus giganteus</i>) which can reach 76 cm (30 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peracarida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mandible.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="614" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Mandible.svg/170px-Mandible.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Mandible.svg/255px-Mandible.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Mandible.svg/340px-Mandible.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mandible.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bauplan" title="Bauplan">bauplan</a> of a peracarid mandibula: 1. Molar process; 2. Spine row; 3. Lacinia mobilis; 4. Incisor process; 5. Palp</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most obvious characteristic of the group is the marsupium in females. This brood pouch is enclosed by the large, flexible oostergites, bristly flaps which extend from the basal segments of the thoracic appendages, which form the floor of a chamber roofed by the animal's <a href="/wiki/Sternum_(arthropod_anatomy)" title="Sternum (arthropod anatomy)">sternum</a>. This chamber is where the eggs are brooded, <a href="/wiki/Marine_larval_ecology#Larval_development_strategies" title="Marine larval ecology">development being direct</a> in most cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-3">[3]</a></sup> Other characteristics include the possession of a single pair of <a href="/wiki/Decapod_anatomy" title="Decapod anatomy">maxillipeds</a> (rarely 2–3), of <a href="/wiki/Mandible_(arthropod_mouthpart)" title="Mandible (arthropod mouthpart)">mandibles</a> with an articulated accessory process between the molar and incisor teeth in the adults (called the <i>lacinia mobilis</i>), and of a <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> which is often reduced in size and is not fused with the posterior thoracic <a href="/wiki/Somite" title="Somite">somites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAFI_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TAFI-5">[5]</a></sup> In some orders, the young hatch at a post-larval, prejuvenile stage called a <i><a href="/wiki/Manca" title="Manca">manca</a></i> which lacks the last pair of legs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAFI_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-TAFI-5">[5]</a></sup> In the underground order <a href="/wiki/Thermosbaenacea" title="Thermosbaenacea">Thermosbaenacea</a>, there are no oostergites and the carapace of the female is expanded to form a dorsal marsupium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orders">Orders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peracarida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is some disagreement as to which orders should be included within Peracarida. Martin &amp; Davies include the following eleven orders:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amphipoda" title="Amphipoda">Amphipoda</a> <small>Latreille, 1816</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cumacea" title="Cumacea">Cumacea</a> <small>Krøyer, 1846</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isopoda" title="Isopoda">Isopoda</a> <small>Latreille, 1817</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lophogastrida" title="Lophogastrida">Lophogastrida</a> <small>G. O. Sars, 1870</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mictacea" title="Mictacea">Mictacea</a> <small>Bowman, Garner, Hessler, Iliffe &amp; Sanders, 1985</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mysida" title="Mysida">Mysida</a> <small>A. H. Haworth, 1825</small></li>
<li>†<a href="/wiki/Pygocephalomorpha" title="Pygocephalomorpha">Pygocephalomorpha</a> <small>Beurlen, 1930</small> (only known from fossil remains)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spelaeogriphacea" title="Spelaeogriphacea">Spelaeogriphacea</a> <small>Gordon, 1957</small></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stygiomysida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stygiomysida (page does not exist)">Stygiomysida</a> <small>Tchindonova, 1981</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tanaidacea" title="Tanaidacea">Tanaidacea</a> <small>Dana, 1849</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thermosbaenacea" title="Thermosbaenacea">Thermosbaenacea</a> <small>Monod, 1927</small></li>
</ul>
<p>but Ruppert, Fox and Barnes exclude Thermosbaenacea and place it in a separate order, Pancarida. They also place Lophogastrida, Mysida and Pygocephalomorpha in a single order, <a href="/wiki/Mysidacea" title="Mysidacea">Mysidacea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-3">[3]</a></sup> This arrangement is disputed by Meland and Willassen who found that <a href="/wiki/Sequencing" title="Sequencing">molecular data</a> shows that the three orders are not closely related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
