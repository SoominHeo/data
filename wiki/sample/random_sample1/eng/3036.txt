<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Word</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about the unit of speech and writing. For the Microsoft Office word processor, see <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">Microsoft Word</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Word_(disambiguation)" title="Word (disambiguation)">Word (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, a <b>word</b> is the smallest element that can be uttered in isolation with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">semantic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatic</a> content (with literal or practical <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(linguistics)" title="Meaning (linguistics)">meaning</a>). This contrasts deeply with a <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morpheme</a>, which is the smallest unit of meaning but will not necessarily stand on its own. A word may consist of a single morpheme (for example: <i>oh!, rock, red, quick, run, expect</i>), or several (<i>rocks, redness, quickly, running, unexpected</i>), whereas a morpheme may not be able to stand on its own as a word (in the words just mentioned, these are <i>-s, -ness, -ly, -ing, un-, -ed</i>). A complex word will typically include a <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">root</a> and one or more <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a> (<i>rock-s, red-ness, quick-ly, run-ning, un-expect-ed</i>), or more than one root in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_word" title="Compound word">compound</a> (<i>black-board, rat-race</i>). Words can be put together to build larger elements of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrases</a> (<i>a red rock</i>), <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clauses</a> (<i>I threw a rock</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentences</a> (<i>He threw a rock too, but he missed</i>).</p>
<p>The term <i>word</i> may refer to a spoken word or to a written word, or sometimes to the abstract concept behind either. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoken_words" title="Spoken words">Spoken words</a> are made up of units of sound called <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a>, and written words of symbols called <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">graphemes</a>, such as the letters of the English alphabet.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions.2FMeanings">Definitions/Meanings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions/Meanings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">Lexeme</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">Lemma (morphology)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ease or difficulty of deciphering a word depends on the language. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dictionaries" title="Dictionaries">Dictionaries</a> categorize a language's <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> (i.e., its vocabulary) into <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">lemmas</a>. These can be taken as an indication of what constitutes a "word" in the opinion of the writers of that language. The most appropriate means of measuring the length of a word is by counting its syllables or morphemes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When a word has multiple definitions or multiple senses, it may result in confusion in a debate or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discussion" title="Discussion">discussion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_definition">Semantic definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Semantic definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bloomfield" title="Leonard Bloomfield">Leonard Bloomfield</a> introduced the concept of "Minimal Free Forms" in 1926. Words are thought of as the smallest meaningful unit of <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a> that can stand by themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This correlates phonemes (units of sound) to <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">lexemes</a> (units of meaning). However, some written words are not minimal free forms as they make no sense by themselves (for example, <i>the</i> and <i>of</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some semanticists have put forward a theory of so-called semantic primitives or <a href="/wiki/Semantic_primes" title="Semantic primes">semantic primes</a>, indefinable words representing fundamental concepts that are intuitively meaningful. According to this theory, semantic primes serve as the basis for describing the meaning, without circularity, of other words and their associated conceptual denotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wierzbicka96_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wierzbicka96-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Minimalist_program" title="Minimalist program">Minimalist</a> school of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_syntax" title="Theoretical syntax">theoretical syntax</a>, words (also called <i>lexical items</i> in the literature) are construed as "bundles" of <a href="/wiki/Feature_(linguistics)" title="Feature (linguistics)">linguistic features</a> that are united into a structure with form and meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For example, the word "koalas" has semantic features (it denotes real-world objects, <a href="/wiki/Koala" title="Koala">koalas</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_category" title="Lexical category">category</a> features (it is a noun), <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a> features (it is plural and must agree with verbs, pronouns, and demonstratives in its domain), <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a> features (it is pronounced a certain way), etc.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_boundaries">Word boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Word boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The task of defining what constitutes a "word" involves determining where one word ends and another word begins—in other words, identifying word boundaries. There are several ways to determine where the word boundaries of spoken language should be placed:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Potential pause</b>: A speaker is told to repeat a given sentence slowly, allowing for pauses. The speaker will tend to insert pauses at the word boundaries. However, this method is not foolproof: the speaker could easily break up polysyllabic words, or fail to separate two or more closely linked words (e.g. "to a" in "He went to a house").</li>
<li><b>Indivisibility</b>: A speaker is told to say a <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentence</a> out loud, and then is told to say the sentence again with extra words added to it. Thus, <i>I have lived in this village for ten years</i> might become <i>My family and I have lived in this little village for about ten or so years</i>. These extra words will tend to be added in the word boundaries of the original sentence. However, some languages have <a href="/wiki/Infix" title="Infix">infixes</a>, which are put inside a word. Similarly, some have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Separable_affix" title="Separable affix">separable affixes</a>; in the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> sentence "Ich <b>komme</b> gut zu Hause <b>an</b>", the verb <i>ankommen</i> is separated.</li>
<li><b>Phonetic boundaries</b>: Some languages have particular rules of <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">pronunciation</a> that make it easy to spot where a word boundary should be. For example, in a language that regularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_stress" title="Lexical stress">stresses</a> the last syllable of a word, a word boundary is likely to fall after each stressed syllable. Another example can be seen in a language that has <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">vowel harmony</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the vowels within a given word share the same <i>quality</i>, so a word boundary is likely to occur whenever the vowel quality changes. Nevertheless, not all languages have such convenient phonetic rules, and even those that do present the occasional exceptions.</li>
<li><b>Orthographic boundaries</b>: See below.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In languages with a <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">literary tradition</a>, there is interrelation between <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> and the question of what is considered a single word. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_separator" title="Word separator">Word separators</a> (typically <a href="/wiki/Space_(punctuation)" title="Space (punctuation)">spaces</a>) are common in modern orthography of languages using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphabetic_script" title="Alphabetic script">alphabetic scripts</a>, but these are (excepting isolated precedents) a relatively modern development (see also <a href="/wiki/History_of_writing" title="History of writing">history of writing</a>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">English orthography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compound expressions</a> may contain spaces. For example, <i>ice cream</i>, <i>air raid shelter</i> and <i>get up</i> each are generally considered to consist of more than one word (as each of the components are free forms, with the possible exception of <i>get</i>).</p>
<p>Not all languages delimit words expressly. <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a> is a very <a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">analytic language</a> (with few inflectional affixes), making it unnecessary to delimit words orthographically. However, there are a great number of multiple-morpheme compounds in Mandarin, as well as a variety of bound morphemes that make it difficult to clearly determine what constitutes a word.</p>
<p>Sometimes, languages which are extremely close grammatically will consider the same order of words in different ways. For example, <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_verb" title="Reflexive verb">reflexive verbs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> infinitive are separate from their respective particle, e.g. <i>se laver</i> ("to wash oneself"), whereas in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> they are hyphenated, e.g. <i>lavar-se</i>, and in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> they are joined, e.g. <i>lavarse</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> uses orthographic cues to delimit words such as switching between <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> (Chinese characters) and the two <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a> syllabaries. This is a fairly soft rule, because <a href="/wiki/Content_word" title="Content word">content words</a> can also be written in <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a> for effect (though if done extensively spaces are typically added to maintain legibility).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a> orthography, although using the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, delimits monosyllabic morphemes rather than words.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">character encoding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Text_segmentation#Word_segmentation" title="Text segmentation">word segmentation</a> depends on which <a href="/wiki/Character_(computing)" title="Character (computing)">characters</a> are defined as word dividers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">Morphology (linguistics)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">Inflection</a></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic languages</a>, a single <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">word stem</a> (for example, <i>love</i>) may have a number of different forms (for example, <i>loves</i>, <i>loving</i>, and <i>loved</i>). However, for some purposes these are not usually considered to be different words, but rather different forms of the same word. In these languages, words may be considered to be constructed from a number of <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> in particular, the morphemes distinguished are</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">root</a>.</li>
<li>Optional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffixes" title="Suffixes">suffixes</a>.</li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desinence" title="Desinence">desinence</a>, or inflectional suffix.</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, the Proto-Indo-European <i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*wr̥dhom</span></i> would be analyzed as consisting of</p>
<ol>
<li><i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*wr̥-</span></i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_grade" title="Zero grade">zero grade</a> of the root <i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*wer-</span></i>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Philosophers have found words objects of fascination since at least the 5th century BC, with the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" title="Philosophy of language">philosophy of language</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> analyzed words in terms of their origins and the sounds making them up, concluding that there was some connection between sound and meaning, though words change a great deal over time. <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> wrote that the use of words "is to be sensible marks of ideas", though they are chosen "not by any natural connexion that there is between particular articulate sounds and certain ideas, for then there would be but one language amongst all men; but by a voluntary imposition, whereby such a word is made arbitrarily the mark of such an idea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wittgenstein" title="Wittgenstein">Wittgenstein</a>'s thought transitioned from a word as representation of meaning to "the meaning of a word is its use in the language."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Archaeology shows that even for centuries prior to this fascination by philosophers in the 5th century BC, many languages had various ways of expressing this verbal unit, which in turn diversified and evolved into a range of expressions with wide philosophical significance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ancient manuscripts of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a> reveal in its 5th chapter the Rabonni Y'shua chastising the pharisees expecting to find life in writings instead of himself. This perhaps could have led to John's introduction in chapter of a description in the Greek translation as "the logos".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A famous early scientist, scholar and priest, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>, influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartesian_philosophy" title="Cartesian philosophy">Cartesian philosophy</a> and mathematics by interpreting such passages consistently with his philosophy of logic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classes">Classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_category" title="Lexical category">Lexical category</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">Grammar</a> classifies a language's lexicon into several groups of words. The basic bipartite division possible for virtually every <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a> is that of <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>.</p>
<p>The classification into such classes is in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Thrax" title="Dionysius Thrax">Dionysius Thrax</a>, who distinguished eight categories: <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronoun</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">preposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverb</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjection</a>.</p>
<p>In Indian grammatical tradition, <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a> introduced a similar fundamental classification into a nominal (nāma, suP) and a verbal (ākhyāta, tiN) class, based on the set of <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">desinences</a> taken by the word.</p>
