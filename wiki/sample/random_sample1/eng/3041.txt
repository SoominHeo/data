<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Leck mich im Arsch</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For the Insane Clown Posse recording, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leck_mich_im_Arsch_(Insane_Clown_Posse_single)" title="Leck mich im Arsch (Insane Clown Posse single)">Leck mich im Arsch (Insane Clown Posse single)</a>.</div>
<p>"<b><span lang="de">Leck mich im Arsch</span></b>" (literally "Lick me in the arse") is a <a href="/wiki/Canon_(music)" title="Canon (music)">canon</a> in <a href="/wiki/B-flat_major" title="B-flat major">B-flat major</a> composed by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6chel_catalogue" title="Köchel catalogue">K.</a> 231 (K. 382c), with lyrics in German. It was one of a set of at least six canons probably written in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1782.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Sung by six voices as a three-part <a href="/wiki/Round_(music)" title="Round (music)">round</a>, it is thought to be a party piece for his friends.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_translation">English translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leck_mich_im_Arsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: English translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A literal translation of the song's title and lyrics into English would be "Lick me in the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/arse" title="wikt:arse">arse</a>". A more <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">idiomatic</a> translation would be the British "Kiss my arse!" or American "Kiss my ass!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_and_modern_discovery">Publication and modern discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leck_mich_im_Arsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Publication and modern discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mozart died in 1791 and his widow, <a href="/wiki/Constanze_Mozart" title="Constanze Mozart">Constanze Mozart</a>, sent the manuscripts of the canons to publishers <a href="/wiki/Breitkopf_%26_H%C3%A4rtel" title="Breitkopf &amp; Härtel">Breitkopf &amp; Härtel</a> in 1799 saying that they would need to be adapted for publication. The publisher changed the title and lyrics of this canon to the more acceptable "<span lang="de">Laßt froh uns sein</span>" ("Let us be glad!"), similar to the traditional German <a href="/wiki/Christmas_carol" title="Christmas carol">Christmas carol</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Lasst_uns_froh_und_munter_sein" title="Lasst uns froh und munter sein">Laßt uns froh und munter sein</a>". Of Mozart's original text, only the first words were documented in the catalogue of his works produced by Breitkopf &amp; Härtel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NMA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NMA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A new text version, which may have been the authentic one, came to light in 1991. Handwritten texts to this and several other similar canons were found added to a printed score of the work in an historical printed edition acquired by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s Music Library. They had evidently been added to the book by a later hand. However, since in six of the pieces these entries matched texts that had, in the meantime, independently come to light in original manuscripts, it was hypothesised that the remaining three may, too, have been original, including texts for K. 231 ("Leck mich im Arsch" itself), and another Mozart work, "<a href="/wiki/Leck_mir_den_Arsch_fein_recht_sch%C3%B6n_sauber" title="Leck mir den Arsch fein recht schön sauber">Leck mir den Arsch fein recht schön sauber</a>" ("Lick my arse nice and clean", K. 233; K. 382d in the revised numbering).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Later research has indicated that the latter composition is probably the work of <a href="/wiki/Wenzel_Trnka" title="Wenzel Trnka">Wenzel Trnka</a> (1739–91).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leck_mich_im_Arsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original incipit attested in the earliest Breitkopf catalogue consisted only of the words "Leck mich im Arsch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NMA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NMA-3">[3]</a></sup> The text rediscovered in 1991 consists only of the repeated phrases:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<table align="right" class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Leck_mich_im_Arsch.mid" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Leck_mich_im_Arsch.mid" title="Leck mich im Arsch.mid">"Leck mich im Arsch"</a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Leck_mich_im_Arsch.mid" title="File:Leck mich im Arsch.mid"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span><br/>
(<a href="/wiki/MIDI" title="MIDI">MIDI</a> file, 9 <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">KB</a>, 2:16)</td>
</tr>
</table>
<div class="poem">
<p><span lang="de">Leck mich im A… g'schwindi, g'schwindi!<br/>
Leck im A... mich g'schwindi.<br/>
Leck mich, leck mich,<br/>
g'schwindi<br/>
etc. etc. etc.</span></p>
</div>
<p>(where 'A...' obviously stands for 'Arsch'; 'g'schwindi' means 'quickly').</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bowdler" title="Thomas Bowdler">bowdlerized</a> text of the early printed editions reads:</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td width="300px">
<div class="poem">
<p><span lang="de">Laßt uns froh sein!<br/>
Murren ist vergebens!<br/>
Knurren, Brummen ist vergebens,<br/>
ist das wahre Kreuz des Lebens,<br/>
das Brummen ist vergebens,<br/>
Knurren, Brummen ist vergebens, vergebens!<br/>
Drum laßt uns froh und fröhlich, froh sein!</span></p>
</div>
</td>
<td>
<div class="poem">
<p>Let us be glad!<br/>
Grumbling is in vain!<br/>
Growling, droning is in vain,<br/>
is the true bane of life,<br/>
Droning is in vain,<br/>
Growling, droning is in vain, in vain!<br/>
Thus let us be cheerful and merry, be glad!</p>
</div>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Another semi-bowdlerized adaptation is found in the recordings of <i>The Complete Mozart</i> edition by <a href="/wiki/Brilliant_Classics" title="Brilliant Classics">Brilliant Classics</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brill_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-brill-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdf_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdf-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<table>
<tr>
<td width="300px">
<div class="poem">
<p><span lang="de">Leck mich im Arsch!<br/>
Goethe, Goethe!<br/>
<a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6tz_von_Berlichingen_(Goethe)" title="Götz von Berlichingen (Goethe)">Götz von Berlichingen</a>! Zweiter Akt;<br/>
Die Szene kennt ihr ja!<br/>
Rufen wir nur ganz summarisch:<br/>
Hier wird Mozart literarisch!</span></p>
</div>
</td>
<td>
<div class="poem">
<p>Kiss my arse!<br/>
Goethe, Goethe!<br/>
Götz von Berlichingen! Second act;<br/>
You know the scene too well!<br/>
Let us now shout the summary:<br/>
Mozart here gets literary!</p>
</div>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>This is a clear allusion to the line "<span lang="de">... er kann mich im Arsche lecken!</span>" attributed to the late medieval German knight <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6tz_von_Berlichingen_(Goethe)#Famous_quote" title="Götz von Berlichingen (Goethe)">Götz von Berlichingen</a>, known best as the title hero of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a>'s <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6tz_von_Berlichingen_(Goethe)" title="Götz von Berlichingen (Goethe)">drama</a>.</p>
<p>This version contains a slight error about the Goethe source: the line occurs in the third act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gotz1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gotz1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gotz2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gotz2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
