<p><i><b>Where Is the Friend's Home?</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">خانه دوست کجاست</span>‎‎, <i>Khane-ye doust kodjast</i>) is a 1987 Iranian film directed and written by Iranian director <a href="/wiki/Abbas_Kiarostami" title="Abbas Kiarostami">Abbas Kiarostami</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKmetalasylum_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AKmetalasylum-1">[1]</a></sup> The title of the film was derived from a poem by <a href="/wiki/Sohrab_Sepehri" title="Sohrab Sepehri">Sohrab Sepehri</a>. It is considered the first film in Kiarostami's <a href="/wiki/Koker_trilogy" title="Koker trilogy">Koker trilogy</a>, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Life,_and_Nothing_More..." title="Life, and Nothing More...">Life, and Nothing More...</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Olive_Trees" title="Through the Olive Trees">Through the Olive Trees</a></i>, all of which take place in <a href="/wiki/Koker,_Iran" title="Koker, Iran">Koker, Iran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film tells a deceptively simple account of a conscientious schoolboy's quest to return his friend's notebook in a neighboring village, since, should his friend fail to hand it in the next day, it is likely he will get expelled. Hence this film has been seen as a metaphor for the sense of civil duty, about loyalty and everyday heroics. The traditional beliefs of Iranian rural people are also shown in many parts of the movie.</p>
<p>The film is among the top ten in the <a href="/wiki/BFI_list_of_the_50_films_you_should_see_by_the_age_of_14" title="BFI list of the 50 films you should see by the age of 14">BFI list of the 50 films you should see by the age of 14</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_Is_the_Friend%27s_Home%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As he prepares to do his homework, Ahmed realizes that he accidentally brought home a notebook belonging to one of his classmates. Knowing that his friend may be expelled if he does not have the notebook in order to complete his homework, Ahmed goes looking for his classmate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_Is_the_Friend%27s_Home%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It won the Bronze Leopard at the 1989 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locarno_Film_Festival" title="Locarno Film Festival">Locarno Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tiff_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tiff-3">[3]</a></sup> It also won the Golden Plate at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fajr_Film_Festival" title="Fajr Film Festival">Fajr Film Festival</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_Is_the_Friend%27s_Home%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Where Is The Friend's Home?</i> was Kiarostami's first film to gain major international attention. The film's title has sometimes been translated as <i>Where Is My Friend's House?</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iranian filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Bahman_Ghobadi" title="Bahman Ghobadi">Bahman Ghobadi</a> said that "I always have this film in mind because of the director's profound perspective on filmmaking and its strange and distinct structure."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tiff_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tiff-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Rosenbaum" title="Jonathan Rosenbaum">Jonathan Rosenbaum</a> called Kiarostami the greatest living filmmaker and called the film (along with <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Olive_Trees" title="Through the Olive Trees">Through the Olive Trees</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_and_Nothing_More" title="Life and Nothing More">Life and Nothing More</a></i>) "sustained meditations on singular landscapes and the way ordinary people live in them; obsessional quests that take on the contours of parables; concentrated inquiries that raise more questions than they answer; and comic as well as cosmic poems about dealing with personal and impersonal disaster. They're about making discoveries and cherishing what's in the world--including things that we can't understand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
