<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Narragansett Bay</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Narragansett Bay</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> on the north side of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_Sound" title="Rhode Island Sound">Rhode Island Sound</a> covering 147 mi<sup>2</sup> (380 km<sup>2</sup>), 120.5 mi<sup>2</sup> (312 km<sup>2</sup>) in <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Bay forms New England's largest estuary, which functions as an expansive natural harbor and includes a small <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Small parts of it extend into <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.</p>
<p>There are more than 30 islands in the Bay; the three largest ones are <a href="/wiki/Aquidneck_Island" title="Aquidneck Island">Aquidneck Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conanicut_Island" title="Conanicut Island">Conanicut Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prudence_Island" title="Prudence Island">Prudence Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Bodies of water that are part of Narragansett Bay include the <a href="/wiki/Sakonnet_River" title="Sakonnet River">Sakonnet River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hope_Bay" title="Mount Hope Bay">Mount Hope Bay</a>, and the southern, tidal part of the <a href="/wiki/Taunton_River" title="Taunton River">Taunton River</a>. The bay opens on <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_Sound" title="Rhode Island Sound">Rhode Island Sound</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>; <a href="/wiki/Block_Island" title="Block Island">Block Island</a> lies less than 20 miles (32 km) southwest of its opening. Narragansett Bay can be seen in NOAA chart <a class="external text" href="http://www.charts.noaa.gov/OnLineViewer/13221.shtml" rel="nofollow">13221</a></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Populations">Populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> state capital and largest city, sits on the west side of the head of the northernmost arm of the bay. Many of Providence's suburbs, including <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cranston,_Rhode_Island" title="Cranston, Rhode Island">Cranston</a>, are also on the bay. <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport</a>, the home of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_War_College" title="United States Naval War College">United States Naval War College</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Undersea_Warfare_Center" title="Naval Undersea Warfare Center">Naval Undersea Warfare Center</a>, and a major <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> training center, is located at the south end of <a href="/wiki/Aquidneck_Island" title="Aquidneck Island">Aquidneck Island</a>, on the ocean. The city of <a href="/wiki/Fall_River,_Massachusetts" title="Fall River, Massachusetts">Fall River, Massachusetts</a> is located at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Taunton_River" title="Taunton River">Taunton River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hope_Bay" title="Mount Hope Bay">Mount Hope Bay</a>, which form the northeasternmost part of Narragansett Bay. The southwest side of the bay include the seaside tourist towns of <a href="/wiki/Narragansett,_Rhode_Island" title="Narragansett, Rhode Island">Narragansett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wickford,_Rhode_Island" title="Wickford, Rhode Island">Wickford</a>. <a href="/wiki/Quonset_Point" title="Quonset Point">Quonset Point</a>, south of Warwick, gives its name to the <a href="/wiki/Quonset_hut" title="Quonset hut">Quonset hut</a>. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Williams_University" title="Roger Williams University">Roger Williams University</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Bristol,_Rhode_Island" title="Bristol, Rhode Island">Bristol, Rhode Island</a> on land overlooking the Bay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology.2C_geography_and_oceanography">Geology, geography and oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology, geography and oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Narragansett Bay comprises an area of about 4836 square kilometers. The watershed has seven river <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">sub-drainage basins</a>; Warren/Barrington, Woonasquatucket river, Ten Mile, Taunton, Moshassuck, Pawtuxet, and Blackstone that provide freshwater input at an average rate of 51 cubic meters per second. The Blackstone and Taunton provide the highest volume of mean, annual freshwater flow into the watershed. River water inflow has a seasonal variability, with the highest flow in the spring and the minimum flow in early fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bay is a <a href="/wiki/Estuary#Drowned_river_valleys" title="Estuary">drowned river valley</a> estuary which is composed of three ancient river valleys; the <a href="/wiki/Sakonnet_River" title="Sakonnet River">Sakonnet River</a>, the East Passage and the West Passage. The <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetry</a> varies greatly between the three passages, with the average depths of the East, West and Sakonnet River passages being 37 m, 10 m and 7.5 m respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbnerr.org_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbnerr.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacial_origins">Glacial origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Glacial origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Narragansett Bay is a <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">ria</a> that consists of a series of flooded river valleys formed of dropped crustal blocks in a <a href="/wiki/Horst_(geology)" title="Horst (geology)">horst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">graben</a> system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> that is slowly subsiding between a still-shifting fault system;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> however, the estuary system is vast compared to the present flow of the four small rivers that enter the bay, in the northeast, the <a href="/wiki/Taunton_River" title="Taunton River">Taunton River</a> and in the northwest, the <a href="/wiki/Providence_River" title="Providence River">Providence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seekonk_River" title="Seekonk River">Seekonk Rivers</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Pawtuxet_River" title="Pawtuxet River">Pawtuxet River</a> from the west. The present shape of Narragansett Bay is instead the result of the most recent glaciation of New England, under the edges of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurentide_ice_sheet" title="Laurentide ice sheet">Laurentide ice sheet</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, about 18,000 B.P.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">Sea level</a> was lowered so much that the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> was exposed, under its weight of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_calving" title="Ice calving">calved</a> into the Atlantic at its foredge south of Block Island. Glaciers flowing through a geologically old sedimentary basin carved channels through the younger sediments and exposed much older bedrock. North-to-south cuts gouged by the ice can be seen clearly on the map: they form the West Passage that separates Conanicut Island from the western mainland and the East Passage that now separates Conanicut Island from Aquidneck Island.</p>
<p>As the ice stalled, then retreated, the region became ice-free by about 14,000 B.P.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A complicated sequence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_ingression" title="Marine ingression">marine ingression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">rebound</a> flooded and emptied the landscape. A fresh water <a href="/wiki/Proglacial_lake" title="Proglacial lake">proglacial lake</a> called by geologists <b>Lake Narragansett</b> formed about 15,500 B.P.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> impounded behind <a href="/wiki/Terminal_moraine" title="Terminal moraine">terminal moraines</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the lake lasted about 500 years, leaving the powerful flow of a post-glacial river running down its north-south axis. Then salt water filled the valley, as rising sea levels permanently flooded the area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_patterns">Tidal patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tidal patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tides in the watershed are measured at 6 stations; Providence, Fall River, Quonset Point, Conimicut Light, Prudence Island, and Newport. Tides are measured with pressure-sensing tide gauges. In shallow water, sound waves are used to measure water height by precisely measuring the travel time of the waves. In deeper water, tides are measured with pressure-sensing tide gauges that are placed on the ocean floor to measure water height.</p>

<p>The bay's <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a> are semi-diurnal, meaning that the region experiences two high and low tides that occur daily. The tides range in height from 1.1 m at the bay's mouth, and 1.4 m at its head. The differences in water depth between high and low tide averages to about 4 feet (1.2 m). The lunar, semi-diurnal M2 tide occurs at a period of 12.42 h, with two tides occurring in the watershed every 24 h and 50 min. The watershed's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semidiurnal_tides#Range_variation:_springs_and_neaps" title="Semidiurnal tides">neap and spring tides</a> occur every 14.8 days. In Narragansett bay, the tides show a distinct double-peak flood during high tide and single peak ebb during low tide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_circulation_patterns">Water circulation patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water circulation patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The movement of water within a system is its <a href="/wiki/Circulation_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Circulation (fluid dynamics)">circulation</a>. Estuaries are given a classification depending on the pattern of their circulations. The <a href="/wiki/Estuary#Fjord-type" title="Estuary">circulation classification</a> can be well-mixed, partially mixed, salt wedge, or Fjord-type. For Narragansett, the circulation is mostly well-mixed, however, the Providence River does show some vertical stratification.</p>
<p>Narragansett Bay circulation is made up of forces provided by the winds, tides and changes in water <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> within the watershed. Within estuaries such Narragansett, its circulation is the result of the flow of fresh water at the head interacting with salt water at the point where the bay meets the open ocean. Residence time of water due to the circulation of Narragansett is between 10–40 days with an average of 26 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbnerr.org_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbnerr.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tidal mixing is the dominant driver of circulation patterns in the bay, where currents can reach up to 77 cm/s on average. Non-tidal currents such as the flow of low salinity water at the surface out of the bay and high salinity deep water into the bay contribute a current of about 10 cm/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbnerr.org_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-nbnerr.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Winds also drive circulation patterns in the bay. In the winter, winds come from the northwest, helping move winds towards the bay's mouth and in the summer, they are out of the southwest and move water towards the head of the bay. Wind-driven waves of over 1.3 meters also help mix surface waters.</p>
<p>Density driven forces are the third factor affecting circulation. Fresh water inflow comes from natural sources such as atmospheric precipitation and inflow from the many rivers that feed into the watershed and man-made sources such as water treatment plants. Fresh and saltwater mixing results in a <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> range in the bay of 24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_thousand" title="Parts per thousand">ppt</a> in the upper Providence River area to 32 ppt at the mouth of the bay.</p>
<p>The bay's currents and circulation patterns greatly influence the sediment deposits within the region. Although the majority of the sediments within the bay are fine grain material such as detritus, clay-silt and sand-silt-clay, Scientists have been able to identify 11 types of sediment that range from course gravels to fine silts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbnerr.org_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-nbnerr.org-5">[5]</a></sup> The bay's currents deposit fine materials through the harbors of the lower and middle sections of the bay, and the course, heavy materials are deposited in the lower areas of the bay, where the water velocities are higher.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Rhode_Island" title="History of Rhode Island">History of Rhode Island</a></div>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">visit by Europeans</a> to the bay was probably in the early 16th century. At the time, the area around the bay was inhabited by two different and distinct groups of natives: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narragansett_(tribe)" title="Narragansett (tribe)">Narragansetts</a> occupied the west side of the bay, and the <a href="/wiki/Wampanoag_people" title="Wampanoag people">Wampanoag</a> lived on the east side, occupying the land east to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>.</p>
<p>It is accepted by most historians that first contact by Europeans was made by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">Giovanni da Verrazzano</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorer</a> who entered the bay in his ship <i><a href="/wiki/La_Dauphine" title="La Dauphine">La Dauphine</a></i> in 1524 after visiting <a href="/wiki/New_York_Bay" title="New York Bay">New York Bay</a>. Verrazzano called the bay <i>Refugio</i>, the "Refuge". The bay has several entrances, however, and the exact route of his voyage and the location where he laid anchor is still a subject of dispute among historians, leading to a corresponding uncertainty over which tribe made contact with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Verrazzano reported that he found clearings and open forests suitable for travel "even by a large army," a far cry from the impenetrable tangle that resulted when the English suppressed <a href="/wiki/Controlled_burn" title="Controlled burn">controlled burns</a> in the seventeenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, in 1614, the bay was explored and mapped by the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> navigator <a href="/wiki/Adriaen_Block" title="Adriaen Block">Adriaen Block</a>, after whom nearby <a href="/wiki/Block_Island" title="Block Island">Block Island</a> is named. The first recorded European settlement was in the 1630s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Williams_(theologian)" title="Roger Williams (theologian)">Roger Williams</a>, a dissatisfied member of the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a>, moved into the area around the year 1636. He made contact with the Narragansett <i><a href="/wiki/Sachem" title="Sachem">sachem</a></i> called <a href="/wiki/Canonicus" title="Canonicus">Canonicus</a> by the Europeans, and set up a trading post on the site of <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence</a>. At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> had established a trading post approximately 12 miles (20 km) to the southwest which was under the authority of <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_Bay" title="New York Bay">New York Bay</a>.</p>
<p>In 1643, Williams traveled to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and was granted a charter for the new colony of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>. He also wrote a <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionary</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohegan-Montauk-Narragansett_language" title="Mohegan-Montauk-Narragansett language">Narragansett language</a>, <i>Keys to the Indian Language</i>, which was published in England that same year.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gaspee_Affair" title="Gaspee Affair">Gaspee Affair</a>, an important naval event of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, occurred in 1772 in the bay; it involved the capture of the <i>Gaspee</i>, a British ship. The American victory contributed to the eventual start of the war at the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a> in Massachusetts three years later. The event is celebrated in <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick</a> as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gaspee_Days_Celebration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gaspee Days Celebration (page does not exist)">Gaspee Days Celebration</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> in June, which event includes a symbolic recreation of the burning of the ship.</p>
<p>Captain <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a>'s <a href="/wiki/HMS_Endeavour" title="HMS Endeavour">HMS Endeavour</a> is believed to have been scuttled in the bay, as part of a blockade by the British, whose occupation of Newport was threatened by a fleet carrying French soldiers in support of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>.</p>
<p>Roger Williams and other early colonists named the islands, it is incorrectly reported that they were named after Roger William's daughters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The islands are named Prudence, Patience, Hope, Despair, and Hog. To remember the names, colonial school children often recited the poem: "Patience, Prudence, Hope and Despair. And the little Hog over there."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_properties_and_phytoplankton_distribution">Water properties and phytoplankton distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Water properties and phytoplankton distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Narragansett Bay is divided into two passages by Conanicut Island-East and West passage. Its average depth is 9 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs.harvard.edu_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-19">[19]</a></sup> Surface temperatures vary seasonally from about -1 to +25 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HICKSSD_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-HICKSSD-20">[20]</a></sup> Surface salinity remained between 24.5 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">ppt</a> and 31 ppt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In most open waters of the Bay, temperature and salinity differences between surface and bottom waters are less than 3.5 °C and 1 ppt in summer and less than 2.5 °C and 2.5 ppt in winter and spring. There is relatively high salinity water coming from the sea at the bottom and there is a seaward movement of fresher water in the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HICKSSD_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-HICKSSD-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most salient feature of the phytoplankton of Narragansett Bay is the high mean level of abundance as compared with New England coastal waters, with main species being diatoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HICKSSD_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-HICKSSD-20">[20]</a></sup> The whole phytoplankton group has a more pronounced seasonal and annual fluctuation and it is largely due to changes in the concentration of diatoms, principally <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeletonema_costatum" title="fr:Skeletonema costatum">skeletonema costatum</a>. A statistical change point test identified two different abundance regimes characterized by a ca. 45% decline in Skeletonema abundance during January 1959 to August 1980 compared to August 1980 to May 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs.harvard.edu_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mystery_wreck">Mystery wreck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narragansett_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mystery wreck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During a routine survey of Narragansett Bay in 2004, the NOAA ship, The Rude, discovered a previously unknown wreck. The ship is a steel hulled and its bow rises to 36' below the surface, and appears to have sunk sometime after 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The shipping lane routinely used by ships in the bay had to be rerouted safely away from the wreck and the Coast Guard has since marked the wreck with a lighted buoy. Divers to the wreck speculate that it was either a Navy ammunition ferry, a privately owned ferry, or some vessel involved in a collision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
