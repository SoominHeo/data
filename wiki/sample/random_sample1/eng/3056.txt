Scalar Product Spaces, over any field, have "scalar products" that are symmetrical and linear in the first(by convention) argument. Hermitian Product Spaces are restricted to the field of complex numbers and have "hermitian products" that are conjugate-symmetrical and linear in the first argument(by convention). Inner Product Spaces may be defined over any field, having "inner products" that are linear in the first(by convention) argument, conjugate-symmetrical, and positive-definite. Unlike Inner Products, Scalar Products and Hermitian Products need not be positive-definite.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>, an <b>inner product space</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vector space</a> with an additional <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_structure" title="Mathematical structure">structure</a> called an <b>inner product</b>. This additional structure associates each pair of vectors in the space with a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics)" title="Scalar (mathematics)">scalar</a> quantity known as the inner product of the vectors. Inner products allow the rigorous introduction of intuitive geometrical notions such as the length of a vector or the <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angle</a> between two vectors. They also provide the means of defining <a href="/wiki/Orthogonality" title="Orthogonality">orthogonality</a> between vectors (zero inner product). Inner product spaces generalize <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean spaces</a> (in which the inner product is the <a href="/wiki/Dot_product" title="Dot product">dot product</a>, also known as the scalar product) to vector spaces of any (possibly infinite) <a href="/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)" title="Dimension (vector space)">dimension</a>, and are studied in <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>.</p>
<p>An inner product naturally induces an associated <a href="/wiki/Norm_(mathematics)" title="Norm (mathematics)">norm</a>, thus an inner product space is also a <a href="/wiki/Normed_vector_space" title="Normed vector space">normed vector space</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complete_space" title="Complete space">complete space</a> with an inner product is called a <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert space</a>. An (incomplete) space with an inner product is called a <b>pre-Hilbert space</b>, since its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complete_space#Completion" title="Complete space">completion</a> with respect to the norm induced by the inner product is a <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert space</a>. Inner product spaces over the field of complex numbers are sometimes referred to as <b>unitary spaces.</b></p>

<p>There are various technical reasons why it is necessary to restrict the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basefield" title="Basefield">basefield</a> to <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b></span> and <span class="texhtml"><b>C</b></span> in the definition. Briefly, the basefield has to contain an <a href="/wiki/Ordered_field" title="Ordered field">ordered subfield</a> in order for non-negativity to make sense,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> and therefore has to have <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra)" title="Characteristic (algebra)">characteristic</a> equal to 0 (since any ordered field has to have such characteristic). This immediately excludes finite fields. The basefield has to have additional structure, such as a distinguished automorphism. More generally any quadratically closed subfield of <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b></span> or <span class="texhtml"><b>C</b></span> will suffice for this purpose, e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number" title="Algebraic number">algebraic numbers</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Constructible_number#Transformation_into_algebra" title="Constructible number">constructible numbers</a>. However in these cases when it is a proper subfield (i.e., neither <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b></span> nor <span class="texhtml"><b>C</b></span>) even finite-dimensional inner product spaces will fail to be metrically complete. In contrast all finite-dimensional inner product spaces over <span class="texhtml"><b>R</b></span> or <span class="texhtml"><b>C</b></span>, such as those used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computation" title="Quantum computation">quantum computation</a>, are automatically <a href="/wiki/Complete_metric_space" title="Complete metric space">metrically complete</a> and hence <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert spaces</a>.</p>

<p><b>Theorem.</b> Any <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">complete inner product space</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i></span> has an orthonormal basis.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operators_on_inner_product_spaces">Operators on inner product spaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operators on inner product spaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

A generalization of the spectral theorem holds for continuous normal operators in Hilbert spaces.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any of the axioms of an inner product may be weakened, yielding generalized notions. The generalizations that are closest to inner products occur where bilinearity and conjugate symmetry are retained, but positive-definiteness is weakened.</p>

<p>Alternatively, one may require that the pairing be a <a href="/wiki/Nondegenerate_form" title="Nondegenerate form">nondegenerate form</a>, meaning that for all non-zero <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span> there exists some <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span> such that <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle \neq 0,}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\langle x,y\rangle \neq 0," aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70cad7232a67fb3c44d692c1614cfe977e991705" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:10.32ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span> though <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span> need not equal <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span>; in other words, the induced map to the dual space <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i> → <i>V</i><sup>*</sup></span> is injective. This generalization is important in <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>: a manifold whose tangent spaces have an inner product is a <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_manifold" title="Riemannian manifold">Riemannian manifold</a>, while if this is related to nondegenerate conjugate symmetric form the manifold is a <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold" title="Pseudo-Riemannian manifold">pseudo-Riemannian manifold</a>. By <a href="/wiki/Sylvester%27s_law_of_inertia" title="Sylvester's law of inertia">Sylvester's law of inertia</a>, just as every inner product is similar to the dot product with positive weights on a set of vectors, every nondegenerate conjugate symmetric form is similar to the dot product with <i>nonzero</i> weights on a set of vectors, and the number of positive and negative weights are called respectively the positive index and negative index. Product of vectors in <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">Minkowski space</a> is an example of indefinite inner product, although, technically speaking, it is not an inner product according to the standard definition above. Minkowski space has four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimension_(mathematics)" title="Dimension (mathematics)">dimensions</a> and indices 3 and 1 (assignment of <a href="/wiki/Sign_(mathematics)" title="Sign (mathematics)">"+" and "−"</a> to them <a href="/wiki/Sign_convention#Metric_signature" title="Sign convention">differs depending on conventions</a>).</p>
<p>Purely algebraic statements (ones that do not use positivity) usually only rely on the nondegeneracy (the injective homomorphism <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i> → <i>V</i><sup>*</sup></span>) and thus hold more generally.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_products">Related products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Related products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "inner product" is opposed to <a href="/wiki/Outer_product" title="Outer product">outer product</a>, which is a slightly more general opposite. Simply, in coordinates, the inner product is the product of a 1×<i>n</i> <i>co</i>vector with an <i>n</i>×1 vector, yielding a 1×1 matrix (a scalar), while the outer product is the product of an <i>m</i>×1 vector with a 1×<i>n</i> covector, yielding an <i>m</i>×<i>n</i> matrix. Note that the outer product is defined for different dimensions, while the inner product requires the same dimension. If the dimensions are the same, then the inner product is the <i>trace</i> of the outer product (trace only being properly defined for square matrices).</p>
<p>On an inner product space, or more generally a vector space with a <a href="/wiki/Nondegenerate_form" title="Nondegenerate form">nondegenerate form</a> (so an isomorphism <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i> → <i>V</i><sup>*</sup></span>) vectors can be sent to covectors (in coordinates, via transpose), so one can take the inner product and outer product of two vectors, not simply of a vector and a covector.</p>
<p>In a quip: "inner is horizontal times vertical and shrinks down, outer is vertical times horizontal and expands out".</p>

<p>The inner product and outer product should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Interior_product" title="Interior product">interior product</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exterior_product" title="Exterior product">exterior product</a>, which are instead operations on <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">vector fields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Differential_form" title="Differential form">differential forms</a>, or more generally on the <a href="/wiki/Exterior_algebra" title="Exterior algebra">exterior algebra</a>.</p>
<p>As a further complication, in <a href="/wiki/Geometric_algebra" title="Geometric algebra">geometric algebra</a> the inner product and the <i>exterior</i> (Grassmann) product are combined in the geometric product (the Clifford product in a <a href="/wiki/Clifford_algebra" title="Clifford algebra">Clifford algebra</a>) – the inner product sends two vectors (1-vectors) to a scalar (a 0-vector), while the exterior product sends two vectors to a bivector (2-vector) – and in this context the exterior product is usually called the "<i>outer</i> (alternatively, wedge) product". The inner product is more correctly called a <i>scalar</i> product in this context, as the nondegenerate quadratic form in question need not be positive definite (need not be an inner product).</p>
