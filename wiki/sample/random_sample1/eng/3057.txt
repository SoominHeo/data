<p>The <b>National Police Agency of the Ministry of the Interior</b> (<b>NPA</b>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">內政部警政署</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Nèizhèngbù Jǐngzhèngshǔ</span></i>) is an agency under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Interior_(Republic_of_China)" title="Ministry of the Interior (Republic of China)">Ministry of the Interior</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> (ROC) which oversees all ROC police forces on a national level. The National Police Agency is headquartered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taipei_City" title="Taipei City">Taipei City</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Police_Organization_.26_Structure">Police Organization &amp; Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Police Organization &amp; Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hierarchy">Hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most actual law enforcement and day-to-day policing duties are delegated to local police departments on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="Administrative divisions of the Republic of China">city and county</a> level which answer to the NPA but are considered agencies of their local government. However the NPA has direct control over several specialized units which may be deployed to assist local forces, as well as the national <a href="/wiki/Highway_patrol" title="Highway patrol">highway patrol</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_the_United_States" title="Law enforcement in the United States">the police system in USA</a>, the central government appoints the head positions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="Administrative divisions of the Republic of China">city and county</a> Police Departments in the ROC and thus forms a solid chain of command for all police personnel. By calling a personnel review board, the Director-General of NPA has the full control of personnel rotation and transfer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-personnel_issue_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-personnel_issue-1">[1]</a></sup> as well as administrative commendations and reprimands over all high ranking police officers, including chiefs of local police departments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>One exception is the President of the Central Police University, who is directly subordinate to the Minister of the Interior, and is not subject to the NPA personnel review board.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_Police_Corps">Special Police Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Special Police Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are 7 individual Special Police Corps, or "Peace Preservation Police Corps"(保安警察隊). These are mobile, rapid deployment police forces. They also execute various sentry duties and serve as a pool of reserve formations for police manpower.</p>
<p>Three task-force-grouped SWAT units (referred to as <b>Peace Enforcing Special Service Forces</b> or "Wei-An" Forces(<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%B6%AD%E5%AE%89%E7%89%B9%E5%8B%A4%E9%9A%8A" title="zh:維安特勤隊">zh:維安特勤隊</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> comprise the 1st, 4th and 5th Special Police Corps. The mission of the "Wei-An" Forces include <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a>, counter-hijacking, high-profile hostage rescue, and presidential protection. In recent years they have also been involved in the arrest of heavily armed fugitives. The uniforms and equipment of the Wei-An units are similar to local SWAT units, which are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Squad" title="Thunder Squad">Thunder Squads</a>. The Thunder Squads are subordinate to local police departments. However, the "Wei-An" Forces are the only police units equipped with <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_Gage_Commando" title="Cadillac Gage Commando">V-150S</a> armor vehicles and M998 <a href="/wiki/Humvee" title="Humvee">Humvee</a>, which were transferred from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Army" title="ROC Army">ROC Army</a> after the 2004 shoot out with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Xi-Ming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhang Xi-Ming (page does not exist)">Zhang Xi-Ming</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%BC%B5%E9%8C%AB%E9%8A%98" title="zh:張錫銘">zh:張錫銘</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_Xi_Ming_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang_Xi_Ming-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_First_Corps">The First Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The First Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>A training base for courses of on-the-job training and mobile task force. <a href="/wiki/Riot_control" title="Riot control">Riot control</a>, <a href="/wiki/SWAT" title="SWAT">Special Weapons and Tactics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a> in northern Taiwan. It commands six special police brigades and one "Wei-an" Forces(<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%B6%AD%E5%AE%89%E7%89%B9%E5%8B%A4%E9%9A%8A" title="zh:維安特勤隊">zh:維安特勤隊</a>). Three brigades of conscripts, 2nd, 5th and 6th, served like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_Korea)#Combat_police" title="National Police Agency (Republic of Korea)">combat police in Korea</a>, are usually deployed for <a href="/wiki/Riot_control" title="Riot control">Riot control</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>Corps Headquarters, 2nd, 3rd, 4th brigades, and one "Wei-An" Forces, are stationed in Si-pai, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taipei_City" title="Taipei City">Taipei City</a>.
<ul>
<li>"Wei-an" Forces: Formed in June 1992. Its mission specialties are <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a>, high-profile hostage rescues, and presidential protection. These forces are composed of SWAT units divided into two companies, which are supported by the 3rd brigade.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>1st and 5th brigades are stationed in <a href="/wiki/Sanxia_District" title="Sanxia District">Sanxia District</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Taipei_City" title="New Taipei City">New Taipei City</a>.</li>
<li>The 6th brigade is stationed in <a href="/wiki/Bade_District" title="Bade District">Bade District</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoyuan_City" title="Taoyuan City">Taoyuan City</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Second_Corps">The Second Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Second Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>There are 4 brigades commended by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Economic_Affairs_(Taiwan)" title="Ministry of Economic Affairs (Taiwan)">Ministry of Economic Affairs</a>. It is for security duty of all governmental business units and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Power_Company" title="Taiwan Power Company">Taiwan Power Company</a> under the Ministry of Economic Affairs and all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hsinchu_Science_Park" title="Hsinchu Science Park">Science Parks</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Science_Council" title="National Science Council">National Science Council</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>The first brigade is especially tasked for the <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> protection and the enforcement of counterfeit and infringement in related to intellectual property. It is usually code-named as <b>Intellectual Property Protection Brigade</b>.</li>
<li>The 2nd is to guard three nuclear power plants (Nuclear No.1, No.2, and No.4) of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Power_Company" title="Taiwan Power Company">Taiwan Power Company</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Taipei_City" title="New Taipei City">New Taipei City</a>.</li>
<li>The 3rd is in northern Taiwan for the security duty of two Science Parks in <a href="/wiki/Hsinchu" title="Hsinchu">Hsinchu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taichung" title="Taichung">Taichung</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Science_Council" title="National Science Council">National Science Council</a>, and the other industrial parks, import-export districts, and certain government installations of the Ministry of Economic Affairs.</li>
<li>The 4th is in southern Taiwan for the security duty of all governmental business installations under the National Science Council and the Ministry of Economic Affairs, which includes one nuclear power plant: "Nuclear No.3."</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Third_Corps">The Third Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Third Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>It is a part of border police and provides supportive and supplemental duty of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Maritime_Customs_Service" title="Chinese Maritime Customs Service">Customs Services</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(Republic_of_China)" title="Ministry of Finance (Republic of China)">Ministry of Finance</a>.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Fourth_Corps">The Fourth Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Fourth Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Riot_control" title="Riot control">Riot control</a>, <a href="/wiki/SWAT" title="SWAT">Special Weapons and Tactics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a> in central Taiwan.</p>
<ul>
<li>"Wei-an" Forces: In March 2003, two more task force-sized SWAT groups of "Wei-an" Forces were formed. One of them is attached to the 4th Special Police Corps in Taichung, which is located in central Taiwan.</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Fifth_Corps">The Fifth Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Fifth Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Riot_control" title="Riot control">Riot control</a>, <a href="/wiki/SWAT" title="SWAT">Special Weapons and Tactics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a> in southern Taiwan.</p>
<ul>
<li>"Wei-an" Forces: On March 2003, two task-force SWAT groups were formed. One is attached to the 5th Special Police Corps in southern Taiwan.</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sixth_Corps">The Sixth Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Sixth Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Responsible for the physical security of central government buildings, high-ranking civilian officials, foreign embassies, and liaison institutes.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan_Special_Police_Corps">Taiwan Special Police Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Taiwan Special Police Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>There are 6 Special Police Brigades inherited from the late Taiwan Provincial Police Administration.</p>
<ul>
<li>The first 3 brigades are for the security duty of 3 governmental financial banks: <a href="/wiki/Land_Bank_of_Taiwan" title="Land Bank of Taiwan">Taiwan Land Bank</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooperative_Bank" title="Cooperative Bank">Taiwan Cooperative Bank</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Taiwan" title="Bank of Taiwan">Bank of Taiwan</a>.</li>
<li>The 4th is to guard the government installations related to the late Taiwan Province.</li>
<li>The 5th and 6th are attached to Water Resources Agency (水利署), <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Economic_Affairs_(Taiwan)" title="Ministry of Economic Affairs (Taiwan)">Ministry of Economic Affairs</a> to protect the water supply and water-and-land-related environmental cases.</li>
</ul>

<li><b>Police Telecommunications Office</b> (警察電訊所)</li>
<li><b>Civil Defense Headquarters</b> (民防防情指揮管制所)</li>
<li><b><a class="external text" href="http://www.prs.gov.tw/" rel="nofollow">Police Radio Station</a></b> (警察廣播電臺): A government public radio station run by civilians. It broadcasts regular radio show programs, news, and real-time traffic situations.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Task-Force_Formations_under_NPA">Task-Force Formations under NPA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Task-Force Formations under NPA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>By separate decrees of The <a href="/wiki/Executive_Yuan" title="Executive Yuan">Executive Yuan</a>, three task-force formations, each consisted of roughly a couple of hundreds police officers with different specialty, are formed under the National Police Agency but attached to two cabinet-level institutions or other units.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Telecommunication Police Corps</b> (電信警察隊): In January 1998, a task force called the "<b>Telecommunication Police Corps</b>" was formed and attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Transportation_and_Communications_(Republic_of_China)" title="Ministry of Transportation and Communications (Republic of China)">Ministry of Transportation and Communications</a>. Since 2 February 2006, the Telecommunication Police Corps are re-attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Communication_Commission" title="National Communication Commission">National Communication Commission</a>. Its job is to enforce laws concerning the radio spectrum and telecommunications.</li>
<li><b>Environmental Protection Police Corps</b> (環保警察隊): It was formed in 1999 and is attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Administration_(Republic_of_China)" title="Environmental Protection Administration (Republic of China)">Environmental Protection Administration</a>. There are currently 192 policemen serving as Environmental Protection Police and assigned to three brigades at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taipei_City" title="Taipei City">Taipei City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taichung_City" title="Taichung City">Taichung City</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fongshan_City" title="Fongshan City">Fongshan City</a> in Northern, Central and Southern Taiwan, respectively.</li>
<li><b>Forest &amp; Nature Conservation Police Unit</b> (森林暨自然保育警察隊): This was formed with 178 policemen on July 1, 2004 by the decree of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Yuan" title="Executive Yuan">Executive Yuan</a> on August 6, 2003, after ten forest arson cases in the mountain area of <a href="/wiki/Taichung_County" title="Taichung County">Taichung County</a> (now part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taichung_City" title="Taichung City">Taichung City</a>) within six years. It is attached to the Forest Bureau of the Council of Agriculture. Besides its headquarters in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taipei_City" title="Taipei City">Taipei City</a>, there are 8 squadrons in Taiwan mountain area. Its main task is to support the forest patrolmen to preserve and protect the ecology system and all historic monuments in all the forests in Taiwan.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_Agencies">Local Agencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Local Agencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Local police departments exist for all administrative divisions above the county/provincial city level. There are 26 local departments in total. All local agencies are subordinate to the NPA as well as their respective local government, and all police officers wear the same uniform regardless of jurisdiction.</p>
<p>Local city and county governments are responsible for allocating funds for their local police departments and local police chiefs must attend city or county council meetings to answer questions from council members. While all high ranking police officers are subject to personnel rotation and transfer by the NPA, under the wake of local autonomy beginning in 1990, the NPA Director-General will usually consult with local officials regarding the appointment of local police chiefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-personnel_issue_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-personnel_issue-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_Police_Departments">Municipal Police Departments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Municipal Police Departments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Police Commissioners of the Taipei and Kaohsiung City Police Departments hold the rank of "Director-General, first level,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which is the same as the Director-General of National Police Agency.</p>
<p>Although the Directors-General of the Taipei and Kaohsiung City Police Departments hold the same rank as the Directors-General of National Police Agency and <a href="/wiki/Central_Police_University" title="Central Police University">Central Police University</a>, the latter two are both presidential commissions and appointed by the Minister of the Interior. As a courtesy, the two local Directors-General usually wear a lower rank in public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Taipei_City_Police_Department"><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Taipei_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Taipei City Police Department (page does not exist)">Taipei City Police Department</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Taipei City Police Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Somehow different from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_system_of_Japan" title="Police system of Japan">Police system of Japan</a>, in the capital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taipei_City" title="Taipei City">Taipei City</a>, the city government has its own <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Taipei_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Taipei City Police Department (page does not exist)">Taipei City Police Department</a> and a subordinate criminal investigative force, "Criminal Investigation Division", which is also an active and famous police force in the field.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Kaohsiung_City_Police_Department"><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kaohsiung_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kaohsiung City Police Department (page does not exist)">Kaohsiung City Police Department</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Kaohsiung City Police Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In retrospect to the organization of police administration of Kaohsiung City, it has gone through history and experienced the great changes of the society. In fact it has to be traced back to the time of Japanese colonization. At that time there was the establishment of Eastern Police Office, Western Police Office, Marine Police Office and Fire Police Office.</p>
<p>After the end of Japanese colonization in October 1945, they were used continuously until the official establishment of Police Office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a> on 8 November 1945. This new police office was in charge of the maintenance of law and order of the whole city. On 14 February 1946, there was the establishment of the 1st and 2nd Precincts, Marine Branch Office and Fire Office. In 1958 the Police Office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a> was permitted to be promoted as a police department of Grade B system. Meanwhile, two more Precinct Offices were added.</p>
<p>On 1 July 1970 the police office was furthermore promoted as the only one belonging to Grade A system in Taiwan Police System. Under it, there were 7 Police Precincts established. On 1 July 1979 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a> was officially promoted as a special municipal city under the central government. The Police Office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a> was officially named as <a class="external text" href="http://www.kmph.gov.tw/" rel="nofollow">Kaohsiung Municipal Police Department</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Island_Police_Departments_in_Fujian">Island Police Departments in Fujian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Island Police Departments in Fujian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matsu_Islands" title="Matsu Islands">Lien-chiang County</a> Police Departments are two local agencies which are historically out of the mainstream of Taiwan police system. The local police forces were merged into the two respective military theater garrison commands once or twice.</p>
<p>Since the direct travel between <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matsu_Islands" title="Matsu Islands">Matsu</a> was opened in 2002, the local police departments are put on heavier duty of border security and immigration checking.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinmen_County_Police_Department"><a class="external text" href="http://www.kpb.gov.tw/" rel="nofollow">Kinmen County Police Department</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Kinmen County Police Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Its root can be traced back to China in 1915. The local police department was once disbanded in 1949 and replaced by the military security forces under the <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> garrison command. Then, in 1953, along with the reinstatement of <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> County government, <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> PD was detached from military command and assigned to the county government, which was still under the oversight of <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> military committee until 1992.</p>
<p>Today, its table of organization contains 312 police officers and three field police brigades: Forces of "Criminal Investigation", "Special Police" and "Traffic Police."</p>
<p>There is no police precincts(分局) in <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a>. The Kinmen County Police Department has direct control over its six police <b>institutes(所),</b> which are somehow between the level of "police precinct" and "police station."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stations_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-stations-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Wu-chiu dispatched Police Station" is attached to the Kinmen County Police Department for the convenience of administration.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Lienchiang_County_Police_Department"><a href="/wiki/Matsu_Islands" title="Matsu Islands">Lienchiang County</a> Police Department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Lienchiang County Police Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>It is stationed at <a href="/wiki/Matsu_Islands" title="Matsu Islands">Matsu Islands</a>.</p>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.lchpd.gov.tw/index.html" rel="nofollow">Lien-chiang County Police Department</a> was formed with 17 police officers in 1956 as a military security detail "Lien-chiang County Police Institute" under Matsu military committee. In December 1965, it was re-designated as "Lien-chiang County Police Department." In May 1967, it was then separated from a military unit and turned into a real civilian police force with four dispatched police stations.</p>
<p>After two expansions in June 1979 and in November 1998, it now contains 124 police officers and two field police brigades: Forces of "Criminal Investigation" and "Special Police and Traffic."</p>
<p>In August 2001, Lien-chiang County Police Department reorganized and upgraded its four dispatched police stations to four "police institutes." Among them, two police stations are added to "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nankan" title="Nankan">Nankan</a> Police Institute" and one "Dongjyu Police Station" on Dungjyu island is formed up and subordinated to "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chukuang" title="Chukuang">Chukuang</a> Police Institute."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current police service in Taiwan traces its roots back to police forces established in Taiwan during Japanese colonial rule, as well as police services established in Mainland China during the early 20th Century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Search_Lines_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Search_Lines-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_Colonial_Police">Japanese Colonial Police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Japanese Colonial Police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Taiwan#Japanese_rule" title="History of Taiwan">Taiwan under Japanese rule</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg/220px-Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg/330px-Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg/440px-Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chinkuashih_Koban.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A restored Japanese colonial era <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koban_(police_box)" title="Koban (police box)">koban</a></i> (police box).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first formal police forces in Taiwan were organized by the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule" title="Taiwan under Japanese rule">Japanese Colonial Government</a> which oversaw Taiwan between 1895 and 1945. In the early years of Japanese rule, rebellions were common especially in rural areas. With the passage of the "Bandit Laws", police forces as well as garrison units from the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Japanese military</a> were tasked with suppression of rebels, though large scale rebellions had largely died out by 1902. Military and police forces were also involved in the efforts of the colonial government to secure control over the mountainous regions of Taiwan from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taiwanese_aborigine" title="Taiwanese aborigine">Taiwanese aborigines</a> around 1910, and a series of smaller attempted rebellions and civil disorders between 1912 and 1916 <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.educ.kyoto-u.ac.jp/~koma/taiwanpaper.htm" rel="nofollow">[3]</a>. With the gradual acceptance of Japanese rule sinking in among the general populace by the 1920s, most agitators turned to seeking political change and reform within the established system in the home rule movement, <a href="/wiki/Secret_police" title="Secret police">Secret police</a> forces were tasked with keeping a close watch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Search_Lines_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Search_Lines-3">[3]</a></sup> on political groups and agitators.</p>
<p>Most of the members of the police forces during this time were expatriate Japanese, though towards the latter period of Japanese rule, locals began to be recruited. Throughout much of this time, the police forces were granted broad power and authority and allegations of <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a> were common, especially during the earlier periods of Japanese rule. Consequently, they were not particularly liked by the general populace,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and were often viewed as a symbol of the more oppressive side of Japanese rule; though this gradually changed with the stabilization of the political situation. Nonetheless, Japan was generally credited for formally establishing law and order in what had previously been a hotbed of rebellion and lawlessness during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ching_Dynasty" title="Ching Dynasty">Ching Dynasty</a> rule. Much of the law enforcement infrastructure and traditions developed during this time would continue to be used under postwar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">ROC</a> rule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Police_in_the_early_ROC">Police in the early ROC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Police in the early ROC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ROC's law enforcement system built upon the foundations laid down during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ching_Dynasty" title="Ching Dynasty">Ching Dynasty</a> with the establishment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peking" title="Peking">Peking</a> Public Inspection Headquarters in 1902. Following the overthrow of the Ching Dynasty and the establishment of the Republic in 1912, a National Police Department was established under the auspices of the Ministry of the Interior. This early centralized system consisted of a national headquarters in the capital, provincial police administrations for each province, police departments and bureaus at the municipal and county level respectively. This system was extended to Taiwan following its transfer to ROC control in 1947 two years after the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, though the basic system from the Japanese era was retained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Search_Lines_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Search_Lines-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Official_History_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Official_History-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Martial_Law">Martial Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Martial Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the period between 19 May 1949 and 14 July 1987, the Taiwan police system had a controversial page in Taiwan history.</p>
<p>Between 1945 and 1988, police officers in Taiwan wore <a href="/wiki/Khaki" title="Khaki">khaki</a> uniforms with khaki combination caps similar to those worn by naval officers, or with white plastic helmets similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Shore_patrol" title="Shore patrol">Shore patrol</a>. Critics noted the similarity to military uniforms, eventually leading to the revision of police uniforms in 1988.</p>
<p>On 5 February 1951, by the power of the now-abolished law of contravention, police officers could grab men with long hairdo and use scissors to cut the men's long hair. So applied to mini-skirts, Hawaii shirts, flared trousers, or other clothes of the men or women with irregular dressing. On 5 February 1972, Taipei City Police Department even launched a mass-detainment against people who dressed like hippies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1972, to streamline organizational costs, the National Police Department was merged with the Taiwan Police Administration to form the new National Police Agency (NPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Official_History_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Official_History-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first 4 Directors-General of NPA, between 1972 and 1990, were active general officers transferred from Army or Marine Corps:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Chou Ju-cun (周菊村), between 1972 and 1976;</dd>
<dd>Kong Ling-Cheng (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%AD%94%E4%BB%A4%E6%99%9F" title="zh:孔令晟">zh:孔令晟</a>), between 1976 and June 1980;</dd>
<dd>Ho En-ting (何恩廷), between June 1980 and 1 August 1984;</dd>
<dd>Luo Chang (羅張),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> between 1 August 1984 and 4 August 1990.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The latter three even served as two-star Marine Corps Commandants.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Secret_police_organizations">Secret police organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Secret police organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Historically speaking, in Taiwan, unlike <b>Special Higher Police</b> (<a href="/wiki/Tokubetsu_K%C5%8Dt%C5%8D_Keisatsu" title="Tokubetsu Kōtō Keisatsu">Tokko</a>) and the <b>Japanese Military police</b> (<a href="/wiki/Kempeitai" title="Kempeitai">Kempeitai</a>) during the Japanese Colonial era, the secret police activity was not the major task of Taiwan police system during the Martial-Law era.</p>
<p>The Taiwan police system at that time only played as a supportive role, like performing frequent surveillance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Search_Lines_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Search_Lines-3">[3]</a></sup> for example. Nevertheless, the governmental body of Taiwan police system back then was and still is subjective to the supervision and coordination of <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Bureau_(Republic_of_China)" title="National Security Bureau (Republic of China)">National Security Bureau</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">ROC</a> National Security Council. The main secret-police work were held up by other security units listed below. Several units in the past like <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Bureau_(Republic_of_China)" title="National Security Bureau (Republic of China)">National Security Bureau</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_Of_Investigation_(Taiwan)" title="Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)">National Bureau of Investigation</a> were much more fearful or despicable to the people of Taiwan. However, by the end of the Martial-Law era, these so-called "secret police" units were legalized, transformed into intelligence-oriented or law-enforcement units, or even disbanded.</p>
<p>The typical secret-police example of the involvement from several security units is <a href="/wiki/Peng_Ming-min" title="Peng Ming-min">Peng Ming-min</a>, the famous Taiwanese political prisoner since the 1960s. Tipped off by several civilians, Peng was at first arrested by a police detail from local police station in Taipei City. Immediately, he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Garrison_Command" title="Taiwan Garrison Command">Taiwan Garrison Command</a> for interrogation, which was led by its Division of Political Warfare. Sequentially, Peng was courted-martial by a military tribunal organized by "Division of Judge Advocate General" of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Garrison_Command" title="Taiwan Garrison Command">Taiwan Garrison Command</a>. Peng was pardoned in 1965 but put under house arrest. In 1966, Peng's case was then discussed by National Security Council and transferred from <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Garrison_Command" title="Taiwan Garrison Command">Taiwan Garrison Command</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_Of_Investigation_(Taiwan)" title="Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)">Bureau of Investigation</a>. Until his escape in January, 1970, Peng was under the regular visits from local policemen and constant surveillance from agents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_Of_Investigation_(Taiwan)" title="Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)">Bureau of Investigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peng_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-peng-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan_Garrison_Command"><a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Garrison_Command" title="Taiwan Garrison Command">Taiwan Garrison Command</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Taiwan Garrison Command">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The influence to Taiwan of this infamous late <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Garrison_Command" title="Taiwan Garrison Command">Taiwan Garrison Command</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Ministry_of_National_Defense" title="ROC Ministry of National Defense">the Ministry of National Defense</a> is much too wide, including every aspect like politics, society, economics, culture, and even education, not just confined to the Taiwan police system. Later, it was disbanded and broken into two different units, which have evolved to the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Coast_Guard" title="Republic of China Coast Guard">Coast Guard Administration</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Yuan" title="Executive Yuan">Executive Yuan</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reserve Command (page does not exist)">Reserve Command</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Ministry_of_National_Defense" title="ROC Ministry of National Defense">the Ministry of National Defense</a>.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_Intelligence_Bureau">Military Intelligence Bureau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Military Intelligence Bureau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Military Intelligence Bureau of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Ministry_of_National_Defense" title="ROC Ministry of National Defense">the Ministry of National Defense</a> is the successor of the controversial <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Investigation_and_Statistics" title="Bureau of Investigation and Statistics">Bureau of Investigation and Statistics</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Military_Council" title="National Military Council">National Military Council</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> in the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">ROC</a> era and during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a>. It was nicknamed "Jun-Tong" (軍統), literally <b>Military-Statistics</b>. Before 1949, many high-ranking police officers were trained by the <b>Military-Statistics</b>. After 1949, many of them, and a lot of intelligence officers from the military intelligence agencies, were flooded into Taiwan police organizations. The typical case is Wang Lu-chiao (王魯翹), Father of Wang Cho-Chiun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the Director-General of National Police Agency after June 1, 2008. Wang Lu-chiao was an intelligence officer of <b>Military-Statistics</b> and sent to <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> to assassinate <a href="/wiki/Wang_Jingwei" title="Wang Jingwei">Wang Jingwei</a> between January and March 1939 without success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Wang Lu-chiao, after 1949 and until his death by a vehicular accident in 1973, became a successful criminal detective and later served as the Police Commissioner of Taipei City Police Department in the 1970s.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Department_of_Political_Warfare">General Department of Political Warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: General Department of Political Warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>This military institution was the equivalence of the Third Chief Directorate of <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> in former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. In the ROC Armed Forces, every military command, down to a company or a battery, has a commissioned officer served as the third-ranking officer and a political commissar, who is either dispatched from or trained by now "Political Warfare General Bureau", which was back then "General Department of Political Warfare" of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Ministry_of_National_Defense" title="ROC Ministry of National Defense">the Ministry of National Defense</a>.</p>
<p>However, the influence of this former General Department of Political Warfare was not just confined within the ROC military. Many commissioned officers from the branch of Political Warfare were directly transferred to the police system. One of them, Chen Bi (陳壁), even were promoted to the Police Commissioner of The Taiwan Provincial Police Administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badges_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-badges-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the ROC, before May 26, 1995, military training lessons were mandatory to all students of senior high schools, colleges and universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As a result, there were and still are many military instructors stationed at every school more advanced than senior high schools. Naturally these military instructors are also commissioned officers in active duty either dispatched from or trained by the branch of Political Warfare. Before 1995, one of their missions at school is to closely watch, report, or even suppress any possible anti-government or political activity launched by students on or off campus.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Bureau_of_Investigation"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_Of_Investigation_(Taiwan)" title="Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)">Bureau of Investigation</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Bureau of Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_Of_Investigation_(Taiwan)" title="Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)">Bureau of Investigation</a> was originated from the other controversial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">ROC</a> security unit, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Bureau_of_Investigation_and_Statistics" title="Central Bureau of Investigation and Statistics">Central Bureau of Investigation and Statistics</a>, so-called "Zhong-Tong" or literally "Central Statistics" Zhong-Tong was subordinate to the Central Executive Committee of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Nationalist_Party" title="Chinese Nationalist Party">Chinese Nationalist Party</a> before 1949.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Security_Bureau"><a href="/wiki/National_Security_Bureau_(Republic_of_China)" title="National Security Bureau (Republic of China)">National Security Bureau</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: National Security Bureau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Bureau_(Republic_of_China)" title="National Security Bureau (Republic of China)">National Security Bureau</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">ROC</a> National Security Council was established at the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Ching-kuo" title="Chiang Ching-kuo">Chiang Ching-kuo</a> in 1956. This bureau served as the primary intelligence unit in Taiwan and headed by Cheng Jie-Min (鄭介民), a heavy-weight three-star army general from <b>Military-Statistics</b>. The purpose of creating such a new intelligence agency was to settle the inter-agency rivalry among the intelligence community in Taiwan and allow more direct access for <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek" title="Chiang Kai-shek">Chiang Kai-shek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Ching-kuo" title="Chiang Ching-kuo">Chiang Ching-kuo</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Martial_Law_era_reforms">Post-Martial Law era reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Post-Martial Law era reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, Chuang Heng-dai (莊亨岱), by then the Commissioner of Railway Police Bureau, became the first career police officer with the background of a criminal investigator to take over the chief of NPA. Since then, all the successive directors-general of NPA are promoted from career police officers in active duty within the Taiwan police system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badges_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-badges-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Taiwan Provincial Police Administration was again separated from the NPA in 1995 with the implementation of local autonomy statutes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Constitution" title="ROC Constitution">ROC Constitution</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_fighting" title="Fire fighting">Fire-fighting</a> units also ceased to be part of the NPA from that year and were reorganized into a separate <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire department</a>. <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Agency" title="National Fire Agency">National Fire Agency</a> of Ministry of Interior was established on 1 March 1995 to be responsible for fire prevention, disaster rescue and emergency medical service.</p>
<p>In 1999, with the downsizing of the provincial level of government, the Taiwan Provincial Police Administration was dissolved and its personnel and responsibilities were once again transferred to the National Police Agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Official_History_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Official_History-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>An increase in crime and liberalization of the mass media in the 1990s led to many questions concerning the effectiveness of the police force in investigating and fighting crime, as opposed to its prior concentration on crowd and <a href="/wiki/Riot_control" title="Riot control">riot control</a>, a carryover from the martial law era.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_years">Recent years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Recent years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With increased media coverage in recent years and the proliferation of tabloid newspapers and 24-hour cable news channels throughout Taiwan, the police force has been faced with new challenges involving high-profile crimes, and increased media involvement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Past concerns of police corruption have largely been replaced by concerns of police ineffectiveness, particularly in light of several high-profile cases in recent years. Gun related crime has also increased, though the overall crime rate in Taiwan remains lower than that of most western and Asian nations.</p>
<p>On 22 July 2000, four workers carrying out riverbed construction work in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pachang_River" title="Pachang River">Pachang River</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chiayi_County" title="Chiayi County">Chiayi County</a>, were surrounded by the quickly rising torrent on Saturday afternoon. The four stood in the center of the river for three hours, waiting for a helicopter that never came, and were finally washed away at around 7:08pm in sight of family members, helpless would-be rescuers, and the lens of news cameras on the riverbank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The delay was attributed to bureaucratic <a href="/wiki/Red_tape" title="Red tape">red tape</a> and three top government officials resigned, including <a href="/wiki/Yu_Shyi-kun" title="Yu Shyi-kun">Yu Shyi-kun</a>, the vice Premier of <a href="/wiki/Executive_Yuan" title="Executive Yuan">Executive Yuan</a> and two Director-Generals from National Police Agency and National Fire Agency. This Pachang-Creek incident (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%85%AB%E6%8E%8C%E6%BA%AA%E4%BA%8B%E4%BB%B6" title="zh:八掌溪事件">zh:八掌溪事件</a>) caused a field day for the news media in Taiwan and triggered a reform of the airborne emergency management system. On 10 March 2004, the newly formed <b>National Airborne Service Corps</b> (NASC) of the Ministry of Interior absorbed four civil airborne squadrons</p>
<dl>
<dd>The Airborne Squadron of National Police Agency,</dd>
<dd>The Preparatory Office of the Airborne Fire Fighting Squadron of National Fire Agency,</dd>
<dd>The Aviation Team of Civil Aeronautics Administration of Ministry of Transportation and Communications,</dd>
<dd>The Air Patrol Squadron of the Coast Guard Administration.</dd>
</dl>
<p>NASC takes over the responsibilities of five major airborne tasks：Search and Rescue, Disaster Relief, Emergency Medical Services, Reconnaissance and Patrol, and Transportation.</p>
<p>On 26 July 2004, members of the Criminal Investigation Bureau engaged in what was arguably the largest gun battle in the history of modern Taiwan with members of a kidnapping gang in <a href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_County" title="Kaohsiung County">Kaohsiung County</a> (now part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a>). Though they held a numerical advantage, the officers found themselves outgunned by the suspects who possessed bulletproof vests and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M16_(rifle)" title="M16 (rifle)">M16</a>'s. In the ensuing gunfight four police officers were injured, and two suspects were shot and arrested. However the ringleader of the gang managed to escape along with a cohort after holding a passing civilian hostage and escaping in a hijacked car on live TV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_Xi_Ming_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang_Xi_Ming-7">[7]</a></sup> The ensuing manhunt was widely covered, the ringleader Zhang Xi-Ming (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%BC%B5%E9%8C%AB%E9%8A%98" title="zh:張錫銘">zh:張錫銘</a>) was finally arrested following another gun battle with police on 13 July 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 February 2006, <a href="/wiki/Hou_You-yi" title="Hou You-yi">Hou You-yi</a>, the Criminal Investigation Bureau Commissioner, assumed command as the director-general of the National Police Agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He, a career criminal investigator, is the youngest-ever to hold that post. Wang Cho-Chiun (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%8E%8B%E5%8D%93%E9%88%9E" title="zh:王卓鈞">zh:王卓鈞</a>), Chief of Taipei City Police Department, was named as the new director-general effective June 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 2 Jan 2007, according to "the Organic Law of the National Immigration Agency" enacted on 30 Nov 2005, the NPA's former <b>Immigration Office</b> was expanded to become the <a href="/wiki/National_Immigration_Agency" title="National Immigration Agency">National Immigration Agency</a> under the direct control of the Ministry of the Interior, and Wu Cheng-chi (吳振吉) was named the first director general of NIA.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_information">General information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: General information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to normal law enforcement duties, police in the ROC are charged with other slightly more unusual duties such as taking <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> data, as well as immigration and visa related issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Most jurisdictions also have a Foreign Affairs squad staffed by English speaking officers tasked with visa enforcement and issues relating to foreigners or the foreign community in Taiwan.</p>
<p>With the exception of traffic police in some local departments, all police officers carry small firearms and batons. A criticism that has emerged in recent years is that police officers generally do not carry any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonlethal_weapons" title="Nonlethal weapons">nonlethal weapons</a> aside from batons such as <a href="/wiki/Taser" title="Taser">tasers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pepper_spray" title="Pepper spray">pepper spray</a>, limiting their options when dealing with violent criminals. Police in Taiwan have sometimes been known to fire warning shots or at the legs of fleeing suspects, though this practice has reportedly decreased in recent years.</p>
<p>Most police officers patrol in marked <a href="/wiki/Police_car" title="Police car">police cars</a> or small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorscooter" title="Motorscooter">motorscooters</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycles" title="Motorcycles">Motorcycles</a> are typically used only by the traffic police. Police officers patrol with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bar" title="Light bar">light bars</a> on their cars turned on. Like many other nations in the Asia Pacific region, the emergency number for police in Taiwan is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-1-0" title="1-1-0">1-1-0</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_Stations">Local Stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Local Stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Taiwan, including islands of <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matsu_Islands" title="Matsu Islands">Matsu</a>, there are a total of 1,615 various local police stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stations_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-stations-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typically, there are 16 police officers, including a station chief and a sergeant, in a local police station.</p>
<p>Usually, a counter is placed in the 24-hour-opened main entrance and a pistol-armed police officer would sit there as the station duty officer, or <b>watch commander</b>, on a 2-or-4-hour watch. The <b>watch commander</b> is the main funnel between that station and civilians within its area of responsibility. He or she is in charge of the safety of the station, including the security of firearms and ammunition. The station duty officer must answer all personal or telephone complaints from civilians, delegate those reports to proper desk police officers, dispatch police officers on patrol to respond, or provide legal advice to common citizens. Also, the station duty officer would reply to all information requests concerning suspicious persons or vehicles over radio communication.</p>
<p>In remote areas or due to manpower shortage, some local stations are allowed by regulation to close the main gate between 2200 and 0600 hrs, but one police officer must remain inside.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_and_training">Selection and training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Selection and training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Career police officers are trained at either the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Police_College" title="Taiwan Police College">Taiwan Police College</a> in Taipei City or the <a href="/wiki/Central_Police_University" title="Central Police University">Central Police University</a> in Taoyuan City. The Taiwan Police College provides basic training for entry level police officers assigned to patrol duty, while the Police University provides more advanced training for manager level or active duty officers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensics" title="Forensics">forensics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administration_(business)" title="Administration (business)">administration</a>, traffic control, fire control and science, border police, crime investigation, maritime police, <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_management" title="Information management">information management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and other related subjects.</p>
<p>In 2006, police selection criteria were amended to include all persons with a high school diploma between ages 18–28 able to pass a police civil service exam and undergo one year of training. This removed the past requirement where only graduates of specialized police colleges and universities were allowed to become police officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conscripts may also choose to fulfill their mandatory twenty month national service requirement in the Police Department following basic training. Conscripts are typically deployed to assist local officers in positions such as traffic control, riot control, secretarial work, and basic patrol. Conscripts serving in the police force are generally not issued firearms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniforms">Uniforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Uniforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Most police officers, since 1988, wear the standard uniform which consists of dark blue pants, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combination_cap" title="Combination cap">Combination cap</a> with a gold band or a baseball cap, a jacket or an overcoat in winter, and a light Grey shirt. Dark-blue or yellow windbreakers attached white reflective strips are sometimes also issued, with white letters "POLICE" and "警察" on the back. All uniform buttons or decorations are golden yellow. A golden rank insignia is worn over the right breast pocket of the shirt. Two patches are worn, one on each shoulder: one with the golden NPA insignia, department name, and serial number. The second patch may be optional and is specific to the squad or unit the officer belongs to.</p>
<p>Court bailiffs, volunteer police officers, and civil defense personnel wear the same uniforms with small variations. The cap insignia worn by volunteer police officers is silver white rather than gold, as are the decorations and the shoulder patches. Instead of the golden rank insignia over the right breast pocket, volunteer policemen wear name tags.</p>
<p>Volunteer traffic police personnel wear similar uniforms with same style but with a high visibility orange shirt, and usually bear no rank.</p>
<p>Members of certain <a href="/wiki/SWAT" title="SWAT">SWAT</a> units will usually wear dark blue <a href="/wiki/Battle_Dress_Uniform" title="Battle Dress Uniform">BDU</a> style uniforms rather than the regular one. Detectives typically dress in plainclothes.</p>
<p>Conscripts serving alternative national service with the police wear <a href="/wiki/Khaki" title="Khaki">khaki</a> uniforms instead of the standard police blue.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interdepartmental_cooperation">Interdepartmental cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Interdepartmental cooperation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bureau_of_Investigation_2">Bureau of Investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Bureau of Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_Of_Investigation_(Taiwan)" title="Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)">Bureau Of Investigation (Taiwan)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MJIB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="1728" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/MJIB.jpg/220px-MJIB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/MJIB.jpg/330px-MJIB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/MJIB.jpg/440px-MJIB.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MJIB.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bureau of Investigation</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Regarding drugs, corruptions, espionage, and economic crimes, the Investigative Bureau of Ministry of Justice (MJIB) has developed stronger capability to cover cases in those categories for decades. The investigation of these cases, police corruption included, are usually transferred to or led by the Bureau of Investigation.</p>
<p>MJIB officers are equipped with a strong laboatory-testing capacity regarding controlled substances. This gives a necessary advantage and training for the investigators of MJIB. However, the police forces in the ROC still maintain several active drug enforcement squads at the local police departments and the Criminal Investigation Bureau of NPA.</p>
<p>Historically, counter-intelligence affairs are under the turf of MJIB. Different from "<a href="/wiki/Special_Branch" title="Special Branch">special branch</a>" in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Policing in the United Kingdom">police system in United Kingdom</a>, the security divisions at all levels of Taiwan police system are mainly staff units, not fielded police details. Under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Bureau_(Republic_of_China)" title="National Security Bureau (Republic of China)">National Security Bureau</a>, all non-mililary cases of espionage would soon be moved to the Bureau of Investigation.</p>
<p>A few years ago, high-ranking police officials tried to develop the strength of investigating <a href="/wiki/White-collar_crime" title="White-collar crime">white-collar crimes</a> in the ROC police system but gained unnoticed progress. The MJIB is still in the lead position of cracking economic crimes or money-laundry. However, the Criminal Investigation Bureau of NPA has built fair reputation on <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> protection and the safety of computers and networks. Also, regarding the <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> issue, the 1st brigade of the 2nd special police corps is especially tasked for the enforcement related to intellectual property.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_Police">Military Police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Military Police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Military_Police" title="Republic of China Military Police">Republic of China Military Police</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2165" data-file-width="3845" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg/220px-ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg/330px-ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg/440px-ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ROC_Military_Police_Headquarters_Main_Gate_20100718.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Military Police</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the ROC, the usual cases for local police to bring in the military police are military deserters or traffic accidents involving military vehicles or personnel.</p>
<p>The latter one is a very interesting situation, in that ROC civilians tend to shake down the related military units when such kind of accidents occur. For some paranoid thinking, people in the ROC military presume that the local policemen would side with civilians. As an unofficial rule in all ROC armed forces, all military drivers, and commissioned or non-commissioned officers are frequently instructed to call in the military police when they find themselves are in the middle of such a vehicular incident.</p>
<p>Contrary to common belief, military police can actively go after civilian criminals, although usually they choose not to muddle the gray area after the lifting of martial law on July 15, 1987. According to the "Law of the dispatching of Judicial Police Personnel," military police can deal with civilian affairs under the supervision of the public prosecutor(s) from all court prosecutors' offices at all levels under the Ministry of Justice. Sometimes public prosecutors will direct military police to handle large-scale searches or arrests when investigating cases of police-related crimes, prostitution, or fugitive recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a>, there are three company-size troops of <a href="/wiki/Special_forces" title="Special forces">special forces</a> under the operational control of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROC_Ministry_of_National_Defense" title="ROC Ministry of National Defense">the Ministry of National Defense</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Northern Taiwan: Military Police Special Service Company (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPSSC" title="MPSSC">MPSSC</a>, Code Name: <b>Night Hawk</b>), <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Military_Police" title="Republic of China Military Police">Military Police Command</a>.</li>
<li>Central Taiwan: Special Warfare Special Service Company, Aviation, Airborne and Special Warfare Command of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Army" title="Republic of China Army">Republic of China Army</a>.</li>
<li>Southern Taiwan: Marine Special Service Company, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Marine_Corps" title="Republic of China Marine Corps">Marine Corps Command</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Navy" title="Republic of China Navy">Republic of China Navy</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>These three are tasked to handle combat situations involving international terrorists, mercenary or para-military criminals. Bound by laws, currently only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPSSC" title="MPSSC">the Military Police Special Service Company</a> can react to non-military cases, like airplane hijacking.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coast_Guard">Coast Guard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Coast Guard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Administration_(Taiwan)" title="Coast Guard Administration (Taiwan)">Coast Guard Administration (Taiwan)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG/220px-Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG/330px-Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG/440px-Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taiwan_Coast_Guard_Administration.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coast Guard</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The shore lines of Taiwan are under multiple protections from several law enforcement units in Taiwan.</p>
<p>The history of Taiwan's <a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Administration_(Taiwan)" title="Coast Guard Administration (Taiwan)">Coast Guard Administration</a> can be traced back to several origins. One of these is the former 7th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Police" title="Special Police">Special Police</a> Corps, which was expanded from the late and unique River Patrol squadron of the <a href="/wiki/Taipei_County" title="Taipei County">Taipei County</a> (now New Taipei City) Police Department. The land branches of Coast Guard Administration originate from the controversial <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Garrison_Command" title="Taiwan Garrison Command">Taiwan Garrison Command</a>, which no longer exists, but the formations of land patrol forces were inherited from the ROC Army. Because of its interesting and complicated origins, the Coast Guard Administration does not always go along well with the police, especially when problems related to legal jurisdiction on land occur.</p>
<p>Smuggling and arms trafficking has been a long been an issue in Taiwan. Human-trafficking and its counterpart, prostitution, are increasingly serious problems in Taiwan. The maritime patrol of the Coast Guard Administration have the responsibility to stop those crimes at sea, and the coastal patrol of Coast Guard Administration are responsible for intercepting such criminal cases along the coast of Taiwan. The in-land territory of Taiwan should be the responsibility of individual local police departments or national law enforcement units from the NPA or MJIB.</p>
<p>Cases like smuggling and human-trafficking may cause turf wars between competing law enforcement agencies. In some examples, when brothels were raided by plain-clothes peace officers, the pimp would quietly go to the local police station and gather possible information about his girls in detention, but sometimes the pimp would later find that his girls were held up by the local coastal patrol units.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_Brigades">Fire Brigades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Police_Agency_(Republic_of_China)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Fire Brigades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Fire brigades</div>

<p>Taiwan Firemen were a part of the Taiwan Police before 1995. At that time, the Fire Policemen were not the most favorite people in the eyes of certain police chiefs. The most extreme case was in <a href="/wiki/Taipei_County" title="Taipei County">Taipei County</a> (now New Taipei City) Police Department: the <a href="/wiki/Taipei_County" title="Taipei County">Taipei County</a> Police Commissioner Yao Kao-Chiao (姚高橋) assigned all his fire policemen to kitchen duty because he felt his fire policemen were doing nothing except waiting for a fire to happen.</p>
<p>It is worth to point out that Yao Kao-Chiao was the Directors-General of <a href="/wiki/Central_Police_University" title="Central Police University">Central Police University</a> from May 1995 to June 1996, of National Police Agency from June 1996 to 1997, and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Coast_Guard" title="Republic of China Coast Guard">Coast Guard Administration</a> from 28 January 2000 until 21 May 2000.</p>
<p>Also, in Taiwan, another contradiction was the radio call signs for the fire trucks or rescue vehicles. For reasons to avoid unwanted attentions and to remain secrecy, all radio call signs for all police vehicles were uncorrelated with the missions of the subject vehicles, unnecessarily including fire trucks, rescue vehicles, and ambulances of fire brigades. When fire police teams were responding to an emergency situation, these confusing call signs inevitably led to chaos and time delay. A successful fire chief, Chao Kang (趙鋼),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> convinced the police high command to rearrange the radio call sign assignment for all fire vehicles. Chao Kang then was appointed as the Commissioner of Taiwan Provincial Fire Administration, and the Director-General of <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Agency" title="National Fire Agency">National Fire Agency</a> from 10 September 2000 to September 2002.</p>
