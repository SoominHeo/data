<p><b>French legislative elections</b> took place on 21 and 28 March 1993 to elect the tenth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_France" title="National Assembly of France">National Assembly</a> of the <a href="/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic" title="French Fifth Republic">Fifth Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Since 1988, President <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a> and his Socialist cabinets had relied on a relative parliamentary majority. In an attempt to avoid having to work with the Communists, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Michel_Rocard" title="Michel Rocard">Michel Rocard</a> tried to gain support from the <a href="/wiki/Union_for_French_Democracy" title="Union for French Democracy">UDF</a> by appointing four UDF ministers. After the UDF withdrew its support for the government in 1991, Rocard and the UDF ministers resigned. The UDF then became allied with the Gaullist <a href="/wiki/Rally_for_the_Republic" title="Rally for the Republic">Rally for the Republic</a> (RPR).</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Socialist_Party" title="French Socialist Party">Socialist Party</a> (PS) was further weakened by scandals (involving illicit financing, contaminated blood and other affairs) and an intense rivalry between François Mitterrand's potential successors (<a href="/wiki/Lionel_Jospin" title="Lionel Jospin">Lionel Jospin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Fabius" title="Laurent Fabius">Laurent Fabius</a>). In March 1992, the Socialists were punished at the <a href="/wiki/French_regional_elections,_1992" title="French regional elections, 1992">local elections</a>. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/%C3%89dith_Cresson" title="Édith Cresson">Édith Cresson</a> was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_B%C3%A9r%C3%A9govoy" title="Pierre Bérégovoy">Pierre Bérégovoy</a>. The latter promised to struggle against economic recession and corruption, but he was himself suspected to have received a loan from a controversial businessman, Roger-Patrice Pelat.</p>
<p>The election was a landslide victory for the RPR-UDF alliance, while the PS and their left-wing allies received their worst result since the 1960s. The PS lost nearly 80% of the seats they had held at the time of the chamber's dissolution. This caused a crisis within the PS; Fabius lost his position as First Secretary in favour of Rocard, who claimed that a political "big bang" was needed. Jospin announced his political retirement after he was defeated in his <a href="/wiki/Haute-Garonne" title="Haute-Garonne">Haute-Garonne</a> constituency. Depressed by the defeat and the accusations about the loan from Pelat, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_B%C3%A9r%C3%A9govoy" title="Pierre Bérégovoy">Pierre Bérégovoy</a> committed suicide on 1 May.</p>
<p>Some traditional PS voters had voted for <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">the Greens</a> in the first round. These ecologists obtained a total of 10.84%, making this the best total score for French Green parties in legislative elections. However, only two ecologists qualified for the runoff, including <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Voynet" title="Dominique Voynet">Dominique Voynet</a> in her constituency in the <a href="/wiki/Doubs" title="Doubs">Doubs</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">département</a></i>. Both of these candidates were eventually defeated. Lack of major political allies for these ecologists explained this failure to take any seats.</p>
<p>The RPR-UDF coalition formed the largest parliamentary majority since 1958, taking a total of 485 seats or 84% of the 577 seats. The RPR leader <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">Jacques Chirac</a> demanded President Mitterrand's resignation and refused to be Prime Minister in a new "cohabitation" government. Finally, he suggested the nomination of his former RPR <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(France)" title="Ministry of Finance (France)">Finance Minister</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edouard_Balladur" title="Edouard Balladur">Edouard Balladur</a> at the head of the government. Balladur promised publicly that he would not run against Chirac for the next presidential election. The second "cohabitation" finished with the 1995 presidential election.</p>
