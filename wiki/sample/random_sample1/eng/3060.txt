<p><b>Nancy Marchand</b> (June 19, 1928 – June 18, 2000) was an American actress. She began her career in theatre in 1951. She was perhaps most famous for her television portrayals of Margaret Pynchon on <i><a href="/wiki/Lou_Grant_(TV_series)" title="Lou Grant (TV series)">Lou Grant</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Livia_Soprano" title="Livia Soprano">Livia Soprano</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sopranos" title="The Sopranos">The Sopranos</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nancy_Marchand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marchand was born in <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>, to Raymond L. Marchand, a physician, and his wife, Marjorie Freeman, a pianist. She was raised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She graduated from the Carnegie Institute of Technology (now <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>) in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nancy_Marchand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A talented member of the <a href="/wiki/Actors_Studio" title="Actors Studio">Actors Studio</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Marchand made her <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> debut in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Taming_of_the_Shrew" title="The Taming of the Shrew">The Taming of the Shrew</a></i> in 1951. Additional theatre credits include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Merchant_of_Venice" title="The Merchant of Venice">The Merchant of Venice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Love%27s_Labour%27s_Lost" title="Love's Labour's Lost">Love's Labour's Lost</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Much_Ado_About_Nothing" title="Much Ado About Nothing">Much Ado About Nothing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Forty_Carats" title="Forty Carats">Forty Carats</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/And_Miss_Reardon_Drinks_A_Little" title="And Miss Reardon Drinks A Little">And Miss Reardon Drinks A Little</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plough_and_the_Stars" title="The Plough and the Stars">The Plough and the Stars</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glass_Menagerie" title="The Glass Menagerie">The Glass Menagerie</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Morning%27s_at_Seven" title="Morning's at Seven">Morning's at Seven</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Awake_and_Sing!" title="Awake and Sing!">Awake and Sing!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Octette_Bridge_Club" title="The Octette Bridge Club">The Octette Bridge Club</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Letters_(play)" title="Love Letters (play)">Love Letters</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Man_and_Superman" title="Man and Superman">Man and Superman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Importance_of_Being_Earnest" title="The Importance of Being Earnest">The Importance of Being Earnest</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_for_Scandal" title="The School for Scandal">The School for Scandal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Balcony" title="The Balcony">The Balcony</a></i>, for which she won a Distinguished Performance <a href="/wiki/Obie_Award" title="Obie Award">Obie Award</a>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Comedy/White_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Black Comedy/White Lies (page does not exist)">Black Comedy/White Lies</a></i>, for which she was nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Performance_by_a_Leading_Actress_in_a_Play" title="Tony Award for Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Play">Tony Award for Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Play</a>. She was nominated four times for the <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award" title="Drama Desk Award">Drama Desk Award</a>, winning handily for <i>Morning's at Seven</i>. She won a second Obie for her performance in <a href="/wiki/A._R._Gurney" title="A. R. Gurney">A. R. Gurney</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cocktail_Hour" title="The Cocktail Hour">The Cocktail Hour</a></i>.</p>
<p>On daytime television, Marchand created the roles of Vinnie Phillips on the <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> <a href="/wiki/Soap_opera" title="Soap opera">soap opera</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Love_of_Life" title="Love of Life">Love of Life</a></i> and Theresa Lamonte on the <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> soap, <i><a href="/wiki/Another_World_(TV_series)" title="Another World (TV series)">Another World</a></i>. She also memorably starred as matriarch Edith Cushing on <i><a href="/wiki/Lovers_and_Friends" title="Lovers and Friends">Lovers and Friends</a></i>, a short-lived soap opera.</p>
<p>On prime time television, Marchand was renowned for her roles as patrician newspaper publisher Margaret Pynchon on <i><a href="/wiki/Lou_Grant_(TV_series)" title="Lou Grant (TV series)">Lou Grant</a></i>—winning four <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Awards</a> as <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Supporting_Actress_in_a_Drama_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Drama Series">Best Supporting Actress in a Dramatic Series</a> for her performance—and matriarch <a href="/wiki/Livia_Soprano" title="Livia Soprano">Livia Soprano</a>, mother of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Soprano" title="Tony Soprano">Tony Soprano</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sopranos" title="The Sopranos">The Sopranos</a></i>, which earned her a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Performance_by_an_Actress_in_a_Supporting_Role_in_a_Series,_Mini-Series_or_Motion_Picture_Made_for_Television" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Performance by an Actress in a Supporting Role in a Series, Mini-Series or Motion Picture Made for Television">Golden Globe Award for Best Performance by an Actress in a Supporting Role in a Series, Mini-Series or Motion Picture Made for Television</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award_for_Outstanding_Performance_by_an_Ensemble_in_a_Drama_Series" title="Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Drama Series">Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Drama Series</a>. She appeared in many <a href="/wiki/Anthology_series" title="Anthology series">anthology series</a> in the early days of television, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philco_Television_Playhouse" title="The Philco Television Playhouse">The Philco Television Playhouse</a></i> (on which she starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Marty_(teleplay)" title="Marty (teleplay)">Marty</a></i> opposite <a href="/wiki/Rod_Steiger" title="Rod Steiger">Rod Steiger</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Kraft_Television_Theatre" title="Kraft Television Theatre">Kraft Television Theatre</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_One_(TV_series)" title="Studio One (TV series)">Studio One</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Playhouse_90" title="Playhouse 90">Playhouse 90</a></i>. Additional television credits include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Law_and_Mr._Jones" title="The Law and Mr. Jones">The Law and Mr. Jones</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Spenser:_For_Hire" title="Spenser: For Hire">Spenser: For Hire</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Homicide:_Life_on_the_Street" title="Homicide: Life on the Street">Homicide: Life on the Street</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Coach_(TV_series)" title="Coach (TV series)">Coach</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Court" title="Night Court">Night Court</a></i>. She played <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hester_Crane" title="Hester Crane">Hester Crane</a>, mother of <a href="/wiki/Frasier_Crane" title="Frasier Crane">Frasier Crane</a>, on an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Cheers" title="Cheers">Cheers</a></i>.</p>
<p>Marchand's <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">feature film</a> credits include <i><a href="/wiki/Ladybug_Ladybug_(film)" title="Ladybug Ladybug (film)">Ladybug Ladybug</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Me,_Natalie" title="Me, Natalie">Me, Natalie</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tell_Me_That_You_Love_Me,_Junie_Moon" title="Tell Me That You Love Me, Junie Moon">Tell Me That You Love Me, Junie Moon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hospital" title="The Hospital">The Hospital</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bostonians_(film)" title="The Bostonians (film)">The Bostonians</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jefferson_in_Paris" title="Jefferson in Paris">Jefferson in Paris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bachelor_Party" title="The Bachelor Party">The Bachelor Party</a> (1957)</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brain_Donors" title="Brain Donors">Brain Donors</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Reckless_(1995_film)" title="Reckless (1995 film)">Reckless</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Naked_Gun" title="The Naked Gun">The Naked Gun</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sabrina_(1995_film)" title="Sabrina (1995 film)">Sabrina</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dear_God_(film)" title="Dear God (film)">Dear God</a></i>, and <i>From the Hip</i> (1986).</p>
<p>At 70, after more than 50 years of acting, she played <a href="/wiki/Livia_Soprano" title="Livia Soprano">Livia Soprano</a>, the mother of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Soprano" title="Tony Soprano">Tony Soprano</a>, the main character on the HBO TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sopranos" title="The Sopranos">The Sopranos</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nancy_Marchand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marchand died from <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> on June 18, 2000 in <a href="/wiki/Stratford,_Connecticut" title="Stratford, Connecticut">Stratford, Connecticut</a>, one day before her 72nd birthday. Her character's death was written into the third season story line of <i>The Sopranos</i>. Her husband of 48 years, actor <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sparer" title="Paul Sparer">Paul Sparer</a> (1923–1999), had died the previous year, also from cancer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The couple had three children: Katie, an actress, David (Rosebud), a lawyer, and Rachel Sparer Bersier, an opera singer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Marchand was posthumously inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Theatre_Hall_of_Fame" title="American Theatre Hall of Fame">American Theatre Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
