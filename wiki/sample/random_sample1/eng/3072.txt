<p>A <b>plank</b> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> that is flat, elongated, and rectangular with parallel faces that are higher and longer than wide. Used primarily in <a href="/wiki/Carpentry" title="Carpentry">carpentry</a>, planks are critical in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a>, <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">houses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a>, and many other structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson2005-1">[1]</a></sup> Planks also serve as supports to form <a href="/wiki/Shelf_(storage)" title="Shelf (storage)">shelves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Table_(furniture)" title="Table (furniture)">tables</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3794" data-file-width="5691" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg/300px-Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg/450px-Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg/600px-Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sandakan_Sabah_Sawmill-20.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cutting a log into planks in a <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmill</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Usually made from sawed timber, planks are usually more than <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in (38 mm) thick, and are generally wider than <span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in (64 mm). In the United States, planks can be any length and are generally a minimum of 2 in (51 mm) deep by 8 in (200 mm) wide, but planks that are 2 in (51 mm) by 10 in (250 mm) and 2 in (51 mm) by 12 in (300 mm) are more commonly stocked by lumber retailers. Planks are often used as a work surface on elevated <a href="/wiki/Scaffolding" title="Scaffolding">scaffolding</a>, and need to be wide enough to provide strength without breaking when walked on. The wood is categorized as a <a href="/wiki/Lumber#Dimensional_lumber" title="Lumber">board</a> if its width is less than <span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in (64 mm), and its thickness is less than <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in (38 mm).</p>
<p>A plank used in a building as a horizontal supporting member that runs between foundations, walls, or beams to support a ceiling or floor is called a <a href="/wiki/Joist" title="Joist">joist</a>.</p>
<p>The plank was the basis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_transport" title="Maritime transport">maritime transport</a>: wood floats on <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, and abundant forests meant wooden logs could be easily obtained and processed, making planks the primary material in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_building" title="Ship building">ship building</a>. However, since the 20th century, wood has largely been supplanted in ship construction by <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, to decrease cost and improve durability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
