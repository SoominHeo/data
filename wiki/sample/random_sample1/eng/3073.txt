<p><b>Number Girl</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ナンバーガール</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Nanbā Gāru</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was a rock band formed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukuoka,_Fukuoka" title="Fukuoka, Fukuoka">Fukuoka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in August, 1995 by guitarist and vocalist Shutoku Mukai. They disbanded in 2002 following bassist Kentarō Nakao's departure from the band.</p>
<p>Number Girl played fast, guitar-driven rock similar to the <a href="/wiki/Pixies" title="Pixies">Pixies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Youth" title="Sonic Youth">Sonic Youth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCsker_D%C3%BC" title="Hüsker Dü">Hüsker Dü</a>. Over the course of seven years their sound evolved greatly, serving as a basis for Mukai's post-Number Girl project, <a href="/wiki/Zazen_Boys" title="Zazen Boys">Zazen Boys</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Number_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Number_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Number Girl was formed in August 1995, when Shutoku Mukai decided to form a band to play at a local event. A solo artist at the time, Mukai was briefly involved in one band called "Number Five". Learning that his new members were previously in a band called "Cowgirl", he combined the names, and christened his new band "Number Girl".</p>
<p>The original incarnation of Number Girl was unsuccessful, and the collaborating musicians quickly left the group. Mukai invited bassist Kentarō Nakao, who in turn invited guitarist Hisako Tabuchi, whom Nakao knew from her former job operating lighting at the Vivre Hall venue. Mukai convinced a fellow Fukuoka scene musician Ahito Inazawa to play drums, finalizing Number Girl's lineup. The band self-released two demos, "Atari Shock" and "Omoide in My Head", and was also featured on several compilations. Number Girl released their first full-length studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/School_Girl_Bye_Bye" title="School Girl Bye Bye">School Girl Bye Bye</a></i> in November 1997 on independent label Automatic Kiss, and released their first single, "Drunken Hearted", several months later.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_Label_Debut">Major Label Debut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Number_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major Label Debut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, the band began playing shows in the <a href="/wiki/Shimokitazawa" title="Shimokitazawa">Shimokitazawa</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. Gaining a higher profile in Japan's indie rock scene, the band quickly attracted major label attention, and signed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toshiba_EMI" title="Toshiba EMI">Toshiba EMI</a>. In May 1999, their major debut single, "Tōmei Shōjo" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">透明少女</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was released with their major debut, <i><a href="/wiki/School_Girl_Distortional_Addict" title="School Girl Distortional Addict">School Girl Distortional Addict</a></i>, released the following July. Number Girl quickly gained popularity throughout Japan, playing shows with other high-profile indie acts such as <a href="/wiki/Bloodthirsty_Butchers" title="Bloodthirsty Butchers">Bloodthirsty Butchers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Youth" title="Eastern Youth">Eastern Youth</a>. In fall of 1999, Number Girl released the single "Destruction Baby", produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flaming_Lips" title="Flaming Lips">Flaming Lips</a> producer <a href="/wiki/Dave_Fridmann" title="Dave Fridmann">Dave Fridmann</a>, as well as a live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Shibuya_Rockstransformed_J%C5%8Dtai" title="Shibuya Rockstransformed Jōtai">Shibuya Rockstransformed Jōtai</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2000 the band again entered the studio with producer Dave Fridmann and released their third full-length studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sappukei" title="Sappukei">Sappukei</a></i>. Fridmann's production helped the band to reach their full potential, and <i>Sappukei</i> served to combine Number Girl's aggressive rock with traditional Japanese sounds. During this time, they embarked on a brief US tour with <a href="/wiki/Polysics" title="Polysics">Polysics</a>, playing in small clubs through several cities.</p>
<p>After a year of touring, the band again enlisted Dave Fridmann to produce what would become their final studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Num-Heavymetallic" title="Num-Heavymetallic">Num-Heavymetallic</a></i>. <i>Num-Heavymetallic</i> took elements of their previous sound and combined it with Mukai's newly found experimentation. Unusual rhythms, effects-laden guitar lines, and a vocal style which alternated between punk-inspired grit and spoken word, would eventually be incorporated into Mukai's subsequent band, <a href="/wiki/Zazen_Boys" title="Zazen Boys">Zazen Boys</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disbanding">Disbanding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Number_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Disbanding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band then set out on their largest tour ever, playing over thirty dates, to support <i>Num-Heavymetallic</i>. However, on September 20, 2002, the band shocked fans by announcing their dissolution. The reason behind the band's demise is not entirely clear, although it is widely known that bassist Kentarō decided to leave the band. The other members decided that the band was a specific group, and opted to disband rather than replace Nakao. Number Girl played their final show on November 30, 2002 in <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a>. The recording of the performance would later be released as <i>Sapporo Omoide in My Head Jōtai</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">サッポロOmoide in My Head状態</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Number_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the demise of Number Girl, the members each went on to other projects. Nakao joined several bands like Spiral Chord, Sloth Love Chunks, Crypt City, younGSounds and later joined his long time friend Aiha Higurashi for the revival of <a href="/wiki/Seagull_Screaming_Kiss_Her_Kiss_Her" title="Seagull Screaming Kiss Her Kiss Her">Seagull Screaming Kiss Her Kiss Her</a>. Nakao also played as support member for the bands like MASS OF THE FERMENTING DREGS, dry as dust, The SALOVERS, hammer, fast Tokyo, and produced the band called THE GIRL. Guitarist Hisako Tabuchi became a member of <a href="/wiki/Bloodthirsty_Butchers" title="Bloodthirsty Butchers">Bloodthirsty Butchers</a>, the supergroup consist of former <a href="/wiki/Supercar_(band)" title="Supercar (band)">Supercar</a> members <a href="/wiki/Koji_Nakamura" title="Koji Nakamura">Koji Nakamura</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Miki_Furukawa" title="Miki Furukawa">Miki Furukawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lama_(Japanese_band)" title="Lama (Japanese band)">Lama</a> and also formed her own band, Toddle. Mukai continued performing as both a solo artist with his "Mukai Shutoku Acoustic &amp; Electric" shows and formed a new band, <a href="/wiki/Zazen_Boys" title="Zazen Boys">Zazen Boys</a> alongside drummer Ahito Inazawa. In 2005, Inazawa left Zazen Boys to form <a href="/wiki/Post-punk_revival" title="Post-punk revival">post-punk revival</a> band <a href="/wiki/Vola_and_the_Oriental_Machine" title="Vola and the Oriental Machine">Vola and the Oriental Machine</a> and later joined BEYONDS. In 2010, Mukai formed another band called KIMONOS.</p>
<p>Toshiba EMI released several posthumous Number Girl recordings under the title <i>Omoide in My Head</i>, consisting of a best-and-b-sides collection, two comprehensive live volumes, a three DVD set consisting of the band's two earlier live albums, and a rare tracks collection.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Number_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many bands have cited NUMBER GIRL as an inspiration, including <a href="/wiki/Base_Ball_Bear" title="Base Ball Bear">Base Ball Bear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_Kung-Fu_Generation" title="Asian Kung-Fu Generation">Asian Kung-Fu Generation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tricot_(band)" title="Tricot (band)">tricot</a>, kafka, TOUMING MAGAZINE, Kinoko Teikoku, MINOR SCHOOL and ART SCHOOL. They are considered as one of the biggest name of indie rock scene in late 90's to early 2000's.</p>
