<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Four Freedoms</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Four Freedoms</b> were goals articulated by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">United States President</a> <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> on January 6, 1941. In an address known as the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Four_Freedoms_speech" title="s:The Four Freedoms speech">Four Freedoms speech</a> (technically the <b>1941 State of the Union address</b>), he proposed four fundamental freedoms that people "everywhere in the world" ought to enjoy:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">Freedom of speech</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_of_worship" title="Freedom of worship">Freedom of worship</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_from_want" title="Freedom from want">Freedom from want</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freedom_from_fear" title="Freedom from fear">Freedom from fear</a></li>
</ol>
<p>Roosevelt delivered his speech 11 months before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_declaration_of_war_upon_Japan" title="United States declaration of war upon Japan">United States declared war on Japan</a>, December 8, 1941. The State of the Union speech before Congress was largely about the <a href="/wiki/National_security_of_the_United_States" title="National security of the United States">national security of the United States</a> and the threat to other democracies from <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">world war</a> that was being waged across the continents in the eastern hemisphere. In the speech, he made a break with the tradition of <a href="/wiki/United_States_non-interventionism" title="United States non-interventionism">United States non-interventionism</a> that had long been held in the United States. He outlined the U.S. role in helping allies already engaged in warfare.</p>
<p>In that context, he summarized the values of democracy behind the bipartisan consensus on international involvement that existed at the time. A famous quote from the speech prefaces those values: "As men do not live by bread alone, they do not fight by armaments alone." In the second half of the speech, he lists the benefits of democracy, which include economic opportunity, employment, social security, and the promise of "adequate health care". The first two freedoms, of speech and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_religion" title="Freedom of religion">religion</a>, are protected by the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. His inclusion of the latter two freedoms went beyond the traditional Constitutional values protected by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bill_of_Rights" title="United States Bill of Rights">U.S. Bill of Rights</a>. Roosevelt endorsed a broader human <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">right</a> to <a href="/wiki/Right_to_an_adequate_standard_of_living" title="Right to an adequate standard of living">economic security</a> and anticipated what would become known decades later as the "<a href="/wiki/Human_security" title="Human security">human security</a>" paradigm in <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>. He also included the "<a href="/wiki/Freedom_from_fear" title="Freedom from fear">freedom from fear</a>" against national aggression and took it to the new <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> he was setting up.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> (1914–18), the United States adopted a policy of isolationism and non-interventionism, having refused to endorse the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Versailles_Treaty" title="Versailles Treaty">Versailles Treaty</a> (1919) or formally enter the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Many Americans remembered the horrors of the Great War and, believing that their involvement in World War I had been a mistake, were adamantly against continued intervention in European affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodnar.2C_John_2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bodnar.2C_John_2010-2">[2]</a></sup> With the Neutrality Acts established after 1935, U.S. law banned the sale of armaments to countries that were at war and placed restrictions on travel with belligerent vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> began in 1939 with Germany's <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland" title="Invasion of Poland">invasion of Poland</a>, the United States was still committed to its non-interventionist ideals. Though Roosevelt, and a large segment of the population, supported the Allied cause, neutrality laws and a very strong isolationist element within Congress ensured that no substantial support could be given. With the revision of the Neutrality Act in 1939, Roosevelt adopted a "methods-short-of-war policy" whereby supplies and armaments could be given to European Allies, provided no declaration of war could be made and no troops committed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> By December 1940, Europe was largely at the mercy of Adolf Hitler and Germany's Nazi regime. With Germany's defeat of France in June 1940, Britain stood virtually alone against the military alliance of Germany, Italy, and Japan. Winston Churchill, as Prime Minister of Britain, called for Roosevelt and the United States to supply them with armaments in order to continue with the war effort.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1939_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1939 New York World's Fair">1939 New York World's Fair</a> had celebrated Four Freedoms - religion, speech, press and assembly - and commissioned <a href="/wiki/Leo_Friedlander" title="Leo Friedlander">Leo Friedlander</a> to create sculptures representing them. <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York_City" title="Mayor of New York City">Mayor of New York City</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiorello_La_Guardia" title="Fiorello La Guardia">Fiorello La Guardia</a> described the resulting statues as the "heart of the fair". Later Roosevelt would declare his own "Four Essential Freedoms" and call on <a href="/wiki/Walter_Russell" title="Walter Russell">Walter Russell</a> to create a <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_Monument" title="Four Freedoms Monument">Four Freedoms Monument</a></i> that was eventually dedicated at <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a> in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Four Freedoms Speech was given on January 6, 1941. Roosevelt's hope was to provide a rationale for why the United States should abandon the isolationist policies that emerged from World War I. In the address, Roosevelt critiqued Isolationism, saying: "No realistic American can expect from a dictator's peace international generosity, or return of true independence, or world disarmament, or freedom of expression, or freedom of religion–or even good business. Such a peace would bring no security for us or for our neighbors. "Those, who would give up essential liberty to purchase a little temporary safety, deserve neither liberty nor safety."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The speech coincided with the introduction of the Lend-Lease Bill, which promoted Roosevelt's plan to become the "arsenal of democracy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and support the Allies (mainly the British) with much-needed supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Furthermore, the speech established what would become the ideological basis for America's involvement in World War II, all framed in terms of individual rights and liberties that are the hallmark of American politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodnar.2C_John_2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bodnar.2C_John_2010-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The speech delivered by President Roosevelt incorporated the following text, known as the "Four Freedoms":</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"In the future days, which we seek to make secure, we look forward to a world founded upon four essential human freedoms.</p>
<p>The first is freedom of speech and expression—everywhere in the world.</p>
<p>The second is freedom of every person to worship God in his own way—everywhere in the world.</p>
<p>The third is freedom from want—which, translated into world terms, means economic understandings which will secure to every nation a healthy peacetime life for its inhabitants—everywhere in the world.</p>
<p>The fourth is freedom from fear—which, translated into world terms, means a world-wide reduction of armaments to such a point and in such a thorough fashion that no nation will be in a position to commit an act of physical aggression against any neighbor—anywhere in the world.</p>
That is no vision of a distant millennium. It is a definite basis for a kind of world attainable in our own time and generation. That kind of world is the very antithesis of the so-called new order of tyranny which the dictators seek to create with the crash of a bomb."—Franklin D. Roosevelt, excerpted from the State of the Union Address to the Congress, January 6, 1941</blockquote>

<p>The declaration of the Four Freedoms as a justification for war would resonate through the remainder of the war, and for decades longer as a frame of remembrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodnar.2C_John_2010_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bodnar.2C_John_2010-2">[2]</a></sup> The Freedoms became the staple of America's war aims, and the center of all attempts to rally public support for the war. With the creation of the Office of War Information (1942), as well as the famous paintings of Norman Rockwell, the Freedoms were advertised as values central to American life and examples of American exceptionalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Four Freedoms Speech was popular, and the goals were influential in the postwar politics. However, in 1941 the speech received heavy criticism from anti-war contingents and many pro-liberty advocates within Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Critics argued that the Four Freedoms were simply a charter for Roosevelt's New Deal, social reforms that had already created sharp divisions within Congress. Conservatives and classical liberals who opposed social programs and increased government intervention argued against Roosevelt's attempt to justify and depict the war as necessary for the defense of leftist policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the Freedoms did become a forceful aspect of American thought on the war, they were never the exclusive justification for the war. Polls and surveys conducted by the Office of War Information (OWI) revealed that "self-defense" of American values, and vengeance for Pearl Harbor were still the most prevalent reasons for war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Though Roosevelt sought to use the Four Freedoms as an ideological counter to fascism and a force to mobilize a nation apathetic to the war in Europe, records suggest that the American people were more concerned with their own personal experience than liberal humanitarianism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypocrisies">Hypocrisies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hypocrisies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 1942 radio address, President Roosevelt declared the Four Freedoms embodied "rights of men of every creed and every race, wherever they live."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Despite these words, many critics still claim that minorities were still treated with fewer rights.</p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a> fighting in World War II were forced to fight in segregated units, and African Americans in the United States experienced job discrimination, with one aviation plant spokesman of the time stating, "the Negro will be considered only as janitors and in other similar capacities...Regardless of their training as aircraft workers, we will not employ them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many Hispanics of the era were discriminated against, as well. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Suit_Riots" title="Zoot Suit Riots">Zoot Suit Riots</a> (1943) white sailors and Marines attacked <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Americans" title="Mexican Americans">Mexican Americans</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>.</p>
<p>On February 19, 1942, President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> authorized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_American_internment" title="Japanese American internment">Japanese American internment</a> with <a href="/wiki/Executive_Order_9066" title="Executive Order 9066">Executive Order 9066</a>, which allowed local military commanders to designate "military areas" as "exclusion zones," from which "any or all persons may be excluded." This power was used to declare that all people of Japanese ancestry were excluded from the entire Pacific coast, including all of California and much of Oregon, Washington and Arizona, except for those in internment camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-korematsu_roberts_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-korematsu_roberts-15">[15]</a></sup> By 1946, the United States had incarcerated 120,000 individuals of Japanese descent, of whom about 80,000 had been born in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations">United Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of the Four Freedoms became part of the personal mission undertaken by <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady</a> <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a> regarding her inspiration behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="United Nations Declaration of Human Rights">United Nations Declaration of Human Rights</a>, General Assembly Resolution 217A. Indeed, these Four Freedoms were explicitly incorporated into the preamble to the Universal Declaration of Human Rights which reads, "<i>Whereas</i> disregard and contempt for human rights have resulted in barbarous acts which have outraged the conscience of mankind, and the advent of a world in which human beings shall enjoy freedom of speech and belief and freedom from fear and want has been proclaimed the highest aspiration of the common people...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disarmament">Disarmament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Disarmament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FDR called for "a world-wide reduction of armaments" as a goal for "the future days, which we seek to make secure" but one that was "attainable in our own time and generation." More immediately, though, he called for a massive build-up of U.S. arms production: "Every realist knows that the democratic way of life is at this moment being directly assailed in every part of the world... The need of the moment is that our actions and our policy should be devoted primarily—almost exclusively—to meeting this foreign peril. ... [T]he immediate need is a swift and driving increase in our armament production. ... I also ask this Congress for authority and for funds sufficient to manufacture additional munitions and war supplies of many kinds, to be turned over to those nations which are now in actual war with aggressor nations. ... Let us say to the democracies...'" - Franklin D. Roosevelt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Franklin_D._Roosevelt_Four_Freedoms_Park">Franklin D. Roosevelt Four Freedoms Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Franklin D. Roosevelt Four Freedoms Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt_Four_Freedoms_Park" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt Four Freedoms Park">Franklin D. Roosevelt Four Freedoms Park</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt_Four_Freedoms_Park" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt Four Freedoms Park">Franklin D. Roosevelt Four Freedoms Park</a> is a park designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Louis_Kahn" title="Louis Kahn">Louis Kahn</a> for the south point of <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Island" title="Roosevelt Island">Roosevelt Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Park celebrates the famous speech, and text from the speech is inscribed on a granite wall in the final design of the Park.</p>

The awards were first presented in 1982 on the centenary of President Roosevelt's birth as well as the bicentenary of diplomatic relations between the United States and the Netherlands.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Freedoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Crowley" title="John Crowley">John Crowley</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_(novel)" title="Four Freedoms (novel)">Four Freedoms</a></i> (2009) is largely based on the themes of Roosevelt's speech.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FDR" title="FDR">FDR</a> commissioned sculptor <a href="/wiki/Walter_Russell" title="Walter Russell">Walter Russell</a> to design a monument to be dedicated to the first hero of the war. The <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_Monument" title="Four Freedoms Monument">Four Freedoms Monument</a></i> was created in 1941 and dedicated at <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a>, in New York City, in 1943.</li>
<li>Artist <a href="/wiki/Kindred_McLeary" title="Kindred McLeary">Kindred McLeary</a> painted <i>America the Mighty</i> (1941), also known as <i>Defense of Human Freedoms</i>, in the State Department's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman_Building" title="Harry S. Truman Building">Harry S. Truman Building</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Artist <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ballin" title="Hugo Ballin">Hugo Ballin</a> painted <i>The Four Freedoms</i> mural (1942) in the Council Chamber of the City Hall of <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>New Jersey muralist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lenson" title="Michael Lenson">Michael Lenson</a> (1903–72) painted <i>The Four Freedoms</i> mural (1943) for the Fourteenth Street School in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenson_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenson-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>Muralist <a href="/wiki/Anton_Refregier" title="Anton Refregier">Anton Refregier</a> painted the <i>History of San Francisco</i> (completed 1948) in the <a href="/wiki/Rincon_Center" title="Rincon Center">Rincon Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, California; panel 27 depicts the four freedoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>Artist Mildred Nungester Wolfe painted a four-panel <i>Four Freedoms</i> mural (complete 1959) depicting the four freedoms for a country store in <a href="/wiki/Richton,_Mississippi" title="Richton, Mississippi">Richton, Mississippi</a>. Those panels now hang in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Museum_of_Art" title="Mississippi Museum of Art">Mississippi Museum of Art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Allyn_Cox" title="Allyn Cox">Allyn Cox</a> painted four <i>Four Freedoms</i> murals (completed 1982) which hang in the Great Experiment Hall in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>; each of the four panels depicts allegorical figures representing the four freedoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the early 1990s, artist David McDonald reproduced Rockwell's <i>Four Freedoms</i> paintings as four large murals on the side of an old grocery building in downtown <a href="/wiki/Silverton,_Oregon" title="Silverton, Oregon">Silverton, Oregon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverton_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silverton-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Florida_International_University" title="Florida International University">Florida International University</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolfsonian" title="Wolfsonian">Wolfsonian</a> museum hosted the <i>Thoughts on Democracy</i> exhibition that displayed posters created by 60 leading contemporary artists and designers, invited to create a new graphic design inspired by American illustrator Norman Rockwell's <i>Four Freedoms</i> posters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>President Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech inspired a set of four <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_(Norman_Rockwell)" title="Four Freedoms (Norman Rockwell)">Four Freedoms</a></i> paintings by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Rockwell" title="Norman Rockwell">Norman Rockwell</a>. The four paintings were published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Evening_Post" title="The Saturday Evening Post">The Saturday Evening Post</a></i> on February 20, February 27, March 6, and March 13 in 1943. The paintings were accompanied in the magazine by matching <a href="/wiki/Essay" title="Essay">essays</a> on the Four Freedoms.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> toured Rockwell's <i>Four Freedoms</i> paintings around the country after their publication in 1943. The Four Freedoms Tour raised over $130,000,000 in <a href="/wiki/War_bond" title="War bond">war bond</a> sales.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Rockwell's <i>Four Freedoms</i> paintings were reproduced as <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Post_Office" title="United States Post Office">United States Post Office</a> in 1941,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_2840_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_2840-29">[29]</a></sup> in 1943,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_908_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_908-30">[30]</a></sup> in 1946,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_933_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_933-31">[31]</a></sup> and in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_2840_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_2840-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
