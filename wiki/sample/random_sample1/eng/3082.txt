<p>The <b>Dutch East Indies campaign</b> of 1941–42 was the conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> (present-day Indonesia) by forces from the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a> in the early days of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific Campaign</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Forces from the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> attempted unsuccessfully to defend the islands. The East Indies were targeted by the Japanese for their rich <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> resources which would become a vital asset during the war. The campaign and subsequent three and a half year <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_the_Dutch_East_Indies" title="Japanese occupation of the Dutch East Indies">Japanese occupation</a> was also a major factor in the end of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Empire" title="Dutch Empire">Dutch colonial rule</a> in the region.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_East_Indies_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The East Indies was one of Japan's primary targets if and when it went to war because the colony possessed abundant valuable resources, the most important of which were its rubber plantations and oil fields;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICECase_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICECase-12">[12]</a></sup> the colony was the fourth-largest exporter of oil in the world, behind the U.S., Iran, and Romania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICECase_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ICECase-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[A 1]</a></sup> The oil made the islands enormously important to the Japanese (see below), so they sought to secure the supply for themselves. They sent four fleet carriers and a light carrier along with the four <a href="/wiki/Fast_battleship" title="Fast battleship">fast battleships</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kong%C5%8D-class_battlecruiser" title="Kongō-class battlecruiser"><i>Kongō</i> class</a>, 13 <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cruiser" title="Heavy cruiser">heavy cruisers</a> and many light cruisers and destroyers to support their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibious_assault" title="Amphibious assault">amphibious assaults</a> in addition to conducting raids on cities, naval units and shipping in both that area and around the Indian Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Access to oil was one of the linchpins of the Japanese war effort, as Japan has no native source of oil;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aussie_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-aussie-15">[14]</a></sup> it could not even produce enough to meet even 10% of its needs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICECase_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ICECase-12">[12]</a></sup> even with the extraction of <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Fushun_process" title="Fushun process">Fushun process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> Japan quickly lost 93 percent of its oil supply after President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> issued an executive order on 26 July 1941 which froze all of Japan's U.S. assets and <a href="/wiki/Embargo" title="Embargo">embargoed</a> all oil exports to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> In addition, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_government_in_exile" title="Dutch government in exile">Dutch government in exile</a>, at the urging of the Allies and with the support of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmina_of_the_Netherlands" title="Wilhelmina of the Netherlands">Queen Wilhelmina</a>, broke its economic treaty with Japan and joined the embargo in August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aussie_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-aussie-15">[14]</a></sup> Japan's military and economic reserves included only a year and a half's worth of oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICECase_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-ICECase-12">[12]</a></sup> As a U.S. declaration of war against Japan was feared if the latter took the East Indies, the Japanese planned to eliminate the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Pacific_Fleet" title="United States Pacific Fleet">U.S. Pacific Fleet</a>, allowing them to overtake the islands; this led to the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Declarations_of_war">Declarations of war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_East_Indies_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Declarations of war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late November, the East Indies government began preparing for war: ships of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Navy" title="Royal Netherlands Navy">Royal Netherlands Navy</a> were sent to sea and the <a href="/wiki/Military_Aviation_of_the_Royal_Netherlands_East_Indies_Army" title="Military Aviation of the Royal Netherlands East Indies Army">KNIL Air Force</a> was mobilised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HJvMook_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-HJvMook-20">[19]</a></sup> On 4 December, three days after having decided on a policy of war against America, Britain and the Netherlands, the Japanese government decided instead to "treat the Netherlands as a quasi enemy until actual hostilities ... occur."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goto52_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goto52-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kluwer_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kluwer-22">[21]</a></sup> This was in the hopes that the Dutch would not preemptively destroy oil installations before the Japanese were ready to invade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goto52_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goto52-21">[20]</a></sup> On 8 December 1941, in a public proclamation, the Netherlands declared war on Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> By 7:00 a.m. on the day of the attack, the East Indies government had warned merchantmen at sea to make for the nearest port. At that hour, the governor general made a public announcement over the radio that the Netherlands "accepts the challenge and takes up arms against the Japanese Empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HJvMook_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-HJvMook-20">[19]</a></sup> Instructions had been telegraphed to the embassy in Tokyo at 2:30 a.m., even before news of the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a> had reached the Dutch government in London at 4:00. The instructions were only received on the evening of the next day, and the declaration of war was finally handed to the Japanese foreign minister, <a href="/wiki/Shigenori_T%C5%8Dg%C5%8D" title="Shigenori Tōgō">Shigenori Tōgō</a>, by the Dutch ambassador, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._C._Pabst&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. C. Pabst (page does not exist)">J. C. Pabst</a>, on the morning of 10 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HJvMook_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-HJvMook-20">[19]</a></sup> The Swedish ambassador agreed to handle Dutch interests for the duration of the conflict.</p>
<p>The Dutch declaration did not alter the Japanese decision, and the latter's declaration of war did not come until 11 January 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goto52_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goto52-21">[20]</a></sup> When Japan was charged with waging a "<a href="/wiki/War_of_aggression" title="War of aggression">war of aggression</a>" before the <a href="/wiki/International_Military_Tribunal_for_the_Far_East" title="International Military Tribunal for the Far East">International Military Tribunal for the Far East</a> in 1946, it was argued that her attitude towards the Netherlands proved otherwise, since the Dutch had declared war first. The tribunal rejected this, on the grounds that Japan's sole intention was "to give less time to the Netherlands for destroying oil wells."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goto52_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Goto52-21">[20]</a></sup> They found that the Netherlands' declaration was in self-defence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kluwer_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kluwer-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_East_Indies_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>General <a href="/wiki/Hisaichi_Terauchi" title="Hisaichi Terauchi">Hisaichi Terauchi</a>, commander of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Expeditionary_Army_Group" title="Southern Expeditionary Army Group">Southern Expeditionary Army Group</a>, began the campaign by sending the <a href="/wiki/Sixteenth_Army_(Japan)" title="Sixteenth Army (Japan)">16th Army</a> under command of General <a href="/wiki/Hitoshi_Imamura" title="Hitoshi Imamura">Hitoshi Imamura</a> to attack <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>. On 17 December, Japanese forces successfully landed on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miri" title="Miri">Miri</a>, an oil production centre in northern <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a>, with support from a battleship, an aircraft carrier, three cruisers and four destroyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, the Japanese forces launched air strikes on key areas and gained air superiority. Following the airstrikes, landings were made at several locations targeting airfields and other important points in the area. In addition to the landings at Miri, the Japanese forces made landings at <a href="/wiki/Seria" title="Seria">Seria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuching" title="Kuching">Kuching</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesselton" title="Jesselton">Jesselton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandakan" title="Sandakan">Sandakan</a> between 15 December 1941 and 19 January 1942. After these main objectives in Borneo were completed, the Japanese forces planned a three-pronged assault southward using three forces named Eastern Force, Center Force and Western Force. The aim of this assault was to capture the oil resources in the East Indies. The Eastern Force was to advance from <a href="/wiki/Jolo" title="Jolo">Jolo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao</a> and move on to capture <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Celebes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">Amboina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a>, while protecting the Center Force's flank. The Center Force was to capture oil fields and airfields in <a href="/wiki/Tarakan_Island" title="Tarakan Island">Tarakan Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balikpapan" title="Balikpapan">Balikpapan</a>. Both these forces would support the Western Force, which was to attack and capture the oil refineries and airfields in <a href="/wiki/Palembang" title="Palembang">Palembang</a>. The Japanese forces launched the assault on 11 January and landed at Tarakan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2WW_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-2WW-25">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>To coordinate the fight against the Japanese, the American, British, Dutch, and Australian forces combined all available land and sea forces under the <a href="/wiki/American-British-Dutch-Australian_Command" title="American-British-Dutch-Australian Command">American-British-Dutch-Australian Command</a> (ABDACOM or ABDA) banner. This command was activated on 15 January 1942, with the overall commander being British Field Marshal Sir <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Wavell,_1st_Earl_Wavell" title="Archibald Wavell, 1st Earl Wavell">Archibald Wavell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> The command structure had the American <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">Army Air Force</a> Lieutenant General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Brett_(military)" title="George Brett (military)">George Brett</a> as deputy commander, the British Lieutenant General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Royds_Pownall" title="Henry Royds Pownall">Henry Royds Pownall</a> as chief of staff; under them were the American Admiral <a href="/wiki/Thomas_C._Hart" title="Thomas C. Hart">Thomas C. Hart</a> as naval commander, the Dutch Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Hein_ter_Poorten" title="Hein ter Poorten">Hein ter Poorten</a> as ground forces commander, and the British Air Chief Marshal Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Peirse" title="Richard Peirse">Richard Peirse</a> as the air commander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Although the forces were combined, they had differing priorities: the British believed the defense of the territory of <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and the eastern entrances to the Indian Ocean (the route to <a href="/wiki/British_Ceylon" title="British Ceylon">British Ceylon</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a>) to be paramount, the Americans and Australians did not want a total penetration of Southwest Asia that would deprive them of bases necessary for any serious counterattack, and the Dutch considered Java and Sumatra, their "second homeland where [they] had been trading and living for over three centuries", to be the most important place to defend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even the combined forces could not stop or even slow the Japanese advance due to their much greater numbers; to face the Japanese attacking naval forces, the ABDA command had a conglomerate of ships drawn from any available units, which included the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Asiatic_Fleet" title="United States Asiatic Fleet">U.S. Asiatic Fleet</a> (fresh from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Philippines_(1941%E2%80%9342)" title="Battle of the Philippines (1941–42)">fall of the Philippines</a>), a few British and Australian surface ships, and Dutch units that had previously been stationed in the East Indies. Major forces included two <a href="/wiki/Seaplane_tender" title="Seaplane tender">seaplane tenders</a> (<a href="/wiki/USS_Langley_(CV-1)" title="USS Langley (CV-1)">USS <i>Langley</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Childs_(DD-241)" title="USS Childs (DD-241)"><i>Childs</i></a>), two heavy cruisers (<a href="/wiki/USS_Houston_(CA-30)" title="USS Houston (CA-30)">USS <i>Houston</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Exeter_(68)" title="HMS Exeter (68)">HMS <i>Exeter</i></a>), seven light cruisers (<a href="/wiki/HNLMS_De_Ruyter_(1935)" title="HNLMS De Ruyter (1935)">HNLMS <i>De Ruyter</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HNLMS_Java" title="HNLMS Java"><i>Java</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HNLMS_Tromp_(1937)" title="HNLMS Tromp (1937)"><i>Tromp</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Marblehead_(CL-12)" title="USS Marblehead (CL-12)">USS <i>Marblehead</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Boise_(CL-47)" title="USS Boise (CL-47)"><i>Boise</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMAS_Hobart_(1939)" title="HMAS Hobart (1939)">HMAS <i>Hobart</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMAS_Perth_(D29)" title="HMAS Perth (D29)"><i>Perth</i></a>), 22 destroyers, and, perhaps their greatest strength, 25 American and 16 Dutch submarines. Being based on Java, these ships had to take on the central and western prongs of the three-headed Japanese assault; the central force's combat ships, the <a href="/wiki/Light_aircraft_carrier" title="Light aircraft carrier">light carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Ry%C5%ABj%C5%8D" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Ryūjō"><i>Ryūjō</i></a>, the seaplane tenders <i>Sanyo Maru</i> and <i>Sanuki Maru</i>, three light cruisers and 16 destroyers, while the western force contained five heavy cruisers, and seven destroyers. In addition, four fleet carriers (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Akagi" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Akagi"><i>Akagi</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Kaga" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Kaga"><i>Kaga</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Hiry%C5%AB" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Hiryū"><i>Hiryū</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_S%C5%8Dry%C5%AB" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Sōryū"><i>Sōryū</i></a>) and the four <a href="/wiki/Kong%C5%8D-class_battlecruiser" title="Kongō-class battlecruiser"><i>Kongō</i>-class</a> battleships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison_1948.2C_pp._275.E2.80.93276_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morison_1948.2C_pp._275.E2.80.93276-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:50%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">
<p>The manner of the Japanese advance resembled the insidious yet irresistible clutching of multiple tentacles. Like some vast octopus it relied on strangling many small points rather than concentration on a vital organ. No one arm attempted to meet the entire strength of the ABDA fleet. Each fastened on a small portion of the enemy and, by crippling him locally, finished by killing the entire animal. [...] The Japanese spread their tentacles cautiously, never extending beyond the range of land-based aircraft unless they had carrier support. The distance of each advance was determined by the radius of fighter planes under their control. This range was generally less than 400 miles, but the Japanese made these short hops in surprisingly rapid succession. Amphibious operations, preceded by air strikes and covered by air power developed with terrifying regularity. Before the Allies had consolidated a new position, they were confronted with a system of air bases from which enemy aircraft operated on their front, flanks and even rear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Japanese forces were using Tarakan airfield as a forward airbase by 17 January, and Balikpapan was also captured a week later. However, the Dutch garrisons had destroyed the oil fields before they were captured by the Japanese in both cases. Several Japanese vessels were destroyed or damaged due to naval and air counterattacks from the Allied forces, but the defending Dutch battalions were overrun by the Japanese forces. By 28 January, the Japanese had taken control of the airfields at Balikpapan, and their aircraft were operating from them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2WW_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-2WW-25">[24]</a></sup> By the end of January, Japanese forces had captured parts of the Celebes and Dutch Borneo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vickers_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-vickers-30">[29]</a></sup> By February, Japanese forces had landed on <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> and encouraged a revolt in <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vickers_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-vickers-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the naval components of the allied force were crushed in the battles of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Java_Sea" title="Battle of the Java Sea">Java Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sunda_Strait" title="Battle of Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_the_Java_Sea" title="Second Battle of the Java Sea">Second Java Sea</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aussie_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-aussie-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aussie2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-aussie2-31">[30]</a></sup> the only American ship larger than a destroyer to survive was the old cruiser <i>Marblehead</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> In addition, the land forces on the islands were quickly overwhelmed and most major resistance was overcome within two months of the initial assaults, although a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Timor" title="Battle of Timor">guerrilla campaign in Timor</a> was successfully waged for a time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aussie_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-aussie-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aussie2_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-aussie2-31">[30]</a></sup> The ABDA command was dissolved at about 01:00 on 1 March, less than two months after its inception, by Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conrad_Emil_Lambert_Helfrich" title="Conrad Emil Lambert Helfrich">Conrad Emil Lambert Helfrich</a>, Governor-General of the East Indies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Allied operations in Indonesia (except <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>) were later controlled by the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_Area_(command)" title="South West Pacific Area (command)">South West Pacific Area command</a>, under General <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_East_Indies_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Indonesia" title="Japanese occupation of Indonesia">Japanese occupation of Indonesia</a></div>
<p>Allied forces did not attempt to retake the islands of Java, Sumatra, Timor, or Bali during the war. Japanese forces on those islands <a href="/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan" title="Surrender of Japan">surrendered</a> at the conclusion of World War II. Most of the Japanese military personnel and civilian colonial administrators were repatriated to Japan following the war, except for several hundred who were detained for investigations of war crimes, for which some were later put on trial. About 1,000 Japanese soldiers deserted from their units and assimilated into local communities. Many of these soldiers provided assistance to Indonesian Republican forces during the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Revolution" title="Indonesian National Revolution">Indonesian National Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
