<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nernst equation</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, the <b>Nernst equation</b> is an equation that relates the <a href="/wiki/Reduction_potential" title="Reduction potential">reduction potential</a> of an electrochemical reaction (<a href="/wiki/Half-cell" title="Half-cell">half-cell</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">full cell</a> reaction) to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">standard electrode potential</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">temperature</a>, and activities (often approximated by concentrations) of the chemical species undergoing reduction and oxidation. It is the most important equation in the field of electrochemistry. It is named after the German physical chemist who first formulated it, <a href="/wiki/Walther_Nernst" title="Walther Nernst">Walther Nernst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-8412-1572-3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-8412-1572-3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wahl2005_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wahl2005-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">R</span> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_gas_constant" title="Universal gas constant">universal gas constant</a>: <span class="texhtml"><i>R</i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000831447200000000♠</span>8.314<span style="margin-left:.25em;">472</span>(15) J K<sup>−1</sup> mol<sup>−1</sup></span></span></li>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span> is the temperature in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvins" title="Kelvins">kelvins</a></li>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> is the chemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activity_(chemistry)" title="Activity (chemistry)">activity</a> for the relevant species, where <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i><sub>Red</sub></span> is the activity of the reduced form and <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i><sub>Ox</sub></span> is the activity of the oxidized form. Similarly to equilibrium constants, activities are always measured with respect to the standard state (1M for solutes, 1 atm for gases). The activity of species x, <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i><sub>X</sub></span> , can be related to the physical concentrations <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i><sub>X</sub></span> via <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i><sub>X</sub> = <i>γ</i><sub>X</sub><i>c</i><sub>X</sub></span>, where <span class="texhtml"><i>γ</i><sub>X</sub></span> is the <a href="/wiki/Activity_coefficient" title="Activity coefficient">activity coefficient</a> of species X. Because activity coefficients tend to unity at low concentrations, activities in the Nernst equation are frequently replaced by simple concentrations.</li>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">F</span> is the <a href="/wiki/Faraday_constant" title="Faraday constant">Faraday constant</a>, the number of <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulombs</a> per <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of electrons: <span class="texhtml"><i>F</i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004964853399000000♠</span>9.648<span style="margin-left:.25em;">533</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">99</span>(24)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>4</sup> C mol<sup>−1</sup></span></span></li>

<p>The potential across the cell membrane that exactly opposes net diffusion of a particular ion through the membrane is called the Nernst potential for that ion. As seen above, the magnitude of the Nernst potential is determined by the ratio of the concentrations of that specific ion on the two sides of the membrane. The greater this ratio the greater the tendency for the ion to diffuse in one direction, and therefore the greater the Nernst potential required to prevent the diffusion.</p>

This takes transporters with unequal exchanges into account. See: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium-potassium_pump" title="Sodium-potassium pump">sodium-potassium pump</a> where the transport ratio would be <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">2</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Using_thermodynamics_.28chemical_potential.29">Using thermodynamics (chemical potential)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nernst_equation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Using thermodynamics (chemical potential)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Quantities here are given per molecule, not per mole, and so <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">Boltzmann constant</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>k</i></span> and the electron charge <span class="texhtml"><i>e</i></span> are used instead of the gas constant <span class="texhtml"><i>R</i></span> and Faraday's constant <span class="texhtml"><i>F</i></span>. To convert to the molar quantities given in most chemistry textbooks, it is simply necessary to multiply by Avogadro's number: <span class="texhtml"><i>R</i> = <i>kN</i><sub>A</sub></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>F</i> = <i>eN</i><sub>A</sub></span>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nernst_equation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In dilute solutions, the Nernst equation can be expressed directly in the terms of concentrations (since activity coefficients are close to unity). But at higher concentrations, the true activities of the ions must be used. This complicates the use of the Nernst equation, since estimation of non-ideal activities of ions generally requires experimental measurements.</p>
<p>The Nernst equation also only applies when there is no net current flow through the electrode. The activity of ions at the electrode surface changes <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_kinetics" title="Electrochemical kinetics">when there is current flow</a>, and there are additional <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> and resistive loss terms which contribute to the measured potential.</p>
<p>At very low concentrations of the potential-determining ions, the potential predicted by Nernst equation approaches toward <span class="texhtml">±∞</span>. This is physically meaningless because, under such conditions, the <a href="/wiki/Exchange_current_density" title="Exchange current density">exchange current density</a> becomes very low, and there is no thermodynamic equilibrium necessary for Nernst equation to hold. The electrode is called to be unpoised in such case. Other effects tend to take control of the electrochemical behavior of the system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_dependence_of_the_potential">Time dependence of the potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nernst_equation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Time dependence of the potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The expression of time dependence has been established by Karaoglanoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_to_related_scientific_domains">Significance to related scientific domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nernst_equation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Significance to related scientific domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The equation has been involved in the scientific controversy involving <a href="/wiki/Cold_fusion" title="Cold fusion">cold fusion</a>. The discoverers of cold fusion, Fleischmann and Pons, calculated that a palladium cathode immersed in a heavy water electrolysis cell could achieve up to 10<sup>27</sup> atmospheres of pressure on the surface of the cathode, enough pressure to cause spontaneous nuclear fusion. In reality, only 10,000–20,000 atmospheres were achieved. <a href="/wiki/John_R._Huizenga" title="John R. Huizenga">John R. Huizenga</a> claimed their original calculation was affected by a misinterpretation of Nernst equation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He cited a paper about Pd–Zr alloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huot1989_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huot1989-9">[9]</a></sup> The equation permits the extent of reaction between two redox systems to be calculated and can be used, for example, to decide whether a particular reaction will go to completion or not.</p>
<p>At equilibrium the emfs of the two half cells are equal. This enables Kc to be calculated hence the extent of the reaction.</p>
