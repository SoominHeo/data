<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_religion" title="Ancient Greek religion">ancient Greek religion</a>, <b>Nemesis</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Àà/ primary stress follows">Àà</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/…õ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">…õ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/…ô/ 'a' in 'about'">…ô</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/…™/ short 'i' in 'bid'">…™</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ŒùŒ≠ŒºŒµœÉŒπœÇ</span>), also called <b>Rhamnousia/Rhamnusia</b> ("the <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhamnous" title="Rhamnous">Rhamnous</a>") was the goddess who enacted <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">retribution</a> against those who succumb to <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubris</a> (arrogance before the gods). Another name was <b>Adrasteia</b>/<b><a href="/wiki/Adrestia" title="Adrestia">Adrestia</a></b>, meaning "the inescapable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Her <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> name/counterpart is <a href="/wiki/Invidia" title="Invidia">Invidia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Nemesis</i> is related to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word ŒΩŒ≠ŒºŒµŒπŒΩ <i>n√©mein</i>, meaning "to give what is due",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">Proto-Indo-European</a> <i>nem-</i> "distribute".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Divine retribution is a major theme in the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenic</a> world view, providing the unifying theme of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_tragedy" title="Greek tragedy">tragedies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a> and many other literary works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> states: "Also deadly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyx_(mythology)" title="Nyx (mythology)">Nyx</a> bore Nemesis an affliction to mortals subject to death" (<i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i>, 223, though perhaps an interpolated line). Nemesis appears in a still more concrete form in a fragment of the epic <i><a href="/wiki/Cypria" title="Cypria">Cypria</a></i>.</p>
<p>She is implacable justice: that of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Olympians" title="Twelve Olympians">Olympian</a> scheme of things, although it is clear she existed prior to him, as her images look similar to several other goddesses, such as <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(mythology)" title="Rhea (mythology)">Rhea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4150" data-file-width="1800" height="346" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg/150px-Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg/225px-Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg/300px-Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Statue_Nemesis_Louvre_Ma4873.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Nemesis</i>, Roman marble from Egypt, second century AD (<a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As the "Goddess of Rhamnous", Nemesis was honored and placated in an archaic sanctuary in the isolated district of Rhamnous, in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Attica" title="Attica">Attica</a>. There she was a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a>, the primeval river-ocean that encircles the world. <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a> noted her iconic statue there. It included a crown of stags and little <a href="/wiki/Nike_(mythology)" title="Nike (mythology)">Nikes</a> and was made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pheidias" title="Pheidias">Pheidias</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marathon" title="Battle of Marathon">Battle of Marathon</a> (490 BC), crafted from a block of <a href="/wiki/Paros" title="Paros">Parian</a> <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> brought by the overconfident Persians, who had intended to make a memorial <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a> after their expected victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>She is portrayed as a winged goddess wielding a whip or a dagger.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nemesis has been described as the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, but according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyginus" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a> she was a child of <a href="/wiki/Erebus" title="Erebus">Erebus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nyx" title="Nyx">Nyx</a>. She has also been described, by <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> as the daughter of Nyx alone. Her cult may have originated at <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrna</a>.</p>
<p>In some metaphysical mythology, Nemesis produced the egg from which hatched two sets of twins: <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">Helen of Troy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clytemnestra" title="Clytemnestra">Clytemnestra</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscuri" title="Dioscuri">Dioscuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castor_and_Pollux" title="Castor and Pollux">Castor and Pollux</a>. While many myths indicate <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leda_(mythology)" title="Leda (mythology)">Leda</a> to be the parents of <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">Helen of Troy</a>, the author of the compilation of myth called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliotheke" title="Bibliotheke">Bibliotheke</a></i> notes the possibility of Nemesis being the mother of Helen. Nemesis, to avoid Zeus, turns into a goose, but he turns into a swan and mates with her. Nemesis in her bird form lays an egg that is discovered in the marshes by a shepherd, who passes the egg to <a href="/wiki/Leda_(mythology)" title="Leda (mythology)">Leda</a>. It is in this way that <a href="/wiki/Leda_(mythology)" title="Leda (mythology)">Leda</a> comes to be the mother of <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">Helen of Troy</a>, as she kept the egg in a chest until it hatched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_and_deeds">Acts and deeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acts and deeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a respected goddess, Nemesis had brought much sorrow to mortals such as <a href="/wiki/Echo_(mythology)" title="Echo (mythology)">Echo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narcissus_(mythology)" title="Narcissus (mythology)">Narcissus</a>. Narcissus was a very beautiful and arrogant hunter from the territory of <a href="/wiki/Thespiae" title="Thespiae">Thespiae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boeotia</a>, who disdained the ones who loved him. Nemesis lured him to a pool where he saw his own reflection in the water and fell in love with it, not realizing it was only an image. He was unable to leave the beauty of his reflection and he eventually died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nemesis believed that no one should ever have too much good, and she had always cursed those who were blessed with countless gifts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortune_and_retribution">Fortune and retribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fortune and retribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>Nemesis</i> originally meant the distributor of fortune, neither good nor bad, simply in due proportion to each according to what was deserved.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Later, <i>nemesis</i> came to suggest the resentment caused by any disturbance of this right proportion, the sense of justice that could not allow it to pass unpunished.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O._Gruppe" title="O. Gruppe">O. Gruppe</a> (1906) and others connect the name with "to feel just resentment". From the fourth century onward, Nemesis, as the just balancer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortuna_(mythology)" title="Fortuna (mythology)">Fortune</a>'s chance, could be associated with <a href="/wiki/Tyche" title="Tyche">Tyche</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_tragedies" title="Greek tragedies">Greek tragedies</a> Nemesis appears chiefly as the avenger of crime and the punisher of <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubris</a>, and as such is akin to <a href="/wiki/At%C3%AB" title="At√´">At√´</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Erinyes" title="Erinyes">Erinyes</a>. She was sometimes called "<a href="/wiki/Adrestia" title="Adrestia">Adrasteia</a>", probably meaning "one from whom there is no escape"; her epithet <i>Erinys</i> ("implacable") is specially applied to Demeter and the <a href="/wiki/Phrygians" title="Phrygians">Phrygian</a> mother goddess, <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_cult">Local cult</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Local cult">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A festival called <b>Nemeseia</b> (by some identified with the <b>Genesia</b>) was held at <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. Its object was to avert the nemesis of the dead, who were supposed to have the power of punishing the living, if their cult had been in any way neglected (<a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electra_(Sophocles)" title="Electra (Sophocles)">Electra</a>,</i> 792; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._Rohde" title="E. Rohde">E. Rohde</a>, <i>Psyche,</i> 1907, i. 236, note I).</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrna</a> there were two manifestations of Nemesis, more akin to <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a> than to Artemis. The reason for this duality is hard to explain. It is suggested that they represent two aspects of the goddess, the kindly and the implacable, or the goddesses of the old city and the new city refounded by Alexander. The martyrology <i>Acts of <a href="/wiki/Pionius" title="Pionius">Pionius</a></i>, set in the "<a href="/wiki/Decius" title="Decius">Decian persecution</a>" of AD 250‚Äì51, mentions a lapsed Smyrnan Christian who was attending to the sacrifices at the altar of the temple of these Nemeses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rome">Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nemesis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HadrianNemesis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1130" data-file-width="2269" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/HadrianNemesis.jpg/220px-HadrianNemesis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/HadrianNemesis.jpg/330px-HadrianNemesis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/HadrianNemesis.jpg/440px-HadrianNemesis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HadrianNemesis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Nemesis</i> on a brass <a href="/wiki/Sestertius" title="Sestertius">sestertius</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a>, struck at <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> AD 136</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Pax-Nemesis</i> was worshipped on occasion at <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> by victorious generals, and in imperial times was the patroness of <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiators</a> and of the <i>venatores</i>, who fought in the arena with wild beasts, and was one of the <a href="/wiki/Tutelary_deity" title="Tutelary deity">tutelary deities</a> of the drilling-ground (<i>Nemesis campestris</i>). Sometimes, but rarely, seen on imperial coinage, mainly under <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a>. In the third century AD there is evidence of the belief in an all-powerful <i>Nemesis-Fortuna</i>. She was worshipped by a society called Hadrian's freedmen. The poet <a href="/wiki/Mesomedes" title="Mesomedes">Mesomedes</a> wrote a hymn to Nemesis in the early second century AD, where he addressed her</p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<p>Nemesis, winged balancer of life,<br/>
dark-faced goddess, daughter of Justice,</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>and mentioned her "adamantine bridles" that restrain "the frivolous insolences of mortals".</p>
<p>In early times the representations of Nemesis resembled Aphrodite, who sometimes bears the epithet Nemesis.</p>
<p>Later, as the maiden goddess of proportion and the <a href="/wiki/Retributive_justice" title="Retributive justice">avenger of crime</a>, she has as attributes a <a href="/wiki/Measuring_rod" title="Measuring rod">measuring rod</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tally_stick" title="Tally stick">tally stick</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Bridle" title="Bridle">bridle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale#Symbolism" title="Weighing scale">scales</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">sword</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Scourge" title="Scourge">scourge</a>, and she rides in a <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariot</a> drawn by <a href="/wiki/Griffin" title="Griffin">griffins</a>.</p>
<p>Nemesis is also known to have been called "<a href="/wiki/Adrestia" title="Adrestia">Adrastia</a>". Ammianus Marcellinus includes her in a digression on Justice following his description of the death of Gallus Caesar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
