<p>The <b>Nymphalidae</b> are the largest <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> with about 6,000 species distributed throughout most of the world. These are usually medium-sized to large butterflies. Most species have a reduced pair of forelegs and many hold their colourful wings flat when resting. They are also called <b>brush-footed butterflies</b> or <b>four-footed butterflies</b>, because they are known to stand on only four legs while the other two are curled up; in some species, these forelegs have a brush-like set of hairs which gives this family its other common name. Many species are brightly coloured and include popular species such as the <a href="/wiki/Apaturinae" title="Apaturinae">emperors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monarch_butterfly" title="Monarch butterfly">monarch butterfly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nymphalini" title="Nymphalini">admirals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aglais" title="Aglais">tortoiseshells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heliconiini" title="Heliconiini">fritillaries</a>. However, the under wings are, in contrast, often dull and in some species look remarkably like dead leaves, or are much paler, producing a <a href="/wiki/Crypsis" title="Crypsis">cryptic</a> effect that helps the butterflies blend into their surroundings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphalidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rafinesque<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> introduced the name Nymphalia as a subfamily name in diurnal Lepidoptera. Rafinesque did not include <i><a href="/wiki/Nymphalis" title="Nymphalis">Nymphalis</a></i> among the listed genera, but <i>Nymphalis</i> was unequivocally implied in the formation of the name (Code Article 11.7.1.1). The attribution of the Nymphalidae to Rafinesque has now been widely adopted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphalidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For terms see <a href="/wiki/External_morphology_of_Lepidoptera" title="External morphology of Lepidoptera">External morphology of Lepidoptera</a>.</p>
<p>In the adult butterflies, the first pair of legs is small or reduced,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> giving the family the other names of four-footed or brush-footed butterflies. The <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> are hairy or spiky with projections on the head, and the <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">chrysalids</a> have shiny spots.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forewing" title="Forewing">forewings</a> have the submedial vein (vein 1) unbranched and in one subfamily forked near the base; the medial vein has three branches, veins 2, 3, and 4; veins 5 and 6 arise from the points of junction of the discocellulars; the subcostal vein and its continuation beyond the apex of cell, vein 7, has never more than four branches, veins 8–11; 8 and 9 always arise from vein 7, 10, and 11 sometimes from vein 7 but more often free, i.e., given off by the subcostal vein before apex of the cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindwing" title="Hindwing">hindwings</a> have internal (1a) and precostal veins. The cell in both wings is closed or open, often closed in the fore, open in the hindwing. The dorsal margin of the hindwing is channelled to receive the abdomen in many of the forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctb_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ctb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a> always have two grooves on the underside; the club is variable in shape. Throughout the family, the front pair of legs in the male, and with three exceptions (<i><a href="/wiki/Libythea" title="Libythea">Libythea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudergolis" title="Pseudergolis">Pseudergolis</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Calinaga" title="Calinaga">Calinaga</a></i>) in the female also, is reduced in size and functionally impotent; in some, the <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">atrophy</a> of the forelegs is considerable, e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danainae" title="Danainae">Danainae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satyrinae" title="Satyrinae">Satyrinae</a>. In many of the forms of these subfamilies, the forelegs are kept pressed against the underside of the <a href="/wiki/Thorax_(insect_anatomy)" title="Thorax (insect anatomy)">thorax</a>, and are in the male often very inconspicuous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctb_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ctb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_phylogeny">Systematics and phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphalidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics and phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogeny</a> of the Nymphalidae is complex. Several <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> are of unclear position, reflecting the fact that some <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">subfamilies</a> were formerly well-recognized as distinct families due to insufficient study.</p>
<p>The five main <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> within the family are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wahlberg_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wahlberg-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>libytheine clade</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basal_(biology)" title="Basal (biology)">basal</a>)</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Libytheinae" title="Libytheinae">Libytheinae</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snout_butterfly" title="Snout butterfly">snout butterflies</a>, earlier treated as the distinct family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libytheidae" title="Libytheidae">Libytheidae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The <b>danaine clade</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basal_(biology)" title="Basal (biology)">basal</a>)</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danainae" title="Danainae">Danainae</a> (milkweed butterflies, earlier treated as the distinct family Danaidae)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Host plant families include <a href="/wiki/Apocynaceae" title="Apocynaceae">Apocynaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asclepiadoideae" title="Asclepiadoideae">Asclepiadoideae</a> (subfamily of <a href="/wiki/Apocynaceae" title="Apocynaceae">Apocynaceae</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Moraceae" title="Moraceae">Moraceae</a>.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ithomiini" title="Ithomiini">Ithomiini</a> (about 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropic_ecozone" title="Neotropic ecozone">neotropical</a> species, sometimes considered a subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ithomiinae" title="Ithomiinae">Ithomiinae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Most species have long wings, and some have transparent wings. Host plants are in the families <a href="/wiki/Apocynaceae" title="Apocynaceae">Apocynaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gesneriaceae" title="Gesneriaceae">Gesneriaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solanaceae" title="Solanaceae">Solanaceae</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tellervini" title="Tellervini">Tellervini</a> (about 6–10 species in <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, sometimes considered a subfamily <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tellervinae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tellervinae (page does not exist)">Tellervinae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Caterpillars resemble those of the Danainae and feed on Apocynaceae.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <b>satyrine clade</b></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calinaginae" title="Calinaginae">Calinaginae</a> (about six species, restricted to the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Mimics of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danainae" title="Danainae">Danainae</a>, they are restricted to host plants in the family <a href="/wiki/Moraceae" title="Moraceae">Moraceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eob_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-eob-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charaxinae" title="Charaxinae">Charaxinae</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Tropical <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">canopy</a> butterflies, the caterpillars often have head spines or projections. Mostly edible, species have some <a href="/wiki/Batesian_mimicry" title="Batesian mimicry">Batesian mimics</a>. Host plants are in the families <a href="/wiki/Annonaceae" title="Annonaceae">Annonaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celastraceae" title="Celastraceae">Celastraceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convolvulaceae" title="Convolvulaceae">Convolvulaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flacourtiaceae" title="Flacourtiaceae">Flacourtiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauraceae" title="Lauraceae">Lauraceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">Myrtaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piperaceae" title="Piperaceae">Piperaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhamnaceae" title="Rhamnaceae">Rhamnaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santalaceae" title="Santalaceae">Santalaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sapindaceae" title="Sapindaceae">Sapindaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eob_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-eob-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Morphinae" title="Morphinae">Morphinae</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Amathusiini" title="Amathusiini">Amathusiini</a>, sometimes considered a subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amathusiinae" title="Amathusiinae">Amathusiinae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Include the spectacular neotropical <i><a href="/wiki/Morpho" title="Morpho">Morpho</a></i>, its food plants include the <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">Arecaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bignoniaceae" title="Bignoniaceae">Bignoniaceae</a>, Fabaceae, <a href="/wiki/Menispermaceae" title="Menispermaceae">Menispermaceae</a>, Poaceae, and Sapindaceae.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brassolini" title="Brassolini">Brassolini</a> (owls, neotropical with 70–80 species, mostly <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a>, sometimes considered a subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brassolinae" title="Brassolinae">Brassolinae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Host plants in the families Arecaceae, <a href="/wiki/Bromeliaceae" title="Bromeliaceae">Bromeliaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliconiaceae" title="Heliconiaceae">Heliconiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musaceae" title="Musaceae">Musaceae</a>, and Poaceae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eob_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-eob-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Satyrinae" title="Satyrinae">Satyrinae</a> (satyrs and browns, earlier treated as distinct family Satyridae)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Host plants are in the families Arecaceae, <a href="/wiki/Araceae" title="Araceae">Araceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">Cyperaceae</a>, Heliconiaceae, Poaceae, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selaginellaceae" title="Selaginellaceae">Selaginellaceae</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <b>heliconiine clade</b> (<a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> of the nymphaline clade, excludes former <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribes</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblidini" title="Biblidini">Biblidini</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrestini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cyrestini (page does not exist)">Cyrestini</a>, and tribes Pseudergolini and Coeini)</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heliconiinae" title="Heliconiinae">Heliconiinae</a> (earlier treated as distinct family Heliconiidae)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Colourful tropical butterflies, they are noted for <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">Müllerian mimicry</a>. All species use host plants in the family <a href="/wiki/Passifloraceae" title="Passifloraceae">Passifloraceae</a>.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acraeini" title="Acraeini">Acraeini</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a>, but some species in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, sometimes considered a family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acraeinae" title="Acraeinae">Acraeinae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Host plants are in the families <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a>, Passifloraceae, <a href="/wiki/Sterculiaceae" title="Sterculiaceae">Sterculiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiliaceae" title="Tiliaceae">Tiliaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urticaceae" title="Urticaceae">Urticaceae</a>.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Limenitidinae" title="Limenitidinae">Limenitidinae</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The <b>nymphaline clade</b> (<a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> of the heliconiine clade, also includes tribes <a href="/wiki/Coeini" title="Coeini">Coeini</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudergolini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pseudergolini (page does not exist)">Pseudergolini</a>)</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Apaturinae" title="Apaturinae">Apaturinae</a> (mostly tropical)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Host plants are in the family <a href="/wiki/Ulmaceae" title="Ulmaceae">Ulmaceae</a>. Caterpillars are smooth with bifid tails and horns on the head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eob_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-eob-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biblidinae" title="Biblidinae">Biblidinae</a> (formerly in <a href="/wiki/Limenitidinae" title="Limenitidinae">Limenitidinae</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cyrestinae" title="Cyrestinae">Cyrestinae</a> (formerly in Limenitidinae)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nymphalinae" title="Nymphalinae">Nymphalinae</a> (a large subfamily that sometimes includes the Limenitidinae and <a href="/wiki/Biblidinae" title="Biblidinae">Biblidinae</a>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Some species <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera_migration" title="Lepidoptera migration">migrate</a>. Caterpillars are sometimes covered in spines. Host plants include Acanthaceae, <a href="/wiki/Caprifoliaceae" title="Caprifoliaceae">Caprifoliaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convolvulaceae" title="Convolvulaceae">Convolvulaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a>, Fagaceae, <a href="/wiki/Flacourtiaceae" title="Flacourtiaceae">Flacourtiaceae</a>, Lamiaceae, <a href="/wiki/Loranthaceae" title="Loranthaceae">Loranthaceae</a>, Moraceae, <a href="/wiki/Plantaginaceae" title="Plantaginaceae">Plantaginaceae</a>, Poaceae, Rubiaceae, Rutaceae, <a href="/wiki/Salicaceae" title="Salicaceae">Salicaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapindaceae" title="Sapindaceae">Sapindaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scrophulariaceae" title="Scrophulariaceae">Scrophulariaceae</a>, Urticaceae, and <a href="/wiki/Verbenaceae" title="Verbenaceae">Verbenaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eob_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-eob-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphalidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The trait for which these butterflies are most known is the use of only four legs; the reason their forelegs have become vestigial is not yet completely clear. Some suggest the forelegs are used to amplify the sense of smell, because some species possess a brush-like set of soft hair called <a href="/wiki/Seta" title="Seta">setae</a>, which has led researchers to believe the forelegs are used to improve signaling and communication between the species, while standing in the other four. This ability proves useful in terms of reproduction and the overall health of the species, and it is the leading theory so far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
