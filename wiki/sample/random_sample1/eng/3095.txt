<p><b>Sir Nevill Francis Mott</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_Honour" title="Companion of Honour">CH</a> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup> (30 September 1905 – 8 August 1996) was an English <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a> who won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Physics" title="Nobel Prize for Physics">Nobel Prize for Physics</a> in 1977 for his work on the electronic structure of magnetic and <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">disordered</a> systems, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous_semiconductors" title="Amorphous semiconductors">amorphous semiconductors</a>. The award was shared with <a href="/wiki/Philip_Warren_Anderson" title="Philip Warren Anderson">Philip W. Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hasbrouck_Van_Vleck" title="John Hasbrouck Van Vleck">J. H. Van Vleck</a>. The three had conducted loosely related research. Mott and Anderson clarified the reasons why magnetic or amorphous materials can some times be metallic and some times insulating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_life">Education and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nevill_Francis_Mott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mott was born in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to Lilian Mary Reynolds and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Francis_Mott" title="Charles Francis Mott">Charles Francis Mott</a> and grew up first in the village of <a href="/wiki/Giggleswick" title="Giggleswick">Giggleswick</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, where his father was Senior Science Master at <a href="/wiki/Giggleswick_School" title="Giggleswick School">Giggleswick School</a>. His mother also taught Maths at the School. The family moved (due to his father's jobs) first to Staffordshire, then to Chester and finally Liverpool, where his father had been appointed Director of Education. Mott was at first educated at home by his mother, who was a Cambridge Mathematics Tripos graduate. His parents had met in the <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratory" title="Cavendish Laboratory">Cavendish Laboratory</a>, when both were engaged in physics research. At age ten, he began formal education at <a href="/wiki/Clifton_College" title="Clifton College">Clifton College</a> in Bristol, then at <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="St John's College, Cambridge">St John's College, Cambridge</a>, where he read the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_Mathematical_Tripos" title="Cambridge Mathematical Tripos">Mathematics Tripos</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_research">Career and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nevill_Francis_Mott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mott was appointed a Lecturer in the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Physics_and_Astronomy,_University_of_Manchester" title="School of Physics and Astronomy, University of Manchester">Physics Department</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> in 1929. He returned to Cambridge in 1930 as a Fellow and lecturer of <a href="/wiki/Gonville_and_Caius_College,_Cambridge" title="Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge">Gonville and Caius College</a>, and in 1933 moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bristol" title="University of Bristol">University of Bristol</a> as Melville Wills Professor in Theoretical Physics.</p>
<p>In 1948 he became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Overton_Wills" title="Henry Overton Wills">Henry Overton Wills</a> Professor of Physics and Director of the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Herbert_Wills" title="Henry Herbert Wills">Henry Herbert Wills</a> Physical Laboratory at Bristol. In 1954 he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Professor_of_Physics" title="Cavendish Professor of Physics">Cavendish Professor of Physics</a> at Cambridge, a post he held until 1971. He was instrumental in the painful cancellation of the planned <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> because of its very high cost. He also served as Master of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonville_and_Caius_College" title="Gonville and Caius College">Gonville and Caius College</a>, 1959–1966.</p>
<p>His early works were on the theoretical analysis of collisions in gases, notably the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_line" title="Hydrogen line">collision with spin flip of an electron against a hydrogen atom</a>, which would stimulate subsequent works by André Blandin and <a href="/wiki/Jun_Kondo" title="Jun Kondo">Jun Kondo</a> about similar effects between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_band" title="Conduction band">conduction electrons</a>, as well as magnetic properties in metals. This sort of activity led Mott to writing two books. The first one, which was edited together with <a href="/wiki/Ian_Sneddon" title="Ian Sneddon">Ian Sneddon</a>, gives a simple and clear description of quantum mechanics, with an emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger equation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Position_and_momentum_space" title="Position and momentum space">real space</a>. The second describes atomic and electronic collisions in gases, using the rotational symmetry of <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">electronic states</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hartree%E2%80%93Fock_method" title="Hartree–Fock method">Hartree–Fock method</a>.</p>
<p>But already in the middle of the 1930s, Mott's interests had broadened to include solid states, leading to two more books that would have a great impact on the development of the field in the years prior and after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In 1936, <i>Theory of the Properties of Metals and Alloys</i> (written together with H. Jones) describes a simplified framework which led to rapid progresses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (October 2013)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The concept of <a href="/wiki/Nearly_free_electron_model" title="Nearly free electron model">nearly free valence electrons</a> in metallic alloys explained the special stability of the <a href="/wiki/Hume-Rothery_rules" title="Hume-Rothery rules">Hume-Rothery phases</a> if the <a href="/wiki/Fermi_surface" title="Fermi surface">Fermi sphere</a> of the <i>sp</i> <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">Valence electron</a>, treated as free, would be scattered by the <a href="/wiki/Brillouin_zone" title="Brillouin zone">Brillouin zone</a> boundaries of the atomic structure. The description of the impurities in metals by the <a href="/wiki/Thomas%E2%80%93Fermi_model" title="Thomas–Fermi model">Thomas Fermi approximation</a> would explain why such impurities would not interact at long range. Finally the delocalisation of the <a href="/wiki/D_electron_count" title="D electron count">valence <i>d</i> electrons</a> in <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transitional metals</a> and alloys would explain the possibility for the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moments</a> of atoms to be expressed as fractions of <a href="/wiki/Bohr_magneton" title="Bohr magneton">Bohr magnetons</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferro</a> or <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetism" title="Antiferromagnetism">antiferromagnetic</a> coupling at short range. This last contribution, produced at the first international conference on magnetism, held in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> in May 1939, reinforced similar points of view defended at the time in France by the future Nobel laureate <a href="/wiki/Louis_N%C3%A9el" title="Louis Néel">Louis Néel</a>. In 1949, Mott suggested to <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Friedel" title="Jacques Friedel">Jacques Friedel</a> to use the approach developed together with Marvey for a more accurate description of the <a href="/wiki/Electric-field_screening" title="Electric-field screening">electric-field screening</a> of the impurity in a metal, leading to the characteristic long range charge oscillations. Friedel also used the concept developed in that book of virtual bound level to describe a situation when the atomic potential considered is not quite strong enough to create a (real) bound level of symmetry e ≠ o.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (October 2013)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The consequences of these remarks on the more exact approaches of cohesion in rp as well as d metals were mostly developed by his students in Orsay.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (October 2013)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The second book, with <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Wilfred_Gurney" title="Ronald Wilfred Gurney">Ronald Wilfred Gurney</a>, <i>On the Physical Chemistry of Solids</i> was more diverse. It treated notably of the oxidation of metals at low temperatures, where it described the growth of the oxide layer as due to the electric field developed between the metal and absorbed oxygen ions, which could force the way of metallic or oxygen ions through a disordered oxide layer. The book also analysed the photographic reactions in ionic silver compound in terms of precipitation of silver ions into metallic clusters.</p>
<p>This second field had a direct and long lasting consequence on the research activity of John (Jack) Mitchell. Mott's accomplishments include explaining theoretically the effect of light on a <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">photographic emulsion</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Latent_image" title="Latent image">latent image</a>). His work on oxidation, besides fostering new research in the field (notably by J. Bénard and <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Cabrera" title="Nicolás Cabrera">Nicolás Cabrera</a>), was the root of the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> produced in semiconductors by gradients in the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor)" title="Doping (semiconductor)">donor and acceptor impurities</a>.</p>
<p>When Mott returned to Bristol after the war, (during that period, he worked on the role of plastic deformation on the progression of fracture cracks), his having met and hired of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Charles_Frank" title="Frederick Charles Frank">Frederick Charles Frank</a> led both of them to develop, with the help of others such as <a href="/wiki/Frank_Nabarro" title="Frank Nabarro">Frank Nabarro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cottrell" title="Alan Cottrell">Alan Cottrell</a>, to attack with the field of <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">dislocations</a>, in which Bristol shone with a new vigor, especially at the end of the 1940s. If Mott only produced early and somewhat minor contributions to that field, notably on alloy hardening with Nabarro and on the topology of a dislocation network lowering the apparent elastic constants of a crystal, there is no doubt that Mott's enthusiasm played its role in the three major steps forward in the field by F. C. Frank on crystal growth and plasticity and later, in Cambridge, by P. Hirsch on the thin film electron microscopy.</p>
<p>At the same time, however, Mott started playing around <a href="/wiki/Electronic_correlation" title="Electronic correlation">electronic correlations</a> and their possible role in <a href="/wiki/DLVO_theory" title="DLVO theory">Verwey's compounds</a> such as nickel oxides which could switch from metals to insulators under various physical conditions (transition of substances from metallic to nonmetallic states (<a href="/wiki/Mott_transition" title="Mott transition">Mott transition</a>). The term <a href="/wiki/Mott_insulator" title="Mott insulator">Mott insulator</a> is also named for him, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Mott_polynomials" title="Mott polynomials">Mott polynomials</a>, which he introduced.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nevill_Francis_Mott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>N. F. Mott revived the old <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_Magazine" title="Philosophical Magazine">Philosophical Magazine</a> and transformed it into a lively publication essentially centred on the then-new field of solid state physics, attracting writers, readers and general interest on a wide scale. After receiving a paper on point defects in crystals by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Seitz" title="Frederick Seitz">Frederick Seitz</a> that was obviously too long for Phil. Mag, Mott decided to create a new publication, <a href="/wiki/Advances_in_Physics" title="Advances in Physics">Advances in Physics</a> for such review papers. Both publications are still active in 2013.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nevill_Francis_Mott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1977, Nevill Mott was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Philip_Warren_Anderson" title="Philip Warren Anderson">Philip Warren Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hasbrouck_Van_Vleck" title="John Hasbrouck Van Vleck">John Hasbrouck Van Vleck</a> "for their fundamental theoretical investigations of the electronic structure of magnetic and disordered systems." The news of having won the Nobel Prize received Mott while having lunch at restaurant <i>Die Sonne</i> in <a href="/wiki/Marburg" title="Marburg">Marburg</a>, Germany, during a visit to fellow solid state scientist at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marburg_University" title="Marburg University">Marburg University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mott was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_1936" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 1936">Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 1936</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup> Mott served as president of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Physics" title="Institute of Physics">Physical Society</a> in 1957. In the early 1960s he was chairman of the British <a href="/wiki/Pugwash_Conferences_on_Science_and_World_Affairs" title="Pugwash Conferences on Science and World Affairs">Pugwash</a> group. He was knighted in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mott received an honorary Doctorate from <a href="/wiki/Heriot-Watt_University" title="Heriot-Watt University">Heriot-Watt University</a> in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1981, Mott became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/World_Cultural_Council" title="World Cultural Council">World Cultural Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>He continued to work until he was about ninety. He was made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_Honour" title="Companion of Honour">Companion of Honour</a> in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nevill_Francis_Mott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mott was married to Ruth Eleanor Horder, and had two daughters, Elizabeth and Alice. He died in <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes" title="Milton Keynes">Milton Keynes</a> in Buckinghamshire. His autobiography, 'A Life in Science', was published in 1986 by Taylor &amp; Francis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
