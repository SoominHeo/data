<p><b>Nesebar</b> (often transcribed as <b>Nessebar</b> and sometimes as <b>Nesebur</b>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg">Несебър</span>, pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Bulgarian_and_Macedonian" title="Help:IPA for Bulgarian and Macedonian">[nɛˈsɛbɐr]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Thracian_language" title="Thracian language">Thracian</a>: <i>Melsambria</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Μεσημβρία</span>, <i>Mesembria</i>) is an ancient <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Bulgaria" title="List of cities and towns in Bulgaria">town</a> and one of the major seaside resorts on the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Black_Sea_Coast" title="Bulgarian Black Sea Coast">Bulgarian Black Sea Coast</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Burgas_Province" title="Burgas Province">Burgas Province</a>. It is the administrative centre of the homonymous <a href="/wiki/Nesebar_Municipality" title="Nesebar Municipality">Nesebar Municipality</a>. Often referred to as the "<i>Pearl of the Black Sea</i>", Nesebar is a rich city-museum defined by more than three millennia of ever-changing history.</p>
<p>It is one of the most prominent tourist destinations and seaports on the Black Sea, in what has become a popular area with several large resorts—the largest, <a href="/wiki/Sunny_Beach" title="Sunny Beach">Sunny Beach</a>, is situated immediately to the north of Nesebar.</p>
<p>Nesebar has on several occasions found itself on the frontier of a threatened empire, and as such it is a town with a rich history. The ancient part of the town is situated on a peninsula (previously an island) connected to the mainland by a narrow man-made <a href="/wiki/Isthmus" title="Isthmus">isthmus</a>, and it bears evidence of occupation by a variety of different civilisations over the course of its existence. Its abundance of historic buildings prompted <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> to include Nesebar in its list of <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a> in 1983.</p>
<p>As of December 2009, the town has a population of 11,626 inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statistika_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-statistika-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The settlement was known in Greek as <i>Mesembria</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Μεσήμβρια</span>), sometimes mentioned as <i>Mesambria</i> or <i>Melsembria</i>, the latter meaning the city of Melsas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> According to a reconstruction the name might derive from Thracian <i>Melsambria</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the Thracian origin of that name seems to be doubtful. Moreover, the tradition pertaining to Melsas, as founder of the city is tenuous and belongs to a cycle of etymological legends abundant among Greek cities. It also appears that the story of Melsas was a latter reconstruction of the Hellenistic era, when Mesembria was an important coastal city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bulgarian archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Lyuba_Ognenova-Marinova" title="Lyuba Ognenova-Marinova">Lyuba Ognenova-Marinova</a> led six underwater archaeological expeditions for the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Bulgarian Academy of Sciences">Bulgarian Academy of Sciences</a> (BAS) between 1961 and 1972<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilieva_.26_Preshenov_.282005.29_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ilieva_.26_Preshenov_.282005.29-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in the waters along the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Black_Sea_Coast" title="Bulgarian Black Sea Coast">Bulgarian Black Sea Coast</a>. Her work led to the identification of five chronological periods of urbanization on the peninsula surrounding Nesebar through the end of the second millennium B.C., which included the Thracian protopolis, the Greek colony Mesambria, a Roman-ruled village to the <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">Early Christian Era</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Medieval</a> settlement and a <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Renaissance era</a> town, known as Mesemvria or Nessebar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilieva_.26_Preshenov_.282005.29_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ilieva_.26_Preshenov_.282005.29-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originally a <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracian</a> settlement, known as <i>Menebria</i>, the town became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_colony" title="Greek colony">Greek colony</a> when settled by <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">Dorians</a> from <a href="/wiki/Megara" title="Megara">Megara</a> at the beginning of the 6th century BC, and was an important trading centre from then on and a rival of Apollonia (<a href="/wiki/Sozopol" title="Sozopol">Sozopol</a>). It remained the only <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">Dorian</a> colony along the Black Sea coast, as the rest were typical <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionian</a> colonies. At 425-424 BC the town joined the <a href="/wiki/Delian_League" title="Delian League">Delian League</a>, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Athens" title="Classical Athens">Athens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Remains from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> period include the <a href="/wiki/Acropolis" title="Acropolis">acropolis</a>, a temple of <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Agora" title="Agora">agora</a>. A wall which formed part of the fortifications can still be seen on the north side of the peninsula. Bronze and silver coins were minted in the city since the 5th century BC and gold coins since the 3rd century BC. The town fell under <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> rule in 71 BC, yet continued to enjoy privileges such as the right to mint its own coinage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_era">Medieval era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medieval era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="908" data-file-width="1278" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png/240px-Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png/360px-Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png/480px-Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mesembria_Fortress_plan.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mesembria Fortress plan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was one of the most important strongholds of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> from the 5th century AD onwards, and was fought over by <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarians</a>, being captured and incorporated in the lands of the <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">First Bulgarian Empire</a> in 812 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khan_Krum" title="Khan Krum">Khan Krum</a> after a two-week siege only to be ceded back to Byzantium by Knyaz <a href="/wiki/Boris_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Boris I of Bulgaria">Boris I</a> in 864 and reconquered by his son Tsar <a href="/wiki/Simeon_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Simeon I of Bulgaria">Simeon the Great</a>. During the time of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bulgarian_Empire" title="Second Bulgarian Empire">Second Bulgarian Empire</a> it was also contested by Bulgarian and Byzantine forces and enjoyed particular prosperity under Bulgarian tsar <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Alexander_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Alexander of Bulgaria">Ivan Alexander</a> (1331–1371) until it was conquered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusade" title="Crusade">Crusaders</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Amadeus_VI,_Count_of_Savoy" title="Amadeus VI, Count of Savoy">Amadeus VI, Count of Savoy</a> in 1366. The Bulgarian version of the name, <i>Nesebar</i> or <i>Mesebar</i>, has been attested since the 11th century.</p>
<p>Monuments from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> include the 5–6th century <i>Stara Mitropoliya</i> ("old bishopric"; also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Sophia_or_the_Old_Bishopric" title="Church of St Sophia or the Old Bishopric">St Sophia</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> without a <a href="/wiki/Transept" title="Transept">transept</a>; the 6th century <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_the_Holy_Mother_of_God_Eleusa,_Nesebar" title="Basilica of the Holy Mother of God Eleusa, Nesebar">church of the Virgin</a>; and the 11th century <i>Nova Mitropoliya</i> ("new bishopric"; also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Stephen_or_the_New_Bishopric" title="Church of St Stephen or the New Bishopric">St Stephen</a>) which continued to be embellished until the 18th century. In the 13th and 14th century a remarkable series of churches were built: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Theodore,_Burgas" title="Church of St Theodore, Burgas">St Theodore</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Paraskeva" title="Church of St Paraskeva">St Paraskeva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Archangels_Michael_and_Gabriel,_Nesebar" title="Church of the Holy Archangels Michael and Gabriel, Nesebar">St Michael St Gabriel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_John_Aliturgetos" title="Church of St John Aliturgetos">St John Aliturgetos</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_rule">Ottoman rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ottoman rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The capture of the town by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a> in 1453 marked the start of its decline, but its architectural heritage remained and was enriched in the 19th century by the construction of wooden houses in style typical for the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Black_Sea_Coast" title="Bulgarian Black Sea Coast">Bulgarian Black Sea Coast</a> during this period. At the early 19th century many locals joined the Greek patriotic organization <a href="/wiki/Filiki_Eteria" title="Filiki Eteria">Filiki Eteria</a>, while at the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek War of Independence</a> (1821) part of the town's youth participated in the struggle under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandros_Ypsilantis" title="Alexandros Ypsilantis">Alexandros Ypsilantis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ehw_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ehw-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nesebar was a kaza centre in <a href="/wiki/Sliven" title="Sliven">İslimye</a> sanjak of <a href="/wiki/Edirne_Province" title="Edirne Province">Edirne Province</a> before 1878.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_Bulgarian_state">Third Bulgarian state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Third Bulgarian state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Liberation_of_Bulgaria" title="Liberation of Bulgaria">Liberation of Bulgaria</a> from Ottoman rule in 1878, Nesebar became part of the autonomous Ottoman province of Eastern Rumelia in <a href="/wiki/Burgas" title="Burgas">Burgaz</a> department until it <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_unification" title="Bulgarian unification">united</a> with the Principality of Bulgaria in 1885. Around the end of the 19th century Nesebar was a small town of <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> fishermen and vinegrowers. In the early 20th century, the total population increased to 1,870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ehw_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ehw-9">[9]</a></sup> It developed as a key Bulgarian seaside resort since the beginning of the 20th century. After 1925 a new town part was built and the historic Old Town was restored.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nesebar is sometimes said to be the town with the highest number of churches per capita.<sup><a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.villabeneia.com/Site/okolnostite.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>, <a class="external autonumber" href="http://bg.zonebulgaria.com/cherno_more/nesebar/istoria/" rel="nofollow">[2]</a></sup> Today, a total of forty churches survive, wholly or partly, in the vicinity of the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ehw_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ehw-9">[9]</a></sup> Some of the most famous include:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Sophia_or_the_Old_Bishopric" title="Church of St Sophia or the Old Bishopric">Church of St Sophia or the Old Bishopric</a> (<i>Stara Mitropoliya</i>) (5th–6th century)</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_the_Holy_Mother_of_God_Eleusa,_Nesebar" title="Basilica of the Holy Mother of God Eleusa, Nesebar">Basilica of the Holy Mother of God Eleusa</a> (6th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_John_the_Baptist" title="Church of John the Baptist">Church of John the Baptist</a> (11th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Stephen_or_the_New_Bishopric" title="Church of St Stephen or the New Bishopric">Church of St Stephen or the New Bishopric</a> (<i>Nova Mitropoliya</i>) (11th century; reconstructed in the 16th–18th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Theodore" title="Church of St Theodore">Church of St Theodore</a> (13th century)</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Saint_Paraskevi,_Nesebar" title="Church of Saint Paraskevi, Nesebar">Church of St Paraskevi</a> (13th–14th century)</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Archangels_Michael_and_Gabriel,_Nesebar" title="Church of the Holy Archangels Michael and Gabriel, Nesebar">Church of the Holy Archangels Michael and Gabriel</a> (13th–14th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ_Pantocrator" title="Church of Christ Pantocrator">Church of Christ Pantocrator</a> (13th–14th century)</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_John_Aliturgetos" title="Church of St John Aliturgetos">Church of St John Aliturgetos</a> (14th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Spas" title="Church of St Spas">Church of St Spas</a> (17th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Church_of_St_Clement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Church of St Clement (page does not exist)">Church of St Clement</a> (17th century)</li>
<li>the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Church_Assumption_of_the_Holy_Virgin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Church Assumption of the Holy Virgin (page does not exist)">Church Assumption of the Holy Virgin</a> (19th century)</li>
</ul>
<p>Whether built during the Byzantine, Bulgarian or Ottoman rule of the city, the churches of Nesebar represent the rich architectural heritage of the Eastern Orthodox world and illustrate the gradual development from Early Christian basilicas to medieval cross-domed churches.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Namesakes">Namesakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nesebar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Namesakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nesebar_Gap" title="Nesebar Gap">Nesebar Gap</a> on <a href="/wiki/Livingston_Island" title="Livingston Island">Livingston Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is named after Nesebar.</p>
