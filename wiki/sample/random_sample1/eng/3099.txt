<p><i><b>Neotrogla</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Psocoptera" title="Psocoptera">barklice</a> noted for its reversed sex roles and organs, traits shared by all species of the genus.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neotrogla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Neotrogla</i> are found in dry cave systems throughout <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. They feed primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bat_guano" title="Bat guano">bat guano</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neotrogla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Neotrogla</i> are about the size of fleas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-2">[2]</a></sup> The genus can be distinguished from other genera of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Speleketorinae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Speleketorinae (page does not exist)">Speleketorinae</a> by the presence of articulated spines on the anterior side of their legs, and by their unique genitalia. Both sexes have hairs on their <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">tarsus</a>, but the hairs on females are markedly longer. <i>Neotrogla</i> have branched hindwings and brown forewings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lienhard2010_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lienhard2010-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_reversal">Sexual reversal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neotrogla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sexual reversal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Female <i>Neotrogla</i> possess a <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>-like organ, properly termed a "<b>gynosome</b>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lienhard2010_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lienhard2010-1">[1]</a></sup> but interchangeably called a penis. They aggressively seek out mates, while males are more selective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YoshizawaFerreira2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-YoshizawaFerreira2014-3">[3]</a></sup> During mating, the female mounts the male and penetrates his small genital opening from behind. Her gynosome swells and tiny barbs on the organ lock the individuals tightly together; when a researcher tried to separate mating individuals the male was torn in two, leaving his reproductive organs attached to the female. During mating, the female uses her gynosome to extract sperm and nutrient-filled seminal fluid from the male. A single mating session can last from 40 to 70 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reversal of sex organs may be explained by the lack of nutrients in the cave environment, which makes it evolutionarily useful for the female to extract nutrients from the male.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zme_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-zme-4">[4]</a></sup> Female <i>Neotrogla</i> drain males of seminal fluid even when they are too young to reproduce, giving weight to this theory, according to entomologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kazunori_Yoshizawa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kazunori Yoshizawa (page does not exist)">Kazunori Yoshizawa</a> who co-authored the first study of the insects' mating behavior. If males spend most of their limited resources producing such nutrient-rich fluid, it would also help explain why males are choosy about their partners. Males of other insects are known to produce similar "<a href="/wiki/Nuptial_gift" title="Nuptial gift">nuptial gifts</a>" of nutrients to be passed over during mating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-2">[2]</a></sup> However, the evolutionary origin of the penis-like organ remains a complete mystery. "Usually, a new structure evolves as a modification of a previously existing structure," Yoshizawa explained. Such an adaptation would be "exceptionally difficult" because of the need for male and female genital structures to change at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zme_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-zme-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Female penetration of males is known in a few species<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but only <i>Neotrogla</i> females have a well-defined organ that can be described as a penis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YoshizawaFerreira2014_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-YoshizawaFerreira2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Likewise, reversal of sex roles has been recorded in a few other species of animals. <i>Neotrogla</i>, however, appears to be unique in having both traits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Yoshizawa, the animal offers a singular chance to study the conflict between the sexes and the role of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">sexual selection</a> in evolution. "It will be important to unveil why, among many sex-role-reversed animals, only <i>Neotrogla</i> evolved the elaborated female penis," he said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomic_history">Taxonomic history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neotrogla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Taxonomic history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Neotrogla</i> was first spotted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologist" title="Ecologist">ecologist</a> Rodrigo Ferreira.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2010, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist">entomologist</a> Charles Lienhard assigned the insects to a new genus, designating <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neotrogla_brasiliensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neotrogla brasiliensis (page does not exist)">Neotrogla brasiliensis</a></i> as the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>. It was the first New World genus of Sensitibillini, a <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribe</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Speleketorinae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Speleketorinae (page does not exist)">Speleketorinae</a> previously known only from Africa. <i>Neotrogla</i> is closely related to the Southern African genus of barklice <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Afrotrogla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Afrotrogla (page does not exist)">Afrotrogla</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lienhard2010_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lienhard2010-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neotrogla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2014, four species have been described:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lienhard2010_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lienhard2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lienhard2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lienhard2013-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>N. aurora</i> <small>Lienhard, 2010</small></li>
<li><i>N. brasiliensis</i> <small>Lienhard, 2010</small></li>
<li><i>N. curvata</i> <small>Lienhard &amp; Ferreira, 2013</small></li>
<li><i>N. truncata</i> <small>Lienhard, 2010</small></li>
</ul>
