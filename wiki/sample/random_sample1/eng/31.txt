<p><b>Pedro Gutiérrez de Valdivia</b> or Valdiva (April 17, 1497 – December 25, 1553<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) was a <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a> and the first royal governor of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. After serving with the Spanish army in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, he was sent to South America in 1534, where he served as lieutenant under <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, acting as his second in command. In 1540 he led an expedition of 150 Spaniards into Chile, where he defeated a large force of Indians and founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago</a> in 1541. He extended Spanish rule south to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%ADo-B%C3%ADo_River" title="Bío-Bío River">Bío-Bío River</a> in 1546, fought again in Peru (1546 – 48), and returned to Chile as governor in 1549. He began to conquer Chile south of the Bío-Bío and founded <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n,_Chile" title="Concepción, Chile">Concepción</a> in 1550. He was captured and killed in a campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Mapuche" title="Mapuche">Araucanian Indians</a>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Valdivia" title="Valdivia">Valdivia</a> in Chile is named after him.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pedro de Valdivia is believed to have been born in <a href="/wiki/Villanueva_de_la_Serena" title="Villanueva de la Serena">Villanueva de la Serena</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.C3.B3ngora_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-G.C3.B3ngora-2">[2]</a></sup> (some say <a href="/wiki/Castuera" title="Castuera">Castuera</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">Extremadura</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.C3.B3ngora_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-G.C3.B3ngora-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> around 1500 (some sources put his date of birth as early as 1497 or as late as 1505) to an impoverished <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hidalgo_(Spanish_nobility)" title="Hidalgo (Spanish nobility)">hidalgo</a> family. In 1520 he joined the Spanish army of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Spain" title="Charles I of Spain">Charles I</a> and fought in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> in 1521 and Italy between 1522 and 1525, participating in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia" title="Battle of Pavia">battle of Pavia</a> as part of the troops of the <a href="/wiki/Fernando_d%27Avalos" title="Fernando d'Avalos">Marquis of Pescara</a>. He reached America in 1535, spent an uneventful year in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, and then moved on to <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> in 1537.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valdivia_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Valdivia-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There he took part on the side of <a href="/wiki/Hernando_Pizarro" title="Hernando Pizarro">Hernando Pizarro</a> in his struggle against <a href="/wiki/Diego_de_Almagro" title="Diego de Almagro">Diego de Almagro</a> and fought in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Las_Salinas" title="Battle of Las Salinas">battle of Las Salinas</a> in 1538, which saw Almagro defeated and captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prescott_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prescott-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:264</sup> Afterwards he accompanied Hernando and Gonzalo Pizarro to conquer both the province of Collao and las Charcas in High Peru (currently <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>). As compensation for his help in conquering these lands, he was awarded a silver mine and became a wealthy man.</p>
<p>Valdivia had married <a href="/wiki/Marina_Ort%C3%ADz_de_Gaete" title="Marina Ortíz de Gaete">Marina Ortíz de Gaete</a> in Spain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mari.C3.B1o_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mari.C3.B1o-5">[5]</a></sup> but in Peru he became attached to the widow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In%C3%A9s_de_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Inés de Suárez">Inés de Suárez</a>, who was to accompany him to Chile as his mistress.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_expedition">The expedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PDEValdivia.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="957" data-file-width="566" height="372" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/PDEValdivia.JPG/220px-PDEValdivia.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/PDEValdivia.JPG/330px-PDEValdivia.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/PDEValdivia.JPG/440px-PDEValdivia.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>After the failure of the expedition of Diego de Almagro in 1536, the lands to the south of Peru (then known as <i>Nueva Toledo</i>, extending from the 14° - close to modern day <a href="/wiki/Pisco,_Peru" title="Pisco, Peru">Pisco</a>, Peru - to the 25° latitude - close to <a href="/wiki/Taltal" title="Taltal">Taltal</a>, Chile) had remained unexplored. Valdivia asked governor <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a> for permission to complete the conquest of that territory. He got his permission but was appointed only <i>Lieutenant Governor</i>, and not Governor as he had wanted.</p>
<p>The expedition was fraught with problems from the beginning. Valdivia had to sell the lands and the mine that had been assigned to him in order to finance the expedition. A shortage of soldiers and adventurers was also problematic since they were not interested in conquering what they were sure were extremely poor lands. Furthermore, while he was preparing the expedition, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Sancho_de_Hoz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedro Sancho de Hoz (page does not exist)">Pedro Sancho de Hoz</a> arrived from Spain with a royal grant for the same country. To avoid difficulties, Pizarro advised the two competitors to join their interests, and on December 28, 1539, a contract of partnership was signed.</p>
<p>The small expedition finally left <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuzco" title="Cuzco">Cuzco</a>, Peru in January, 1540, with Pizarro's permission and Pedro Sancho de Hoz as partner. They carried with them a plethora of seeds for planting, a drove of swine and brood mares, and almost a thousand native Indians but were composed of only a few Spaniards. Only one woman was among the travelers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In%C3%A9s_de_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Inés de Suárez">Inés de Suárez</a>, Valdivia's mistress. En route more Spaniards joined the expedition, attracted by Valdivia's fame as a brilliant leader. These conquistadores had formed part of the failed campaigns to the highlands of Bolivia and all in all around 150 Spaniards joined the expedition.</p>

<p>Valdivia resolved to avoid the road over the Andes, which had proved fatal to Almagro's army, and set out resolutely through the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>. On the way, Pedro Sancho de Hoz, seeking sole leadership, tried to murder Valdivia but failed. He was pardoned but from then on had to accept subordinate status. The natives of the region were not pleased by the return of the Spaniards due to the maltreatment they had suffered under Almagro. With many promises, Valdivia was able to regain their trust. After a march of five months, and suffering great privations, they arrived at the Copiapo valley, where Valdivia officially took possession of the land in the name of the Spanish king.</p>
<p>Soon thereafter they continued south and in December 1540, eleven months after they left Cuzco, Valdivia and his expedition reached the valley of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mapocho" title="Mapocho">Mapocho</a> river, where they were able to establish the capital of the territory. The valley was extensive and well populated with natives. Its soil was fertile and there was abundant fresh water. Two high hills provided defensive positions. Soon after their arrival, Valdivia tried to convince the native inhabitants of his good intentions, sending out delegations bearing gifts for the <a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">caciques</a>.</p>
<p>Finally on February 12, 1541, Valdivia officially founded the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago de la Nueva Extremadura</a> (named after Saint James, <i>Santiago</i> in Spanish, and Valdivia's home region of <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">Extremadura</a>, Spain). The ceremony was held at the foot of the <i>Huelén</i> hill (now known as <i>Santa Lucia hill</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After arriving in Chile, Valdivia and his men went out of their way to restore the relationship between conquistador and Indian which had been greatly harmed by Almagro and his merciless ways. At first, Valdivia was successful in his efforts to deal benevolently with the native population, but this peaceful coexistence did not last long. One of the first orders that Valdivia gave was to have a ship constructed at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Aconcagua_River" title="Aconcagua River">Aconcagua River</a> to send to Peru for further supplies and to serve as a courier service, but soon was obliged to return in haste to Santiago to subdue a mutiny. The Spaniards' greed quickly surfaced and overshadowed previous intentions when rumors of gold at the Marga Marga mines, in the vicinity of Valparaiso arose, and the settlers began forcing the natives to work there.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_the_Colony">Birth of the Colony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Birth of the Colony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="944" data-file-width="1330" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg/220px-Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg/330px-Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg/440px-Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fundacion_de_Santiago.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Pedro_Lira" title="Pedro Lira">Pedro Lira</a>'s 1889 painting of the founding of Santiago by Pedro de Valdivia at <a href="/wiki/Santa_Luc%C3%ADa_Hill" title="Santa Lucía Hill">Huelén Hill</a>.</div>

<p>On learning of Francisco Pizarro's murder in 1541, Valdivia had himself appointed governor of the territory by the council of the new city, and removed Chile from Peruvian control, acknowledging only the royal authority, an arrangement the Crown found acceptable. Secure now in his own domain, he pushed exploration southward and aided the development of the country by dividing the land among his ablest followers and parceling out the Indians in encomiendas. Chile possessed minerals, but Valdivia definitely subordinated mining to agriculture and stock raising. Still, the colony was not prosperous; gold was scarce and the Araucanians warlike.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction_of_Santiago">Destruction of Santiago</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Destruction of Santiago">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After an apparent peaceful period the Indians began to resist the invaders. Valdivia marched against the tribes and defeated them at Cachapoal. While away, on September 11, 1541, local Indians led by <a href="/wiki/Michimalonco" title="Michimalonco">Michimalonco</a> attacked Santiago. The defense of the city was led by his mistress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In%C3%A9s_de_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Inés de Suárez">Inés de Suárez</a>. The Spaniards, desperate and willing to fight until death, were able to eventually push the Indians back; Valdivia and his troops made it back just in time to relieve the capital.</p>
<p>By the time the battle ended the entire town had been destroyed and burned to the ground, animals were killed and the fields and stores were decimated. Only a small amount of property was not destroyed, including a handful of seeds, two sows, one pig and a pair of chickens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Valdivia organized his men into groups to keep watch over the crops and protect the city against attack. For the next two years, there were men always saddled and armed, ready to fight in case the Indians posed a threat to Spanish authority.</p>
<p>This event meant a real setback for the conquest of the Chilean territory. The resistance of the Indians became daily stronger, and as the ship that he had constructed in Aconcagua was also destroyed by the natives, Valdivia sent in 1542 overland to Peru his lieutenant <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alonso_de_Monroy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alonso de Monroy (page does not exist)">Alonso de Monroy</a> with five followers to seek reinforcements, but, on account of the disturbance in that country in consequence of the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Diego_de_Almagro_II" title="Diego de Almagro II">El Mozo Almagro</a> by <a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_Vaca_de_Castro" title="Cristóbal Vaca de Castro">Cristóbal Vaca de Castro</a>, Monroy could not obtain much aid, and returned in September 1543, with only seventy horsemen, also sending by sea a vessel with provisions and ammunition to the port of Aconcagua.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_of_the_great">Expansion of the great</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion of the great">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In September 1543 new arms, clothes and other equipment arrived from Peru on the ship <i>Santiaguillo</i>; thanks to these new supplies, Valdivia was in the position to start the rebuilding of Santiago and to send an expedition, led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Bohon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Bohon (page does not exist)">Juan Bohon</a>, to explore and populate the northern region of Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This expedition founded <a href="/wiki/La_Serena,_Chile" title="La Serena, Chile">La Serena</a> halfway between Santiago and the northern <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>, in the valley of <i>Coquimbo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mari.C3.B1o_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mari.C3.B1o-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valpara%C3%ADso,_Chile" title="Valparaíso, Chile">Valparaíso</a>, though used as a port by the Spaniards from the start, had no considerable population until much later.</p>
<p>In 1544 Valdivia sent a naval expedition consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">barks</a> <i>San Pedro</i> and <i>Santiaguillo</i>, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Pastene" title="Juan Bautista Pastene">Juan Bautista Pastene</a>, to reconnoiter the southwestern coast of South America, ordering him to reach the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The expedition set sail from Valparaíso and although Pastene did not reach this goal, he explored much of the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He entered the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Concepci%C3%B3n" title="Bay of Concepción">bay of San Pedro</a>, and made landings at what are now known as <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n,_Chile" title="Concepción, Chile">Concepción</a> and at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdivia,_Chile" title="Valdivia, Chile">Valdivia</a>, which was later named in honor of the commander. Encountering severe storms further south, he then returned to Valparaiso.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_(1872).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="341" data-file-width="479" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_%281872%29.jpg/220px-Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_%281872%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_%281872%29.jpg/330px-Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_%281872%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_%281872%29.jpg/440px-Santiago_-_Casa_de_Pedro_Valdivia_i_Capilla_de_Vera_Cruz_%281872%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In February 1546 Valdivia himself set out, with sixty horsemen plus native guides and porters, and crossed the <a href="/wiki/Itata_River" title="Itata River">Itata River</a>. He got to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%ADo-B%C3%ADo_River" title="Bío-Bío River">Bío-Bío River</a> where he planned to found another town. However, the party was attacked by <a href="/wiki/Mapuche" title="Mapuche">Mapuche</a> warriors at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Quilacura" title="Battle of Quilacura">Battle of Quilacura</a>. Realizing that it would be impossible to proceed in such hostile territory with so limited a force, Valdivia wisely elected to return to Santiago shortly thereafter, after finding a site for a new city at what is now <a href="/wiki/Penco" title="Penco">Penco</a> and would become the first site of <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n,_Chile" title="Concepción, Chile">Concepción</a>. Still, Valdivia managed to subdue the country between Santiago and the <a href="/wiki/Maule_River" title="Maule River">Maule River</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Peru">Return to Peru</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Return to Peru">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To secure additional aid and confirm his claims to the conquered territory, Valdivia returned in 1547 to Peru, leaving <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Villagra" title="Francisco de Villagra">Francisco de Villagra</a> as governor in his stead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There he tried to gather more resources and men to continue the conquest. When the <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Pizarro" title="Gonzalo Pizarro">Gonzalo Pizarro</a> rebellion began in Peru, the insurgents attempted unsuccessfully to win Valdivia to their side. Nonetheless, early in 1548 Valdivia joined the royal army of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceroy_of_Peru" title="Viceroy of Peru">Viceroy</a> <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_la_Gasca" title="Pedro de la Gasca">Pedro de la Gasca</a>, and his military experience counted heavily in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jaquijahuana" title="Battle of Jaquijahuana">victory of Xaquixahuana</a> on April 9 of that year.</p>
<p>Nonetheless, a discontented faction from Chile managed to have him tried in Lima, accused of tyranny, malfeasance of public funds and public immorality. One of the charges levelled against him was that he, being married, openly lived with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In%C3%A9s_de_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Inés de Suárez">Inés de Suárez</a> <i>"...in the manner of man and wife and they sleep in one bed and they eat in one dish..."</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barros-12">[12]</a></sup> In exchange for being freed, and for his confirmation as Royal Governor, he agreed to relinquish her and to bring to Chile his wife, <a href="/wiki/Marina_Ort%C3%ADz_de_Gaete" title="Marina Ortíz de Gaete">Marina Ortíz de Gaete</a>, who only arrived after Valdivia's death in 1554. He was also ordered to marry Ines off, which he did, upon his return to Chile in 1549, to one of his captains, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_de_Quiroga" title="Rodrigo de Quiroga">Rodrigo de Quiroga</a>. As recognition for his services Valdivia was finally appointed as <i><a href="/wiki/Adelantado" title="Adelantado">adelantado</a></i> and won the royal assent to his coveted title of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Governor_of_Chile" title="Royal Governor of Chile">Governor of Chile</a>, returning to the settlement with his position and prestige considerably strengthened.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arauco_War">Arauco War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arauco War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arauco_War" title="Arauco War">Arauco War</a></div>
<p>Between 1549 and 1553, after his arrival back in Santiago, Valdivia again undertook the conquest of southern Chile, but faced heavy resistance from the indigenous population. Valdivia had a clash with the warlike Araucanians beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bio-Bio_River" title="Bio-Bio River">Bio-Bio River</a> in 1550 in which he defeated them but by no means broke their will to resist, a will that grew stronger when the conquistador established settlements in their territory. In spite of the fierce resistance at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Penco" title="Battle of Penco">Battle of Penco</a>, he founded <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n,_Chile" title="Concepción, Chile">Concepción</a> in 1550. Later he founded the more southern villages of <a href="/wiki/La_Imperial,_Chile" title="La Imperial, Chile">La Imperial</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdivia,_Chile" title="Valdivia, Chile">Valdivia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angol,_Chile" title="Angol, Chile">Angol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villarrica,_Chile" title="Villarrica, Chile">Villarrica</a>, in 1551 and 1552.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_uprising_of_1553">The uprising of 1553</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The uprising of 1553">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After a brief stay in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago</a>, Valdivia returned to the south again in December 1552. To keep the connection open between <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n,_Chile" title="Concepción, Chile">Concepción</a> and the southern settlements, Valdivia had a number of forts built in <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_de_Nahuelbuta" title="Cordillera de Nahuelbuta">Cordillera de Nahuelbuta</a>. He moved against the Araucanians again in 1553 and built a fort at Tucapel. By the advice of the cacique <a href="/wiki/Colocolo_(tribal_chief)" title="Colocolo (tribal chief)">Colocolo</a>, the Araucanians united their efforts choosing as <a href="/wiki/Toqui" title="Toqui">toqui</a> (general-in-chief) the famous warrior <a href="/wiki/Caupolic%C3%A1n" title="Caupolicán">Caupolicán</a>.</p>
<p>Valdivia had earlier captured and presumably made friends with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lautaro_(toqui)" title="Lautaro (toqui)">Lautaro</a>, an Araucanian youth who became his groom. Lautaro secretly remained true to his own people and rejoined them to show <a href="/wiki/Caupolic%C3%A1n" title="Caupolicán">Caupolicán</a> a means by which Valdivia could be defeated. Toward the end of 1553, the Araucanians under Lautaro revolted and they fell on the over-extended Spanish forces in the south. One of the first signs that a big rebellion was building was the attack on the fort at Tucapel, where they managed to destroy the fortress on December 2, 1553. Valdivia was at Concepcion when he received notice of this event, and, believing that he could easily subdue the uprising, he hurried southward, sallying forth with only 40 men to stamp out the rebellion.</p>
<p>Near the ruins of the fortress Valdivia gathered the remnant of the garrison. He was ambushed before arriving to his destination and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tucapel" title="Battle of Tucapel">Battle of Tucapel</a> would be Valdivia's last. As each successive wave of attackers was wiped out or beaten off by the Spaniards, Lautaro sent another, until the entire Spanish company was massacred. The dreaded conquistador was captured still alive along with a priest by the <a href="/wiki/Mapuche" title="Mapuche">Mapuche</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_(Santiago,_Chile).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3780" data-file-width="2298" height="362" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_%28Santiago%2C_Chile%29.jpg/220px-Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_%28Santiago%2C_Chile%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_%28Santiago%2C_Chile%29.jpg/330px-Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_%28Santiago%2C_Chile%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_%28Santiago%2C_Chile%29.jpg/440px-Statue_of_Pedro_de_Valdivia_%28Santiago%2C_Chile%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>There are many versions of how Valdivia's killing took place. According to <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_de_Vivar" title="Jerónimo de Vivar">Jerónimo de Vivar</a>, an author contemporary with the events, the execution of Valdivia was personally ordered by <a href="/wiki/Caupolic%C3%A1n" title="Caupolicán">Caupolicán</a>, who had him killed with a lance and later his head, along with those of two of his bravest companions, were put on display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vivar_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vivar-13">[13]</a></sup> Another contemporary chronicler, <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_G%C3%B3ngora_Marmolejo" title="Alonso de Góngora Marmolejo">Alonso de Góngora Marmolejo</a> writes that Valdivia offered as a ransom for his life the evacuation of all the Spanish settlements in the <a href="/wiki/Mapuche" title="Mapuche">Mapuche</a> lands and to give them large herds of animals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.C3.B3ngora_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-G.C3.B3ngora-2">[2]</a></sup> but this offer was rejected and the Mapuche first cut off his forearms, roasted and ate them in front of him before killing him and his accompanying priest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.C3.B3ngora_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-G.C3.B3ngora-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Ercilla" title="Alonso de Ercilla">Alonso de Ercilla</a> refers that Valdivia was killed with the blow of a club, then with a knife a warrior cut open his breast and ripped his still quivering heart which was then handed to the toqui, who sucked its blood. The heart was passed round from one to another, and a drinking cup was made from his skull. The warriors keep running round the corpse brandishing their lances and uttering cries, while the rest of the assembly stamped with their feet until the earth shook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ercilla_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ercilla-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yet another contemporary chronicler, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Mari%C3%B1o_de_Lobera" title="Pedro Mariño de Lobera">Pedro Mariño de Lobera</a>, also wrote that Valdivia offered to evacuate the lands of the Mapuche but says he was shortly thereafter killed with a large club by a vengeful warrior named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilmaiquen" title="Pilmaiquen">Pilmaiquen</a>, who said that Valdivia could not be trusted to keep his word once freed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mari.C3.B1o_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mari.C3.B1o-5">[5]</a></sup> Lobera also says that a common story in Chile at the time was that Valdivia had been killed by forcing him to drink molten gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mari.C3.B1o_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mari.C3.B1o-5">[5]</a></sup> According to an even later legend, Lautaro took Valdivia to the Mapuche camp and put him to death after three days of torture, extracting his beating heart and eating it with the Mapuche leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The fact remains that probably all the stories about his death are apocryphal, since none of Valdivia's party survived the battle, and the only witnesses that could be found were Indians that were captured in subsequent battles.</p>
<p>The site of his death is close to the modern city of <a href="/wiki/Valdivia" title="Valdivia">Valdivia</a> named in his honor.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Valdivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Valdivia was an educated man and wielded the pen as well as the sword. In 1552 Valdivia despatched Captain <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_de_Alderete" title="Jerónimo de Alderete">Jerónimo de Alderete</a> with a narrative of his exploits directly to the king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Spain" title="Charles I of Spain">Charles I</a>. His twelve letters, addressed to the king and mostly preserved in the archives of the Indies, are models of a vigorous and fluent style, and of great historical interest.</p>
<p>His career and death are part of the epic poem <i><a href="/wiki/La_Araucana" title="La Araucana">La Araucana</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Ercilla" title="Alonso de Ercilla">Alonso de Ercilla</a>. He is also a major character in several historical novels, such as <i>Inés y las raíces de la tierra</i>, by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mar%C3%ADa_Correa_Morande&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="María Correa Morande (page does not exist)">María Correa Morande</a> (1964), <i>Ay Mamá Inés - Crónica Testimonial</i> (1993) by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jorge_Guzm%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jorge Guzmán (page does not exist)">Jorge Guzmán</a>, <i>Arauco: A Novel (2013)</i>, by John Caviglia, and <i><a href="/wiki/In%C3%A9s_of_My_Soul" title="Inés of My Soul">Inés of My Soul</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Inés del alma mía</i></span>) by <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Allende" title="Isabel Allende">Isabel Allende</a> (2006).</p>
