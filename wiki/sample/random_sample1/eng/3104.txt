<p><b>Nyatri Tsenpo</b> (<a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a>: <i>gnya' khri btsan po</i> "King of the Throne on the Shoulders") was a <a href="/wiki/List_of_emperors_of_Tibet" title="List of emperors of Tibet">king of Tibet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dam-pa1994_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dam-pa1994-1">[1]</a></sup> He was a <a href="/wiki/Legendary_progenitor" title="Legendary progenitor">legendary progenitor</a> of the so-called "Yarlung dynasty". His reign is said to have begun in 127 BC. In traditional <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibetan</a> history, he was the first ruler of the kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a> chronicles report that he is said to have descended from <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a> onto the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_mountains" title="Sacred mountains">sacred mountain</a> Yarlha Shampo. Due to certain physical peculiarities – his hands were <a href="/wiki/Webbing" title="Webbing">webbed</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Eyelid" title="Eyelid">eyelids</a> closed from the bottom and not the top – he was hailed as a <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">god</a> by locals, and they took him as their king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_religion" title="Ethnic religion">Tibetan mythology</a>, the first Tibetan building, <a href="/wiki/Yungbulakang_Palace" title="Yungbulakang Palace">Yungbulakang Palace</a>, was erected for the king. The year of his enthronement marks the first year of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_calendar" title="Tibetan calendar">Tibetan calendar</a>; <a href="/wiki/Losar" title="Losar">Losar</a>, the Tibetan New Year, is celebrated in his honor. Traditions hold that the first kings were immortal, and would be pulled up to heaven by the <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">cord</a> that had first deposited them on earth. This is what is said to have happened to Nyatri Tsenpo as well.</p>
