<p><b>Etiquette in technology</b> governs what conduct is socially acceptable in an online or digital situation. While <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a> is ingrained into culture, etiquette in technology is a fairly recent concept. The rules of etiquette that apply when communicating over the Internet or social networks or devices are different from those applying when communicating in person or by audio (such as telephone) or <a href="/wiki/Videophone" title="Videophone">videophone</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skype_video" title="Skype video">Skype video</a>). It is a social code of network communication.</p>
<p>Communicating with others via the Internet without misunderstandings in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_the_moment" title="Heat of the moment">heat of the moment</a> can be challenging, mainly because <a href="/wiki/Facial_expression" title="Facial expression">facial expressions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Body_language" title="Body language">body language</a> cannot be interpreted in <a href="/wiki/Cyberspace" title="Cyberspace">cyberspace</a>. Therefore, several recommendations to attempt to safeguard against these misunderstandings have been proposed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Netiquette">Netiquette</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Netiquette">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Rules of the Internet" redirects here. For the 4chan internet meme, see <a href="/wiki/Rule_34_(Internet_meme)#Variations_and_corollaries" title="Rule 34 (Internet meme)">Rule 34 (Internet meme) § Variations and corollaries</a>.</div>
<p><b>Netiquette</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloquial" title="Colloquial">colloquial</a> <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">network</a> <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> etiquette</i>, is a set of social conventions that facilitate interaction over networks, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mailing_list" title="Mailing list">mailing lists</a> to <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">forums</a>.</p>
<p>Like the network itself, these developing norms remain in a state of flux and vary from community to community. The points most strongly emphasized about Usenet netiquette often include using simple <a href="/wiki/Signature_block" title="Signature block">electronic signatures</a>, and avoiding multiposting, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-posting" title="Cross-posting">cross-posting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Off-topic" title="Off-topic">off-topic</a> posting, hijacking a discussion thread, and other techniques used to minimize the effort required to read a post or a thread. Similarly, some Usenet guidelines call for use of unabbreviated English<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while users of <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a> protocols like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_message_service" title="Short message service">SMS</a> occasionally encourage just the opposite, bolstering use of <a href="/wiki/SMS_language" title="SMS language">SMS language</a>. However, many online communities frown upon this practice.</p>
<p>Common rules for <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">e-mail</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Usenet such as avoiding <a href="/wiki/Flaming_(Internet)" title="Flaming (Internet)">flamewars</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a> are constant across most mediums and communities. Another rule is to avoid typing in <a href="/wiki/All_caps" title="All caps">all caps</a> or grossly enlarging script for emphasis, which is considered to be the equivalent of shouting or yelling. Other commonly shared points, such as remembering that one's posts are (or can easily be made) public, are generally intuitively understood by publishers of Web pages and posters to Usenet, although this rule is somewhat flexible depending on the environment. On more private protocols, however, such as e-mail and SMS, some users take the privacy of their posts for granted. One-on-one communications, such as private messages on chat forums and direct SMSs, may be considered more private than other such protocols, but infamous breaches surround even these relatively private media. For example, <a href="/wiki/Paris_Hilton" title="Paris Hilton">Paris Hilton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Danger_Hiptop" title="Danger Hiptop">Sidekick</a> <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">PDA</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)#Black_hat" title="Hacker (computer security)">cracked</a> in 2005, resulting in the publication of her private photos, SMS history, address book, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group e-mail sent by <a href="/wiki/Cerner" title="Cerner">Cerner</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Neal_Patterson" title="Neal Patterson">Neal Patterson</a> to managers of a facility in <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a> concerning "Cerner's declining work ethic" read, in part, "The parking lot is sparsely used at 8 A.M.; likewise at 5 P.M. As managers—you either do not know what your EMPLOYEES are doing; or YOU do not CARE ... In either case, you have a problem and you will fix it or I will replace you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After the e-mail was forwarded to hundreds of other employees, it quickly leaked to the public. On the day that the e-mail was posted to <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>, Cerner's stock price fell by over 22%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> from a high market capitalization of US$1.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E-Mail_Rules_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-E-Mail_Rules-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beyond matters of basic courtesy and privacy, e-mail syntax (defined by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2822" rel="nofollow">RFC 2822</a>) allows for different types of recipients. The primary recipient, defined by the To: line, can reasonably be expected to respond, but recipients of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_copies" title="Carbon copies">carbon copies</a> cannot be, although they still might.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Likewise, misuse of the CC: functions in lieu of traditional <a href="/wiki/Mailing_list" title="Mailing list">mailing lists</a> can result in serious technical issues. In late 2007, employees of the United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> used large CC: lists in place of a mailing list to broadcast messages to several hundred users. Misuse of the "reply to all" caused the number of responses to that message to quickly expand to some two million messages, bringing down their mail server.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In cases like this, rules of netiquette have more to do with efficient sharing of resources—ensuring that the associated technology continues to function—rather than more basic etiquette. On Usenet, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-posting" title="Cross-posting">cross-posting</a>, in which a single copy of a message is posted to multiple groups is intended to prevent this from happening, but many newsgroups frown on the practice, as it means users must sometimes read many copies of a message in multiple groups.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"When someone makes a mistake – whether it's a spelling error or a spelling flame, a stupid question or an unnecessarily long answer – be kind about it. If it's a minor error, you may not need to say anything. Even if you feel strongly about it, think twice before reacting. Having good manners yourself doesn't give you license to correct everyone else. If you do decide to inform someone of a mistake, point it out politely, and preferably by private email rather than in public. Give people the benefit of the doubt; assume they just don't know any better. And never be arrogant or self-righteous about it. Just as it's a law of nature that spelling flames always contain spelling errors, notes pointing out Netiquette violations are often examples of poor Netiquette."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Due to the large variation between what is considered acceptable behavior in various professional environments and between professional and social networks, codified internal <a href="/wiki/Style_guide" title="Style guide">manuals of style</a> can help clarify acceptable limits and boundaries for user behavior. For instance, failure to publish such a guide for e-mail style was cited among the reasons for a NZ$17,000 <a href="/wiki/Wrongful_dismissal" title="Wrongful dismissal">wrongful dismissal</a> finding against a firm that fired a woman for misuse of <a href="/wiki/All_caps" title="All caps">all caps</a> in company-wide e-mail traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_Herald_10594014_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NZ_Herald_10594014-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Online_etiquette">Online etiquette</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Online etiquette">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Digital_citizen" title="Digital citizen">Digital citizen</a></div>
<p><i>Digital citizenship</i> is a term that describes how a <a href="/wiki/Digital_citizen" title="Digital citizen">person</a> should act while using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_technology" title="Digital technology">digital technology</a> online and has also been defined as "the ability to participate in society online".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The term is often mentioned in relation to <a href="/wiki/Internet_safety" title="Internet safety">Internet safety</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Netiquette" title="Netiquette">netiquette</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term has been used as early as 1998 and has gone through several changes in description as newer technological advances have changed the method and frequency of how people interact with one another online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Classes on digital citizenship have been taught in some public education systems and some argue that the term can be "measured in terms of economic and political activities online".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>The issue of mobile communication and etiquette has also become an issue of academic interest. The rapid adoption of the device has resulted in the intrusion of telephony into situations where it was previously not used. This has exposed the implicit rules of courtesy and opened them to reevaluation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_phone_etiquette_in_the_education_system">Cell phone etiquette in the education system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cell phone etiquette in the education system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most schools in the United States and Europe and Canada have prohibited mobile phones in the classroom, citing class disruptions and the potential for cheating via text messaging<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In the UK, possession of a mobile phone in an examination can result in immediate disqualification from that subject or from all that student's subjects. This still applies even if the mobile phone was not turned on at the time. In <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, students are banned from taking cell phones to school. This has been a debate for several years, but finally passed legislature in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medina_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medina-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Most schools allow students to have cell phones for safety purposes"—a reaction to the Littleton, Colorado, high school shooting incident of 1999 (Lipscomb 2007: 50). Apart from emergency situations, most schools don't officially allow students to use cell phones during class time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_phone_etiquette_in_the_public_sphere">Cell phone etiquette in the public sphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cell phone etiquette in the public sphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Talking or texting on a cell phone in public may seem a distraction for many individuals. When in public there are two times when one uses a phone. The first is when someone is alone and the other is when he/she is in a group. The main issue for most people is when they are in a group, and the cell phone becomes a distraction or a barrier for successful socialization among family and friends. In the past few years, society has become less tolerant of cell phone use in public areas for example, public transportation, restaurants and much more. This is exemplified by the widespread recognition of campaigns such as Stop <a href="/wiki/Phubbing" title="Phubbing">Phubbing</a>, which prompted global discussion as to how mobile phones should be used in the presence of others. "Some have suggested that mobile phones 'affect every aspect of our personal and professional lives either directly or indirectly'" (Humphrey). Every culture's tolerance of cell phone usage varies, for instance in Western society cell phones are permissible during free time at schools, whereas in the eastern countries, cell phones are strictly prohibited on school property.</p>
<p>Mobile phone use can be an important matter of social discourtesy: phones ringing during funerals or weddings; in toilets, cinemas and theatres. Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_shops" title="Book shops">book shops</a>, libraries, bathrooms, cinemas, doctors' offices and <a href="/wiki/Place_of_worship" title="Place of worship">places of worship</a> prohibit their use, so that other patrons will not be disturbed by conversations. Some facilities install <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone_jammer" title="Mobile phone jammer">signal-jamming equipment</a> to prevent their use, although in many countries, including the US, such equipment is illegal. Some new <a href="/wiki/Auditorium" title="Auditorium">auditoriums</a> have installed wire mesh in the walls to make a <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cage" title="Faraday cage">Faraday cage</a>, which prevents signal penetration without violating signal jamming laws.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A working group made up of Finnish telephone companies, public transport operators and communications authorities has launched a campaign to remind mobile phone users of courtesy, especially when using mass transit—what to talk about on the phone, and how to. In particular, the campaign wants to impact loud mobile phone usage as well as calls regarding sensitive matters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Trains, particularly those involving long-distance services, often offer a "quiet carriage" where phone use is prohibited, much like the designated non-smoking carriage of the past. In the UK however many users tend to ignore this as it is rarely enforced, especially if the other carriages are crowded and they have no choice but to go in the "quiet carriage".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In Japan, it is generally considered impolite to talk using a phone on any train—e-mailing is generally the mode of mobile communication. Mobile phone usage on local public transport is also increasingly seen as a nuisance; the city of <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, for instance, has mandated a total ban of mobile phones on its tram and bus network in 2008 (though texting and emailing is still allowed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nancy_J._Friedman" title="Nancy J. Friedman">Nancy J. Friedman</a> has spoken widely about landline and cell phone etiquette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotarian_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-rotarian-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doane_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-doane-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emily_Post" title="Emily Post">Emily Post</a> has also written on her essential rules for using a cell phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post.2CCellRules_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post.2CCellRules-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post.2CNewsFlash_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post.2CNewsFlash-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_phone_etiquette_within_social_relationships">Cell phone etiquette within social relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cell phone etiquette within social relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When critically assessing the family structure, it is important to examine the parent/child negotiations which occur in the household, in relation to the increased use of cell phones. Teenagers use their cell phones as a way to negotiate spatial boundaries with their parents (Williams 2005:316). This includes extending curfews in the public space and allowing more freedom for the teenagers when they are outside of the home (Williams 2005:318). More importantly, cell phone etiquette relates to kinship groups and the family as an institution. This is because cell phones act as a threat due to the rapid disconnect within families. Children are often so closely affiliated with their technological gadgets, and they tend to interact with their friends constantly and this has a negative impact on their relationship with their parents (Williams 2005:326). Teenagers see themselves as gaining a sense of empowerment from the mobile phone. Cell phone etiquette in the household from an anthropological perspective has shown an evolution in the institution of family. The mobile phone has now been integrated into family practices and perpetuated a wider concern which is the fracture between parent and child relationships. We are able to see the traditional values disappearing; however, reflexive monitoring is occurring (Williams 2005:320). Through this, parents are becoming friendlier with their children and critics emphasize that this change is problematic because children should be subjected to social control. One way of social control is limiting the time spent interacting with friends, which is difficult to do in today's society because of the rapid use of cell phones.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Netiquette_vs._cell_phone_etiquette">Netiquette vs. cell phone etiquette</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Netiquette vs. cell phone etiquette">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cell phone etiquette is largely dependent on the cultural context and what is deemed to be socially acceptable. For instance, in certain cultures using your hand held devices while interacting in a group environment is considered bad manners, whereas, in other cultures around the world it may be viewed differently. In addition, cell phone etiquette also encompasses the various types of activities which are occurring and the nature of the messages which are being sent. More importantly, messages of an inappropriate nature can be sent to an individual and this could potentially orchestrate problems such as verbal/ cyber abuse.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_technology_and_behaviors">New technology and behaviors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Etiquette_in_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: New technology and behaviors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps the biggest obstacle to communication in online settings is the lack of emotional cues. Facial cues dictate the mood and corresponding diction of two people in a conversation. During phone conversations, tone of voice communicates the emotions of the person on the other line. But with chat rooms, instant messaging apps and texting, any signals that would indicate the tone of a person's words or their state of emotion are absent. Because of this, there have been some interesting accommodations. Perhaps the two most prevalent compensating behaviors are the use of emoticons and abbreviations. Emoticons use punctuation marks to illustrate common symbols that pertain to facial cues. For example, one would combine a colon and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parenthesis" title="Parenthesis">parenthesis</a> to recreate the symbol of the smiley face indicating the happiness or satisfaction of the other person. To symbolize laughter, the abbreviation "LOL" standing for "laughing out loud" developed. Along with these, countless other symbols and abbreviations have developed including, "BRB" ("be right back"), "TTYL" (talk to you later) and specific designs incorporated by apps of a laughing face, sad face, crying face, angry face etc.</p>
<p>Now, as newer modes of communication are becoming more common, the rules of communication must adapt as fast as the technology. For example, one of the most popular new apps, Snapchat, is growing to have its own rules and etiquette. This app lets a user send pictures or videos that disappear after a couple seconds. Initially, the thought that occurs to people when confronted by this app is its implications for sexting. Although it's entirely possible to make use of Snapchat for that purpose, what the app has developed into is a form of communication that shares funny or interesting moments. Originally compared to Instagram<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> by way of the app's ability to broadcast pictures to many people, it has now become standard to communicate through Snapchat by sending pictures back and forth and using the caption bar for messages. The reply option on Snapchat specifically promotes this behavior, but Snapchat etiquette is not set in stone. It is becoming clear that Snaps personalized for the receiver expect a reply, but where ends this obligation? Some people use Snapchat specifically for the purpose of communication, while some use it to simply provide a visual update of their day. The newest update of Snapchat, an instant messaging add-on, seems to be catered to those who use the app to send messages back and forth. This new messaging add-on, along with the video chat feature will warrant new forms of social construct and expectations of behavior in accordance with this application.</p>
