<p>The <b>Nepalese Royal Massacre</b> occurred on 1 June 2001, at a house on the grounds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narayanhity_Royal_Palace" title="Narayanhity Royal Palace">Narayanhity Royal Palace</a>, the residence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nepalese_monarchy" title="Nepalese monarchy">Nepalese monarchy</a>. Ten members of the family were killed during a party or monthly reunion dinner of the royal family in the house. The dead included <a href="/wiki/Birendra_of_Nepal" title="Birendra of Nepal">King Birendra of Nepal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Aishwarya_of_Nepal" title="Queen Aishwarya of Nepal">Queen Aishwarya</a>.</p>
<p>Later, upon his father's death, <a href="/wiki/Dipendra_of_Nepal" title="Dipendra of Nepal">Prince Dipendra</a> became <i>de jure</i> King of Nepal while in coma, and he died in the hospital three days after the massacre without recovering from this coma.</p>
<p>Birendra's brother <a href="/wiki/Gyanendra_of_Nepal" title="Gyanendra of Nepal">Gyanendra</a> became king after the massacre and the death of King Dipendra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dipendra_was_innocent:_witness_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dipendra_was_innocent:_witness-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_events">Overview of events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nepalese_royal_massacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview of events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to reports, at the dinner, Crown Prince Dipendra had been drinking heavily, had smoked large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Hashish" title="Hashish">hashish</a> and "misbehaved" with a guest which resulted in his father King <a href="/wiki/Birendra_of_Nepal" title="Birendra of Nepal">Birendra</a> telling Dipendra, who was his oldest son, to leave the party. Crown Dipendra was escorted to his room by his brother <a href="/wiki/Prince_Nirajan_of_Nepal" title="Prince Nirajan of Nepal">Prince Nirajan</a> and cousin <a href="/wiki/Paras,_Crown_Prince_of_Nepal" title="Paras, Crown Prince of Nepal">Prince Paras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>About an hour later, Dipendra returned to the party armed with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%26K_MP5" title="H&amp;K MP5">H&amp;K MP5</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Franchi_SPAS-12" title="Franchi SPAS-12">Franchi SPAS-12</a> and an <a href="/wiki/M16_rifle" title="M16 rifle">M16</a>. He fired a single shot into the ceiling before pointing the gun at his father, King Birendra. When his uncle Dhirendra tried to dissuade Dipendra from doing so, he shot his uncle in the chest at point-blank range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-2">[2]</a></sup> This was the beginning of the massacre. During the attack, Dipendra darted in and out of the hall several times, firing shots at each return. Although King Birendra managed to stay alive at the first attack, he sustained some injuries. Excerpts from the official probe report, prepared by a two-member committee in Kathmandu, states that King Birendra made an abortive last-minute attempt to shoot at Dipendra as the latter fired indiscriminately at the royals. Dipendra had thrown the 9mm caliber MP5 automatic submachine gun into the billiards room, when he returned for a second time. The king managed to take hold of it, however, his sister <a href="/wiki/Princess_Shova_Shahi_of_Nepal" title="Princess Shova Shahi of Nepal">Princess Shova Shahi</a> snatched the weapon from him and pulled out the magazine of the gun assuming it to be the only weapon Dipendra had. While this continued, Prince Paras suffered slight injuries and managed to save at least three royals, including two children, by pulling a sofa over them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-one-2">[2]</a></sup> The above version of the story is reportedly the one that Shova Shahi told the official committee. Corroborating Shova Shahi's version, Prince Paras is quoted as having said, "She [Shova] must have thought that it was the only weapon Dai (Dipendra) had but I saw that he had much more weapons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rediff.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rediff.com-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the story later told, the Queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aishwarya_of_Nepal" title="Aishwarya of Nepal">Aishwarya</a>, who came into the room when the first shots were fired, escaped the room looking for help.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-two-4">[4]</a></sup> She was accompanied in her pursuit by her younger son, Prince Nirajan. Unfortunately, they confronted and were assaulted, fatally, in the garden by the Crown Prince Dipendra. Both of them were shot multiple times. Dipendra then proceeded to a small bridge over a stream running through the palace gardens, where he shot himself six times in the back and once in the left hand, which left him critically injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-one-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nepalese_royal_massacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dipendra was proclaimed king while in a coma, but he died on 4 June 2001, after a three-day reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gyanendra_of_Nepal" title="Gyanendra of Nepal">Gyanendra</a> was appointed regent for the three days, then ascended the throne himself after Dipendra died.</p>
<p>While Dipendra lived, Gyanendra maintained that the deaths were the result of an "accidental discharge of an automatic weapon". However, he later said that he made this claim due to "legal and constitutional hurdles", since under the constitution, and by tradition, Dipendra could not have been charged with murder had he survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A full investigation took place, and Crown Prince Dipendra was found to be responsible for the killing.</p>
<p>A two-man committee comprising Keshav Prasad Upadhaya, the Supreme Court Chief Justice, and <a href="/wiki/Taranath_Ranabhat" title="Taranath Ranabhat">Taranath Ranabhat</a>, the Speaker of the House of Representatives, carried out the week-long investigation into the <a href="/wiki/Massacre" title="Massacre">massacre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The investigation concluded, after interviewing more than a hundred people including eyewitnesses and palace officials, guards and staff, that Dipendra had carried out the massacre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A large number of critics and Nepalese, both inside Nepal and abroad, disputed the official report because many facts and evidence reported by the investigation team seemed contradictory in many aspects. A close aide of Dipendra when he was prince said of Dipendra, "He can give up the throne for the sake of his love, but he can never do this kind of thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rumours_regarding_cause_of_massacre">Rumours regarding cause of massacre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nepalese_royal_massacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rumours regarding cause of massacre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The widely circulated rumour is that Prince Dipendra was angry over a marriage dispute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Dipendra's choice of bride was <a href="/wiki/Devyani_Rana" title="Devyani Rana">Devyani Rana</a>, daughter of Pashupati SJB Rana, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Rana_dynasty" title="Rana dynasty">Rana</a> clan, which the <a href="/wiki/Shah_dynasty" title="Shah dynasty">Shah dynasty</a> have a historic animosity against.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Rana clan had served as the hereditary prime ministers of Nepal, with the title <a href="/wiki/Maharaja" title="Maharaja">Maharaja</a>, until 1951, and the two clans have a long history of inter-marriages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It is also speculated that the reason for the marriage dispute over Dipendra's choice of wife was that the royal family had a position that the crown prince should not marry someone having relatives in India, as Devyani did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nepalitimes.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nepalitimes.com-14">[14]</a></sup> Also, the fact that Devyani Rana's mother, Usharaje Scindia was of Gwalior royal lineage, wasn't considered impressive by the Nepal royal family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outlookindia.com_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-outlookindia.com-15">[15]</a></sup> Prince Dipendra also courted Supriya Shah, who was the granddaughter of Queen Mother Ratna's own sister. Queen Aishwarya, though initially against the relationship due to family ties and the view that Supriya would be incompetent as a queen, as to which expressions by the Queen were heard by an aide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nepalitimes.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nepalitimes.com-14">[14]</a></sup> nevertheless favored Supriya over Devyani Rana, since if Supriya became Queen, the Shah dynasty would not have to share its power with the Ranas, entailing formation of an unwanted political alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outlookindia.com_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-outlookindia.com-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceremonial_response">Ceremonial response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nepalese_royal_massacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ceremonial response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 11 June 2001, a Hindu <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Katto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Katto (page does not exist)">katto</a> ceremony was held to exorcise or banish the spirit of the dead King from Nepal. A <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">brahmin</a> Durga Prasad Sapkota, dressed as Birendra to symbolise the late King, rode an elephant out of Kathmandu and into symbolic exile, taking many of the actual belongings of the King with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conspiracy_theories">Conspiracy theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nepalese_royal_massacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conspiracy theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Nepalese people are skeptical of the official report that the then Crown Prince <a href="/wiki/Dipendra_of_Nepal" title="Dipendra of Nepal">Dipendra</a> carried out the murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> King Birendra and his son Dipendra were very popular and well respected by the Nepalese population. Subsequently, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prachanda" title="Prachanda">Pushpa Kamal Dahal Prachanda</a>, the chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unified_Communist_Party_of_Nepal_(Maoist)" title="Unified Communist Party of Nepal (Maoist)">Nepalese Maoist Party</a>, in a public gathering claimed that the massacre was planned by the Indian intelligence agency <a href="/wiki/Research_and_Analysis_Wing" title="Research and Analysis Wing">RAW</a> or the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Promoters of these ideas allege Gyanendra had a hand in the massacre so that he could assume the throne himself. His ascension to the throne would only be possible if both of his nephews Dipendra and Nirajan were eliminated. Moreover, Gyanendra and especially his son <a href="/wiki/Paras,_Crown_Prince_of_Nepal" title="Paras, Crown Prince of Nepal">Prince Paras</a> were grossly unpopular with the public. On the day of the massacre he was in <a href="/wiki/Pokhara" title="Pokhara">Pokhara</a> whilst other royals were attending a dinner function. His wife <a href="/wiki/Queen_Komal_of_Nepal" title="Queen Komal of Nepal">Komal</a>, Paras and daughter <a href="/wiki/Princess_Prerana_of_Nepal" title="Princess Prerana of Nepal">Prerana</a> were in the room at the royal palace during the massacre. While the entire families of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birendra" title="Birendra">Birendra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dipendra_of_Nepal" title="Dipendra of Nepal">Dipendra</a> were wiped out, nobody in Gyanendra's family died: his son escaped with slight injuries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and his wife sustained a life-threatening bullet wound but survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the fact that two survivors have publicly confirmed that Dipendra did the shooting, as was documented in a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> documentary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-two-4">[4]</a></sup> the chain of events is disputed by some Nepalese. After the monarchy was abolished through a populist uprising there have been several claims refuting the official report, among them is a book published in Nepal named <i>Raktakunda</i> recounting the massacre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It looks at the incident through the eyes of one of the surviving witnesses, Queen Mother Ratna's personal maid, identified in the book as Shanta. The book, which the author says is a "historical novel", posits that two men masked as Crown Prince Dipendra fired the shots that led to the massacre. Shanta's husband, Trilochan Acharya, also a royal palace employee, was killed along with 10 royal family members, including the entire family of King Birendra. In addition to details of the royal massacre, Shanta alleged many other cover-ups by the royal family, including a claim that the previous king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Mahendra" title="King Mahendra">King Mahendra</a> committed suicide.</p>
