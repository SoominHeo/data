<p><b>Nefertem</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span>; possibly "beautiful one who closes" or "one who does not close"; also spelled <b>Nefertum</b> or <b>Nefer-temu</b>) was, in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_mythology" title="Egyptian mythology">Egyptian mythology</a>, originally a lotus flower at the creation of the world, who had arisen from the primal waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Nefertem represented both the first sunlight and the delightful smell of the Egyptian blue lotus flower, having arisen from the primal waters within an Egyptian blue water-lily, <i><a href="/wiki/Nymphaea_caerulea" title="Nymphaea caerulea">Nymphaea caerulea</a></i>. Some of the titles of Nefertem were "He Who is Beautiful" and "Water-Lily of the Sun", and a version of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Dead" title="Book of the Dead">Book of the Dead</a></i> says,</p>
<p>"Rise like Nefertem from the blue water lily, to the nostrils of Ra (the creator and sungod), and come forth upon the horizon each day."</p>
<p>Nefertem the child comes from his earth father <a href="/wiki/Nu_(mythology)" title="Nu (mythology)">Nun</a>'s black primordial waters, and his sky mother is <a href="/wiki/Nut_(goddess)" title="Nut (goddess)">Nut</a>. When he matures, he is <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>.</p>
<p>Nefertem was eventually seen as the son of the Creator god <a href="/wiki/Ptah" title="Ptah">Ptah</a>, and the goddesses <a href="/wiki/Sekhmet" title="Sekhmet">Sekhmet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bast</a> were sometimes called his mother. In <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, Nefertem is usually depicted as a beautiful young man having blue water-lily flowers around his head. As the son of Bastet, he also sometimes has the head of a <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a> or is a lion or cat reclining. The ancient Egyptians often carried small statuettes of him as good-luck charms.</p>
