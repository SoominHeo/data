<p><b>Nexus Q</b> is a discontinued <a href="/wiki/Digital_media_player" title="Digital media player">digital media player</a> that was developed by <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>. Unveiled at the <a href="/wiki/Google_I/O" title="Google I/O">Google I/O</a> developers' conference on June 2012, the device was expected to be released to the public in the United States shortly thereafter for US$300. The Nexus Q was designed to leverage Google's online media offerings, such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Play_Music" title="Google Play Music">Google Play Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Play_Movies_%26_TV" title="Google Play Movies &amp; TV">Google Play Movies &amp; TV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>, to provide a "shared" experience. Users could stream content from the supported services to a connected television, or speakers connected to an integrated amplifier, using their <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> device and the services' respective <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">apps</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Remote_control" title="Remote control">remote control</a> for queueing content and controlling playback.</p>
<p>The Nexus Q received mixed reviews from critics following its unveiling. While its unique spherical design was praised, the Q was criticized for its lack of functionality in comparison to similar devices such as <a href="/wiki/Apple_TV" title="Apple TV">Apple TV</a>, including a lack of third-party content services, no support for streaming content directly from other devices using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DLNA" title="DLNA">DLNA</a> standard, as well as other software issues that affected the usability of the device. The unclear market positioning of the Nexus Q was also criticized, as it carried a significantly higher price than competing media players with wider capabilities; <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> technology columnist <a href="/wiki/David_Pogue" title="David Pogue">David Pogue</a> characterized the device as being "wildly overbuilt" for its limited functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-baffling_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-baffling-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nexus Q was given away at no cost to attendees of Google I/O 2012, but the product's consumer launch was postponed in July 2012, purportedly to collect additional feedback. Those who had pre-ordered the Nexus Q following its unveiling received the device at no cost. The Nexus Q was quietly shelved in January 2013, and support for the device in the Google Play apps was phased out beginning in May 2013. Some of the Nexus Q's concepts would be a later product known as <a href="/wiki/Chromecast" title="Chromecast">Chromecast</a>, which similarly allows users to wirelessly queue content for playback using functions found in supported apps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recode-newchromecast_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-recode-newchromecast-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zdnet-chromecast_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-zdnet-chromecast-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nexus_Q&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An early iteration of the Nexus Q was first demoed at <a href="/wiki/Google_I/O" title="Google I/O">Google I/O</a> in 2011 under the name "Project Tungsten"; the device could stream music wirelessly from another Android device to attached speakers. It served as a component of a <a href="/wiki/Home_automation" title="Home automation">home automation</a> concept known as "Android@Home", which aimed to provide an Android-based framework for connected devices within a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag-projecttungsten_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-projecttungsten-5">[5]</a></sup> Following the launch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Music" title="Google Music">Google Music</a> service in November 2011, a decision was made to develop a hardware device to serve as a tie-in—a project that eventually resulted in the Nexus Q. Google engineering director Joe Britt explained that the device was designed to make music a "social, shared experience", encouraging real-world interaction between its users. He also felt that there had been "a generation of people who’ve grown up with white earbuds", who had thus not experienced the difference of music played on speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-itsasphere_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-itsasphere-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nexus Q was the first hardware product developed entirely in-house by Google, and was manufactured in a U.S.-based factory—which allowed Google engineers to inspect the devices during their production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-itsasphere_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-wired-itsasphere-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware_and_software">Hardware and software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nexus_Q&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hardware and software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nexus Q takes the form of a sphere with a flat base; Google designer Mike Simonian explained that its form factor was meant to represent a device that pointed towards "<a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">the cloud</a>", and towards "people all around" to reflect its communal nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-itsasphere_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-wired-itsasphere-6">[6]</a></sup> The sphere is divided into two halves; the top half can be rotated to adjust the audio volume being output over attached speakers or to other home theater equipment, and tapped to mute. In between the two halves is a ring of 32 LEDs; these lights serve as a <a href="/wiki/Music_visualization" title="Music visualization">music visualizer</a> that animate in time to music, and can be set to one of five different color schemes. The rear of the device contains a power connector, <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">ethernet</a> jack, micro <a href="/wiki/HDMI" title="HDMI">HDMI</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_audio" title="Optical audio">optical audio</a> outputs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banana_plug" title="Banana plug">banana plugs</a> for connecting speakers to the device's built-in 25-watt "stereo-grade" amplifier, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro_USB" title="Micro USB">micro USB</a> connector meant to "connect future accessories and encourage general hack-ability".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-itsasphere_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-wired-itsasphere-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-nqreview_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nqreview-7">[7]</a></sup> The Nexus Q includes an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OMAP4" title="OMAP4">OMAP4</a> processor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-nqkitkat_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-nqkitkat-8">[8]</a></sup> 1 GB of RAM, and 16 GB of storage used for caching of streamed content. It also supports <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-field_communication" title="Near-field communication">near-field communication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> for pairing devices and initial setup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-itsasphere_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-wired-itsasphere-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-nqreview_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nqreview-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget-review_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-review-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nexus Q runs a stripped-down version of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Android_4.0" title="Android 4.0">Android 4.0 "Ice Cream Sandwich"</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-nqkitkat_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ars-nqkitkat-8">[8]</a></sup> and is controlled solely via supported apps on Android devices running <a href="/wiki/Android_Jelly_Bean" title="Android Jelly Bean">Android 4.1 "Jelly Bean"</a>. Google announced plans to support older versions of Android following the device's official launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget-review_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-review-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-baffling_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-baffling-2">[2]</a></sup> Media could be queued to play on the device using a "Play to" button shown within the Google Play Music, Google Play Movies &amp; TV, and YouTube apps. Content is streamed directly from the services by the Nexus Q, with the Android device used like a <a href="/wiki/Remote_control" title="Remote control">remote control</a>. For music, multiple users could collaboratively queue songs from Google Play Music onto a playlist. A management app could be used to adjust Nexus Q hardware settings. Nexus Q did not support any third-party media services, nor could media be stored to the device, or streamed to it using the standardized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DLNA" title="DLNA">DLNA</a> protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-itsasphere_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-wired-itsasphere-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-nqreview_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nqreview-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget-review_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-review-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nexus_Q&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most criticism of the Nexus Q centered on its relatively high price in comparison to contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_streaming" title="Media streaming">media streaming</a> devices and set-top boxes, such as <a href="/wiki/Apple_TV" title="Apple TV">Apple TV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roku" title="Roku">Roku</a>, especially considering its lack of features when compared to these devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-nqreview_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nqreview-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget-review_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-review-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-baffling_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-baffling-2">[2]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> technology columnist <a href="/wiki/David_Pogue" title="David Pogue">David Pogue</a> described the Nexus Q as being a "baffling" device, stating that "Google must have bigger plans for this thing. It's wildly overbuilt for its incredibly limited functions, and far too expensive. For now, I can think of only one class of customer who should consider buying the black Nexus Q sphere: people whose living rooms are dominated by <a href="/wiki/Bowling_ball" title="Bowling ball">bowling-ball</a> collections."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-baffling_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-baffling-2">[2]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Engadget" title="Engadget">Engadget</a></i> was similarly mixed, arguing that while the Nexus Q was a "sophisticated, beautiful device with such a fine-grained degree of engineering you can't help but respect it", and an amplifier capable of producing "very clean sound", the device was ultimately a "high-price novelty" that lacked support for DLNA, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_audio" title="Lossless audio">lossless audio</a>, and playback of content from external or internal storage among other features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget-review_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-review-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuation">Discontinuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nexus_Q&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Discontinuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6016" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg/220px-Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg/330px-Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg/440px-Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chromecast_plugged_into_TV.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Chromecast" title="Chromecast">Chromecast</a> has been seen as a successor to the Nexus Q.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zdnet-chromecast_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-zdnet-chromecast-4">[4]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nexus Q units were distributed as a gift to attendees of Google I/O 2012, with online pre-orders to the public opening at a price of US$300. On July 31, 2012, Google announced that it would delay the official launch of the Nexus Q in order to address early feedback, and that all customers who pre-ordered the device would receive it for free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet-delayed_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet-delayed-10">[10]</a></sup> By January 2013, the device was no longer listed for sale on the Google Play website, implying that its official release had been cancelled indefinitely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-nqnotforsale_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-nqnotforsale-11">[11]</a></sup> Google began to discontinue software support for the Nexus Q in May 2013, beginning with an update to the Google Play Music app, and a similar update to Google Play Movies &amp; TV in June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-droidlife-nqmoviesbroken_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-droidlife-nqmoviesbroken-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-gmunsupported_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge-gmunsupported-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nexus Q has also been the subject of third-party development and experimentation; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XDA-developers" title="XDA-developers">XDA-developers</a> users discovered means for side-loading Android applications onto the Nexus Q to expand its functionality. One user demonstrated the ability to use a traditional Android home screen with keyboard and mouse input, as well as the official Netflix app.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-hacked_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge-hacked-14">[14]</a></sup> In December 2013, an unofficial build of <a href="/wiki/Android_KitKat" title="Android KitKat">Android 4.4 "KitKat"</a> based on <a href="/wiki/CyanogenMod" title="CyanogenMod">CyanogenMod</a> code was also released for the Nexus Q, although it was unstable and lacked reliable Wi-Fi support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-nqkitkat_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ars-nqkitkat-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nexus Q received a <i>de facto</i> successor in July 2013 with the unveiling of <a href="/wiki/Chromecast" title="Chromecast">Chromecast</a>, a streaming dongle that similarly allows users to queue the playback of remote content ("<a href="/wiki/Google_Cast" title="Google Cast">cast</a>") via a mobile device. Chromecast is contrasted by its compact form factor, the availability of an <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">SDK</a> that allows third-party apps and services to integrate with the device, and its considerably lower price in comparison to the Nexus Q.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zdnet-chromecast_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-zdnet-chromecast-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recode-newchromecast_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-recode-newchromecast-3">[3]</a></sup> In late-2014, Google and <a href="/wiki/Asus" title="Asus">Asus</a> released a second Nexus-branded digital media player known as the <a href="/wiki/Nexus_Player" title="Nexus Player">Nexus Player</a>, which served as a launch device for the digital media player and smart TV platform <a href="/wiki/Android_TV" title="Android TV">Android TV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-nxplayer_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nxplayer-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
