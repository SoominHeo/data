<p><b>Nelson's Pillar</b> (also known as the <b>Nelson Pillar</b> or simply <b>the Pillar</b>) was a large granite column capped by a statue of <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Horatio Nelson</a>, built in the centre of what was then Sackville Street (later renamed <a href="/wiki/O%27Connell_Street" title="O'Connell Street">O'Connell Street</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland. Completed in 1809 when Ireland was part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a>, it survived until March 1966, when it was severely damaged by explosives planted by <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Irish republicans</a>. Its remnants were later destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Army" title="Irish Army">Irish Army</a>.</p>
<p>The decision to build the monument was taken by <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Corporation" title="Dublin Corporation">Dublin Corporation</a> in the euphoria following Nelson's victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Battle of Trafalgar</a> in 1805. The original design by <a href="/wiki/William_Wilkins_(architect)" title="William Wilkins (architect)">William Wilkins</a> was greatly modified by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Johnston_(architect)" title="Francis Johnston (architect)">Francis Johnston</a>, on grounds of cost. The statue was sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kirk_(sculptor)" title="Thomas Kirk (sculptor)">Thomas Kirk</a>. From its opening on 29 October 1809 the Pillar was a popular tourist attraction, but provoked aesthetic and political controversy from the outset. A prominent city centre monument honouring an Englishman rankled as nationalist sentiment grew, and throughout the 19th century there were calls for it to be removed, or replaced with a memorial to an Irish hero.</p>
<p>Parts of central Dublin were destroyed during the 1916 <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a>, but although it was near the rebels' headquarters, the Pillar remained unscathed. It remained in the city as Ireland became an independent <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Free State</a> in 1922, and a <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">republic</a> in 1949. The chief legal barrier to its removal was the trust created at the Pillar's inception, the terms of which gave the trustees a duty in perpetuity to preserve the monument. Successive Irish governments failed to deliver legislation overriding the trust. Although influential literary figures such as <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>, <a href="/wiki/W._B._Yeats" title="W. B. Yeats">W. B. Yeats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_St._John_Gogarty" title="Oliver St. John Gogarty">Oliver St. John Gogarty</a> defended the Pillar on historical and cultural grounds, pressure for its removal intensified in the years preceding the 50th anniversary of the Rising, and its sudden demise was, on the whole, well received by the public. Although it was widely believed that the action was the work of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1922%E2%80%9369)" title="Irish Republican Army (1922–69)">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA), the police were unable to identify any of those responsible.</p>
<p>After years of debate and numerous proposals, the site was occupied in 2003 by the <a href="/wiki/Spire_of_Dublin" title="Spire of Dublin">Spire of Dublin</a>, a slim needle-like structure rising almost three times the height of the Pillar. In 2000 a former republican activist gave a radio interview in which he admitted planting the explosives in 1966, but after questioning him the police decided not to take action. Relics of the Pillar are found in Dublin museums and appear as decorative stonework elsewhere, and its memory is preserved in numerous works of Irish literature.</p>

</div>
<p>The redevelopment of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Liffey" title="River Liffey">River Liffey</a> began in the early 18th century, largely through the enterprise of the property speculator <a href="/wiki/Luke_Gardiner" title="Luke Gardiner">Luke Gardiner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews_and_Coleman_2009_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews_and_Coleman_2009-1">[1]</a></sup> His best-known work was the transformation in the 1740s of a narrow lane called Drogheda Street, which he demolished and turned into a broad thoroughfare lined with large and imposing town houses. He renamed it Sackville Street, in honour of <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Sackville,_1st_Duke_of_Dorset" title="Lionel Sackville, 1st Duke of Dorset">Lionel Sackville, 1st Duke of Dorset</a>, who served as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a> from 1731 to 1737 and from 1751 to 1755.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHopkins2002114_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins2002114-2">[2]</a></sup> After Gardiner's death in 1755 Dublin's growth continued, with many fine public buildings and grand squares, the city's status magnified by the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Parliament of Ireland</a> for six months of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKilfeather200554_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKilfeather200554-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union of 1800</a>, which united Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> under a single <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster polity</a>, ended the Irish parliament and presaged a period of decline for the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHunt2014137_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHunt2014137-4">[4]</a></sup> The historian <a href="/wiki/Tristram_Hunt" title="Tristram Hunt">Tristram Hunt</a> writes: "[T]he capital's dynamism vanished, absenteeism returned and the big houses lost their patrons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHunt2014137_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHunt2014137-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first monument in Sackville Street was built in 1759 in the location where the Nelson Pillar would eventually stand. The subject was <a href="/wiki/William_Blakeney,_1st_Baron_Blakeney" title="William Blakeney, 1st Baron Blakeney">William Blakeney, 1st Baron Blakeney</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>-born army officer whose career extended over more than 60 years and ended with his surrender to the French after the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Fort_St_Philip_(1756)" title="Siege of Fort St Philip (1756)">Siege of Minorca</a> in 1756.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStephens_and_Harding_2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStephens_and_Harding_2008-5">[5]</a></sup> A brass statue sculpted by <a href="/wiki/John_van_Nost_the_younger" title="John van Nost the younger">John van Nost the younger</a> was unveiled on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_Day" title="St Patrick's Day">St Patrick's Day</a>, 17 March 1759.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon20145.E2.80.938_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon20145.E2.80.938-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[n 1]</a></sup> Donal Fallon, in his history of the Pillar, states that almost from its inception the Blakeney statue was a target for vandalism. Its fate is uncertain; Fallon records that it might have been melted down for cannon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon20148_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon20148-8">[7]</a></sup> but it had certainly been removed by 1805.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy20137.E2.80.938_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy20137.E2.80.938-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trafalgar">Trafalgar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trafalgar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fall_of_Nelson.jpg"><img alt="Painting of Nelson's death scene" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1422" data-file-width="2000" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Fall_of_Nelson.jpg/220px-Fall_of_Nelson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Fall_of_Nelson.jpg/330px-Fall_of_Nelson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Fall_of_Nelson.jpg/440px-Fall_of_Nelson.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fall_of_Nelson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Nelson's death aboard HMS <i>Victory</i>, 21 October 1805</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 21 October 1805, a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Naval</a> fleet commanded by <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Vice Admiral Lord Nelson</a> defeated the combined fleets of the French and Spanish navies in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Battle of Trafalgar</a>. At the height of the battle Nelson was mortally wounded on board his flagship, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Victory" title="HMS Victory">HMS <i>Victory</i></a>; by the time he died later that day, victory was assured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERodger_2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERodger_2009-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nelson had been hailed in Dublin seven years earlier, after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Nile" title="Battle of the Nile">Battle of the Nile</a>, as defender of the Harp and Crown, the respective symbols of Ireland and Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPakenham1992337_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPakenham1992337-11">[10]</a></sup> When news of Trafalgar reached the city on 8 November, there were similar scenes of patriotic celebration, together with a desire that the fallen hero should be commemorated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201424_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201424-12">[11]</a></sup> The mercantile classes had particular reason to be grateful for a victory that restored the freedom of the high seas and removed the threat of a French invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> Many of the city's population had relatives who had been involved in the battle: up to one-third of the sailors in Nelson's fleet were from Ireland, including around 400 from Dublin itself. In his short account of the Pillar, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Campbell_Kennedy" title="Dennis Campbell Kennedy">Dennis Kennedy</a> considers that Nelson would have been regarded in the city as a hero, not just among the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a> but by many Catholics among the rising middle and professional classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201325.E2.80.9327_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201325.E2.80.9327-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first step towards a permanent memorial to Nelson was taken on 18 November 1805 by the city <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldermen" title="Aldermen">aldermen</a>, who after sending a message of congratulation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_George_III" title="King George III">King George III</a>, agreed that the erection of a statue would form a suitable tribute to Nelson's memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194853_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194853-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201426_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201426-16">[15]</a></sup> On 28 November, after a public meeting had supported this sentiment, a "Nelson committee" was established, chaired by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_Dublin" title="Lord Mayor of Dublin">Lord Mayor</a>. It contained four of the city's Westminster MPs, alongside other city notables including <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Guinness_II" title="Arthur Guinness II">Arthur Guinness</a>, the son of the brewery founder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194854_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194854-17">[16]</a></sup> The committee's initial tasks were to decide precisely what form the monument should take and where it should be put. They had also to raise the funds to pay for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy20133.E2.80.934_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy20133.E2.80.934-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inception.2C_design_and_construction">Inception, design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inception, design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At its first meeting the Nelson committee established a public subscription, and early in 1806 invited artists and architects to submit design proposals for a monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201427.E2.80.9328_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201427.E2.80.9328-19">[18]</a></sup> No specifications were provided, but the contemporary European vogue in commemorative architecture was for the classical form, typified by <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan's Column">Trajan's Column</a> in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy20133.E2.80.934_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy20133.E2.80.934-18">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Victory_column" title="Victory column">Monumental columns</a>, or "pillars of victory", were uncommon in Ireland at the time; the Cumberland Column in <a href="/wiki/Birr,_County_Offaly" title="Birr, County Offaly">Birr, County Offaly</a>, erected in 1747, was a rare exception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEContae_U.C3.ADbh_Fhail.C3.AD_County_Council.2C_February_2009_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEContae_U.C3.ADbh_Fhail.C3.AD_County_Council.2C_February_2009-20">[19]</a></sup> From the entries submitted, the Nelson committee's choice was that of a young English architect, <a href="/wiki/William_Wilkins_(architect)" title="William Wilkins (architect)">William Wilkins</a>, then in the early stages of a distinguished career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[n 2]</a></sup> Wilkins's proposals envisaged a tall <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric column</a> on a plinth, surmounted by a sculpted Roman <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy20136_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy20136-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The choice of the Sackville Street site was not unanimous. The <a href="/wiki/Wide_Streets_Commission" title="Wide Streets Commission">Wide Streets Commissioners</a> were worried about traffic congestion, and argued for a riverside location visible from the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> Another suggestion was for a seaside position, perhaps <a href="/wiki/Howth_Head" title="Howth Head">Howth Head</a> at the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Bay" title="Dublin Bay">Dublin Bay</a>. The recent presence of the Blakeney statue in Sackville Street, and a desire to arrest the street's decline in the post-parliamentary years, were factors that may have influenced the final selection of that site which, Kennedy says, was the preferred choice of the Lord Lieutenant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy20136.E2.80.938_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy20136.E2.80.938-24">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>By mid-1807, fundraising was proving difficult; sums raised at that point were well short of the likely cost of erecting Wilkins's column. The committee informed the architect with regret that "means were not placed in their hands to enable them to gratify him, as well as themselves, by executing his design precisely as he had given it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201310.E2.80.9311_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201310.E2.80.9311-26">[24]</a></sup> They employed <a href="/wiki/Francis_Johnston_(architect)" title="Francis Johnston (architect)">Francis Johnston</a>, architect to the City Board of Works, to make cost-cutting adjustments to Wilkins's scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurphy201011_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurphy201011-27">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[n 3]</a></sup> Johnston simplified the design, substituting a large functional block or pedestal for Wilkins's delicate plinth, and replacing the proposed galley with a statue of Nelson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201310.E2.80.9311_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201310.E2.80.9311-26">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kirk_(sculptor)" title="Thomas Kirk (sculptor)">Thomas Kirk</a>, a sculptor from <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>, was commissioned to provide the statue, to be fashioned from <a href="/wiki/Portland_stone" title="Portland stone">Portland stone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194859_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194859-30">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKilfeather2005260_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKilfeather2005260-31">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>By December 1807 the fund stood at £3,827, far short of the estimated £6,500 required to finance the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201315_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201315-25">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[n 4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, by the beginning of 1808 the committee felt confident enough to begin the work, and organised the laying of the foundation stone. This ceremony took place on 15 February 1808—the day following the anniversary of Nelson's victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_St_Vincent_(1797)" title="Battle of Cape St Vincent (1797)">Battle of Cape St Vincent</a> in 1797<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201316.E2.80.9317_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201316.E2.80.9317-34">[30]</a></sup>—amid much pomp, in the presence of the new Lord Lieutenant, the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lennox,_4th_Duke_of_Richmond" title="Charles Lennox, 4th Duke of Richmond">Duke of Richmond</a>, along with various civic dignitaries and city notables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurphy20109_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurphy20109-35">[31]</a></sup> A memorial plaque eulogising Nelson's Trafalgar victory was attached to the stone. The committee continued to raise money as construction proceeded;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201316.E2.80.9317_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201316.E2.80.9317-34">[30]</a></sup> when the project was complete in the autumn of 1809, costs totalled £6,856, but contributions had reached £7,138, providing the committee with a surplus of £282.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357-36">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>When finished, the monument complete with its statue rose to a height of 134 feet (40.8 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[n 5]</a></sup> The four sides of the pedestal were engraved with the names and dates of Nelson's greatest victories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357-36">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[n 6]</a></sup> A spiral stairway of 168 steps ascended the hollow interior of the column, to a viewing platform immediately beneath the statue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201433_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201433-41">[35]</a></sup> According to the committee's published report, 22,090 cubic feet (626 m<sup>3</sup>) of black <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and 7,310 cubic feet (207 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> had been used to build the column and its pedestal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201432_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201432-42">[36]</a></sup> The Pillar opened to the public on 21 October 1809, on the fourth anniversary of the Battle of Trafalgar; for ten <a href="/wiki/Penny_(British_pre-decimal_coin)" title="Penny (British pre-decimal coin)">pre-decimal</a> pence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357-36">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[n 7]</a></sup> visitors could climb to a viewing platform just below the statue, and enjoy what an early report describes as "a superb panoramic view of the city, the country and the fine bay".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWarburton.2C_Whitelaw_and_Walsh_18181101_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarburton.2C_Whitelaw_and_Walsh_18181101-39">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[n 8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_1809.E2.80.931966">History 1809–1966</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History 1809–1966">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1809.E2.80.931916">1809–1916</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1809–1916">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Pillar quickly became a popular tourist attraction; Kennedy writes that "for the next 157 years its ascent was a must on every visitor's list".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201320_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201320-46">[38]</a></sup> Yet from the beginning there were criticisms, on both political and aesthetic grounds. The September 1809 issue of the <i>Irish Monthly Magazine</i>, edited by the revolution-minded Walter "Watty" Cox,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWebb:_A_Compendium_of_Irish_Biography_1878_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWebb:_A_Compendium_of_Irish_Biography_1878-47">[39]</a></sup> reported that "our independence has been wrested from us, not by the arms of France but by the gold of England. The statue of Nelson records the glory of a mistress and the transformation of our senate into a discount office".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> In an early (1818) history of the city of Dublin, the writers express awe at the scale of the monument, but are critical of several of its features: its proportions are described as "ponderous", the pedestal as "unsightly" and the column itself as "clumsy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWarburton.2C_Whitelaw_and_Walsh_18181102.E2.80.9303_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarburton.2C_Whitelaw_and_Walsh_18181102.E2.80.9303-37">[33]</a></sup> However, the <i>Hibernian Magazine</i> thought the statue was a good likeness of its subject, and that the Pillar's position in the centre of the wide street gave the eye a focal point in what was otherwise "wastes of pavements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194860_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194860-48">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>By 1830, rising nationalist sentiment in Ireland made it likely that the Pillar was "the Ascendancy's last hurrah"—Kennedy observes that it probably could not have been built at any later date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201330_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201330-49">[41]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the monument often attracted favourable comment from visitors; in 1842 the writer <a href="/wiki/William_Makepeace_Thackeray" title="William Makepeace Thackeray">William Makepeace Thackeray</a> noted Nelson "upon a stone-pillar" in the middle of the "exceedingly broad and handsome" Sackville Street: "The Post Office is on his right hand (only it is cut off); and on his left, 'Gresham's' and the 'Imperial Hotel' ".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThackeray191122.E2.80.9323_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThackeray191122.E2.80.9323-50">[42]</a></sup> A few years later, the monument was a source of pride to some citizens, who dubbed it "Dublin's Glory" when <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> visited the city in 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1840 and 1843 <a href="/wiki/Nelson%27s_Column" title="Nelson's Column">Nelson's Column</a> was erected in London's <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>. With an overall height of 170 feet (52 m) it was taller than its Dublin equivalent and, at £47,000, much more costly to erect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201440_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201440-51">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[n 9]</a></sup> It has no internal staircase or viewing platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201313_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201313-53">[44]</a></sup> The London column was the subject of an attack during the <a href="/wiki/Fenian_dynamite_campaign" title="Fenian dynamite campaign">Fenian dynamite campaign</a> in May 1884, when a quantity of explosives was placed at its base but failed to detonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201440_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201440-51">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1853 the queen attended the <a href="/wiki/Great_Industrial_Exhibition_(1853)" title="Great Industrial Exhibition (1853)">Dublin Great Industrial Exhibition</a>, where a city plan was displayed that envisaged the removal of the Pillar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> This proved impossible, as since 1811 legal responsibility for the Pillar had been vested in a trust,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201318_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201318-54">[45]</a></sup> under the terms of which the trustees were required "to embellish and uphold the monument in perpetuation of the object for which it was subscribed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201347_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201347-55">[46]</a></sup> Any action to remove or resite the Pillar, or replace the statue, required the passage of an <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Acts of Parliament in the United Kingdom">Act of Parliament</a> in London; <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Corporation" title="Dublin Corporation">Dublin Corporation</a> (the city government) had no authority in the matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201337_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201337-56">[47]</a></sup> No action followed the city plan suggestion, but the following years saw regular attempts to remove the monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> A proposal was made in 1876 by Alderman Peter McSwiney, a former Lord Mayor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDublin_City_Council_.28Lord_Mayors.29_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDublin_City_Council_.28Lord_Mayors.29-57">[48]</a></sup> to replace the "unsightly structure" with a memorial to the recently deceased <a href="/wiki/John_Gray_(Irish_politician)" title="John Gray (Irish politician)">Sir John Gray</a>, who had done much to provide Dublin with a clean water supply. The Corporation was unable to advance this idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201442_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201442-58">[49]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1882 the Moore Street Market and Dublin City Improvement Act was passed by the Westminster parliament, overriding the trust and giving the Corporation authority to resite the Pillar, but subject to a strict timetable, within which the city authorities found it impossible to act. The Act lapsed and the Pillar remained;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201338_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201338-59">[50]</a></sup> a similar attempt, with the same result, was made in 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> Not all Dubliners favoured demolition; some businesses considered the Pillar to be the city's focal point, and the tramway company petitioned for its retention as it marked the central tram terminus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194861_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194861-60">[51]</a></sup> "In many ways", says Fallon, "the pillar had become part of the fabric of the city".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201444_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201444-61">[52]</a></sup> Kennedy writes: "A familiar and very large if rather scruffy piece of the city's furniture, it was <i>The</i> Pillar, Dublin's Pillar rather than Nelson's Pillar ... it was also an outing, an experience".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201341_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201341-62">[53]</a></sup> The Dublin sculptor <a href="/wiki/John_Hughes_(sculptor)" title="John Hughes (sculptor)">John Hughes</a> invited students at the Metropolitan School of Art to "admire the elegance and dignity" of Kirk's statue, "and the beauty of the silhouette".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurphy201012_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurphy201012-63">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1894 there were some significant alterations to the Pillar's fabric. The original entry on the west side, whereby visitors entered the pedestal by a flight of steps taking them down below street level, was replaced by a new ground level entrance on the south side, with a grand porch. The whole monument was surrounded by heavy iron railings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenchy194856.E2.80.9357-36">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[n 10]</a></sup> In the new century, despite the growing nationalism within Dublin—80 per cent of the Corporation's councillors were nationalists of some description—the pillar was liberally decorated with flags and streamers to mark the 1905 Trafalgar centenary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201445.E2.80.9346_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201445.E2.80.9346-67">[57]</a></sup> The changing political atmosphere had long been signalled by the arrival in Sackville Street of further monuments, all celebrating distinctively Irish heroes, in what the historian Yvonne Whelan describes as defiance of the British Government, a "challenge in stone". Between the 1860s and 1911, Nelson was joined by monuments to <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O'Connell">Daniel O'Connell</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_O%27Brien" title="William Smith O'Brien">William Smith O'Brien</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a>, as well as Sir John Gray and the temperance campaigner <a href="/wiki/Theobald_Mathew_(temperance_reformer)" title="Theobald Mathew (temperance reformer)">Father Matthew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhelan201494_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhelan201494-68">[58]</a></sup> Meanwhile, in 1861, after decades of construction, the <a href="/wiki/Wellington_Monument,_Dublin" title="Wellington Monument, Dublin">Wellington Monument</a> in Dublin's <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Park" title="Phoenix Park">Phoenix Park</a> was completed, the foundation stone having been laid in 1817.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGarnett195254_and_61_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGarnett195254_and_61-69">[59]</a></sup> This vast obelisk, 220 feet (67 m) high and 120 feet (37 m) square at the base,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECasey2005308_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECasey2005308-70">[60]</a></sup> honoured <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington</a>, Dublin-born and a former <a href="/wiki/Chief_Secretary_for_Ireland" title="Chief Secretary for Ireland">Chief Secretary for Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201451_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201451-71">[61]</a></sup> Unlike the Pillar, Wellington's obelisk has attracted little controversy and has not been the subject of physical attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIndependent.ie._26_August_2003_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIndependent.ie._26_August_2003-72">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Easter_Rising.2C_April_1916">Easter Rising, April 1916</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Easter Rising, April 1916">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Easter_Monday" title="Easter Monday">Easter Monday</a>, 24 April 1916, units of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Volunteers" title="Irish Volunteers">Irish Volunteers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Citizen_Army" title="Irish Citizen Army">Irish Citizen Army</a> seized several prominent buildings and streets in central Dublin, including the <a href="/wiki/General_Post_Office,_Dublin" title="General Post Office, Dublin">General Post Office</a> (GPO) in Sackville Street, one of the buildings nearest the Pillar. They set up headquarters at the GPO where they declared an Irish Republic under a provisional government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201453.E2.80.9354_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201453.E2.80.9354-73">[63]</a></sup> One of the first recorded actions of the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> occurred near the Pillar when <a href="/wiki/Lancer" title="Lancer">lancers</a> from the nearby <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_Street,_Dublin" title="Marlborough Street, Dublin">Marlborough Street</a> barracks, sent to investigate the disturbance, were fired on from the GPO. They withdrew in confusion, leaving four soldiers and two horses dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETownshend2006184_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETownshend2006184-74">[64]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Life_goes_on..._(6937669789).jpg"><img alt="Black-and-white photograph of The Pillar surrounded by ruins of destroyed buildings" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2105" data-file-width="3000" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Life_goes_on..._%286937669789%29.jpg/220px-Life_goes_on..._%286937669789%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Life_goes_on..._%286937669789%29.jpg/330px-Life_goes_on..._%286937669789%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Life_goes_on..._%286937669789%29.jpg/440px-Life_goes_on..._%286937669789%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Life_goes_on..._(6937669789).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sackville Street after the Rising, showing the burnt out shell of the General Post Office and the intact Pillar in the background</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the days that followed, Sackville Street and particularly the area around the Pillar became a battleground. According to some histories, insurgents attempted to blow up the Pillar. The accounts are unconfirmed and were disputed by many that fought in the Rising,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201455.E2.80.9356_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201455.E2.80.9356-75">[65]</a></sup> on the grounds that the Pillar's large base provided them with useful cover as they moved to and from other rebel positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201343.E2.80.9344_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201343.E2.80.9344-76">[66]</a></sup> By Thursday night, British artillery fire had set much of Sackville Street ablaze, but according to the writer Peter De Rosa's account: "On his pillar, Nelson surveyed it all serenely, as though he were lit up by a thousand lamps".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990350_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990350-77">[67]</a></sup> The statue was visible against the fiery backdrop from as far as <a href="/wiki/Killiney" title="Killiney">Killiney</a>, 9 miles (14 km) away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990351_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990351-78">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>By Saturday, when the provisional government finally surrendered, many of the Sackville Street buildings between the Pillar and the Liffey had been destroyed or badly damaged, including the Imperial Hotel that Thackeray had admired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETownshend2006266_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETownshend2006266-79">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990358.E2.80.9359_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990358.E2.80.9359-80">[70]</a></sup> Of the GPO, only the façade remained; against the tide of opinion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_Shaw" title="Bernard Shaw">Bernard Shaw</a> said the demolition of the city's classical architecture scarcely mattered: "What does matter is the Liffey slums have not been demolished".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaw19166_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaw19166-81">[71]</a></sup> An account in a New York newspaper reported that the Pillar had been lost in the destruction of the street,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201461_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201461-82">[72]</a></sup> but it had sustained only minor damage, chiefly bullet marks on the column and statue itself—one shot is said to have taken off Nelson's nose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201457_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201457-83">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_an_independent_Ireland">In an independent Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In an independent Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Anglo-Irish war of 1919–21</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">treaty</a> that followed, Ireland was partitioned; Dublin became the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominion</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="British Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth of Nations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.22The_Partition_of_Ireland.22_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.22The_Partition_of_Ireland.22-84">[74]</a></sup> From December 1922, when the Free State was inaugurated, the Pillar became an issue for the Irish rather than the British government. In 1923, when Sackville Street was again in ruins during the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201465_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201465-85">[75]</a></sup> <i>The Irish Builder and Engineer</i> magazine called the original siting of the Pillar a "blunder" and asked for its removal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Builder_and_Engineer.27.27_30_June_1923497_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Builder_and_Engineer.27.27_30_June_1923497-86">[76]</a></sup> a view echoed by the Dublin Citizens Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201344.E2.80.9345_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201344.E2.80.9345-87">[77]</a></sup> The poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats">William Butler Yeats</a>, who had become a member of the <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann_(Irish_Free_State)" title="Seanad Éireann (Irish Free State)">Irish Senate</a>, favoured its re-erection elsewhere, but thought it should not, as some wished, be destroyed, because "the life and work of the people who built it are part of our tradition."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sackville Street was renamed O'Connell Street in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECasey2005212_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECasey2005212-88">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[n 11]</a></sup> The following year the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Metropolitan_Police" title="Dublin Metropolitan Police">Dublin Metropolitan Police</a> and the Dublin Civic Survey demanded legislation to allow the Pillar's removal, without success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201344.E2.80.9345_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201344.E2.80.9345-87">[77]</a></sup> Pressure continued, and in 1926 <i>The Manchester Guardian</i> reported that the Pillar was to be taken down, "as it was a hindrance to modern traffic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Manchester_Guardian.27.27_26_March_19269_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Manchester_Guardian.27.27_26_March_19269-90">[79]</a></sup> Requests for action—removal, destruction or the replacement of the statue with that of an Irish hero—continued up to the Second World War and beyond; the main stumbling blocks remained the trustees' strict interpretation of the terms of the trust, and the unwillingness of successive Irish governments to take legislative action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201344.E2.80.9345_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201344.E2.80.9345-87">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201468.E2.80.9369.2C_71.E2.80.9372_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201468.E2.80.9369.2C_71.E2.80.9372-91">[80]</a></sup> In 1936 the magazine of the ultra-nationalist <a href="/wiki/Blueshirts" title="Blueshirts">Blueshirts</a> movement remarked that this inactivity showed a failure in the national spirit: "The conqueror is gone, but the scars which he left remain, and the victim will not even try to remove them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201470.E2.80.9371.2C_quoting_from_.27.27The_Blueshirt.27.27.2C_1_March_1935_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201470.E2.80.9371.2C_quoting_from_.27.27The_Blueshirt.27.27.2C_1_March_1935-92">[81]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #E0E6F8;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Man and boy I have lived in Dublin, on and off, for 68 years. When I was a young fellow we didn't talk about Nelson's Column or Nelson's Pillar, we spoke of the Pillar, and everyone knew what we meant".</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Thomas Bodkin, 1955<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201477_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201477-93">[82]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>By 1949 the Irish Free State had evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and left the British Commonwealth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990505_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Rosa1990505-94">[83]</a></sup> but not all Irish opinion favoured the removal of the Pillar. That year the architectural historian <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_(historian)" title="John Harvey (historian)">John Harvey</a> called it "a grand work", and argued that without it, "O'Connell Street would lose much of its vitality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarvey194931_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarvey194931-95">[84]</a></sup> Most of the pressure to get rid of it, he said, came from "traffic maniacs who ... fail to visualise the chaos which would result from creating a through current of traffic at this point".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarvey194931_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarvey194931-95">[84]</a></sup> In a 1955 radio broadcast <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bodkin" title="Thomas Bodkin">Thomas Bodkin</a>, former director of the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Ireland" title="National Gallery of Ireland">National Gallery of Ireland</a>, praised not only the monument, but Nelson himself: "He was a man of extraordinary gallantry. He lost his eye fighting bravely, and his arm in a similar fashion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201477_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201477-93">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 October 1955, a group of nine students from <a href="/wiki/University_College_Dublin" title="University College Dublin">University College Dublin</a> obtained keys from the Pillar's custodian and locked themselves inside, with an assortment of equipment including <a href="/wiki/Flamethrower" title="Flamethrower">flame throwers</a>. From the gallery they hung a poster of <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Barry" title="Kevin Barry">Kevin Barry</a>, a Dublin <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA) volunteer executed by the British during the War of Independence. A crowd gathered below, and began to sing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_rebel_music" title="Irish rebel music">Irish rebel</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Kevin_Barry_(song)" title="Kevin Barry (song)">Kevin Barry</a>". Eventually members of the <a href="/wiki/Garda_S%C3%ADoch%C3%A1na" title="Garda Síochána">Gardaí</a> (Irish police) broke into the Pillar and ended the demonstration. No action was taken against the students, whose principal purpose, the Gardaí claimed, was publicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201487.E2.80.9389_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201487.E2.80.9389-96">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1956, members of the <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a> party, then in opposition, proposed that the statue be replaced by one of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmet" title="Robert Emmet">Robert Emmet</a>, Protestant leader of <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1803" title="Irish Rebellion of 1803">an abortive rebellion in 1803</a>. They thought that such a gesture might inspire Protestants in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> to fight for a reunited Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201472_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201472-97">[86]</a></sup> In the North the possibility of dismantling and re-erecting the monument in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> was raised in the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Executive Committee of the Privy Council of Northern Ireland">Stormont parliament</a>, but the initiative failed to gain the support of the Northern Ireland government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201474.E2.80.9375_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201474.E2.80.9375-98">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1959 a new Fianna Fáil government under <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Lemass" title="Seán Lemass">Seán Lemass</a> deferred the question of the Pillar's removal on the grounds of cost; five years later Lemass agreed to "look at" the question of replacing Nelson's statue with one of <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Pearse" title="Patrick Pearse">Patrick Pearse</a>, the leader of the Easter Rising, in time for the 50th anniversary of the Rising in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201347.E2.80.9348_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201347.E2.80.9348-99">[88]</a></sup> An offer from the Irish-born American trade union leader <a href="/wiki/Mike_Quill" title="Mike Quill">Mike Quill</a> to finance the removal of the Pillar was not taken up, and as the anniversary approached, Nelson remained in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201474.E2.80.9375_98-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201474.E2.80.9375-98">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction">Destruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Destruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg"><img alt="Half-destroyed Pillar with top part and statue missing" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="541" data-file-width="800" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg/220px-Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg/330px-Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg/440px-Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nelson%27s_Pillar_destroyed.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The pillar on the morning of 8 March 1966</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Shortly after 1:30 on the morning of 8 March 1966, a powerful explosion destroyed the upper portion of the Pillar and brought Nelson's statue crashing to the ground amid hundreds of tons of rubble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201350.E2.80.9351_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201350.E2.80.9351-100">[89]</a></sup> O'Connell Street was almost deserted at the time, although a dance in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Metropole,_Dublin" title="Hotel Metropole, Dublin">Hotel Metropole</a>'s ballroom was about to end and bring crowds on to the street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> There were no casualties—a taxi-driver parked close by had a narrow escape—and damage to property was relatively light given the strength of the blast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201494_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201494-101">[90]</a></sup> What was left of the Pillar was a jagged stump, 70 feet (21 m) high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first government response to the action the <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Justice_and_Equality" title="Minister for Justice and Equality">Justice minister</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brian_Lenihan,_Snr" title="Brian Lenihan, Snr">Brian Lenihan</a>, condemned what he described as "an outrage which was planned and committed without any regard to the lives of the citizens".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Times.27.27_9_March_1966_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Times.27.27_9_March_1966-102">[91]</a></sup> This response was considered "tepid" by <i>The Irish Times</i>, whose editorial deemed the attack "a direct blow to the prestige of the state and the authority of the government".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Times.27.27_9_March_1966_102-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Times.27.27_9_March_1966-102">[91]</a></sup> Kennedy suggests that government anger was mainly directed at what they considered a distraction from the official 50th anniversary celebrations of the Rising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201350.E2.80.9351_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201350.E2.80.9351-100">[89]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #E0E6F8;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"There was an air of inevitability about Horatio Nelson's eventual demise; King William of Orange, King George II and Viscount Gough in the Phoenix Park had all fallen victim to republican bombings, while Queen Victoria had been rather unceremoniously dumped from her vantage point in Leinster House, removed on her back through the front gates."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Donal Fallon: "Dispelling the myths about the bombing of Nelson's Pillar"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2016_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2016-103">[92]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The absence of the pillar was regretted by some who felt the city had lost one of its most prominent landmarks. The Irish Literary Association was anxious that, whatever future steps were taken, the lettering on the pedestal should be preserved; the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Times" title="Irish Times">Irish Times</a></i> reported that the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Academy_of_Music" title="Royal Irish Academy of Music">Royal Irish Academy of Music</a> was considering legal measures to prevent removal of the remaining stump.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-10"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> Reactions among the general public were relatively light-hearted, typified by the numerous songs inspired by the incident. These included the immensely popular "<a href="/wiki/Up_Went_Nelson" title="Up Went Nelson">Up Went Nelson</a>", set to the tune of "<a href="/wiki/The_Battle_Hymn_of_the_Republic" title="The Battle Hymn of the Republic">The Battle Hymn of the Republic</a>" and performed by a group of Belfast schoolteachers, which remained at the top of the Irish charts for eight weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014114.E2.80.9316_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014114.E2.80.9316-104">[93]</a></sup> An American newspaper reported that the mood in the city was one of gaiety, with shouts of "Nelson has lost his last battle!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201496_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201496-105">[94]</a></sup> Some accounts relate that the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">Irish president</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="Éamon de Valera">Éamon de Valera</a>, phoned <i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Press" title="The Irish Press">The Irish Press</a></i> to suggest the headline: "British Admiral Leaves Dublin By Air"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFleming2016_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFleming2016-106">[95]</a></sup>—according to the senator and presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/David_Norris_(politician)" title="David Norris (politician)">David Norris</a>, "the only recorded instance of humour in that lugubrious figure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEO.27Riordan2016_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO.27Riordan2016-107">[96]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Butler_House_gardens,_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg"><img alt="Ornamental garden with stone blocks with incised lettering around the edge of a pond" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Butler_House_gardens%2C_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg/220px-Butler_House_gardens%2C_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Butler_House_gardens%2C_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg/330px-Butler_House_gardens%2C_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Butler_House_gardens%2C_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg/440px-Butler_House_gardens%2C_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Butler_House_gardens,_Kilkenny_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1537832.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lettering from Nelson's Pillar in the Butler House Walled Garden in 2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Pillar's fate was sealed when Dublin Corporation issued a "dangerous building" notice. The trustees agreed that the stump should be removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998_13-11"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C3.93_Riain1998-13">[12]</a></sup> A last-minute request by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_the_Architects_of_Ireland" title="Royal Institute of the Architects of Ireland">Royal Institute of the Architects of Ireland</a> for an injunction to delay the demolition on planning grounds was rejected by Justice <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Teevan_(attorney_general)" title="Thomas Teevan (attorney general)">Thomas Teevan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Guardian.27.27_15_March_1966_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Guardian.27.27_15_March_1966-108">[97]</a></sup> On 14 March the Army destroyed the stump by a controlled explosion, watched at a safe distance by a crowd who, the press reported, "raised a resounding cheer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Times.27.27_14_March_1966_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Irish_Times.27.27_14_March_1966-109">[98]</a></sup> There was a scramble for souvenirs, and many parts of the stonework were taken from the scene. Some of these relics, including Nelson's head, eventually found their way into museums;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[n 12]</a></sup> parts of the lettered stonework from the pedestal are displayed in the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Butler_House,_Kilkenny" title="Butler House, Kilkenny">Butler House</a> hotel in <a href="/wiki/Kilkenny" title="Kilkenny">Kilkenny</a>, while smaller remnants were used to decorate private gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014106.E2.80.9313_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014106.E2.80.9313-112">[100]</a></sup> Contemporary and subsequent accounts record that the army's explosion caused more damage than the first, but this, Fallon says, is a myth; damage claims arising from the second explosion amounted to less than a quarter of the sum claimed as a result of the original blast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201499_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201499-113">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.22A_colonel_writes....22_19_March_2006_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.22A_colonel_writes....22_19_March_2006-114">[102]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigations">Investigations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Investigations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was initially assumed that the monument was destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1922%E2%80%9369)" title="Irish Republican Army (1922–69)">IRA</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> reported on 9 March that six men had been arrested and questioned, but their identities were not revealed and there were no charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Guardian.27.27_9_March_1966_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Guardian.27.27_9_March_1966-115">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201352_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201352-116">[104]</a></sup> An IRA spokesman denied involvement, stating that they had no interest in demolishing mere symbols of foreign domination: "We are interested in the destruction of the domination itself".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014100_.28quoted_from_.27.27.5B.5BThe_Irish_Independent.5D.5D.27.27.2C_9_March_1966.29_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014100_.28quoted_from_.27.27.5B.5BThe_Irish_Independent.5D.5D.27.27.2C_9_March_1966.29-117">[105]</a></sup> In the absence of any leads, rumours suggested that the <a href="/wiki/ETA_(separatist_group)" title="ETA (separatist group)">Basque separatist movement ETA</a> might be responsible, perhaps as part of a training exercise with an Irish republican splinter group; in the mid-1960s the explosives expertise of ETA was generally acknowledged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMyles2009312_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMyles2009312-118">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>No further information was forthcoming until 2000, when during a <a href="/wiki/Raidi%C3%B3_Teilif%C3%ADs_%C3%89ireann" title="Raidió Teilifís Éireann">Raidió Teilifís Éireann</a> interview a former IRA member, Liam Sutcliffe claimed he had placed the bomb which detonated in the Pillar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFleming2016_106-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFleming2016-106">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014101.E2.80.9303_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014101.E2.80.9303-119">[107]</a></sup> In the 1950s Sutcliffe was associated with a group of dissident volunteers led by Joe Christle (1927–98), who had been expelled from the IRA in 1956 for "recklessness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite2009_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite2009-120">[108]</a></sup> In early 1966 Sutcliffe learned that Christle's group was planning "Operation Humpty Dumpty", an attack on the Pillar, and offered his services. According to Sutcliffe, on 28 February he placed a bomb within the Pillar, timed to go off in the early hours of the next morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014101.E2.80.9303_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014101.E2.80.9303-119">[107]</a></sup> The explosive was a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Gelignite" title="Gelignite">gelignite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonal" title="Ammonal">ammonal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFleming2016_106-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFleming2016-106">[95]</a></sup> It failed to detonate; Sutcliffe says that he returned early the next morning, recovered the device and redesigned its timer. On 7 March, shortly before the Pillar closed for the day, he climbed the inner stairway and placed the refurbished bomb near to the top of the shaft before going home. He learned of the success of his mission the next day, he says, having slept undisturbed through the night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014101.E2.80.9303_119-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014101.E2.80.9303-119">[107]</a></sup> Following his revelations, Sutcliffe was questioned by the police but not charged. He did not name others involved in the action, apart from Christle and his brother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFleming2016_106-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFleming2016-106">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacements">Replacements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Replacements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 29 April 1969 the Irish parliament passed the Nelson Pillar Act, terminating the Pillar Trust and vesting ownership of the site in Dublin Corporation. The trustees received £21,170 in compensation for the Pillar's destruction, and a further sum for loss of income.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENelson_Pillar_Act.2C_1969_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENelson_Pillar_Act.2C_1969-121">[109]</a></sup> In the debate, Senator <a href="/wiki/Owen_Sheehy-Skeffington" title="Owen Sheehy-Skeffington">Owen Sheehy-Skeffington</a> argued that the Pillar had been capable of repair and should have been re-assembled and rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeanad_.C3.89ireann_Debate_23_April_1969_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeanad_.C3.89ireann_Debate_23_April_1969-122">[110]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Spire,_Dublin.jpg"><img alt="Tall pole rising high into the sky above buildings lining a city centre shopping street" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/The_Spire%2C_Dublin.jpg/220px-The_Spire%2C_Dublin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/The_Spire%2C_Dublin.jpg/330px-The_Spire%2C_Dublin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/The_Spire%2C_Dublin.jpg/440px-The_Spire%2C_Dublin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Spire,_Dublin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Spire of Dublin, erected in 2003, viewed from <a href="/wiki/Henry_Street,_Dublin" title="Henry Street, Dublin">Henry Street</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For more than twenty years the site stood empty, while various campaigns sought to fill the space. In 1970 the Arthur Griffith Society suggested a monument to <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Griffith" title="Arthur Griffith">Arthur Griffith</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>, and Pearse, whose centenary would fall in 1979, was the subject of several proposals. None of these schemes were accepted by the Corporation. A request in 1987 by the Dublin Metropolitan Streets Commission that the Pillar be rebuilt—with a different statue—was likewise rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014118.E2.80.9319_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014118.E2.80.9319-123">[111]</a></sup> In 1988, as part of the city's 1,000th anniversary celebrations, the Smurfit Millennium Fountain was erected close to the site. This was commissioned by a Dublin businessman Michael Smurfit in memory of his father; it incorporated a bronze statue of a woman, sculpted by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amonn_O%27Doherty_(sculptor)" title="Éamonn O'Doherty (sculptor)">Éamonn O'Doherty</a>. The monument, known colloquially as the <a href="/wiki/Anna_Livia_(monument)" title="Anna Livia (monument)">Anna Livia</a>, was not universally appreciated; O'Doherty's fellow-sculptor <a href="/wiki/Edward_Delaney" title="Edward Delaney">Edward Delaney</a> called it an "atrocious eyesore".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014120_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014120-124">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[n 13]</a></sup></p>
<p>1988 saw the launch of the Pillar Project, aimed at encouraging artists and architects to bring forward new ideas for an appropriate permanent memorial to replace Nelson. Suggestions included a 110 metres (360 ft) flagpole, a triumphal arch modelled on the Paris <a href="/wiki/Arc_de_Triomphe" title="Arc de Triomphe">Arc de Triomphe</a>, and a "Tower of Light" with a platform that would restore Nelson's view over the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014120.E2.80.9322_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014120.E2.80.9322-128">[115]</a></sup> In 1997 Dublin Corporation announced a formal design competition for a monument to mark the new millennium in 2000. The winning entry was <a href="/wiki/Ian_Ritchie_(architect)" title="Ian Ritchie (architect)">Ian Ritchie</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Spire_of_Dublin" title="Spire of Dublin">Spire of Dublin</a>, a plain, needle-like structure rising 120 metres (390 ft) from the street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201353_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201353-129">[116]</a></sup> The design was approved; on 22 January 2003 it was completed, despite some political and artistic opposition. During the excavations preceding the Spire's construction, the foundation stone of the Nelson Pillar was recovered. Press stories that a time capsule containing valuable coins had also been discovered fascinated the public for a while, but proved illusory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014124.E2.80.9326_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014124.E2.80.9326-130">[117]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson%27s_Pillar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #E0E6F8;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Before Nelson's Pillar trams slowed, shunted, changed trolley, started for Blackrock, Kingstown and Dalkey, Clonskea, Rathgar and Terenure ... Sandymount Green, Rathmines, Ringsend and Sandymount Tower, Harold's Cross. The hoarse Dublin United Tramway Company's timekeeper bawled them off."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">James Joyce: <i>Ulysses</i>. Section 7: "In the Heart of the Hibernian Metropolis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJoyce2002112_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoyce2002112-131">[118]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The destruction of the Pillar brought a temporary glut of popular songs, including "<a href="/wiki/Nelson%27s_Farewell" title="Nelson's Farewell">Nelson's Farewell</a>", by <a href="/wiki/The_Dubliners" title="The Dubliners">The Dubliners</a>, in which Nelson's airborne demise is presented as Ireland's contribution to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_race" title="Space race">space race</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon2014114.E2.80.9316_104-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon2014114.E2.80.9316-104">[93]</a></sup> During its more than 150 years, the Pillar was an integral if controversial part of Dublin life, and was often reflected in Irish literature of the period. <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">Ulysses</a></i> (1922) is a meticulous depiction of the city on a single day, 16 June 1904. At the base of the Pillar trams from all parts of the city come and go; meanwhile the character <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Dedalus" title="Stephen Dedalus">Stephen Dedalus</a> fantasises a scene involving two elderly spinsters, who climb the steps to the viewing gallery where they eat plums and spit the stones down on those below, while gazing up at "the one-handled adulterer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKilfeather200560_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKilfeather200560-132">[119]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joyce shared Yeats's view that Ireland's association with England was an essential element in a shared history, and asked: "Tell me why you think I ought to change the conditions that gave Ireland and me a shape and a destiny?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy201362_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy201362-133">[120]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oliver_St._John_Gogarty" title="Oliver St. John Gogarty">Oliver St. John Gogarty</a>, in his literary memoir <i><a href="/wiki/As_I_Was_Going_Down_Sackville_Street" title="As I Was Going Down Sackville Street">As I Was Going Down Sackville Street</a></i>, considers the Pillar "the grandest thing we have in Dublin", where "the statue in whiter stone gazed forever south towards Trafalgar and the Nile".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGogarty1937259_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGogarty1937259-134">[121]</a></sup> That Pillar, says Gogarty, "marks the end of a civilization, the culmination of the great period of eighteenth century Dublin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGogarty1937259_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGogarty1937259-134">[121]</a></sup> Yeats's 1927 poem "The Three Monuments" has Parnell, Nelson and O'Connell on their respective monuments, mocking Ireland's post-independence leaders for their rigid morality and lack of courage, the obverse of the qualities of the "three old rascals".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESteinman198386_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinman198386-135">[122]</a></sup> A later writer, <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Behan" title="Brendan Behan">Brendan Behan</a>, in his <i>Confessions of an Irish Rebel</i> (1965) wrote from a nationalist perspective that Ireland owed Nelson nothing and that Dublin's poor regarded the Pillar as "a gibe at their own helplessness in their own country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehan1991221_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehan1991221-136">[123]</a></sup> In his poem "Dublin" (1939), written as the remaining vestiges of British overlordship were being removed from Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Louis_MacNeice" title="Louis MacNeice">Louis MacNeice</a> envisages "Nelson on his pillar/ Watching his world collapse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERT.C3.89:_A_Poem_for_Ireland_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERT.C3.89:_A_Poem_for_Ireland-137">[124]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201478.E2.80.9379_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201478.E2.80.9379-138">[125]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Austin_Clarke_(poet)" title="Austin Clarke (poet)">Austin Clarke</a>'s 1957 poem "Nelson's Pillar, Dublin" scorns the various schemes to remove the monument and concludes "Let him watch the sky/ With those who rule. Stone eye/ And telescopes can prove/ Our blessings are above".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDodsworth2001484_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDodsworth2001484-139">[126]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFallon201478.E2.80.9379_138-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallon201478.E2.80.9379-138">[125]</a></sup></p>
