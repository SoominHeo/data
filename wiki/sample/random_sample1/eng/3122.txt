<p><b>Nemrut</b> or <b>Nemrud</b> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Nemrut Dağı</i></span> ; <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>: <span lang="hy" xml:lang="hy">Նեմրութ լեռ</span>) is a 2,134-metre-high (7,001 ft) mountain in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, notable for the summit where a number of large statues are erected around what is assumed to be a royal tomb from the 1st century BC.</p>
<p>The name is a relatively modern one, dating back to the Middle Ages. In Armenian legend, <a href="/wiki/Hayk" title="Hayk">Hayk</a> defeated the Biblical king <a href="/wiki/Nimrod" title="Nimrod">Nimrod</a> (equated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bel_(god)" title="Bel (god)">Bel</a>) and buried him in these mountains. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">conquering Arabs</a> gave many ancient ruins they encountered the name Nimrud, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalhu" title="Kalhu">famous Assyrian capital</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_description">Location and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nemrut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Nemrut.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2240" data-file-width="1680" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Mount_Nemrut.jpg/150px-Mount_Nemrut.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Mount_Nemrut.jpg/225px-Mount_Nemrut.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Mount_Nemrut.jpg/300px-Mount_Nemrut.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Nemrut.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Some of the statues near the peak of Mount Nemrut</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The mountain lies 40 km (25 mi) north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kahta" title="Kahta">Kahta</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Ad%C4%B1yaman" title="Adıyaman">Adıyaman</a>. In 62 BC, King <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_I_Theos_of_Commagene" title="Antiochus I Theos of Commagene">Antiochus I Theos of Commagene</a> built on the mountain top a tomb-sanctuary flanked by huge statues 8–9-metre-high (26–30 ft) of himself, two lions, two eagles and various <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_mythology" title="Armenian mythology">Armenian</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Median_Empire" title="Median Empire">Medes</a> gods, such as <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>-<a href="/wiki/Aramazd" title="Aramazd">Aramazd</a> or Oromasdes (associated with <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrian</a> god <a href="/wiki/Ahura_Mazda" title="Ahura Mazda">Ahura Mazda</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules</a>-<a href="/wiki/Vahagn" title="Vahagn">Vahagn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyche" title="Tyche">Tyche</a>-Bakht, and <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Armenian_mythology" title="Armenian mythology">Mihr</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithras" title="Mithras">Mithras</a>. These statues were once seated, with names of each god inscribed on them. The heads of the statues have at some stage been removed from their bodies, and they are now scattered throughout the site.</p>
<p>The pattern of damage to the heads (notably to noses) suggests that they were deliberately damaged as a result of <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">iconoclasm</a>. The statues have not been restored to their original positions. The site also preserves stone slabs with bas-relief figures that are thought to have formed a large <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a>. These slabs display the ancestors of <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_I_Theos_of_Commagene" title="Antiochus I Theos of Commagene">Antiochus</a>, who included <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persians</a>.</p>
<p>The same statues and ancestors found throughout the site can also be found on the <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">tumulus</a> at the site, which is 49-metre-tall (161 ft) and 152 m (499 ft) in diameter. It is possible that the tumulus was built to protect a tomb from tomb-robbers since any excavation would quickly fill with loose rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The statues appear to have Greek-style facial features, but Armenian clothing and hair-styling.</p>
<p>The western terrace contains a large slab with a lion, showing the arrangement of stars and the planets <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> on 7 July 62 BC. This may be an indication of when construction began on this monument. The eastern portion is well preserved, being composed of several layers of rock, and a path following the base of the mountain is evidence of a walled passageway linking the eastern and western terraces. Possible uses for this site is thought to have included religious ceremonies, due to the astronomical and religious nature of the monument.</p>
<p>The arrangement of such statues is known by the term <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hierothesion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hierothesion (page does not exist)">hierothesion</a>. Similar arrangements have been found at Arsameia on Nymphaios at the hierothesion of the father of Antiochus, <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_I_Callinicus" title="Mithridates I Callinicus">Mithridates I Callinicus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_history">Ancient history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nemrut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> was defeated by the Romans in 190 BC at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Magnesia" title="Battle of Magnesia">Battle of Magnesia</a> it began to fall apart and new kingdoms were established on its territory by local authorities. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commagene" title="Commagene">Commagene</a>, one of the Seleucid successor states, occupied a land between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taurus_mountains" title="Taurus mountains">Taurus mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a>. The state of Commagene had a wide range of cultures which left its leader from 62 BC – 38 BC <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_I_Theos_of_Commagene" title="Antiochus I Theos of Commagene">Antiochus I Theos</a> to carry on a peculiar dynastic religious program, which included not only <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persian</a> deities but Antiochus and his family as well. This religious program was very possibly an attempt by Antiochus to unify his multiethnic kingdom and secure his dynasty's authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siliotti217_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-siliotti217-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antiochus supported the cult as a propagator of happiness and salvation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siliotti218_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-siliotti218-3">[3]</a></sup> Many of the ruins on Mount Nemrud are monuments of the imperial cult of Commagene. The most important area to the cult was the tomb of Antiochus I, which was decorated with colossal statues made of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. Although the imperial cult did not last long after Antiochus, several of his successors had their own tombs built on Mount Nemrud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siliotti220_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-siliotti220-4">[4]</a></sup> For around half of the year, Mount Nemrud is covered in snow, the effect of which increases weathering, which has in part caused the statues to fall in ruin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siliotti217_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-siliotti217-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nemrut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The site was excavated in 1881 by Karl Sester, a German <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> assessing transport routes for the Ottomans. Subsequent excavations have failed to reveal the tomb of Antiochus. This is nevertheless still believed to be the site of his burial. The statues, all of them "beheaded", have not been restored to their original condition.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visiting_Nemrut">Visiting Nemrut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nemrut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Visiting Nemrut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1987, Mount Nemrut was made a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>. Tourists typically visit Nemrut during April through October. The nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Ad%C4%B1yaman" title="Adıyaman">Adıyaman</a> is a popular place for car and bus trips to the site, and one can also travel from there by helicopter. There are also overnight tours running out of Malatya or Kahta.</p>
