<p><b>Ernest Dion Wilson</b>, professionally known as <b>No I.D.</b> (also known as <b>Immenslope</b>, born June 28, 1971),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_producer" title="Music producer">music producer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. Wilson is also a <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a> (DJ), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_arranger" title="Music arranger">music arranger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a>, having released an album in 1997, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Accept_Your_Own_and_Be_Yourself_(The_Black_Album)" title="Accept Your Own and Be Yourself (The Black Album)">Accept Your Own and Be Yourself (The Black Album)</a></i>, under <a href="/wiki/Relativity_Records" title="Relativity Records">Relativity Records</a>. He is perhaps best known for his early work with Chicago-based <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>. He has since become a heavily sought-out and high-profile producer, producing <a href="/wiki/Hit_single" title="Hit single">hit singles</a> such as "<a href="/wiki/Smile_(G-Unit_song)" title="Smile (G-Unit song)">Smile</a>" by G-Unit, "<a href="/wiki/Outta_My_System" title="Outta My System">Outta My System</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Let_Me_Hold_You" title="Let Me Hold You">Let Me Hold You</a>" by Bow Wow, "<a href="/wiki/Heartless_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Heartless (Kanye West song)">Heartless</a>" by Kanye West, "<a href="/wiki/D.O.A._(Death_of_Auto-Tune)" title="D.O.A. (Death of Auto-Tune)">D.O.A.</a>" by Jay Z and "<a href="/wiki/My_Last" title="My Last">My Last</a>" by Big Sean.</p>
<p>Wilson, who has served as a musical mentor for several artists, such as <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, is considered "The Godfather of <a href="/wiki/Chicago_hip_hop" title="Chicago hip hop">Chicago hip hop</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Wilson was once <a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">president</a> of West's <a href="/wiki/GOOD_Music" title="GOOD Music">GOOD Music</a> record company and although he would resign from that position, he stayed contracted as an in-house producer. In June 2011, Wilson announced he formed the supergroup <a href="/wiki/Cocaine_80s" title="Cocaine 80s">Cocaine 80s</a>, alongside Common and several other artists. In August 2011, Wilson became the Executive Vice President of A&amp;R for <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a>. He subsequently launched his own record label imprint, ARTium Recordings. He is currently the Executive Vice President and Head of Creative at Def Jam Recordings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_I.D.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, Wilson released an album under the pseudonym No I.D., titled <i><a href="/wiki/Accept_Your_Own_and_Be_Yourself_(The_Black_Album)" title="Accept Your Own and Be Yourself (The Black Album)">Accept Your Own and Be Yourself (The Black Album)</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The moniker No I.D. is a half-palindrome of his birth name, Dion. He also released a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instrumental_hip_hop" title="Instrumental hip hop">beat</a> tape, titled <i>Invisible Beats</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In Wilson's early career he was working as a co-producer (and sometimes ghost producer) for <a href="/wiki/Jermaine_Dupri" title="Jermaine Dupri">Jermaine Dupri</a>. No I.D. went on to work on hit singles such as "<a href="/wiki/My_Boo_(Usher_and_Alicia_Keys_song)" title="My Boo (Usher and Alicia Keys song)">My Boo</a>" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usher_(entertainer)" title="Usher (entertainer)">Usher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alicia_Keys" title="Alicia Keys">Alicia Keys</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Let_Me_Hold_You" title="Let Me Hold You">Let Me Hold You</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Bow_Wow_(rapper)" title="Bow Wow (rapper)">Bow Wow</a> featuring <a href="/wiki/Omarion" title="Omarion">Omarion</a>, as well as "Resurrection" and the ode to hip hop "<a href="/wiki/I_Used_to_Love_H.E.R." title="I Used to Love H.E.R.">I Used to Love H.E.R.</a>", which garnered Chicago-based rapper <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a> (formerly Common Sense) his early fame. Wilson also introduced Chicago-based rapper <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_production" title="Hip hop production">hip hop production</a>, inviting him to his sessions with Common, when West was only beginning. He also introduced West to a long-time friend named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyambo_%22Hip_Hop%22_Joshua" title='Kyambo "Hip Hop" Joshua'>Kyambo "Hip Hop" Joshua</a>, who was A&amp;R for <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>, who eventually signed West to his imprint <a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Since_1978" title="Hip Hop Since 1978">Hip Hop Since 1978</a>, which launched West's career as an artist and into stardom. West cites Wilson as his mentor on "Last Call," the outro to his highly acclaimed debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i> (2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> West also referenced Wilson's mentorship on songs such as "<a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Big Brother (Kanye West song)">Big Brother</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Made_in_America_(Jay-Z_and_Kanye_West_song)" title="Made in America (Jay-Z and Kanye West song)">Made in America</a>." Wilson's second official release was with Dug Infinite, a two-album package titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sampler,_vol._1" title="The Sampler, vol. 1">The Sampler, vol. 1</a></i> (2002).</p>

<p>In 2007, he was the focus of perhaps the most attention of his career for producing two songs from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/American_Gangster_(album)" title="American Gangster (album)">American Gangster</a></i>. At the time he worked with artists such as Jay-Z, <a href="/wiki/Rhymefest" title="Rhymefest">Rhymefest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plies_(rapper)" title="Plies (rapper)">Plies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Sean" title="Big Sean">Big Sean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killer_Mike" title="Killer Mike">Killer Mike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ross" title="Rick Ross">Rick Ross</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" title="Drake (entertainer)">Drake</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Thank_Me_Later" title="Thank Me Later">Thank Me Later</a></i>), and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/808%27s_%26_Heartbreak" title="808's &amp; Heartbreak">808's &amp; Heartbreak</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Beautiful_Dark_Twisted_Fantasy" title="My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy">My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Young_Jeezy" title="Young Jeezy">Young Jeezy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a> on their then-upcoming albums. Wilson produced "<a href="/wiki/D.O.A._(Death_of_Auto-Tune)" title="D.O.A. (Death of Auto-Tune)">D.O.A. (Death of Auto-Tune)</a>", the first single for Jay-Z's eleventh studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blueprint_3" title="The Blueprint 3">The Blueprint 3</a></i> (2009), as well as the second single "<a href="/wiki/Run_This_Town" title="Run This Town">Run This Town</a>", which features Barbadian singer <a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>. He once again teamed up with Common for the first time since 1997, when he handled the production for his ninth album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dreamer/The_Believer" title="The Dreamer/The Believer">The Dreamer/The Believer</a></i> (2011). In June 2011, Wilson formed <a href="/wiki/Cocaine_80s" title="Cocaine 80s">Cocaine 80s</a>, a musical ensemble composed of several musicians, including Common, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Fauntleroy_II" title="James Fauntleroy II">James Fauntleroy II</a>, Kevin Randolph, <a href="/wiki/Makeba_Riddick" title="Makeba Riddick">Makeba Riddick</a>, Rob "The Mixer" Kinelski, Steve Wyreman, Free Bass, Keys of Coke and Sam Lewis, among several others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>After resigning as President of Kanye West <a href="/wiki/GOOD_Music" title="GOOD Music">GOOD Music</a> record company, in August 2011, it was announced No I.D. was appointed Executive Vice President of A&amp;R for Def Jam Recordings. In addition, Def Jam has signed an exclusive joint venture label deal for No I.D.'s Artium Recordings. The announcements were made by Barry Weiss, Chairman and CEO of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Republic" title="Universal Republic">Universal Republic</a> and Island Def Jam Motown and Karen Kwak, EVP / Head of A&amp;R, Island Def Jam Music Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artium_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-artium-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2012, Wislon was an executive producer on New York City-based rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>' critically acclaimed eleventh album <i><a href="/wiki/Life_Is_Good_(Nas_album)" title="Life Is Good (Nas album)">Life Is Good</a></i>, producing five songs, including the twice <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>-nominated single "<a href="/wiki/Daughters_(Nas_song)" title="Daughters (Nas song)">Daughters</a>", as well as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loco-Motive" title="Loco-Motive">Loco-Motive</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accident_Murderers" title="Accident Murderers">Accident Murderers</a>".In April 2013, it was revealed Wilson signed up-and-coming rapper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logic_(rapper)" title="Logic (rapper)">Logic</a>, to Def Jam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2013, Wilson served as the primary producer of GOOD Music recording artist Big Sean's second album <i><a href="/wiki/Hall_of_Fame_(Big_Sean_album)" title="Hall of Fame (Big Sean album)">Hall of Fame</a></i>. In an August 2013 interview with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_Magazine" title="Complex Magazine">Complex</a></i>, Wilson said he was currently working on Jhene Aiko and Logic's upcoming respective debut albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complex1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-complex1-3">[3]</a></sup> Since the inception of Artium, Wilson has signed Common, Los Angeles-based singer <a href="/wiki/Jhen%C3%A9_Aiko" title="Jhené Aiko">Jhené Aiko</a> and singer Elijah Blake.</p>

<p><b>ARTium Recordings</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Record_label" title="Record label">record label</a> imprint, founded by No I.D.. In August 2011, it was announced No I.D. was appointed Executive Vice President of A&amp;R for Def Jam Recordings. In addition, Def Jam has signed an exclusive joint venture label deal for No I.D.'s Artium Recordings. The announcements were made by Barry Weiss, Chairman and CEO of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Republic" title="Universal Republic">Universal Republic</a> and Island Def Jam Motown and Karen Kwak, EVP / Head of A&amp;R, Island Def Jam Music Group. No I.D. would report directly to Mr. Weiss and Ms. Kwak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artium_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-artium-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2012, No I.D. signed American neo-soul singer <a href="/wiki/Jhen%C3%A9_Aiko" title="Jhené Aiko">Jhené Aiko</a>. By September 2013, No I.D. had signed up-and-coming American R&amp;B singers Elijah Blake and Snoh Aalegra<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On June 4, 2014, it was announced No I.D.'s longtime collaborator and Chicago-bred rapper <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>, signed a recording contract with Def Jam Recordings and ARTium Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
