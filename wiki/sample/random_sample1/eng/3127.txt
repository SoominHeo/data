<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Controversies surrounding <i>Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2" title="Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2">Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> video game developed by <a href="/wiki/Infinity_Ward" title="Infinity Ward">Infinity Ward</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a>. Released worldwide on November 10, 2009, it generated several controversies. In the game's fourth <a href="/wiki/Level_(video_gaming)" title="Level (video gaming)">level</a>, "<a href="/wiki/No_Russian" title="No Russian">No Russian</a>", the player controls an undercover <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> agent who participates in a <a href="/wiki/Mass_shooting" title="Mass shooting">mass shooting</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> airport to gain the trust of a Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist" title="Terrorist">terrorist</a> group. This level was largely criticized for allowing players to partake in a terrorist attack, and international versions of the game were subject to censorship due to the level's content. Allegations of <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobic</a> content arose when Infinity Ward released a video titled "Fight Against Grenade Spam". Some video game journalists chastised the video's use of <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a>, and that the video's title forms the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> <a href="/wiki/Faggot_(slang)" title="Faggot (slang)">F.A.G.S.</a>, a pejorative for gay people. In response, Infinity Ward removed the video from <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Easter_egg_(media)" title="Easter egg (media)">easter egg</a> referencing the United States policy "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_ask,_don%27t_tell" title="Don't ask, don't tell">don't ask, don't tell</a>" was also seen as homophobic, though most journalists belittled these allegations. The <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> version of the game was also met with criticism from players, when it was revealed that the game would not support dedicated <a href="/wiki/Game_server" title="Game server">servers</a> among a litany of other changes. Windows players created an <a href="/wiki/Online_petition" title="Online petition">online petition</a> to allow for dedicated servers, receiving over 150,000 signatures in ten days. However, despite these controversies, <i>Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</i> grossed <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>310 million in its first day of release.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="No_Russian">No Russian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Controversies_surrounding_Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: No Russian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/No_Russian" title="No Russian">No Russian</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NoRussian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="600" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/NoRussian.jpg/256px-NoRussian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/NoRussian.jpg/384px-NoRussian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/NoRussian.jpg/512px-NoRussian.jpg 2x" width="256"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NoRussian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In "No Russian", the player can shoot at a group of civilians at an airport.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the game's fourth <a href="/wiki/Level_(video_gaming)" title="Level (video gaming)">level</a>, "No Russian", the player controls an undercover <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> agent, Joseph Allen, who participates in a <a href="/wiki/Mass_shooting" title="Mass shooting">mass shooting</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> airport to gain the trust of a Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist" title="Terrorist">terrorist</a> group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-After_911_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-After_911-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It begins with the player walking out of an elevator with four other gunmen, who proceed to open fire on a large group of civilians at a <a href="/wiki/Airport_security" title="Airport security">security checkpoint</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-After_911_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-After_911-1">[1]</a></sup> The player then accompanies the gunmen as they walk through the airport, killing any remaining civilians. The level is very graphic, as screams can be heard throughout, and the injured crawl away leaving blood trails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Artful_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Artful-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the player is never forced to partake in the massacre, and may instead let their comrades kill the civilians. If the player does not feel comfortable during the level, they are allowed to skip to the next level with no penalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-That_Time_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-That_Time-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the release of <i>Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</i>, footage taken from the "No Russian" level was leaked on the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Though some journalists were cautious towards the level's content, they decided to wait until they could actually play the level to judge its merits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Artful_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Artful-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After the game's release, "No Russian" was largely criticized for allowing players to partake in a terrorist attack. Vince Horiuchi of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Salt_Lake_Tribune" title="The Salt Lake Tribune">The Salt Lake Tribune</a></i> felt that the level was in poor taste following the <a href="/wiki/2009_Fort_Hood_shooting" title="2009 Fort Hood shooting">2009 Fort Hood shooting</a>, and questioned why the level couldn't have been told through a non-interactive <a href="/wiki/Cutscene" title="Cutscene">cutscene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Marc Cieslak of <a href="/wiki/BBC_News" title="BBC News">BBC News</a> was saddened by "No Russian", as he felt it disproved his theory that the <a href="/wiki/Video_game_industry" title="Video game industry">video game industry</a> had "grown up".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Several prominent British religious leaders condemned "No Russian": <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Goldberg" title="Alexander Goldberg">Alexander Goldberg</a> of the <a href="/wiki/London_Jewish_Forum" title="London Jewish Forum">London Jewish Forum</a> was worried that children would play the level; Fazan Mohammed of the <a href="/wiki/British_Muslim_Forum" title="British Muslim Forum">British Muslim Forum</a> described the level as an intimate experience of enacting terrorism; and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Lowe_(bishop_of_Hulme)" title="Stephen Lowe (bishop of Hulme)">Stephen Lowe</a>, the retired <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Hulme" title="Bishop of Hulme">Bishop of Hulme</a>, felt that the level was "sickening".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to the graphic content featured in "No Russian", <i>Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</i> was subject to censorship in international versions of the game, including the entire removal of the level from the Russian version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_homophobic_content">Allegations of homophobic content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Controversies_surrounding_Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Allegations of homophobic content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2009, Infinity Ward posted a video titled "Fight Against Grenade Spam" on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>. In the video, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> player <a href="/wiki/Cole_Hamels" title="Cole Hamels">Cole Hamels</a> delivers a <a href="/wiki/Public_service_announcement" title="Public service announcement">public service announcement</a> that advocates against the use of grenade <a href="/wiki/Spam_(gaming)" title="Spam (gaming)">spam</a>. Hamels uses <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a> in the video, calling grenade spam "for <a href="/wiki/Pussy#weakness" title="Pussy">pussies</a>", and says "what the fuck" when he is blown up with grenades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_1-14">[14]</a></sup> Soon after the video was released, it was quickly discovered that the video's title forms the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> <a href="/wiki/Faggot_(slang)" title="Faggot (slang)">F.A.G.S.</a>, which is a pejorative term against <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a> people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamasutra_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gamasutra-15">[15]</a></sup> Phillip Kollar of <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">Game Informer</a></i> criticized that the title's acronym and use of profanity, and felt that Infinity Ward was condoning homophobic slurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_2-16">[16]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freelance" title="Freelance">Freelance</a> journalist Mitchell Dyer echoed Kollar's statement, and commented that the video "seems to enforce the asshole-ry" of homophobic slurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In response to the backlash, Infinity Ward community manager Robert Bowling remarked that while the video was intended to be a <a href="/wiki/Social_commentary" title="Social commentary">social commentary</a> joke, he understood why people found it offensive, and removed the video from YouTube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamasutra_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gamasutra-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further allegations of homophobic content arose when a <a href="/wiki/Easter_egg_(media)" title="Easter egg (media)">easter egg</a> was discovered in the game's opening <a href="/wiki/Tutorial" title="Tutorial">tutorial</a> level. If the player remains idle for a brief period of time, they are able to hear one soldier ask another soldier about his sexuality, and then remark "don't ask, don't tell".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Destructoid_Easter_Egg_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Destructoid_Easter_Egg-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Register_Easter_Egg_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Register_Easter_Egg-19">[19]</a></sup> This easter egg references the United States policy "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_ask,_don%27t_tell" title="Don't ask, don't tell">Don't ask, don't tell</a>", which prevented openly gay people from serving in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Unlike the "Fight Against Grenade Spam" video, few journalists commented on the easter egg. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Sterling" title="Jim Sterling">Jim Sterling</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Destructoid" title="Destructoid">Destructoid</a></i> felt that the easter egg was "too silly/stupid" for him to be angry at,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Destructoid_Easter_Egg_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Destructoid_Easter_Egg-18">[18]</a></sup> while James Sherwood of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Register" title="The Register">The Register</a></i> commented that people were trying to generate controversy over unimportant dialogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Register_Easter_Egg_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Register_Easter_Egg-19">[19]</a></sup> Lyle Masaki of <a href="/wiki/Logo_TV" title="Logo TV">Logo TV</a>'s NewNowNext.com deemed the easter egg to be homophobic, but not representative of the game as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_Windows_version">Criticism of the Windows version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Controversies_surrounding_Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism of the Windows version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Webcast" title="Webcast">webcast</a> conducted prior to the game's release, Bowling revealed that the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> version of <i>Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</i> would not support dedicated <a href="/wiki/Game_server" title="Game server">servers</a>, and would instead be powered by the IWNet server.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Infinity Ward would go on to state that the Windows version would also lack <a href="/wiki/Command_(computing)" title="Command (computing)">console commands</a>, not support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplayer" title="Multiplayer">multiplayer</a> matches larger than 18 players, and the inability for players to ban other players who are <a href="/wiki/Cheating_in_online_games" title="Cheating in online games">cheating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstechnica1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-arstechnica1-23">[23]</a></sup> These decisions angered many Windows gamers, with <a href="/wiki/Ben_Kuchera" title="Ben Kuchera">Ben Kuchera</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Ars_Technica" title="Ars Technica">Ars Technica</a></i> commenting: "at launch, this will be one of the most locked-down, inflexible, and gamer-unfriendly game ever created."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstechnica1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-arstechnica1-23">[23]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Online_petition" title="Online petition">online petition</a> was created to have Infinity Ward allow dedicated servers, which surpassed 150,000 signatures in ten days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ars_Technica_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ars_Technica-24">[24]</a></sup> Additionally, users on <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> submitted over 125 one-star reviews for the Windows version of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-25">[25]</a></sup> Mike Griffiths, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CEO" title="CEO">CEO</a> of Activision, stated that while Infinity Ward was aware of the petition, they weren't overly worried. He commented that IWNet would allow for a "friendly consumer experience", and that the benefits outweighed the negatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <i>Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</i> would go on to sell more than 4.7 million copies and gross <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>310 million in the United States and United Kingdom in its first day of release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> According to Bowling, while only 3% of the game's sales came from the Windows version, it still outsold the Windows version of <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty_4:_Modern_Warfare" title="Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare">Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare</a></i> in its first week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
