<p><b>Labor rights</b> or <b>workers' rights</b> are a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_rights" title="Legal rights">legal rights</a> and claimed <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> having to do with <a href="/wiki/Labor_relations" title="Labor relations">labor relations</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workers" title="Workers">workers</a> and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Employers" title="Employers">employers</a>, usually obtained under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_and_employment_law" title="Labor and employment law">labor and employment law</a>. In general, these rights' debates have to do with negotiating workers' pay, benefits, and <a href="/wiki/Occupational_safety_and_health" title="Occupational safety and health">safe working conditions</a>. One of the most central of these rights is the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">right to unionize</a>. Unions take advantage of <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective bargaining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_action" title="Industrial action">industrial action</a> to increase their members' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wages" title="Wages">wages</a> and otherwise change their working situation. Labor rights can also take in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worker%27s_control" title="Worker's control">worker's control</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worker%27s_self_management" title="Worker's self management">worker's self management</a> in which workers have a democratic voice in decision and policy making. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_movement" title="Labor movement">labor movement</a> initially focused on this "right to unionize", but attention has shifted elsewhere.</p>
<p>Critics of the labor rights movement claim that regulation promoted by labor rights activists may limit opportunities for work. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, critics objected to unions establishing <a href="/wiki/Closed_shop" title="Closed shop">closed shops</a>, situations where employers could only hire union members. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taft%E2%80%93Hartley_Act" title="Taft–Hartley Act">Taft–Hartley Act</a> banned the closed shop but allowed the less restrictive <a href="/wiki/Union_shop" title="Union shop">union shop</a>. Taft–Hartley also allowed states to pass <a href="/wiki/Right-to-work_law" title="Right-to-work law">right-to-work laws</a>, which require an <a href="/wiki/Open_shop" title="Open shop">open shop</a> where a worker's employment is not affected by his or her union membership. Labor counters that the open shop leads to a <a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" title="Free rider problem">free rider problem</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_Background">Labor Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Labor Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout history, workers claiming some sort of right have attempted to pursue their interests. During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Peasants%27_Revolt" title="Peasants' Revolt">Peasants' Revolt</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> expressed demand for better wages and working conditions. One of the leaders of the revolt, <a href="/wiki/John_Ball_(priest)" title="John Ball (priest)">John Ball</a> famously argued that people were born equal saying, "When <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a> delved and <a href="/wiki/Eve" title="Eve">Eve</a> span, who was then the gentleman?" Laborers often appealed to traditional rights. For instance, English peasants fought against the <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">enclosure</a> movement, which took traditionally communal lands and made them private.</p>
<p>In England 1833, a law was passed saying that any child under the age of 9 could not work, children age 9-13 could only work 8 hours a day, and children aged 14–18 could only work 12 hours a day.</p>
<p>Labor rights are a relatively new addition to the modern corpus of human rights. The modern concept of labor rights dates to the 19th century after the creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_unions" title="Labor unions">labor unions</a> following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> processes. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> stands out as one of the earliest and most prominent advocates for workers rights. His philosophy and economic theory focused on labor issues and advocates his economic system of <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>, a society which would be ruled by the workers. Many of the <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movements</a> for the rights of the workers were associated with groups influenced by Marx such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialists" title="Socialists">socialists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communists" title="Communists">communists</a>. More moderate <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democrats</a> supported worker's interests as well. More recent workers rights advocacy has focused on the particular role, exploitation, and needs of women workers, and of increasingly mobile global flows of casual, service, or guest workers.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Labour_Organization" title="International Labour Organization">International Labour Organization</a> was formed in 1919 as part of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> to protect worker's rights. The ILO later became incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>. The UN itself backed workers rights by incorporating several into two articles of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="United Nations Declaration of Human Rights">United Nations Declaration of Human Rights</a>, which is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a> (article 6-8). These read:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_23">Article 23</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Article 23">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ol>
<li>Everyone has the right to work, to free <a href="/wiki/Choice" title="Choice">choice</a> of employment, to just and favorable conditions of work and to protection against unemployment.</li>
<li>Everyone, without any <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>, has the right to <a href="/wiki/Equal_pay_for_equal_work" title="Equal pay for equal work">equal pay for equal work</a>.</li>
<li>Everyone who works has the right to just and favorable <a href="/wiki/Remuneration" title="Remuneration">remuneration</a> ensuring for himself and his family an existence worthy of human <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">dignity</a>, and supplemented, if necessary, by other means of <a href="/wiki/Social_protection" title="Social protection">social protection</a>.</li>
<li>Everyone has the right to form and to join <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a> for the protection of his interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udhr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-udhr-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_24">Article 24</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Article 24">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ol>
<li>Everyone has the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_rest_and_leisure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Right to rest and leisure (page does not exist)">right to rest and leisure</a>, including reasonable limitation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_hours" title="Working hours">working hours</a> and periodic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paid_holidays" title="Paid holidays">holidays with pay</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>The ILO and several other groups have sought <a href="/wiki/International_labor_standards" title="International labor standards">international labor standards</a> to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_rights" title="Legal rights">legal rights</a> for workers across the world. Recent movements have also been made to encourage countries to promote labor rights at the international level through <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udhr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-udhr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_Labor_Standards">Core Labor Standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Core Labor Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Identified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Labour_Organisation" title="International Labour Organisation">International Labour Organisation</a> (ILO) in the ‘Declaration of the Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work’,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb.org_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-adb.org-2">[2]</a></sup> core labor standards are “widely recognized to be of particular importance”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They are universally applicable, regardless of whether the relevant conventions have been ratified, the level of development of a country or cultural values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These standards are composed of qualitative, not quantitative standards and don’t establish a particular level of working conditions, wages or health and safety standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb.org_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-adb.org-2">[2]</a></sup> They are not intended to undermine the comparative advantage that developing countries may hold. Core labor standards are important human rights and are recognized in widely ratified <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> (CROC), the most widely ratified human rights treaty with 193 parties, and the ICCPR with 160 parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_Labor_Standards" title="Core Labor Standards">core labor standards</a> are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Freedom of association:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> workers are able to join trade unions that are independent of government and employer influence;</li>
<li>The right to collective bargaining:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> workers may negotiate with employers collectively, as opposed to individually;</li>
<li>The prohibition of all forms of forced labor:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> includes security from prison labor and slavery, and prevents workers from being forced to work under duress;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Elimination of the worst forms of child labor:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> implementing a minimum working age and certain working condition requirements for children;</li>
<li>Non-discrimination in employment : <a href="/wiki/Equal_pay_for_equal_work" title="Equal pay for equal work">equal pay for equal work</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Very few ILO member countries have ratified all of these conventions due to domestic constraints yet as these rights are also recognised in the UDHR, and form a part of <a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">customary international law</a> they are committed to respect these rights. For a discussion on the incorporation of these core labor rights into the mechanisms of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Recognition_of_Labour_Standards_within_the_World_Trade_Organisation" title="The Recognition of Labour Standards within the World Trade Organisation">The Recognition of Labour Standards within the World Trade Organisation</a>. There are many other issues outside of this core, in the UK employee rights includes the right to employment particulars, an itemised pay statement, a disciplinary process at which they have the right to be accompanied, daily breaks, rest breaks, paid holidays and more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_rights_issues">Labor rights issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Labor rights issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aside from the right to organize, labor movements have campaigned on various other issues that may be said to relate to labor rights.</p>
<p>Many labor movement campaigns have to do with limiting hours in the work place. 19th century labor movements campaigned for an <a href="/wiki/Eight-hour_day" title="Eight-hour day">Eight-hour day</a>. Worker advocacy groups have also sought to limit work hours, making a working week of 40 hours or less standard in many countries. A <a href="/wiki/35-hour_workweek" title="35-hour workweek">35-hour workweek</a> was established in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 2000, although this standard has been considerably weakened since then. Workers may agree with employers to work for longer, but the extra hours are payable <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">overtime</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> the working week is limited to a maximum of 48 hours <i>including</i> overtime (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_Time_Directive" title="Working Time Directive">Working Time Directive</a>).</p>
<p>Labor rights advocates have also worked to combat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_labor" title="Child labor">child labor</a>. They see child labor as exploitative, cruel, and often economically damaging. Child labor opponents often argue that working children are deprived of an education. In 1948 and then again in 1989, the United Nations declared that children have a right to social protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2007, Massachusetts updated their child labor laws that required all minors to have work permits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Labor rights advocates have worked to improve workplace conditions which meet established standards. During the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Era" title="Progressive Era">Progressive Era</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> began workplace reforms, which received publicity boosts from <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">Upton Sinclair</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">The Jungle</a></i> and events such as the 1911 <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Shirtwaist_Factory_fire" title="Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire">Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire</a>. Labor advocates and other groups often criticize production facilities with poor working conditions as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweatshops" title="Sweatshops">sweatshops</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupational_health" title="Occupational health">occupational health</a> hazards, and campaign for better labor practices and recognition of workers rights throughout the world.</p>
<p>Recent initiatives in the field of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> have included a focus on <a href="/wiki/Social_sustainability" title="Social sustainability">social sustainability</a>, which includes promoting workers' rights and safe working conditions, prevention of <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">human trafficking</a>, and elimination of illegal child labor from the sustainably sourced products and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sftool.gov_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sftool.gov-14">[14]</a></sup> Organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Labor" title="United States Department of Labor">U.S. Department of Labor</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">Department of State</a> have released studies on products that have been identified as using child labor and industries using or funded by human trafficking. Labor rights are defined internationally by sources such as the <a class="external text" href="http://www.anskaffelser.no/english/socially-responsible-public-procurement/information-about-high-risk-products" rel="nofollow">Norwegian Agency for Public Management and eGovernment (DIFI)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Finance_Corporation" title="International Finance Corporation">International Finance Corporation</a> performance standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sftool.gov_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-sftool.gov-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The labor movement pushes for guaranteed <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> laws, and there are continuing negotiations about increases to the minimum wage. However, opponents see minimum wage laws as limiting employment opportunities for unskilled and entry level workers.</p>
<p>Illegal immigrants cannot complain to the authorities about underpayment and mistreatment as they would be deported; and their willingness to work for low rates may depress rates of pay for others. Similarly, legal migrant workers are sometimes abused. For instance, migrants have faced a number of alleged abuses <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates" title="Human rights in the United Arab Emirates">in the United Arab Emirates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dubai#Human_Rights_and_Labor" title="Dubai">including Dubai</a>). <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a> lists several problems including "nonpayment of wages, extended working hours without overtime compensation, unsafe working environments resulting in death and injury, squalid living conditions in labor camps, and withholding of passports and travel documents by employers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrw.org_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrw.org-15">[15]</a></sup> Despite laws against the practice, employers confiscate migrant workers' passports. Without their passports, workers cannot switch jobs or return home.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.dailystar.com.lb/article.asp?edition_id=10&amp;categ_id=2&amp;article_id=16862" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> These workers have little recourse for labor abuses, but conditions have been improving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Labor and social welfare minister Ali bin Abdullah al-Kaabi has undertaken a number of reforms to help improve labor practices in his country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrw.org_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hrw.org-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The right to equal treatment, regardless of <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">origin</a> and appearance, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a>, is also seen by many as a worker's right. <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">Discrimination</a> in the work place is illegal in many countries, but some see the <a href="/wiki/Income_inequality_metrics" title="Income inequality metrics">wage gap</a> between genders and other groups as a persistent problem.</p>
