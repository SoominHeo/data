<p><b>Labor Day</b> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Public holidays in the United States">public holiday</a> celebrated on the first Monday in September. It honors the <a href="/wiki/Labor_history_of_the_United_States" title="Labor history of the United States">American labor movement</a> and the contributions that workers have made to the strength, prosperity, <a href="/wiki/United_States_labor_law" title="United States labor law">laws</a> and well-being of the country. It is the Monday of the <a href="/wiki/Long_weekend" title="Long weekend">long weekend</a> known as <b>Labor Day Weekend</b> and it is considered the unofficial end of summer. The holiday is also a <a href="/wiki/Federal_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Federal holidays in the United States">federal holiday</a>.</p>
<p>Beginning in the late 19th century, as the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_movement" title="Labor movement">labor movements</a> grew, trade unionists proposed that a day be set aside to celebrate labor. "Labor Day" was promoted by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Labor_Union" title="Central Labor Union">Central Labor Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Labor" title="Knights of Labor">Knights of Labor</a>, which organized the first parade in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. In 1887, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> was the first state of the United States to make it an official <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a>. By the time it became an official <a href="/wiki/Federal_holiday" title="Federal holiday">federal holiday</a> in 1894, thirty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._states" title="U.S. states">U.S. states</a> officially celebrated Labor Day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridgemens1921_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgemens1921-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Canada's <a href="/wiki/Labour_Day#Canada" title="Labour Day">Labour Day</a> is also celebrated on the first Monday of September. More than 80 countries celebrate <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a> on May 1 – the ancient European holiday of <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a> – and several countries have chosen their own dates for <a href="/wiki/Labour_Day" title="Labour Day">Labour Day</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beginning in the late 19th century, as the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_movement" title="Labor movement">labor movements</a> grew, different groups of trade unionists chose a variety of days on which to celebrate labor. In the United States and Canada, a September holiday, called Labor or <a href="/wiki/Labour_Day" title="Labour Day">Labour Day</a>, was first proposed in the 1880s. In 1882, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Maguire_(labor_activist)" title="Matthew Maguire (labor activist)">Matthew Maguire</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Machinist" title="Machinist">machinist</a>, first proposed a Labor Day holiday on the first Monday of September<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 1]</a></sup> while serving as secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Labor_Union" title="Central Labor Union">Central Labor Union</a> (CLU) of New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-labordept_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-labordept-2">[2]</a></sup> Some maintain that <a href="/wiki/Peter_J._McGuire" title="Peter J. McGuire">Peter J. McGuire</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor" title="American Federation of Labor">American Federation of Labor</a> put forward the first proposal in May 1882,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridgemens1921_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgemens1921-1">[1]</a></sup> after witnessing the annual <a href="/wiki/Labour_Day#Canada_.28country.29" title="Labour Day">labour festival</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-origins-4">[3]</a></sup> In 1887 <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> became the first state of the United States to make Labor Day an official <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a>. By the time it became an official <a href="/wiki/Federal_holiday" title="Federal holiday">federal holiday</a> in 1894, thirty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._states" title="U.S. states">U.S. states</a> officially celebrated Labor Day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridgemens1921_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgemens1921-1">[1]</a></sup> Thus by 1887 in North America, Labor Day was an established, official holiday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the deaths of workers at the hands of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service" title="United States Marshals Service">United States Marshals Service</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Pullman_Strike" title="Pullman Strike">Pullman Strike</a> of 1894 in Chicago, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> unanimously voted to approve legislation to make Labor Day a national holiday and President <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> signed it into law six days after the end of the strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-6">[5]</a></sup> Cleveland supported the creation of the national holiday in an attempt to shore up support among trade unions following the Pullman Strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-7">[6]</a></sup> The date of May 1 (an ancient European holiday known as <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>) was an alternative date, celebrated then (and now) as <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a>, but President Cleveland was concerned that observance of Labor Day on May 1 would encourage Haymarket-style protests and would strengthen <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_States" title="History of the socialist movement in the United States">socialist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_in_the_United_States" title="Anarchism in the United States">anarchist</a> movements that, though distinct from one another, had rallied to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_affair" title="Haymarket affair">Haymarket Affair</a> on International Workers' Day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>All U.S. states, the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">United States territories</a> have made Labor Day a statutory holiday.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrations">Celebrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Celebrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The form for the celebration of Labor Day was outlined in the first proposal for the holiday: A street parade to exhibit to the public "the strength and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esprit_de_corps" title="Esprit de corps">esprit de corps</a> of the trade and labor organizations",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-labordept_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-labordept-2">[2]</a></sup> followed by a festival for the workers and their friends and families. This became the pattern for Labor Day celebrations. Speeches by prominent men and women were introduced later, as more emphasis was placed upon the civil significance of the holiday. Still later, by a resolution of the American Federation of Labor convention of 1909, the Sunday preceding Labor Day was adopted as Labor Sunday and dedicated to the spiritual and educational aspects of the Labor movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-labordept_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-labordept-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unofficial_end_of_summer">Unofficial end of summer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unofficial end of summer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Labor Day is called the "unofficial end of summer"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WBIR_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WBIR-9">[8]</a></sup> because it marks the end of the cultural summer season. Many take their two-week <a href="/wiki/Vacation" title="Vacation">vacations</a> during the two weeks ending Labor Day weekend. Many fall activities, such as school and sports begin about this time.</p>
<p>In the United States, many school districts resume classes around the Labor Day holiday weekend (see <a href="/wiki/First_day_of_school" title="First day of school">First day of school</a>). Most begin the week before, making Labor Day weekend the first three-day weekend of the school calendar, while others return the Tuesday following Labor Day, allowing families one final getaway before the school year begins. Many districts across the Midwest are opting to begin school after Labor Day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CharlesSenter2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CharlesSenter2008-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement park</a> industry has successfully lobbied for legislation requiring most school districts in the state to have their first day of school after Labor Day, in order to give families another weekend to visit amusement parks in the state. The relevant statute has been nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Kings_Dominion" title="Kings Dominion">Kings Dominion</a> law" after one such park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In U.S. sports, Labor Day weekend marks the beginning of many <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fall_sports&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fall sports (page does not exist)">fall sports</a>. <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">National Collegiate Athletic Association</a> (NCAA) teams usually play their first games that weekend and the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL) traditionally play their <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League_Kickoff_game" title="National Football League Kickoff game">kickoff game</a> the Thursday following Labor Day. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_500" title="Southern 500">Southern 500</a> <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> auto race has been held on Labor Day weekend at <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Raceway" title="Darlington Raceway">Darlington Raceway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Darlington,_South_Carolina" title="Darlington, South Carolina">Darlington, South Carolina</a> from 1950 to 2003 and since 2015. At <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil_Raceway_at_Indianapolis" title="Lucas Oil Raceway at Indianapolis">Indianapolis Raceway Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hot_Rod_Association" title="National Hot Rod Association">National Hot Rod Association</a> hold their finals of the <a href="/wiki/NHRA_U.S._Nationals" title="NHRA U.S. Nationals">NHRA U.S. Nationals</a> drag race that weekend. Labor Day is the middle point between weeks one and two of the <a href="/wiki/US_Open_(tennis)" title="US Open (tennis)">U.S. Open Tennis Championships</a> held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flushing_Meadows" title="Flushing Meadows">Flushing Meadows</a>, New York.</p>
<p>In fashion, Labor Day is (or was) considered the last day when it is acceptable to wear white<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Seersucker" title="Seersucker">seersucker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "unofficial beginning of summer" in the U.S. is <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> at the end of May.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_Day_sales">Labor Day sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Labor Day sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To take advantage of large numbers of potential customers with time to shop, Labor Day has become an important weekend for <a href="/wiki/Discounts_and_allowances" title="Discounts and allowances">discounts and allowances</a> by many retailers in the United States, especially for <a href="/wiki/Back_to_school_(marketing)" title="Back to school (marketing)">back-to-school sales</a>. Some retailers claim it is one of the largest sale dates of the year, second only to the Christmas season's <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
