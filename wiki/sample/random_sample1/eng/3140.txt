<p><b>Nora Astorga Gadea de Jenkins</b> (1948 – February 14, 1988) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicaraguan" title="Nicaraguan">Nicaraguan</a> <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla</a> fighter in the <a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_Revolution" title="Nicaraguan Revolution">Nicaraguan Revolution</a>, a lawyer, politician, judge and the <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaraguan</a> ambassador to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> from 1986 to 1988.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nora_Astorga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Astorga was born to a religious, upper-middle-class family in <a href="/wiki/Managua" title="Managua">Managua</a>. She was the first child of a lumber exporter and rancher with connections to the powerful ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somoza" title="Somoza">Somoza</a> family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">[1]</a></sup> In her youth she was a devout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>, often doing charitable work in the poor neighborhoods of Managua.</p>
<p>In 1967, she announced to her family's dismay that she supported <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Ag%C3%BCero" title="Fernando Agüero">Fernando Agüero</a>, not his opponent <a href="/wiki/Anastasio_Somoza_Debayle" title="Anastasio Somoza Debayle">Anastasio Somoza Debayle</a>, in the presidential election. For her personal safety and to "straighten her out," her family sent her to study medicine in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where she remained from 1967 to 1969. However, the animal dissections disturbed her and she had to abandon her studies. She said of the years she spent in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, "What impressed me most about the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> were the social contrasts and above all the <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>. I had never seen racism like that in Nicaragua ... [m]y political consciousness was born then."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RE_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RE-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Astorga married Jorge Jenkins when she was 22. Astorga had four children, two with her husband, two with Jose Maria Alvarado, a member of the Sandinistas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_revolutionary">A revolutionary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nora_Astorga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A revolutionary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:15em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #c6dbf7;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p><i>"...Revolutions are not exportable like Coca-Cola or paperbacks or something like that... You don't produce it internally and send it away. Revolutions are made in a country when the conditions in that particular country are for a process of change."</i></p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Nora Astorga<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">[1]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Astorga later returned to Nicaragua and studied law at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universidad_Centroamericana" title="Universidad Centroamericana">Universidad Centroamericana</a> in Managua. Her association with Nicaragua's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandinista" title="Sandinista">Sandinista</a> revolutionaries began during her years as a university student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> From 1969 to 1973, she was responsible for finding safe houses and transportation for the revolutionary leader Oscar Turcios.</p>
<p>At age 22, she married Jorge Jenkins, a student activist. The young couple spent the following year in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, where he studied anthropology and she studied banking law and computer programming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">[1]</a></sup> They had two children and separated after five years of marriage. During this time, Astorga led a double life as a mother of two and a corporate lawyer for one of Nicaragua's largest construction companies, while <a href="/wiki/Clandestine_operation" title="Clandestine operation">clandestinely</a> aiding the Sandinistas.</p>
<p>After the assassination of newspaper editor <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Joaqu%C3%ADn_Chamorro_Cardenal" title="Pedro Joaquín Chamorro Cardenal">Pedro Chamorro</a> in 1978, Astorga decided to take up arms against the Somoza regime. "I finally understood that armed struggle was the only solution, that a rifle cannot be met with a flower, that we were in the streets, but if that force didn't get organized we wouldn't achieve much", she said. "For me, it was the moment of conviction: either I took up arms and made a total commitment or I wasn't going to change anything."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RE_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RE-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She gained national attention for her participation in the botched kidnapping and murder of General Reynaldo Pérez Vega (nicknamed "El Perro," or "the dog"). Pérez Vega was deputy commander of Anastasio Somoza’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardia_Nacional_(Nicaragua)" title="Guardia Nacional (Nicaragua)">National Guard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On March 8, 1978, Astorga invited the general to her apartment in <a href="/wiki/Managua" title="Managua">Managua</a>, hinting to him that the sexual favors he had long sought would be granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> When he arrived, however, three members of the <a href="/wiki/Sandinista_National_Liberation_Front" title="Sandinista National Liberation Front">Sandinista National Liberation Front</a> (FSLN) — Hilario Sánchez, Raúl Venerio and Walter Ferreti — burst out of her bedroom closet and seized the general. The plan was to <a href="/wiki/Ransom" title="Ransom">ransom</a> him for jailed Sandinista revolutionaries, but Pérez Vega put up a struggle and was murdered. Later, with his throat slit, he was found wrapped in a Sandinista flag. Astorga said of his murder, "It was not murder. He was too much of a monster."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview shortly before her death, she described her feelings about her role in the Pérez Vega murder this way:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>...Sometimes people ask me why I don't have any guilty feelings in regard to "the dog." They want to know how I could do something so fierce without feeling guilty. I believe I don't feel guilty for three reasons. First, we were supposed to kidnap him, not kill him. Second, I wasn't there at the moment of his death. And third, he represented repression. He was practically Somoza's second in command, the one who conducted all those murderous operations in the north, the one who massacred so many in <a href="/wiki/Masaya" title="Masaya">Masaya</a>. He really was a monster. I understood his death as part of the liberation struggle...</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Nora Astorga<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RE_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-RE-2">[2]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>She became the subject of a national manhunt and next appeared to the Nicaraguan public on the pages of <i>La Prensa</i>, the nation's opposition newspaper. She was wearing jungle fatigues and carrying an <a href="/wiki/AK-47" title="AK-47">AK-47 assault rifle</a>. Astorga had escaped to the jungle and joined the Sandinista revolutionaries. There, she became pregnant with her third child by José María Alvarado, a leading Sandinista.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Justice_Minister_and_UN_Representative">Justice Minister and UN Representative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nora_Astorga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Justice Minister and UN Representative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the Sandinistas took power in July 1979, Astorga was appointed Vice Minister of Justice. In that position she oversaw the trials of some 7,500 members of Somoza's National Guard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984, her appointment as ambassador to the United States was refused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a> because of her involvement in the killing of General Reynaldo Pérez Vega. Vega had been a <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a> operative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Astorga became a deputy representative to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> in 1984, and in March 1986, became the Nicaraguan ambassador to that body, a position she held until her death in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She was instrumental in getting the United Nations to recognize a ruling by the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nicaragua_v._United_States" title="Nicaragua v. United States">Nicaragua v. United States</a></i>, that declared the United States' support for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contra_(guerrillas)" title="Contra (guerrillas)">Contras</a> illegal.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_at_age_39">Death at age 39</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nora_Astorga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death at age 39">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 14, 1988, "La Norita" died of <a href="/wiki/Cervical_cancer" title="Cervical cancer">cervical cancer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Managua" title="Managua">Managua</a>, aged 39. She was awarded the title "Hero of the Fatherland and Revolution" and the Order of <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Fonseca" title="Carlos Fonseca">Carlos Fonseca</a> on July 1987, which was the highest order of Nicaragua at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RE_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-RE-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She appears as one of the twelve apostles in the mural of the Visitación at Casa Ave Maria in Managua. A <a href="/wiki/Barrio" title="Barrio">barrio</a>, or neighborhood, in Managua was named for her.</p>
<p>The 1986 song "Mariel" by the <a href="/wiki/KBC_Band" title="KBC Band">KBC Band</a> was inspired by Nora Astorga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> KBC members Jack Casady and Paul Kantner played this song at her memorial service.</p>
