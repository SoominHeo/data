<p><i><b>Drosophila melanogaster</b></i> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">fly</a> (the taxonomic order Diptera) in the family <a href="/wiki/Drosophilidae" title="Drosophilidae">Drosophilidae</a>. The species is known generally as the <b>common fruit fly</b> or <b>vinegar fly</b>. Starting with <a href="/wiki/Charles_W._Woodworth" title="Charles W. Woodworth">Charles W. Woodworth</a>'s proposal of the use of this species as a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a>, <i>D. melanogaster</i> continues to be widely used for biological research in studies of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, microbial <a href="/wiki/Pathogenesis" title="Pathogenesis">pathogenesis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Life_history_theory" title="Life history theory">life history evolution</a>. It is typically used because it is an animal species that is easy to care for, has four pairs of <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a>, breeds quickly, and lays many eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_of_genetics_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_of_genetics-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>D. melanogaster</i> is a common pest in homes, restaurants, and other occupied places where food is served.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ento.psu.edu_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ento.psu.edu-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flies belonging to the family <a href="/wiki/Tephritidae" title="Tephritidae">Tephritidae</a> are also called "fruit flies". This can cause confusion, especially in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, where the Mediterranean fruit fly <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratitis_capitata" title="Ceratitis capitata">Ceratitis capitata</a></i> is an economic pest.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_appearance">Physical appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="450" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg/220px-Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg/330px-Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg/440px-Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Biology_Illustration_Animals_Insects_Drosophila_melanogaster.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Female (left) and male (right) <i>D. melanogaster</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildtype" title="Wildtype">Wildtype</a> fruit flies are yellow-brown, with brick-red eyes and transverse black rings across the abdomen. They exhibit <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>: females are about 2.5 millimeters (0.098 in) long; males are slightly smaller with darker backs. Males are easily distinguished from females based on colour differences, with a distinct black patch at the abdomen, less noticeable in recently emerged flies (see fig.), and the sexcombs (a row of dark bristles on the <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">tarsus</a> of the first leg). Furthermore, males have a cluster of spiky hairs (claspers) surrounding the reproducing parts used to attach to the female during mating. There are extensive images at <a href="/wiki/FlyBase" title="FlyBase">FlyBase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle_and_reproduction">Lifecycle and reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lifecycle and reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:170px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosophila_egg.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="140" data-file-width="168" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Drosophila_egg.png" width="168"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Egg of <i>D. melanogaster</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>D. melanogaster</i> lifespan is about 30 days at 29 °C (84 °F).</p>
<p>The developmental period for <i>D. melanogaster</i> varies with temperature, as with many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectothermic" title="Ectothermic">ectothermic</a> species. The shortest development time (egg to adult), 7 days, is achieved at 28 °C (82 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup> Development times increase at higher temperatures (11 days at 30 °C or 86 °F) due to heat stress. Under ideal conditions, the development time at 25 °C (77 °F) is 8.5 days,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> at 18 °C (64 °F) it takes 19 days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup> and at 12 °C (54 °F) it takes over 50 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup> Under crowded conditions, development time increases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaing_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chaing-8">[8]</a></sup> while the emerging flies are smaller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaing_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chaing-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Females lay some 400 eggs (embryos), about five at a time, into rotting fruit or other suitable material such as decaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mushrooms" title="Mushrooms">mushrooms</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sap_flux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sap flux (page does not exist)">sap fluxes</a>. The eggs, which are about 0.5 mm long, hatch after 12–15 hours (at 25 °C or 77 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup> The resulting <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> grow for about 4 days (at 25 °C) while <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">molting</a> twice (into second- and third-instar larvae), at about 24 and 48 h after hatching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup> During this time, they feed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a> that decompose the fruit, as well as on the sugar of the fruit itself. The mother puts feces on the egg sacs to establish the same microbial composition in the larvae's guts which has worked positively for herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Then the larvae encapsulate in the <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">puparium</a> and undergo a four-day-long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphosis_(biology)" title="Metamorphosis (biology)">metamorphosis</a> (at 25 °C), after which the adults eclose (emerge).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_1978_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_1978-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashburner_2005_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ashburner_2005-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

Mating fruit flies: Note the sex combs on the forelegs of the male (insert)</div>

<p>Females become receptive to courting males at about 8–12 hours after emergence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Specific <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neuron</a> groups in females have been found to affect copulation behavior and mate choice. One such group in the <a href="/wiki/Ventral_nerve_cord" title="Ventral nerve cord">abdominal nerve cord</a> allows the female fly to pause her body movements to copulate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily.com-12">[12]</a></sup> Activation of these neurons induces the female to cease movement and orient herself towards the male to allow for mounting. If the group is inactivated, the female remains in motion and does not copulate. Various chemical signals such as male <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pheromones" title="Pheromones">pheromones</a> often are able to activate the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily.com_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The female fruit fly prefers a shorter duration when it comes to sex. Males, on the other hand, prefer it to last longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGN212009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGN212009-13">[13]</a></sup> Males perform a sequence of five behavioral patterns to court females. First, males orient themselves while playing a courtship song by horizontally extending and vibrating their wings. Soon after, the male positions itself at the rear of the female's abdomen in a low posture to tap and lick the female genitalia. Finally, the male curls its abdomen and attempts copulation. Females can reject males by moving away, kicking, and extruding their ovipositor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Copulation lasts around 15–20 minutes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> during which males transfer a few hundred, very long (1.76 mm) <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> cells in seminal fluid to the female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Females <a href="/wiki/Female_sperm_storage" title="Female sperm storage">store the sperm</a> in a tubular receptacle and in two mushroom-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spermathecae" title="Spermathecae">spermathecae</a>; sperm from multiple matings compete for fertilization. A last male precedence is believed to exist in which the last male to mate with a female sires about 80% of her offspring. This precedence was found to occur through both displacement and incapacitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sperm_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sperm-17">[17]</a></sup> The displacement is attributed to sperm handling by the female fly as multiple matings are conducted and is most significant during the first 1–2 days after copulation. Displacement from the seminal receptacle is more significant than displacement from the spermathecae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sperm_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-sperm-17">[17]</a></sup> Incapacitation of first male sperm by second male sperm becomes significant 2–7 days after copulation. The seminal fluid of the second male is believed to be responsible for this incapacitation mechanism (without removal of first male sperm) which takes effect before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sperm_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-sperm-17">[17]</a></sup> The delay in effectiveness of the incapacitation mechanism is believed to be a protective mechanism that prevents a male fly from incapacitating its own sperm should it mate with the same female fly repetitively. Sensory neurons in the uterus of female <i>D. melanogaster</i> respond to a male protein, sex <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a>, which is found in <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily.com_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily.com-12">[12]</a></sup> This protein makes the female reluctant to copulate for about 10 days after <a href="/wiki/Insemination" title="Insemination">insemination</a>. The signal pathway leading to this change in behavior has been determined. The signal is sent to a brain region that is a homolog of the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a> and the hypothalamus then controls sexual behavior and desire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily.com_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>D. melanogaster</i> is often used for <a href="/wiki/Life_extension" title="Life extension">life extension</a> studies, such as to identify <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> purported to increase lifespan when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutate" title="Mutate">mutated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mate-choice_copying">Mate-choice copying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mate-choice copying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>D. melanogaster</i> females exhibit <a href="/wiki/Mate_choice_copying" title="Mate choice copying">mate choice copying</a>. When virgin females are shown other females copulating with a certain type of male, they tend to copulate more with this type of male afterwards than naive females (which have not observed the copulation of others). This behavior is sensitive to environmental conditions, and females copy less in bad weather conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dagaeff2016_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dagaeff2016-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Male_sexual_behavior_and_learning">Male sexual behavior and learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Male sexual behavior and learning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><i>D. melanogaster</i> males exhibit a strong reproductive learning curve. That is, with sexual experience, these flies tend to modify their future mating behavior in multiple ways. These changes include increased selectivity for courting only intraspecifically, as well as decreased <a href="/wiki/Courtship" title="Courtship">courtship</a> times.</p>
<p>Sexually naïve <i>D. melanogaster</i> males are known to spend significant time courting interspecifically, such as with <i>D. simulans</i> flies. Naïve <i>D. melanogaster</i> will also attempt to court females that are not yet sexually mature, and other males. <i>D. melanogaster</i> males show little to no preference for <i>D. melanogaster</i> females over females of other species or even other male flies. However, after <i>D. simulans</i> or other flies incapable of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copulation" title="Copulation">copulation</a> have rejected the males’ advances, <i>D. melanogaster</i> males are much less likely to spend time courting nonspecifically in the future. This apparent learned behavior modification seems to be evolutionarily significant, as it allows the males to avoid investing energy into futile sexual encounters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, males with previous sexual experience will modify their courtship dance when attempting to mate with new females — the experienced males spend less time courting and therefore have lower mating latencies, meaning that they are able to reproduce more quickly. This decreased mating latency leads to a greater mating efficiency for experienced males over naïve males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This modification also appears to have obvious evolutionary advantages, as increased mating efficiency is extremely important in the eyes of <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polygamy">Polygamy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Polygamy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both male and female <i>D. melanogaster</i> act <a href="/wiki/Polygamy" title="Polygamy">polygamously</a> (having multiple sexual partners at the same time).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Successive_Polygamy_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Successive_Polygamy-22">[22]</a></sup> In both males and females, polygamy results in a decrease in evening activity compared to virgin flies, more so in males than females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Successive_Polygamy_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Successive_Polygamy-22">[22]</a></sup> Evening activity consists of the activities that the flies participate in other than mating and finding partners, such as finding food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup> The reproductive success of males and females varies, due to the fact that a female only needs to mate once to reach maximum fertility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup> Mating with multiple partners provides no advantage over mating with one partner, and therefore females exhibit no difference in evening activity between polygamous and monogamous individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup> For males, however, mating with multiple partners increases their reproductive success by increasing the genetic diversity of their offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup> This benefit of genetic diversity is an evolutionary advantage because it increases the chance that some of the offspring will have traits that increase their fitness in their environment.</p>
<p>The difference in evening activity between polygamous and monogamous male flies can be explained with courtship. For polygamous flies, their reproductive success increases by having offspring with multiple partners, and therefore they spend more time and energy on courting multiple females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup> On the other hand, monogamous flies only court one female, and expend less energy doing so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup> While it requires more energy for male flies to court multiple females, the overall reproductive benefits it produces has kept polygamy as the preferred sexual choice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been shown that the mechanism that affects courtship behavior in Drosophila is controlled by the oscillator neurons DN1s and LNDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-24">[24]</a></sup> Oscillation of the DN1 neurons was found to be effected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_interaction" title="Social interaction">socio-sexual interactions</a>, and is connected to mating-related decrease of evening activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_use_in_genetic_analysis">History of use in genetic analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History of use in genetic analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>D. melanogaster</i> was among the first <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Genetic_analysis" title="Genetic analysis">genetic analysis</a>, and today it is one of the most widely used and genetically best-known of all <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> organisms. All organisms use common genetic systems; therefore, comprehending processes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a> and <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">replication</a> in fruit flies helps in understanding these processes in other eukaryotes, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans">humans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pierce_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pierce-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan" title="Thomas Hunt Morgan">Thomas Hunt Morgan</a> began using fruit flies in experimental studies of heredity at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in 1910 in a laboratory known as the Fly Room. The Fly Room was cramped with eight desks, each occupied by students and their experiments. They started off experiments using milk bottles to rear the fruit flies and handheld lenses for observing their traits. The lenses were later replaced by microscopes, which enhanced their observations. Morgan and his students eventually elucidated many basic principles of heredity, including sex-linked inheritance, <a href="/wiki/Epistasis" title="Epistasis">epistasis</a>, multiple alleles, and <a href="/wiki/Gene_mapping" title="Gene mapping">gene mapping</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pierce_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pierce-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_organism_in_genetics">Model organism in genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Model organism in genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>D. melanogaster</i> is one of the most studied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisms" title="Organisms">organisms</a> in biological research, particularly in genetics and developmental biology. The several reasons include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Its care and culture requires little equipment and uses little space even when using large cultures, and the overall cost is low.</li>
<li>It is small and easy to grow in the laboratory and its morphology is easy to identify once anesthetized (usually with <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas, by cooling, or with products like <a href="/wiki/FlyNap" title="FlyNap">FlyNap</a>).</li>
<li>It has a short generation time (about 10 days at room temperature), so several generations can be studied within a few weeks.</li>
<li>It has a high <a href="/wiki/Fecundity" title="Fecundity">fecundity</a> (females lay up to 100 eggs per day, and perhaps 2000 in a lifetime).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_of_genetics_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_of_genetics-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Males and females are readily distinguished and virgin females are easily isolated, facilitating genetic crossing.</li>
<li>The mature larvae show giant chromosomes in the salivary glands called <a href="/wiki/Polytene_chromosome" title="Polytene chromosome">polytene chromosomes</a>—"puffs" indicate regions of transcription and hence gene activity.</li>
<li>It has only four pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomes" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a>: three <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosomes</a>, and one pair of <a href="/wiki/XY_sex-determination_system" title="XY sex-determination system">sex chromosomes</a>.</li>
<li>Males do not show meiotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiotic_recombination" title="Meiotic recombination">recombination</a>, facilitating genetic studies.</li>
<li>Recessive lethal "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balancer_chromosomes" title="Balancer chromosomes">balancer chromosomes</a>" carrying visible genetic markers can be used to keep stocks of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lethal_alleles" title="Lethal alleles">lethal alleles</a> in a heterozygous state without recombination due to multiple inversions in the balancer.</li>
<li>Genetic transformation techniques have been available since 1987.</li>
<li>Its complete <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> was <a href="/wiki/Genome_project" title="Genome project">sequenced</a> and first published in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_2000_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams_2000-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_markers">Genetic markers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Genetic markers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Genetic markers are commonly used in <i>Drosophila</i> research, for example within balancer chromosomes or P-element inserts, and most phenotypes are easily identifiable either with the naked eye or under a microscope. In the list of example common markers below, the allele symbol is followed by the name of the gene affected and a description of its phenotype. <i>(Note: Recessive alleles are in lower case, while dominant alleles are capitalised.)</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Cy<sup>1</sup>: Curly; the wings curve away from the body, flight may be somewhat impaired.</li>
<li>e<sup>1</sup>: ebony; black body and wings (heterozygotes are also visibly darker than wild type).</li>
<li>Sb<sup>1</sup>: stubble; bristles are shorter and thicker than wild type.</li>
<li>w<sup>1</sup>: <a href="/wiki/White_(mutation)" title="White (mutation)">white</a>; eyes lack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_pigment" title="Visual pigment">pigmentation</a> and appear white.</li>
<li>y<sup>1</sup>: yellow; body pigmentation and wings appear yellow. This is the fly analog of <a href="/wiki/Albinism" title="Albinism">albinism</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p><i>Drosophila</i> genes are traditionally named after the <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> they cause when mutated. For example, the absence of a particular gene in <i>Drosophila</i> will result in a mutant embryo that does not develop a heart. Scientists have thus called this gene <i>tinman</i>, named after the <a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">Oz</a> <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">character of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This system of nomenclature results in a wider range of gene names than in other organisms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genome">Genome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Genome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="845" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg/400px-Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg/600px-Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg/800px-Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosophila-chromosome-diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> of <i>D. melanogaster</i> (sequenced in 2000, and curated at the <a href="/wiki/FlyBase" title="FlyBase">FlyBase</a> database<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_2000_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Adams_2000-26">[26]</a></sup>) contains four pairs of chromosomes: an X/Y pair, and three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomes" title="Autosomes">autosomes</a> labeled 2, 3, and 4. The fourth chromosome is so tiny, it is often ignored, aside from its important <i>eyeless</i> gene. The <i>D. melanogaster</i> sequenced genome of 139.5 million base pairs has been annotated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and contains around 15,682 genes according to Ensemble release 73. More than 60% of the genome appears to be functional non-protein-coding DNA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halligan_2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halligan_2006-29">[29]</a></sup> involved in gene expression control. Determination of sex in <i>Drosophila</i> occurs by the <a href="/wiki/X:A_ratio" title="X:A ratio">X:A ratio</a> of X chromosomes to autosomes, not because of the presence of a Y chromosome as in human sex determination. Although the Y chromosome is entirely <a href="/wiki/Heterochromatin" title="Heterochromatin">heterochromatic</a>, it contains at least 16 genes, many of which are thought to have male-related functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carvalho_2002_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carvalho_2002-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similarity_to_humans">Similarity to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Similarity to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A March 2000 study by <a href="/wiki/National_Human_Genome_Research_Institute" title="National Human Genome Research Institute">National Human Genome Research Institute</a> comparing the fruit fly and human genome estimated that about 60% of genes are conserved between the two species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> About 75% of known human disease genes have a recognizable match in the genome of fruit flies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and 50% of fly protein sequences have mammalian homologs. An online database called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Homophila&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Homophila (page does not exist)">Homophila</a> is available to search for human disease gene homologues in flies and vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <i>Drosophila</i> is being used as a genetic model for several human diseases including the neurodegenerative disorders <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s" title="Parkinson's">Parkinson's</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huntington%27s" title="Huntington's">Huntington's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spinocerebellar_ataxia" title="Spinocerebellar ataxia">spinocerebellar ataxia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alzheimers" title="Alzheimers">Alzheimer's disease</a>. The fly is also being used to study mechanisms underlying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical_theory" title="Free radical theory">aging and oxidative stress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immunity</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_abuse" title="Drug abuse">drug abuse</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drosophila_embryogenesis" title="Drosophila embryogenesis">Drosophila embryogenesis</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Embryogenesis" title="Embryogenesis">Embryogenesis</a> in <i>Drosophila</i> has been extensively studied, as its small size, short generation time, and large brood size makes it ideal for genetic studies. It is also unique among model organisms in that cleavage occurs in a <a href="/wiki/Syncytium" title="Syncytium">syncytium</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:207px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drosophila_m_oogenesis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="191" data-file-width="205" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/81/Drosophila_m_oogenesis.png" width="205"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drosophila_m_oogenesis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>D. melanogaster</i> <a href="/wiki/Oogenesis" title="Oogenesis">oogenesis</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During oogenesis, cytoplasmic bridges called "ring canals" connect the forming oocyte to nurse cells. Nutrients and developmental control molecules move from the nurse cells into the oocyte. In the figure to the left, the forming oocyte can be seen to be covered by follicular support cells.</p>
<p>After fertilization of the oocyte, the early embryo (or <a href="/wiki/Syncytium" title="Syncytium">syncytial embryo</a>) undergoes rapid DNA replication and 13 nuclear divisions until about 5000 to 6000 nuclei accumulate in the unseparated cytoplasm of the embryo. By the end of the eighth division, most nuclei have migrated to the surface, surrounding the yolk sac (leaving behind only a few nuclei, which will become the yolk nuclei). After the 10th division, the pole cells form at the posterior end of the embryo, segregating the germ line from the syncytium. Finally, after the 13th division, cell membranes slowly invaginate, dividing the syncytium into individual somatic cells. Once this process is completed, <a href="/wiki/Gastrulation" title="Gastrulation">gastrulation</a> starts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flymove_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-flymove-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nuclear division in the early <i>Drosophila</i> embryo happens so quickly, no proper checkpoints exist, so mistakes may be made in division of the DNA. To get around this problem, the nuclei that have made a mistake detach from their <a href="/wiki/Centrosome" title="Centrosome">centrosomes</a> and fall into the centre of the embryo (yolk sac), which will not form part of the fly.</p>
<p>The gene network (transcriptional and protein interactions) governing the early development of the fruit fly embryo is one of the best understood gene networks to date, especially the patterning along the anteroposterior (AP) and dorsoventral (DV) axes (See under <a href="/wiki/Morphogenesis#Anterior-posterior_axis_patterning_in_Drosophila" title="Morphogenesis">morphogenesis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flymove_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-flymove-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The embryo undergoes well-characterized <a href="/wiki/Morphogenesis" title="Morphogenesis">morphogenetic</a> movements during gastrulation and early development, including germ-band extension, formation of several furrows, ventral invagination of the mesoderm, and posterior and anterior invagination of endoderm (gut), as well as extensive body segmentation until finally hatching from the surrounding cuticle into a first-instar larva.</p>
<p>During larval development, tissues known as <a href="/wiki/Imaginal_disc" title="Imaginal disc">imaginal discs</a> grow inside the larva. Imaginal discs develop to form most structures of the adult body, such as the head, legs, wings, thorax, and genitalia. Cells of the imaginal disks are set aside during embryogenesis and continue to grow and divide during the larval stages—unlike most other cells of the larva, which have differentiated to perform specialized functions and grow without further cell division. At metamorphosis, the larva forms a <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupa</a>, inside which the larval tissues are reabsorbed and the imaginal tissues undergo extensive morphogenetic movements to form adult structures.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_determination">Sex determination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sex determination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Drosophila</i> flies have both X and Y chromosomes, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomes" title="Autosomes">autosomes</a>. Unlike humans, the Y chromosome does not confer maleness; rather, it encodes genes necessary for making sperm. Sex is instead determined by the ratio of X chromosomes to autosomes. Furthermore, each cell "decides" whether to be male or female independently of the rest of the organism, resulting in the occasional occurrence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gynandromorph" title="Gynandromorph">gynandromorphs</a>.</p>

<p>Three major genes are involved in determination of <i>Drosophila</i> sex. These are <i>sex-lethal, sisterless</i>, and <i>deadpan</i>. Deadpan is an autosomal gene which inhibits <i>sex-lethal</i>, while <i>sisterless</i> is carried on the X chromosome and inhibits the action of <i>deadpan</i>. An AAX cell has twice as much <i>deadpan</i> as <i>sisterless</i>, so <i>sex-lethal</i> will be inhibited, creating a male. However, an AAXX cell will produce enough <i>sisterless</i> to inhibit the action of <i>deadpan</i>, allowing the <i>sex-lethal</i> gene to be transcribed to create a female.</p>
<p>Later, control by <i>deadpan</i> and <i>sisterless</i> disappears and what becomes important is the form of the <i>sex-lethal</i> gene. A secondary promoter causes transcription in both males and females. Analysis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CDNA" title="CDNA">cDNA</a> has shown that different forms are expressed in males and females. <i>Sex-lethal</i> has been shown to affect the <a href="/wiki/RNA_splicing" title="RNA splicing">splicing</a> of its own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a>. In males, the third <a href="/wiki/Exon" title="Exon">exon</a> is included which encodes a <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codon</a>, causing a truncated form to be produced. In the female version, the presence of <i>sex-lethal</i> causes this exon to be missed out; the other seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> are produced as a full <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a> chain, again giving a difference between males and females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sin_2000_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sin_2000-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Presence or absence of functional sex-lethal proteins now go on to affect the transcription of another protein known as doublesex. In the absence of sex-lethal, doublesex will have the fourth exon removed and be translated up to and including exon 6 (DSX-M[ale]), while in its presence the fourth exon which encodes a stop codon will produce a truncated version of the protein (DSX-F[emale]). DSX-F causes transcription of Yolk proteins 1 and 2 in <a href="/wiki/Somatic_(biology)" title="Somatic (biology)">somatic</a> cells, which will be pumped into the <a href="/wiki/Oocyte" title="Oocyte">oocyte</a> on its production.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Immunity">Immunity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Immunity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike mammals, <i>Drosophila</i> flies only have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innate_immunity" title="Innate immunity">innate immunity</a> and lack an adaptive immune response. The <i>D. melanogaster</i> immune system can be divided into two responses: humoral and cell-mediated. The former is a systemic response mediated through the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toll_(gene)" title="Toll (gene)">Toll</a></i> and <i>imd</i> pathways, which are parallel systems for detecting microbes. The <i>Toll</i> pathway in <i>Drosophila</i> is known as the homologue of <i>Toll-like</i> pathways in mammals. Spatzle, a known ligand for the <i>Toll</i> pathway in flies, is produced in response to <a href="/wiki/Gram-positive_bacteria" title="Gram-positive bacteria">Gram-positive bacteria</a>, parasites, and fungal infection. Upon infection, pro-Spatzle will be cleaved by protease SPE (Spatzle processing enzyme) to become active Spatzle, which then binds to the <i>Toll</i> receptor located on the cell surface (Fat body, hemocytes) and dimerise for activation of downstream <a href="/wiki/NF-%CE%BAB" title="NF-κB">NF-κB</a> signaling pathways. The imd pathway, though, is triggered by <a href="/wiki/Gram-negative_bacteria" title="Gram-negative bacteria">Gram-negative bacteria</a> through soluble and surface receptors (PGRP-LE and LC, respectively). <i>D. melanogaster</i> has a "fat body", which is thought to be <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> to the human liver. It is the primary secretory organ and produces <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_peptides" title="Antimicrobial peptides">antimicrobial peptides</a>. These peptides are secreted into the <a href="/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">hemolymph</a> and bind infectious bacteria, killing them by forming pores in their <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a>. Years ago<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> many drug companies wanted to purify these peptides and use them as antibiotics. Other than the fat body, hemocytes, the blood cells in <i>Drosophila</i>, are known as the homologue of mammalian monocyte/macrophages, possessing a significant role in immune responses. It is known from the literature that in response to immune challenge, hemocytes are able to secrete cytokines, for example Spatzle, to activate downstream signaling pathways in the fat body. However, the mechanism still remains unclear.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioral_genetics_and_neuroscience">Behavioral genetics and neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Behavioral genetics and neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1971, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Konopka" title="Ron Konopka">Ron Konopka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seymour_Benzer" title="Seymour Benzer">Seymour Benzer</a> published "Clock mutants of <i>Drosophila melanogaster</i>", a paper describing the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutations" title="Mutations">mutations</a> that affected an animal's behavior. Wild-type flies show an activity rhythm with a frequency of about a day (24 hours). They found mutants with faster and slower rhythms, as well as broken rhythms—flies that move and rest in random spurts. Work over the following 30 years has shown that these mutations (and others like them) affect a group of genes and their products that comprise a biochemical or <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">biological clock</a>. This clock is found in a wide range of fly cells, but the clock-bearing cells that control activity are several dozen neurons in the fly's central brain.</p>
<p>Since then, Benzer and others have used behavioral screens to isolate genes involved in vision, olfaction, audition, learning/memory, courtship, pain, and other processes, such as longevity.</p>
<p>The first learning and memory mutants (<i>dunce</i>, <i>rutabaga</i>, etc.) were isolated by William "Chip" Quinn while in Benzer's lab, and were eventually shown to encode components of an intracellular signaling pathway involving <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_adenosine_monophosphate" title="Cyclic adenosine monophosphate">cyclic AMP</a>, protein kinase A, and a transcription factor known as CREB. These molecules were shown to be also involved in synaptic plasticity in <i>Aplysia</i> and mammals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Male flies sing to the females during courtship using their wings to generate sound, and some of the genetics of sexual behavior have been characterized. In particular, the <i><a href="/wiki/Fruitless_(gene)" title="Fruitless (gene)">fruitless</a></i> gene has several different splice forms, and male flies expressing female splice forms have female-like behavior and vice versa. The TRP channels <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NompC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NompC (page does not exist)">nompC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanchung" title="Nanchung">nanchung</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Inactive" title="Inactive">inactive</a> are expressed in sound-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Johnston%27s_organ" title="Johnston's organ">Johnston's organ</a> neurons and participate in the transduction of sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, <i>Drosophila</i> has been used in neuropharmacological research, including studies of cocaine and alcohol consumption. Models for <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a> also exist for flies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23452092_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23452092-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision">Vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:374px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fly_eye_stereo_pair.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="169" data-file-width="372" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4d/Fly_eye_stereo_pair.png" width="372"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Stereo images of the fly eye</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_eye" title="Compound eye">compound eye</a> of the fruit fly contains 760 unit eyes or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ommatidia" title="Ommatidia">ommatidia</a>, and are one of the most advanced among insects. Each ommatidium contains eight photoreceptor cells (R1-8), support cells, pigment cells, and a cornea. Wild-type flies have reddish pigment cells, which serve to absorb excess blue light so the fly is not blinded by ambient light.</p>
<p>Each photoreceptor cell consists of two main sections, the cell body and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhabdom" title="Rhabdom">rhabdomere</a>. The cell body contains the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>, while the 100-μm-long rhabdomere is made up of toothbrush-like stacks of membrane called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microvilli" title="Microvilli">microvilli</a>. Each microvillus is 1–2 μm in length and about 60 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardie_2001_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardie_2001-39">[39]</a></sup> The membrane of the rhabdomere is packed with about 100 million <a href="/wiki/Rhodopsin" title="Rhodopsin">rhodopsin</a> molecules, the visual protein that absorbs light. The rest of the visual proteins are also tightly packed into the microvillar space, leaving little room for <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a>.</p>
<p>The photoreceptors in <i>Drosophila</i> express a variety of rhodopsin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoform" title="Isoform">isoforms</a>. The R1-R6 photoreceptor cells express rhodopsin1 (Rh1), which absorbs blue light (480 nm). The R7 and R8 cells express a combination of either Rh3 or Rh4, which absorb UV light (345 nm and 375 nm), and Rh5 or Rh6, which absorb blue (437 nm) and green (508 nm) light, respectively. Each rhodopsin molecule consists of an opsin protein covalently linked to a <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> chromophore, 11-cis-3-hydroxyretinal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nichols_1985_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nichols_1985-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg/220px-Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg/330px-Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg/440px-Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Expression_of_Rhodopsin-1_in_Drosophila_eye.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Expression of rhodopsin1 (Rh1) in photoreceptors R1-R6</div>

<p>As in <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">vertebrate vision</a>, visual transduction in <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> occurs via a G protein-coupled pathway. However, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>, the <a href="/wiki/G_protein" title="G protein">G protein</a> is transducin, while the G protein in invertebrates is Gq (dgq in <i>Drosophila</i>). When rhodopsin (Rh) absorbs a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> of light its chromophore, 11-cis-3-hydroxyretinal, is isomerized to all-trans-3-hydroxyretinal. Rh undergoes a conformational change into its active form, metarhodopsin. Metarhodopsin activates Gq, which in turn activates a <a href="/wiki/Phospholipase" title="Phospholipase">phospholipase</a> Cβ (PLCβ) known as NorpA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raghu_2000_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raghu_2000-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>PLCβ hydrolyzes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphatidylinositol_(4,5)-bisphosphate" title="Phosphatidylinositol (4,5)-bisphosphate">phosphatidylinositol (4,5)-bisphosphate</a> (PIP<sub>2</sub>), a <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipid</a> found in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a>, into soluble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inositol_triphosphate" title="Inositol triphosphate">inositol triphosphate</a> (IP<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Diglyceride" title="Diglyceride">diacylglycerol</a> (DAG), which stays in the cell membrane. DAG or a derivative of DAG causes a <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>-selective <a href="/wiki/Ion_channel" title="Ion channel">ion channel</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transient_receptor_potential" title="Transient receptor potential">transient receptor potential</a> (TRP) to open and calcium and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> flows into the cell. IP<sub>3</sub> is thought to bind to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inositol_triphosphate_receptor" title="Inositol triphosphate receptor">IP<sub>3</sub> receptors</a> in the subrhabdomeric cisternae, an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>, and cause release of calcium, but this process does not seem to be essential for normal vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raghu_2000_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Raghu_2000-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Calcium binds to proteins such as <a href="/wiki/Calmodulin" title="Calmodulin">calmodulin</a> (CaM) and an eye-specific <a href="/wiki/Kinase" title="Kinase">protein kinase C</a> (PKC) known as InaC. These proteins interact with other proteins and have been shown to be necessary for shut off of the light response. In addition, proteins called <a href="/wiki/Arrestin" title="Arrestin">arrestins</a> bind metarhodopsin and prevent it from activating more Gq. A <a href="/wiki/Sodium-calcium_exchanger" title="Sodium-calcium exchanger">sodium-calcium exchanger</a> known as CalX pumps the calcium out of the cell. It uses the inward <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_gradient" title="Electrochemical gradient">sodium gradient</a> to export calcium at a <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a> of 3 Na<sup>+</sup>/ 1 Ca<sup>++</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_2005_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang_2005-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>TRP, InaC, and PLC form a signaling complex by binding a <a href="/wiki/Transcriptional_regulation#Regulation_of_transcription_machinery" title="Transcriptional regulation">scaffolding protein</a> called InaD. InaD contains five binding domains called <a href="/wiki/PDZ_domain" title="PDZ domain">PDZ domain</a> proteins, which specifically bind the C termini of target proteins. Disruption of the complex by mutations in either the PDZ domains or the target proteins reduces the efficiency of signaling. For example, disruption of the interaction between InaC, the protein kinase C, and InaD results in a delay in inactivation of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Light_response&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Light response (page does not exist)">light response</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike vertebrate metarhodopsin, invertebrate metarhodopsin can be converted back into rhodopsin by absorbing a photon of orange light (580 nm).</p>
<p>About two-thirds of the <i>Drosophila</i> brain is dedicated to visual processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spatial_resolution" title="Spatial resolution">spatial resolution</a> of their vision is significantly worse than that of humans, their <a href="/wiki/Temporal_resolution" title="Temporal resolution">temporal resolution</a> is around 10 times better.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wings of a fly are capable of beating up to 220 times per second.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Flies fly via straight sequences of movement interspersed by rapid turns called saccades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fry_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-fry-44">[44]</a></sup> During these turns, a fly is able to rotate 90° in less than 50 milliseconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fry_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-fry-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Characteristics of <i>Drosophila</i> flight may be dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the air, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> of the fly body, but the opposite case with inertia as the dominant force may occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fry_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-fry-44">[44]</a></sup> However, subsequent work showed that while the viscous effects on the insect body during flight may be negligible, the aerodynamic forces on the wings themselves actually cause fruit flies' turns to be damped viscously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_pest">As a pest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drosophila_melanogaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: As a pest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Drosophila</i> is commonly considered a pest due to its tendency to infest habitations and establishments where fruit is found; the flies may collect in homes, restaurants, stores, and other locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ento.psu.edu_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ento.psu.edu-3">[3]</a></sup> Removal of an infestation can be difficult, as larvae may continue to hatch in nearby fruit even as the adult population is eliminated.</p>
