<p>The <b>Nordic model</b> (also called <b>Nordic capitalism</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nordic_Way.2C_2011._1-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nordic_Way.2C_2011.-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>Nordic social democracy</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> refers to the economic and <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">social policies</a> common to the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a> (<a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>). This includes a combination of <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> with a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective bargaining</a> at the national level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nordic_Model:_Pros_and_Cons_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nordic_Model:_Pros_and_Cons-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although there are significant differences among the Nordic countries, they all share some common traits. These include support for a "universalist" welfare state aimed specifically at enhancing individual autonomy and promoting <a href="/wiki/Social_mobility" title="Social mobility">social mobility</a>; a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporatist" title="Corporatist">corporatist</a> system involving a tripartite arrangement where representatives of labor and employers negotiate wages and labor market policy mediated by the government;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and a commitment to widespread <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_ownership" title="Private ownership">private ownership</a>, free markets and <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_E._McWhinney_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-James_E._McWhinney-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each of the Nordic countries has its own economic and social models, sometimes with large differences from its neighbours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> According to sociologist <a href="/wiki/Lane_Kenworthy" title="Lane Kenworthy">Lane Kenworthy</a>, in the context of the Nordic model, "social democracy" refers to a set of policies for promoting economic security and opportunity within the framework of capitalism rather than a system to replace capitalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nordiske-flag.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="562" data-file-width="720" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Nordiske-flag.jpg/225px-Nordiske-flag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Nordiske-flag.jpg/338px-Nordiske-flag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Nordiske-flag.jpg/450px-Nordiske-flag.jpg 2x" width="225"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nordiske-flag.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Flags of the Nordic countries</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>"The Nordic Model – Embracing globalization and sharing risks" characterises the system as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>An elaborate <a href="/wiki/Social_safety_net" title="Social safety net">social safety net</a> in addition to public services such as <a href="/wiki/Free_education" title="Free education">free education</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_healthcare" title="Universal healthcare">universal healthcare</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Strong property rights, contract enforcement, and overall ease of doing business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Public <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a> plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Low barriers to free trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economicfreedom_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-economicfreedom-12">[12]</a></sup> This is combined with collective risk sharing (<a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">social programs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labour_economics" title="Labour economics">labour market</a> institutions) which has provided a form of protection against the risks associated with economic openness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Little <a href="/wiki/Product_market" title="Product market">product market</a> regulation. <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a> rank very high in product market freedom according to <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">OECD</a> rankings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Low levels of corruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup> In Transparency International's 2015 <a href="/wiki/Corruption_Perceptions_Index" title="Corruption Perceptions Index">Corruption Perceptions Index</a>, Denmark, Finland, Sweden, and Norway were ranked among the top 10 least corrupt of the 167 countries evaluated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>High percentage of workers belonging to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_union" title="Labour union">labour union</a>. In 2013, labour union density was 85.5% in Iceland, 69% in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, 67.7% in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, and 52.1% in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. In comparison, labour union density was 13.6% in Mexico and 10.8% in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The lower union density in Norway is mainly explained by the absence of a <a href="/wiki/Ghent_system" title="Ghent system">Ghent system</a> since 1938. In contrast, Denmark, Finland and Sweden all have union-run unemployment funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>A partnership between employers, trade unions and the government, whereby these social partners negotiate the terms to regulating the workplace among themselves, rather than the terms being imposed by law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Sweden has decentralised wage co-ordination, while Finland is ranked the least flexible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup> The changing economic conditions have given rise to fear among workers as well as resistance by trade unions in regards to reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup> At the same time, reforms and favourable economic development seem to have reduced unemployment, which has traditionally been higher. <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Denmark's Social Democrats</a> managed to push through reforms in 1994 and 1996 (see <a href="/wiki/Flexicurity" title="Flexicurity">flexicurity</a>).</li>
<li>Sweden at 56.6% of <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a>, Denmark at 51.7%, and Finland at 48.6% reflect very high <a href="/wiki/Government_spending" title="Government spending">public spending</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economicfreedom_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-economicfreedom-12">[12]</a></sup> One key reason for public spending is the large number of <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">public employees</a>. These employees work in various fields including education, healthcare, and for the government itself. They often have lifelong job security and make up around a third of the workforce (more than 38% in Denmark). Public spending in social transfers such as unemployment benefits and early-retirement programmes is high. In 2001, the wage-based unemployment benefits were around 90% of wage in Denmark and 80% in Sweden, compared to 75% in the Netherlands and 60% in Germany. The unemployed were also able to receive benefits several years before reductions, compared to quick benefit reduction in other countries.</li>
<li>Public expenditure for health and education is significantly higher in Denmark, Sweden, and Norway in comparison to the OECD average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_burden" title="Tax burden">tax burdens</a> (as a percentage of GDP) are among the world's highest; Sweden (51.1%), Denmark (46% in 2011),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and Finland (43.3%)</li>
<li>The United Nations <i><a href="/wiki/World_Happiness_Report" title="World Happiness Report">World Happiness Report</a> 2013</i> shows that the happiest nations are concentrated in Northern Europe. The Nordics ranked highest on the metrics of real GDP per capita, healthy life expectancy, having someone to count on, perceived freedom to make life choices, generosity and freedom from corruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Nordic countries received the highest ranking for protecting workers rights on the <a href="/wiki/International_Trade_Union_Confederation" title="International Trade Union Confederation">International Trade Union Confederation's</a> 2014 Global Rights Index, with Denmark being the only nation to receive a perfect score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects">Aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_market_policy">Labor market policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Labor market policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Social_corporatism" title="Social corporatism">Social corporatism</a></div>
<p>The Nordic countries share active labor market policies as part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporatist" title="Corporatist">corporatist</a> economic model intended to reduce conflict between labor and the interests of capital. The corporatist system is most extensive in Sweden and Norway, where employer federations and labor representatives bargain at the national level mediated by the government. Labor market interventions are aimed at providing job retraining and relocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nordic labor market is flexible, with laws making it easy for employers to hire and shed workers or introduce labor-saving technology. To mitigate the negative effect on workers, the government labor market policies are designed to provide generous social welfare, job retraining and relocation to limit any conflicts between capital and labor that might arise from this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_E._McWhinney_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-James_E._McWhinney-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_system">Economic system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nordic model is underpinned by a free market capitalist economic system that features high degrees of private ownership<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nordic_Model:_Pros_and_Cons_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nordic_Model:_Pros_and_Cons-5">[5]</a></sup> with the exception of Norway, which includes a large number of state-owned enterprises and state ownership in publicly listed firms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nordic model is described as a system of competitive capitalism combined with a large percentage of the population employed by the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a> (roughly 30% of the work force).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_leaders_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economist_leaders-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2013, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> described its countries as "stout free-traders who resist the temptation to intervene even to protect iconic companies" while also looking for ways to temper capitalism's harsher effects, and declared that the Nordic countries "are probably the best-governed in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_leaders_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Economist_leaders-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Some economists have referred to the Nordic economic model as a form of "cuddly" capitalism, with low levels of inequality, generous welfare states and reduced concentration of top incomes, and contrast it with the more "cut-throat" capitalism of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, which has high levels of inequality and <a href="/wiki/Income_inequality_in_the_United_States" title="Income inequality in the United States">a larger concentration of top incomes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordicmodel_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-nordicmodel-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in the 1990s, the Swedish economy pursued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoliberal" title="Neoliberal">neoliberal</a> reforms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> that reduced the role of the public sector, leading to the fastest growth in inequality of any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, Sweden's income inequality still remains lower than most other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nordic_welfare_model">Nordic welfare model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nordic welfare model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nordic welfare model refers to the <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">welfare</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">policies</a> of the Nordic countries, which also tie into their labor market policies. The Nordic model of welfare is distinguished from other types of welfare states by its emphasis on maximizing labor force participation, promoting <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarian</a> and extensive benefit levels, the large magnitude of income redistribution, and liberal use of expansionary fiscal policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esping-Andersen_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esping-Andersen-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>While there are differences among different Nordic countries, they all share a broad commitment to social cohesion, a universal nature of welfare provision in order to safeguard individualism by providing protection for vulnerable individuals and groups in society, and maximizing public participation in social decision-making. It is characterized by flexibility and openness to innovation in the provision of welfare. The Nordic welfare systems are mainly funded through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxation" title="Taxation">taxation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nordic_Council_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nordic_Council-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the common values, the Nordic countries take different approaches to the practical administration of the welfare state. Denmark features a high degree of private sector provision of public services and welfare, alongside an assimilation immigration policy. Iceland's welfare model is based on a "welfare-to-work" (see: <a href="/wiki/Workfare" title="Workfare">workfare</a>) model, while part of Finland's welfare state includes the <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_sector" title="Voluntary sector">voluntary sector</a> playing a significant role in providing care for the elderly. Norway relies most extensively on public provision of welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nordic_Council_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nordic_Council-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty_reduction">Poverty reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Poverty reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Nordic model has been successful at significantly reducing poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2011, poverty rates, before taking into account the effects of taxes and transfers, stood at 24.7% in Denmark, 31.9% in Finland, 21.6% in Iceland, 25.6% in Norway, and 26.5% in Sweden. After accounting for taxes and transfers the poverty rates for the same year became 6%, 7.5%, 5.7%, 7.7%, and 9.7% respectively, for an average reduction of 18.7 p.p.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compareyourcountry.org_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-compareyourcountry.org-34">[34]</a></sup> Compared to the US, which has a poverty level pre-tax of 28.3% and post-tax of 17.4% for a reduction of 10.9 p.p., the effects of tax and transfers on poverty in all the Nordic countries are substantially bigger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compareyourcountry.org_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-compareyourcountry.org-34">[34]</a></sup> In comparison to France (27 p.p. reduction) and Germany (24.2 p.p. reduction), however, the taxes and transfers in the Nordic countries are smaller on average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compareyourcountry.org_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-compareyourcountry.org-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jerry_Mander" title="Jerry Mander">Jerry Mander</a> has likened the Nordic model to a kind of "hybrid" system which features a blend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitalist" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> economics and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Lane Kenworthy advocates for the U.S. to make a gradual transition to an economic system similar to those of the Nordic countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> Senator <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> (I-VT), a self-described <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_socialist" title="Democratic socialist">democratic socialist</a>, has been a strong proponent of the Nordic system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Nobel Prize-winning economist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a> has noted that there is higher social mobility in the Scandinavian countries than in the United States, and argues that Scandinavia is now the land of opportunity that the United States once was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> American author <a href="/wiki/Ann_Jones_(author)" title="Ann Jones (author)">Ann Jones</a>, who lived in Norway for four years, contends "the Nordic countries give their populations freedom <i>from</i> the market by using capitalism as a tool to benefit everyone," whereas in the United States "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoliberal" title="Neoliberal">neoliberal</a> politics puts the foxes in charge of the henhouse, and capitalists have used the wealth generated by their enterprises (as well as financial and political manipulations) to capture the state and pluck the chickens."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Klein" title="Naomi Klein">Naomi Klein</a>, former Soviet leader <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> sought to move the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a> in a similar direction to the Nordic system, combining free markets with a social safety net—but still retaining public ownership of key sectors—ingredients that he believed would transform the USSR into "a socialist beacon for all mankind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nordic combination of extensive public provision of <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">welfare</a> and a culture of <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a> has been described by Lars Trägårdh, of <a href="/wiki/Ersta_Sk%C3%B6ndal_University_College" title="Ersta Sköndal University College">Ersta Sköndal University College</a>, as "<a href="/wiki/Statism" title="Statism">statist</a> individualism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2016 survey by the think tank <a href="/wiki/Israel_Democracy_Institute" title="Israel Democracy Institute">Israel Democracy Institute</a> found that nearly 60 percent of <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Jews" title="Israeli Jews">Israeli Jews</a> prefer a "Scandinavian model" economy, with high taxes and a robust welfare state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>George Lakey, author of <i>Viking Economics</i>, asserts that Americans generally misunderstand the nature of the Nordic "welfare state":</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Americans imagine that “welfare state” means the U.S. welfare system on steroids. Actually, the Nordics scrapped their American-style welfare system at least 60 years ago, and substituted universal services, which means everyone—rich and poor—gets free higher education, free medical services, free eldercare, etc. Universal totally beats the means-testing characteristic of their dreadful old welfare system that they discarded and that the United States still has.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordic_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The socialist economists <a href="/wiki/John_Roemer" title="John Roemer">John Roemer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pranab_Bardhan" title="Pranab Bardhan">Pranab Bardhan</a> criticize Nordic-style social democracy by questioning its effectiveness at promoting relative <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarianism</a> as well as its <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. They point out that social democracy requires a strong <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">labor movement</a> to sustain the heavy redistribution required, arguing that it is idealistic to think such redistribution can be accomplished in other countries with weaker labor movements. They note that, even in the Scandinavian countries, social democracy has been in decline since the weakening of the labor movement, arguing that the sustainability of social democracy is limited. Roemer and Bardham argue that establishing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_socialist" title="Market socialist">market socialist</a> economy by changing enterprise ownership would be more effective at promoting egalitarianism than social democratic redistribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historian Guðmundur Jónsson argues that it would be inaccurate to include Iceland in one aspect of the Nordic model, that of <a href="/wiki/Consensus_democracy" title="Consensus democracy">consensus democracy</a>. He writes, "Icelandic democracy is better described as more adversarial than consensual in style and practice. The labour market was rife with conflict and strikes more frequent than in Europe, resulting in strained government–trade union relationship. Secondly, Iceland did not share the Nordic tradition of power-sharing or corporatism as regards labour market policies or macro-economic policy management, primarily because of the weakness of Social Democrats and the Left in general. Thirdly, the legislative process did not show a strong tendency towards consensus-building between government and opposition with regard to government seeking consultation or support for key legislation. Fourthly, the political style in legislative procedures and public debate in general tended to be adversarial rather than consensual in nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
