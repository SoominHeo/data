<p>The <b>Norwegian Forest cat</b> (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <span lang="no" xml:lang="no"><i><b>Norsk skogkatt</b></i></span> or <i><b><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Norsk skaukatt</span></b></i>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cat_breeds" title="Cat breeds">breed</a> of domestic <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cat</a> originating in Northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_breed" title="Natural breed">natural breed</a> is adapted to a very cold climate, with top coat of glossy, long, water-shedding hairs and a woolly undercoat for insulation. Although this is uncertain, the breed's ancestors may have been a <a href="/wiki/Landrace" title="Landrace">landrace</a> of short-haired cats brought to <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Vikings</a> around 1000 AD, who may also have brought with them long-haired cats, like those ancestral to the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_(cat)" title="Siberian (cat)">Siberian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Angora" title="Turkish Angora">Turkish Angora</a> breeds. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the breed became nearly extinct until efforts by the Norwegian Forest Cat Club helped the breed by creating an official breeding program. It was registered as a breed with the European <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_F%C3%A9line" title="Fédération Internationale Féline">Fédération Internationale Féline</a> in the 1970s, when a local cat fancier, Carl-Fredrik Nordane, took notice of the breed and made efforts to register it. Currently, the Norwegian Forest breed is very popular in Norway, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p>It is a big, strong cat, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Maine_Coon" title="Maine Coon">Maine Coon</a> breed, with long legs, a bushy tail and a sturdy body. The breed is very good at climbing, since they have strong claws. The lifespan is usually 14 to 16 years, though kidney and heart diseases have been reported in the breed. Specifically in this breed, complex rearrangements of <a href="/wiki/Glycogen_branching_enzyme" title="Glycogen branching enzyme">glycogen branching enzyme</a> (GBE1) can cause a perinatal hypoglycaemic collapse and a late-juvenile-onset neuromuscular degeneration in <a href="/wiki/Glycogen_storage_disease" title="Glycogen storage disease">glycogen storage disease</a> <a href="/wiki/Glycogen_storage_disease_type_IV" title="Glycogen storage disease type IV">type IV</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Forest_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Norwegian Forest cat is adapted to survive <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>'s cold weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Its ancestors may include black and white shorthair cats brought to Norway from Great Britain some time after 1000 AD by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Vikings</a>, and longhaired cats brought to Norway by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusaders" title="Crusaders">Crusaders</a>. These cats could have reproduced with farm and feral stock and may have eventually evolved into the modern-day Norwegian Forest breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASPCA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASPCA-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Cat:_Its_Behavior.2C_Nutrition_and_Health_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Cat:_Its_Behavior.2C_Nutrition_and_Health-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ultimate_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ultimate-5">[5]</a></sup> The Siberian and the Turkish Angora, longhaired cats from Russia and Turkey, respectively, are also possible ancestors of the breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASPCA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASPCA-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">legends</a> refer to the <i>skogkatt</i> as a "mountain-dwelling fairy cat with an ability to climb sheer rock faces that other cats could not manage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat-6">[6]</a></sup> Since the Norwegian Forest cat is a very adept climber,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> author Claire Bessant believes that the <i>skogkatt</i> folktale could be about the ancestor of the modern Norwegian Forest breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat-6">[6]</a></sup> The name <i>Norse skogkatt</i> is used by some breeder and fancier organisations for the modern breed.</p>
<p>Most likely the ancestors of the Norwegian Forest cat served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ships%27_cats" title="Ships' cats">ships' cats</a> (mousers) on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking_ship" title="Viking ship">Viking ships</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfainc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfainc-8">[8]</a></sup> The original <a href="/wiki/Landrace" title="Landrace">landrace</a> lived in the Norwegian forests for many centuries, but were later prized for their hunting skills and were used on Norwegian farms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furstinger_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Furstinger-9">[9]</a></sup> until they were discovered in the early twentieth century by cat enthusiasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1938 the first organisation devoted to the breed, the Norwegian Forest Cat Club, was formed in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oslo_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-oslo-11">[11]</a></sup> The club's movement to preserve the breed was interrupted by <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Owing to <a href="/wiki/Crossbreed" title="Crossbreed">cross-breeding</a> with free-ranging domestic cats during the war, the Norwegian Forest cat became endangered and nearly extinct until the Norwegian Forest Cat Club helped the breed make a comeback by developing an official breeding program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (June 2014)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Since the cat did not leave Norway until the 1970s, it was not registered as a breed in the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_F%C3%A9line" title="Fédération Internationale Féline">Fédération Internationale Féline</a> (FIFe), the pan-European federation of <a href="/wiki/Cat_registry" title="Cat registry">cat registries</a>, until Carl-Fredrik Nordane, a Norwegian cat fancier, took notice of the breed, and made efforts to register it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oslo_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-oslo-11">[11]</a></sup> The breed was registered in Europe by the 1970s, and in the <a href="/wiki/American_Cat_Fanciers_Association" title="American Cat Fanciers Association">American Cat Fanciers Association</a> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cat_Lover.27s_Daily_Companion_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cat_Lover.27s_Daily_Companion-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1978, it was recognized in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="By what organisation? (June 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and in 1989, they were accepted as a breed in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> by the Norwegian Cat Club of Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norwegian Forest breed is very popular in Norway and Sweden. Since 2003, it has been the fifth most popular cat breed in France, where there are about 400 to 500 births per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breed_description">Breed description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Forest_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Breed description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Norwegian Forest Cat is strongly built and larger than an average cat. Adult females of the species will typically weigh in at 3.5 - 4.5 kg (7.7 - 10 lbs), while males tends to weigh in at 5.5 to 7.5 kg (12 - 16.5 lbs) . The breed has a long, sturdy body, long legs and a bushy tail. The coat consists of a long, glossy, thick and water-repellent top layer and a woolly undercoat and is thickest at the legs, chest and head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat-6">[6]</a></sup> The profile of the breed is generally straight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfainc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfainc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The head is long with an overall shape similar to an equilateral triangle, a strong chin, and a muzzle of medium length; a square or round-shaped head is considered to be a defect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedie_Active_Le_Chat_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedie_Active_Le_Chat-20">[20]</a></sup> The eyes are almond shaped and oblique, and may be of any colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The ears are large, wide at the base, and high set, have a tufted top, are placed in the extension of the triangle formed by the head, and end with a tuft of hair like the ears of the <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynx</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedie_Active_Le_Chat_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedie_Active_Le_Chat-20">[20]</a></sup> All coat colors are accepted except chocolate and lilac and the dilutions fawn and cinnamon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedie_Active_Le_Chat_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedie_Active_Le_Chat-20">[20]</a></sup> Since the cats have very strong claws, they are very good climbers, and can even climb rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cat_Lover.27s_Daily_Companion_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cat_Lover.27s_Daily_Companion-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norwegian Forest cats have a quiet voice but can develop a loud voice if kept in a house with a dog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Complete_Guide_to_the_Cat-6">[6]</a></sup> They are friendly, intelligent, and generally good with people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASPCA_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ASPCA-3">[3]</a></sup> The Norwegian Forest cat has a lot of energy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASPCA_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ASPCA-3">[3]</a></sup> and can be very demanding of attention. Those cats that live primarily outdoors become swift and effective hunters, but the breed can also adapt to indoor life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ultimate_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ultimate-5">[5]</a></sup> If bought from a registered breeder in the USA, they tend to cost from $550 to $800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burke_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burke-22">[22]</a></sup> The cats usually live to be 14 to 16 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burke_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burke-22">[22]</a></sup> As they are heavy-boned and tall, they require more food than most other domestic breeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Cat:_Its_Behavior.2C_Nutrition_and_Health_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Cat:_Its_Behavior.2C_Nutrition_and_Health-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burke_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burke-22">[22]</a></sup> Males are considerably heavier and larger-boned than females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_issues">Health issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Forest_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Health issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kidney and heart diseases have been reported in the breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burke_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Burke-22">[22]</a></sup> In an experiment directed by John C. Fyfea, Rebeccah L. Kurzhals, and others, it was concluded that a complex rearrangement in the breed's <a href="/wiki/Glycogen_branching_enzyme" title="Glycogen branching enzyme">Glycogen branching enzyme</a> (GBE1) can cause both a perinatal hypoglycemic collapse and a late-juvenile-onset neuromuscular degeneration in <a href="/wiki/Glycogen_storage_disease" title="Glycogen storage disease">glycogen storage disease</a> <a href="/wiki/Glycogen_storage_disease_type_IV" title="Glycogen storage disease type IV">type IV</a> in the breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This disorder, while rare, can prove fatal to cats that have it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASPCA_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ASPCA-3">[3]</a></sup> There are DNA tests available for GSD IV, and it is highly recommended (some cat associations obligate their Norwegian Forest cat breeder members) to carry out the DNA test before using such animals for breeding. PawPeds provide a pedigree database which comes together with health programmes, through publishing each single cat's test result, to provide useful information for breeders to make a well-informed breeding decision. The breed has also been known to suffer from <a href="/wiki/Hip_dysplasia_(canine)" title="Hip dysplasia (canine)">hip dysplasia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> which is a rare, partially hereditary disease of the hip joint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
