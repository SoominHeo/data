<p><b>Norwegian Air Shuttle ASA</b> (<a href="/wiki/Oslo_Stock_Exchange" title="Oslo Stock Exchange">OSE</a>: <a class="external text" href="http://www.oslobors.no/ob_eng/markedsaktivitet/stockOverview?newt__ticker=NAS" rel="nofollow">NAS</a>), trading as <b>Norwegian</b>, is the third largest <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost carrier</a> in Europe, the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> in Scandinavia, and the ninth-largest airline in Europe in terms of passenger numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It offers a high-frequency <a href="/wiki/Domestic_flight" title="Domestic flight">domestic flight</a> schedule within Scandinavia and Finland, and to business destinations such as London, as well as to holiday destinations in the Mediterranean and the Canary Islands, transporting over 30 million people in 2015.</p>
<p>As of July 2016, Norwegian operates 110 aircraft, of which 100 are <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737s</a> and 10 are <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliners</a>. The airline is known for its distinctive livery of white with a red nose, with individual portraits of noteworthy Scandinavians on the aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Vertical_stabilizer" title="Vertical stabilizer">tail fin</a>.</p>
<p>Norwegian launched its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-haul" title="Long-haul">long-haul</a> operation in May 2013. The long-haul flights were intended to be operated by two fully owned subsidiaries, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Long_Haul" title="Norwegian Long Haul">Norwegian Long Haul</a> and Irish based <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Air_International" title="Norwegian Air International">Norwegian Air International</a> (NAI), which has a unique <a href="/wiki/Air_operator%27s_certificate" title="Air operator's certificate">air operator's certificate</a> (AOC) but shares branding and commercial set-up with the rest of the Group. Due to delayed approval of a Foreign Air Service Permit from the U.S. Department of Transportation for NAI however, all long-haul flights have been performed by the parent company under its original AOC, NAX. Many flights from the European bases are now operated by NAI, under its unique AOC, using the IBK ICAO code to distinguish these flights. These are operated using Norwegian's Irish registered aircraft. Crew bases for the long haul Boeing 787 fleet are at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bangkok_Suvarnabhumi_Airport" title="Bangkok Suvarnabhumi Airport">Bangkok Suvarnabhumi Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">New York JFK Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">London Gatwick Airport</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale-Hollywood_International_Airport" title="Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport">Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_airline_.E2.80.93_1993.E2.80.932002">Regional airline â 1993â2002</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regional airline â 1993â2002">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Norwegian Air Shuttle (NAS) was founded on 22 January 1993 to take over the <a href="/wiki/Regional_airline" title="Regional airline">regional airline</a> services produced by <a href="/wiki/Busy_Bee" title="Busy Bee">Busy Bee</a> for <a href="/wiki/Braathens" title="Braathens">Braathens</a> in Western Norway. Busy Bee, founded in 1966, was a subsidiary of Braathens that operated a fleet of <a href="/wiki/Fokker_50" title="Fokker 50">Fokker 50</a> aircraft on <a href="/wiki/Air_charter" title="Air charter">charter</a> services. This included the network of regional services between cities on the west coast of Norway operated on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wet_lease" title="Wet lease">wet lease</a> for the mother company. Following Busy Bee's bankruptcy in December 1992, NAS took over three leased Fokker 50 aircraft, and started operating from <a href="/wiki/Bergen_Airport,_Flesland" title="Bergen Airport, Flesland">Bergen Airport, Flesland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Haugesund_Airport,_Karm%C3%B8y" title="Haugesund Airport, KarmÃ¸y">Haugesund Airport, KarmÃ¸y</a>, as well as from Bergen to <a href="/wiki/Molde_Airport,_%C3%85r%C3%B8" title="Molde Airport, ÃrÃ¸">Molde Airport, ÃrÃ¸</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kristiansund_Airport,_Kvernberget" title="Kristiansund Airport, Kvernberget">Kristiansund Airport, Kvernberget</a>, and onwards to <a href="/wiki/Trondheim_Airport,_V%C3%A6rnes" title="Trondheim Airport, VÃ¦rnes">Trondheim Airport, VÃ¦rnes</a>. The company was established and owned by former Busy Bee employees and initially had a workforce of fifty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was based in Bergen, but later established a technical base in Stavanger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-base_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-base-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1 April 1994, the airline also began service from Bergen to <a href="/wiki/%C3%85lesund_Airport,_Vigra" title="Ãlesund Airport, Vigra">Ãlesund Airport, Vigra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1995, the company received its fourth Fokker 50s, and had a revenue of NOKÂ 86.6 million and a profit of NOKÂ 2.9 million. It flew 50 daily services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1999, the company had six Fokker 50s and flew 500,000 passengers on 20,000 flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-base_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-base-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buylufttransport_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-buylufttransport-7">[7]</a></sup> The company had a revenue of NOKÂ 172 million and a profit of NOKÂ 13 million. On 2 June 2000, NAS bought the helicopter operator <a href="/wiki/Lufttransport" title="Lufttransport">Lufttransport</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helikopter_Service" title="Helikopter Service">Helikopter Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buylufttransport_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-buylufttransport-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2000, the NAS fleet was expanded to seven Fokker 50s. From 2 January 2001, several Braathens routes were terminated, including the NAS-operated services from Kristiansund to Trondheim and Molde. The route from Bergen to Haugesund, and BergenâMoldeâTrondheim were reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 January 2002, NAS took over the responsibility for the route from Stavanger to Newcastle, flying two round trips per day. This was the first route on which the airline did not wet lease the aircraft to Braathens, but instead operated the route in its own right. After Braathens was bought by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Airlines_System" title="Scandinavian Airlines System">Scandinavian Airlines System</a> (SAS) in November 2001, all contracts that Norwegian had with Braathens for the routes on the Norwegian west coast were cancelled by SAS, as it wanted its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/SAS_Commuter" title="SAS Commuter">SAS Commuter</a> to take the routes over. NAS had an 18-month cancellation period in its contract with Braathens, however this was not respected by SAS; the contracts were terminated without any notice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-cost_carrier_.E2.80.93_2002_onwards">Low-cost carrier â 2002 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Low-cost carrier â 2002 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Following the decision by SAS to purchase Braathens, and the subsequent termination of all contracts between Braathens and NAS, NAS announced in April 2002 that it would start domestic scheduled services as a low-cost carrier on the busiest routes. From 1 September 2002, the airline re-branded as Norwegian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The airline opened its second hub at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warsaw_Frederic_Chopin_Airport" title="Warsaw Frederic Chopin Airport">Warsaw Frederic Chopin Airport</a> in Poland, flying to Central European destinations. There were two Boeing 737 operating from Warsaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rep-2006-03_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-rep-2006-03-11">[11]</a></sup> The base was closed in 2010. Norwegian Air Shuttle ASA announced 24 April 2007 that it had bought 100% of the Swedish low-cost airline <a href="/wiki/FlyNordic" title="FlyNordic">FlyNordic</a> from <a href="/wiki/Finnair" title="Finnair">Finnair</a> plc; becoming the largest low-cost airline in Scandinavia. As payment for the shares in FlyNordic, Finnair received a 5% share stake in Norwegian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 30 August 2007, Norwegian ordered 42 new <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a> aircraft, with <a href="/wiki/Option_(aircraft_purchasing)" title="Option (aircraft purchasing)">options</a> for 42 more, an order worth US$3.1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-order_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-order-13">[13]</a></sup> This order was later increased by six aircraft in November 2009. In July 2010 15 of the options were converted to orders, and in June 2011 15 more options were converted, bringing the total order of new, owned 737-800s to 78 aircraft with 12 remaining options. Additionally, Norwegian introduced leased Boeing 737-800 aircraft into the fleet. The first leased 737-800 arrived at <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Airport,_Gardermoen" title="Oslo Airport, Gardermoen">Oslo Airport, Gardermoen</a>, Norway, on 26 January 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2010, Norwegian started flights from Oslo-Gardermoen and Stockholm to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helsinki-Vantaa_Airport" title="Helsinki-Vantaa Airport">Helsinki-Vantaa Airport</a>. During early 2011, Norwegian had three aircraft stationed there, introducing domestic flights to <a href="/wiki/Oulu_Airport" title="Oulu Airport">Oulu Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rovaniemi_Airport" title="Rovaniemi Airport">Rovaniemi Airport</a> on 31 March 2011. In May, flights to nine additional international destinations began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2009, Norwegian announced it intended to start flights from Oslo to New York City and Bangkok, requiring new intercontinental aircraft. In 2010, it said it was considering up to 15 intercontinental destinations from Scandinavia, and would also consider services to South America and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 8 November 2010, Norwegian announced that it had contracted to lease two new <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliners</a> with delivery in 2012; and that it was negotiating the leasing of additional aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 25 January 2012, Norwegian announced the largest orders of aircraft in European history. The orders consist of 22 Boeing 737-800 and 100 Boeing 737 MAX 8 aircraft with options for another 100 of the latter; and for 100 <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320neo_family" title="Airbus A320neo family">Airbus A320neos</a> with options for another 50.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late October 2012, the airline announced a new base at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Gatwick" title="London Gatwick">London Gatwick</a> from spring 2013 with three Boeing 737-800s to be used on new international routes from London to leisure destinations in Spain, Portugal, France, Italy and Croatia. All announced routes are flown in competition with airlines such as easyJet, Monarch, Ryanair and Thomson Airways. Gatwick is also served by Norwegian from a large number of cities in Scandinavia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016 Norwegian won its first charter contract in the United States, flying three Boeing 737-800s out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago/Rockford_International_Airport" title="Chicago/Rockford International Airport">Chicago/Rockford International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_Mitchell_International_Airport" title="General Mitchell International Airport">General Mitchell International Airport</a> in Milwaukee to Mexico and the Caribbean from December 2016 to April 2017 for <a href="/wiki/Apple_Leisure_Group" title="Apple Leisure Group">Apple Vacations</a> and Funjet Vacations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>The company is headed by CEO and largest shareholder <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rn_Kjos" title="BjÃ¸rn Kjos">BjÃ¸rn Kjos</a>, and the board is chaired by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B8rn_H._Kise&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="BjÃ¸rn H. Kise (page does not exist)">BjÃ¸rn H. Kise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The airline is listed on the <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Stock_Exchange" title="Oslo Stock Exchange">Oslo Stock Exchange</a>.</p>
<p>The company's head office is in <a href="/wiki/Diamanten" title="Diamanten">Diamanten</a>, an office building at <a href="/wiki/Fornebu" title="Fornebu">Fornebu</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A6rum" title="BÃ¦rum">BÃ¦rum</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diamanten_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-diamanten-23">[23]</a></sup> Previously, the airline had its head office functions inside other buildings in Fornebu,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> but in 2010 moved to Diamanten, which had been the former <a href="/wiki/Braathens" title="Braathens">Braathens</a>, and later SAS Norway, head office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diamanten_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-diamanten-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norwegian Group consists of the parent company Norwegian Air Shuttle ASA, and the fully owned subsidiaries Norwegian Air Shuttle Polska Sp.zo.o and Norwegian Air Shuttle Sweden AB. All flights are operated by the parent company Norwegian Air Shuttle ASA; the subsidiaries manage personnel, sales and marketing within certain geographical areas.</p>
<p>Norwegian Air Shuttle ASA owns 100% of the telephone company Call Norwegian AS, 99.9% of NAS Asset Management which owns the new 737-800 aircraft purchased from Boeing, 100% of NAS Asset Management Norway AS, and 100% of Norwegian Long Haul AS, as well as 20% of Norwegian Finans Holding ASA (<a href="/wiki/Bank_Norwegian" title="Bank Norwegian">Bank Norwegian AS</a>).</p>
<p>Norwegian is a member of <a href="/wiki/European_Low_Fares_Airline_Association" title="European Low Fares Airline Association">European Low Fares Airline Association</a>.</p>

<p>Norwegian serves Europe, North Africa and the Middle East for both business and leisure markets. In total the airline operates 416 routes to 126 destinations in 35 countries on four continents.</p>
<p>Domestic, intra-Nordic and typical European business and leisure destinations have the most service. The busiest routes in Norwegians network are the Oslo to Bergen and the Oslo to Trondheim routes with 15 daily round-trips. Norwegianâs largest non-Scandinavian operation is to London Gatwick with up to 24 daily round-trips.</p>
<p>Typical leisure destinations in Southern Europe are typically served once or twice a day from the main Nordic cities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-haul_operations">Long-haul operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Long-haul operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Long_Haul" title="Norwegian Long Haul">Norwegian Long Haul</a></div>
<p>Norwegian started long-haul flights on 30 May 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The first scheduled <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Long_Haul" title="Norwegian Long Haul">Norwegian Long Haul</a> flights were from Oslo and Stockholm to New York City and Bangkok, originally with wet-leased <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340-300</a> aircraft while the airline awaited delivery of its new <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliner</a> aircraft. In March 2013 Norwegian Air Shuttle confirmed new long haul routes from Copenhagen, Oslo and Stockholm to Fort Lauderdale in Florida, beginning on 29 November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_network">International network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: International network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Intra-Scandinavian routes, and in particular "the capital triangle" between Oslo, Stockholm and Copenhagen, is attractive due to extensive traffic for both business and leisure travellers. Other modes of transportation are generally slow between these cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>From 1993 to 2002, the company solely operated Fokker F-50 turbo-prop aircraft primarily as a commuter airline, having a total fleet of six in 2002. The company ceased all F-50 operations at the end of 2003 in order to focus on the Boeing 737-300 jet operations and sold the last three of the Fokker F-50 in early 2004. For a limited period in the early years of the 737 operation Norwegian operated a 737-500 as an interim solution while waiting for 737-300 deliveries. Following the acquisition of Swedish low cost airline <a href="/wiki/FlyNordic" title="FlyNordic">FlyNordic</a> in 2007, Norwegian inherited eight MD-80 aircraft. The last of the MD-80 aircraft was phased out two years later.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Livery">Livery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Livery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Norwegian's aircraft livery is white with a signal red nose. Slightly further down the aircraft is a dark blue line. The vertical stabilizer of the aircraft in Norwegian's fleet is either white with red and dark blue lines at the top with white Norwegians titles in the blue or features <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Air_Shuttle_/_Norwegian_Long_Haul_tail_art" title="Norwegian Air Shuttle / Norwegian Long Haul tail art">depictions of historically significant Norwegians, Finns, Danes, and Swedes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Norwegian has also operated a single aircraft in a special promotional livery for the insurance company Silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<p>All flight operations are performed under one single <a href="/wiki/Air_operator%27s_certificate" title="Air operator's certificate">air operator's certificate</a> (AOC) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICAO_airline_designator" title="ICAO airline designator">ICAO airline designator</a> NAX). The Group also held a Swedish AOC (ICAO airline designator NDC) up until 2009, but the double AOC operation was discontinued for efficiency purposes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The main technical base is at Stavanger, although heavy maintenance (<a href="/wiki/Aircraft_maintenance_checks" title="Aircraft maintenance checks">C/D checks</a>) and engine maintenance are put out on tender.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Norwegian contracts out all <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_ground_handling" title="Aircraft ground handling">aircraft ground handling</a> to third party companies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Norwegian, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-cost_airline" title="Low-cost airline">low-cost airline</a>, operates aircraft with all-<a href="/wiki/Economy_class" title="Economy class">economy class</a> seating. Surcharges are taken for on-board food and drinks, check-in baggage, payment by <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit card</a> and other non-core services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The airline runs a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequent_flyer_program" title="Frequent flyer program">frequent flyer program</a> called Norwegian Reward. Passengers can earn points based on the price of the ticket and the ticket class (20% on Flex tickets, 2% on LowFare tickets). Norwegian supported the ban on point accrual that was in force on Norwegian domestic flights until 16 May 2013, but when that ban was lifted, the reward programs were extended to that market as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norwegian also offers free WiFi on most of the services operated by its 737-800 fleet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> but not on its 787 aircraft.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customer_service_related_criticism">Customer service related criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Customer service related criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Norwegian Air customers have lodged a record number of <a href="/wiki/Airline_complaints" title="Airline complaints">complaints</a>, with a tribunal judge stating to <i><a href="/wiki/Dagens_N%C3%A6ringsliv" title="Dagens NÃ¦ringsliv">Dagens NÃ¦ringsliv</a></i>, "We have never before seen this scope of complaints in a single case".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> With more than 200 complaints having been registered with the Transport Complaints Board alone, passengers have created the <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> <a href="/wiki/Hashtag" title="Hashtag">hashtag</a>, #NeverFlyNorwegian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norwegian's policies have also been criticized by passengers who were left without food, drinks and blankets on board for up to 12 hours (available for pay but only with correct type of credit card).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In August 2014, 35,000 people were reportedly hit with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_delay" title="Flight delay">delays</a> when flying with Norwegian, and 1,200 passengers ultimately sued Norwegian for compensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, for the most part, the tribunal did not agree with the complaints and only in a few cases has Norwegian had to compensate the passenger(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour_related_criticism">Labour related criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Labour related criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 2011 and 2013, Norwegian Air Shuttle (NAS) has received harsh criticism regarding its treatment of employees. The media first reported NAS's announced intention to open a base in Helsinki, from where it hired pilots on short-term contracts (in Estonia) rather than as employees within the company. The Norwegian tax-office authorities suspected in August 2012 that many Norwegian citizens were working for NAS on these contracts and not paying Norwegian taxes despite operating on flights originating from Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norwegian Pilot's Union (NPU) brought NAS to court over the short-term contracts. NAS CEO BjÃ¸rn Kjos only inflamed matters when he declared that NAS would no longer hire employees on Norwegian terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the fall of 2012, NAS started to use contract-employed pilots on routes within Scandinavia, which was considered by the NPU to be an abrogation of labor terms regarding non-Scandinavian pilots on routes within Scandinavia. NPU soon after sued NAS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2013, the NPU announced their intention to strike as NAS forced its pilots to face dismissal or transfer to Norwegian Air Norway or Norwegian Air Resources AB, both subsidiaries of NAS. The respective subsidiary would then lease the pilots back to NAS. NPU and their Swedish counterpart SPF accused NAS of using this ploy to break the solidarity and organization of the pilots, with the eventual goal of co-ercing pilots to converting their jobs to contract positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>In mid-December, NAS faced its Swedish non-contract flight-attendants with either dismissal or transference to Proffice Aviation, an external staffing company. According to the Swedish cabin-crew union, Unionen, it managed to save the jobs of 53 NAS employees, but it was dissatisfied with the direction NAS had taken. The situation led to the leader for the <a href="/wiki/Left_Party_(Sweden)" title="Left Party (Sweden)">Swedish Left Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Sj%C3%B6stedt" title="Jonas SjÃ¶stedt">Jonas SjÃ¶stedt</a>, to state that stricter regulation is needed for the use of staffing-companies in Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norwegian_Long_Haul">Norwegian Long Haul</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_Air_Shuttle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Norwegian Long Haul">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Long_Haul#Criticism" title="Norwegian Long Haul">Norwegian Long Haul Â§Â Criticism</a></div>
<p>Norwegian has also been criticised for the terms of its contracts with its long-haul flight-attendants on contracts based in Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> This has caused the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Line_Pilots_Association" title="Air Line Pilots Association">Air Line Pilots Association</a> to further accuse Norwegian of unfair competition practices.</p>
<p>The airline contests these accusations and has disclosed the pay scale for its Thai employees, who earn between USD 33,300 and USD 39,200 per annum which is under the $42.2K USD <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> average pay for US flight attendants (though these comparisons are made between solely intercontinental Norwegian Long Haul flights versus domestic and intercontinental flights of US paid flight attendants).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
