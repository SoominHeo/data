<p>The <b>Norwegian heavy water sabotage</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nynorsk_language" title="Nynorsk language">Nynorsk</a>: <span lang="nn" xml:lang="nn"><i>Tungtvassaksjonen</i></span>) was a series of actions undertaken by Norwegian saboteurs during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> to prevent the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_nuclear_energy_project" title="German nuclear energy project">German nuclear energy project</a> from acquiring <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">heavy water</a> (<a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> oxide), which could have been used by the Nazis to produce <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>. In 1934, at Vemork, Norway, <a href="/wiki/Norsk_Hydro" title="Norsk Hydro">Norsk Hydro</a> built the first commercial plant capable of producing heavy water as a byproduct of fertilizer production. It had a capacity of 12 tonnes (13 short tons) per year. During World War II, the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> decided to remove the heavy water supply and destroy the heavy water plant in order to inhibit the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> development of nuclear weapons. Raids were aimed at the 60 MW Vemork power station at the <a href="/wiki/Rjukan" title="Rjukan">Rjukan</a> waterfall in <a href="/wiki/Telemark" title="Telemark">Telemark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.</p>
<p>Prior to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_invasion_of_Norway" title="German invasion of Norway">German invasion of Norway</a> on 9 April 1940, the <i><a href="/wiki/Deuxi%C3%A8me_Bureau" title="Deuxième Bureau">Deuxième Bureau</a></i> (French military intelligence) removed 185 kg (408 lb) of heavy water from the plant in Vemork in then-neutral Norway. The plant's managing director, Aubert, agreed to lend the heavy water to France for the duration of the war. The French transported it secretly to <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth, Scotland</a>, and then to France. The plant remained capable of producing heavy water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahl1999_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dahl1999-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Allies remained concerned that the occupation forces would use the facility to produce more heavy water for their weapons programme. Between 1940 and 1944, a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sabotage</a> actions, by the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_resistance_movement" title="Norwegian resistance movement">Norwegian resistance movement</a>—as well as Allied <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bomber" title="Strategic bomber">bombing</a>—ensured the destruction of the plant and the loss of the heavy water produced. These operations—codenamed <i>Grouse</i>, <i>Freshman</i>, and <i>Gunnerside</i>—finally managed to knock the plant out of production in early 1943.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Grouse_(Norway)" title="Operation Grouse (Norway)">Operation <i>Grouse</i></a>, the British <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">Special Operations Executive</a> (SOE) successfully placed four Norwegian nationals as an advance team in the region of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardanger_Plateau" title="Hardanger Plateau">Hardanger Plateau</a> above the plant in October 1942. The unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Operation_Freshman" title="Operation Freshman">Operation <i>Freshman</i></a> was mounted the following month by British paratroopers; they were to rendezvous with the Norwegians of Operation <i>Grouse</i> and proceed to Vemork. This attempt failed when the <a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">military gliders</a> crashed short of their destination, as did one of the tugs, a <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Handley Page Halifax</a> bomber. The other Halifax returned to base, but all the other participants were killed in the crashes or captured, interrogated, and executed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a></i>.</p>
<p>In February 1943, a team of SOE-trained Norwegian commandos succeeded in destroying the production facility with a second attempt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Gunnerside" title="Operation Gunnerside">Operation <i>Gunnerside</i></a>. Operation <i>Gunnerside</i> was later evaluated by SOE as the most successful act of sabotage in all of World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>These actions were followed by Allied bombing raids. The Germans elected to cease operation and remove the remaining heavy water to Germany. Norwegian resistance forces sank the ferry, <a href="/wiki/SF_Hydro" title="SF Hydro">SF <i>Hydro</i></a>, on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tinn" title="Lake Tinn">Lake Tinn</a>, preventing the heavy water from being removed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_background">Technical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg"><img alt="Table top with various pieces of experimental equipment." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg/220px-Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg/330px-Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg/440px-Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nuclear_Fission_Experimental_Apparatus_1938_-_Deutsches_Museum_-_Munich.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The experimental apparatus with which the chemists <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Strassmann" title="Fritz Strassmann">Fritz Strassmann</a> discovered nuclear fission of uranium in 1938</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> and his colleagues studied the results of bombarding uranium with neutrons in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The first person who mentioned the idea of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> in 1934 was <a href="/wiki/Ida_Noddack" title="Ida Noddack">Ida Noddack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Four years after the Fermi publication, in December 1938, <a href="/wiki/Lise_Meitner" title="Lise Meitner">Lise Meitner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_Robert_Frisch" title="Otto Robert Frisch">Otto Frisch</a> correctly interpreted the radiochemical experimental results of <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Strassmann" title="Fritz Strassmann">Fritz Strassmann</a> as evidence of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>. News of this discovery spread quickly among physicists and it was realised that if chain reactions could be tamed, fission could lead to a promising new source of power. What was needed was a substance that could <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">"moderate"</a> the energy of neutrons emitted in radioactive decay, so that they could be captured by other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> nuclei. <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">Heavy water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> were the prime candidates for moderating the energy of neutrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> investigated the production of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_nuclear_energy_project" title="German nuclear energy project">German nuclear energy project</a>), a range of options was identified. Although historical records provide limited detail on the German decision to pursue the heavy water approach, it became clear after the war that they had explored the option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Although ultimately unsuccessful, the approach chosen has been demonstrated to be technically viable:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">Plutonium</a>-239 (<sup>239</sup>Pu) makes an effective weapons material (although requiring an <i>implosion-type</i> mechanism as a simpler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_Man_nuclear_bomb" title="Thin Man nuclear bomb">Thin Man</a> <i>gun-type</i> bomb is not feasible).</li>
<li>Heavy water has been demonstrated as an effective <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">moderator</a> for <sup>239</sup>Pu production.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">Heavy water</a> may be separated from ordinary water by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches_to_developing_a_weapon">Approaches to developing a weapon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Approaches to developing a weapon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In nuclear weapon development, the main problem is securing sufficient "weapons grade" material. In particular, it is difficult to acquire either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>-235 (<sup>235</sup>U) or of <sup>239</sup>Pu. Weapons grade uranium requires mining, extracting and <a href="/wiki/Enriched_uranium" title="Enriched uranium">enriching</a> natural ore. Alternatively, plutonium can be "bred" in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">reactors</a> fueled by unenriched uranium, which requires chemical separation of the <sup>239</sup>Pu produced.</p>
<p>Unlike the Allies, who pursued both the enrichment of uranium and the production of plutonium, German scientists only focused on plutonium production, as this method was less expensive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plutonium_production">Plutonium production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plutonium production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the most common isotope of uranium, <a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238">uranium-238</a> (<sup>238</sup>U), can be used as secondary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissionable" title="Fissionable">fissionable</a> material in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bombs</a>, it cannot be used as the primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> material for an atomic bomb.<sup>238</sup>U can be used to produce <sup>239</sup>Pu, through the fission of <sup>235</sup>U which produces neutrons, some of which will be absorbed by <sup>238</sup>U creating <sup>239</sup>U. After a few days the <sup>239</sup>U will decay, turning into weapons-usable <sup>239</sup>Pu.</p>
<p>The Germans did not examine ultra-pure <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> because they did not know that the graphite they had tried was too impure to sustain a chain reaction, and abandoned it as a possible moderator. They instead settled on the <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">heavy-water</a>-based reactor design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A heavy water moderated nuclear reactor could be used to do nuclear fission research, and, ultimately, to breed plutonium from which a bomb could be constructed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_water_production">Heavy water production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heavy water production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="680" data-file-width="1024" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg/220px-Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg/330px-Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg/440px-Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deuterium_oxide_Norsk.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">Heavy water</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Norsk_Hydro" title="Norsk Hydro">Norsk Hydro</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In normal water, there is only one deuterium atom for every 6400 hydrogen atoms, but deuterium is more prevalent in the residue of water used as an <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>. An analysis of the residues from the <a href="/wiki/Vemork" title="Vemork">Vemork hydroelectric plant</a>, a large-scale hydrogen production plant using <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of water for ammonia production, showed a hydrogen/deuterium ratio of 48, most of the deuterium being bound in HDO molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leif_Tronstad" title="Leif Tronstad">Leif Tronstad</a>, then a lecturer at the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Institute_of_Technology" title="Norwegian Institute of Technology">Norwegian Institute of Technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jomar_Brun" title="Jomar Brun">Jomar Brun</a>, head of the hydrogen plant put forward a proposal in 1933, the year heavy water was first isolated, for a project, which was accepted by Norsk Hydro and production started in 1935.</p>
<p>The technology is straightforward. Heavy water (D<sub>2</sub>O) is separated from normal water by electrolysis because the difference in mass between the two hydrogen isotopes translates into a slight difference in the speed at which the reaction proceeds. To produce pure heavy water by electrolysis requires a large cascade of electrolysis chambers, and consumes large amounts of power. Since there was excess power available, heavy water could be purified from the existing electrolyte. As a result, Norsk Hydro became the heavy water supplier for the world's scientific community, as a by-product of fertilizer production, for which the ammonia was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hans_Suess#Notes" title="Hans Suess">Hans Suess</a> was a German adviser to the production of heavy water. Suess had assessed the Vemork plant as being incapable of producing militarily useful quantities of heavy water in less than five years at its then capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahl1999_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dahl1999-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_to_limit_German_access_to_heavy_water">Operations to limit German access to heavy water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operations to limit German access to heavy water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-invasion_efforts">Pre-invasion efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pre-invasion efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>French research considered production of <sup>239</sup>Pu using both heavy water and graphite moderated reactors. Preliminary French research indicated that the graphite which was then available commercially was not pure enough to serve the purpose, and that heavy water would be required. The German research community had reached a similar conclusion and in January 1940 had procured additional heavy water from Vemork. The German firm <a href="/wiki/IG_Farben" title="IG Farben">IG Farben</a>, which was a partial owner of Norsk Hydro, had ordered 100 kg (220 lb)/month; Norsk Hydro's maximum production rate was then limited to 10 kg (22 lb)/month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahl1999_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dahl1999-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1940, the "<a href="/wiki/Deuxi%C3%A8me_Bureau" title="Deuxième Bureau">Deuxième Bureau</a>" (French military intelligence) directed three French agents, Captain Muller and Lieutenants Mossé and Knall-Demars, to remove the world's extant supply, 185 kg (408 lb) of heavy water from the Vemork plant in then-neutral Norway. The Norsk Hydro General Director, <a href="/wiki/Axel_Aubert" title="Axel Aubert">Axel Aubert</a>, agreed to lend the heavy water to France for the duration of the war, observing that if Germany won the war, he was likely to be shot. Transportation was difficult as German Military Intelligence (the <i><a href="/wiki/Abwehr" title="Abwehr">Abwehr</a></i>) maintained a presence in Norway and had been alerted of ongoing French activities in Norway (although they had not been specifically warned about heavy water). Had they become aware of the shipment, they might have attempted to intercept it. The French transported it secretly to <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and then to France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahl1999_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dahl1999-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>When France was invaded the French nuclear scientist <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Joliot-Curie" title="Frédéric Joliot-Curie">Frédéric Joliot-Curie</a> took charge of the material, hiding it first in a Banque de France vault and then in a prison. Joliot-Curie then moved it to <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a>, where it, plus research papers and most of the scientists (Joliot-Curie remained in France) boarded the British tramp steamer <a href="/wiki/SS_Broompark_(1939)" title="SS Broompark (1939)"><i>Broompark</i></a>, which was one of the many merchant ships involved in saving over 200,000 troops and civilians in the three weeks after <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dunkirk" title="Battle of Dunkirk">Dunkirk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The ship already had industrial diamonds, machinery and a number of British evacuees aboard. SS <i>Broompark</i> delivered its passengers and cargo, together with all of the free supply of heavy water, to Falmouth on 21 June. The award of an OBE to Captain Paulsen was recorded in the London Gazette of 4 February 1941. Crucial to the success of the mission was the role played by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Howard,_20th_Earl_of_Suffolk" title="Charles Howard, 20th Earl of Suffolk">Charles Howard, 20th Earl of Suffolk</a>.</p>
<p>Although the ready inventory of heavy water was removed, the plant remained capable of producing heavy water. In investigations of collaboration launched by Norwegian authorities after the war, Norsk Hydro management's collaboration with the Germans was considered. General Director Aubert's cooperation with the French aided the Norsk Hydro case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahl1999_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dahl1999-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SNL-A_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SNL-A-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_Grouse_and_Freshman">Operations <i>Grouse</i> and <i>Freshman</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operations Grouse and Freshman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Freshman" title="Operation Freshman">Operation Freshman</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La2-demis-mosvatn.png"><img alt="Regional map" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="479" data-file-width="1043" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/La2-demis-mosvatn.png/220px-La2-demis-mosvatn.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/La2-demis-mosvatn.png/330px-La2-demis-mosvatn.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/La2-demis-mosvatn.png/440px-La2-demis-mosvatn.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La2-demis-mosvatn.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing Rjukan between lakes Møsvatn (west, upstream) and Lake Tinn (east)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Destruction of the Vemork plant was mounted by the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Operations_Headquarters" title="Combined Operations Headquarters">Combined Operations Headquarters</a> in November 1942. The plan consisted of two operations: the first would drop a number of Norwegian locals into the area as an advance force, and once they were in place a party of British engineers would be landed by <a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">military glider</a> to attack the plant itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berglyd_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berglyd-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 October 1942, a four-man team of <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">Special Operations Executive</a> (SOE)-trained <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_resistance_movement" title="Norwegian resistance movement">Norwegian commandos</a> parachuted into Norway. From their drop point in the wilderness they had to ski a long distance to the plant, so considerable time was given to complete this part of the mission, known as Operation <i>Grouse</i>. This plan, unlike prior failures, included the team's studying and memorising blueprints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the Norwegian Grouse team managed to make contact with the British, the British were suspicious, as they had not heard from the SOE team for a long time: they had been dropped at the wrong place and had gone off course from there several times. The secret question took the form of: "What did you see in the early morning of (a day)?" The Grouse team replied: "Three pink elephants." The British were ecstatic at the success of the Norwegian team's insertion, and the next phase of operations commenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berglyd_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Berglyd-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 November 1942, Operation <i>Freshman</i> followed with the planned glider-borne landing on frozen lake <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B8svatn" title="Møsvatn">Møsvatn</a> near the plant. Two <a href="/wiki/Airspeed_Horsa" title="Airspeed Horsa">Airspeed Horsa</a> gliders, towed by <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Handley Page Halifax</a> bombers, each glider carrying two pilots and 15 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Engineers" title="Royal Engineers">Royal Engineers</a> of the 9th Field Company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_British_Airborne_Division" title="1st British Airborne Division">1st British Airborne Division</a>, took off from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Skitten" title="RAF Skitten">RAF Skitten</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wick,_Highland" title="Wick, Highland">Wick</a> in <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>. The towing of gliders had always been hazardous, but in this case it was made worse by the long flying distance to Norway and poor weather conditions which severely restricted visibility. One of the Halifax tugs crashed into a mountain, killing all seven aboard; its glider was able to cast off, but crashed nearby, resulting in several casualties. The other Halifax arrived at the area of the landing zone, but although the conditions had substantially improved it was impossible to locate the landing zone itself, owing to the failure of the link between the <a href="/wiki/Rebecca/Eureka_transponding_radar" title="Rebecca/Eureka transponding radar">Eureka</a> (ground) and <a href="/wiki/Rebecca/Eureka_transponding_radar" title="Rebecca/Eureka transponding radar">Rebecca</a> (aircraft) beacons. After much endeavour and with fuel running low, the Halifax pilot decided to abort the operation and return to base. Shortly afterwards, however, the tug and glider combination encountered heavy cloud and in the resulting turbulence the tow rope broke. The glider made a crash landing, not far from where the other glider had come down, similarly inflicting several deaths and injuries. The Norwegians were unable to reach the crash sites in time, and the survivors eventually came into the hands of the <i><a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a></i>, who tortured them during interrogation (not sparing the badly injured) and later had them executed under <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Commando_Order" title="Commando Order">Commando Order</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berglyd_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Berglyd-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most important consequence of the unsuccessful raid was that the Germans were now alerted to a determined Allied interest in their heavy water production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norwegian Grouse team thereafter had a long arduous wait in their mountain hideaway, subsisting on <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> during the winter until, just before <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeer</a> was encountered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Gunnerside">Operation <i>Gunnerside</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Operation Gunnerside">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg"><img alt="Two saboteurs and a facility employee shown adjacent to the electrolysis chambers." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg/220px-NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg/330px-NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg/440px-NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NorwegianHeavyWaterSabotage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A reconstruction of the Operation <i>Gunnerside</i> team planting explosives to destroy the cascade of electrolysis chambers</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>British authorities were aware the Grouse team was still operational, and decided to mount another operation in concert with them. By this time the original Grouse team was being referred to as <b>Swallow</b>. On the night of 16 February 1943, in Operation <i>Gunnerside</i> (named after the <a href="/wiki/Gunnerside" title="Gunnerside">village</a> where SOE head <a href="/wiki/Charles_Jocelyn_Hambro" title="Charles Jocelyn Hambro">Sir Charles Hambro</a> and his family used to shoot grouse), an additional six Norwegian commandos were dropped by parachute by a <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Halifax bomber</a> of 138 Squadron from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Tempsford" title="RAF Tempsford">RAF Tempsford</a>. They were successful in landing, and encountered the Swallow team after a few days of searching on cross country skis. The combined team made final preparations for their assault, which was to take place on the night of 27/28 February 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riste_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riste-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supplies required by the commandos were dropped with them in special <a href="/wiki/Central_Landing_Establishment#Equipment" title="Central Landing Establishment">CLE containers</a>. (One of these was buried in the snow by a Norwegian patriot to hide it from the Germans; he later recovered it and in August 1976 handed it over to an officer of the British <a href="/wiki/Army_Air_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="Army Air Corps (United Kingdom)">Army Air Corps</a>, which was conducting exercises in the area. The container was brought back to England and was displayed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airborne_Museum_at_Aldershot" title="Airborne Museum at Aldershot">Airborne Museum at Aldershot</a>. The Museum closed in 2008 and is now part of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_War_Museum_Duxford" title="Imperial War Museum Duxford">Imperial War Museum Duxford</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Following the failed Freshman attempt, the Germans put mines, floodlights, and additional guards around the plant. While the mines and lights remained in place, security of the actual plant had slackened somewhat over the winter months. However, the single 75 m (246 ft 1 in) bridge spanning the deep ravine, 200 m (660 ft) above the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=River_M%C3%A5ne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="River Måne (page does not exist)">River Måne</a>, was fully guarded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The force elected to descend into the ravine, ford the icy river and climb the steep hill on the far side. The winter river level was very low, and on the far side, where the ground levelled, they followed a single railway track straight into the plant area without encountering any guards. Even before Grouse landed in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, SOE had a Norwegian agent within the plant who supplied detailed plans and schedule information. The demolition party used this information to enter the main basement by a cable tunnel and through a window. Inside the plant the only person they came across was the Norwegian caretaker (Johansen), who was very willing to cooperate with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The saboteurs then placed explosive charges on the heavy water electrolysis chambers, and attached a fuse allowing sufficient time for their escape. A <a href="/wiki/Thompson_submachine_gun" title="Thompson submachine gun">Thompson submachine gun</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> was purposely left behind to indicate that this was the work of <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> forces and not of the local resistance, in order to try to avoid reprisals. A bizarre episode ensued when fuses were about to be lit: the caretaker was worried about his spectacles which were lying somewhere in the room (during the war new glasses were nearly impossible to acquire). A frantic search for the caretaker's spectacles ensued, they were found — and the fuses lit. The explosive charges detonated, destroying the electrolysis chambers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gall_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-Gall-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The raid was considered successful. The entire inventory of heavy water produced during the German occupation, over 500 kg (1,102 lb), was destroyed along with equipment critical to operation of the electrolysis chambers. Although 3,000 German soldiers were dispatched to search the area for the commandos, all of them escaped; five of them <a href="/wiki/Ski_warfare" title="Ski warfare">skied</a> 400 kilometres to <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, two proceeded to Oslo where they assisted <a href="/wiki/Milorg" title="Milorg">Milorg</a>, and four remained in the region for further work with the resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riste_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riste-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resumed_operation_and_Allied_air_raids">Resumed operation and Allied air raids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Resumed operation and Allied air raids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although this attack did no irreparable damage to the plant, it did stop production for several months. The Vemork plant was restored by April and SOE concluded that a repeat commando raid would be extremely difficult, as German security had been considerably improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhodes_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rhodes-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Almost as soon as production restarted, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">USAAF</a> started a series of raids on Vemork. In November, the plant was attacked by a massed daylight bombing raid of 143 <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">B-17</a> <a href="/wiki/Heavy_bomber" title="Heavy bomber">heavy bombers</a> dropping 711 bombs, of which at least 600 missed the plant. The damage, however, was quite extensive.<br/>
Then, on November 16 and 18, 35 <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_B-24_Liberator" title="Consolidated B-24 Liberator">B-24</a> <a href="/wiki/Heavy_bomber" title="Heavy bomber">heavy bombers</a> from the 392nd Bomber Group of out of Wendling, Station 118, attacked the hydro-electric power station at Rjukan with excellent coverage of the target. These missions were the longest for this bomber group, lasting 9 1/2 and 10 1/2 hours respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><br/>
The need for ground assaults was reduced from a year earlier as there was now an available alternative of night bombing, which had previously been unrealistic owing to German air cover. The Germans were convinced that air raids would result in further serious "hits", and they decided to abandon the plant and move remaining stocks and critical components to Germany in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haukelid_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haukelid-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinking_the_SF_Hydro">Sinking the SF Hydro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sinking the SF Hydro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/SF_Hydro" title="SF Hydro">SF Hydro</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg"><img alt="Historic photo of the ferry preparing to cross Lake Tinn." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="640" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg/220px-DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg/330px-DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg/440px-DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DF_Hydro_at_M%C3%A6l.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/SF_Hydro" title="SF Hydro">SF <i>Hydro</i></a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A6l" title="Mæl">Mæl</a> in 1925</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Knut_Haukelid" title="Knut Haukelid">Knut Haukelid</a>, who was the only trained commando in the immediate area, was informed of the German plan to remove the heavy water and advised he would have to muster support and destroy the shipment. He recruited two people. They decided to sabotage a ferry that would be carrying the heavy water across <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tinn" title="Lake Tinn">Lake Tinn</a>. One of the people he recruited recognised a ferry crew member and talked to him, taking this advantage to slip into the bottom of the ship and plant the bomb, after which he slipped away. Eight and a half kilograms of plastic explosive with two alarm-clock fuses were fixed to the keel of the ferry, <a href="/wiki/SF_Hydro" title="SF Hydro">SF <i>Hydro</i></a>, which was to carry the railway cars with the heavy water drums across Lake Tinn. On 20 February 1944, shortly after setting off around midnight, the ferry and its cargo sank in deep water, finally capping the original mission's objective and halting Germany's atomic bomb development programme. A number of Norwegian civilians were killed as the ferry sank. Witnesses reported seeing steel drums floating after the sinking, leading to speculation that they did not really contain heavy water, but an examination of records after the war showed that some barrels were only half full, and therefore would have floated. A few of these may have been salvaged and transported to Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhodes_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rhodes-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, an expedition retrieved a barrel (numbered "26") from the bottom of the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Its contents of heavy water matched the concentration noted in the German records, and confirmed that the shipment was not a decoy. However, it also supported the notion that the concentration of heavy water in a number of the barrels was too small to be of value to a weapons program. This might explain the absence of heavy security measures around the shipment, including why the ferry itself was not searched for delayed charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGBH_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-23">[23]</a></sup> In the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heroes_of_Telemark" title="Heroes of Telemark">Heroes of Telemark</a></i>, the locomotive and train is shown, somewhat implausibly covered with German soldiers. In the Ray Mears BBC coverage, it is stated that in fact the General in command had ordered this specific disposition of troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Documentaryabout_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Documentaryabout-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unknown to the saboteurs, a "Plan B" had been established by the SOE, who arranged a second team to attack the shipment at <a href="/wiki/Her%C3%B8ya" title="Herøya">Herøya</a> should the first attempt fail. The disassembled factory was later found in southern Germany during the closing stages of the war by members of the <a href="/wiki/Alsos_Mission" title="Alsos Mission"><i>Alsos</i> Mission</a> nuclear seizure force.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_perspective">Historical perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Historical perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2769" data-file-width="1842" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG/170px-Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG/255px-Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG/340px-Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vemork_2008-02-08.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Hydroelectric Plant in 2008. The Hydrogen Production Plant building was demolished in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Recent investigation of production records at Norsk Hydro and analysis of an intact barrel that was salvaged in 2004 revealed that although the barrels in this shipment contained water of <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> 14—indicative of the alkaline electrolytic refinement process, they did not contain high concentrations of D<sub>2</sub>O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-26">[26]</a></sup> Despite the apparent size of shipment, the total quantity of pure heavy water was limited, with most barrels only containing between 1/2–1% pure heavy water, confirming the success of the Operation <i>Gunnerside</i> raid in destroying the higher purity heavy water. The Germans would have needed a total of about 5 t (5.5 short tons) of heavy water to get a nuclear reactor running; while the manifest indicated that there was only 500 kg (0.55 short tons) of heavy water being transported to Germany. Hence the <i>Hydro</i> was carrying too little heavy water to supply one reactor, let alone the 10 or more tons of heavy water needed to make enough plutonium for a nuclear weapon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the benefit of hindsight, the consensus on the German wartime nuclear program is that it was a long way from producing a bomb,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="while this is known and is referenced by many sources, this requires a RS citation (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> even if the Norwegian heavy water had been produced and shipped at the maximum rate. Nevertheless, the unsuccessful British raid (Freshman) and the feats of the Norwegian saboteurs (Swallow, Grouse, Gunnerside) made the top secret war against the heavy water production internationally known and the saboteurs national heroes.</p>

<p>As of November 2015, Joachim Rønneberg, age 96, was the last surviving member of the Gunnerside team. <i>The New York Times</i> reported that Rønneberg was "still mentally sharp at his advanced age and still possessed of the unflappable calm that so impressed British military commanders more than 70 years ago".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Published_histories">Published histories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Published histories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 1962 book by John D. Drummond, titled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=But_For_These_Men&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="But For These Men (page does not exist)">But For These Men</a></i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0705700453">ISBN 0705700453</a>), tells a true account of two dramatic raids—one on the Norsk Hydro heavy water factory at Vemork, and another on the railway ferry "Hydro" to destroy Germany's heavy water production efforts.</p>
<p>The book <i>The Real Heroes of Telemark: The True Story of the Secret Mission to Stop Hitler's Atomic Bomb</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray_Mears_(author)" title="Ray Mears (author)">Ray Mears</a>, published by Hodder &amp; Stoughton 2003 (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0340830166">ISBN 0-340-83016-6</a>) describes the events from the perspective of the unique survival skills of the Norwegian commandos. It accompanied a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television documentary series, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Real_Heroes_of_Telemark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Real Heroes of Telemark (page does not exist)">The Real Heroes of Telemark</a></i>, which sticks more to the facts than the film it is named after. It also describes the survival aspects of the attack — how to survive for months in a mountain cabin.</p>
<p>The book <i>Skis Against the Atom</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0942323076">ISBN 0-942323-07-6</a>) is a first-hand account by <a href="/wiki/Knut_Haukelid" title="Knut Haukelid">Knut Haukelid</a>, one of the <i>Gunnerside</i> raiders who stayed behind.</p>
<p>Jens-Anton Poulsson (<i>Swallow</i>/<i>Grouse</i>) has told the story in the book <i>The Heavy Water Raid: The Race for the Atom Bomb 1942–1944</i>, Orion forlag As (2009), <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9788245808698">ISBN 978-82-458-0869-8</a>.</p>
<p>The ill-fated Operation <i>Freshman</i> is covered extensively in two books: Richard Wiggan's <i>Operation Freshman: The Rjukan Heavy Water Raid 1942</i>, William Kimber &amp; Co Ltd (1986), <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780718305710">ISBN 978-0-7183-0571-0</a>, and the more recent, Jostein Berglyd's <i>Operation Freshman: The Actions and the Aftermath</i>, Leandoer &amp; Ekholm (2007), <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9789197589598">ISBN 978-91-975895-9-8</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Rhodes" title="Richard Rhodes">Richard Rhodes</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_General_Non-Fiction" title="Pulitzer Prize for General Non-Fiction">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Making_of_the_Atomic_Bomb" title="The Making of the Atomic Bomb">The Making of the Atomic Bomb</a></i> includes details on the events in chapters 14-15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leo_Marks" title="Leo Marks">Leo Marks</a>' 1998 book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Between_Silk_and_Cyanide:_A_Codemaker%27s_Story_1941-1945" title="Between Silk and Cyanide: A Codemaker's Story 1941-1945">Between Silk and Cyanide: A Codemaker's Story 1941-1945</a></i> covers the story in some detail. Marks was SOE's cryptographer. He knew the Norwegian team, trained them in cryptography so they could communicate with SOE back in England, and avidly followed their progress after they were dropped in Norway. Published by HarperCollins. <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/068486780X">ISBN 0-684-86780-X</a></p>
<p>The raid is also the subject of the book, <i>Assault in Norway: Sabotaging the Nazi Nuclear Program</i> by Thomas Gallagher, published by Lyons Press (2002), <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9781585747504">ISBN 978-1-58574-750-4</a>. This book is based on the author's interviews with many of the commandos.</p>
<p>An account of Operation Gunnerside is told in <i>The Winter Fortress: The Epic Mission to Sabotage Hitlers Atomic Bomb</i>, by Neal Bascomb, published by Houghton Mifflin (2016), <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780544368057">ISBN 978-0-544-36805-7</a>.</p>

<p>A 1948 Norwegian film based on Operations <i>Freshman</i> and <i>Grouse</i>, called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fight_Over_the_Heavy_Water" title="The Fight Over the Heavy Water">Kampen om tungtvannet</a></i>, features performances by at least four of the original participants in the raid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1965 British film based on the Operation <i>Gunnerside</i> raid, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heroes_of_Telemark" title="The Heroes of Telemark">The Heroes of Telemark</a></i>. It features a performance by one of the original participants in the raid – as the Nazi pursuer of the escapees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Documentaryabout_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Documentaryabout-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1966 book by Czech author <a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_B%C4%9Bhounek" title="František Běhounek">František Běhounek</a>, titled <i>Rokle u Rjukanu</i> (<i>Gorge at Rjukan</i>), is a fiction inspired by the events.</p>
<p>A 1979 Canadian movie and TV-series titled <i>A Man Called Intrepid</i>, based on the book of the same name by <a href="/wiki/William_Stevenson_(Canadian_writer)" title="William Stevenson (Canadian writer)">William Henry Stevenson</a>. It features <a href="/wiki/David_Niven" title="David Niven">David Niven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_York" title="Michael York">Michael York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hershey" title="Barbara Hershey">Barbara Hershey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 8, 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Corporation_for_Public_Broadcasting" title="Corporation for Public Broadcasting">Corporation for Public Broadcasting</a> – <a href="/wiki/WGBH-TV" title="WGBH-TV">WGBH</a> Educational Foundation in Boston, MA aired a program which documented the work of a team of underwater archaeologists exploring the sunken ferry, SF <i>Hydro</i> in Lake Tinn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGBH_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The games <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blazing_Angels_Squadrons_of_WWII" title="Blazing Angels Squadrons of WWII">Blazing Angels Squadrons of WWII</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Battlefield_1942:_Secret_Weapons_of_WWII" title="Battlefield 1942: Secret Weapons of WWII">Battlefield 1942: Secret Weapons of WWII</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor_(video_game)" title="Medal of Honor (video game)">Medal of Honor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Enemy_Front" title="Enemy Front">Enemy Front</a></i> each include missions involving the destruction of the heavy water plant.</p>
<p>On their 2010 album <i><a href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_(album)" title="Coat of Arms (album)">Coat of Arms</a></i>, Swedish <a href="/wiki/Power_metal" title="Power metal">power metal</a> band <a href="/wiki/Sabaton_(band)" title="Sabaton (band)">Sabaton</a> has a song named "Saboteurs", detailing the operation.</p>
<p>In 2013, for the 70th anniversary of Operation Gunnerside, BBC News interviewed Joachim Rønneberg, the leader and last surviving member of the Gunnerside team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A six-episode TV mini-series titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heavy_Water_War" title="The Heavy Water War">The Heavy Water War</a></i> (<i>The Saboteurs</i> in the UK)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> tells the story with a particular emphasis on the role of <a href="/wiki/Leif_Tronstad" title="Leif Tronstad">Leif Tronstad</a>. This Norwegian-Danish-British co-production is in 6 episodes, the first of which was initially broadcast on 4 January 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
