<p><b>Nord-Trøndelag</b> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Norwegian" title="Help:IPA for Norwegian">[ˈnuːɽ ˈtrœndəˈlɑːɡ]</a></span><small>  <span class="nowrap">(<img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Nord-Trondelag.ogg" title="Nord-Trondelag.ogg">listen</a>)</span></small> ("North Trøndelag") is a <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Norway" title="Counties of Norway">county</a> constituting the northern part of <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Trøndelag">Trøndelag</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. As of January 1, 2014, the county had 135,142 inhabitants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> making it the country's fourth-least populated county. The largest municipalities are <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdal" title="Stjørdal">Stjørdal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steinkjer" title="Steinkjer">Steinkjer</a>—the county seat, <a href="/wiki/Levanger" title="Levanger">Levanger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Namsos" title="Namsos">Namsos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verdal" title="Verdal">Verdal</a>, all with between 21,000 and 12,000 inhabitants. The economy is primarily centered on services, although there are significant industries in agriculture, fisheries, hydroelectricity and forestry. It has the lowest <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> per capita of any county in the country.</p>
<p>Nord-Trøndelag covers 22,412 square kilometres (8,653 sq mi), making it the sixth-largest county, and consists of 23 municipalities. The district of <a href="/wiki/Innherred" title="Innherred">Innherred</a> runs along the east side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trondheimsfjord" title="Trondheimsfjord">Trondheimsfjord</a>, and is the most populated area, with much farming. To the south lies the district of <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdalen" title="Stjørdalen">Stjørdalen</a>, while in the north, the larger district of <a href="/wiki/Namdalen" title="Namdalen">Namdalen</a> stretches from the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a> to the mountains bordering Sweden. West of the Trondheimsfjord lays <a href="/wiki/Fosen" title="Fosen">Fosen</a>. Nord-Trøndelag borders <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8r-Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Sør-Trøndelag">Sør-Trøndelag</a> to the south and <a href="/wiki/Nordland" title="Nordland">Nordland</a> to the north. The western part of the county has several large valleys and consists largely of unpopulated wilderness, including four <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_Norway" title="List of national parks of Norway">national parks</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sn%C3%A5savatnet" title="Snåsavatnet">Snåsavatnet</a> is the largest lake, while major rivers include <a href="/wiki/Namsen" title="Namsen">Namsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verdalselva" title="Verdalselva">Verdalselva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdalselva" title="Stjørdalselva">Stjørdalselva</a>.</p>
<p>Innherred was an important area during the <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking Age</a> and featured the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stiklestad" title="Battle of Stiklestad">Battle of Stiklestad</a>. The county was created in 1804 and was known as "Nordre Trondhjems amt" until 1919. Since the 1950s, the county has experienced a population growth below national levels. The axis north–south through the country past <a href="/wiki/Grong" title="Grong">Grong</a> and along the west side of Trondheim Fjord is a main transport artery, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_route_E06" title="European route E06">European Route E6</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nordland_Line" title="Nordland Line">Nordland Line</a>.</p>
<p>The regions was divided into two administrative counties in 1804. In 2016, the two county councils voted to merge into a single county in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1045" data-file-width="1000" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg/220px-Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg/330px-Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg/440px-Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_county_map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Most of the county is lower than 600 metres (2,000 ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">amsl</a>, much of it covered by spruce forest. The mountains are mostly in the eastern, inland part.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nord-Trøndelag borders <a href="/wiki/Nordland" title="Nordland">Nordland</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8r-Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Sør-Trøndelag">Sør-Trøndelag</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to the east and the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a> to the west. The county seat is <a href="/wiki/Steinkjer" title="Steinkjer">Steinkjer</a>, with 20,527 inhabitants (2005). The largest lake is <a href="/wiki/Sn%C3%A5savatnet" title="Snåsavatnet">Snåsavatnet</a> and the largest river is <a href="/wiki/Namsen" title="Namsen">Namsen</a>, one of the best <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> rivers in Europe. Other well known salmon rivers are as <a href="/wiki/Verdalselva" title="Verdalselva">Verdalselva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdalselva" title="Stjørdalselva">Stjørdalselva</a>. <a href="/wiki/Salsvatnet" title="Salsvatnet">Salsvatnet</a> is the second-deepest lake in Europe, with a maximum depth of 482 m. Another lake in the area is <a href="/wiki/Byavatnet" title="Byavatnet">Byavatnet</a>. Stjørdal is the biggest town in the county. There are local hospitals in <a href="/wiki/Levanger" title="Levanger">Levanger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namsos" title="Namsos">Namsos</a>.</p>
<p>A large part of the population lives near the large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trondheimsfjord" title="Trondheimsfjord">Trondheimsfjord</a>, which is a central feature of the southern part of this county. In the north are other fjords, mainly the <a href="/wiki/Namsenfjorden" title="Namsenfjorden">Namsenfjord</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folda,_Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Folda, Nord-Trøndelag">Foldafjord</a>. Areas on the eastern and northeastern shore of Trondheimsfjord (mainly in <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdal" title="Stjørdal">Stjørdal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frosta" title="Frosta">Frosta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Levanger" title="Levanger">Levanger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inder%C3%B8y" title="Inderøy">Inderøy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verdal" title="Verdal">Verdal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steinkjer" title="Steinkjer">Steinkjer</a>) are fertile agricultural lowland, with <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a> fields and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>. Together with the grain fields in the <a href="/wiki/Namdalen" title="Namdalen">Namdalen</a> lowland, this forms the most northern grain cultivation area in Norway today.</p>

<p>However, the spruce dominated forest (some birch) covers a much larger area, and Nord-Trøndelag is the second largest timber producing county in Norway (after <a href="/wiki/Hedmark" title="Hedmark">Hedmark</a>). The forest and highland in Nord-Trøndelag is one of few places in Norway with four species of <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> (moose, roe deer, red deer and reindeer). There are mountains near the border with Sweden, and coastal mountains with bare rock at the northern coast. The spruce forests occurs even at the coast, where some areas are classified as <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate rainforest</a> (boreal rainforest, see <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_coastal_conifer_forests" title="Scandinavian coastal conifer forests">Scandinavian coastal conifer forests</a>). There are several national parks in the county, among them <a href="/wiki/Bl%C3%A5fjella%E2%80%93Skj%C3%A6kerfjella_National_Park" title="Blåfjella–Skjækerfjella National Park">Blåfjella–Skjækerfjella National Park</a> (one of the largest in Norway), <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B8rgefjell_National_Park" title="Børgefjell National Park">Børgefjell National Park</a> (partly), <a href="/wiki/Lierne_National_Park" title="Lierne National Park">Lierne National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skarvan_and_Roltdalen_National_Park" title="Skarvan and Roltdalen National Park">Skarvan and Roltdalen National Park</a> (partly).</p>

<p>The first people in Nord-Trøndelag settled in Flatanger and Leka between 7500 and 6000 BCE, and were migrating northwards along the coast. In about the same time, people moved upwards along the Trondheimsfjord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The first farmers migrated to Stjørdal before 2000 BCE in the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a43_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-a43-4">[4]</a></sup> Early agriculture was based mostly on animals, which allowed people to remain nomads and combine stockbreeding with gathering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a45_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-a45-5">[5]</a></sup> Around 2300 to 2000 BCE, the spruce spread into the county, and by 1300 CE, the landscape was dominated by spruce like today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_46_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_46-6">[6]</a></sup> During the early <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, from 1800 to 1000 BCE, the first large graves were built in the Trondheimsfjord area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a43_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-a43-4">[4]</a></sup> The earliest species of cereals grown was <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> around 500 BCE, which was later supplemented with other cereals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a45_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-a45-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first century CE, iron mining in swamps started in the easternmost parts of the country. Several small communities with <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> were established, located several days walk from the good agricultural land, generating trade and occupational specialization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_46_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_46-6">[6]</a></sup> However, the mining industry stopped in the fifth century. In the following centuries, as part of the increased immigration due to the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a>, then a Germanic judicial system was introduced, which was a further development of the system launched during the mining era. In the fifth century, the first organizing of military took place, with constructions of small forts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Around this time, the area was split into counties, with the current Nord-Trøndleag consisting of parts of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stj%C3%B8rd%C3%B8lafylke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stjørdølafylke (page does not exist)">Stjørdølafylke</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sk%C3%B8ynafylke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Skøynafylke (page does not exist)">Skøynafylke</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%98ynafylke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Øynafylke (page does not exist)">Øynafylke</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Verd%C3%B8lafylke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Verdølafylke (page does not exist)">Verdølafylke</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sparbyggjafylke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sparbyggjafylke (page does not exist)">Sparbyggjafylke</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Naumd%C3%B8lafylke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Naumdølafylke (page does not exist)">Naumdølafylke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_64_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_64-8">[8]</a></sup> From the tenth century, the <a href="/wiki/Frostating" title="Frostating">Frostating</a> was established as a <a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">thing</a> for all of the Trondheimsfjord area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heathen_hofs" title="Heathen hofs">hof</a> for worshiping <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nordic_mythology" title="Nordic mythology">Nordic mythology</a> was at <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A6re" title="Mære">Mære</a> and was a common site for <a href="/wiki/Animal_sacrifice" title="Animal sacrifice">animal sacrifice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 997, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olaf_I_of_Norway" title="Olaf I of Norway">Olaf Tryggvason</a> established <a href="/wiki/Nidaros" title="Nidaros">Nidaros</a> (current-day <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>), in Sør-Trøndelag, started a series of attacks to conquer Innherred. To counteract, the Innherred warlords established a marketplace in Steinkjer. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stiklestad" title="Battle of Stiklestad">Battle of Stiklestad</a>, which took place in 1030, King <a href="/wiki/Olaf_II_of_Norway" title="Olaf II of Norway">Olaf Haraldsson</a> was killed by a peasant army in a battle for supremacy over Trøndelag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_64_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_64-8">[8]</a></sup> In the following decades, despite the defeat of the Christian Olaf, Trøndelag was gradually Christianized, resulting in the construction of churches<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and two monasteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By the 13th century, the Frostating laws had been codified, by which time all of Central Norway was under the thing, which developed into a court, which was moved to Nidaros in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking Age</a>, the population increased, reaching 20,000 by the mid-13th century. The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> killed off many people, and Nordli and Meråker were depopulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami people</a> immigrated from the north in the following two centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Archdiocese_of_Nidaros" title="Archdiocese of Nidaros">Archbishop of Nidaros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olav_Engelbrektsson" title="Olav Engelbrektsson">Olav Engelbrektsson</a>, started building a fortress in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steinvikholmen" title="Steinvikholmen">Steinvikholmen</a> in Stjørdal, which was completed in 1532, and became the center of the archdiocese. It remained until the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> in 1537, in which the king took over all church assets in the country, which consisted of nearly 40% of the land. Subsequently, the number of self-owning farmers increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The 16th century also saw the beginning of export of fish and lumber, and the first sawmills were established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a70_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-a70-17">[17]</a></sup> In the 17th century, administration gradually shifted from that of warlords to <a href="/wiki/Vogt" title="Vogt">vogt</a>, a representative of the king in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>. Taxes were increased to finance the military, and in Nord-Trøndelag, farmers had to join the military to fight Sweden in <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4mtland" title="Jämtland">Jämtland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a> and schools were introduced, and the potato was introduced during the 1770s. Parts of Namdalen was the target of immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Norway" title="Eastern Norway">Eastern Norway</a>, who cleared new land. In 1804, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trondhjems_amt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trondhjems amt (page does not exist)">Trondhjems amt</a> was split into two, with the northern part becoming Nordre Trondhjems amt. In 1919, it changed its name to Nord-Trøndelag. In 1838, the municipalities were created, and Nordre Trondhjems amt received 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a76_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-a76-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1836, Levanger received status as a market-town, followed by Namsos in 1845 and Steinkjer in 1857.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a74_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-a74-20">[20]</a></sup> The number would gradually increase to 48.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1851, <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Thrane" title="Marcus Thrane">Marcus Thrane</a>'s public meetings caused riots demanding increased labor rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a76_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-a76-19">[19]</a></sup> The first steam-powered sawmill was established at <a href="/wiki/Spillum" title="Spillum">Spillum</a> in 1853, and forestry started becoming a profession, rather than a part-time work during the winter. From the late 18th century, sail ships started running in regular traffic along the coast and in the fjord to Trondheim. Most natural resources were owned by the burgoise in Trondheim, as royal concession was needed for any exploitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a70_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-a70-17">[17]</a></sup> During the last half of the 19th century, tens of thousands of people emigrated to North America, with some communities losing just under half their population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a74_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-a74-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">Industrialization</a> started in the 1838 with <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamships</a> in regular traffic along the coast, and later on Trondheimsfjord and Snåsavatnet. At the end of the 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Transformation_(Norway)" title="The Great Transformation (Norway)">Great Transformation</a> took place, whereby the economy changed from being predominantly based on self-production to a professional trades. <a href="/wiki/Croft_(land)" title="Croft (land)">Crofts</a> were abolished. The <a href="/wiki/Mer%C3%A5ker_Line" title="Meråker Line">Meråker Line</a> reached <a href="/wiki/Hell,_Norway" title="Hell, Norway">Hell</a> in 1881, and in 1905 the <a href="/wiki/Hell%E2%80%93Sunnan_Line" title="Hell–Sunnan Line">Hell–Sunnan Line</a> reached <a href="/wiki/Sunnan" title="Sunnan">Sunnan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a77_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-a77-22">[22]</a></sup> The first flight took off from Værnes in 1914, and in 1948 scheduled services were started to <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of 2010, Nord-Trøndelag had 131,555 inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> There were 55,910 households, or 2.3 people per household.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">Life expectancy</a> is 77.7 years for males and 82.4 years for females, both above the national average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> There were 5,942 foreigners, or 4.5%, including Norwegian-born to immigrant parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssbimmigration_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssbimmigration-30">[30]</a></sup> There were 4,699 people with foreign citizenship, the lowest for any county both in relative and absolute numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The largest sources of immigration are Eastern Europe and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssbimmigration_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ssbimmigration-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christianity is the dominant religion. As of 2010, 5,061 (3.8%) people were not registered as members of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Norway" title="Church of Norway">Church of Norway</a>. Of these, 2,581 (1.9%) were members of other Christian groups, while 1,762 (1.3%) were irreligious, 508 (0.3%) were Muslims, 158 were Buddhists, and 52 belonged to other religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndersk" title="Trøndersk">Trøndersk</a> is a dialect of <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> which with minor modifications is spoken throughout Trøndelag and <a href="/wiki/Nordm%C3%B8re" title="Nordmøre">Nordmøre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hjs399_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-hjs399-33">[33]</a></sup> It is characterized by the use of <a href="/wiki/Apocope" title="Apocope">apocope</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hjs399_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-hjs399-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">palatalization</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_flap" title="Retroflex flap">retroflex flaps</a> (thick "L").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hjs399_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-hjs399-33">[33]</a></sup> The Trøndersk spoken in Nord-Trøndelag is broader and closer to <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> than what is spoken in Trondheim, with the broadest language being spoken in Innherred. The towns in Nord-Trøndelag have been more influenced by written Norwegian and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_%C3%98stnorsk" title="Standard Østnorsk">Standard Østnorsk</a> and have a less broad dialect. Compared to Sør-Trøndelag, there is a tendency of <i>utjamning</i> rather than <i>tiljamning</i>. The use of <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative case</a> is gradually disappearing. About 300 Sami people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> which are concentrated around Snåsa, speak the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Sami_language" title="Southern Sami language">Southern Sami language</a>. It is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" title="Mutually intelligible">mutually intelligible</a> with other Sami languages, and belongs to <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic</a>, a different <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a> than Norwegian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_County_Municipality" title="Nord-Trøndelag County Municipality">Nord-Trøndelag County Municipality</a> runs 3,000 kilometers (1,900 mi) of county roads,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roads_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-roads-37">[37]</a></sup> public transport,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> eleven upper secondary schools with 7,000 pupils,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> regional development and other minor issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The county municipality is led by a 35-member <a href="/wiki/County_council_(Norway)" title="County council (Norway)">county council</a>, which in the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_local_elections,_2007" title="Norwegian local elections, 2007">2007 election</a> has consisted of members of seven parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The administration is led by a <a href="/wiki/Subnational_parliamentary_system_in_Norway" title="Subnational parliamentary system in Norway">county cabinet</a> with six members, representing the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Norway)" title="Labour Party (Norway)">Labour Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(Norway)" title="Conservative Party (Norway)">Conservative Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Norway)" title="Liberal Party (Norway)">Liberal Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Party_(Norway)" title="Christian Democratic Party (Norway)">Christian Democratic Party</a>. The cabinet is led by <a href="/wiki/Alf_Daniel_Moen" title="Alf Daniel Moen">Alf Daniel Moen</a> of the Labour Party,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Gunnar_Viken" title="Gunnar Viken">Gunnar Viken</a> of the Conservative Party is county mayor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/County_governor_(Norway)" title="County governor (Norway)">Nord-Trøndelag County Governor</a> is the state's representative in the county. Since 2009, the governor has been <a href="/wiki/Inge_Ryan" title="Inge Ryan">Inge Ryan</a>, a former parliamentarian for the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Left_Party_(Norway)" title="Socialist Left Party (Norway)">Socialist Left Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The county is covered by three <a href="/wiki/District_court_(Norway)" title="District court (Norway)">district courts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inder%C3%B8y_District_Court" title="Inderøy District Court">Inderøy District Court</a>, located in Steinkjer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8r-_and_Verdal_District_Court" title="Stjør- and Verdal District Court">Stjør- and Verdal District Court</a> in Levanger,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Namdal_District_Court" title="Namdal District Court">Namdal District Court</a> in Namsos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> All are subordinate <a href="/wiki/Frostating_Court_of_Appeal" title="Frostating Court of Appeal">Frostating Court of Appeal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The county is covered by Nord-Trøndelag Police District.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nord-Trøndelag also constitutes an <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district" title="Electoral district">electoral district</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Norway" title="Parliament of Norway">Parliament of Norway</a>, consisting of six representatives. In the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_parliamentary_election,_2009" title="Norwegian parliamentary election, 2009">2009 election</a>, the Labour Party received three representatives, <a href="/wiki/Gerd_Janne_Kristoffersen" title="Gerd Janne Kristoffersen">Gerd Janne Kristoffersen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arild_Stokkan-Grande" title="Arild Stokkan-Grande">Arild Stokkan-Grande</a> and <a href="/wiki/Susanne_Bratli" title="Susanne Bratli">Susanne Bratli</a>, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Eriksson" title="Robert Eriksson">Robert Eriksson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Progress_Party_(Norway)" title="Progress Party (Norway)">Progress Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lars_Peder_Brekk" title="Lars Peder Brekk">Lars Peder Brekk</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Norway)" title="Centre Party (Norway)">Centre Party</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lars_Myraune" title="Lars Myraune">Lars Myraune</a> of the Conservative Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Since 2008, Brekk has been <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Agriculture_and_Food_(Norway)" title="Ministry of Agriculture and Food (Norway)">Minister of Agriculture and Food</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In 2007, Nord-Trøndelag had a gross domestic product (GDP) per capita NOK 242,895 and an income per capita of NOK 165,075, less than any other Norwegian county. For GDP per capita, Nord-Trøndelag lay at 67% of the national average, excluding the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_continental_shelf" title="Norwegian continental shelf">continental shelf</a>, and lay just above a third of <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agriculture is most common east of the Trondheimsfjord, the lower parts of Indre Namdal and Nærøy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_137_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_137-53">[53]</a></sup> 876 square kilometers (338 sq mi) of land is used for agriculture, of which 310 square kilometers (120 sq mi) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereals" title="Cereals">cereals</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Statistics_Norway_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Statistics_Norway-54">[54]</a></sup> which is more dominant along the Trondheimsfjord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a139_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-a139-55">[55]</a></sup> The county has ten percent of the country's agricultural output, and no other county has so high a percentage of its production from farming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_131_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_131-56">[56]</a></sup> Farms traditionally have a square lot of buildings, with the house, called a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tr%C3%B8nderl%C3%A5n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trønderlån (page does not exist)">trønderlån</a>, being thin and long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a139_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-a139-55">[55]</a></sup> Fishing is an important industry along the coast, particularly in Ytre Namdal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_101_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_101-57">[57]</a></sup> and Trondheim Fjord is the fjord with the highest yield in Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_102_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_102-58">[58]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">Fish farming</a>, particularly of <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, has seen a rapid growth since the 1970s. Most of the fish is exported to Continental Europe, and to a less degree the Far East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aune_1995_108_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aune_1995_108-59">[59]</a></sup></p>

<p>Forty percent of the county is covered by <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, but about half of it is not profitable to log.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Parts of the forests are preserved, including the Coastal Spruce Forest, which is the only place in Europe where <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> grows out to the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Up to 800,000 cubic meters (28,000,000 cu ft) of lumber is harvested each year, of which 95% is spruce and 3% is <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Statskog" title="Statskog">Statskog</a>, a government agency, owns 7,330 square kilometres (2,830 sq mi) of Nord-Trøndelag, of which1,068 square kilometers (412 sq mi) is productive forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Large private forest owners include <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A6rdalsbruket" title="Værdalsbruket">Værdalsbruket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meraker_Brug" title="Meraker Brug">Meraker Brug</a>, while municipalities own 178 square kilometers (69 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Norske_Skog_Skogn" title="Norske Skog Skogn">Norske Skog Skogn</a>, established in 1962 and located at <a href="/wiki/Skogn" title="Skogn">Skogn</a>, is one of the largest companies in the county, and among Europe's largest producers of <a href="/wiki/Newsprint" title="Newsprint">newsprint</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%B6dra_Cell_Folla" title="Södra Cell Folla">Södra Cell Folla</a> in <a href="/wiki/Follafoss" title="Follafoss">Follafoss</a> is a producer of <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)" title="Pulp (paper)">pulp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</a> production in Nord-Trøndelag is for 2.9 TWh per year, all of which is owned by <a href="/wiki/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_Elektrisitetsverk" title="Nord-Trøndelag Elektrisitetsverk">Nord-Trøndelag Elektrisitetsverk</a> (NTE). Owned by the county municipality and established in 1919,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> it also operates two <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vikna_Wind_Farm" title="Vikna Wind Farm">Vikna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hundhammerfjellet_Wind_Farm" title="Hundhammerfjellet Wind Farm">Hundhammerfjellet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aker_Verdal" title="Aker Verdal">Aker Verdal</a> manufacturers jackets for <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil platforms</a>; established in 1976, it is among the largest employers in the county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>

<p>The county has four <a href="/wiki/Folk_high_school" title="Folk high school">folk high schools</a>: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sund_Folk_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sund Folk High School (page does not exist)">Sund</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Namdal_Folk_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Namdal Folk High School (page does not exist)">Namdal</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Skogn_Folk_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Skogn Folk High School (page does not exist)">Skogn</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bakketun_Folk_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bakketun Folk High School (page does not exist)">Bakketun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The state-owned <a href="/wiki/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_University_College" title="Nord-Trøndelag University College">Nord-Trøndelag University College</a> has campuses in Levanger, Steinkjer, Stjørdal and Namsos, and provides undergraduate education to 4,460 pupils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Central_Norway_Regional_Health_Authority" title="Central Norway Regional Health Authority">Central Norway Regional Health Authority</a> is based in Stjørdal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_Hospital_Trust" title="Nord-Trøndelag Hospital Trust">Nord-Trøndelag Hospital Trust</a> operates two hospitals, <a href="/wiki/Levanger_Hospital" title="Levanger Hospital">Levanger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namsos_Hospital" title="Namsos Hospital">Namsos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>The traditional cuisine consisted of five meals per day, and contained <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porridge" title="Porridge">porridge</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dairy_products" title="Dairy products">dairy products</a> and flat bread, with the potato coming into use during the 19th century. Herring and potato became the standard meal for commoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Local specialties include <a href="/wiki/Ginger_ale" title="Ginger ale">ginger ale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akvavit" title="Akvavit">akvavit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodd" title="Sodd">sodd</a>, while it was common to use <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%A6vfisk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grævfisk (page does not exist)">grævfisk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rakfisk" title="Rakfisk">rakfisk</a> (raw rotten fish) in the mountainous areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag_Teater&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nord-Trøndelag Teater (page does not exist)">Nord-Trøndelag Teater</a>, located in Verdalsøra, is the only all-year professional theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Since 1954, <a href="/wiki/The_Saint_Olav_Drama" title="The Saint Olav Drama">The Saint Olav Drama</a> has been performed at <a href="/wiki/Stiklestad" title="Stiklestad">Stiklestad</a>, portraying the battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Similarly, an outdoor midnight opera is held on Steinvikholmen portraying the historical events there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Amateur <a href="/wiki/Revue" title="Revue">revue</a> is popular, and the Norwegian Revue Festival is held in Høylandet every other year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Three bands <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ge_Aleksandersen" title="Åge Aleksandersen">Åge Aleksandersen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Rotmo" title="Hans Rotmo">Hans Rotmo</a> and <a href="/wiki/D.D.E._(band)" title="D.D.E. (band)">DDE</a> are the most successful music artists, having created the genre trønderrock. The former two had their breakthrough in the 1970s, while the latter had it in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Levanger_HK&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Levanger HK (page does not exist)">Levanger HK</a> plays in Premier Women's Handball League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saemien_Sijte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saemien Sijte (page does not exist)">Saemien Sijte</a>, located in Snåsa, is a center for <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami culture</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8nder-Avisa" title="Trønder-Avisa">Trønder-Avisa</a></i>, published in Steinkjer, is the only county-wide <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a>, although the Trondheim-based <i><a href="/wiki/Adresseavisen" title="Adresseavisen">Adresseavisen</a></i> also covers the county. <i><a href="/wiki/Namdalsavisa" title="Namdalsavisa">Namdalsavisa</a></i>, published in Namsos, covers Namdalen. Local newspapers, most of which cover a single municipality, are <i><a href="/wiki/Frostingen" title="Frostingen">Frostingen</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Inder%C3%B8yningen" title="Inderøyningen">Inderøyningen</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Innherreds_Folkeblad_og_Verdalingen" title="Innherreds Folkeblad og Verdalingen">Innherreds Folkeblad og Verdalingen</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Levanger-Avisa" title="Levanger-Avisa">Levanger-Avisa</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lokalavisa_Verran_Namdalseid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lokalavisa Verran Namdalseid (page does not exist)">Lokalavisa Verran Namdalseid</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mer%C3%A5kerposten&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meråkerposten (page does not exist)">Meråkerposten</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sn%C3%A5sningen" title="Snåsningen">Snåsningen</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Steinkjer-Avisa" title="Steinkjer-Avisa">Steinkjer-Avisa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdalens_Blad" title="Stjørdalens Blad">Stjørdalens Blad</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ytringen" title="Ytringen">Ytringen</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Norwegian Broadcasting Corporation">Norwegian Broadcasting Corporation</a> has offices in Steinkjer and runs radio programs exclusively for the county.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nord-Tr%C3%B8ndelag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_route_E06" title="European route E06">European Route E6</a> runs north–south throughout eastern part the county, partially as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorway" title="Motorway">motorway</a>. This route runs from Stjørdal via Steinkjer and Grong to Nordland. Other important routes in the county include <a href="/wiki/European_route_E14" title="European route E14">E14</a> between Stjørdal via Meråker to Sweden, and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_County_Road_17" title="Norwegian County Road 17">County Road 17</a> from Steinkjer via Namsos and Nærøy to Nordland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roads_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-roads-37">[37]</a></sup> Both passenger and car ferries operate on the coast, and in Trondheimsfjord is the <a href="/wiki/Levanger%E2%80%93Hokstad_Ferry" title="Levanger–Hokstad Ferry">Levanger–Hokstad Ferry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a186_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-a186-83">[83]</a></sup> Private road transport is dominant, as <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> is sparsely operated. The largest bus company is <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8nderBilene" title="TrønderBilene">TrønderBilene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a189_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-a189-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nordland_Line" title="Nordland Line">Nordland Line</a> is a railway that runs from Trondheim to <a href="/wiki/Bod%C3%B8" title="Bodø">Bodø</a>, and it runs north–south through the county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a77_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-a77-22">[22]</a></sup> South of Steinkjer, the <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag_Commuter_Rail" title="Trøndelag Commuter Rail">Trøndelag Commuter Rail</a> operates to Trondheim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a189_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-a189-84">[84]</a></sup> There are also two other lines; the <a href="/wiki/Mer%C3%A5ker_Line" title="Meråker Line">Meråker Line</a>, part of the line between Trondheim and <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> runs from Stjørdal to Meråker and onwards to Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a77_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-a77-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Namsos_Line" title="Namsos Line">Namsos Line</a> is purely used for freight and goes from Grong to Namsos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> All the railways are unelectrified. <a href="/wiki/Trondheim_Airport,_V%C3%A6rnes" title="Trondheim Airport, Værnes">Trondheim Airport, Værnes</a>, Norway's third-largest <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Stj%C3%B8rdal" title="Stjørdal">Stjørdal</a>, and serves most major airports in Norway, as well as European destinations. There are two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_airport" title="Regional airport">regional airports</a>, <a href="/wiki/Namsos_Airport,_H%C3%B8knes%C3%B8ra" title="Namsos Airport, Høknesøra">Namsos Airport, Høknesøra</a> and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B8rvik_Airport,_Ryum" title="Rørvik Airport, Ryum">Rørvik Airport, Ryum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a186_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-a186-83">[83]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hurtigruten" title="Hurtigruten">Coastal Express</a> ferry service calls at <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B8rvik" title="Rørvik">Rørvik</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
