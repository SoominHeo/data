<p><b>Norilsk</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Норильск</span>; <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Russian" title="Help:IPA for Russian">[nɐˈrʲilʲsk]</a></span>) is an industrial <a href="/wiki/Types_of_inhabited_localities_in_Russia" title="Types of inhabited localities in Russia">city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Krasnoyarsk_Krai" title="Krasnoyarsk Krai">Krasnoyarsk Krai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, located above the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>, east of the <a href="/wiki/Yenisei_River" title="Yenisei River">Yenisei River</a> and south of the western <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a>. It has a permanent population of 175,000. With temporary inhabitants included, its population reaches 220,000.</p>
<p>Norilsk was <a href="/wiki/Closed_city" title="Closed city">closed</a> in November 2001 to all non-Russians, except for Belarusians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is the world's northernmost city with more than <a href="/wiki/List_of_towns_and_cities_with_100,000_or_more_inhabitants" title="List of towns and cities with 100,000 or more inhabitants">100,000 inhabitants</a> and the second largest city (after <a href="/wiki/Murmansk" title="Murmansk">Murmansk</a>) inside the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>. Norilsk, <a href="/wiki/Yakutsk" title="Yakutsk">Yakutsk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vorkuta" title="Vorkuta">Vorkuta</a> are the only large cities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_permafrost" title="Continuous permafrost">continuous permafrost</a> zone.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="540" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg/220px-Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg/330px-Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg/440px-Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
False-color satellite image of Norilsk and the surrounding area (<a href="/wiki/File:Norilsk_L7_20010809.jpg" title="File:Norilsk L7 20010809.jpg">more information</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Norilsk was founded at the end of the 1920s, but the official date of founding is traditionally 1935, when Norilsk was expanded as a settlement for the Norilsk mining-metallurgic complex and became the center of the <a href="/wiki/Norillag" title="Norillag">Norillag</a> system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GULAG" title="GULAG">GULAG</a> <a href="/wiki/Labor_camp" title="Labor camp">labor camps</a>. It was granted <a href="/wiki/Urban-type_settlement" title="Urban-type settlement">urban-type settlement</a> status in 1939 and town status in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norilsk is located between the <a href="/wiki/West_Siberian_Plain" title="West Siberian Plain">West Siberian Plain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Siberian_Plateau" title="Central Siberian Plateau">Central Siberian Plateau</a> at the foot of the 1,700-meter (5,600 ft) high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Putoran_Mountains" title="Putoran Mountains">Putoran Mountains</a>, on some of the largest <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> deposits on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Consequently, mining and <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> ore are the major industries. Norilsk is the center of a region where nickel, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> are mined. Mineral deposits in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_Craton" title="Siberian Craton">Siberian Craton</a> had been known for two centuries before Norilsk was founded, but mining began only in 1939, when the buried portions of the Norilsk-Talnakh intrusions were found beneath mountainous terrain.</p>
<p>Talnakh is the major mine/enrichment site now from where an enriched ore emulsion is pumped to Norilsk metallurgy plants.</p>
<p>To support the new city, a <a href="/wiki/Norilsk_railway" title="Norilsk railway">railway</a> to the port of <a href="/wiki/Dudinka" title="Dudinka">Dudinka</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Yenisei_River" title="Yenisei River">Yenisei River</a> was established, first as a narrow-gauge line (winter 1935–36), later as <a href="/wiki/5_ft_and_1520_mm_gauge_railways" title="5 ft and 1520 mm gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">1,520 mm</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">11<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>27</sup>⁄<sub>32</sub></span> in</span>) Russian standard gauge line (completed in the early 1950s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rail_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-rail-10">[10]</a></sup> From Dudinka, enriched nickel and copper are transported to <a href="/wiki/Murmansk" title="Murmansk">Murmansk</a> by sea and, then, to the <a href="/wiki/Monchegorsk" title="Monchegorsk">Monchegorsk</a> enrichment and smelting plant on the <a href="/wiki/Kola_Peninsula" title="Kola Peninsula">Kola Peninsula</a>, while more precious content goes up the river to <a href="/wiki/Krasnoyarsk" title="Krasnoyarsk">Krasnoyarsk</a>. This transportation only takes place during the summer. The port of Dudinka is closed and dismantled during spring's ice barrier floods of up to 20 meters (66 ft) in late May (a typical spring occurrence on all Siberian rivers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Salekhard%E2%80%93Igarka_Railway" title="Salekhard–Igarka Railway">another railway</a> was under construction from the European coal city <a href="/wiki/Vorkuta" title="Vorkuta">Vorkuta</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Salekhard" title="Salekhard">Salekhard</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ob_River" title="Ob River">Ob River</a>, and Norilsk got a spacious railway station built in the expectation of train service to Moscow,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rail_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-rail-10">[10]</a></sup> but construction stopped after <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> died.</p>
<p>According to the archives of Norillag, 16,806 prisoners died in Norilsk under the conditions of forced labor, starvation and intense cold during the existence of the camp (1935–1956).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Fatalities were especially high during the war years of 1942–1944 when food supplies were particularly scarce. Prisoners organised the nonviolent <a href="/wiki/Norilsk_uprising" title="Norilsk uprising">Norilsk uprising</a> in 1953. Unknown but significant numbers of prisoners continued to serve and die in the mines until around 1979. Norilsk-Talknakh continues to be a dangerous mine to work in: according to the mining company, there were 2.4 accidents per thousand workers in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Since the early 2000s the city has been rebuilding itself and reshaping its image. Bars on the top floors of apartments are appearing and buildings are getting renovated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_and_municipal_status">Administrative and municipal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Administrative and municipal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Russia#Administrative_divisions" title="Subdivisions of Russia">framework of administrative divisions</a>, it is, together with the <a href="/wiki/Urban-type_settlement" title="Urban-type settlement">urban-type settlement</a> of <a href="/wiki/Snezhnogorsk,_Krasnoyarsk_Krai" title="Snezhnogorsk, Krasnoyarsk Krai">Snezhnogorsk</a>, incorporated as the <b><a href="/wiki/City_of_federal_subject_significance" title="City of federal subject significance">krai city</a> of Norilsk</b>—an administrative unit with the status equal to that of the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Krasnoyarsk_Krai" title="Administrative divisions of Krasnoyarsk Krai">districts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref66_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ref66-1">[1]</a></sup> As a <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Russia#Municipal_divisions" title="Subdivisions of Russia">municipal division</a>, the krai city of Norilsk is incorporated as <b>Norilsk Urban Okrug</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref763_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ref763-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a mosque in Norilsk. Built in 1998 belonging to the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tatar" title="Tatar">Tatar</a> community, it is considered to be the northernmost Muslim prayer house in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> There is also a Russian Orthodox cathedral, several Russian Orthodox churches and a <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Orthodox_Church_(Moscow_Patriarchate)" title="Ukrainian Orthodox Church (Moscow Patriarchate)">Ukrainian Orthodox church</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="nowrap">175,365 (<a href="/wiki/Russian_Census_(2010)" title="Russian Census (2010)">2010 Census</a>)</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010Census_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-2010Census-3">[3]</a></sup>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010CensusNote_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010CensusNote-15">[15]</a></sup> after the fall of the USSR, its population went down by 40,000, but this was offset by the subsequent merger of the towns of <a href="/wiki/Kayerkan" title="Kayerkan">Kayerkan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Talnakh" title="Talnakh">Talnakh</a> into Norilsk, maintaining a permanent population of 175,000. Including temporary residents, the population reaches 220,000 people. Around 80% of the permanent population are thought to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russified" title="Russified">Russified</a> <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainians</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography.2C_climate.2C_and_natural_environment">Geography, climate, and natural environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography, climate, and natural environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Norilsk is the world's northernmost city with more than <a href="/wiki/List_of_towns_and_cities_with_100,000_or_more_inhabitants" title="List of towns and cities with 100,000 or more inhabitants">100,000 inhabitants</a> and the second largest city (after <a href="/wiki/Murmansk" title="Murmansk">Murmansk</a>) inside the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>. Norilsk, <a href="/wiki/Yakutsk" title="Yakutsk">Yakutsk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vorkuta" title="Vorkuta">Vorkuta</a> are the only large cities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_permafrost" title="Continuous permafrost">continuous permafrost</a> zone. It lies between <a href="/wiki/Taymyrsky_Dolgano-Nenetsky_District" title="Taymyrsky Dolgano-Nenetsky District">Taymyrsky Dolgano-Nenetsky District</a> to the north, and <a href="/wiki/Turukhansky_District" title="Turukhansky District">Turukhansky District</a> to the south. Norilsk has an extremely harsh <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">subarctic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a> <i>Dfc</i>), and is covered with snow for about 250–270 days a year, with snow storms for about 110–130 days. The "<a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">midnight sun</a>" is above the horizon from 21 May to 24 July, and the period when the sun does not rise, <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">polar night</a>, is from approximately 30 November to 13 January. Temperatures can sometimes rise above 25 °C (77 °F) in July.</p>
<p>Much of the surrounding areas are naturally treeless <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. Only a few trees exist in Norilsk.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nickel ore is smelted on site at Norilsk. The smelting is directly responsible for severe pollution, generally <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> and smog. By some estimates, 1 percent of global emissions of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> comes from Norilsk's nickel mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">Heavy metal</a> pollution near Norilsk is so severe that mining the surface soil is now economically feasible due to the soil acquiring such high concentrations of platinum and palladium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blacksmith_Institute" title="Blacksmith Institute">Blacksmith Institute</a> included Norilsk in its 2007 list of the ten most polluted places on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-black-18">[18]</a></sup> The list cites air pollution by particulates (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a>-90, and <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a>-137 and metals nickel, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>) and by gases (such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a>, sulfur dioxide, <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>). The Institute estimates four million tons of cadmium, copper, lead, nickel, arsenic, selenium and zinc are released into the air every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_State_Statistics_Service" title="Russian Federal State Statistics Service">Russian Federal State Statistics Service</a> named Norilsk the most polluted city in Russia. In 2010, Norilsk produced 1.924 million tons of carbon pollutants, nearly six times more than the 333 thousand tons generated by Russia's second most polluted city, <a href="/wiki/Cherepovets" title="Cherepovets">Cherepovets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to an April 2007 BBC News report,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Norilsk Nickel accepted responsibility for what had happened to the forests, and insisted they were taking action to cut the pollution. For the period up to 2015–2020 the company expects to reduce sulfur dioxide emissions by approximately two-thirds, but claims it is hard to guarantee this pace of reduction because they are still developing the technology.</p>
<p>In September 2016, images surfaced on social media of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Daldykan" title="Daldykan">Daldykan</a> River which had been turned red. Russia's Environment Ministry issued a statement claiming that preliminary evidence pointed towards wastewater pipes from a nearby smelting plant owned by Norilsk Nickel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norilsk-Talnakh_nickel_deposits">Norilsk-Talnakh nickel deposits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Norilsk-Talnakh nickel deposits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore,_Norilsk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1611" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore%2C_Norilsk.jpg/350px-Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore%2C_Norilsk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore%2C_Norilsk.jpg/525px-Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore%2C_Norilsk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore%2C_Norilsk.jpg/700px-Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore%2C_Norilsk.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sperrylite_in_rich_platinum-copper_ore,_Norilsk.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rich platinum-copper ore, Oktyabersky Mine, Norilsk. Click image for details.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> deposits of Norilsk-<a href="/wiki/Talnakh" title="Talnakh">Talnakh</a> are the largest known nickel-copper-palladium deposits in the world. The deposit was formed 250 million years ago during the eruption of the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Traps" title="Siberian Traps">Siberian Traps</a> igneous province (STIP). The STIP erupted over one million cubic kilometers of lava, a large portion of it through a series of flat-lying lava conduits below Norilsk and the Talnakh Mountains.</p>
<p>The ore was formed when the erupting magma became saturated in <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, forming globules of <a href="/wiki/Pentlandite" title="Pentlandite">pentlandite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalcopyrite" title="Chalcopyrite">chalcopyrite</a>, and other sulfides. These sulfides were then "washed" by the continuing torrent of erupting magma, and upgraded their tenor with nickel, copper, platinum and palladium<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>The current resource known for these mineralized intrusion exceeds 1.8 billion tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMC_Norilsk_Nickel" title="MMC Norilsk Nickel">MMC Norilsk Nickel</a>, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, is the principal mining operator in Norilsk-Talnakh. The ore is mined underground via several shafts, and a decline. The ore deposits are currently being extracted at more than 1,200 m (3,900 ft) below ground and drilled from the surface. Nickel production for 2008 amounted to 299.7 thousand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_tonne" title="Metric tonne">metric tonnes</a>. Copper production for 2008 amounted to 419 thousand metric tonnes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The deposits are being explored by a Russian government-controlled company. The company is known to be using <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic field</a> <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, with loops on surface which are over 1,000 m (3,300 ft) on a side. They are<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>conclusively able to image the conductive nickel ore at depths in excess of 1,800 m (5,900 ft).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG/220px-%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG/330px-%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG/440px-%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%82_%D0%9D%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B0.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMC_Norilsk_Nickel" title="MMC Norilsk Nickel">MMC Norilsk Nickel</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> company, is the principal employer in the Norilsk area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-black-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city is served by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norilsk_Airport" title="Norilsk Airport">Norilsk Alykel Airport</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norilsk_Valek_Airfield" title="Norilsk Valek Airfield">Norilsk Valek Airfield</a>. There is a freight-only railway, the <a href="/wiki/Norilsk_railway" title="Norilsk railway">Norilsk railway</a> between Norilsk and the port of Dudinka. There is a road network around Norilsk (such as the <a href="/wiki/Russian_federal_highways" title="Russian federal highways">A-382</a> which links to Dudinka and Norilsk Alykel Airport), but no road or railway to the main cities of Russia. Freight transport is by boat on the Arctic Ocean or on the <a href="/wiki/Yenisei_River" title="Yenisei River">Yenisei River</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norilsk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among its cultural highlights, Norilsk boasts a history museum and an art gallery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the Norilsk Polar Drama Theater,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> a zoo,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> a cultural center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> a sports and entertainment complex and arena,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> many monuments and historical buildings<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and other attractions.</p>
