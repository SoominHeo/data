<p><b>Nomophobia</b> is a proposed name for the <a href="/wiki/Phobia" title="Phobia">phobia</a> of being out of mobile phone contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbs2008-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is, however, arguable that the word "<a href="/wiki/Phobia" title="Phobia">phobia</a>" is misused and that in the majority of cases it is another form of <a href="/wiki/Anxiety_disorder" title="Anxiety disorder">anxiety disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Although nomophobia does not appear in the current <a href="/wiki/DSM-5" title="DSM-5"><i>Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders</i>, Fifth Edition</a> (DSM-5), it has been proposed as a "specific phobia", based on definitions given in the DSM-IV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proposal1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-proposal1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Nomophobia#Dubious" title="Talk:Nomophobia">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> According to Bianchi and Philips (2005) psychological factors are involved in the overuse of a mobile phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bianchi2005_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bianchi2005-5">[5]</a></sup> These could include low self-esteem, when individuals looking for reassurance use the mobile phone in inappropriate ways, and extroverted personality, when naturally social individuals use the mobile phone to excess. It is also highly possible that nomophobic symptoms may be caused by other underlying and preexisting mental disorders, with likely candidates including social phobia or social anxiety disorder, social anxiety,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup> and panic disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingValencaNardi_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-KingValencaNardi-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term, an abbreviation for "no-mobile-phone phobia",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> was coined during a 2010 study by the UK Post Office who commissioned <a href="/wiki/YouGov" title="YouGov">YouGov</a>, a UK-based research organization to look at anxieties suffered by mobile phone users. The study found that nearly 53% of mobile phone users in Britain tend to be anxious when they "lose their mobile phone, run out of battery or credit, or have no network coverage". The study found that about 58% of men and 47% of women suffer from the phobia, and an additional 9% feel stressed when their mobile phones are off. The study sampled 2,163 people. Fifty-five percent of those surveyed cited keeping in touch with friends or family as the main reason that they got anxious when they could not use their mobile phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbs2008-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The study compared stress levels induced by the average case of nomophobia to be on-par with those of "wedding day jitters" and trips to the dentist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Another study found that out of 547 male, undergraduate students in Health Services 23% of the students were classified as nomophobic while an additional 64% were at risk of developing nomophobia. Of these students, ~77% checked their mobile phones 35 or more times a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than one in two nomophobes never switch off their mobile phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The study and subsequent coverage of the phobia resulted in two editorial columns authored by individuals who minimized their mobile phone use or chose not to own one at all, treating the condition with light undertones of or outright disbelief and amusement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_evidence">Research evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nomophobia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Research evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the changes of technologies, new challenges are coming up on a daily basis. New kinds of phobias have emerged, the so-called techno-phobias. Since the first mobile phone in the market in 1983, it has become one of the mainstreams in the majority of societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Shambare, Rugimbana &amp; Zhowa (2012) claimed that cell phones are "possibly the biggest non-drug addiction of the 21st century," and that colleges students may spend up to nine hours every day on their phones that can lead to dependence on such technologies as a driver of modern life and an example of "a paradox of technology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> that is both freeing and enslaving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A survey conducted by SecurEnvoy showed that youngsters and adolescents are more likely to suffer from nomophobia. The same survey reported that 77% of the teens had anxiety and worries in the case of being without their mobile phones, followed by the 25-34 age group and people over 55 years old. Some psychological predictors to look for in a person who might be suffering of this phobia are: "self negative views, younger age, low esteem and self-efficacy, high extroversion or introversion, impulsiveness and sense of urgency and sensation seeking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bianchi2005_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bianchi2005-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among students, frequent cell phone usage has been correlated with decreases in grade point average (GPA) and increased anxiety that negatively impacts self-reported life satisfaction (well-being and happiness) in comparison to students with less frequent usage. GPA decreases may be due to the over-use of cell phone or computer usage consuming time and focus during studying, attending class, working on assignments, and the distraction of cell phones during class. Over-usage of cell phones may increase anxiety due to the pressure to be continually connected to social networks and could rob chances of perceived solitude, relieving daily stress, that has been linked as a component of well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_experiments">Other experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nomophobia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Australia 946 adolescents and emerging adults between ages 15 and 24 participated in a mobile phone research study; 387 males, 557 females, and two chose not to report a gender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">[19]</a></sup> The study focused on the relationship between the participants frequency of mobile phone use and the participants mobile phone psychological involvement. Researchers assessed several psychological factors that might influence participants' mobile phone use with the following questionnaires: Mobile Phone Involvement Questionnaire (MPIQ), Frequency of Mobile Phone Use, Self Identity, and Validation from others. The MPIQ assessed behavioral addictions using a seven-point Likert scale (<i>1 - strongly agree</i>) and (<i>7-strongly disagree</i>) that included statements such as: "I often think about my mobile phone when I am not using it. ... I feel connected to others when I use my mobile phone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">[19]</a></sup> The results demonstrated moderate difference between the participants' mobile phone use and their psychological relationships with the mobile phones. No pathological conditions were found, but there was an excessive use of mobile phone indicating signs of attachment. Participants who demonstrated signs of excessive mobile phone use were more likely to increase their use when receiving validation from others. Other factors considered, the population studied was focused on adolescents and emerging adults are more likely to develop mobile phone dependency because they may be going through a self-identity, self-esteem, and social identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suffers of with panic disorders and anxiety disorders are prone to mobile phone dependency. A study in Brazil compared the symptoms experienced due to mobile phone use by heterosexual participants with panic disorders and a control group of healthy participants. Group 1 consisted of 50 participants with panic disorder and agoraphobia with an average age of 43 and group 2 consisted of 70 healthy participants with no disorders and an average age of 35. During the experiment participants were given a self-report mobile phone questionnaire which assessed the mobile phone use and symptoms reported by both groups. Interestingly, 44% of group 1 reported that they felt "secure" when they had their mobile phones versus 46% of group 2 reported they would not feel the same without their mobile phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-20">[20]</a></sup> The results demonstrated that 68% of all participants reported mobile phone dependency, but overall the participants with panic disorder and agoraphobia reported significantly more emotional symptoms and dependency on mobile phones when compared to the control group when access to the mobile phone was prohibited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms_and_signs">Symptoms and signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nomophobia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symptoms and signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nomophobia occurs in situations when an individual experiences anxiety due to the fear of not having access to a mobile phone. Anxiety is provoked by several reasons, such as the loss of a mobile phone, loss of reception, and a dead mobile phone battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proposal1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-proposal1-4">[4]</a></sup> Some clinical characteristics of nomophobia include using the device impulsively, or as protection, sometimes from social communication, or as a transitional object, having one or more devices with access to internet, always carrying a charger, and experiencing feelings of anxiety when thinking about losing the mobile. The "over-connection syndrome" occurs when mobile phone use reduces the amount of face-to-face interactions thereby interfering significantly with an individual’s social and family interactions. The term "<a href="/wiki/Technostress" title="Technostress">techno-stress</a>" is another way to describe an individual who avoids face-to-face interactions by engaging in isolation including psychological mood disorders such as depression. Other clinical characteristics of nomophobia are a considerably decreased number of face-to-face interactions with humans, and a growing preference for communication through technologies; keeping the device in reach when sleeping and never turned off; looking at the phone screen frequently to avoid missing any message, phone call, or notification, also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ringxiety" title="Ringxiety">ringxiety</a>; nomophobia can also lead to an increase of debt due to the excessive use of data and the different devices the person can have.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proposal1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-proposal1-4">[4]</a></sup> Signs of mobile phone dependency include obsessively checking a mobile phone and using a mobile phone to avoid feelings of discomfort, anguish, or stress.</p>
<p>Irrational reactions and extreme reactions due to anxiety and stress may be experienced by the individual in public settings where mobile phone use is restricted such as airports, academic institutions, hospitals and work. Overusing a mobile phone for day-to-day activities such as purchasing items causing the individual financial problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proposal1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-proposal1-4">[4]</a></sup> Signs of distress and depression occur when the individual does not receive any contact through a mobile phone. Attachment signs of a mobile phone include the urge to sleep with a mobile phone. The ability to communicate through a mobile phone gives the individual peace of mind and security.</p>
<p>Nomophobia may act as a proxy to other disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proposal1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-proposal1-4">[4]</a></sup> Those suffering from an underlying social disorder are likely to experience nervousness, anxiety, anguish, perspiration, and trembling when separated or unable to use (low battery, out of service area, no connection, etc.) from their digital devices—mobile phone, tablet, personal computer, or other digital and virtual communication devices. These modern devices may make those suffering from nomophobia or related disorder feel safer, more confident, and less anxious. Such people will often insist on keeping their devices on hand at all times, typically returning to their homes to retrieve forgotten cell phones. Nomophobic behavior may reinforce social anxiety tendencies and dependency on using virtual and digital communications as a method of reducing stress generated by social anxiety and social phobia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup> Those suffering from panic disorders may also show nomophobic behavior, however, will probably report feelings of rejection, loneliness, insecurity, and low self-esteem in regard to their cell phones, especially when times with little to no contact (few incoming calls and messages). Those with panic disorder will probably feel significantly more anxious and depressed with their cellphone use. Despite this, those suffering from panic disorder were significantly less likely to place voice calls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatments">Treatments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nomophobia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Treatments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, scholarly accepted and empirically proven treatments are very limited due to its relatively new concept. However, promising treatments include cognitive-behavioral psychotherapy and combined with pharmacological interventions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proposal1_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-proposal1-4">[4]</a></sup> Treatments using tranylcypromine and clonazepam were successful in reducing the effects of nomophobia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingValencaNardi_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-KingValencaNardi-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cognitive behavioral therapy seems to be effective by reinforcing autonomous behavior independent from technological influences, however, this form of treatment lacks randomized trails. Another possible treatment is "Reality Approach," or Reality therapy asking patient to focus behaviors away from cell phones.<sup>[14]</sup> In extreme or severe cases, neuropsychopharmacology may be advantageous, ranging from benzodiazepines to antidepressants in usual doses.<sup>[14]</sup> Patients were also successfully treated using tranylcypromine combined with clonazepam. However, it is important to note that these medications were designed to treat social anxiety disorder and not nomophobia directly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup> It may be rather difficult to treat nomophobia directly, but more plausible to investigate, identify, and treat any underlying mental disorders if any exist.</p>
<p>Even though nomophobia is a fairly new concept, there are validated psychometric scales available to help in the diagnostic, an example of one of these scales is the "Questionnaire of Dependence of Mobile Phone/Test of Mobile Phone Dependence (QDMP/TMPD)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
