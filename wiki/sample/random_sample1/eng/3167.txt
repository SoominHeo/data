<p><b>Novarupta</b> (meaning "newly erupted"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPSDeriv_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPSDeriv-2">[2]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> that was created in 1912, located on the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Peninsula" title="Alaska Peninsula">Alaska Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Katmai_National_Park_and_Preserve" title="Katmai National Park and Preserve">Katmai National Park and Preserve</a>, about 290 miles (470 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage</a>. Formed during the largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century, Novarupta released 30 times the volume of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eruption_of_1912">Eruption of 1912</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Novarupta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Eruption of 1912">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The eruption of Novarupta within the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Range" title="Aleutian Range">Aleutian Range</a> began on June 6, 1912, and culminated in a series of violent eruptions from the original Novarupta volcano. Rated a 6 on the <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">Volcanic Explosivity Index</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simkin_siebert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-simkin_siebert-3">[3]</a></sup> the 60-hour-long eruption expelled 13 to 15 cubic kilometers (3.1 to 3.6 cu mi) of ash, 30 times as much as the <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brantley1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brantley1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fierstein1998_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fierstein1998-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fierstein1992_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fierstein1992-6">[6]</a></sup> The erupted magma of <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolitic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dacite" title="Dacite">dacitic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesitic</a> composition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VolcNAmer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-VolcNAmer-7">[7]</a></sup> resulted in more than 17 cubic kilometers (4.1 cu mi) of <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_fall" title="Pyroclastic fall">air fall</a> <a href="/wiki/Tuff" title="Tuff">tuff</a> and approximately 11 cubic kilometers (2.6 cu mi) of <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">pyroclastic ash-flow</a> tuff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fierstein2001_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fierstein2001-8">[8]</a></sup> During the 20th century, only the 1991 eruption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Pinatubo" title="Mt. Pinatubo">Mt. Pinatubo</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> was of a similar magnitude; Pinatubo ejected 11 cubic kilometers (2.6 cu mi) of <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">tephra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At least two larger eruptions occurred in the 19th century: the <a href="/wiki/1815_eruption_of_Mount_Tambora" title="1815 eruption of Mount Tambora">1815 eruption</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">Tambora</a> (150 km<sup>3</sup> (36.0 cu mi) of tephra)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Tambora_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Tambora-10">[10]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/1883_eruption_of_Krakatoa" title="1883 eruption of Krakatoa">1883 eruption</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Krakatoa" title="Krakatoa">Krakatoa</a> (20 km<sup>3</sup> (4.8 cu mi) of tephra).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Novarupta occurred about 2.5 miles from the peak of volcanic <a href="/wiki/Mount_Katmai" title="Mount Katmai">Mount Katmai</a> and 4,000 feet below the post-eruption summit of Mount Katmai. During the eruption a large quantity of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> was removed from underneath the Mount Katmai area, resulting in the formation of a 2-kilometer (1.2 mi) wide, funnel-shaped vent and the collapse of Mount Katmai's summit, creating a 600-meter (2,000 ft) deep,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brantley1999_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brantley1999-4">[4]</a></sup> 3 by 4 km (1.9 by 2.5 mi) <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Katmai_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Katmai-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eruption ended with the extrusion of a <a href="/wiki/Lava_dome" title="Lava dome">lava dome</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolite</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VolcNAmer_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-VolcNAmer-7">[7]</a></sup> that <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_plug" title="Volcanic plug">plugged</a> the vent. The 295-foot (90 m) high and 1,180-foot (360 m) wide dome and the caldera it created form what is now referred to as Novarupta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosi2003_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosi2003-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the magnitude of the eruption, no deaths directly resulted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griggs1922_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griggs1922-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alaska_edu_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-alaska_edu-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eruption is said to have had an effect on the level of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes">Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Novarupta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes" title="Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes">Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg/220px-Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg/330px-Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg/440px-Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colorful_ash_Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Colorful ash in the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">Pyroclastic</a> ash flow from the eruption formed the <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_Ten_Thousand_Smokes" title="Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes">Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes</a>, named by botanist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fiske_Griggs" title="Robert Fiske Griggs">Robert F. Griggs</a>, who explored the volcano's aftermath for the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griggs1922_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Griggs1922-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clemens1999_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clemens1999-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eruption forming the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes is one of the few in recorded history to have produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welded_tuff#Welded_tuff" title="Welded tuff">welded tuff</a>, producing numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumaroles" title="Fumaroles">fumaroles</a> that persisted for 15 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Katmai_National_Park">Katmai National Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Novarupta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Katmai National Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Katmai_National_Park_and_Preserve" title="Katmai National Park and Preserve">Katmai National Park and Preserve</a></div>
<p>Established as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_areas_in_the_United_States_National_Park_System" title="List of areas in the United States National Park System">National Park &amp; Preserve</a> in 1980, Katmai is located on the Alaska Peninsula, across from Kodiak Island, with headquarters in nearby <a href="/wiki/King_Salmon,_Alaska" title="King Salmon, Alaska">King Salmon</a>, about 290 mi (470 km) southwest of Anchorage. The area was originally designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._National_Monument" title="U.S. National Monument">National Monument</a> in 1918 to protect the area around the 1912 eruption of Novarupta and the 40-square-mile (104 km<sup>2</sup>), 100-to-700-foot (30 to 210 m) deep, pyroclastic flow of the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KNPP_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-KNPP-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
