<p><b>New Siberia</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Но́вая Сиби́рь</span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transliteration_of_Russian_into_English" title="Transliteration of Russian into English">English transliteration</a>: <i>Novaya Sibir</i>) is the easternmost of the <a href="/wiki/Anzhu_Islands" title="Anzhu Islands">Anzhu Islands</a>, the northern subgroup of the <a href="/wiki/New_Siberian_Islands" title="New Siberian Islands">New Siberian Islands</a> lying between the <a href="/wiki/Laptev_Sea" title="Laptev Sea">Laptev Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Siberian_Sea" title="East Siberian Sea">East Siberian Sea</a>. Its area of approximately 6,200 square kilometres (2,394 square miles) places it just outside the 100 <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">largest islands in the world</a>. New Siberia Island is low lying, rising to only 76 metres (249 feet) and covered with <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> vegetation. The island is a part of the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Sakha_Republic" title="Sakha Republic">Sakha (Yakutia) Republic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</p>
<p>The fact that this island is called "New Siberia" and is located in the "New Siberian Islands" could cause some confusion.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Siberia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New Siberia Island consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clastic" title="Clastic">clastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> ranging from Late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> in age. The Late Cretaceous sediments consist of extensively folded layers of gray and greenish gray tuffaceous <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, tuffaceous <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>, pebbly sand, and layers of brown <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> exposed in sea cliffs along it southwest coast. The sand and silt often contain either <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_glass" title="Volcanic glass">volcanic glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> plants, <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolite</a> <a href="/wiki/Pebble" title="Pebble">pebbles</a>, or some combination of them. <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> sand, silt, <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, and brown coal overlies an erosional <a href="/wiki/Unconformity" title="Unconformity">unconformity</a> cut into the Late Cretaceous sediments. Within the northwest part of New Siberia Island, these sediments grade into clays that contain fragments of <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalves" title="Bivalves">bivalves</a>. Directly overlying the Eocene sediments and another erosional unconformity are sands of <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> and Early <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> age. They contain thin beds of silt, mud, clay, and pebbles. These sands contain fossil plants and lagoonal, swamp, and lacustrine <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>. These sands are overlain by <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> sediments consisting of layers of sand, silt, mud, <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, and pebbles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujita1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fujita1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kosko2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-kosko2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Except for the Derevyannye Hills, <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> sediments blanket almost the entire surface of New Siberia Island. These deposits consist of layers of <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> sediments overlain by terrestrial sediments. The lower marine sediments are composed of three superimposed beds of marine to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brackish" title="Brackish">brackish</a> water clay containing fossil <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a> and capped with peat. The overlying terrestrial sediments consist of an ice complex composed of ice-rich wind-blown silt in which ice wedges have developed. This ice complex accumulated over tens of thousands of years during the Late Pleistocene, through the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, until it stopped at about 10,000 BP. During this period of tens of thousands of years, the formation of ice complex buried and preserved in permafrost an enormous number of <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a> <a href="/wiki/Tusk" title="Tusk">tusks</a> and bones and the bones of other “<a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafauna</a>”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kosko2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-kosko2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basilyan1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basilyan1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorofeevothers1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dorofeevothers1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>New Siberia Island is noted for abundant upright tree trunks, logs, leaf prints, and other plant debris that occur within sediments that are exposed along sea cliffs and within the uplands of the Derevyannye Hills along its southern coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Armstrong1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonToll1890_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-vonToll1890-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of the abundance of exposed coalified logs and upright trunks, early explorers and paleobotanists referred to the Derevyannye Hills as either the "Wood Mountains", "Wood Hills", or "Tree Mountain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Armstrong1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kropotkin1900_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kropotkin1900-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TollKropotkin1898_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TollKropotkin1898-9">[9]</a></sup> At one time, the highly folded layers of sand, silt, mud, clay, and brown coal containing these coalified tree fossils were once thought to have accumulated during either the Miocene or Eocene Epoch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonToll1890_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-vonToll1890-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kropotkin1900_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kropotkin1900-8">[8]</a></sup> These sediments and the fossil trunks and logs, which they contain, are now known to date to the Late Cretaceous Period (<a href="/wiki/Turonian" title="Turonian">Turonian</a> Stage).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klubovothers1976_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klubovothers1976-10">[10]</a></sup> Baron Von Toll,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonToll1890_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-vonToll1890-7">[7]</a></sup> Dr. Klubov and others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klubovothers1976_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klubovothers1976-10">[10]</a></sup> Dr. Dorofeev and others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorofeevothers1999_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dorofeevothers1999-4">[4]</a></sup> and other publications all demonstrate that the claims by some authors, i.e. Mr. Southall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southall_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Southall-11">[11]</a></sup> that the "Wood Hills" of New Siberia Island are either partially or completely "formed of driftwood" are completely erroneous.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation">Vegetation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Siberia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vegetation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rush/grass, forb, <a href="/wiki/Cryptogam" title="Cryptogam">cryptogam</a> tundra covers the New Siberia Island. It is tundra consisting mostly of very low-growing grasses, rushes, forbs, mosses, lichens, and liverworts. These plants either mostly or completely cover the surface of the ground. The soils are typically moist, fine-grained, and often hummocky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAM1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAM1-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Siberia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yakov_Sannikov" title="Yakov Sannikov">Yakov Sannikov</a> was the first recorded European to set foot on New Siberia Island, in 1806. He discovered it during one of several hunting expeditions financed by merchants, Semyon and Lev Syrovatsky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mills1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
