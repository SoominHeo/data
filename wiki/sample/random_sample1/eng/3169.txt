<p>The <b>Nobel Prize in Physics</b> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv"><i>Nobelpriset i fysik</i></span>) is a yearly award given by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a> for those who conferred the most outstanding contributions for mankind in the field of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>. It is one of the five <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prizes</a> established by the will of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Nobel" title="Alfred Nobel">Alfred Nobel</a> in 1895 and awarded since 1901; the others being the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a>.</p>
<p>The first Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to German physicist <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">Wilhelm Röntgen</a> in recognition of the extraordinary services he has rendered by the discovery of the remarkable rays (or <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a>). This award is administered by the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Foundation" title="Nobel Foundation">Nobel Foundation</a> and widely regarded as the most prestigious award that a scientist can receive in physics. It is presented in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> at an annual ceremony on December 10, the anniversary of Nobel's death. Through 2016, a total of 203 individuals have been awarded the prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelOrg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelOrg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only two women have won the Nobel prize in physics, Maria Goeppert Mayer in 1963 and Marie Curie in 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Nobel" title="Alfred Nobel">Alfred Nobel</a>, in his last will and testament, stated that his wealth be used to create a series of prizes for those who confer the "greatest benefit on mankind" in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, peace, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCHistory_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCHistory-4">[4]</a></sup> Though Nobel wrote several wills during his lifetime, the last one was written a year before he died and was signed at the Swedish-Norwegian Club in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> on 27 November 1895.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Nobel bequeathed 94% of his total assets, 31 million <a href="/wiki/Swedish_krona" title="Swedish krona">Swedish <i>kronor</i></a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">US</a>$198 million, <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a>€176 million in 2016), to establish and endow the five Nobel Prizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Due to the level of skepticism surrounding the will, it was not until April 26, 1897 that it was approved by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Norway" title="Parliament of Norway">Storting</a> (Norwegian Parliament).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinovitz13_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levinovitz13-9">[9]</a></sup> The executors of his will were <a href="/wiki/Ragnar_Sohlman" title="Ragnar Sohlman">Ragnar Sohlman</a> and Rudolf Lilljequist, who formed the Nobel Foundation to take care of Nobel's fortune and organise the prizes.</p>
<p>The members of the Norwegian Nobel Committee who were to award the Peace Prize were appointed shortly after the will was approved. The prize-awarding organisations followed: the Karolinska Institutet on June 7, the Swedish Academy on June 9, and the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences on June 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Nobel Foundation then reached an agreement on guidelines for how the Nobel Prize should be awarded. In 1900, the Nobel Foundation's newly created statutes were promulgated by King <a href="/wiki/Oscar_II_of_Sweden" title="Oscar II of Sweden">Oscar II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinovitz13_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levinovitz13-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BritannicaWill_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaWill-12">[12]</a></sup> According to Nobel's will, The Royal Swedish Academy of sciences were to award the Prize in Physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BritannicaWill_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaWill-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomination_and_selection">Nomination and selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomination and selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_(1879-1955),_Physicist.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3995" data-file-width="5231" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_%281879-1955%29%2C_Physicist.jpg/250px-Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_%281879-1955%29%2C_Physicist.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_%281879-1955%29%2C_Physicist.jpg/375px-Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_%281879-1955%29%2C_Physicist.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_%281879-1955%29%2C_Physicist.jpg/500px-Portrait_of_Albert_Einstein_and_Others_%281879-1955%29%2C_Physicist.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>A maximum of three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_laureate" title="Nobel laureate">Nobel laureates</a> and two different works may be selected for the Nobel Prize in Physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Compared with other Nobel Prizes, the nomination and selection process for the prize in Physics is long and rigorous. This is a key reason why it has grown in importance over the years to become the most important prize in Physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nobel laureates are selected by the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Committee_for_Physics" title="Nobel Committee for Physics">Nobel Committee for Physics</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Committee" title="Nobel Committee">Nobel Committee</a> that consists of five members elected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="The Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">The Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>. In the first stage that begins in September, around 3,000 people – selected university professors, Nobel Laureates in Physics and Chemistry, etc – are sent confidential forms to nominate candidates. The completed nomination forms arrive at the Nobel Committee no later than 31 January of the following year. These nominees are scrutinized and discussed by experts who narrow it to approximately fifteen names. The committee submits a report with recommendations on the final candidates into the Academy, where, in the Physics Class, it is further discussed. The Academy then makes the final selection of the Laureates in Physics through a majority vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nomination_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nomination-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The names of the nominees are never publicly announced, and neither are they told that they have been considered for the prize. Nomination records are sealed for fifty years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fiftyyearsrule_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fiftyyearsrule-17">[17]</a></sup> While posthumous nominations are not permitted, awards can be made if the individual died in the months between the decision of the prize committee (typically in October) and the ceremony in December. Prior to 1974, posthumous awards were permitted if the recipient had died after being nominated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rules for the Nobel Prize in Physics require that the significance of achievements being recognized has been "tested by time". In practice, it means that the lag between the discovery and the award is typically on the order of 20 years and can be much longer. For example, half of the 1983 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Subrahmanyan_Chandrasekhar" title="Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar">Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar</a> for his work on stellar structure and evolution that was done during the 1930s. As a downside of this approach, not all scientists live long enough for their work to be recognized. Some important scientific discoveries are never considered for a prize, as the discoverers die by the time the impact of their work is appreciated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prizes">Prizes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prizes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Physics Nobel Prize laureate earns a gold medal, a diploma bearing a citation, and a sum of money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The amount of money awarded depends on the income of the Nobel Foundation that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize2-22">[22]</a></sup> If a prize is awarded to more than one laureate, the money is either split evenly among them or, for three laureates, it may be divided into a half and two quarters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BritannicaPrizes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-BritannicaPrizes-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medals">Medals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nobel Prize medals, minted by <a href="/wiki/Myntverket" title="Myntverket">Myntverket</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in Sweden and the Mint of Norway since 1902, are registered trademarks of the Nobel Foundation. Each medal has an image of Alfred Nobel in left profile on the obverse. The Nobel Prize medals for Physics, Chemistry, Physiology or Medicine, and Literature have identical obverses, showing the image of Alfred Nobel and the years of his birth and death (1833–1896). Nobel's portrait also appears on the obverse of the Nobel Peace Prize medal and the Medal for the Prize in Economics, but with a slightly different design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PaulingPeacePrize_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-PaulingPeacePrize-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thenobelmedals_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-thenobelmedals-26">[26]</a></sup> The image on the reverse of a medal varies according to the institution awarding the prize. The reverse sides of the Nobel Prize medals for Chemistry and Physics share the same design of Nature, as a Goddess, whose veil is held up by the Genius of Science. These medals and the ones for Physiology/Medicine and Literature were designed by Erik Lindberg in 1902.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_Prize_for_Physics_and_Chemistry_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_Prize_for_Physics_and_Chemistry-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomas">Diplomas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diplomas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nobel laureates receive a diploma directly from the hands of the King of Sweden. Each diploma is uniquely designed by the prize-awarding institutions for the laureate that receives it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize5_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize5-28">[28]</a></sup> The diploma contains a picture and text which states the name of the laureate and normally a citation of why they received the prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize5_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize5-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Award_money">Award money</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Award money">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The laureate is also given a sum of money when they receive the Nobel Prize in the form of a document confirming the amount awarded; in 2009, the monetary award was 10 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Krona" title="Swedish Krona">SEK</a> (US$1.4 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize2_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize2-22">[22]</a></sup> Due to budget cuts, in 2012, the amount for each Nobel prize was 8 million SEK, or US$1.1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The amount may differ depending on how much money the Nobel Foundation can award that year. If there are two laureates in a particular category, the award grant is divided equally between the recipients. If there are three, the awarding committee has the option of dividing the grant equally, or awarding one-half to one recipient and one-quarter to each of the others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceremony">Ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobel_Prize_in_Physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The committee and institution serving as the selection board for the prize typically announce the names of the laureates in October. The prize is then awarded at formal ceremonies held annually in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Concert_Hall" title="Stockholm Concert Hall">Stockholm Concert Hall</a> on 10 December, the anniversary of Nobel's death. The laureates receive a diploma, a medal and a document confirming the prize amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awardceremony_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-awardceremony-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-BritannicaWill-12"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-BritannicaWill_12-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-BritannicaWill_12-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/416856/Nobel-Prize" rel="nofollow">"Nobel Prize</a>" (2007), in <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>, accessed 15 January 2009, from <i>Encyclopædia Britannica Online</i>:</span>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><span class="reference-text">After Nobel’s death, the Nobel Foundation was set up to carry out the provisions of his will and to administer his funds. In his will, he had stipulated that four different institutions—three Swedish and one Norwegian—should award the prizes. From Stockholm, the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences confers the prizes for physics, chemistry, and economics, the Karolinska Institute confers the prize for physiology or medicine, and the Swedish Academy confers the prize for literature. The Norwegian Nobel Committee based in Oslo confers the prize for peace. The Nobel Foundation is the legal owner and functional administrator of the funds and serves as the joint administrative body of the prize-awarding institutions, but it is not concerned with the prize deliberations or decisions, which rest exclusively with the four institutions.</span></p>
</blockquote>
