<p>The <b>Kingdom of Northumbria</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">Old English</a>: <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang"><i>Norþhymbra rīce</i></span>, "kingdom of the Northumbrians") was a medieval <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Anglian</a> kingdom in what is now <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">northern England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lothian" title="Lothian">south-east Scotland</a>, which subsequently became an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earldom" title="Earldom">earldom</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">unified English kingdom</a>. The name reflects the approximate southern limit to the kingdom's territory, the <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber estuary</a>.</p>
<p>Northumbria was formed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%86thelfrith_of_Northumbria" title="Æthelfrith of Northumbria">Æthelfrith</a> in central Great Britain in Anglo-Saxon times. At the beginning of the 7th century, the two kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deira" title="Deira">Deira</a> were unified. (In the 12th century writings of <a href="/wiki/Henry_of_Huntingdon" title="Henry of Huntingdon">Henry of Huntingdon</a>, the kingdom was defined as one of the <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">Heptarchy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> kingdoms). At its height, the kingdom extended at least from just south of the <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber</a> to the <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">River Mersey</a> and to the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Forth</a> (roughly, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> to <a href="/wiki/Runcorn" title="Runcorn">Runcorn</a> to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>)—and there is some evidence that it may have been much greater (see map).</p>
<p>The later (and smaller) earldom came about when the southern part of Northumbria (ex-Deira) was lost to the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a>. The northern part (ex-Bernicia) at first retained its status as a kingdom but, when it became subordinate to the Danish kingdom, it had its powers curtailed to that of an earldom and retained that status when England was reunited by the <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>-led reconquest of the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a>. The earldom was bounded by the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">River Tees</a> in the south and the <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">River Tweed</a> in the north (broadly similar to the modern <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>).</p>
<p>Much of this land was "debated" between England and Scotland, but the Earldom of Northumbria was eventually recognised as part of England by the Anglo-Scottish <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_York" title="Treaty of York">Treaty of York</a> in 1237. On the northern border, <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>, which is north of the Tweed but had changed hands many times, was defined as subject to the laws of England by the <a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Berwick_Act_1746" title="Wales and Berwick Act 1746">Wales and Berwick Act 1746</a>. The land once part of Northumbria at its peak is now divided by modern administrative boundaries:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_and_the_Humber" title="Yorkshire and the Humber">Yorkshire and the Humber</a>, including <a href="/wiki/North_Lincolnshire" title="North Lincolnshire">North Lincolnshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_East_Lincolnshire" title="North East Lincolnshire">North East Lincolnshire</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, though Cumbria was more of a Northumbrian colony with its own client kings for most of its history in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Medieval era</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Lothian" title="West Lothian">West Lothian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midlothian" title="Midlothian">Midlothian</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Lothian" title="East Lothian">East Lothian</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Northumbria is also used in the names of some regional institutions, particularly the police force (<a href="/wiki/Northumbria_Police" title="Northumbria Police">Northumbria Police</a>, which covers <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a>), a university (<a href="/wiki/Northumbria_University" title="Northumbria University">Northumbria University</a>) based in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> and Northumbria Army Cadet Force. The local Environment Agency office, located in Newcastle Business Park, also uses the term Northumbria to describe its patch. Otherwise, the term is not the official name for the <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">UK and EU region of North East England</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingdom_.28654.E2.80.93954.29">Kingdom (654–954)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kingdom (654–954)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>

<dd><i>See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Northumbria" title="List of monarchs of Northumbria">List of monarchs of Northumbria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Northumbria_and_Northumberland" title="Timeline of Northumbria and Northumberland">Timeline of Northumbria</a></i></dd>

<p>Northumbria was originally formed from the union of two independent kingdoms, <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deira" title="Deira">Deira</a>. Bernicia covered lands north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">Tees</a>, while Deira corresponded roughly to modern-day <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. Bernicia and Deira were first united by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aethelfrith_of_Northumbria" title="Aethelfrith of Northumbria">Aethelfrith</a>, a king of Bernicia who conquered Deira around the year 604. He was defeated and killed around the year 616 in battle at the <a href="/wiki/River_Idle" title="River Idle">River Idle</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raedwald_of_East_Anglia" title="Raedwald of East Anglia">Raedwald of East Anglia</a>, who installed <a href="/wiki/Edwin_of_Northumbria" title="Edwin of Northumbria">Edwin</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86lla_of_Deira" title="Ælla of Deira">Ælla</a>, a former king of Deira, as king.</p>
<p>Edwin, who accepted Christianity in 627, soon grew to become the most powerful king in England: he was recognised as <a href="/wiki/Bretwalda" title="Bretwalda">Bretwalda</a> and conquered the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. He was, however, himself defeated by an alliance of the exiled king of Gwynedd, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadwallon_ap_Cadfan_of_Gwynedd" title="Cadwallon ap Cadfan of Gwynedd">Cadwallon ap Cadfan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Penda_of_Mercia" title="Penda of Mercia">Penda</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hatfield_Chase" title="Battle of Hatfield Chase">Battle of Hatfield Chase</a> in 633.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="King_Oswald">King Oswald</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: King Oswald">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg"><img alt="Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg" data-file-height="1794" data-file-width="1726" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg/260px-Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg/390px-Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg/520px-Northumbria.rise.600.700.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a></div>
<p>After Edwin's death, Northumbria was split between Bernicia, where <a href="/wiki/Eanfrith_of_Bernicia" title="Eanfrith of Bernicia">Eanfrith</a>, a son of Aethelfrith, took power, and Deira, where a cousin of Edwin, <a href="/wiki/Osric_of_Deira" title="Osric of Deira">Osric</a>, became king. Cumbria tended to remain a country frontier with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" title="Britons (historical)">Britons</a>. Both of these rulers were killed during the year that followed, as <a href="/wiki/Cadwallon_ap_Cadfan" title="Cadwallon ap Cadfan">Cadwallon</a> continued his devastating invasion of Northumbria. After the murder of Eanfrith, his brother, <a href="/wiki/Oswald_of_Northumbria" title="Oswald of Northumbria">Oswald</a>, backed by warriors sent by <a href="/wiki/Domnall_Brecc" title="Domnall Brecc">Domnall Brecc</a> of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="Dál Riata">Dál Riata</a>, defeated and killed Cadwallon at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Heavenfield" title="Battle of Heavenfield">Battle of Heavenfield</a> in 634.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Oswald expanded his kingdom considerably. He incorporated <a href="/wiki/Gododdin" title="Gododdin">Gododdin</a> lands northwards up to the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Firth of Forth</a> and also gradually extended his reach westward, encroaching on the remaining <a href="/wiki/Cumbric" title="Cumbric">Cumbric</a> speaking kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Strathclyde" title="Kingdom of Strathclyde">Strathclyde</a>. Thus, Northumbria became not only part of modern England's far north, but also covered much of what is now the south-east of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. King Oswald re-introduced Christianity to the Kingdom by appointing <a href="/wiki/Aidan_of_Lindisfarne" title="Aidan of Lindisfarne">St. Aidan</a>, an Irish monk from the Scottish island of <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a> to convert his people. This led to the introduction of the practices of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christianity</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> was established on <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne" title="Lindisfarne">Lindisfarne</a>.</p>
<p>War with Mercia continued, however. In 642, Oswald was killed by the Mercians under Penda at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Maserfield" title="Battle of Maserfield">Battle of Maserfield</a>. In 655, Penda launched a massive invasion of Northumbria, aided by the sub-king of Deira, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aethelwald_of_Deira" title="Aethelwald of Deira">Aethelwald</a>, but suffered a crushing defeat at the hands of an inferior force under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oswiu_of_Northumbria" title="Oswiu of Northumbria">Oswiu</a>, Oswald's successor, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Winwaed" title="Battle of Winwaed">Battle of Winwaed</a>. This battle marked a major turning point in Northumbrian fortunes: Penda died in the battle, and Oswiu gained supremacy over Mercia, making himself the most powerful king in England.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_consolidation">Religious consolidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religious consolidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the year 664, the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Whitby" title="Synod of Whitby">Synod of Whitby</a> was held to discuss the controversy regarding the timing of the Easter festival. Dispute had arisen between the practices of the Celtic church influencing Northumbria and those of the Roman church predominating throughout southern England and Western Europe. Eventually, Northumbria was persuaded to move to the Roman practice and the Celtic Bishop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colman_of_Lindisfarne" title="Colman of Lindisfarne">Colman of Lindisfarne</a> returned to Iona. The episcopal seat of Northumbria was transferred from Lindisfarne to York, which indicates the growing religious, political and economic importance of connections to southern Great Britain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_the_Kingdom_of_Northumbria">Decline of the Kingdom of Northumbria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline of the Kingdom of Northumbria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Northumbria lost control of <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a> in the late 650s, after a successful revolt under Penda's son <a href="/wiki/Wulfhere_of_Mercia" title="Wulfhere of Mercia">Wulfhere</a>, but it retained its dominant position until it suffered a disastrous defeat at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dun_Nechtain" title="Battle of Dun Nechtain">Battle of Dun Nechtain</a> in 685; Northumbria's king, <a href="/wiki/Ecgfrith_of_Northumbria" title="Ecgfrith of Northumbria">Ecgfrith</a> (son of Oswiu), was killed, and its power in the north was gravely weakened. The peaceful reign of <a href="/wiki/Aldfrith_of_Northumbria" title="Aldfrith of Northumbria">Aldfrith</a>, Ecgfrith's half-brother and successor, did something to limit the damage done, but it is from this point that Northumbria's power began to decline, and chronic instability followed Aldfrith's death in 704.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="541" data-file-width="517" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg/220px-Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg/330px-Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg/440px-Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eric_Bloodaxe_Norse_king_of_York_952_954.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coin of Eric Bloodaxe at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>. The legend reads <i>ERIC REX</i> ("King Eric")</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 867 Northumbria became the northern kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a>, after its conquest by the brothers <a href="/wiki/Halfdan_Ragnarsson" title="Halfdan Ragnarsson">Halfdan Ragnarsson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ivar_the_Boneless" title="Ivar the Boneless">Ivar the Boneless</a> who installed an Englishman, <a href="/wiki/Ecgberht_I_of_Northumbria" title="Ecgberht I of Northumbria">Ecgberht</a>, as a puppet king. Despite the pillaging of the kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking rule</a> brought lucrative trade to Northumbria, especially at their capital York. The kingdom passed between <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse-Gaelic" title="Norse-Gaelic">Norse-Gaelic</a> kings until it was finally absorbed by King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eadred_of_England" title="Eadred of England">Eadred</a> after the death of the last independent Northumbrian monarch, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erik_Bloodaxe" title="Erik Bloodaxe">Erik Bloodaxe</a>, in 954.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ealdormanships_and_earldoms_in_Northumbria_.28954.E2.80.931217.29">Ealdormanships and earldoms in Northumbria (954–1217)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ealdormanships and earldoms in Northumbria (954–1217)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Northumbria" title="Earl of Northumbria">Earl of Northumbria</a></div>
<p>After the English regained the territory of the former kingdom, Scots invasions reduced Northumbria to an earldom stretching from the Humber to the Tweed. Northumbria was disputed between the emerging kingdoms of England and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The land north of the Tweed was finally ceded to Scotland in 1018 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Carham" title="Battle of Carham">battle of Carham</a>. Yorkshire and Northumberland were first mentioned as separate in the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> in 1065.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_invasion_and_partition_of_the_earldom">Norman invasion and partition of the earldom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Norman invasion and partition of the earldom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> became king of England in 1066. He realised he needed to control Northumbria, which had remained virtually independent of the Kings of England, to protect his kingdom from Scottish invasion. In 1067, William appointed <a href="/wiki/Copsi" title="Copsi">Copsi</a> (sometimes Copsig) as Earl. However, just five weeks into his reign as earl, Copsi was murdered by <a href="/wiki/Osulf_II_of_Bamburgh" title="Osulf II of Bamburgh">Osulf II of Bamburgh</a>.</p>
<p>To acknowledge the remote independence of Northumbria and ensure that England was properly defended from the Scots, William gained the allegiance of both the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a> and the Earl and confirmed their powers and privileges. However, anti-Norman rebellions followed. William therefore attempted to install <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Comine" title="Robert Comine">Robert Comine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> noble, as the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Northumbria" title="Earl of Northumbria">Earl of Northumbria</a>, but before Comine could take up office, he and his 700 men were massacred in the city of <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">Durham</a>. In revenge, the Conqueror led his army in a bloody raid into Northumbria, an event that became known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Harrying_of_the_North" title="Harrying of the North">Harrying of the North</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethelwin" title="Ethelwin">Ethelwin</a>, the Anglo-Saxon <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a>, tried to flee Northumbria at the time of the raid, with Northumbrian treasures. The bishop was subsequently caught, imprisoned, and later died in confinement; his seat was left vacant.</p>
<p>Rebellions continued, and William's son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rufus" title="William Rufus">William Rufus</a> decided to partition Northumbria. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_of_St._Carilef" title="William of St. Carilef">William of St. Carilef</a> was made Bishop of Durham, and was also given the powers of Earl for the region south of the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Derwent,_North_East_England" title="River Derwent, North East England">Derwent</a>, which became the <a href="/wiki/County_Palatine_of_Durham" title="County Palatine of Durham">County Palatine of Durham</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The remainder, to the north of the rivers, became <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, where the political powers of the Bishops of Durham were limited to only certain districts, and the earls continued to rule as clients of the English throne. The city of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a> was founded by the Normans in 1080 to control the region by holding the strategically important crossing point of the river Tyne.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_history">Subsequent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Subsequent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Northumbrian region continued a history of revolt and rebellion against the government, as seen in the <a href="/wiki/Rising_of_the_North" title="Rising of the North">Rising of the North</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor</a> times. A major reason was the strength of Catholicism in the area after the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a>. Rural, thinly populated, and sharing a border with an often hostile Scotland, the region became a wild place where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reivers" title="Reivers">reivers</a> raided across the border and outlaws took refuge from justice. However, after the union of the crowns of Scotland and England under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">King James VI and I</a>, peace was largely established. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Restoration" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a>, many inhabitants of the Northumbrian region supported the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> cause.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Northumbria during its "golden age" was the most important centre of religious learning and arts in the British Isles. Initially the kingdom was <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Scottish_mission" title="Hiberno-Scottish mission">evangelised</a> by Irish monks from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_Church" title="Celtic Church">Celtic Church</a>, based at <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a> in modern Scotland, which led to a flowering of monastic life. <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne" title="Lindisfarne">Lindisfarne</a> on the east coast was founded from Iona by <a href="/wiki/Aidan_of_Lindisfarne" title="Aidan of Lindisfarne">Saint Aidan</a> in about 635, and was to remain the major Northumbrian monastic centre, producing figures like <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid" title="Wilfrid">Wilfrid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuthbert_of_Lindisfarne" title="Cuthbert of Lindisfarne">Saint Cuthbert</a>. The nobleman <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Biscop" title="Benedict Biscop">Benedict Biscop</a> had visited Rome and headed the monastery at <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> in Kent and his <a href="/wiki/Double_monastery" title="Double monastery">twin-foundation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monkwearmouth-Jarrow_Abbey" title="Monkwearmouth-Jarrow Abbey">Monkwearmouth-Jarrow Abbey</a> added a direct Roman influence to Northumbrian culture, and produced figures such as <a href="/wiki/Ceolfrith" title="Ceolfrith">Ceolfrith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>. Northumbria played an important role in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a>, a unique style combining Anglo-Saxon, <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictish" title="Pictish">Pictish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> and other elements, producing works such as the <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne_Gospels" title="Lindisfarne Gospels">Lindisfarne Gospels</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Cuthbert_Gospel" title="St Cuthbert Gospel">St Cuthbert Gospel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ruthwell_Cross" title="Ruthwell Cross">Ruthwell Cross</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bewcastle_Cross" title="Bewcastle Cross">Bewcastle Cross</a>, and later the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a>, which was probably created at Iona. After the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Whitby" title="Synod of Whitby">Synod of Whitby</a> in 664 Roman church practices officially replaced the Celtic ones but the influence of the Celtic style continued, the most famous examples of this being the Lindisfarne Gospels. The Venerable Bede (673–735) wrote his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum</a></i> (Ecclesiastical History of the English People, completed in 731) in Monkwearmouth-Jarrow, and much of it focuses on the kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The devastating Viking raid on Lindisfarne in 793 marked the beginning of a century of Viking invasions that severely checked all Anglo-Saxon culture, and heralded the end of Northumbria's position as a centre of influence, although in the years immediately following confident works like the <a href="/wiki/Easby_Cross" title="Easby Cross">Easby Cross</a> were still being produced. A <a href="/wiki/Bullion" title="Bullion">bullion</a>-weight economy was still in use at the end of the 9th-century AD, as demonstrated in the twenty-nine <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> ingots found in the <a href="/wiki/Bedale_Hoard" title="Bedale Hoard">Bedale Hoard</a>, along with sword fittings and necklaces in gold and silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Northumbria has its own <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northumbrian_tartan" title="Northumbrian tartan">check</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northumbrian_tartan" title="Northumbrian tartan">tartan</a></i>, which is similar to many ancient tartans (especially those from Northern Europe, such as one found near <a href="/wiki/Falkirk" title="Falkirk">Falkirk</a> and those discovered in <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a> that date from <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times (and even earlier).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Modern Border Tartans are almost invariably a bold black and white check, but historically the light squares were the yellowish colour of untreated wool, with the dark squares any of a range of dark greys, blues, greens or browns; hence the alternative name of "Border Drab". At a distance, the checks blend together making the fabric ideal <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a> for stalking game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Northumbria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from standard English, Northumbria has a series of closely related but distinctive <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a>, descended from the early <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> of the Angles, of which 80% of its vocabulary is derived,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and Vikings with a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic" title="Insular Celtic">Celtic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> loanwords. The <a href="/wiki/Early_Scots" title="Early Scots">Scots</a> language began to diverge from early Northumbrian <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, which was called <i>Ynglis</i> as late as the early 16th century (until the end of the 15th century the name <i>Scottis</i> (modern form: <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>) referred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>). There are many similarities between <a href="/wiki/Modern_Scots" title="Modern Scots">Modern Scots</a> dialects and those of Northumbria.</p>
<p>The major Northumbrian dialects are <a href="/wiki/Geordie" title="Geordie">Geordie</a> (Tyneside), Northern (north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Coquet" title="River Coquet">River Coquet</a>), Western (from <a href="/wiki/Allendale,_Northumberland" title="Allendale, Northumberland">Allendale</a> through <a href="/wiki/Hexham" title="Hexham">Hexham</a> up to <a href="/wiki/Kielder,_Northumberland" title="Kielder, Northumberland">Kielder</a>), Southern or <a href="/wiki/Pitmatic" title="Pitmatic">Pitmatic</a> (the mining towns such as <a href="/wiki/Ashington" title="Ashington">Ashington</a> and much of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">Durham</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mackem" title="Mackem">Mackem</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wearside" title="Wearside">Wearside</a>), Smoggie (<a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yorkshire_dialect_and_accent" title="Yorkshire dialect and accent">Tyke</a> (Yorkshire).</p>
