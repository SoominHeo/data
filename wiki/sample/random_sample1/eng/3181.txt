<p><b>North American Aviation</b> (<b>NAA</b>) was a major <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aerospace manufacturer</a>, responsible for a number of historic aircraft, including the <a href="/wiki/North_American_T-6_Texan" title="North American T-6 Texan">T-6 Texan</a> trainer, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_P-51_Mustang" title="North American P-51 Mustang">P-51 Mustang</a> <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter</a>, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_B-25_Mitchell" title="North American B-25 Mitchell">B-25 Mitchell</a> <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bomber</a>, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-86_Sabre" title="North American F-86 Sabre">F-86 Sabre</a> <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">jet</a> fighter, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">X-15</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft" title="Rocket-powered aircraft">rocket plane</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">XB-70</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_spacecraft" title="Apollo spacecraft">Command and Service Module</a>, the second stage of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter" title="Space Shuttle orbiter">orbiter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_B-1_Lancer" title="Rockwell B-1 Lancer">B-1 Lancer</a>. Through a series of mergers and sales, North American Aviation became part of <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">North American Rockwell</a>, which later became Rockwell International and is now part of <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clement_Melville_Keys" title="Clement Melville Keys">Clement Melville Keys</a> founded North American on December 6, 1928, as a <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> that bought and sold interests in various <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airlines</a> and aviation-related companies. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Mail_Act_of_1934" title="Air Mail Act of 1934">Air Mail Act of 1934</a> forced the breakup of such holding companies. North American became a manufacturing company, run by <a href="/wiki/James_H._Kindelberger" title="James H. Kindelberger">James H. "Dutch" Kindelberger</a>, who had been recruited from <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Aircraft_Company" title="Douglas Aircraft Company">Douglas Aircraft Company</a>. NAA did retain ownership of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a> until 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors Corporation</a> took a controlling interest in NAA and merged it with its <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> division in 1933, but retained the name North American Aviation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kindelberger moved the company's operations from <a href="/wiki/Dundalk,_Maryland" title="Dundalk, Maryland">Dundalk, Maryland</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, which allowed flying year-round, and decided to focus on training aircraft, on the theory that it would be easier than trying to compete with established companies on larger projects. Its first planes were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_GA-15" title="North American GA-15">GA-15</a> observation plane and the GA-16 trainer, followed by the <a href="/wiki/North_American_O-47" title="North American O-47">O-47</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_American_BT-9" title="North American BT-9">BT-9</a>, also called the GA-16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_BC-1" title="North American BC-1">BC-1</a> of 1937 was North American's first combat aircraft; it was based on the GA-16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1940, like other manufacturers, North American started gearing up for war, opening factories in <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas, Texas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Kansas" title="Kansas City, Kansas">Kansas City, Kansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup> North American ranked eleventh among United States corporations in the value of wartime production contracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_American_Aviation_plant,_Inglewood,_CA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="728" data-file-width="940" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/North_American_Aviation_plant%2C_Inglewood%2C_CA.jpg/220px-North_American_Aviation_plant%2C_Inglewood%2C_CA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/North_American_Aviation_plant%2C_Inglewood%2C_CA.jpg/330px-North_American_Aviation_plant%2C_Inglewood%2C_CA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/North_American_Aviation_plant%2C_Inglewood%2C_CA.jpg/440px-North_American_Aviation_plant%2C_Inglewood%2C_CA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>North American's follow-on to the BT-9 was the <a href="/wiki/North_American_T-6_Texan" title="North American T-6 Texan">T-6 Texan</a> trainer, of which 17,000 were built, making it the most widely used trainer ever. The twin-engine <a href="/wiki/North_American_B-25_Mitchell" title="North American B-25 Mitchell">B-25 Mitchell</a> bomber achieved fame in the <a href="/wiki/Doolittle_Raid" title="Doolittle Raid">Doolittle Raid</a> and was used in all combat theaters of operation. The <a href="/wiki/North_American_P-51_Mustang" title="North American P-51 Mustang">P-51 Mustang</a> was initially produced for Britain as an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_P-40_Warhawk" title="Curtiss P-40 Warhawk">Curtiss P-40 Warhawk</a>, which North American had declined to produce under licence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The derivative <a href="/wiki/North_American_A-36_Apache" title="North American A-36 Apache">A-36 Apache</a> was developed as a ground attack aircraft and dive bomber. A suggestion by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> that North American switch the P-51's powerplant from its original Allison engine to the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Merlin" title="Rolls-Royce Merlin">Rolls-Royce Merlin</a> engine may have been one of the most significant events in WWII aviation, as it transformed the P-51 into what many consider to be the best American fighter of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_years">Post-war years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-war years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Post-war, North American's employment dropped from a high of 91,000 to 5,000 in 1946. On <a href="/wiki/Victory_over_Japan_Day" title="Victory over Japan Day">V-J Day</a>, North American had orders from the U.S. government for 8,000 aircraft. A few months later, that had dropped to 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two years later in 1948, General Motors divested NAA as a public company. Nevertheless, NAA continued with new designs, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-28_Trojan" title="T-28 Trojan">T-28 Trojan</a> trainer and attack aircraft, the odd-looking <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-82_Twin_Mustang" title="North American F-82 Twin Mustang">F-82 Twin Mustang</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_American_B-45_Tornado" title="North American B-45 Tornado">B-45 Tornado</a> jet bomber, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_FJ_Fury" title="North American FJ Fury">FJ Fury</a> fighter, <a href="/wiki/North_American_AJ_Savage" title="North American AJ Savage">AJ Savage</a>, the revolutionary <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">XB-70 Valkyrie</a> Mach-3 <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bomber" title="Strategic bomber">strategic bomber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aero_Commander_500_family" title="Aero Commander 500 family">Shrike Commander</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_American_Sabreliner" title="North American Sabreliner">T-39 Sabreliner</a> business jet.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1529" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg/220px-North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg/330px-North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg/440px-North_American_XB-70_above_runway_ECN-792.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The North American <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">XB-70 Valkyrie</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a> division of North American Aviation was instrumental in the exclusive development and production of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_A-5_Vigilante" title="North American A-5 Vigilante">A-5 Vigilante</a>, an advanced high speed bomber that would see significant use as a naval <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> aircraft during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OV-10_Bronco" title="OV-10 Bronco">OV-10 Bronco</a>, the first aircraft specifically designed for <a href="/wiki/Forward_air_control" title="Forward air control">forward air control</a> (FAC), and <a href="/wiki/Counter-insurgency" title="Counter-insurgency">counter-insurgency</a> (COIN) duties, and the <a href="/wiki/North_American_T-2_Buckeye" title="North American T-2 Buckeye">T-2 Buckeye</a> Naval trainer, which would serve from the late 1950s until 2008 and be flown in training by virtually every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Aviator" title="Naval Aviator">Naval Aviator</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Flight_Officer" title="Naval Flight Officer">Naval Flight Officer</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marine_Corps" title="US Marine Corps">US Marine Corps</a> for four decades. The Buckeye's name would be an acknowledgment to the <a href="/wiki/Aesculus_glabra" title="Aesculus glabra">state tree of</a> <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, as well as the mascot of <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-86_Sabre" title="North American F-86 Sabre">North American F-86 Sabre</a> started out as a redesigned Fury and achieved fame shooting down <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan" title="Mikoyan">MiGs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. Over 9,000 F-86s were produced. Its successor, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-100_Super_Sabre" title="North American F-100 Super Sabre">North American F-100 Super Sabre</a>, was also popular.</p>
<p>Some 6,656 F-86s were produced in the United States, the most postwar military aircraft in the West, as well as another 2,500 elsewhere. To accommodate its Sabre production, North American opened facilities in a former Curtiss-Wright plant in <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>. It also moved into a former Consolidated-Vultee Aircraft plant at <a href="/wiki/Downey,_California" title="Downey, California">Downey, California</a>, and in 1948, built a new plant at Downey. By the end of 1952, North American sales topped $315 million. Employment at the Columbus plant grew from 1,600 in 1950 to 18,000 in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCoFC_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-USCoFC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-107" title="North American F-107">F-107</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XF-108_Rapier" title="XF-108 Rapier">F-108</a> programs in the late 1950s, as well as the cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/SM-64_Navaho" title="SM-64 Navaho">Navaho</a> intercontinental <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise missile</a> program, was a blow to North American from which it never fully recovered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_development">Nuclear development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nuclear development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Atomics International</b> was a division of North American Aviation which began as the <i>Atomic Energy Research Department</i> at the Downey plant in 1948. In 1955, the department was renamed <a href="/wiki/Atomics_International" title="Atomics International">Atomics International</a> and engaged principally in the early development of nuclear technology and nuclear reactors for both commercial and government applications. Atomics International was responsible for a number of accomplishments relating to nuclear energy: design, construction and operation of the first nuclear reactor in California (a small <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_homogeneous_reactor" title="Aqueous homogeneous reactor">aqueous homogeneous reactor</a> located at the NAA Downey plant),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the first nuclear reactor to produce power for a commercial power grid in the United States (the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_Reactor_Experiment" title="Sodium Reactor Experiment">Sodium Reactor Experiment</a> located at the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susana_Field_Laboratory" title="Santa Susana Field Laboratory">Santa Susana Field Laboratory</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the first nuclear reactor launched into outer space by the United States (the <a href="/wiki/SNAP-10A" title="SNAP-10A">SNAP-10A</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As overall interest in nuclear power declined, Atomics International transitioned to non-nuclear energy-related projects such as coal gasification and gradually ceased designing and testing nuclear reactors. Atomics International was eventually merged with the Rocketdyne division in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation_and_guidance.2C_radar.2C_and_data_systems">Navigation and guidance, radar, and data systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Navigation and guidance, radar, and data systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Autonetics" title="Autonetics">Autonetics</a> began in 1945 at North American's Technical Research Laboratory, a small unit in the Los Angeles Division's engineering department based in <a href="/wiki/Downey,_California" title="Downey, California">Downey, California</a>. The evolution of the <a href="/wiki/SM-64_Navaho" title="SM-64 Navaho">Navaho</a> missile program resulted in the establishment of Autonetics as a separate division of North American Aviation in 1955, first located in Downey, later moving to <a href="/wiki/Anaheim,_California" title="Anaheim, California">Anaheim, California</a> in 1963. The division was involved in the development of <a href="/wiki/Guidance_system" title="Guidance system">guidance systems</a> for the <a href="/wiki/LGM-30_Minuteman#Guidance_system" title="LGM-30 Minuteman">Minuteman</a> ballistic missile system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_program">Space program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Space program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The North American Apollo spacecraft being prepared for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_7" title="Apollo 7">Apollo 7</a> mission</div>
</div>

<p>In 1955, the rocket engine operations were spun off into a separate division as <a href="/wiki/Rocketdyne" title="Rocketdyne">Rocketdyne</a>. This division furnished engines for the <a href="/wiki/PGM-11_Redstone" title="PGM-11 Redstone">Redstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/PGM-19_Jupiter" title="PGM-19 Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/PGM-17_Thor" title="PGM-17 Thor">Thor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_(rocket_family)" title="Delta (rocket family)">Delta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">Atlas</a> missiles, and for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(rocket_family)" title="Saturn (rocket family)">Saturn family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicles</a>.</p>
<p>North American designed and built the airframe for the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">X-15</a>, a rocket-powered aircraft that first flew in 1959.</p>
<p>In 1959, North American built the first of several <a href="/wiki/Little_Joe_(rocket)" title="Little Joe (rocket)">Little Joe</a> boosters used to test the <a href="/wiki/Launch_escape_system" title="Launch escape system">launch escape system</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury#Spacecraft" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury spacecraft</a>. In 1960, the new CEO <a href="/wiki/John_Leland_Atwood" title="John Leland Atwood">Lee Atwood</a> decided to focus on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">space program</a>, and the company became the prime contractor for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Apollo Command/Service Module</a>, a larger <a href="/wiki/Little_Joe_II" title="Little Joe II">Little Joe II</a> rocket to test Apollo's launch escape system, and the <a href="/wiki/S-II" title="S-II">S-II</a> second stage of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_and_acquisition">Merger and acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Merger and acquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fatal <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> fire in January 1967 was partly blamed on the company. In March, it merged with Rockwell-Standard, and the merged company became known as <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International#Predecessor_companies" title="Rockwell International">North American Rockwell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global-14">[14]</a></sup> Within two years the new company was studying concepts for the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, and won the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter" title="Space Shuttle orbiter">orbiter</a> contract in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1973, the company changed its name again to <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">Rockwell International</a> and named its aircraft division North American Aircraft Operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Rockwell International's defense and space divisions (including the North American Aviation divisions Autonetics and Rocketdyne) were sold to <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> in December 1996. Initially called Boeing North American, these groups were integrated with Boeing's <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Defense,_Space_%26_Security" title="Boeing Defense, Space &amp; Security">Defense</a> division. <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Rocketdyne" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Rocketdyne">Rocketdyne</a> was eventually sold by Boeing to <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">UTC Pratt &amp; Whitney</a> in 2005. UTC later sold Rocketdyne to Aerojet (GenCorp) in 2013.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
