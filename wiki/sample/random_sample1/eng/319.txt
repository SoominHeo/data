<p>A <b>bracket</b> is a tall <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a> mark typically used in matched pairs within text, to set apart or interject other text. Used unqualified, brackets refer to different types of brackets in different parts of the world and in different contexts. The matched pair may be described as opening and closing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msterm_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-msterm-1">[1]</a></sup> or left and right symbols.</p>
<p>Brackets include round brackets/parentheses, square brackets, curly brackets/braces, angle brackets, and various other pairs of symbols.</p>
<p>In addition to referring to the class of all types of brackets, the unqualified word <i>bracket</i> is most commonly used to refer to a specific type of bracket. In modern American usage this is usually the square bracket and in modern British usage this is usually the parenthesis.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chevrons (&lt; &gt;) were the earliest type of bracket to appear in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Written_English" title="Written English">written English</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus" title="Desiderius Erasmus">Desiderius Erasmus</a> coined the term <i>lunula</i> to refer to the rounded parentheses (), recalling the shape of the crescent <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_for_various_bracket_symbols">Names for various bracket symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names for various bracket symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the following names are regional or contextual.</p>
<ul>
<li>( ) – parentheses, brackets (UK, Canada, New Zealand, South Africa and Australia), parens, round brackets, soft brackets, first brackets or circle brackets</li>
<li>[ ] – square brackets, closed brackets, hard brackets, second brackets, crotchets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or brackets (US)</li>
<li>{ } – braces are "two connecting marks used in printing"; and in music "to connect staves to be performed at the same time"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (UK and US), flower brackets (India), French brackets, curly brackets, definite brackets, swirly brackets, curly braces, birdie brackets, Scottish brackets, squirrelly brackets, gullwings, seagulls, squiggly brackets, twirly brackets, Tuborg brackets (DK), accolades (NL), pointy brackets, third brackets, or fancy brackets</li>
<li>〈 〉– pointy brackets, angle brackets, triangular brackets, diamond brackets, tuples, or chevrons</li>
<li>&lt; &gt; – inequality signs, pointy brackets, or brackets. Sometimes referred to as <i>angle brackets</i>, in such cases as <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> markup. Occasionally known as <i>broken brackets</i> or <i>brokets</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>⸤ ⸥; ｢ ｣ – corner brackets</li>
<li>⟦ ⟧ – double square brackets, white square brackets</li>
<li>Guillemets, ‹ › and « », are sometimes referred to as chevrons or [double] angle brackets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msterm_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msterm-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typography">Typography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The characters ‹ › and « », known as <a href="/wiki/Guillemet" title="Guillemet">guillemets</a> or <i>angular quote brackets</i>, are actually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quotation_mark_glyphs" title="Quotation mark glyphs">quotation mark glyphs</a> used in several European languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Which one of each pair is the opening quote mark and which is the closing quote varies between languages.</p>
<p>In English, typographers generally prefer to not set brackets in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italics" title="Italics">italics</a>, even when the enclosed text is italic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, in other languages like German, if brackets enclose text in italics, they are usually set in italics too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span> (singular, parenthesis <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (also called simply brackets, or round brackets, curved brackets, oval brackets, or, colloquially, parens <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>) contain material that serves to clarify (in the manner of a <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">gloss</a>) or is aside from the main point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A milder effect may be obtained by using a pair of commas as the <a href="/wiki/Delimiter" title="Delimiter">delimiter</a>, though if the sentence contains commas for other purposes, visual confusion may result.</p>
<p>In American usage, parentheses are usually considered separate from other brackets, and calling them "brackets" is unusual.</p>
<p>Parentheses may be used in formal writing to add supplementary information, such as "Sen. John McCain (R-Arizona) spoke at length". They can also indicate shorthand for "<a href="/wiki/Uncertain_plural" title="Uncertain plural">either singular or plural</a>" for nouns, e. g. "the claim(s)". It can also be used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_neutral_language" title="Gender neutral language">gender neutral language</a>, especially in languages with <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">grammatical gender</a>, e. g. "(s)he agreed with his (her) physician", .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parenthetical phrases have been used extensively in informal writing and stream of consciousness literature. Examples include the southern American author <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a> (see <i><a href="/wiki/Absalom,_Absalom!" title="Absalom, Absalom!">Absalom, Absalom!</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/The_Sound_and_the_Fury#Part_2:_June_2.2C_1910" title="The Sound and the Fury">the Quentin section of <i>The Sound and the Fury</i></a>) as well as poet <a href="/wiki/E._E._Cummings" title="E. E. Cummings">E. E. Cummings</a>.</p>
<p>Parentheses have historically been used where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Em_dash" title="Em dash">dash</a> is currently used in alternatives, such as "parenthesis)(parentheses". Examples of this usage can be seen in editions of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Dictionary_of_Modern_English_Usage" title="A Dictionary of Modern English Usage">Fowler's</a></i>.</p>
<p>Parentheses may be nested (generally with one set (such as this) inside another set). This is not commonly used in formal writing (though sometimes other brackets [especially square brackets] will be used for one or more inner set of parentheses [in other words, secondary {or even tertiary} phrases can be found within the main parenthetical sentence]).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Any punctuation inside parentheses or other brackets is independent of the rest of the text: "Mrs. Pennyfarthing (What? Yes, that was her name!) was my landlady." In this usage, the explanatory text in the parentheses is a <a href="/wiki/Parenthesis_(rhetoric)" title="Parenthesis (rhetoric)">parenthesis</a>. (Parenthesized text is usually short and within a single sentence. Where several sentences of supplemental material are used in parentheses the final <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">full stop</a> would be within the parentheses, or simply omitted. Again, the parenthesis implies that the meaning and flow of the text is supplemental to the rest of the text and the whole would be unchanged were the parenthesized sentences removed.)</p>
<p>Parentheses are included in the syntaxes of many computer programming languages. Typically needed to denote an argument; to tell the compiler what data type the Method/Function needs to look for first in order to initialise. In some cases, such as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LISP" title="LISP">LISP</a>, parentheses are a fundamental construct of the language.</p>
<p>Parentheses in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> signify a different <a href="/wiki/Order_of_operations" title="Order of operations">precedence</a> of operators. For example: <span class="texhtml">2 + 3 × 4</span> equals 14, since the multiplication is done before the addition. However, <span class="texhtml">(2 + 3) × 4</span> equals 20, because the parentheses override normal precedence, causing the addition to be done first. Some authors follow the convention in mathematical equations that, when parentheses have one level of nesting, the inner pair are parentheses and the outer pair are square brackets. Example:</p>

<p>A related convention is that when parentheses have two levels of nesting, curly brackets (braces) are the outermost pair. This helps to distinguish between one such level and the next.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parentheses are also used to set apart the <a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">arguments</a> in mathematical <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">functions</a>. For example, <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i>(<i>x</i>)</span> is the function <i><span class="texhtml">f</span></i> applied to the <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variable</a> <i><span class="texhtml">x</span></i>. In <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinate systems</a> parentheses are used to denote a set of coordinates; so in the <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian coordinate system</a> <span class="texhtml">(4, 7)</span> may represent the point located at 4 on the <i>x</i>-axis and 7 on the <i>y</i>-axis. Parentheses may also represent <a href="/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)" title="Interval (mathematics)">intervals</a>; <span class="texhtml">(0, 5)</span>, for example, is the interval between 0 and 5, not including 0 or 5.</p>
<p>Parentheses may also be used to represent a <a href="/wiki/Binomial_coefficient" title="Binomial coefficient">binomial coefficient</a>, and in chemistry to denote a <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">polyatomic ion</a>.</p>
<p>Since 2014, antisemites have used <a href="/wiki/Triple_parentheses" title="Triple parentheses">triple parentheses</a> around the names of people to denote them as Jewish.</p>

<p>In more formal usage, "<a href="/wiki/Parenthesis_(rhetoric)" title="Parenthesis (rhetoric)">parenthesis</a>" may refer to the entire bracketed text, not just to the punctuation marks used (so all the text in this set of round brackets may be said to be "<a href="/wiki/Parenthesis_(rhetoric)" title="Parenthesis (rhetoric)">a parenthesis</a>", "a parenthetical", or "a parenthetical phrase").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Square_brackets">Square brackets <span id="square_bracket"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Square brackets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_journalism">Usage in journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usage in journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Square brackets—also called crotchets or simply brackets (US)—are mainly used to insert explanatory material or to mark where a passage was omitted from an original material by someone other than the original author, or to mark modifications in quotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A bracketed <a href="/wiki/Ellipsis" title="Ellipsis">ellipsis</a> […] is often used to indicate omitted material: "I'd like to thank [several unimportant people] for their tolerance..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Bracketed comments inserted into a quote indicate when the original has been modified for clarity: "I appreciate it [the honor], but I must refuse", and "the future of psionics [see definition] is in doubt". Or one can quote the original statement "I hate to do laundry" with a (sometimes grammatical) modification inserted: He "hate[s] to do laundry".</p>
<p>Additionally, a small letter can be replaced by a capital one, when the beginning of the original text is omitted for <a href="/wiki/Succinctness" title="Succinctness">succinctness</a>, for example, when referring to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbose" title="Verbose">verbose</a> original: "To the extent that policymakers and elite opinion in general have made use of economic analysis at all, they have, as the saying goes, done so the way a drunkard uses a lamppost: for support, not illumination", it can be quoted succinctly as: "[P]olicymakers (...) made use of economic analysis (...) the way a drunkard uses a lamppost: for support, not illumination." When nested parentheses are needed, brackets are used as a substitute for the inner pair of parentheses within the outer pair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> When deeper levels of nesting are needed, convention is to alternate between parentheses and brackets at each level.</p>
<p>Alternatively, empty square brackets can also indicate omitted material, usually single letter only. The original "Reading is also a process and it also changes you." can be rewritten in a quote as: It has been suggested that reading can "also change[] you".</p>
<p>The bracketed expression "<a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">[<i>sic</i>]</a>" is used after a quote or reprinted text to indicate the passage appears exactly as in the original source, where it may otherwise appear that a mistake has been made in reproduction.</p>
<p>In translated works, brackets are used to signify the same word or phrase in the original language to avoid ambiguity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For example: <i>He is trained in the way of the open hand [karate].</i></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_scientific_fields">Usage in scientific fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Usage in scientific fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Brackets are used in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> in a variety of notations, including standard notations for <a href="/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)" title="Interval (mathematics)">intervals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commutator" title="Commutator">commutators</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Floor_and_ceiling_functions" title="Floor and ceiling functions">floor function</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields" title="Lie bracket of vector fields">Lie bracket</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iverson_bracket" title="Iverson bracket">Iverson bracket</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrices</a>.</p>
<p>Brackets can also be used in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> to represent the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> or to denote distributed charge in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" title="Complex (chemistry)">complex ion</a>.</p>
<p>Brackets are used in many computer <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>, especially those <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_C-based_programming_languages" title="List of C-based programming languages">derived or inspired by</a> the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C language</a>, to indicate <a href="/wiki/Array_data_structure" title="Array data structure">array</a> indexing operators. In this context, the opening bracket is often pronounced as "sub", indicating a <i>sub</i>script.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In linguistics, <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_transcription" title="Phonetic transcription">phonetic transcriptions</a> are generally enclosed within brackets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> often using the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>, whereas <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemic</a> transcriptions typically use paired <a href="/wiki/Slash_(punctuation)" title="Slash (punctuation)">slashes</a>. Pipes (| |) are often used to indicate a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphophoneme" title="Morphophoneme">morphophonemic</a> rather than phonemic representation. Other conventions are double slashes (// //), double pipes (|| ||) and curly brackets ({ }). In <a href="/wiki/Lexicography" title="Lexicography">lexicography</a>, square brackets usually surround the section of a dictionary entry which contains the <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of the word the entry defines.</p>
<p>Brackets are used to denote parts of the text that need to be checked when preparing drafts prior to finalizing a document. They often denote points that have not yet been agreed to in legal drafts and the year in which a report was made for certain <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a> decisions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curly_brackets">Curly brackets <span id="Braces"></span><span id="Curly_bracket"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Curly brackets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/curly_bracket" title="wiktionary:curly bracket">Wiktionary:curly bracket</a></div>
<p>Curly brackets- { } —also called <b>braces</b> in the US (or, colloquially, <i>squiggly brackets</i>)—are used in specialized ways in poetry and music (to mark repeats or joined lines). The musical terms for this mark joining staves are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accolade_(notation)" title="Accolade (notation)">accolade</a> and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brace_(music)" title="Brace (music)">brace</a>", and connect two or more lines of music that are played simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> they delimit <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">sets</a>, and in writing, they may be used similarly, "Select your animal {goat, sheep, cow, horse} and follow me". In many programming languages, they enclose groups of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statement_(programming)" title="Statement (programming)">statements</a>. Such languages (<a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> being one of the best-known examples) are therefore called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curly_bracket_language" title="Curly bracket language">curly brace languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>General usage in North American English favours the term <i>brace</i> rather than <i>curly bracket</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Indian programmers often use the name "flower bracket".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, curly brackets are often also used to denote the <a href="/wiki/Poisson_bracket" title="Poisson bracket">Poisson bracket</a> between two quantities.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a>, chevrons or parentheses are used to denote <a href="/wiki/Ordered_pair" title="Ordered pair">ordered pairs</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Tuple" title="Tuple">tuples</a>, whereas curly brackets are used for unordered sets.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, chevrons indicate <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">graphemes</a> (i.e., written letters) or <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>, as in "The English word <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kæt/</span> is spelled ⟨cat⟩."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sampson_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sampson-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trask-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Epigraphy" title="Epigraphy">epigraphy</a>, they may be used for mechanical transliterations of a text into the Latin script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sampson_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sampson-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Textual_criticism" title="Textual criticism">textual criticism</a>, and hence in many editions of pre-modern works, chevrons denote sections of the text which are illegible or otherwise lost; the editor will often insert their own reconstruction where possible within them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trask-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chevrons are infrequently used to denote words that are thought instead of spoken, such as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>⟨ What an unusual flower! ⟩</dd>
</dl>
<p>The mathematical or logical symbols for <i>greater-than</i> (&gt;) and <i>less-than</i> (&lt;) are <a href="/wiki/Inequality_(mathematics)" title="Inequality (mathematics)">inequality</a> symbols; when either symbol is bisected by a vertical line, it represents "not greater than" or "not less than," respectively. These <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a> are not punctuation marks when used, as intended, to represent an inequality. However, as true chevrons are not present on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keyboard_(computing)" title="Keyboard (computing)">computer keyboards</a>, the available less-than and greater-than symbols are often used instead. They are loosely referred to as <i>angle[d] brackets</i> or <i>chevrons</i> in this case.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Single and double pairs of comparison operators (&lt;&lt;, &gt;&gt;) (meaning <i>much smaller than</i> and <i>much greater than</i>) are sometimes used instead of <a href="/wiki/Guillemet" title="Guillemet">guillemets</a> («, ») (used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-English_usage_of_quotation_marks" title="Non-English usage of quotation marks">quotation marks in many languages</a>) when the proper characters are not available.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>, chevrons are often used to mark dialogue that has been translated notionally from another language; in other words, if a character is speaking another language, instead of writing in the other language and providing a translation, one writes the translated text within chevrons. Of course, since no foreign language is actually written, this is only <i>notionally</i> translated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chevron-like symbols are part of standard <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a> punctuation, where they generally enclose the titles of books: ︿ and ﹀ or ︽ and ︾ for traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tategaki" title="Tategaki">vertical printing</a>, and 〈 and 〉 or 《 and 》 for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yokogaki" title="Yokogaki">horizontal</a> printing. See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-English_usage_of_quotation_marks" title="Non-English usage of quotation marks">non-English usage of quotation marks</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">continuum mechanics</a>, chevrons may be used as <a href="/wiki/Macaulay_brackets" title="Macaulay brackets">Macaulay brackets</a>.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-English_usage_of_quotation_marks#Chinese.2C_Japanese_and_Korean_quotation_marks" title="Non-English usage of quotation marks">East Asian punctuation</a>, angle brackets are used as quotation marks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lenticular_brackets">Lenticular brackets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lenticular brackets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> languages use lenticular brackets 【 】, a combination of brackets and parentheses called <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">方頭括號</span> in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> and <i>sumitsuki</i> in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>. They are used for inference in Chinese and used in titles and headings in Japanese.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Floor_and_ceiling_corners">Floor and ceiling corners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Floor and ceiling corners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The floor corner brackets ⌊ and ⌋, the ceiling corner brackets ⌈ and ⌉ are used to denote the integer <a href="/wiki/Floor_and_ceiling_functions" title="Floor and ceiling functions">floor and ceiling functions</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quine_corners_and_half_brackets">Quine corners and half brackets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Quine corners and half brackets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Quine corners ⌜ and ⌝ have at least two uses in mathematical logic: either as <a href="/wiki/Quasi-quotation" title="Quasi-quotation">quasi-quotation</a>, a generalization of quotation marks, or to denote the <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6del_numbering" title="Gödel numbering">Gödel number</a> of the enclosed expression.</p>
<p>Half brackets are used in English to mark added text, such as in translations: "Bill saw ⸤her⸥".</p>
<p>In editions of <a href="/wiki/Papyrology" title="Papyrology">papyrological</a> texts, half brackets, ⸤ and ⸥ or ⸢ and ⸣, enclose text which is lacking in the papyrus due to damage, but can be restored by virtue of another source, such as an ancient quotation of the text transmitted by the papyrus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">Callimachus</a> <i>Iambus</i> 1.2 reads: ἐκ τῶν ὅκου βοῦν κολλύ⸤βου π⸥ιπρήσκουσιν. A hole in the papyrus has obliterated βου π, but these letters are supplied by an ancient commentary on the poem. Second intermittent sources can be between ⸢ and ⸣. Quine corners are sometimes used instead of half brackets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unicode_Miscellaneous_Technical_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unicode_Miscellaneous_Technical-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_brackets">Double brackets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Double brackets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Double brackets (or white square brackets), ⟦ ⟧, are used to indicate the <i>semantic evaluation function</i> in <a href="/wiki/Formal_semantics_(linguistics)" title="Formal semantics (linguistics)">formal semantics</a> for natural language and <a href="/wiki/Denotational_semantics" title="Denotational semantics">denotational semantics</a> for programming languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The brackets stand for a function that maps a linguistic expression to its “denotation” or semantic value. Double brackets may also refer to the mathematical <a href="/wiki/Floor_and_ceiling_functions" title="Floor and ceiling functions">floor function</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brackets_with_quills">Brackets with quills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Brackets with quills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Known as "spike parentheses" (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv"><i>piggparenteser</i></span>) ⁅ and ⁆ are used in Swedish dictionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_uses">Specific uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Specific uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computing">Computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The various bracket characters are frequently used in many computer languages as operators or for other syntax markup. For instance, in C-like languages, <code>{</code> and <code>}</code> are often used to delimit a code block, and the parameters of method calls are generally enclosed by <code>(</code> and <code>)</code>. In C, C++, Java and other C-derived languages—as well as in Scheme-influenced languages that have adopted C/Java syntax, such as JavaScript—the "{}" symbols are referred to as "braces" or "curly braces" and never as brackets. Since the term "brace" is documented in the definitive programming specifications for these languages, it is preferable to use the correct term brace so there is no confusion between the brace (used to denote compound statements) and the bracket, used to denote arrays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">Mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bracket_(mathematics)" title="Bracket (mathematics)">Bracket (mathematics)</a></div>
<p>In addition to the use of <a href="#Parentheses_.28_.29">parentheses</a> to specify the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_operations" title="Order of operations">order of operations</a>, both parentheses and brackets are used to denote an <a href="/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)" title="Interval (mathematics)">interval</a>, also referred to as a half-open range. The notation <span class="texhtml">[<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span>,<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span>)</span> is used to indicate an interval from <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> to <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span> that is inclusive of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> but exclusive of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span>. That is, <span class="texhtml">[5, 12)</span> would be the set of all real numbers between 5 and 12, including 5 but not 12. The numbers may come as close as they like to 12, including 11.999 and so forth (with any <a href="/wiki/Finite_set" title="Finite set">finite</a> number of 9s), but 12.0 is not included. In some European countries, the notation <span class="texhtml">[5, 12[</span> is also used for this. The endpoint adjoining the bracket is known as <i>closed</i>, whereas the endpoint adjoining the parenthesis is known as <i>open</i>. If both types of brackets are the same, the entire interval may be referred to as <i>closed</i> or <i>open</i> as appropriate. Whenever <a href="/wiki/Extended_real_number_line" title="Extended real number line">+∞</a> or −∞ is used as an endpoint, it is normally considered <i>open</i> and adjoined to a parenthesis. See <a href="/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)" title="Interval (mathematics)">Interval (mathematics)</a> for a more complete treatment.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, chevrons are also used as part of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Dirac's</a> formalism, <a href="/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket_notation" title="Bra–ket notation">bra–ket notation</a>, to note vectors from the <a href="/wiki/Dual_space" title="Dual space">dual spaces</a> of the Bra ⟨<span class="texhtml"><i>A</i>|</span> and the Ket <span class="texhtml">|<i>B</i></span>⟩. Mathematicians will also commonly write ⟨<span class="texhtml"><i>a</i>, <i>b</i></span>⟩ for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_product" title="Inner product">inner product</a> of two vectors. In statistical mechanics, chevrons denote ensemble or time average. Chevrons are used in <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> to write <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_presentation" title="Group presentation">group presentations</a>, and to denote the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_generators" title="Group generators">subgroup generated</a> by a collection of elements. Note that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obtuse_angle" title="Obtuse angle">obtuse angled</a> chevrons are not always (and even not by all users) distinguished from a pair of less-than and greater-than signs &lt;&gt;, which are sometimes used as a <a href="/wiki/Typographic_approximation" title="Typographic approximation">typographic approximation</a> of chevrons.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">ring theory</a>, brackets denote the <a href="/wiki/Commutator" title="Commutator">commutator</a>. In group theory, the commutator <span class="texhtml">[<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">g</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">h</span>]</span> is commonly defined as <span class="texhtml"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">g</span><sup> −1</sup> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">h</span><sup> −1</sup> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">g</span> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">h</span> </span>. In ring theory, the commutator <span class="texhtml">[<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span>]</span> is defined as <span class="texhtml"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span> − <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> </span>. Furthermore, in ring theory, braces denote the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticommutator" title="Anticommutator">anticommutator</a> where <span class="texhtml">{<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span></span>} is defined as <span class="texhtml"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span> + <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> </span>. The bracket is also used to denote the <a href="/wiki/Lie_derivative" title="Lie derivative">Lie derivative</a>, or more generally the Lie bracket in any <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">Lie algebra</a>.</p>
<p>Various notations, like the <a href="/wiki/Vinculum_(symbol)" title="Vinculum (symbol)">vinculum</a> have a similar effect to brackets in specifying order of operations, or otherwise grouping several characters together for a common purpose.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Z_notation" title="Z notation">Z</a> <a href="/wiki/Formal_specification" title="Formal specification">formal specification</a> language, braces define a set and chevrons define a sequence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting">Accounting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Accounting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accountancy" title="Accountancy">accounting</a>, contra amounts are placed in parentheses. A debit balance account in a series of credit balances will have brackets and vice versa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brackets are used in some countries in the citation of <a href="/wiki/Law_report" title="Law report">law reports</a> to identify parallel citations to non-official reporters. For example: Chronicle Pub. Co. v. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superior_Court" title="Superior Court">Superior Court</a>, (1998) 54 Cal.2d 548, [7 Cal.Rptr. 109]. In some other countries (such as <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>), square brackets are used to indicate that the year is part of the citation and parentheses are used to indicate the year the judgment was given. For example, National Coal Board v England [1954] AC 403, is in the 1954 volume of the Appeal Cases reports although the decision may have been given in 1953 or earlier, whereas (1954) 98 Sol Jo 176 reports a decision from 1954, in volume 98 of the Solicitor's Journal which may be published in 1955 or later.</p>
<p>When quoted material is in any way altered, the alterations are enclosed in brackets within the quotation. For example: Plaintiff asserts his cause is just, stating, "[m]y causes is <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">[<i>sic</i>]</a> just." Although in the original quoted sentence the word "my" was capitalized, it has been modified in the quotation and the change signalled with brackets. Similarly, where the quotation contained a grammatical error, the quoting author signalled that the error was in the original with "[<i>sic</i>]" (Latin for 'thus'). (<i>California Style Manual</i>, section 4:59 (4th ed.))</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bracket_(tournament)" title="Bracket (tournament)">Tournament brackets</a>, the diagrammatic representation of the series of games played during a tournament usually leading to a single winner, are so named for their resemblance to brackets or braces.</p>

<p>Braces (curly brackets) first became part of a character set with the 8-bit code of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_7030_Stretch" title="IBM 7030 Stretch">IBM 7030 Stretch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The angle brackets or chevrons at U+27E8 and U+27E9 are for mathematical use and Western languages, whereas U+3008 and U+3009 are for East Asian languages. The chevrons at U+2329 and U+232A are deprecated in favour of the U+3008 and U+3009 East Asian angle brackets. Unicode discourages their use for mathematics and in Western texts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unicode_Miscellaneous_Technical_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-Unicode_Miscellaneous_Technical-27">[27]</a></sup> because they are canonically equivalent to the CJK code points U+300x and thus likely to render as double-width symbols. The <i>less-than</i> and <i>greater-than</i> symbols are often used as replacements for chevrons.</p>
