<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Slave states and free states</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States" title="History of the United States">history of the United States of America</a>, a <b>slave state</b> was a <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> in which the practice of <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery</a> was legal, and a <b>free state</b> was one in which slavery was prohibited or being legally phased out. Historically, slavery had been practiced in all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_British_Empire" title="First British Empire">the British colonies</a>. The division between slave and free states began during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> (1775–1783). Slavery was a divisive issue and was the primary <a href="/wiki/Origins_of_the_American_Civil_War" title="Origins of the American Civil War">cause of the American Civil War</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Thirteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a>, ratified in 1865, abolished slavery throughout the United States, and the distinction ended.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_states_and_free_states&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_colonial_United_States" title="Slavery in the colonial United States">Slavery</a> was legal and practiced in each of the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> that became the US. During British colonization, the enslaved population expanded, primarily drawn from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Organized political and social movements to end slavery began in the mid-18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Painter_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Painter-2">[2]</a></sup> The sentiments of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> (1775-1783) and the equality evoked by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a> stood in contrast to the status of most black Americans. Despite this, thousands of black Americans fought against the British in hopes of a new order. Thousands also joined the British army, encouraged by British offers of freedom in exchange for military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Painter_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Painter-2">[2]</a></sup> (<i>See <a href="/wiki/Black_Patriot" title="Black Patriot">Black Patriot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Loyalist" title="Black Loyalist">Black Loyalist</a></i>).</p>
<p>In the 1770s, blacks throughout <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> began sending petitions to northern legislatures demanding freedom. Five of the Northern self-declared states adopted policies to at least gradually abolish slavery: <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> (1780), <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> (1783), <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> (1784). <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> abolished slavery in 1777, while it was still independent, and when it joined the United States as the 14th state in 1791 it was the first state to join untainted by slavery. These state jurisdictions thus enacted the first abolition laws in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By 1804 (including, New York (1799), New Jersey (1804)), all of the northern states had abolished slavery or set measures in place to gradually reduce it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Painter_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Painter-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson15_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson15-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the south, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> was created a slave state from <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> (1792), and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> was created a slave state from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> (1796). By 1804, before the creation of new states from the federal western territories, the number of slave and free states was eight each. In popular usage, the geographic divide between the slave and free states was called the <a href="/wiki/Mason%E2%80%93Dixon_line" title="Mason–Dixon line">Mason–Dixon line</a>.</p>
<p>The 1787, United States <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_(United_States)" title="Constitutional Convention (United States)">Constitutional Convention</a> debated slavery and for a time slavery was a major impediment to passage of the new constitution. As a compromise the institution was acknowledged though never mentioned directly in the constitution, as in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Fugitive_Slave_Clause" title="Fugitive Slave Clause">Fugitive Slave Clause</a>. In 1808, the United States <a href="/wiki/Act_Prohibiting_Importation_of_Slaves" title="Act Prohibiting Importation of Slaves">outlawed the international slave import trade</a> but the domestic trade in half the states continued.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Ordinance" title="Northwest Ordinance">Northwest Ordinance</a> of 1787, passed just before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Constitution" title="U.S. Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a> was ratified, prohibited slavery in the federal <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territory" title="Northwest Territory">Northwest Territory</a>. The southern boundary of the territory was the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, which was regarded as a westward extension of the Mason–Dixon line. The territory was generally settled by <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New Englanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> veterans granted land there. The states created from the territory – <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> (1803), <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> (1816), <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> (1818), <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> (1837), <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a> (1846), <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> (1848), and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> (1858) – were all free states.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, the British accepted as free all slaves who came into their hands, with no conditions as to military service such as had been made in <a href="/wiki/Dunmore%27s_Proclamation" title="Dunmore's Proclamation">Dunmore's Proclamation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a>. By the end of the War of 1812, the momentum for antislavery reform, state by state, appeared to run out of steam, with half of the states having already abolished slavery (Northeast), prohibited from the start (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a>) or committed to eliminating slavery, and half committed to continuing the institution indefinitely (South).</p>
<p>The potential for political conflict over slavery at a federal level made politicians concerned about the balance of power in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>, where each State was represented by two Senators. With an equal number of slave states and free states, the Senate was equally divided on issues important to the South. As the population of the free states began to outstrip the population of the slave states, leading to control of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> by free states, the Senate became the preoccupation of slave state politicians interested in maintaining a Congressional veto over federal policy in regard to slavery and other issues important to the South. As a result of this preoccupation, slave states and free states were often admitted into the Union in pairs to maintain the existing Senate balance between slave and free states.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Missouri_Compromise">Missouri Compromise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_states_and_free_states&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Missouri Compromise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Controversy over whether <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> should be admitted as a slave state, resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Compromise" title="Missouri Compromise">Missouri Compromise</a> of 1820, which specified that <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana Purchase</a> territory north of latitude 36° 30', which described most of Missouri's southern boundary, would be organized as free states and territory south of that line would be reserved for organization as slave states. As part of the compromise, the admission of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> (1820) as a free state was secured to balance Missouri's admission as a slave state (1820).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas_and_the_Mexican_Cession">Texas and the Mexican Cession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_states_and_free_states&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Texas and the Mexican Cession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Wilmot_Proviso" title="Wilmot Proviso">Wilmot Proviso</a></div>
<p>The admission of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> (1845) and the acquisition of the vast new <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Cession" title="Mexican Cession">Mexican Cession</a> territories (1848) after the <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican–American War</a> created further North-South conflict. Although the settled portion of Texas was an area rich in cotton plantations and dependent on slavery, the territory acquired in the Mountain West did not seem hospitable to cotton or slavery.</p>
<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/Compromise_of_1850" title="Compromise of 1850">Compromise of 1850</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> was admitted as a free state (1850), without a slave state pair. To avoid creating a free state majority in the Senate, California agreed to send one pro-slavery and one anti-slavery senator to Congress.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_battles">Last battles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_states_and_free_states&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Last battles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The difficulty of identifying territory that could be organized into additional slave states stalled the process of opening the western territories to settlement, while slave state politicians sought a solution, with efforts being made to acquire <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Ostend_Manifesto" title="Ostend Manifesto">Ostend Manifesto</a>, 1852) and to annex <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Walker_affair" title="Walker affair">Walker affair</a>, 1856–57), both to be slave states.</p>
<p>In 1854, the Missouri Compromise of 1820 was superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Kansas%E2%80%93Nebraska_Act" title="Kansas–Nebraska Act">Kansas–Nebraska Act</a>, which allowed white male settlers in the new territories to determine through <a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty_in_the_United_States" title="Popular sovereignty in the United States">popular sovereignty</a> whether they would allow slavery within each territory. The result was that pro- and anti-slavery elements flooded into Kansas with the goal of voting slavery up or down, leading to <a href="/wiki/Bleeding_Kansas" title="Bleeding Kansas">bloody fighting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> An effort was initiated to organize <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> for admission as a slave state, paired with <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, but the admission of Kansas as a slave state was blocked because of questions over the legitimacy of its slave state constitution. Anti-slavery settlers in "<a href="/wiki/Bleeding_Kansas" title="Bleeding Kansas">Bleeding Kansas</a>" in the 1850s were called <i><a href="/wiki/Free-Stater_(Kansas)" title="Free-Stater (Kansas)">Free-Soilers</a></i>, because they fought (successfully) to include Kansas in the Union as a free state in 1861.</p>
<p>When the admission of Minnesota proceeded unimpeded in 1858, the balance in the Senate was lost; a loss that was compounded by the subsequent admission of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> in 1859.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Virginia">West Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_states_and_free_states&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: West Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the Civil War, a <a href="/wiki/Restored_Government_of_Virginia" title="Restored Government of Virginia">Unionist government</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wheeling,_West_Virginia" title="Wheeling, West Virginia">Wheeling, Virginia</a>, presented a statehood bill to Congress in order to create a new state from 48 counties in western Virginia. The new state would eventually incorporate 50 counties. The issue of slavery in the new state delayed approval of the bill. In the Senate <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sumner" title="Charles Sumner">Charles Sumner</a> objected to the admission of a new slave state, while <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Wade" title="Benjamin Wade">Benjamin Wade</a> defended statehood as long as a gradual emancipation clause would be included in the new state constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Two senators represented the Unionist Virginia government, <a href="/wiki/John_S._Carlile" title="John S. Carlile">John S. Carlile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waitman_T._Willey" title="Waitman T. Willey">Waitman T. Willey</a>. Senator Carlile objected that Congress had no right to impose emancipation on <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, while Willey proposed a compromise amendment to the state constitution for gradual abolition. Sumner attempted to add his own amendment to the bill, which was defeated, and the statehood bill passed both houses of Congress with the addition of what became known as the Willey Amendment. President Lincoln signed the bill on December 31, 1862. Voters in western Virginia approved the Willey Amendment on March 26, 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>President Lincoln had issued the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a> on January 1, 1863, which exempted slave states loyal to the Union, as well as some territories occupied by Union forces within Confederate states, from emancipation. Two additional counties were added to West Virginia in late 1863, <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_County,_West_Virginia" title="Berkeley County, West Virginia">Berkeley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_West_Virginia" title="Jefferson County, West Virginia">Jefferson</a>. The slaves in Berkeley were also under exemption but not those in Jefferson County. As of the census of 1860, the 49 exempted counties held some 6000 slaves over 21 years of age who would not have been emancipated, about 40% of the total slave population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The terms of the Willey Amendment only freed children, at birth or as they came of age, and prohibited the importation of slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>West Virginia became the 35th state on June 20, 1863, and the last slave state admitted to the Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Eighteen months later, the West Virginia legislature completely abolished slavery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and also ratified the <a href="/wiki/Thirteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">13th Amendment</a> on February 3, 1865.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_slavery">End of slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_states_and_free_states&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: End of slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="784" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png/300px-USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png/450px-USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png/600px-USA_Map_1864_including_Civil_War_Divisions.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Division of states during the Civil War. <p>At the start of the Civil War, there were 34 states in the United States, 15 of which were slave states. 11 of these slave states declared their secession from the United States to form the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederacy</a>. The slave states that stayed in the Union were Maryland, Missouri, Delaware, and Kentucky, and were referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Border_states_(American_Civil_War)" title="Border states (American Civil War)">border states</a>. By the time the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a> was made in 1863 Tennessee was already in Union control. Accordingly, the Proclamation applied only to the 10 remaining Confederate states. Abolition of slavery also became a condition of the return of local rule in those states that had declared their secession.</p>
<p>The border states of Maryland (November 1864),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Missouri (January 1865),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> one of the Confederate states, Tennessee (January 1865),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the new state of West Virginia (February 1865),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> abolished slavery just prior to the end of the Civil War (May 1865). In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>, slavery was abolished in 1862.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, slavery persisted in Delaware,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Kentucky,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and 10 of the 11 of the former Confederate states, until the <a href="/wiki/Thirteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a> abolished slavery throughout the United States on December 6, 1865, ending the distinction between slave and free states. Ratifications continued, with the last states to formally ratify the 13th Amendment being Kentucky and Mississippi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

"By 1775, inspired by those 'self-evident' truths which were to be expressed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a>, a considerable number of colonists felt that the time had come to end slavery and give the free Negroes some fruits of liberty. This sentiment, added to economic considerations, led to the immediate or gradual abolition of slavery in six northern states, while there was a swelling flood of private manumissions in the South. Little actual gain was made by the free Negro even in this period, and by the turn of the century the downward trend had begun again. Thereafter the only important change in that trend before the Civil War was that after 1831 the decline in the status of the free Negro became more precipitate."</span></li>
