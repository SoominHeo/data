<p><b>Ryōji Noyori</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">野依 良治</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Noyori Ryōji</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born September 3, 1938)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>. He won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 2001. Noyori shared half of the prize with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_S._Knowles" title="William S. Knowles">William S. Knowles</a> for the study of <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Asymmetric hydrogenation">chirally catalyzed hydrogenations</a>; the second half of the prize went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K._Barry_Sharpless" title="K. Barry Sharpless">K. Barry Sharpless</a> for his study in chirally catalyzed oxidation reactions (<a href="/wiki/Sharpless_epoxidation" title="Sharpless epoxidation">Sharpless epoxidation</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_career">Education and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ry%C5%8Dji_Noyori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ryōji Noyori was born in <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe, Japan</a>. He became fascinated with chemistry at age twelve, after hearing a presentation on <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a>. He saw the power of chemistry as being the ability to "produce high value from almost nothing". He was a student at <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_University" title="Kyoto University">Kyoto University</a>, an instructor in the research group of Hitoshi Nozaki, and an associate professor at <a href="/wiki/Nagoya_University" title="Nagoya University">Nagoya University</a>. After postdoctoral work with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elias_J._Corey" title="Elias J. Corey">Elias J. Corey</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> he returned to Nagoya, becoming a full professor in 1972. He is still based at Nagoya, and served as president of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIKEN" title="RIKEN">RIKEN</a>, a multi-site national research initiative with an annual budget of $800 million, from 2003 to 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Noyori believes strongly in the power of <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green chemistry</a>; in a recent article he argues for the pursuit of "practical elegance in synthesis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In this article he states that "<i>our ability to devise straightforward and practical chemical syntheses is indispensable to the survival of our species.</i>" Elsewhere he has said that "<i>Research is for nations and mankind, not for researchers themselves.</i>" He encourages scientists to be politically active: "<i>Researchers must spur public opinions and government policies toward constructing the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable society</a> in the 21st century.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Noyori is currently a chairman of the Education Rebuilding Council, which was set up by Japan's PM <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a> after he came to power in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Noyori is most famous for <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Asymmetric hydrogenation">asymmetric hydrogenation</a> using as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" title="Complex (chemistry)">complexes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, particularly those based on the <a href="/wiki/BINAP" title="BINAP">BINAP</a> <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a>. (See <a href="/wiki/Noyori_asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Noyori asymmetric hydrogenation">Noyori asymmetric hydrogenation</a>) Asymmetric <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkene</a> in the presence of ((<i>S</i>)-<a href="/wiki/BINAP" title="BINAP">BINAP</a>)Ru(<a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">OAc</a>)<sub>2</sub> is used for the commercial production of enantiomerically pure (97% <a href="/wiki/Enantiomeric_excess" title="Enantiomeric excess">ee</a>) <a href="/wiki/Naproxen" title="Naproxen">naproxen</a>, used as an <a href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatory" title="Anti-inflammatory">anti-inflammatory</a> drug. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibacterial</a> agent <a href="/wiki/Levofloxacin" title="Levofloxacin">levofloxacin</a> is manufactured by asymmetric <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> in the presence of a Ru(II) BINAP halide complex.</p>
<p>He has also worked on other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetric_synthesis" title="Asymmetric synthesis">asymmetric processes</a>. Each year 3000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> (after new expansion) of <a href="/wiki/Menthol" title="Menthol">menthol</a> are produced (in 94% <a href="/wiki/Enantiomeric_excess" title="Enantiomeric excess">ee</a>) by <a href="/wiki/Takasago_International_Corporation" title="Takasago International Corporation">Takasago International Corporation</a>, using Noyori's method for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomerisation" title="Isomerisation">isomerisation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allyl" title="Allyl">allylic</a> <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>More recently with Philip G. Jessop, Nyori has developed an industrial process for the manufacture of <i>N,N</i>-<a href="/wiki/Dimethylformamide" title="Dimethylformamide">dimethylformamide</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethylamine" title="Dimethylamine">dimethylamine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">Ru</a><a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl</a><sub>2</sub>(P<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">Me</a><sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub> as catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ry%C5%8Dji_Noyori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Ryoji_Noyori_Prize" title="The Ryoji Noyori Prize">The Ryoji Noyori Prize</a> is named in his honour. In 2000 Noyori became Honorary Doctor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rennes_1" title="University of Rennes 1">University of Rennes 1</a>, where he taught in 1995,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and in 2005, he became Honorary Doctor at <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Munich" title="Technical University of Munich">Technical University of Munich</a> and <a href="/wiki/RWTH_Aachen_University" title="RWTH Aachen University">RWTH Aachen University</a>, Germany. Noyori was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_2005" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 2005">Foreign Member of the Royal Society (ForMemRS) in 2005</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formemrs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-formemrs-1">[1]</a></sup> He has also been awarded the <a href="/wiki/Asahi_Prize" title="Asahi Prize">Asahi Prize</a> in 1992, the <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron_Prize" title="Tetrahedron Prize">Tetrahedron Prize</a> in 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Cope_Award" title="Arthur C. Cope Award">Arthur C. Cope Award</a> in 1997, the <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Wolf Prize in Chemistry">Wolf Prize in Chemistry</a> in 2001 and the <a href="/wiki/Lomonosov_Gold_Medal" title="Lomonosov Gold Medal">Lomonosov Gold Medal</a> in 2009.</p>
