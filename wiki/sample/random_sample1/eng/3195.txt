<p>The <b>Neue Wache</b> (English: New Guardhouse) is a building in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It serves as the "Central Memorial of the Federal Republic of Germany for the Victims of War and Dictatorship". It is located on the north side of the <a href="/wiki/Unter_den_Linden" title="Unter den Linden">Unter den Linden</a> boulevard in the central <a href="/wiki/Mitte_(locality)" title="Mitte (locality)">Mitte</a> district. Dating from 1816, the Neue Wache was designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schinkel" title="Karl Friedrich Schinkel">Karl Friedrich Schinkel</a> and is a leading example of German <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival_architecture" title="Greek Revival architecture">Greek Revival architecture</a>. Originally built as a <a href="/wiki/Guardhouse" title="Guardhouse">guardhouse</a> for the troops of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">crown prince</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>, the building has been used as a war memorial since 1931.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neue_Wache&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William III of Prussia">Frederick William III of Prussia</a> ordered the construction of the Neue Wache as a guardhouse for the <a href="/wiki/Kronprinzenpalais" title="Kronprinzenpalais">Königliches Palais</a> (Royal Palace), his palace across the road, to replace the old Artillery Guardhouse. He commissioned Schinkel, the leading exponent of <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical architecture</a>, to design the building: this was Schinkel's first major commission in Berlin. The Neue Wache was inaugurated on 18 September 1818 by the Prussian <a href="/wiki/1st_(Emperor_Alexander)_Guards_Grenadiers" title="1st (Emperor Alexander) Guards Grenadiers">1st Guards Grenadiers</a> on occasion of the official visit of Tsar <a href="/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Russia" title="Alexander I of Russia">Alexander I of Russia</a>.</p>

<p>Located between the <a href="/wiki/Zeughaus" title="Zeughaus">Zeughaus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">Humboldt University</a>, the plain building is characterised by four massive corner <a href="/wiki/Avant-corps" title="Avant-corps">risalits</a> and a portico of <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric</a> columns, of the original Greek form, without bases. Schinkel wrote of his design: "The plan of this completely exposed building, free on all sides, is approximately the shape of a Roman castrum, thus the four sturdier corner towers and the inner courtyard." The statuary in the <a href="/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)" title="Tympanum (architecture)">tympanum</a> is intended as a memorial to Prussia's role in the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, whose final stage is known in Germany as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_campaign_(Napoleonic_Wars)" title="German campaign (Napoleonic Wars)">Wars of Liberation</a>. It shows <a href="/wiki/Nike_(mythology)" title="Nike (mythology)">Nike</a>, the goddess of victory, deciding a battle. The <a href="/wiki/Triglyph" title="Triglyph">triglyphs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guttae" title="Guttae">guttae</a> of the Doric order are omitted.</p>
<p>The building served as a royal guard house until the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and the fall of the monarchy in the <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" title="German Revolution of 1918–19">German Revolution of 1918–19</a>. In 1931 the architect <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Tessenow" title="Heinrich Tessenow">Heinrich Tessenow</a> was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Prussia" title="Free State of Prussia">Free State of Prussia</a> to redesign the building as a <a href="/wiki/War_memorial" title="War memorial">memorial</a> to commemorate those who died in the Great War. Tessenow converted the interior into a memorial hall centered around a black <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> block with an oak wreath designed by the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Gies" title="Ludwig Gies">Ludwig Gies</a>, situated under an <a href="/wiki/Oculus" title="Oculus">oculus</a> (circular skylight). The Neue Wache was then known as the "Memorial of the Prussian State Government". After the Nazi <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machtergreifung" title="Machtergreifung">Machtergreifung</a></i> the building played a vital role as the site of the annual <i><a href="/wiki/Volkstrauertag" title="Volkstrauertag">Heldengedenktag</a></i> celebrations held by the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a></i> armed forces. The Neue Wache was heavily damaged by <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Berlin_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Berlin in World War II">bombing</a> and artillery during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Berlin" title="Battle of Berlin">Battle of Berlin</a> in last months of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>

<p>After the war, the Mitte district was located within the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> sector of <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany" title="Allied-occupied Germany">Allied-occupied</a> Berlin, and from 1949 was part of <a href="/wiki/East_Berlin" title="East Berlin">East Berlin</a>, capital of the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a> (GDR). Prior to the 1951 <a href="/wiki/3rd_World_Festival_of_Youth_and_Students" title="3rd World Festival of Youth and Students">World Festival of Youth and Students</a>, the East German leader <a href="/wiki/Walter_Ulbricht" title="Walter Ulbricht">Walter Ulbricht</a> had two neoclassical <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> statues of <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_von_Scharnhorst" title="Gerhard von Scharnhorst">Gerhard von Scharnhorst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Freiherr_von_B%C3%BClow" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Freiherr von Bülow">Friedrich Wilhelm Bülow</a> by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Daniel_Rauch" title="Christian Daniel Rauch">Christian Daniel Rauch</a> removed from the sides of the portico (positioned on the south side of Unter den Linden since 2002).</p>

<p>From 1957 the <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communist</a> authorities had the Neue Wache rebuilt and reopened in 1960 as a "Memorial to the Victims of <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">Fascism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">Militarism</a>". In 1969, the 20th anniversary of the GDR, a glass prism structure with an <a href="/wiki/Eternal_flame" title="Eternal flame">eternal flame</a> was placed in the centre of the hall. The remains of an <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldier" title="Tomb of the Unknown Soldier">Unknown Soldier</a> and of a nameless <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Nazi concentration camp</a> victim were enshrined in the building. Two soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels_Guard_Regiment" title="Friedrich Engels Guard Regiment">Friedrich Engels Guard Regiment</a> served as permanent <a href="/wiki/Honor_guard" title="Honor guard">honor guards</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Guard_Mounting" title="Guard Mounting">Guard Mounting</a> ceremony was held every Wednesday and Saturday, becoming a major tourist attraction.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>, the Neue Wache was again rededicated in 1993, as the "Central <a href="/wiki/Memorial" title="Memorial">Memorial</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> for the Victims of War and Dictatorship." At the personal suggestion of Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a>, the GDR memorial piece was removed and replaced by an enlarged version of <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A4the_Kollwitz" title="Käthe Kollwitz">Käthe Kollwitz</a>'s sculpture <i>Mother with her Dead Son</i>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Piet%C3%A0" title="Pietà">pietà</a></i>-style sculpture is directly placed under the <a href="/wiki/Oculus" title="Oculus">oculus</a>, and so is exposed to the rain, snow and cold of the Berlin climate, symbolizing the suffering of civilians during World War II.</p>
