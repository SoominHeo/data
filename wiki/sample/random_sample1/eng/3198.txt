<p><i><b>Knowing</b></i> is a 2009 <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Thriller_film" title="Thriller film">thriller</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Proyas" title="Alex Proyas">Alex Proyas</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cage" title="Nicolas Cage">Nicolas Cage</a>. The project was originally attached to a number of directors under <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>, but it was placed in <a href="/wiki/Turnaround_(filmmaking)" title="Turnaround (filmmaking)">turnaround</a> and eventually picked up by <a href="/wiki/Escape_Artists" title="Escape Artists">Escape Artists</a>. Production was financially backed by <a href="/wiki/Summit_Entertainment" title="Summit Entertainment">Summit Entertainment</a>. <i>Knowing</i> was filmed in <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Studios_Melbourne" title="Docklands Studios Melbourne">Docklands Studios Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, using various locations to represent the film's <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>-area setting.</p>
<p>The film was released on March 20, 2009, in the United States. The DVD and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> media were released on July 7, 2009. <i>Knowing</i> met with mixed reviews, with praise for the acting performances, visual style and atmosphere, but criticism over some implausibilities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1959, student Lucinda Embry (<a href="/wiki/Lara_Robinson" title="Lara Robinson">Lara Robinson</a>) hears whispers as she stares at the Sun. When her class is chosen to contribute to the school's <a href="/wiki/Time_capsule" title="Time capsule">time capsule</a>, each child is asked to draw what they believe the future will look like. Lucinda writes a page of seemingly random numbers and adds it to her elementary school's time capsule, which is set to be opened in 50 years. Lucinda's teacher, Mrs. Taylor (<a href="/wiki/Danielle_Carter_(actress)" title="Danielle Carter (actress)">Danielle Carter</a>), calls for the pupils to finish but Lucinda continues until her teacher takes the page off her desk unfinished. Lucinda then goes missing after the time capsule is dedicated, and is found by her teacher in a utility closet scratching numbers into the door with her fingernails bleeding.</p>
<p>In 2009, Caleb Koestler (<a href="/wiki/Chandler_Canterbury" title="Chandler Canterbury">Chandler Canterbury</a>) is a pupil at the same elementary school. When the time capsule is opened, Caleb is supposed to read and write about some of the capsule's contents. He's given the page of numbers written by Lucinda. His widowed father John (<a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cage" title="Nicolas Cage">Nicolas Cage</a>), a professor of <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a> at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>, notices the numbers have a specific set of sequences, with some digits referring to the dates and death tolls of major disasters over the last 50 years, including 911012996, apparently representing the date ("9/11/01", in m/d/yy format) and death toll ("2996") of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9/11_attacks" title="9/11 attacks">9/11 attacks</a>. The last three sets of digits on the page are dated in the immediate future.</p>
<p>In the following days, a car drives by the family home with two strangers. They give Caleb a small smooth stone. Caleb later dreams of one of the strange men, who points to the window showing the world on fire with burning animals running out from a forest.</p>
<p>John witnesses a plane crash on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeway" title="Freeway">freeway</a> on the day that the paper had predicted that a disaster would next occur. He then learns that the remaining unexplained digits on the paper are the geographic coordinates of the location of each disaster predicted on the paper.</p>

<p>John tracks down Lucinda's daughter Diana (<a href="/wiki/Rose_Byrne" title="Rose Byrne">Rose Byrne</a>) and granddaughter Abby. Though initially apprehensive and scared, after seeing one of the number sets result in another disaster (a major <a href="/wiki/Derailment" title="Derailment">derailment</a> on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a>, which John had tried to prevent), Diana decides to help John. She says that her mother used to hear voices, and that the next (and also last) date in the document, October 19, was the day Lucinda always said Diana would die. Searching Lucinda's mobile home, they find pictures of the disasters she predicted, a copy of <a href="/wiki/Matth%C3%A4us_Merian" title="Matthäus Merian">Matthäus Merian</a>'s engraving of <a href="/wiki/Merkabah_mysticism" title="Merkabah mysticism">Ezekiel's "chariot vision"</a>, and a pile of small smooth stones near Lucinda's bed. The last number in the document appears at first to be "33", but they notice that it is really "EE" written backwards. They figure out that EE means "Everyone Else", representing an <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">Extinction Level Event</a> which no one will escape.</p>
<p>The next day, Abby shows John that she has coloured in the sun on the Ezekiel's "Chariot vision" engraving and John has a sudden realisation that this could be how the world will end. He rushes them to the <a href="/wiki/Haystack_Observatory" title="Haystack Observatory">MIT observatory</a>, where he discovers that a <a href="/wiki/Superflare" title="Superflare">massive solar flare</a> will soon reach <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, making it <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">uninhabitable</a>. John and Diana argue over what is best to do to prepare for the incoming solar flare. After the argument, Diana leaves with both children. As Diana stops for gas, an emergency broadcast is transmitted to alert the world of the solar flare. While Diana is on the phone with John, the strangers take the children. Diana chases after them but her car is hit by a truck. John, rushing to catch up with them, arrives just as Diana dies at exactly midnight of October 19 and finds the smooth, black stone in Diana's hand. He goes back to Lucinda's mobile home, finding the children and the strangers waiting in a dry river bed covered with the similar black stones. A space ship descends from the sky. John is refused entry and the ship departs with the children. A distant shot shows many similar space ships leaving Earth.</p>
<p>The next morning John drives to his estranged father's home. John and his family embrace as the flare sets the entire planet ablaze, destroying everything and killing all life still on the surface. Caleb and Abby are deposited by the beings on an Earth-like planet. In the final scene, they are seen running through a beautiful field of wheat towards a giant white tree which resembles the <a href="/wiki/Tree_of_life" title="Tree of life">Tree of Life</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001, novelist <a href="/wiki/Ryne_Douglas_Pearson" title="Ryne Douglas Pearson">Ryne Douglas Pearson</a> approached producers Todd Black and Jason Blumenthal with his idea for a film, where a <a href="/wiki/Time_capsule" title="Time capsule">time capsule</a> from the 1950s is opened revealing fulfilled prophecies, the last one of which ended with 'EE' - "everyone else". The producers liked the concept and bought his script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-all_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-all-3">[3]</a></sup> The project was set up at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>. Both <a href="/wiki/Rod_Lurie" title="Rod Lurie">Rod Lurie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Kelly_(director)" title="Richard Kelly (director)">Richard Kelly</a> were attached as directors, but the film eventually went into <a href="/wiki/Turnaround_(filmmaking)" title="Turnaround (filmmaking)">turnaround</a>. The project was picked up by the production company Escape Artists, and the script was rewritten by <a href="/wiki/Stiles_White" title="Stiles White">Stiles White</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juliet_Snowden" title="Juliet Snowden">Juliet Snowden</a>. Director <a href="/wiki/Alex_Proyas" title="Alex Proyas">Alex Proyas</a> was attached to direct the project in February 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Proyas said the aspect that attracted him the most was the "very different script" and the notion of people seeing the future and "how it shape their lives".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-all_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-all-3">[3]</a></sup> Summit Entertainment took on the responsibility to fully finance and distribute the film. Proyas and <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Hazeldine" title="Stuart Hazeldine">Stuart Hazeldine</a> rewrote the draft for production,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which began on March 25, 2008 in Melbourne, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The director hoped to emulate <i><a href="/wiki/The_Exorcist_(film)" title="The Exorcist (film)">The Exorcist</a></i> in melding "realism with a fantastical premise".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film is set primarily in the town of <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Massachusetts" title="Lexington, Massachusetts">Lexington</a> with some scenes set in the nearby cities of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>. However, it was shot in Australia, where director Proyas resides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-all_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-all-3">[3]</a></sup> Locations included the <a href="/wiki/Geelong_Ring_Road" title="Geelong Ring Road">Geelong Ring Road</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Museum" title="Melbourne Museum">Melbourne Museum</a>; "Cooinda", a residence in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Macedon,_Victoria" title="Mount Macedon, Victoria">Mount Macedon</a> which was the location for all of the "home and garden" scenes; and <a href="/wiki/Collins_Street,_Melbourne" title="Collins Street, Melbourne">Collins Street</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-night_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-night-1">[1]</a></sup> Filming also took place at <a href="/wiki/Camberwell_High_School" title="Camberwell High School">Camberwell High School</a>, which was converted into the fictional William Dawes Elementary, located in 1959 Lexington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Interior shots took place at the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Synchrotron" title="Australian Synchrotron">Australian Synchrotron</a> to represent an observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Filming also took place at the <a href="/wiki/Haystack_Observatory" title="Haystack Observatory">Haystack Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Westford,_Massachusetts" title="Westford, Massachusetts">Westford</a>, Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition to practical locations, filming also took place at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melbourne_Central_City_Studios" title="Melbourne Central City Studios">Melbourne Central City Studios</a> in Docklands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The plane crash, which was mostly shown in <a href="/wiki/Long_take" title="Long take">one take</a> in the film, was done in a nearly-finished freeway outside Melbourne, mixing practical effects and pieces of a plane with computer-generated elements. The scenographic rain led to the usage of a new gel for the flames so the fire would not be put out, and semi-permanent make-up to make them last the long shooting hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-all_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-all-3">[3]</a></sup> The solar flare destruction sequence is set in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, showing notable landmarks such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metlife_Building" title="Metlife Building">Metlife Building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> being completely obliterated as the flare spreads across the Earth's surface, destroying everything in its path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proyas used a <a href="/wiki/Red_Digital_Cinema_Camera_Company" title="Red Digital Cinema Camera Company">Red One</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4K_UHDTV" title="4K UHDTV">4K</a> digital camera, making the film the first time the director used digital cameras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He sought to capture a gritty and realistic look to the film, and his approach involved a continuous two-minute scene in which Cage's character sees a plane crash and attempts to rescue passengers. The scene was an arduous task, taking two days to set up and two days to shoot. Proyas explained the goal, "I did that specifically to not let the artifice of visual effects and all the cuts and stuff we can do, get in the way of the emotion of the scene."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Knowing</i> received mixed reviews. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reported that 34% of critics gave the film positive reactions based upon a sample of 179 critics with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_average" title="Weighted average">weighted average</a> score of 4.7/10. The site's consensus states: "<i>Knowing</i> has some interesting ideas and a couple good scenes, but it's weighted down by its absurd plot and over-seriousness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomatoes_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomatoes-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gave the film a score of 41% based on 27 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">A. O. Scott</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> gave the film a negative review and wrote, "If your intention is to make a brooding, hauntingly allegorical terror-thriller, it's probably not a good sign when spectacles of mass death and intimations of planetary destruction are met with hoots and giggles ... The draggy, lurching two hours of "Knowing" will make you long for the end of the world, even as you worry that there will not be time for all your questions to be answered."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i>, Peter Hartlaub called the film "an excitement for fans of Proyas" and "a surprisingly messy effort." He thought Nicolas Cage "borders on ridiculous here, in part because of a script that gives him little to do but freak out or act depressed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, Michael O'Sullivan thought the film was "creepy, at least for the first two-thirds or so, in a moderately satisfying, if predictable, way ... But the narrative corner into which this movie... paints itself is a simultaneously brilliant and exciting one. Well before the film neared its by turns dismal and ditzy conclusion, I found myself knowing—yet hardly able to believe—what was about to happen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Betsy Sharkey of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> found it to be "moody and sometimes ideologically provocative" and added, "<i>Knowing</i> has its grim moments—and by that I mean the sort of cringe- (or laugh-) inducing lines of dialogue that have haunted disaster films through the ages ... So visually arresting are the images that watching a deconstructing airliner or subway train becomes more mesmerising than horrifying."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> was enthusiastic, rating it four stars out of four and writing, "<i>Knowing</i> is among the best science-fiction films I've seen—frightening, suspenseful, intelligent and, when it needs to be, rather awesome"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebert2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebert2-26">[26]</a></sup> He continued, "With expert and confident storytelling, Proyas strings together events that keep tension at a high pitch all through the film. Even a few quiet, human moments have something coiling beneath. Pluck this movie, and it vibrates."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Ebert later listed it as the sixth best film of 2009.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> suggested <i>Knowing</i> was saved by its ending, concluding that "the film sticks to its apocalyptic guns with a spectacular and thoroughly unexpected finish."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philip_French" title="Philip French">Philip French</a>'s review in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> suggested the premise was "intriguing B-feature apocalypse, <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">determinism</a> versus <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a> stuff" and that the ending has something for everyone: "A chosen few will apparently be swept away by angels to a better place. If you're a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_fundamentalist" title="Christian fundamentalist">Christian fundamentalist</a> who believes that <a href="/wiki/Armageddon" title="Armageddon">Armageddon</a> is nigh, you'll have a family hug and wake up to be greeted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peter" title="St Peter">St Peter</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearly_Gates" title="Pearly Gates">Pearly Gates</a>. On the other hand, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darwinist" title="Darwinist">Darwinists</a> will be gratified to see <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a> and her stellar opposite numbers sock it to an unconcerned mankind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Knowing</i> was released in 3,332 theatres in the United States and Canada on March 20, 2009 and grossed $24,604,751 in its opening weekend,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-bom-30">[30]</a></sup> placing first at the box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> According to exit polling, 63% of the audience was 25 years old and up and evenly split between genders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On the weekend of March 17, 2009, <i>Knowing</i> ranked first in the international box office, grossing $9.8 million at 1,711 theatres in ten markets, including first with $3.55 million in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> As of July 26, 2009, the film had grossed $79,957,634 in the United States and Canada and $107,901,008 in other territories for a worldwide total of $187,858,642.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knowing_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knowing-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media_release">Home media release <span id="Home_media"></span><span id="DVD"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Home media release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Knowing</i> was released on DVD on July 7, 2009 opening at No.1 for the week, selling 773,000 DVD units for $12,508,192 in revenue. As per the latest figures, 1,521,797 DVD units have been sold, bringing in $22,968,367 in revenue. This does not include DVD/Blu-rentals or Blu-ray sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Litigation">Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Litigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 25, 2009, Global Findability filed a patent infringement lawsuit against <a href="/wiki/Summit_Entertainment" title="Summit Entertainment">Summit Entertainment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Escape_Artists" title="Escape Artists">Escape Artists</a> in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia, claiming that a geospatial entity object code was used in the film <i>Knowing</i> which infringed Patent <span class="citation patent"><a class="external text" href="https://worldwide.espacenet.com/textdoc?DB=EPODOC&amp;IDX=US7107286" rel="nofollow">US 7107286</a></span><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Apatent&amp;rft.number=7107286&amp;rft.cc=US&amp;rft.title="><span style="display: none;"> </span></span> Integrated information processing system for geospatial media .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The case was dismissed on January 10, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_controversy">Science controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowing_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Science controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Regarding the film's grounding in science, Director Alex Proyas said at a press conference, "The science was important. I wanted to make the movie credible. So of course we researched as much as we could and tried to give it as much authenticity as we could."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popularmechanics.com_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-popularmechanics.com-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ian O'Neill of Discovery News criticized the film's <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flare</a> plot line, pointing out that the most powerful solar flares could never incinerate Earthly cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Erin McCarthy of Popular Mechanics calls attention to the film's confusion of <a href="/wiki/Numerology" title="Numerology">numerology</a>, the occult's study of how numbers like dates of birth influence human affairs, with the ability of science to describe the world <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematically" title="Mathematically">mathematically</a> to make predictions about things like weather or create technology like cell phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popularmechanics.com_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-popularmechanics.com-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Steve Biodrowski of Cinefantastique refers to the film's approach as disappointingly "pseudo-scientific." He writes, "Cage plays an astronomer, and his discussions with a colleague hint that the film may actually grapple with the question of predicting the future, perhaps even offer a plausible theory. Unfortunately, this approach is abandoned as Koestler pursues the disasters, and the film eventually moves into a mystical approach."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Asked about his research for the role, Nicolas Cage stated, "I grew up with a professor, so that was all the research I ever needed." His father, <a href="/wiki/August_Coppola" title="August Coppola">August Coppola</a>, was a professor of <a href="/wiki/Comparative_literature" title="Comparative literature">comparative literature</a> at <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Long_Beach" title="California State University, Long Beach">Cal State Long Beach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Cage plays an astrophysicist at MIT in the film.</p>
