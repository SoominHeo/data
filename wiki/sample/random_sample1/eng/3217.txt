<p><b>Pucciniomycotina</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subdivision_(botany)" title="Subdivision (botany)">subdivision</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a> within the division <a href="/wiki/Basidiomycota" title="Basidiomycota">Basidiomycota</a>. The subdivision contains 9 <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">classes</a>, 20 <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">orders</a>, and 37 <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a>. Over 8400 species of Pucciniomycotina have been described - more than 8% of all described fungi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shell2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shell2011-4">[4]</a></sup> The subdivision is considered a sister group to <a href="/wiki/Ustilaginomycotina" title="Ustilaginomycotina">Ustilaginomycotina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agaricomycotina" title="Agaricomycotina">Agaricomycotina</a>, which may share the basal lineage of Basidiomycota, although this is uncertain due to low support for placement between the three groups. The group was known as Urediniomycetes until 2006, when it was elevated from a class to a subdivision and named after the largest order in the group, Puccinales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bauer2006_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bauer2006-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pucciniomycotina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pucciniomycotina have a diverse range of ecologies as insect parasites, mycoparasites, and orchid mycorrhiza; some have been detected in soil and water or asymptotic members living on leafs. Most are plant pathogens. Many Pucciniomycotina are <a href="/wiki/Rust_(fungus)" title="Rust (fungus)">rust fungi</a> and are placed in the order <a href="/wiki/Rust_(fungus)" title="Rust (fungus)">Puccinales</a> that contains roughly 7800 species(ca. 90% of the group).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aime2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aime2006-5">[5]</a></sup> Some members of the group are of economical importance as a pathogen on a wide range of commercial plants, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>. Pucciniomycotina is a cosmopolite and exists all over the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Park_219.E2.80.93239_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Park_219.E2.80.93239-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pucciniomycotina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a high morphological diversity in the Pucciniomycontina. The sporulating forms range from macrobasidiocarp to single-celled yeasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014-3">[3]</a></sup> The presence of simple septal pores unites Pucciniomycotina and distinguishes it from the sister groups. The predominance of <a href="/wiki/Mannose" title="Mannose">mannose</a> in the cell walls is also a uniting feature of the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_Cycle">Life Cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pucciniomycotina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life Cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some members are only known from their <a href="/wiki/Teleomorph,_anamorph_and_holomorph" title="Teleomorph, anamorph and holomorph">anamorphs</a>, and asexual stages predominate in most; in some species this is the only known form. A striking characteristic of Puccinomycotina is the unique developmental pattern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-M.C._Aime.2C_et_al.2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Rust Fungi or plant pathogenic members in Puccinales have the most complex life cycles known in the fungal kingdom, with five different spore stages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Studies have shown that Puccinales also has one the largest genomes in the fungi kingdom, and that genome size expansion may be common. This explains the complex life cycles within the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Park_219.E2.80.93239_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Park_219.E2.80.93239-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
