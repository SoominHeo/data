<p><i><b>Cinnamomum camphora</b></i> (commonly known as <b>camphor tree</b>, <b>camphorwood</b> or <b>camphor laurel</b>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> that grows up to 20â30Â m (66â98Â ft) tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FoC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FoC-1">[1]</a></sup> The leaves have a glossy, waxy appearance and smell of <a href="/wiki/Camphor" title="Camphor">camphor</a> when crushed. In spring, it produces bright green foliage with masses of small white <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a>. It produces clusters of black, berry-like <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> around 1Â cm (0.39Â in) in diameter. Its pale <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> is very rough and fissured vertically.</p>
<p><i>Cinnamomum camphora</i> is native to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, and has been introduced to many other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FoC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FoC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Japan, where the tree is called <i>kusunoki</i>, five camphor trees are known with a trunk circumference above 20 m, with the largest tree (<i>Kamou no Ohkusu</i>) reaching the circumference of 24.22 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_constituents">Chemical constituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinnamomum_camphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical constituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Camphor laurel contains <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> in all plant parts, and the wood and leaves are <a href="/wiki/Steam_distillation" title="Steam distillation">steam distilled</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_oils" title="Essential oils">essential oils</a>. Camphor laurel has six different chemical variants called <a href="/wiki/Chemotype" title="Chemotype">chemotypes</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Camphor" title="Camphor">camphor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linalool" title="Linalool">linalool</a>, 1,8-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cineole" title="Cineole">cineole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nerolidol" title="Nerolidol">nerolidol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safrole" title="Safrole">safrole</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Borneol" title="Borneol">borneol</a>. In China, field workers avoid mixing chemotypes when harvesting by their odour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The cineole fraction of camphor laurel is used in China to manufacture fake "<a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_oil" title="Eucalyptus oil">Eucalyptus oil</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The chemical variants (or chemotypes) seem dependent upon the country of origin of the tree. The tree is native to China, Japan, and Taiwan. It has been introduced to the other countries where it has been found, and the chemical variants are identifiable by country. e.g., <i>C. camphora</i> grown in Taiwan and Japan is normally very high in linalool, often between 80 and 85%. In India and Sri Lanka, the high camphor variety/chemotype remains dominant. <i>C. camphora</i> grown in Madagascar, though, is high in 1,8 cineole (averaging between 40 and 50%). The essential oil from the Madagascar trees is commercially known as <i>ravintsara</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwrr_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cwrr-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camphor">Camphor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinnamomum_camphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Camphor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Camphor" title="Camphor">Camphor</a></div>
<p>Camphor is a white crystalline substance, obtained from the tree <i>C. camphora</i>. Camphor has been used for many centuries as a culinary spice, a component of incense, and as a medicine. It is also an <a href="/wiki/Insect_repellent" title="Insect repellent">insect repellent</a> and a flea-killing substance.</p>
<p><i>C. camphora</i> is native to <a href="/wiki/Jeju_Province" title="Jeju Province">Jeju</a> off the coast of Korea, Taiwan, southern Japan, southeast China, and <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>, where it is also cultivated for camphor and timber production. The production and shipment of camphor, in a solid, waxy form, was a major industry in Taiwan prior to and during the Japanese colonial era (1895â1945). It was used medicinally and was also an important ingredient in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smokeless_gunpowder" title="Smokeless gunpowder">smokeless gunpowder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">celluloid</a>. Primitive stills were set up in the mountainous areas in which the tree is usually found. The wood was chipped; these chips were steamed in a retort, allowing the camphor to crystallize on the inside of a crystallization box, after the vapour had passed through a cooling chamber. It was then scraped off and packed out to government-run factories for processing and sale. Camphor was one of the most lucrative of several important government monopolies under the Japanese.</p>

<p>Camphor laurel was introduced to Australia in 1822 as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornamental_tree" title="Ornamental tree">ornamental tree</a> for use in gardens and public parks. It has become a <a href="/wiki/Noxious_weed" title="Noxious weed">noxious weed</a> throughout <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> and central to northern <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, where it is suited to the wet, subtropical climate. However, the tree provides hollows quickly in younger trees, whereas natives can take hundreds of years to develop hollows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Its massive and spreading root systems disrupt urban drainage and sewer systems and degrade river banks. Its leaves have a very high <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> content, which damages water quality and freshwater fish habitats when they fall into streams and rivers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The camphor content of the leaf litter helps prevent other plants from germinating successfully, helping to ensure the camphor's success against any potentially competing vegetation, and the seeds are attractive to birds and pass intact through the digestive system, ensuring rapid distribution. Camphor laurel invades rainforests and pastures, and also competes against <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a> trees, certain species of which are the sole food source of <a href="/wiki/Koala" title="Koala">koalas</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_United_States">In the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinnamomum_camphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a> around 1875, <i>C. camphora</i> has become <a href="/wiki/Naturalisation_(biology)" title="Naturalisation (biology)">naturalized</a> in portions of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrcs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrcs-8">[8]</a></sup> It has been declared a category I <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insect_herbivores">Insect herbivores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinnamomum_camphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Insect herbivores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Australia, two native <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> insects, the <a href="/wiki/Chaetocneme_porphyropis" title="Chaetocneme porphyropis">purple brown-eye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chaetocneme_beata" title="Chaetocneme beata">common red-eye</a>, larval stages feed on camphor despite it being an introduced plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinnamomum_camphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A huge camphor tree features in the movie <a href="/wiki/My_Neighbor_Totoro" title="My Neighbor Totoro">My Neighbor Totoro</a>.</p>
