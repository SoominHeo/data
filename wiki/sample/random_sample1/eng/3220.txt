<p>The <b>mung bean</b> (<i>Vigna radiata</i>), alternatively known as the <b>moong bean</b>, <b>green gram</b>, or <b>mung</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Sanskrit ‡§Æ‡•Å‡§¶‡•ç‡§ó / mudga, is a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">legume family</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brief_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-brief-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vocab_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-vocab-4">[4]</a></sup> The mung bean is mainly cultivated in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. It is used as an ingredient in both savory and sweet dishes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mung_bean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English word <i>mung</i> is derived from the Hindi word ‡§Æ‡•Ç‡§Ç‡§ó moong derived from the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word ‡§Æ‡•Å‡§¶‡•ç‡§ó (mudga).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mung_bean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are one of many <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> recently moved from the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Phaseolus" title="Phaseolus">Phaseolus</a></i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Vigna" title="Vigna">Vigna</a></i>, and is still often seen incorrectly cited as <i>Phaseolus aureus</i> or <i>Phaseolus radiatus</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mung_bean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mung beans are commonly used in various cuisines across Asia.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Whole_beans_and_mung_bean_paste">Whole beans and mung bean paste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mung_bean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Whole beans and mung bean paste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whole cooked mung beans are generally prepared from dried beans by boiling until they are soft. Mung beans are light yellow in colour when their skins are removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brief_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-brief-3">[3]</a></sup> Mung bean paste can be made by dehulling, cooking, and pulverizing the beans to a dry paste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brief_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-brief-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although whole mung beans are also occasionally used in Indian cuisine, beans without skins are more commonly used; but in Kerala &amp; Tamil Nadu, whole mung beans are commonly boiled to make a dry preparation often served with rice gruel (<a href="/wiki/Congee" title="Congee"><i>kanji</i></a> ‡Æï‡Æû‡Øç‡Æû‡Æø). Dehulled mung beans can also be used in a similar fashion as whole beans for the purpose of making sweet soups. Mung beans in some regional <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">cuisines of India</a> are stripped of their outer coats to make mung <i><a href="/wiki/Dal" title="Dal">dal</a></i>. In <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telangana" title="Telangana">Telangana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>, steamed whole beans are seasoned with spices and fresh grated coconut in a preparation called <i>sundal</i> <i>‡Æö‡ØÅ‡Æ£‡Øç‡Æü‡Æ≤‡Øç</i>. In south and north Indian states, mung beans are also eaten as pancakes. They are soaked in water for six to 12 hours (the higher the temperature, the lesser soaking time). Then they are ground into fine paste along with ginger and salt. Then pancakes are made on a very hot griddle. These are usually eaten for breakfast. This provides high quality protein that is rare in most Indian regional cuisines. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pongal" title="Pongal">Pongal</a></i> or <i>kichdi</i> is another recipe that is made with rice and mung beans without skin. In <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, it is commonly used to make the <i>parippu</i> preparation in the <a href="/wiki/Travancore" title="Travancore">Travancore</a> region (unlike <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochin" title="Cochin">Cochin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malabar_(Northern_Kerala)" title="Malabar (Northern Kerala)">Malabar</a>, where <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toor_dal" title="Toor dal">toor dal</a></i>, <i>tuvara parippu</i>,<i>‡Æ§‡ØÅ‡Æµ‡Æ∞‡Æ™‡Øç‡Æ™‡Æ∞‡ØÅ‡Æ™‡Øç‡Æ™‡ØÅ</i> is used). It is also used, with coconut milk and <a href="/wiki/Jaggery" title="Jaggery">jaggery</a>, to make a type of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payasam" title="Payasam">payasam</a></i>. Soaked Moong ( both full or split ) called Hesaru in <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada">Kannada</a> is one of ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Kosambari" title="Kosambari">Kosambari</a> a salad.</p>
<p>In Chinese cuisine, whole mung beans are used to make a <i><a href="/wiki/Tong_sui" title="Tong sui">t√°ngshu«ê</a></i>, or dessert, otherwise literally translated, "sugar water", called <i>l«úd√≤u t√°ngshu«ê</i>, which is served either warm or chilled. In Indonesia, they are made into a popular dessert snack called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Es_kacang_hijau" title="Es kacang hijau">es kacang hijau</a></i>, which has the consistency of a <a href="/wiki/Porridge" title="Porridge">porridge</a>. The beans are cooked with sugar, coconut milk, and a little ginger.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, dehulled mung beans and mung bean paste are made into <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a> or frozen <a href="/wiki/Ice_pop" title="Ice pop">ice pops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brief_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-brief-3">[3]</a></sup> Mung bean paste is used as a common filling for Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mooncakes" title="Mooncakes">mooncakes</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_China" title="East China">East China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brief_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-brief-3">[3]</a></sup> Also in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the boiled and shelled beans are used as filling in glutinous rice dumplings eaten during the dragon boat festival (Á´ØÂçàÁØÄ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brief_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-brief-3">[3]</a></sup> The beans may also be cooked until soft, blended into a liquid, sweetened, and served as a beverage, popular in many parts of China.</p>
<p>In the Philippines, <i>ginis√°ng mongg√≥</i> (saut√©ed mung bean stew), also known as <i>mongg√≥ guisado</i> or <i>balatong</i>, is a savoury stew of whole mung beans with prawns or fish. It is traditionally served on Fridays of <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a>, when the majority <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_the_Philippines" title="Catholic Church in the Philippines">Roman Catholic</a> Filipinos traditionally abstain from meat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Variants of <i>ginis√°ng mongg√≥</i> may also be made with chicken or pork.</p>
<p>Mung bean paste is also a common filling of pastries known as <i><a href="/wiki/Bakpia" title="Bakpia">hopia</a></i> (or <i>bakpia</i>) popular in Indonesia, the Philippines and further afield in <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> (where it is known as <i>black eye cake</i>) and originating from southern China.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bean_sprouts">Bean sprouts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mung_bean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bean sprouts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mung bean are germinated by leaving them in water for four hours of daytime light and spending the rest of the day in the dark. Mung bean sprouts can be grown under artificial light for four hours over the period of a week. They are usually simply called "bean sprouts". However, when bean sprouts are called for in recipes, it generally refers to mung bean or soybean sprouts.</p>
<p>Mung bean sprouts are <a href="/wiki/Stir_frying" title="Stir frying">stir-fried</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese</a> vegetable accompaniment to a meal, usually with <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scallion" title="Scallion">spring onions</a>, or pieces of salted dried fish to add flavour. Uncooked bean sprouts are used in filling for <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_cuisine" title="Vietnamese cuisine">Vietnamese</a> <a href="/wiki/Spring_roll" title="Spring roll">spring rolls</a>, as well as a garnish for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph%E1%BB%9F" title="Ph·ªü">ph·ªü</a></i>. They are a major ingredient in a variety of <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_cuisine" title="Malaysian cuisine">Malaysian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peranakan_cuisine" title="Peranakan cuisine">Peranakan cuisine</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Char_kway_teow" title="Char kway teow">char kway teow</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hokkien_mee" title="Hokkien mee">hokkien mee</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mee_rebus" title="Mee rebus">mee rebus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rojak#Mamak_rojak" title="Rojak">pasembor</a></i>. In Korea, slightly cooked mung bean sprouts, called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sukjunamul" title="Sukjunamul">sukjunamul</a></i> (hangul: ÏàôÏ£ºÎÇòÎ¨º), are often served as a side dish. They are <a href="/wiki/Blanching_(cooking)" title="Blanching (cooking)">blanched</a> (placed into boiling water for less than a minute), immediately cooled in cold water, and mixed with sesame oil, garlic, salt, and often other ingredients. In the Philippines, mung bean sprouts are made into <i><a href="/wiki/Lumpia" title="Lumpia">lumpia</a></i> rolls called <i>lumpiang togue</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> the food are often used as fillings like <i>Tahu Isi</i> (stuffed tofu) and complimentary ingredient in many cookings such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rawon" title="Rawon">rawon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Soto_(food)" title="Soto (food)">soto</a></i>.</p>
<p>In northern China and Korea, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> sprouts, called <i>kongnamul</i> (hangul: ÏΩ©ÎÇòÎ¨º) in Korean, are more widely used in a variety of dishes. Don't use the 'blue sprouts' since they contain a little bit of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">Hydrogen cyanide</a>, like potatosprouts do.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Starch">Starch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mung_bean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Starch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mung bean <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, which is extracted from ground mung beans, is used to make transparent <a href="/wiki/Cellophane_noodles" title="Cellophane noodles">cellophane noodles</a> (also known as bean thread noodles, bean threads, glass noodles, <i>fensi</i> (Á≤âÁµ≤), <i>tung hoon</i> (ÂÜ¨Á≤â), <i>mi·∫øn</i>, <i>b√∫n t√†u</i>, or <i>b√∫n t√†o</i>). Cellophane noodles become soft and slippery when they are soaked in hot water. A variation of cellophane noodles, called mung bean sheets or green bean sheets, are also available. In Korea, a jelly called <i><a href="/wiki/Nokdumuk" title="Nokdumuk">nokdumuk</a></i> (hangul: ÎÖπÎëêÎ¨µ; also called <i>cheongpomuk</i>; hangul: Ï≤≠Ìè¨Î¨µ) is made from mung bean starch; a similar jelly, colored yellow with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Gardenia" title="Gardenia">gardenia</a> coloring, is called <i><a href="/wiki/Hwangpomuk" title="Hwangpomuk">hwangpomuk</a></i> (hangul: Ìô©Ìè¨Î¨µ). In northern China, mung bean jelly is called <i><a href="/wiki/Liangfen" title="Liangfen">liangfen</a></i> (Ê∂ºÁ≤â, meaning chilled bean jelly), which is very popular food during summer. <a href="/wiki/Jidou_liangfen" title="Jidou liangfen">Jidou liangfen</a> is another flavor of mung bean jelly food in Yunnan, in southern China.</p>
<p>Mung batter is used to make crepes named <i><a href="/wiki/Pesarattu" title="Pesarattu">pesarattu</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>, India and pancakes named <i><a href="/wiki/Bindaetteok" title="Bindaetteok">Bindaetteok</a></i> in Korea.</p>

<p>The mung bean was domesticated in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Persia</a> (Iran), where its progenitor (<i>Vigna radiata</i> subspecies <i>sublobata</i>) occurs wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aob.oxfordjournals.org_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-aob.oxfordjournals.org-7">[7]</a></sup> Archaeology has turned up carbonized mung beans in many sites in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Areas with early finds include the eastern zone of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harappan_Civilisation" title="Harappan Civilisation">Harappan</a> civilization in Punjab and Haryana, where finds date back about 4500 years, and South India in the modern state of <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a> where finds date back more than 4000 years. Some scholars therefore infer two separate domestications in the northwest and south of India. In South India there is evidence for evolution of larger-seeded mung beans 3500 to 3000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aob.oxfordjournals.org_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-aob.oxfordjournals.org-7">[7]</a></sup> By about 3500 years ago mung beans were widely cultivated throughout India. Cultivated mung beans later spread from India to China and Southeast Asia. Archaeobotanical research at the site of Khao Sam Kaeo in southern Thailand indicates that mung beans had arrived in Thailand by at least 2200 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Finds on <a href="/wiki/Pemba_Island" title="Pemba Island">Pemba Island</a> indicate that during the era of Swahili trade, in the 9th or 10th century, mung beans also came to be cultivated in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
