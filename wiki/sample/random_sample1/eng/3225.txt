<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Logical positivism</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Logical positivism</b> and <b>logical empiricism</b>, which together formed <b>neopositivism</b>, was a movement in <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">Western philosophy</a> that sought to legitimize philosophical discourse by placing it on a basis shared with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical_sciences" title="Empirical sciences">empirical sciences</a>' best examples, such as Einstein's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">general theory of relativity</a>. Its central thesis was <a href="/wiki/Verificationism" title="Verificationism">verificationism</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">theory of knowledge</a> which asserted that only statements verifiable through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">empirical</a> observation are <i>cognitively meaningful</i>. Efforts to convert philosophy to this new <i>scientific philosophy</i> were intended to prevent confusion rooted in unclear language and unverifiable claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxiv_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxiv-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Circle_(philosophy)" title="Berlin Circle (philosophy)">Berlin Circle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Circle" title="Vienna Circle">Vienna Circle</a>—groups of philosophers, scientists, and mathematicians in Berlin and in Vienna—propounded logical <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">positivism</a> starting in the late 1920s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Logical positivists culled from <a href="/wiki/Tractatus_Logico-Philosophicus" title="Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus">Ludwig Wittgenstein's early philosophy of language</a> the verifiability principle or criterion of meaningfulness. As in <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mach" title="Ernst Mach">Ernst Mach</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Phenomenalism" title="Phenomenalism">phenomenalism</a>, whereby the mind can know only actual or potential sensory experience, verificationists took all sciences' basic content to be only sensory experience. And some influence came from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percy_Bridgman" title="Percy Bridgman">Percy Bridgman</a>'s musings that others proclaimed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operationalism" title="Operationalism">operationalism</a>, whereby a physical theory is understood by what laboratory procedures scientists perform to test its predictions. In verificationism, only the <i>verifiable</i> was scientific, and thus meaningful (or <i>cognitively meaningful</i>), whereas the unverifiable, being unscientific, was meaningless "pseudostatements" (just <i>emotively meaningful</i>). Unscientific discourse, as in ethics and metaphysics, would be unfit for discourse by philosophers, newly tasked to organize knowledge, not develop new knowledge.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Logical positivism is sometimes stereotyped as forbidding talk of <a href="/wiki/Unobservable" title="Unobservable">unobservables</a>, such as microscopic entities or such notions as causality and general principles, but that is an exaggeration. Rather, most neopositivists viewed talk of unobservables as <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphorical</a> or elliptical: direct observations phrased abstractly or indirectly. So <i>theoretical terms</i> would garner meaning from <i>observational terms</i> via <i>correspondence rules</i>, and thereby <i>theoretical laws</i> would be reduced to <i>empirical laws</i>. Via <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Logicism" title="Logicism">logicism</a>, reducing mathematics to logic, physics' mathematical formulas would be converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolic_logic" title="Symbolic logic">symbolic logic</a>. And via Russell's <a href="/wiki/Logical_atomism" title="Logical atomism">logical atomism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">ordinary language</a> would break into discrete units of meaning. <a href="/wiki/Rational_reconstruction" title="Rational reconstruction">Rational reconstruction</a>, then, would convert ordinary statements into standardized equivalents, all networked and united by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_syntax" title="Logical syntax">logical syntax</a>. A scientific theory would be stated with its method of verification, whereby a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_calculus" title="Logical calculus">logical calculus</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operationalism" title="Operationalism">empirical operation</a> could <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deductive_inference" title="Deductive inference">verify</a> its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Truth-value" title="Truth-value">falsity or truth</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late 1930s, logical positivists fled Germany and Austria for Britain and United States. By then, many had replaced Mach's phenomenalism with <a href="/wiki/Otto_Neurath" title="Otto Neurath">Otto Neurath</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Physicalism" title="Physicalism">physicalism</a>, whereby science's content is not actual or potential sensations, but instead is entities publicly observable. And <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a>, who had sparked logical positivism in the Vienna Circle, had sought to replace <i>verification</i> with simply <i>confirmation</i>. With <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>'s close in 1945, logical positivism became milder, <i>logical empiricism</i>, led largely by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Hempel" title="Carl Gustav Hempel">Carl Hempel</a>, in America, who expounded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covering_law_model" title="Covering law model">covering law model</a> of scientific explanation. Logical positivism became a major underpinning of <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> and dominated <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">Anglosphere</a> philosophy, including <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a>, while influencing sciences, but especially social sciences, into the 1960s. Yet the movement failed to resolve its central problems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith1986_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith1986-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunge1996_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunge1996-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and its doctrines were increasingly criticized, most trenchantly by <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">W. V. O. Quine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwood_Russell_Hanson" title="Norwood Russell Hanson">Norwood Hanson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Raimund_Popper" title="Karl Raimund Popper">Karl Popper</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Samuel_Kuhn" title="Thomas Samuel Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Hempel" title="Carl Gustav Hempel">Carl Hempel</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roots">Roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Tractatus_Logico-Philosophicus" title="Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus">Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus</a></i>, by the young <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a>, introduced the view of philosophy as "critique of language", offering the possibility of a theoretically principled distinction of intelligible versus nonsensical discourse. <i>Tractatus</i> adhered to a <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_theory_of_truth" title="Correspondence theory of truth">correspondence theory of truth</a> (versus a <a href="/wiki/Coherence_theory_of_truth" title="Coherence theory of truth">coherence theory of truth</a>). Wittgenstein's influence also shows in some versions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verifiability_principle" title="Verifiability principle">verifiability principle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In tractarian doctrine, truths of logic are <a href="/wiki/Tautology_(logic)" title="Tautology (logic)">tautologies</a>, a view widely accepted by logical positivists who were also influenced by Wittgenstein's interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> although, according to Neurath, some logical positivists found <i>Tractatus</i> to contain too much metaphysics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logicism">Logicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Logicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Gottlob Frege</a> began the program of reducing mathematics to logic, continued it with <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a>, but lost interest in this <a href="/wiki/Logicism" title="Logicism">logicism</a>, and Russell continued it with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead" title="Alfred North Whitehead">Alfred North Whitehead</a> in their monumental <i><a href="/wiki/Principia_Mathematica" title="Principia Mathematica">Principia Mathematica</a></i>, inspiring some of the more mathematical logical posivists, such as <a href="/wiki/Hans_Hahn_(mathematician)" title="Hans Hahn (mathematician)">Hans Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hintikka_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hintikka-9">[9]</a></sup> (Carnap's early anti-metaphysical works employed Russell's <a href="/wiki/Type_theory" title="Type theory">theory of types</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Carnap envisioned a universal language that could reconstruct mathematics and thereby encode physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hintikka_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hintikka-9">[9]</a></sup> Yet <a href="/wiki/Kurt_G%C3%B6del" title="Kurt Gödel">Kurt Gödel</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incompleteness_theorem" title="Incompleteness theorem">incompleteness theorem</a> showed this impossible except in trivial cases, and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Tarski" title="Alfred Tarski">Alfred Tarski</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undefinability_theorem" title="Undefinability theorem">undefinability theorem</a> shattered all hopes of reducing mathematics to logic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hintikka_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hintikka-9">[9]</a></sup> Thus, a universal language failed to stem from Carnap's 1934 work <i>Logische Syntax der Sprache</i> (<i>Logical Syntax of Language</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hintikka_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hintikka-9">[9]</a></sup> Still, some logical positivists, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Hempel" title="Carl Hempel">Carl Hempel</a>, continued support of logicism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hintikka_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hintikka-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Empiricism">Empiricism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Empiricism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Germany, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegelian_metaphysics" title="Hegelian metaphysics">Hegelian metaphysics</a> was a dominant movement, and Hegelian successors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F_H_Bradley" title="F H Bradley">F H Bradley</a> explained reality by postulating metaphysical entities lacking empirical basis, drawing reaction in the form of positivism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe.2C_Frederick_1999_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe.2C_Frederick_1999-11">[11]</a></sup> Starting in the late 19th century, there was "back to Kant" movement. <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mach" title="Ernst Mach">Ernst Mach</a>'s positivism and <a href="/wiki/Phenomenalism" title="Phenomenalism">phenomenalism</a> were a major influence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vienna">Vienna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vienna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Circle" title="Vienna Circle">Vienna Circle</a>, gathering around <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">University of Vienna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_Central" title="Café Central">Café Central</a>, was led principally by <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Schlick" title="Moritz Schlick">Moritz Schlick</a>. Schlick had held a <a href="/wiki/Neo-Kantianism" title="Neo-Kantianism">neo-Kantian</a> position, but later converted, via Carnap's 1928 book <i>Der logische Aufbau der Welt</i>—that is, <i>The Logical Structure of the World</i>—which became Vienna Circle's "bible", <i>Aufbau</i>. A 1929 pamphlet written by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Neurath" title="Otto Neurath">Otto Neurath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Hahn_(mathematician)" title="Hans Hahn (mathematician)">Hans Hahn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a> summarized the Vienna Circle's positions. Another member of Vienna Circle to later prove very influential was <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Hempel" title="Carl Gustav Hempel">Carl Hempel</a>. A friendly but tenacious critic of the Circle was <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a>, whom <a href="/wiki/Otto_Neurath" title="Otto Neurath">Neurath</a> nicknamed the "Official Opposition".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Carnap</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Circle" title="Vienna Circle">Vienna Circle</a> members, including <a href="/wiki/Hans_Hahn_(mathematician)" title="Hans Hahn (mathematician)">Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_Neurath" title="Otto Neurath">Neurath</a>, saw need for a weaker criterion of meaningfulness than verifiability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> A radical "left" wing—led by Neurath and Carnap—began the program of "liberalization of empiricism", and they also emphasized <a href="/wiki/Fallibilism" title="Fallibilism">fallibilism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a>, which latter Carnap even suggested as empiricism's basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> A conservative "right" wing—led by <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Schlick" title="Moritz Schlick">Schlick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Waismann" title="Friedrich Waismann">Waismann</a>—rejected both the liberalization of empiricism and the epistemological nonfoundationalism of a move from <a href="/wiki/Phenomenalism" title="Phenomenalism">phenomenalism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Physicalism" title="Physicalism">physicalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> As Neurath and somewhat Carnap posed science toward social reform, the split in Vienna Circle also reflected political views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Berlin">Berlin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Berlin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Circle_(philosophy)" title="Berlin Circle (philosophy)">Berlin Circle</a> was led principally by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Reichenbach" title="Hans Reichenbach">Hans Reichenbach</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivals">Rivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Schlick" title="Moritz Schlick">Moritz Schlick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a> had been influenced by and sought to define logical positivism versus the neo-Kantianism of <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Cassirer" title="Ernst Cassirer">Ernst Cassirer</a>—the then leading figure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marburg_school" title="Marburg school">Marburg school</a>, so called—and against <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Edmund Husserl</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>. Logical positivists especially opposed <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Martin Heidegger</a>'s obscure metaphysics, the epitome of what logical positivism rejected. In the early 1930s, Carnap debated Heidegger over "metaphysical pseudosentences".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup> Despite its revolutionary aims, logical positivism was but one view among many vying within Europe, and logical positivists initially spoke their language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Export">Export</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Export">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the movement's first emissary to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Schlick" title="Moritz Schlick">Moritz Schlick</a> visited <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> in 1929, yet otherwise remained in Vienna and was murdered at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">University</a>, reportedly by a deranged student, in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup> That year, a British attendee at some Vienna Circle meetings since 1933, <a href="/wiki/A._J._Ayer" title="A. J. Ayer">A J Ayer</a> saw his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language,_Truth_and_Logic" title="Language, Truth and Logic">Language, Truth and Logic</a></i>, written in English, import logical positivism to the <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">Anglosphere</a>. By then, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> political party's 1933 rise to power in Germany had triggered flight of intellectuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup> In exile in England, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Neurath" title="Otto Neurath">Otto Neurath</a> died in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Reichenbach" title="Hans Reichenbach">Hans Reichenbach</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Hempel" title="Carl Hempel">Carl Hempel</a>—Carnap's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prot%C3%A9g%C3%A9" title="Protégé">protégé</a> who had studied in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> with Reichenbach—settled permanently in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup> Upon Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Austria" title="Annexation of Austria">annexation of Austria</a> in 1938, remaining logical positivists, many of whom were also Jewish, were targeted and continued flight. Logical positivism thus became dominant in the Anglosphere.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytic.2Fsynthetic_gap">Analytic/synthetic gap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Analytic/synthetic gap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Concerning <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">reality</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Logical_truth" title="Logical truth">necessary</a> is a state true in all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possible_worlds" title="Possible worlds">possible worlds</a>—mere <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_validity" title="Logical validity">logical validity</a>—whereas the <a href="/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy)" title="Contingency (philosophy)">contingent</a> hinges on the way the particular world is. Concerning <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">knowledge</a>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_priori_knowledge" title="A priori knowledge">a priori</a></i> is knowable before or without, whereas the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_posteriori" title="A posteriori">a posteriori</a></i> is knowable only after or through, relevant experience. Concerning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propositions" title="Propositions">statements</a>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic-synthetic_distinction" title="Analytic-synthetic distinction">analytic</a></i> is true via terms' <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">arrangement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">meanings</a>, thus a <a href="/wiki/Tautology_(logic)" title="Tautology (logic)">tautology</a>—true by logical necessity but uninformative about the world—whereas the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic-synthetic_distinction" title="Analytic-synthetic distinction">synthetic</a></i> adds reference to a state of facts, a <a href="/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy)" title="Contingency (philosophy)">contingency</a>.</p>
<p>In 1739, <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">Hume</a> cast a <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_fork" title="Hume's fork">fork</a> aggressively dividing "relations of ideas" from "matters of fact and real existence", such that all truths are of one type or the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flew_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flew-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By Hume's fork, truths by relations among ideas (abstract) all align on one side (analytic, necessary, <i>a priori</i>), whereas truths by states of actualities (concrete) always align on the other side (synthetic, contingent, <i>a posteriori</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flew_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flew-14">[14]</a></sup> At any treatises containing neither, Hume orders, "Commit it then to the flames, for it can contain nothing but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophistry" title="Sophistry">sophistry</a> and illusion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flew_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Flew-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus awakened from "dogmatic slumber", <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant</a> quested to answer Hume's challenge—but by explaining how <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a> is possible. Eventually, in his <a href="/wiki/Critique_of_Pure_Reason" title="Critique of Pure Reason">1781 work</a>, Kant crossed the tines of Hume's fork to identify another range of truths by necessity—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_a_priori" title="Synthetic a priori">synthetic <i>a priori</i></a>, statements claiming states of facts but known true before experience—by arriving at <a href="/wiki/Transcendental_idealism" title="Transcendental idealism">transcendental idealism</a>, attributing the mind a constructive role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenomena" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a> by arranging <a href="/wiki/Sense_data" title="Sense data">sense data</a> into the very experience <i>space</i>, <i>time</i>, and <i>substance</i>. Thus, Kant saved <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton's law of universal gravitation">Newton's law of universal gravitation</a> from Hume's <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_induction" title="Problem of induction">problem of induction</a> by finding uniformity of nature to be <i>a priori</i> knowledge. Logical positivists rejected Kant's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_a_priori" title="Synthetic a priori">synthethic <i>a priori</i></a>, and staked <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_fork" title="Hume's fork">Hume's fork</a>, whereby a statement is either analytic and <i>a priori</i> (thus <a href="/wiki/Logical_truth" title="Logical truth">necessary</a> and verifiable logically) or synthetic and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_posteriori" title="A posteriori">a posteriori</a></i> (thus <a href="/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy)" title="Contingency (philosophy)">contingent</a> and verifiable empirically).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flew_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Flew-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation.2Ftheory_gap">Observation/theory gap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Observation/theory gap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early, most logical positivists proposed that all knowledge is based on logical inference from simple "protocol sentences" grounded in observable facts. In the 1936 and 1937 papers "Testability and meaning", individual terms replace sentences as the units of meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> Further, theoretical terms no longer need to acquire meaning by explicit definition from observational terms: the connection may be indirect, through a system of implicit definitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> (Carnap also provides an important, pioneering discussion of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Disposition_predicate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Disposition predicate (page does not exist)">disposition predicates</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_meaningfulness">Cognitive meaningfulness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cognitive meaningfulness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Verification">Verification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Verification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The logical positivists' initial stance was that a statement is "cognitively meaningful" only if some finite procedure conclusively determines its truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> By this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verifiability_principle" title="Verifiability principle">verifiability principle</a>, only statements verifiable either by their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic_proposition" title="Analytic proposition">analyticity</a> or by empiricism were <i>cognitively meaningful</i>. <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">Metaphysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a>, as well as much of <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> failed this criterion, and so were found <i>cognitively meaningless</i>. Moritz Schlick, however, did not view ethical or aesthetic statements as <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(philosophy_of_language)" title="Meaning (philosophy of language)">cognitively meaningless</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>Cognitive meaningfulness</i> was variously defined: having a <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth value</a>; corresponding to a possible state of affairs; naming a proposition; intelligible or understandable as are scientific statements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">Ethics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> were subjective preferences, while <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a> contained "pseudostatements", neither true nor false. This meaningfulness was cognitive, although other types of meaningfulness—for instance, emotive, expressive, or figurative—occurred in metaphysical discourse, dismissed from further review. Thus, logical positivism indirectly asserted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hume%27s_law" title="Hume's law">Hume's law</a>, the principle that <i>is</i> statements cannot justify <i>ought</i> statements, but are separated by an unbridgeable gap. <a href="/wiki/A._J._Ayer" title="A. J. Ayer">A J Ayer</a>'s 1936 book asserted an extreme variant—the <a href="/wiki/Emotivism" title="Emotivism">boo/hooray doctrine</a>—whereby all evaluative judgments are but emotional reactions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Confirmation">Confirmation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Confirmation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In an important pair of papers in 1936 and 1937, "Testability and meaning", Carnap replaced <i>verification</i> with <i>confirmation</i>, on the view that although universal laws cannot be verified they can be confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> Later, Carnap employed abundant logical and mathematical methods in researching inductive logic while seeking to provide and account of probability as "degree of confirmation", but was never able to formulate a model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEP-Carnap_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEP-Carnap-19">[19]</a></sup> In Carnap's inductive logic, every universal law's degree of confirmation is always zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEP-Carnap_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEP-Carnap-19">[19]</a></sup> In any event, the precise formulation of what came to be called the "criterion of cognitive significance" took three decades (Hempel 1950, Carnap 1956, Carnap 1961).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Hempel" title="Carl Hempel">Carl Hempel</a> became a major critic within the logical positivism movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep-hempel_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-sep-hempel-20">[20]</a></sup> Hempel elucidated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paradox_of_the_ravens" title="Paradox of the ravens">paradox of confirmation</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Weak_verification">Weak verification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Weak verification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The second edition of <a href="/wiki/A._J._Ayer" title="A. J. Ayer">A J Ayer</a>'s book arrived in 1946, and discerned <i>strong</i> versus <i>weak</i> forms of verification. Ayer concluded, "A proposition is said to be verifiable, in the strong sense of the term, if, and only if, its truth could be conclusively established by experience", but is verifiable in the weak sense "if it is possible for experience to render it probable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ayer-p50_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ayer-p50-21">[21]</a></sup> And yet, "no proposition, other than a <a href="/wiki/Tautology_(logic)" title="Tautology (logic)">tautology</a>, can possibly be anything more than a probable <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ayer-p50_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ayer-p50-21">[21]</a></sup> Thus, all are open to weak verification.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_science">Philosophy of science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Philosophy of science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Upon the global defeat of <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>, and the removal from philosophy of rivals for radical reform—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marburg_school" title="Marburg school">Marburg</a> neo-Kantianism, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Husserlian</a> phenomenology, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Heidegger</a>'s "existential hermeneutics"—and while hosted in the climate of American <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatism</a> and commonsense empiricism, the neopositivists shed much of their earlier, revolutionary zeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxiv_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxiv-1">[1]</a></sup> No longer crusading to revise traditional philosophy into a new <i>scientific philosophy</i>, they became respectable members of a new philosophy subdiscipline, <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxiv_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxiv-1">[1]</a></sup> Receiving support from <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Nagel" title="Ernest Nagel">Ernest Nagel</a>, logical empiricists were especially influential in the social sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p546_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p546-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">Comtean positivism</a> had viewed science as <i>description</i>, whereas the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_positivists" title="Logical positivists">logical positivists</a> posed science as <i>explanation</i>, perhaps to better realize the envisioned <a href="/wiki/Unity_of_science" title="Unity of science">unity of science</a> by covering not only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_science" title="Fundamental science">fundamental science</a>—that is, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_interactions" title="Fundamental interactions">fundamental physics</a>—but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_science" title="Special science">special sciences</a>, too, for instance <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woodward-Background_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woodward-Background-23">[23]</a></sup> The most widely accepted concept of scientific explanation, held even by neopositivist critic <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a>, was the <a href="/wiki/Deductive-nomological_model" title="Deductive-nomological model">deductive-nomological model</a> (DN model).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woodward-introduction_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woodward-introduction-24">[24]</a></sup> Yet DN model received its greatest explication by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Hempel" title="Carl Gustav Hempel">Carl Hempel</a>, first in his 1942 article "The function of general laws in history", and more explicitly with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Oppenheim" title="Paul Oppenheim">Paul Oppenheim</a> in their 1948 article "Studies in the logic of explanation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woodward-introduction_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Woodward-introduction-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In DN model, the stated phenomenon to be explained is the <i>explanandum</i>—which can be an event, <a href="/wiki/Scientific_law" title="Scientific law">law</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">theory</a>—whereas premises stated to explain it are the <i>explanans</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe-25">[25]</a></sup> Explanans must be true or highly confirmed, contain at least one law, and entail the explanandum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe-25">[25]</a></sup> Thus, given initial conditions <i>C<sub>1</sub>, C<sub>2</sub> . . . C<sub>n</sub></i> plus general laws <i>L<sub>1</sub>, L<sub>2</sub> . . . L<sub>n</sub></i>, event <i>E</i> is a deductive consequence and scientifically explained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe-25">[25]</a></sup> In DN model, a law is an unrestricted generalization by conditional proposition—<i>If A, then B</i>—and has empirical content testable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> (Differing from a merely true regularity—for instance, <i>George always carries only $1 bills in his wallet</i>—a law suggests what <i>must</i> be true,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and is consequent of a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">scientific theory</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axiomatic</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">Humean</a> empiricist view that humans observe sequence of events, not cause and effect—as causality and causal mechanisms are unobservable—DN model neglects causality beyond mere <a href="/wiki/Constant_conjunction" title="Constant conjunction">constant conjunction</a>, first event <i>A</i> and then always event <i>B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woodward-Background_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Woodward-Background-23">[23]</a></sup> Hempel's explication of DN model held <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_laws" title="Natural laws">natural laws</a>—empirically confirmed regularities—as satisfactory and, if formulated realistically, approximating causal explanation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe-25">[25]</a></sup> In later articles, Hempel defended DN model and proposed a probabilistic explanation, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Inductive-statistical_model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Inductive-statistical model (page does not exist)">inductive-statistical model</a> (IS model).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe-25">[25]</a></sup> DN model and IS model together form <i>covering law model</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe-25">[25]</a></sup> as named by a critic, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Dray" title="William Dray">William Dray</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> (Derivation of statistical laws from other statistical laws goes to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Deductive-statistical_model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Deductive-statistical model (page does not exist)">deductive-statistical model</a> (DS model).)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glennan_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glennan-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Georg_Henrik_von_Wright" title="Georg Henrik von Wright">Georg Henrik von Wright</a>, another critic, named it <i>subsumption theory</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riedel_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riedel-31">[31]</a></sup> fitting the ambition of theory reduction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unity_of_science">Unity of science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Unity of science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Logical positivists were generally committed to "<a href="/wiki/Unified_Science" title="Unified Science">Unified Science</a>", and sought a common language or, in Neurath's phrase, a "universal slang" whereby which all scientific propositions could be expressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The adequacy of proposals or fragments of proposals for such a language was often asserted on the basis of various "reductions" or "explications" of the terms of one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_science" title="Special science">special science</a> to the terms of another, putatively more fundamental. Sometimes these reductions consisted of set-theoretic manipulations of a few logically primitive concepts (as in Carnap's <i>Logical Structure of the World</i> (1928)). Sometimes, these reductions consisted of allegedly analytic or <i>a priori</i> deductive relationships (as in Carnap's "Testability and meaning"). A number of publications over a period of thirty years would attempt to elucidate this concept.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_reduction">Theory reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Theory reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As in <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">Comptean positivism</a>'s envisioned <a href="/wiki/Unity_of_science" title="Unity of science">unity of science</a>, neopositivists aimed to network all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_science" title="Special science">special sciences</a> through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covering_law_model" title="Covering law model">covering law model of scientific explanation</a>. And ultimately, by supplying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_condition" title="Boundary condition">boundary conditions</a> and supplying bridge laws within the covering law model, all the special sciences' laws would reduce to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_interactions" title="Fundamental interactions">fundamental physics</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_science" title="Fundamental science">fundamental science</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics">Critics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Critics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-technical" role="presentation">

<p>After the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>'s close in 1945, key tenets of logical positivism, including its atomistic philosophy of science, the verifiability principle, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hume%27s_law" title="Hume's law">fact/value gap</a>, drew escalated criticism. It was clear that empirical claims cannot be verified to be universally true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarkar2005_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-Sarkar2005-12">[12]</a></sup> Thus, as initially stated, the verifiability criterion made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_proposition" title="Universal proposition">universal statements</a> meaningless, and even made statements beyond empiricism for technological but not conceptual reasons meaningless, which would pose significant problems for science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep-hempel_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-sep-hempel-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep-problem-of-induction_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sep-problem-of-induction-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep-vienna-circle_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-sep-vienna-circle-34">[34]</a></sup> These problems were recognized within the movement, which hosted attempted solutions—Carnap's move to <i>confirmation</i>, Ayer's acceptance of <i>weak verification</i>—but the program drew sustained criticism from a number of directions by the 1950s. Even philosophers disagreeing among themselves on which direction general <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a> ought to take, as well as on <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a>, agreed that the logical empiricist program was untenable, and it became viewed as self-contradictory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putnam1985_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam1985-35">[35]</a></sup> The verifiability criterion of meaning was itself unverified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putnam1985_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam1985-35">[35]</a></sup> Notable critics were <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Goodman" title="Nelson Goodman">Nelson Goodman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">Willard Van Orman Quine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwood_Russell_Hanson" title="Norwood Russell Hanson">Norwood Hanson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Raimund_Popper" title="Karl Raimund Popper">Karl Popper</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Samuel_Kuhn" title="Thomas Samuel Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._L._Austin" title="J. L. Austin">J L Austin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Strawson" title="Peter Strawson">Peter Strawson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilary_Putnam" title="Hilary Putnam">Hilary Putnam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rorty" title="Richard Rorty">Richard Rorty</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quine">Quine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Quine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although quite empiricist, American logician <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">Willard Van Orman Quine</a> published the 1951 paper <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Dogmas_of_Empiricism" title="Two Dogmas of Empiricism">Two Dogmas of Empiricism</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> which challenged conventional empiricist presumptions. Quine attacked the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic-synthetic_distinction" title="Analytic-synthetic distinction">analytic/synthetic division</a>, which the verificationist program had been hinged upon in order to entail, by consequence of <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_fork" title="Hume's fork">Hume's fork</a>, both <a href="/wiki/Logical_truth" title="Logical truth">necessity</a> and apriocity. Quine's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological_relativity" title="Ontological relativity">ontological relativity</a> explained that every term in any statement has its meaning contingent on a vast network of knowledge and belief, the speaker's conception of the entire world. Quine later proposed <a href="/wiki/Naturalized_epistemology" title="Naturalized epistemology">naturalized epistemology</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hanson">Hanson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Hanson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1958, <a href="/wiki/Norwood_Russell_Hanson" title="Norwood Russell Hanson">Norwood Hanson</a>'s <i>Patterns of Discovery</i> undermined the division of <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a> versus <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p527_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p527-37">[37]</a></sup> as one can predict, collect, prioritize, and assess data only via some horizon of expectation set by a theory. Thus, any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dataset" title="Dataset">dataset</a>—the direct observations, the scientific facts—is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory-laden" title="Theory-laden">laden with theory</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popper">Popper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Popper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An early, tenacious critic was <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a> whose 1934 book <i>Logik der Forschung</i>, arriving in English in 1959 as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Logic_of_Scientific_Discovery" title="The Logic of Scientific Discovery">The Logic of Scientific Discovery</a></i>, directly answered verificationism. Popper heeded the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_induction" title="Problem of induction">problem of induction</a> as rendering empirical verification logically impossible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> And the <a href="/wiki/Deductive_fallacy" title="Deductive fallacy">deductive fallacy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Affirming_the_consequent" title="Affirming the consequent">affirming the consequent</a> reveals any phenomenon's capacity to host over one logically possible explanation. Accepting <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> as <a href="/wiki/Hypothetico-deductive_model" title="Hypothetico-deductive model">hypotheticodeduction</a>, whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argument_form" title="Argument form">inference form</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denying_the_consequent" title="Denying the consequent">denying the consequent</a>, Popper finds scientific method unable to proceed without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falsifiable" title="Falsifiable">falsifiable</a> predictions. Popper thus identifies <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">falsifiability</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Problem_of_demarcation" title="Problem of demarcation">demarcate</a> not <i>meaningful</i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Meaningless_statement" title="Meaningless statement">meaningless</a></i> but simply <i>scientific</i> from <i>unscientific</i>—a label not in itself unfavorable.</p>
<p>Popper finds virtue in <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, required to develop new scientific theories. And an unfalsifiable—thus unscientific, perhaps metaphysical—concept in one era can later, through evolving knowledge or technology, become falsifiable, thus scientific. Popper also found science's quest for truth to rest on values. Popper disparages the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudoscientific" title="Pseudoscientific">pseudoscientific</a></i>, which occurs when an unscientific theory is proclaimed true and coupled with seemingly scientific method by "testing" the unfalsifiable theory—whose predictions are confirmed by necessity—or when a scientific theory's falsifiable predictions are strongly falsified but the theory is persistently protected by "immunizing stratagems", such as the appendage of <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a></i> clauses saving the theory or the recourse to increasingly speculative hypotheses shielding the theory.</p>
<p>Popper's <i>scientific</i> epistemology is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falsificationism" title="Falsificationism">falsificationism</a>, which finds that no number, degree, and variety of empirical successes can either verify or confirm scientific theory. Falsificationism finds science's aim as <i>corroboration</i> of scientific theory, which strives for <a href="/wiki/Scientific_realism" title="Scientific realism">scientific realism</a> but accepts the maximal status of strongly corroborated <a href="/wiki/Verisimilitude" title="Verisimilitude">verisimilitude</a> ("truthlikeness"). Explicitly denying the positivist view that all knowledge is scientific, Popper developed the <i>general</i> epistemology <a href="/wiki/Critical_rationalism" title="Critical rationalism">critical rationalism</a>, which finds human knowledge to evolve by <i>conjectures and refutations</i>. Popper thus acknowledged the value of the positivist movement, driving evolution of human understanding, but claimed that he had "killed positivism".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kuhn">Kuhn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Kuhn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With his landmark, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Structure_of_Scientific_Revolutions" title="The Structure of Scientific Revolutions">The Structure of Scientific Revolutions</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Samuel_Kuhn" title="Thomas Samuel Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a> critically destabilized the verificationist program, which was presumed to call for <a href="/wiki/Foundationalism" title="Foundationalism">foundationalism</a>. (Actually, even in the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Neurath" title="Otto Neurath">Otto Neurath</a> had argued for nonfoundationalism via <a href="/wiki/Coherentism" title="Coherentism">coherentism</a> by likening science to a boat (<a href="/wiki/Neurathian_bootstrap" title="Neurathian bootstrap">Neurath's boat</a>) that scientists must rebuild at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONPSP_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ONPSP-39">[39]</a></sup>) Although Kuhn's thesis itself was attacked even by opponents of neopositivism, in the 1970 postscript to <i>Structure</i>, Kuhn asserted, at least, that there was no <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a> to science—and, on that, even most of Kuhn's critics agreed.</p>
<p>Powerful and persuasive, Kuhn's book, unlike the vocabulary and symbols of logic's <a href="/wiki/Formal_language" title="Formal language">formal language</a>, was written in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a> open to the layperson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p526_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p526-40">[40]</a></sup> Ironically, Kuhn's book was first published in a volume of <i>Encyclopedia of Unified Science</i>—a project begun by logical positivists—and some sense unified science, indeed, but by bringing it into the realm of historical and social assessment, rather than fitting it to the model of physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p526_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p526-40">[40]</a></sup> Kuhn's ideas were rapidly adopted by scholars in disciplines well outside natural sciences,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p526_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p526-40">[40]</a></sup> and, as logical empiricists were extremely influential in the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p546_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p546-22">[22]</a></sup> ushered academia into <a href="/wiki/Postpositivism" title="Postpositivism">postpositivism</a> or postempiricism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p526_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p526-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Putnam">Putnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Putnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "<a href="/wiki/Received_view_of_theories" title="Received view of theories">received view</a>" operates on the <i>correspondence rule</i> that states, "The observational terms are taken as referring to specified phenomena or phenomenal properties, and the only interpretation given to the theoretical terms is their explicit definition provided by the correspondence rules".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suppe.2C_Frederick_1999_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suppe.2C_Frederick_1999-11">[11]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Hilary_Putnam" title="Hilary Putnam">Hilary Putnam</a>, a former student of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Reichenbach" title="Hans Reichenbach">Reichenbach</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Carnap</a>, the dichotomy of observational terms versus theoretical terms introduced a problem within scientific discussion that was nonexistent until this dichotomy was stated by logical positivists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Putnam's four objections:</p>
<ol>
<li>Something is referred to as "observational" if it is observable directly with our senses. Then an observation term cannot be applied to something unobservable. If this is the case, there are no observation terms.</li>
<li>With Carnap's classification, some unobservable terms are not even theoretical and belong to neither observation terms nor theoretical terms. Some theoretical terms refer primarily to observation terms.</li>
<li>Reports of observation terms frequently contain theoretical terms.</li>
<li>A scientific theory may not contain any theoretical terms (an example of this is Darwin's original theory of evolution).</li>
</ol>
<p>Putnam also alleged that positivism was actually a form of <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">metaphysical idealism</a> by its rejecting scientific theory's ability to garner knowledge about nature's unobservable aspects. With his "no miracles" argument, posed in 1974, Putnam asserted <a href="/wiki/Scientific_realism" title="Scientific realism">scientific realism</a>, the stance that science achieves true—or approximately true—knowledge of the world as it exists independently of humans' sensory experience. In this, Putnam opposed not only the positivism but other <a href="/wiki/Instrumentalism" title="Instrumentalism">instrumentalism</a>—whereby scientific theory as but a human tool to predict human observations—filling the void left by positivism's decline.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrospect">Retrospect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_positivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Retrospect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the late 1960s, logical positivism had clearly run its course.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fotionarticle_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fotionarticle-42">[42]</a></sup> Interviewed in the late 1970s, <a href="/wiki/A._J._Ayer" title="A. J. Ayer">A J Ayer</a> supposed that "the most important" defect "was that nearly all of it was false".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Routledge_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Routledge-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Although logical positivism tends to be recalled as a pillar of <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">scientism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahl-etal-p180_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stahl-etal-p180-45">[45]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Hempel" title="Carl Hempel">Carl Hempel</a> was key in establishing the philosophy subdiscipline <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup> where <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kuhn" title="Thomas Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a> brought in the era <a href="/wiki/Postpositivism" title="Postpositivism">postpositivism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novick-p526_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-Novick-p526-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Passmore" title="John Passmore">John Passmore</a> found logical positivism to be "dead, or as dead as a philosophical movement ever becomes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Routledge_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Routledge-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Logical positivism's fall reopened debate over the metaphysical merit of scientific theory, whether it can offer knowledge of the world beyond human experience (<a href="/wiki/Scientific_realism" title="Scientific realism">scientific realism</a>) versus whether it is but a human tool to predict human experience (<a href="/wiki/Instrumentalism" title="Instrumentalism">instrumentalism</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Meanwhile, it became popular among philosophers to rehash the faults and failures of logical positivism without investigation of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-p1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-p1-48">[48]</a></sup> Thereby, logical positivism has been generally misrepresented, sometimes severely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-p2_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-p2-49">[49]</a></sup> Arguing for their own views, often framed versus logical positivism, many philosophers have reduced logical positivism to simplisms and stereotypes, especially the notion of logical positivism as a type of <a href="/wiki/Foundationalism" title="Foundationalism">foundationalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-p2_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-p2-49">[49]</a></sup> In any event, the movement helped anchor <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">Anglosphere</a>, and returned Britain to <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricism</a>. Without the logical positivists, who have been tremendously influential outside philosophy, especially in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and social <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sciences" title="Sciences">sciences</a>, intellectual life of the 20th century would be unrecognizable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman-pxii_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-pxii-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-sep-hempel-20"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-sep-hempel_20-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-sep-hempel_20-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Fetzer, James (2012). Edward N. Zalta, ed. <a class="external text" href="http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/hempel/#SciRea" rel="nofollow">"Carl Hempel"</a>. <i>The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy</i> (Summer 2012 ed.). <q>It would fall to Hempel to become perhaps the most astute critic of that movement and to contribute to its refinement as logical empiricism... Hempel himself attained a certain degree of prominence as a critic of this movement... The analytic/synthetic distinction and the observational/theoretical distinction were tied together by the <i>verifiability criterion of meaningfulness</i>... By this standard, sentences that are non-analytic but also non-verifiable, including various theological or metaphysical assertions concerning God or The Absolute, qualify as cognitively meaningless. This was viewed as a desirable result. But, as Hempel would demonstrate, its scope was far too sweeping, since it also rendered meaningless the distinctively scientific assertions made by laws and theories... The analytic/synthetic distinction took a decided hit when the noted logician, Willard van Orman Quine, published "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1953), challenging its adequacy... While the analytic/synthetic distinction appears to be justifiable in modeling important properties of languages, the observational/theoretical distinction does not fare equally well. Within logical positivism, observation language was assumed to consist of names and predicates whose applicability or not can be ascertained, under suitable conditions, by means of direct observation... Karl Popper (1965, 1968), however, would carry the argument in a different direction by looking at the ontic nature of properties... Hempel (1950, 1951), meanwhile, demonstrated that the verifiability criterion could not be sustained. Since it restricts empirical knowledge to observation sentences and their deductive consequences, scientific theories are reduced to logical constructions from observables. In a series of studies about cognitive significance and empirical testability, he demonstrated that the verifiability criterion implies that existential generalizations are meaningful, but that universal generalizations are not, even though they include general laws, the principal objects of scientific discovery. Hypotheses about relative frequencies in finite sequences are meaningful, but hypotheses concerning limits in infinite sequences are not. The verifiability criterion thus imposed a standard that was too strong to accommodate the characteristic claims of science and was not justifiable... Both theoretical and dispositional predicates, which refer to non-observables, posed serious problems for the positivist position, since the verifiability criterion implies they must be reducible to observables or are empirically meaningless... The need to dismantle the verifiability criterion of meaningfulness together with the demise of the observational/theoretical distinction meant that logical positivism no longer represented a rationally defensible position. At least two of its defining tenets had been shown to be without merit. Since most philosophers believed that Quine had shown the analytic/synthetic distinction was also untenable, moreover, many concluded that the enterprise had been a total failure. Among the important benefits of Hempel's critique, however, was the production of more general and flexible criteria of cognitive significance... Hempel suggested multiple criteria for assessing the cognitive significance of different theoretical systems, where significance is not categorical but rather a matter of degree... The elegance of Hempel's study laid to rest any lingering aspirations for simple criteria of cognitive significance and signaled the demise of logical positivism as a philosophical movement. Precisely what remained, however, was in doubt. Presumably, anyone who rejected one or more of the three principles defining positivism—the analytic/synthetic distinction, the observational/theoretical distinction, and the verifiability criterion of significance—was not a logical positivist. The precise outlines of its philosophical successor, which would be known as "logical empiricism", were not entirely evident. Perhaps this study came the closest to defining its intellectual core. Those who accepted Hempel's four criteria and viewed cognitive significance as a matter of degree were members, at least in spirit. But some new problems were beginning to surface with respect to Hempel's covering-law explication of explanation and old problems remained from his studies of induction, the most remarkable of which was known as "the paradox of confirmation".</q></cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ALogical+positivism&amp;rft.atitle=Carl+Hempel&amp;rft.aufirst=James&amp;rft.aulast=Fetzer&amp;rft.date=2012&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fplato.stanford.edu%2Fentries%2Fhempel%2F%23SciRea&amp;rft.jtitle=The+Stanford+Encyclopedia+of+Philosophy&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></span></li>

<li id="cite_note-sep-vienna-circle-34"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-sep-vienna-circle_34-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Uebel, Thomas (2008). In the first place, this liberalization meant the accommodation of universally quantified statements and the return, as it were, to salient aspects of Carnap's 1928 conception. Everybody had noted that the Wittgensteinian verificationist criterion rendered universally quantified statements meaningless. Schlick (1931) thus followed Wittgenstein's own suggestion to treat them instead as representing rules for the formation of verifiable singular statements. (His abandonment of conclusive verifiability is indicated only in Schlick 1936a.) A second element that began to do so soon was the recognition of the problem of the irreducibility of disposition terms to observation terms... A third element was that disagreement arose as to whether the in-principle verifiability or support turned on what was merely logically possible or on what was nomologically possible, as a matter of physical law etc. A fourth element, finally, was that differences emerged as to whether the criterion of significance was to apply to all languages or whether it was to apply primarily to constructed, formal languages. Schlick retained the focus on logical possibility and natural languages throughout, but Carnap had firmly settled his focus on nomological possibility and constructed languages by the mid-thirties. Concerned with natural language, Schlick (1932, 1936a) deemed all statements meaningful for which it was logically possible to conceive of a procedure of verification; concerned with constructed languages only, Carnap (1936-37) deemed meaningful only statements for whom it was nomologically possible to conceive of a procedure of confirmation of disconfirmation.<br/>
Many of these issues were openly discussed at the Paris congress in 1935. Already in 1932 Carnap had sought to sharpen his previous criterion by stipulating that those statements were meaningful that were syntactically well-formed and whose non-logical terms were reducible to terms occurring in the basic observational evidence statements of science. While Carnap's focus on the reduction of descriptive terms allows for the conclusive verification of some statements, his criterion also allowed universally quantified statements to be meaningful, provided they were syntactically and terminologically correct (1932a, §2). It was not until one of his Paris addresses, however, that Carnap officially declared the meaning criterion to be mere confirmability. Carnap's new criterion required neither verification nor falsification but only partial testability so as now to include not only universal statements but also the disposition statements of science... Though plausible initially, the device of introducing non-observational terms in this way gave rise to a number of difficulties which impugned the supposedly clear distinctions between logical and empirical matters and analytic and synthetic statements (Hempel 1951). Independently, Carnap himself (1939) soon gave up the hope that all theoretical terms of science could be related to an observational base by such reduction chains.
