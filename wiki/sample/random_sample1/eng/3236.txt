<p><b>Nördlingen</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Donau-Ries" title="Donau-Ries">Donau-Ries</a> district, in <a href="/wiki/Swabia_(Bavaria)" title="Swabia (Bavaria)">Swabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, with a population of approximately 24,000. It was first mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a> in 898, and in 1998 the town celebrated its 1100th anniversary. The town was the location of two battles during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, which took place between 1618–1648. Today it is one of only three towns in Germany that still has a completely established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_wall" title="City wall">city wall</a>, the other two being <a href="/wiki/Rothenburg_ob_der_Tauber" title="Rothenburg ob der Tauber">Rothenburg ob der Tauber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinkelsb%C3%BChl" title="Dinkelsbühl">Dinkelsbühl</a>.</p>
<p>Another attraction in the town is the Saint Georg's Church's 90 m steeple, called "Daniel", which is made of a <a href="/wiki/Suevite" title="Suevite">suevite</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impact_breccia" title="Impact breccia">impact breccia</a> that contains <a href="/wiki/Shocked_quartz" title="Shocked quartz">shocked quartz</a>. Other notable buildings are the town hall (which dates to the 13th century), St. Salvator church and the <i>Spital</i>, a former medieval hospital. The <a href="/wiki/Rieskrater_Museum" title="Rieskrater Museum">Ries crater museum</a> is located in the well-preserved medieval tanner's quarter.</p>
<p>The city is home to several other museums, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Railway_Museum" title="Bavarian Railway Museum">Bavarian Railway Museum</a>, the Nördlingen city museum (<i>Stadtmuseum</i>), the city wall museum (<i>Stadtmauermuseum</i>) and <i>Augenblick</i> museum with panoramas, magic lanterns, silent films, barrel organs, pianolas, music boxes and gramophones.</p>
<p>Nördlingen is also known for the <i>Scharlachrennen</i>, a horse riding tournament that was first mentioned in 1463.</p>

<p>The remains of a Roman <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castellum</a>, built in the 85AD and probably called <b>Septemiacum</b>, have been found under the city. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1998, Nördlingen celebrated its 1100-year-old history.</p>
<p>Nördlingen was one of Germany's major trading towns, until its importance declined with the battles of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. In 1215 <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Frederick II</a> declared Nördlingen a <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">Free Imperial City</a>, and it remained so until 1802 when it changed to become part of present-day Bavaria.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Nördlingen trade fair (<i>Pfingstmesse</i>) was first mentioned in 1219.</p>
<p>A well-documented legal case of 1471 involved the prostitute <a href="/wiki/Els_von_Eystett" title="Els von Eystett">Els von Eystett</a> who worked in Nördlingen's <i>Frauenhaus</i>, an officially sanctioned municipal brothel.</p>
<p>Nördlingen was one of the first <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> cities and took part in the <a href="/wiki/Protestation_at_Speyer" title="Protestation at Speyer">Protestation at Speyer</a> in 1529.</p>
<p>In 1604 a shortened and simplified version of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare%27s" title="William Shakespeare's">William Shakespeare's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i> was performed in Nördlingen; this was one of the first performances of any Shakespearean play outside England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nördlingen saw two major battles during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_N%C3%B6rdlingen_(1634)" title="Battle of Nördlingen (1634)">Battle of Nördlingen (1634)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_N%C3%B6rdlingen_(1645)" title="Battle of Nördlingen (1645)">Battle of Nördlingen (1645)</a>.</p>
<p>Nördlingen was seen from above in the final scene and closing credits of the 1971 film <a href="/wiki/Willy_Wonka_%26_the_Chocolate_Factory" title="Willy Wonka &amp; the Chocolate Factory">Willy Wonka &amp; the Chocolate Factory</a>.</p>

<p>Important companies in Nördlingen are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Strenesse – fashion</li>
<li>C.H. Beck – book publisher</li>
<li>Kathrein – antenna manufacturer</li>
<li>Ankerbräu – brewery</li>

<p>Nördlingen has a <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B6rdlingen_station" title="Nördlingen station">station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Ries_Railway" title="Ries Railway">Ries Railway</a>, which is served hourly on weekdays.</p>

<p>The local sports club, the <a href="/wiki/TSV_1861_N%C3%B6rdlingen" title="TSV 1861 Nördlingen">TSV 1861 Nördlingen</a>, has a very successful <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> department with the men's and the women's team both in the Basketball Bundesliga. The clubs football team is traditionally the strongest side in northern Swabia. Its most successful former player is <a href="/wiki/Gerd_M%C3%BCller" title="Gerd Müller">Gerd Müller</a>, who was born and raised in Nördlingen. Its stadium was renamed in his honour in 2008.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_diamonds">Impact diamonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B6rdlingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impact diamonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mondgestein.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="400" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Mondgestein.jpg/220px-Mondgestein.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Mondgestein.jpg/330px-Mondgestein.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/Mondgestein.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mondgestein.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Moon rock in the Ries crater museum</div>

<p>Stone buildings in the town contain millions of tiny <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamonds" title="Diamonds">diamonds</a>, all less than 0.2 millimeters across. The impact that caused the <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B6rdlinger_Ries" title="Nördlinger Ries">Nördlinger Ries</a> crater created an estimated 72000 tons of them when it impacted a local graphite deposit. Stone from this area was later quarried and used to build the stone buildings.</p>
