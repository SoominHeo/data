<p><b>Corpora arenacea</b> (or <b>brain sand</b> or <b>acervuli</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or <b>corpus arenaceum</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) are <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcified</a> structures in the <a href="/wiki/Pineal_gland" title="Pineal gland">pineal gland</a> and other areas of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Choroid_plexus" title="Choroid plexus">choroid plexus</a>. Older organisms have numerous corpora arenacea, whose function, if any, is unknown. Concentrations of "brain sand" increase with <a href="/wiki/Ageing" title="Ageing">age</a>, so the pineal gland becomes increasingly visible on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">X-rays</a> over time, usually by the third or fourth decade. They are sometimes used as anatomical landmarks in <a href="/wiki/Radiology" title="Radiology">radiological examinations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chemical analysis shows that they are composed of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphate" title="Calcium phosphate">calcium phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_phosphate" title="Magnesium phosphate">magnesium phosphate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_phosphate" title="Ammonium phosphate">ammonium phosphate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Recently, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> deposits have been described as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
