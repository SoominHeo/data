<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dura_mater">Dura mater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dura mater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dura_mater" title="Dura mater">Dura mater</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dura_mater" title="Dura mater">dura mater</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>tough mother</i></span>) (also rarely called <i>meninx fibrosa</i> or <i>pachymeninx</i>) is a thick, durable membrane, closest to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a> and vertebrae. The dura mater, the outermost part, is a loosely arranged, fibroelastic layer of cells, characterized by multiple interdigitating cell processes, no extracellular collagen, and significant extracellular spaces. The middle region is a mostly fibrous portion. It consists of two layers: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosteal" title="Endosteal">endosteal</a> layer, which lies closest to the <a href="/wiki/Calvaria_(skull)" title="Calvaria (skull)">calvaria</a> (skullcap), and the inner meningeal layer, which lies closer to the brain. It contains larger blood vessels that split into the capillaries in the <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pia mater</a>. It is composed of dense fibrous tissue, and its inner surface is covered by flattened cells like those present on the surfaces of the pia mater and arachnoid mater. The dura mater is a sac that envelops the arachnoid mater and surrounds and supports the large <a href="/wiki/Dural_venous_sinuses" title="Dural venous sinuses">dural sinuses</a> carrying blood from the brain toward the heart.</p>
<p>The dura has four areas of infolding:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Falx_cerebri" title="Falx cerebri">Falx cerebri</a>, the largest, sickle-shaped; separates the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere" title="Cerebral hemisphere">cerebral hemispheres</a>. Starts from the frontal crest of <a href="/wiki/Frontal_bone" title="Frontal bone">frontal bone</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crista_galli" title="Crista galli">crista galli</a> running to the <a href="/wiki/Internal_occipital_protuberance" title="Internal occipital protuberance">internal occipital protuberance</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tentorium_cerebelli" title="Tentorium cerebelli">Tentorium cerebelli</a>, the second largest, crescent-shaped; separates the <a href="/wiki/Occipital_lobe" title="Occipital lobe">occipital lobes</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cerebellum" title="Cerebellum">cerebellum</a>. The falx cerebri attaches to it giving a tentlike appearance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Falx_cerebelli" title="Falx cerebelli">Falx cerebelli</a>, vertical infolding; lies inferior to the tentorium cerebelli, separating the <a href="/wiki/Cerebellar_hemisphere" title="Cerebellar hemisphere">cerebellar hemispheres</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diaphragma_sellae" title="Diaphragma sellae">Diaphragma sellae</a>, smallest infolding; covers the <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sella_turcica" title="Sella turcica">sella turcica</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arachnoid_mater">Arachnoid mater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arachnoid mater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arachnoid_mater" title="Arachnoid mater">Arachnoid mater</a></div>
<p>The middle element of the meninges is the <a href="/wiki/Arachnoid_mater" title="Arachnoid mater">arachnoid mater</a>, so named because of its <a href="/wiki/Spider_web" title="Spider web">spider web</a>-like appearance. It cushions the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>. This thin, transparent membrane is composed of fibrous tissue and, like the pia mater, is covered by flat cells also thought to be impermeable to fluid.</p>
<p>The shape of the arachnoid does not follow the convolutions of the surface of the brain and so looks like a loosely fitting sac. In particular, in the region of the brain a large number of fine filaments called arachnoid trabeculae pass from the arachnoid through the subarachnoid space to blend with the tissue of the pia mater. The arachnoid is composed of an outermost portion (arachnoid barrier cell layer) with tightly packed cells and no extracellular collagen; that is why it is considered to represent an effective morphological and physiological meningeal barrier between the cerebrospinal fluid and subarachnoid space and the blood circulation in the dura.</p>
<p>The arachnoid barrier layer is characterized by a distinct continuous basal lamina on its inner surface toward the innermost collagenous portion of the arachnoid reticular layer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pia_mater">Pia mater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pia mater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">Pia mater</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pia mater</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>tender mother</i></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merriam-webster.com_pia_mater_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster.com_pia_mater-5">[5]</a></sup>) is a very delicate membrane. It is the meningeal envelope that firmly adheres to the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, following all of the brain's contours (the <a href="/wiki/Gyrus" title="Gyrus">gyri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulcus_(neuroanatomy)" title="Sulcus (neuroanatomy)">sulci</a>). It is a very thin membrane composed of fibrous tissue covered on its outer surface by a sheet of flat cells thought to be impermeable to fluid. The pia mater is pierced by blood vessels to the brain and spinal cord, and its <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a> nourish the brain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leptomeninges">Leptomeninges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leptomeninges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnoid_(brain)" title="Arachnoid (brain)">arachnoid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pia mater</a> together are sometimes called the <i>leptomeninges</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> literally "thin meninges" (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">λεπτός</span> leptos - thin). Acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meningococcal_meningitis" title="Meningococcal meningitis">meningococcal meningitis</a> can lead to an <a href="/wiki/Exudate" title="Exudate">exudate</a> within the leptomeninges along the surface of the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Because the arachnoid is connected to the pia by cob-web like strands, it is structurally continuous with the pia, hence the name pia-arachnoid or leptomeninges. They are responsible for the production of beta-trace.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spaces">Spaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subarachnoid_space" title="Subarachnoid space">Subarachnoid space</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Subarachnoid_space" title="Subarachnoid space">subarachnoid space</a> is the space that normally exists between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnoid_(brain)" title="Arachnoid (brain)">arachnoid</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pia mater</a>, which is filled with <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dura_mater" title="Dura mater">dura mater</a> is attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a>, whereas in the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, the dura mater is separated from the bone (vertebrae) by a space called the <a href="/wiki/Epidural_space" title="Epidural space">epidural space</a>, which contain fat and blood vessels. The arachnoid is attached to the dura mater, while the pia mater is attached to the central nervous system tissue. When the dura mater and the arachnoid separate through injury or illness, the space between them is the <a href="/wiki/Subdural_space" title="Subdural space">subdural space</a>. There is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpial_space" title="Subpial space">subpial space</a> underneath the pia mater that separates it from the <a href="/wiki/Glia_limitans" title="Glia limitans">glia limitans</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage">hemorrhage</a> involving the meninges:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Subarachnoid_hemorrhage" title="Subarachnoid hemorrhage">subarachnoid hemorrhage</a> is acute bleeding under the arachnoid; it may occur spontaneously or as a result of trauma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Subdural_hematoma" title="Subdural hematoma">subdural hematoma</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a> (collection of blood) located in a separation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnoid_(brain)" title="Arachnoid (brain)">arachnoid</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Dura_mater" title="Dura mater">dura mater</a>. The small veins that connect the <a href="/wiki/Dura_mater" title="Dura mater">dura mater</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnoid_(brain)" title="Arachnoid (brain)">arachnoid</a> are torn, usually during an accident, and blood leaks into this area.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Epidural_hematoma" title="Epidural hematoma">epidural hematoma</a> may arise after an accident or spontaneously.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other medical conditions that affect the meninges include <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a> (usually from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungal" title="Fungal">fungal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial">bacterial</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viral</a> <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Meningioma" title="Meningioma">meningiomas</a> that arise from the meninges, or from <a href="/wiki/Meningeal_carcinomatosis" title="Meningeal carcinomatosis">meningeal carcinomatoses</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumor" title="Tumor">tumors</a>) that form elsewhere in the body and <a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasize</a> to the meninges.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meninges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, there is a single membrane known as the primitive meninx. In other vertebrates – <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, the meninges include a thick outer <a href="/wiki/Dura_mater" title="Dura mater">dura mater</a> and a thick inner secondary meninx. In the early 1900s, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Sterzi" title="Giuseppe Sterzi">Giuseppe Sterzi</a> an Italian anatomist carried out comparative studies on the meninges from the <a href="/wiki/Lancelet" title="Lancelet">lancelet</a> to man. Contrary to previous reports the spinal meninges were seen to be very simple, both in the adult lower vertebrates and in the early developmental stages of the more advanced vertebrates. From the <a href="/wiki/Mesenchyme" title="Mesenchyme">mesenchyme</a> surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Neural_tube" title="Neural tube">neural tube</a> only a single leaflet forms the primitive meninx. In the following <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ontogeny" title="Ontogeny">ontogenetic</a> stages, the latter divides into an internal leaflet: the secondary meninx, and into an external one: the dura mater. Finally, in higher vertebrates, even the secondary meninx divides into the arachnoid and the pia. In the same animals, Sterzi demonstrated that, while in the spinal medulla the dura keeps its identity, in the skull it fuses with the periosteum. He also demonstrated the continuity of all meninges with the envelopes of nerves and with the filum terminale.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">Mammals</a> (as higher vertebrates) retain the dura mater, and the secondary meninx divides into the <a href="/wiki/Arachnoid_mater" title="Arachnoid mater">arachnoid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pia mater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
