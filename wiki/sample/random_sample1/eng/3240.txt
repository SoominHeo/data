<p><b>Noonan syndrome</b> (NS) is a relatively common <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosomal</a> <a href="/wiki/Dominance_(genetics)" title="Dominance (genetics)">dominant</a> <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital disorder</a> and is named after <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Noonan" title="Jacqueline Noonan">Jacqueline Noonan</a>, a pediatric cardiologist. It is referred to as the male version of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turner%27s_syndrome" title="Turner's syndrome">Turner's syndrome</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> however, the genetic causes of Noonan syndrome and Turner syndrome are distinct. The principal features include <a href="/wiki/Congenital_heart_defect" title="Congenital heart defect">congenital heart defect</a> (typically <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_valve_stenosis" title="Pulmonary valve stenosis">pulmonary valve stenosis</a>; also <a href="/wiki/Atrial_septal_defect" title="Atrial septal defect">atrial septal defect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophic_cardiomyopathy" title="Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy">hypertrophic cardiomyopathy</a>), short stature, learning problems, <a href="/wiki/Pectus_excavatum" title="Pectus excavatum">pectus excavatum</a>, impaired blood clotting, and a characteristic configuration of facial features including a webbed neck and a flat nose bridge. NS is a <a href="/wiki/RASopathy" title="RASopathy">RASopathy</a>, and is one of several disorders that are caused by a disruption of RAS-MAPK signaling pathway.</p>
<p>It is believed that between approximately 1 in 1,000 and 1 in 2,500 children worldwide are born with NS. It is one of the most common genetic syndromes associated with congenital <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">heart disease</a>, similar in frequency to <a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down syndrome</a>. However, the range and severity of features can vary greatly in patients with NS. Therefore, the syndrome is not always identified at an early age.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organ_system">Organ system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organ system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anesthesia_risk">Anesthesia risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anesthesia risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>There have been few reports of patients with Noonan syndrome who have subsequently suffered from Malignant Hyperthermia (MH). A similar genetic disease, King-Denborough syndrome, has a high risk for MH. King-Denborough syndrome has been linked to a mutation on chromosome 19 located near the gene that encodes the ryanodine receptor whereas Noonan syndrome is associated with a mutation on chromosome 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Some individuals with NS have been reported to develop respiratory depression from narcotics given during and after surgery, which has sometimes required cardiac resuscitation. Impaired anesthetic clearance in the 24–48 hours after surgery has also been reported.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heart">Heart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Up to ~85% of people with NS have one of the following heart defects:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pulmonary valvular stenosis (50–60%)</li>
<li>Septal defects: <a href="/wiki/Atrial_septal_defect" title="Atrial septal defect">atrial</a> (10–25%) or <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_septal_defect" title="Ventricular septal defect">ventricular</a> (5–20%)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypertrophic_cardiomyopathy" title="Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy">Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy</a> (12–35%)</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1283" data-file-width="738" height="382" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg/220px-Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg/330px-Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg/440px-Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Autosomal_dominant_-_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Recurrence in siblings and apparent transmission from parent to child has long suggested a genetic defect with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomal_dominant" title="Autosomal dominant">autosomal dominant</a> inheritance and variable expression. Mutations in the <a href="/wiki/Ras_subfamily" title="Ras subfamily">Ras</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAP_kinase" title="MAP kinase">mitogen activated protein kinase</a> signaling pathways are known to be responsible for ~70% of NS cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Razzaque2012_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Razzaque2012-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A person with NS has up to a 50% chance of transmitting it to their offspring. The fact that an affected parent is not always identified for children with NS suggests several possibilities:</p>
<ol>
<li>Manifestations could be so subtle as to go unrecognized (<a href="/wiki/Expressivity_(genetics)" title="Expressivity (genetics)">variable expressivity</a>)</li>
<li>NS is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous" title="Heterogeneous">heterogeneous</a>, comprising more than one similar condition of differing causes, and some of these may not be inherited.</li>
<li>A high proportion of cases may represent new, sporadic <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a>.</li>
</ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NS can be confirmed <a href="/wiki/Medical_genetics" title="Medical genetics">genetically</a> by the presence of any of the known mutations listed above. However, despite identification of fourteen causative genes, the absence of a known mutation will not exclude the diagnosis, as there are more, as-yet-undiscovered genes that cause NS. Thus, the diagnosis of NS is still based on clinical features. In other words, it is made when a physician feels that a patient has enough of the features to warrant the label. The principal values of making a genetic diagnosis are that it guides additional medical and developmental evaluations, it excludes other possible explanations for the features, and it allows more accurate recurrence risk estimates. With more genotype-phenotype correlation studies being performed, a positive genetic diagnosis will help the clinician to be aware of possible anomalies specific to that certain gene mutation. For example, there is an increase in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy in patients with a mutation of <i>KRAS</i> and an increased risk of juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia for a mutation of <i>PTPN11</i>. In the future, studies may lead to a targeted management of NS symptoms that depends on what genetic mutation a patient has.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2007 study followed 112 individuals for a mean of 12 years (mean age 25.3, range 12-71). No patient died during follow-up, but several required medical interventions. The mean final heights were 167 and 153 cm for men and women, respectively, which is approximately 2 standard deviations below <a href="/wiki/Human_height" title="Human height">normal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noonan_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noonan1883.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="403" data-file-width="565" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Noonan1883.JPG/220px-Noonan1883.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Noonan1883.JPG/330px-Noonan1883.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Noonan1883.JPG/440px-Noonan1883.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noonan1883.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The oldest known case of NS, described in 1883 by Kobylinski</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Noonan" title="Jacqueline Noonan">Jacqueline Noonan</a> was practicing as a pediatric cardiologist at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a> when she noticed that children with a rare type of heart defect, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_stenosis" title="Pulmonary stenosis">valvular pulmonary stenosis</a>, often had a characteristic physical appearance, with <a href="/wiki/Short_stature" title="Short stature">short stature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Webbed_neck" title="Webbed neck">webbed neck</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wide_spaced_eyes" title="Wide spaced eyes">wide spaced eyes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Low-set_ears" title="Low-set ears">low-set ears</a>. Both boys and girls were affected. These characteristics were sometimes seen running in families but were not associated with gross <a href="/wiki/Karyotype" title="Karyotype">chromosomal</a> abnormalities. She studied 833 patients at the congenital heart disease clinic, looking for other congenital abnormalities, and in 1963 presented a paper: "Associated non-cardiac malformations in children with congenital heart disease". This described 9 children who in addition to congenital heart disease had characteristic facial features, chest deformities and short stature.</p>
<p>Dr. John Opitz, a former student of Dr. Noonan, first began to call the condition "Noonan syndrome" when he saw children who looked like those whom Dr. Noonan had described. Dr. Noonan produced a paper entitled "Hypertelorism with Turner Phenotype" in 1968,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and in 1971 at the Symposium of Cardiovascular defects, the name 'Noonan syndrome' became officially recognized.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Turner_syndrome" title="Turner syndrome">Turner syndrome</a>, a different disorder often confused with NS because of several physical features that they share</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_syndrome" title="Fetal alcohol syndrome">Fetal alcohol syndrome</a>, another disorder sometimes confused with NS because of some common facial features and mental retardation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Other <a href="/wiki/RASopathy" title="RASopathy">RASopathies</a>, particularly:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Costello_syndrome" title="Costello syndrome">Costello syndrome</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Legius_syndrome" title="Legius syndrome">Legius syndrome</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noonan_Syndrome_with_Multiple_Lentigines" title="Noonan Syndrome with Multiple Lentigines">Noonan Syndrome with Multiple Lentigines</a>, as known as LEOPARD syndrome, a related disorder caused by mutations in <a href="/wiki/PTPN11" title="PTPN11">PTPN11</a> that are catalytically inactivating.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cardiofaciocutaneous_syndrome" title="Cardiofaciocutaneous syndrome">Cardiofaciocutaneous syndrome</a>, a related disorder which also affects genes encoding elements of the Ras/MAP kinase pathway.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dermatoglyphics" title="Dermatoglyphics">Dermatoglyphics</a></li>
</ul>
