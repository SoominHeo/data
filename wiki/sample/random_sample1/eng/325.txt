<p>The <b>1998 <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> season</b> ended with the <a href="/wiki/1998_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1998 New York Yankees season">New York Yankees</a> defeating the <a href="/wiki/1998_San_Diego_Padres_season" title="1998 San Diego Padres season">San Diego Padres</a> in Game 4 of the <a href="/wiki/1998_World_Series" title="1998 World Series">World Series</a>.</p>
<p>The 1998 season was also marked by an <a href="/wiki/1998_Major_League_Baseball_expansion" title="1998 Major League Baseball expansion">expansion</a> to 30 teams (16 in the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">NL</a>, 14 in the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">AL</a>), with two new teams–the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> in the National League, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Devil_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Devil Rays">Tampa Bay Devil Rays</a> in the American League–added to the MLB. To keep the leagues with even numbers of teams<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while allowing both leagues to have a new team, the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a> were moved from the American League Central Division to the National League Central Division. The <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> were shifted from the American League East to the American League Central, while the Devil Rays were added to the American League East. The Diamondbacks were added to the National League West, making the NL have more teams than the AL for the first time.</p>
<p>The biggest story of the season was the <a href="/wiki/1998_Major_League_Baseball_home_run_record_chase" title="1998 Major League Baseball home run record chase">historic chase of the single-season home run record</a> held at the time by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Maris" title="Roger Maris">Roger Maris</a>. Initially, the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>' <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Griffey,_Jr." title="Ken Griffey, Jr.">Ken Griffey, Jr.</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> started the season on a pace to both break Maris' record. In June, the chase was joined by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>' <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a>, who broke the decades-old record of <a href="/wiki/Rudy_York" title="Rudy York">Rudy York</a> for most home runs in a calendar month with 20 that month. Eventually, Griffey fell off the record pace, but still ended with 56 homers. Both McGwire and Sosa broke the record in September, with McGwire ultimately finishing with 70 homers to Sosa's 66. McGwire's record would last only three years, with <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a> hitting 73 in 2001. The 1998 season was also the first in MLB history with four players <a href="/wiki/50_home_run_club" title="50 home run club">hitting 50 or more homers</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Greg_Vaughn" title="Greg Vaughn">Greg Vaughn</a> of the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> hitting 50. In a postscript to the record chase, both McGwire and Sosa have since been widely accused of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banned_substances_in_baseball" title="Banned substances in baseball">having used performance-enhancing drugs</a> during that period, and McGwire would admit in 2010 that he had used steroids during the record-setting season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The defending World Series champions Florida Marlins finished last in the NL East Division at 54-108, making it the first, and only, time that a team went from winning the World Series one year to finishing with 100 or more losses and last in their division the following year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1998_Major_League_Baseball_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="January.E2.80.93March">January–March</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1998_Major_League_Baseball_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: January–March">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>January 5 – <a href="/wiki/Don_Sutton" title="Don Sutton">Don Sutton</a>, a 324-game winner, is elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> on his fifth try. Sutton, who missed election by nine votes in 1990, is named on 81.6% of the ballots.</li>
<li>February 2 – <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> general manager <a href="/wiki/Bob_Watson" title="Bob Watson">Bob Watson</a> announces his resignation. He is replaced by 30-year-old <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cashman" title="Brian Cashman">Brian Cashman</a>.</li>
<li>February 18 – Long-time <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> TV and radio announcer <a href="/wiki/Harry_Caray" title="Harry Caray">Harry Caray</a> dies at the age of 83.</li>
<li>March 3 – <a href="/wiki/Larry_Doby" title="Larry Doby">Larry Doby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_MacPhail" title="Lee MacPhail">Lee MacPhail</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Davis_(baseball)" title="George Davis (baseball)">George Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bullet_Rogan" title="Bullet Rogan">Bullet Joe Rogan</a> are elected to the Hall of Fame by the Veterans Committee.</li>
<li>March 31 – The <a href="/wiki/1998_Tampa_Bay_Devil_Rays_season" title="1998 Tampa Bay Devil Rays season">Tampa Bay Devil Rays</a> lose to the <a href="/wiki/1998_Detroit_Tigers_season" title="1998 Detroit Tigers season">Detroit Tigers</a> 11–6, in their first game ever. Pitcher <a href="/wiki/Wilson_%C3%81lvarez" title="Wilson Álvarez">Wilson Álvarez</a> takes the loss for Tampa while third baseman <a href="/wiki/Wade_Boggs" title="Wade Boggs">Wade Boggs</a> hit the first home run in team history and drives in three runs.</li>
<li>March 31 – The <a href="/wiki/1998_Arizona_Diamondbacks_season" title="1998 Arizona Diamondbacks season">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> drop a 9–2 decision to the <a href="/wiki/1998_Colorado_Rockies_season" title="1998 Colorado Rockies season">Colorado Rockies</a> in their first game ever. <a href="/wiki/Andy_Benes" title="Andy Benes">Andy Benes</a> is tagged with the loss, and rookies <a href="/wiki/Travis_Lee" title="Travis Lee">Travis Lee</a>, who gets three hits, and <a href="/wiki/Karim_Garc%C3%ADa" title="Karim García">Karim García</a> hit the franchises second home run. <a href="/wiki/Vinny_Castilla" title="Vinny Castilla">Vinny Castilla</a> drives in five runs for Colorado.</li>
<li>March 31 – The <a href="/wiki/1998_New_York_Mets_season" title="1998 New York Mets season">New York Mets</a> beat <a href="/wiki/Mets%E2%80%93Phillies_rivalry" title="Mets–Phillies rivalry">rival</a> <a href="/wiki/1998_Philadelphia_Phillies_season" title="1998 Philadelphia Phillies season">Philadelphia Phillies</a> 1–0 in the longest scoreless opening day game in the National League and the longest one in the MLB since 1926 when the <a href="/wiki/1926_Washington_Senators_season" title="1926 Washington Senators season">Washington Senators</a> beat the <a href="/wiki/1926_Philadelphia_Athletics_season" title="1926 Philadelphia Athletics season">Philadelphia Athletics</a> 1–0 in 15 innings when backup catcher <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Castillo_(catcher)" title="Alberto Castillo (catcher)">Alberto Castillo</a> delivered a full-count, two-out, pinch-hit single to right with the bases loaded off Philadelphia closer <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Bottalico" title="Ricky Bottalico">Ricky Bottalico</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="April.E2.80.93June">April–June</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1998_Major_League_Baseball_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: April–June">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>April 1 – The expansion Tampa Bay Devil Rays win their first game in franchise history, beating the Tigers 11–8. <a href="/wiki/Fred_McGriff" title="Fred McGriff">Fred McGriff</a> has four RBI on three hits.</li>
<li>April 2 – By hitting a home run in Colorado's 6–4 win over Arizona at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_One_Ballpark" title="Bank One Ballpark">Bank One Ballpark</a>, Rockies outfielder <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Burks" title="Ellis Burks">Ellis Burks</a> sets a major league record by having homered in 33 different stadiums.</li>
<li>April 5 – The Arizona Diamondbacks win their first game in franchise history 3–2, over the <a href="/wiki/1998_San_Francisco_Giants_season" title="1998 San Francisco Giants season">San Francisco Giants</a>. <a href="/wiki/Andy_Benes" title="Andy Benes">Andy Benes</a> gets the win for the 1–5 D'backs.</li>
<li>April 10 – The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>' <a href="/wiki/Mike_Piazza" title="Mike Piazza">Mike Piazza</a> becomes the fifth NL player in history to hit <a href="/wiki/Grand_slam_(baseball)" title="Grand slam (baseball)">grand slams</a> in consecutive games by homering in a 7–2 win over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Houston_Astros_season" title="1998 Houston Astros season">Houston Astros</a>. Piazza also homered with the bags full, while driving in six runs, in last night's 7–2 win over Arizona. He'll hit another on April 24 to tie the major-league record for slams in a month.</li>
<li>April 13 – The <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Griffey,_Jr." title="Ken Griffey, Jr.">Ken Griffey, Jr.</a> slugs two home runs in a 6–5 loss to the <a href="/wiki/1998_Cleveland_Indians_season" title="1998 Cleveland Indians season">Cleveland Indians</a>. In doing so, he becomes the second–youngest player in big league history to reach 300 homers for his career, at 28 years and 143 days. <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Foxx" title="Jimmie Foxx">Jimmie Foxx</a>, at 27 years 328 days, was younger.</li>
<li>May 3 – The <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a>' <a href="/wiki/Dan_Wilson_(baseball)" title="Dan Wilson (baseball)">Dan Wilson</a> becomes just the seventh catcher in major league history to hit an <a href="/wiki/Inside-the-park_home_run" title="Inside-the-park home run">inside-the-park</a> <a href="/wiki/Grand_slam_(baseball)" title="Grand slam (baseball)">grand slam</a>, as <a href="/wiki/1998_Seattle_Mariners_season" title="1998 Seattle Mariners season">Seattle</a> defeats Detroit 10–6. It's a first for the Mariners and the first in the AL since <a href="/wiki/Mike_Greenwell" title="Mike Greenwell">Mike Greenwell</a> did it on September 1, 1990.</li>
<li>May 6 – In one of the finest pitching efforts ever, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> rookie right-hander <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kerry_Wood_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kerry Wood (baseball) (page does not exist)">Kerry Wood</a> fans 20 <a href="/wiki/1998_Houston_Astros_season" title="1998 Houston Astros season">Houston Astros</a> in a 2–0, one-hit victory to tie the major league mark for strikeouts in a nine-inning game. The 20-year-old ties the record held by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a>, who performed the feat twice. The only Houston baserunners come from an infield single to <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Guti%C3%A9rrez" title="Ricky Gutiérrez">Ricky Gutiérrez</a> in the 3rd inning and a hit batter. Wood also becomes the second pitcher in baseball history to record a single-game strikeout total equal to his age (in 1936, 17-year-old <a href="/wiki/Bob_Feller" title="Bob Feller">Bob Feller</a> struck out 17 batters). Wood strikes out the first five batters of the game, and seven in a row between the 7th and 9th innings, tying <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Moyer" title="Jamie Moyer">Jamie Moyer</a>'s Cubs record for most consecutive strikeouts.</li>
<li>May 11 – In a 4–2 win over Arizona, Kerry Wood strikes out 13 Diamondbacks in seven innings. By doing so, Wood sets a major league record with 33 strikeouts over two consecutive games.</li>
<li>May 13 – The <a href="/wiki/1998_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1998 Atlanta Braves season">Atlanta Braves</a> set an NL record by homering in their 25th straight game, a 10–2 win over the <a href="/wiki/1998_St._Louis_Cardinals_season" title="1998 St. Louis Cardinals season">St. Louis Cardinals</a>. This ties the major league mark held by the <a href="/wiki/1941_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1941 New York Yankees season">1941 Yankees</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1994_Detroit_Tigers_season" title="1994 Detroit Tigers season">1994 Tigers</a>. The streak will be stopped by the Cardinals the next day.</li>
<li>May 15 – In one of the biggest trades in recent years, the Dodgers send All-Star catcher <a href="/wiki/Mike_Piazza" title="Mike Piazza">Mike Piazza</a> and third baseman <a href="/wiki/Todd_Zeile" title="Todd Zeile">Todd Zeile</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Marlins" title="Florida Marlins">Florida Marlins</a> in exchange for outfielders <a href="/wiki/Gary_Sheffield" title="Gary Sheffield">Gary Sheffield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Eisenreich" title="Jim Eisenreich">Jim Eisenreich</a>, catcher <a href="/wiki/Charles_Johnson_(catcher)" title="Charles Johnson (catcher)">Charles Johnson</a>, third baseman <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Bonilla" title="Bobby Bonilla">Bobby Bonilla</a>, and pitcher <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Barrios" title="Manuel Barrios">Manuel Barrios</a>. On May 22, the Mets will acquire Piazza from the Marlins in exchange for outfielder <a href="/wiki/Preston_Wilson" title="Preston Wilson">Preston Wilson</a>, pitcher <a href="/wiki/Ed_Yarnall" title="Ed Yarnall">Ed Yarnall</a> and a minor league player.</li>
<li>May 17 – Yankees pitcher <a href="/wiki/David_Wells" title="David Wells">David Wells</a> hurls the 15th <a href="/wiki/Perfect_game" title="Perfect game">perfect game</a> in modern major league history with a 4–0 win over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Minnesota_Twins_season" title="1998 Minnesota Twins season">Minnesota Twins</a>. Wells fans 11 batters in his masterpiece. <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Williams" title="Bernie Williams">Bernie Williams</a> strokes three hits for New York, including a home run.</li>
<li>May 18 – The <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a>' <a href="/wiki/Mike_Blowers" title="Mike Blowers">Mike Blowers</a> <a href="/wiki/Hitting_for_the_cycle" title="Hitting for the cycle">hits for the cycle</a> and drives home four runs in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Oakland_Athletics_season" title="1998 Oakland Athletics season">A's</a> 14–0 win over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Chicago_White_Sox_season" title="1998 Chicago White Sox season">White Sox</a>. Blowers become only the second player in franchise history to accomplish the feat.</li>
<li>May 19 – The Cardinals' <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> hits three home runs in a game for the second time this season, leading St. Louis to a 10–8 victory over the Philadelphia Phillies. He is only the 12th player in history to have a pair of 3–HR games in the same season. McGwire drives in six of the Cardinal runs as he reaches the 20 home run mark faster than other player in history.</li>
<li>May 25 – Cleveland's <a href="/wiki/David_Bell_(baseball)" title="David Bell (baseball)">David Bell</a> becomes the third player in major league history to play against a team managed by his father. Bell's two–run double brings home the go–ahead run in the Indians 7–4 win over <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Bell" title="Buddy Bell">Buddy Bell</a>'s Detroit Tigers. <a href="/wiki/Bump_Wills" title="Bump Wills">Bump Wills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mois%C3%A9s_Alou" title="Moisés Alou">Moisés Alou</a> are the only other players to appear in games against their fathers (<a href="/wiki/Maury_Wills" title="Maury Wills">Maury Wills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Alou" title="Felipe Alou">Felipe Alou</a>).</li>
<li>May 28 – With Arizona leading the Giants, 8–6, in the bottom of the 9th with the bases loaded, manager <a href="/wiki/Buck_Showalter" title="Buck Showalter">Buck Showalter</a> orders reliever <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Olson" title="Gregg Olson">Gregg Olson</a> to intentionally walk <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a> to bring home the Giants' 7th run. It is only the fourth bases–loaded intentional walk in major league history, and the first since <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nicholson_(baseball)" title="Bill Nicholson (baseball)">Bill "Swish" Nicholson</a> on July 23, 1944.</li>
<li>June 6 – Hall of Fame second baseman <a href="/wiki/Joe_Morgan" title="Joe Morgan">Joe Morgan</a> has his uniform number 8 retired by the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> in a ceremony at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinergy_Field" title="Cinergy Field">Cinergy Field</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="July.E2.80.93September">July–September</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1998_Major_League_Baseball_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: July–September">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>July 5 – <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a> records his 3000th career strikeout in the Jays' 2–1 win over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Devil_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Devil Rays">Tampa Bay Devil Rays</a>. Tampa left fielder <a href="/wiki/Randy_Winn" title="Randy Winn">Randy Winn</a> is the victim of Clemens' milestone.</li>
<li>June 7 – At <a href="/wiki/Oriole_Park_at_Camden_Yards" title="Oriole Park at Camden Yards">Camden Yards</a>, Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murray" title="Eddie Murray">Eddie Murray</a> has his uniform number 33 retired by the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>.</li>
<li>June 10 – Colorado's <a href="/wiki/Dante_Bichette" title="Dante Bichette">Dante Bichette</a> becomes the first Rockies player ever to <a href="/wiki/Hitting_for_the_cycle" title="Hitting for the cycle">hit for the cycle</a> in the team's 9–8, 10–inning victory over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Texas_Rangers_season" title="1998 Texas Rangers season">Rangers</a>.</li>
<li>June 10 – The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>' <a href="/wiki/Tim_Raines" title="Tim Raines">Tim Raines</a> steals the 800th base of his career in the Yankees' 6–2 win over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Montreal_Expos_season" title="1998 Montreal Expos season">Montreal Expos</a>. He is the fifth player in history to reach the milestone.</li>
<li>June 15 – <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a> hits three home runs, helping the <a href="/wiki/1998_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1998 Chicago Cubs season">Chicago Cubs</a> beat the <a href="/wiki/1998_Milwaukee_Brewers_season" title="1998 Milwaukee Brewers season">Milwaukee Brewers</a> 6–5.</li>
<li>June 20 – The <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> retire <a href="/wiki/Bob_Lemon" title="Bob Lemon">Bob Lemon</a>'s uniform number 19 prior to the team's 5–3 loss to the Yankees.</li>
<li>June 30 – The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>' <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a> hits his 33rd home run of the season in a game against the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a>. Sosa's 20th home run in the month of June is a new MLB record for most home runs in one month.</li>
<li>July 7 – The <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> defeats the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> 13–8, in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1998 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">69th All–Star Game</a> at <a href="/wiki/Coors_Field" title="Coors Field">Coors Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>. Baltimore's <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Alomar" title="Roberto Alomar">Roberto Alomar</a> is named the game's MVP, going 3–for–4 with a home run, one RBI, one stolen base, and two runs scored.</li>
<li>July 9 – <a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a> is elected as the ninth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_commissioner" title="Baseball commissioner">Commissioner of Baseball</a> by a vote of club owners.</li>
<li>July 17 – <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Palmeiro" title="Rafael Palmeiro">Rafael Palmeiro</a> hits his 300th career home run, helping the <a href="/wiki/1998_Baltimore_Orioles_season" title="1998 Baltimore Orioles season">Baltimore Orioles</a> beat the <a href="/wiki/1998_Anaheim_Angels_season" title="1998 Anaheim Angels season">Anaheim Angels</a> 4–1.</li>
<li>July 26 – <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hoffman" title="Trevor Hoffman">Trevor Hoffman</a>'s bid to set a major league record with 42 straight saves ended when the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego</a> closer gives up a home run to <a href="/wiki/Mois%C3%A9s_Alou" title="Moisés Alou">Moisés Alou</a> on his first delivery in the ninth inning, tying the game. The Padres defeat <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston</a> 5–4 in the 10th, but Hoffman blows his chance at history.</li>
<li>July 31 – The Houston Astros acquire flame-throwing pitcher <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> for three minor league players; Johnson caught fire upon arriving in Houston. In 11 regular-season starts with the Astros, he had a 10–1 record, a 1.28 ERA, and 116 strikeouts in <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">84<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> innings, and pitched 4 shutouts. Johnson finished seventh in the National League Cy Young voting despite pitching only two months in the league, and helped Houston win their second straight National League Central division title.</li>
<li>August 4 – <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Delgado" title="Carlos Delgado">Carlos Delgado</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a> hits three home runs, but the Jays lose to the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> 11–9 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rangers_Ballpark_in_Arlington" title="Rangers Ballpark in Arlington">Arlington</a>.</li>
<li>August 9 – <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Mart%C3%ADnez" title="Dennis Martínez">Dennis Martínez</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> defeats the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> 7–5, for his 244th career victory, to set the record for most wins by a Latin American pitcher. <a href="/wiki/Juan_Marichal" title="Juan Marichal">Juan Marichal</a> held the old mark. <a href="/wiki/Chipper_Jones" title="Chipper Jones">Chipper Jones</a> backs Martínez' pitching with four hits and four RBI.</li>
<li>August 13 – <a href="/wiki/Harold_Baines" title="Harold Baines">Harold Baines</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> becomes the all–time leader in RBI by a <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> when he drives in his 824th in a 7–4 win over the Indians. <a href="/wiki/Hal_McRae" title="Hal McRae">Hal McRae</a> was the previous record–holder.</li>
<li>August 14 – Baltimore catcher <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hoiles" title="Chris Hoiles">Chris Hoiles</a> becomes the ninth player – and first catcher – to hit two <a href="/wiki/Grand_slam_(baseball)" title="Grand slam (baseball)">grand slams</a> in a single game, doing so in a 15–3 win over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Cleveland_Indians_season" title="1998 Cleveland Indians season">Cleveland Indians</a>.</li>
<li>August 23 – <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a> swats his 400th career home run, a two-run shot off <a href="/wiki/Kirt_Ojala" title="Kirt Ojala">Kirt Ojala</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a>' 10–5 victory over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Florida_Marlins_season" title="1998 Florida Marlins season">Florida Marlins</a>.</li>
<li>August 25 – The <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>' <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a> strikes out 18 in a 3–0 victory over the <a href="/wiki/1998_Kansas_City_Royals_season" title="1998 Kansas City Royals season">Kansas City Royals</a>. He becomes the first pitcher ever to record three games of 18 or more strikeouts. Clemens allows only three hits and does not walk a batter.</li>
<li>August 31 – Oakland's <a href="/wiki/Rickey_Henderson" title="Rickey Henderson">Rickey Henderson</a> scores the 2,000th run of his career in the Athletics' 15–6 loss to Cleveland. He joins <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rose" title="Pete Rose">Pete Rose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a> as the only players to reach the milestone.</li>
<li>August 31 – Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Wood" title="Kerry Wood">Kerry Wood</a> throws 135 pitches in a game against a mediocre <a href="/wiki/1998_Cincinnati_Reds_season" title="1998 Cincinnati Reds season">Cincinnati Reds</a> team and wakes up the next morning with a sore right elbow. It is the 9th game of the season that manager <a href="/wiki/Jim_Riggleman" title="Jim Riggleman">Jim Riggleman</a> allowed the 20-year old rookie to throw more than 120 pitches. The Cubs decided to shut-down the super-star for the remainder of the regular season. Kerry would return for one game in the playoffs, before blowing out his elbow in spring training the next year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>September 4 – The New York Yankees win their 100th game of the season, defeating the Chicago White Sox 11–6. The Yankees reach that mark five days faster than the 1906 <a href="/wiki/1906_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1906 Chicago Cubs season">Chicago Cubs</a> and 1954 <a href="/wiki/1954_Cleveland_Indians_season" title="1954 Cleveland Indians season">Cleveland Indians</a>.</li>
<li>September 5 – <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> becomes the third player in major league history to reach 60 home runs, as the St. Louis Cardinals beat the Cincinnati Reds 7–0. McGwire joins <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Maris" title="Roger Maris">Roger Maris</a> with 60 home runs in a single season.</li>
<li>September 6 – Atlanta's <a href="/wiki/Andruw_Jones" title="Andruw Jones">Andruw Jones</a> hits his 50th career home run in a 4–0 win over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998_New_York_mets_season" title="1998 New York mets season">New York Mets</a>. He becomes the third–youngest player in history to reach that level; only <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Conigliaro" title="Tony Conigliaro">Tony Conigliaro</a> did so at a younger age.</li>
<li>September 7 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Griffey,_Jr." title="Ken Griffey, Jr.">Ken Griffey, Jr.</a> hits his fiftieth home run of the season, becoming the third player (<a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a>) to hit 50 or more home runs in consecutive seasons.</li>
<li>September 8 – Mark McGwire breaks <a href="/wiki/Roger_Maris" title="Roger Maris">Roger Maris</a>' 37-year-old home run record, lining historic No. 62 just over the wall in left field with two outs in the fourth inning. McGwire's solo shot off the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>' <a href="/wiki/Steve_Trachsel" title="Steve Trachsel">Steve Trachsel</a>—among the shortest he would hit all year—sets off a wild celebration at <a href="/wiki/Busch_Memorial_Stadium" title="Busch Memorial Stadium">Busch Stadium</a>. The Cubs' <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a>, who hit his 58th home run earlier in the game, is on the field to congratulate McGwire, creating an iconic image of the 1998 home run race. In the sixth inning of the same game, the Cardinals' <a href="/wiki/J._D._Drew" title="J. D. Drew">J. D. Drew</a> makes his major league debut pinch-hitting for pitcher <a href="/wiki/Kent_Mercker" title="Kent Mercker">Kent Mercker</a>.</li>
<li>September 11 – The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Marlins" title="Florida Marlins">Florida Marlins</a> lose to the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> 8–2, becoming the first World Series champion in history to lose 100 games the next season.</li>
<li>September 15 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Griffey,_Jr." title="Ken Griffey, Jr.">Ken Griffey, Jr.</a> hits homer #52 and drives in the 1,000th run of his career in the Mariners' 12–7 win over the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Minnesota Twins</a>. He becomes the fourth–youngest player in history to reach the milestone, after <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Foxx" title="Jimmie Foxx">Jimmie Foxx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a>. A day later, Griffey would collect his 20th stolen base of the season to become just the third player in major league history to record at least 50 homers and 20 steals in the same season; <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brady_Anderson" title="Brady Anderson">Brady Anderson</a> are the others.</li>
<li>September 16 – <a href="/wiki/Mike_Piazza" title="Mike Piazza">Mike Piazza</a> hits his 200th career home run, helping the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> beat the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> 4–3.</li>
<li>September 17 – <a href="/wiki/Denny_Neagle" title="Denny Neagle">Denny Neagle</a> puts the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> pitching staff into the baseball record books as he limits the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> to four hits in six innings for a 1–0 win. Neagle improves to 15–11, making the Braves (featuring Neagle, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Maddux" title="Greg Maddux">Greg Maddux</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Smoltz" title="John Smoltz">John Smoltz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Millwood" title="Kevin Millwood">Kevin Millwood</a>) the first major league team with five 15-game winners since the 1930 <a href="/wiki/1930_Washington_Senators_season" title="1930 Washington Senators season">Washington Senators</a>.</li>
<li>September 19 – <a href="/wiki/Alex_Rodriguez" title="Alex Rodriguez">Alex Rodriguez</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> hits his 40th <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> of the season and becomes the third player (after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Canseco" title="José Canseco">José Canseco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a>) to join the <a href="/wiki/40%E2%80%9340_club" title="40–40 club">40–40 club</a>.</li>
<li>September 20 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cal_Ripken,_Jr." title="Cal Ripken, Jr.">Cal Ripken, Jr.</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> takes himself out of the lineup prior to the game with the New York Yankees to end his major league record consecutive game streak at 2,632. The Orioles lose the historic game by a score of 5–4. <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Minor" title="Ryan Minor">Ryan Minor</a>, Ripken's replacement at third base, gets one hit in four at bats.</li>
<li>September 21 – <a href="/wiki/Jason_Kendall" title="Jason Kendall">Jason Kendall</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> steals his 26th base of the season to set a new NL record for catchers. The previous mark was set by <a href="/wiki/John_Stearns" title="John Stearns">John Stearns</a> in 1978.</li>
<li>September 23 – At <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_County_Stadium" title="Milwaukee County Stadium">Milwaukee County Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a> hits his 64th and 65th home runs as the Chicago Cubs jump out to a 7–0 lead against the Milwaukee Brewers. However, the Brewers erase the deficit by scoring eight runs in the last three innings, the last three coming when <a href="/wiki/Brant_Brown" title="Brant Brown">Brant Brown</a> drops a <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Jenkins" title="Geoff Jenkins">Geoff Jenkins</a> fly ball with two out in the ninth inning; the error allows <a href="/wiki/Mark_Loretta" title="Mark Loretta">Mark Loretta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Cirillo" title="Jeff Cirillo">Jeff Cirillo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeromy_Burnitz" title="Jeromy Burnitz">Jeromy Burnitz</a> to score. The Cubs stay tied with the San Francisco Giants for the wild-card lead when they could have led by one game with three games left. Ironically, the error comes 90 years to the day of <a href="/wiki/Fred_Merkle" title="Fred Merkle">Fred Merkle</a>'s baserunning mistake, which led to the Cubs ultimately winning the National League pennant and, to date, <a href="/wiki/1908_World_Series" title="1908 World Series">their last World Series title</a>.</li>
<li>September 24 – <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Tom_Gordon" title="Tom Gordon">Tom Gordon</a> records his 42nd consecutive save of the year for a new major league mark as Boston defeats the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> 9–6. <a href="/wiki/Rod_Beck" title="Rod Beck">Rod Beck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hoffman" title="Trevor Hoffman">Trevor Hoffman</a> shared the old mark.</li>
<li>September 25 – Just hours after <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a> hits his league-leading 66th home run, pulling ahead of <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> for the first time all season, McGwire hits his 66th in a game against the Montreal Expos.</li>
<li>September 26 – <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Eckersley" title="Dennis Eckersley">Dennis Eckersley</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> gets a standing ovation from the <a href="/wiki/Fenway_Park" title="Fenway Park">Fenway Park</a> crowd as he appears in his 1,071st game, breaking <a href="/wiki/Hoyt_Wilhelm" title="Hoyt Wilhelm">Hoyt Wilhelm</a>'s record for most appearances by a pitcher. However, the Red Sox lose to Baltimore 5–2.</li>
<li>September 26 – The <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>' <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> hits his 67th and 68th home runs against the Montreal Expos, pulling two ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>' <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a>, who goes 2-for-4 but fails to homer against the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>.</li>
<li>September 27 – The <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> defeat the <a href="/wiki/1998_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1998 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>, 4–1. Cincinnati uses a pair of brothers in the infield: <a href="/wiki/Bret_Boone" title="Bret Boone">Bret Boone</a> (second base) and his brother <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Boone" title="Aaron Boone">Aaron</a> (third base); <a href="/wiki/Barry_Larkin" title="Barry Larkin">Barry Larkin</a> (shortstop) and his brother <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Larkin" title="Stephen Larkin">Stephen</a> (first base).</li>
<li>September 27 – In the St. Louis Cardinals' final game of the season, <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a> hits two home runs against the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> for the second straight night, establishing a new MLB record with 70 home runs in a season. <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a> fails to hit a home run in the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Cubs</a>' 4–3 loss to the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>, leaving him at 66 homers. However, the Cubs' loss forces a <a href="/wiki/One-game_playoff" title="One-game playoff">one-game playoff</a> with the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998_National_League_wild-card_tie-breaker_game" title="1998 National League wild-card tie-breaker game">National League wild card</a>, giving Sosa one final chance to reach McGwire.</li>
<li>September 27 – In the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a>' final regular season game, left fielder <a href="/wiki/Greg_Vaughn" title="Greg Vaughn">Greg Vaughn</a> hits his 50th home run of the season, a career high and a San Diego Padres record for home runs in a season. This marks the first time in major league history that four players – Vaughn (50), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Griffey,_Jr." title="Ken Griffey, Jr.">Griffey</a> (56), <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sosa</a> (66) and <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">McGwire</a> (70) – hit at least 50 home runs in the same season. Also during this game, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hoffman" title="Trevor Hoffman">Trevor Hoffman</a> records his 53rd save of the season, tying the National League record set by the Cubs' <a href="/wiki/Randy_Myers" title="Randy Myers">Randy Myers</a> in 1993.</li>
<li>September 27 – The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> win their seventh-straight game, defeating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Devil_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Devil Rays">Tampa Bay Devil Rays</a> 8–3. The Yankees finish the season with an American League record 114 wins.</li>
<li>September 27 – In recording his first-ever Major League win, a 2–1 decision over the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SkyDome" title="SkyDome">SkyDome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Halladay" title="Roy Halladay">Roy Halladay</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>, a week removed from his Major League debut, has what would have been the second <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> in Blue Jay history broken up by a <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Higginson" title="Bobby Higginson">Bobby Higginson</a> home run with two out in the ninth, the only hit he will allow. The no-hitter also would have been the third to be pitched on the final day of a regular season, joining the combination of <a href="/wiki/Vida_Blue" title="Vida Blue">Vida Blue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Abbott" title="Glenn Abbott">Glenn Abbott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lindblad" title="Paul Lindblad">Paul Lindblad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rollie_Fingers" title="Rollie Fingers">Rollie Fingers</a> in 1975 and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Witt" title="Mike Witt">Mike Witt</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Perfect_game" title="Perfect game">perfect game</a> in 1984. The home run ball is caught, ironically, by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Stieb" title="Dave Stieb">Dave Stieb</a>, himself a three-time victim of a no-hitter being broken up with two out in the ninth (his last two starts of the 1988 season and a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_game" title="Perfect game">perfect game</a> bid in 1989) before finally pitching the Blue Jays' only no-hitter to date, in 1990.</li>
<li>September 28 – In a <a href="/wiki/One-game_playoff" title="One-game playoff">one-game playoff</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> defeat the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> 5–3 to secure the final playoff spot in the National League. For the third game in a row, the Cubs' <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Sosa" title="Sammy Sosa">Sammy Sosa</a> gets two hits, but no home runs, leaving him at 66 home runs for the season, four fewer than <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a>, who pulled ahead of Sosa with five home runs in his final three games.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="October.E2.80.93December">October–December</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1998_Major_League_Baseball_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: October–December">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>October 3 – The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> are defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> in the National League Divisional Series.</li>
<li>October 21 – The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> win the <a href="/wiki/1998_World_Series" title="1998 World Series">World Series</a>, sweeping the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> in four straight games. Yankees third baseman <a href="/wiki/Scott_Brosius" title="Scott Brosius">Scott Brosius</a> is named the Series MVP. The Yankees end the season with a major league record 125 combined regular season and postseason wins.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> wins his second National League <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a> in an extremely close vote over two <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> pitchers: <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hoffman" title="Trevor Hoffman">Trevor Hoffman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Brown_(right-handed_pitcher)" title="Kevin Brown (right-handed pitcher)">Kevin Brown</a>. Glavine, who receives 11 first-place votes to Hoffman's 13 (Brown receives the remaining 8), becomes the first National League pitcher since the league instituted its four-vote system in 1970 to win the award despite receiving fewer first-place votes than another player. Glavine tallied 99 points (Hoffman – 88, Brown – 76), with 5 points being awarded for each first place vote, 3 for each second-place vote, 2 for third, and 1 for fourth. Another oddity is the fact that Hoffman, Brown, and <a href="/wiki/Rod_Beck" title="Rod Beck">Rod Beck</a> (who did not receive a single point in the Cy Young Award voting) finished higher than Glavine in the MVP voting, despite Glavine's Braves finishing with the best record in the National League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>November 9 – It is revealed that Hall of Fame pitcher <a href="/wiki/Catfish_Hunter" title="Catfish Hunter">Jim "Catfish" Hunter</a> is suffering from <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a>, the progressive, ultimately fatal neurological condition better known as <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a>'s disease.</li>
<li>November 30 – The <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> sign free agent <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a> to a four-year contract worth approximately $50 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>December 12 – The Dodgers set the salary bar higher by signing free agent <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Brown_(right-handed_pitcher)" title="Kevin Brown (right-handed pitcher)">Kevin Brown</a> to a seven-year, $105 million contract, the largest in the majors.</li>
</ul>
