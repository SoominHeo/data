<p><b>Gnutella2</b>, often referred to as <b>G2</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protocol_(computing)" title="Protocol (computing)">protocol</a> developed mainly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Stokes_(programmer)" title="Michael Stokes (programmer)">Michael Stokes</a> and released in 2002.</p>
<p>While inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Gnutella" title="Gnutella">gnutella</a> protocol, G2 shares little of its design with the exception of its <a href="/wiki/Handshaking" title="Handshaking">connection handshake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">download</a> mechanics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gnutella2_Handshakes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gnutella2_Handshakes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>G2 adopts an extensible binary <a href="/wiki/Network_packet" title="Network packet">packet</a> format and an entirely new <a href="/wiki/Search_algorithm" title="Search algorithm">search algorithm</a>.</p>
<p>Furthermore, G2 has a related (but significantly different) <a href="/wiki/Network_topology" title="Network topology">network topology</a> and an improved <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> system, which helps effectively to reduce fake files, such as <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">viruses</a>, on the network.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2002, Michael Stokes announced the Gnutella2 protocol to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella_Developers_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gnutella Developers Forum (page does not exist)">Gnutella Developers Forum</a>. While some thought the goals stated for Gnutella2 are primarily to make a clean break with the gnutella 0.6 protocol and start over, so that some of gnutella's less clean parts would be done more elegantly and, in general, be impressive and desirable; other <a href="/wiki/Software_developer" title="Software developer">developers</a>, primarily those of <a href="/wiki/LimeWire" title="LimeWire">LimeWire</a> and <a href="/wiki/BearShare" title="BearShare">BearShare</a>, thought it to be a "cheap <a href="/wiki/Publicity_stunt" title="Publicity stunt">publicity stunt</a>" and discounted technical merits. Some still refuse to refer to the network as "Gnutella2", and instead, refer to it as "Mike's Protocol" ("MP").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GDF_Discussion_on_the_Gnutella2_name_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GDF_Discussion_on_the_Gnutella2_name-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gnutella2 protocol still uses the old "GNUTELLA CONNECT/0.6" handshake string for its connections<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gnutella2_Handshakes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gnutella2_Handshakes-1">[1]</a></sup> as defined in the gnutella 0.6 specifications. This backwardly compatible handshake method was criticized by the Gnutella Developers Forum as an attempt to use the gnutella network for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bootstrapping_(computing)" title="Bootstrapping (computing)">bootstrapping</a> the new, unrelated network, while proponents of the network claimed that its intent was to remain <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backwards-compatible</a> with gnutella and to allow current gnutella clients to add Gnutella2 at their leisure.</p>
<p>With the developers entrenched in their positions, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flame_war" title="Flame war">flame war</a> soon erupted, further cementing both sides' resolve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.281.29_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.281.29-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.282.29_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.282.29-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.283.29_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.283.29-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.284.29_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gnutella2_Flame_War_.284.29-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Draft_document" title="Draft document">draft specifications</a> were released on March 26, 2003, and more detailed <a href="/wiki/Specification_(technical_standard)" title="Specification (technical standard)">specifications</a> soon followed. G2 is not supported by many of the "old" gnutella network clients, however, many Gnutella2 clients still also connect to gnutella. Many Gnutella2 proponents claim that this is because of <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> reasons, while gnutella supporters claim that the drastic changes don't have enough merit to outweigh the cost of deep rewrites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Developer_discussion_on_migration_to_Gnutella2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Developer_discussion_on_migration_to_Gnutella2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gnutella2 divides <a href="/wiki/Node_(networking)" title="Node (networking)">nodes</a> into two groups: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_node" title="Leaf node">Leaves</a> and Hubs. Most Leaves maintain two <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication_circuit" title="Telecommunication circuit">connections</a> to Hubs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G2_Network_history_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-G2_Network_history-8">[8]</a></sup> while Hubs accept hundreds of Leaf connections, and an average of 7 connections to other Hubs. When a search is initiated, the node obtains a list of Hubs, if needed, and contacts the Hubs in the list, noting which have been searched, until the list is exhausted or a predefined search limit has been reached. This allows a user to find a popular file easily without loading the network, while, theoretically, maintaining the ability for a user to find a single file located anywhere on the network.</p>
<p>Hubs index what <a href="/wiki/Computer_file" title="Computer file">files</a> a Leaf has by means of a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Query_Routing_Table&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Query Routing Table (page does not exist)">Query Routing Table</a>, which is filled with single <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> entries of <a href="/wiki/Hash_function" title="Hash function">hashes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Index_term" title="Index term">keywords</a>, which the Leaf <a href="/wiki/Upload" title="Upload">uploads</a> to the Hub, and which the Hub then combines with all the <a href="/wiki/Hash_table" title="Hash table">hash tables</a> its Leaves have sent, it in order to create a version to send to their neighboring Hubs. This allows for Hubs to reduce <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> greatly by simply not forwarding <a href="/wiki/Query_string" title="Query string">queries</a> to Leaves and neighboring Hubs, if the entries which match the search are not found in the <a href="/wiki/Routing_table" title="Routing table">routing tables</a>.</p>
<p>Gnutella2 relies extensively on <a href="/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol" title="User Datagram Protocol">UDP</a>, rather than <a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">TCP</a>, for searches. The <a href="/wiki/Overhead_(computing)" title="Overhead (computing)">overhead</a> of setting up a TCP connection would make a <a href="/wiki/Random_walk" title="Random walk">random walk</a> search system, requiring the contacting of large numbers of nodes with small volumes of data, unworkable. However, UDP is not without its own drawbacks. Because UDP is connectionless, there is no standard method to inform the sending client that a <a href="/wiki/Message_passing" title="Message passing">message</a> was received, and so if the packet is lost, there is no way to know. Because of this, UDP packets in Gnutella2 have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_field" title="Flag field">flag</a> to enable a reliability setting. When an UDP packet with enabled reliability flag is received, the client will respond with an <a href="/wiki/Acknowledgement_(data_networks)" title="Acknowledgement (data networks)">acknowledge packet</a> to inform the sending client that their packet arrived at its destination. If the acknowledge packet is not sent, the reliable packet will be retransmitted in an attempt to ensure delivery. Low importance packets, which do not have the flag enabled, do not require an acknowledge packet, reducing reliability, but also reducing overhead, as no acknowledge packet needs to be sent and waited upon.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_features">Protocol features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protocol features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gnutella2 has an extensible binary packet format, comparable to an <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> document tree, which was conceived as an answer for some of gnutella's less elegant parts. The packet format was designed so that future network improvements and individual vendor features could be added without worry of causing <a href="/wiki/Software_bug" title="Software bug">bugs</a> in other clients on the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the purpose file identification and secure <a href="/wiki/Data_integrity" title="Data integrity">integrity</a> check of files it employs <a href="/wiki/SHA-1" title="SHA-1">SHA-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Cryptographic_hash_function" title="Cryptographic hash function">hashes</a>. To allow for a file to be reliably <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">downloaded</a> in parallel from multiple sources, as well as to allow for the reliable uploading of parts while the file is still being downloaded (<a href="/wiki/Segmented_file_transfer" title="Segmented file transfer">swarming</a>), <a href="/wiki/Merkle_tree" title="Merkle tree">Tiger tree hashes</a> are used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G2_standard_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-G2_standard-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>To create a more <a href="/wiki/Robustness_(computer_science)" title="Robustness (computer science)">robust</a> and complete system for searching, Gnutella2 also has a <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> system for more complete labeling, rating, and quality information to be given in the search results than would simply be gathered by the file names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Nodes can even share this information after they have deleted the file, allowing users to mark <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">viruses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_worm" title="Computer worm">worms</a> on the network, without requiring them to keep a copy.</p>
<p>Gnutella2 also utilizes <a href="/wiki/HTTP_compression" title="HTTP compression">compression</a> in its network connections to reduce the bandwidth used by the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G2_standard_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-G2_standard-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shareaza" title="Shareaza">Shareaza</a> has the additional feature to request previews of <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_file_format" title="Video file format">videos</a> though only <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=FilesScope&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="FilesScope (page does not exist)">FilesScope</a> takes limited advantage of this.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gtk-gnutella" title="Gtk-gnutella">gtk-gnutella</a> extended the protocol to further reduce the gap between <a href="/wiki/Gnutella" title="Gnutella">Gnutella</a> and G2. In particular, the semi-reliable UDP layer was enhanced to add cumulative and extended acknowledgments in a way that is backward compatible with legacy G2 clients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Further extensions include the "A" string in /Q2/I <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the introduction of /QH2/H/ALT, /QH2/H/PART/MT, /QH2/HN, /QH2/BH and /QH2/G1 in the query hits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_from_gnutella">Differences from gnutella</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Differences from gnutella">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Overall, the two networks are fairly similar, with the primary differences being in the packet format and the search methodology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol">Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gnutella's packet format has been criticized because it was not originally designed with <a href="/wiki/Extensibility" title="Extensibility">extensibility</a> in mind, and has had many additions over the years, leaving the packet structure cluttered and inefficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Developer_discussion_of_gnutella_and_Gnutella2_packet_formats_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Developer_discussion_of_gnutella_and_Gnutella2_packet_formats-15">[15]</a></sup> Gnutella2 learned from this, and aside from having many of the added features of gnutella standard in Gnutella2, designed in future extensibility from the start.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_algorithm">Search algorithm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Search algorithm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While gnutella uses a <a href="/wiki/Query_flooding" title="Query flooding">query flooding</a> method of searching, Gnutella2 uses a <a href="/wiki/Random_walk" title="Random walk">random walk</a> system, where a searching node gathers a list of Hubs and contacts them directly, one at a time. However, as Hub organize themselves in, so called, "Hub <a href="/wiki/Computer_cluster" title="Computer cluster">clusters</a>", where each Hub mirrors the information stored by its neighbors, the Leaf is returned the information of the entire Hub cluster (usually 7 Hubs). This has several advantages over the gnutella's query flooding system. It is more efficient, as continuing a search does not increase the <a href="/wiki/Network_traffic" title="Network traffic">network traffic</a> <a href="/wiki/Exponential_function" title="Exponential function">exponentially</a>, queries are not routed through as many nodes, and it increases the <a href="/wiki/Granularity" title="Granularity">granularity</a> of a search, allowing a client to stop, once a pre-defined threshold of results has been obtained, more effectively than in gnutella. However, the walk system also increases the <a href="/wiki/Complex_network" title="Complex network">complexity of the network</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Network_management" title="Network management">network maintenance and management</a> required, as well as requiring safeguards to prevent a <a href="/wiki/Cyber-attack" title="Cyber-attack">malicious attacker</a> from using the network for <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial-of-service attacks</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnutella2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is also a difference in terminology: while the more capable nodes, which are used to condense the network, are referred to as <i>Ultrapeers</i> in gnutella, they are called <i>Hubs</i> in Gnutella2, and they are also used slightly differently in topology. In gnutella, the Ultrapeers generally maintain as many leaves as peer connections, while Gnutella2 Hubs maintain far more leaves, and fewer peer (Hub-to-Hub) connections. The reason for this is that the search methods of the various networks have different optimum topologies.</p>
