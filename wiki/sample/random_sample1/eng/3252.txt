<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Snowball effect</h1>

<p><a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">Metaphorically</a>, a <b>snowball effect</b> is a process that starts from an initial state of small significance and builds upon itself, becoming larger (graver, more serious), and also perhaps potentially dangerous or disastrous (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicious_circle" title="Vicious circle">vicious circle</a>, a "spiral of decline"), though it might be beneficial instead (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtuous_circle" title="Virtuous circle">virtuous circle</a>). This is a very common <a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Cliché">cliché</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a> and modern <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatrics</a> and it is also used in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>.</p>
<p>The common analogy is with the rolling of a <a href="/wiki/Snowball" title="Snowball">snowball</a> down a snow-covered hillside. As it rolls the ball will pick up more snow, gaining more mass and surface area, and picking up even more snow and momentum as it rolls along.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a>, it is used to describe the multiplication effect in an original weight saving. A reduction in the weight of the fuselage will require less lift, meaning the wings can be smaller. Hence less thrust is required and therefore smaller engines, resulting in a greater weight saving than the original reduction. This iteration can be repeated several times, although the decrease in weight gives <a href="/wiki/Diminishing_returns" title="Diminishing returns">diminishing returns</a>.</p>
<p>The startup process of a feedback <a href="/wiki/Electronic_oscillator" title="Electronic oscillator">electronic oscillator</a>, when power to the circuit is switched on, is a technical application of the snowball effect. Electronic noise is amplified by the oscillator circuit and returned to its input filtered to contain primarily the selected (desired) frequency, gradually getting stronger in each cycle, until a steady-state oscillation is established, when the circuit parameters satisfy the <a href="/wiki/Barkhausen_stability_criterion" title="Barkhausen stability criterion">Barkhausen stability criterion</a>.</p>
