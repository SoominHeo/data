<p><i><b>Blindness</b></i> is a 2008 <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> film, an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Blindness_(novel)" title="Blindness (novel)">1995 novel of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> author <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Saramago" title="José Saramago">José Saramago</a> about a society suffering an epidemic of blindness. The film was written by <a href="/wiki/Don_McKellar" title="Don McKellar">Don McKellar</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Meirelles" title="Fernando Meirelles">Fernando Meirelles</a> with <a href="/wiki/Julianne_Moore" title="Julianne Moore">Julianne Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Ruffalo" title="Mark Ruffalo">Mark Ruffalo</a> as the main characters. Saramago originally refused to sell the rights for a film adaptation, but the producers were able to acquire it with the condition that the film would be set in an unnamed and unrecognizable city. <i>Blindness</i> premiered as the opening film at the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> on May 14, 2008, and the film was released in the United States on October 3, 2008.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A young Japanese professional is struck blind in his car at a crossing and is then approached by a few people, one offers to drive him home and steals his car. The blinded man describes his sudden affliction: an expanse of dazzling white. Upon arriving home and noticing her husband's blindness, the man's wife takes him to a local ophthalmologist who, after testing the man's eyes, can identify nothing wrong and recommends further evaluation at a hospital. Among the doctor's patients are an old man with a black eye-patch, a woman with dark glasses and a young boy. During a dinner with his wife, the doctor discusses the strange case. The woman with dark glasses, revealed to be a call-girl, becomes the third victim of the strange blindness after an appointment with a <a href="/wiki/Prostitution#Etymology_and_terminology" title="Prostitution">john</a> in a hotel.</p>
<p>The next day, the doctor goes blind as well. Around the city, more citizens are struck blind, causing widespread panic, and the government organizes a quarantine for the blind in a derelict asylum. When a <a href="/wiki/Hazmat_suit" title="Hazmat suit">hazmat</a> crew arrives to pick up the doctor, his wife climbs into the van, lying that she has gone blind in order to accompany him.</p>
<p>In the asylum, the doctor and his wife are first to arrive and both agree they will keep her sight a secret. Several others arrive: the woman with dark glasses, the Japanese man, the car thief, and the young boy. The wife comes across the old man with the eye-patch, who describes the condition of the world outside. The sudden blindness, known as the "white sickness", is now international, with hundreds of cases reported every day. The increasingly totalitarian government resorts to increasingly ruthless measures to try to staunch the epidemic, refusing the sick aid or medicines.</p>
<p>As more blind people are crammed into the prison, overcrowding and lack of outside support causes hygiene and living conditions to degrade. Soon, the walls and floors are caked in filth and human feces. Anxiety over the availability of food undermines the morale and the lack of organization prevents the fair distribution of food. The soldiers who guard the asylum become hostile.</p>
<p>Living conditions degenerate further when an armed clique of men, led by an ex-barman who declares himself the king of ward 3 gains control over the food deliveries. The MRE rations are distributed only in exchange for valuables, and then for the women of the other wards. Faced with starvation, the doctor's wife kills the king. His death initiates a chaotic war between the wards, which culminates with the asylum being burned down and many inmates dying in the fire. The survivors discover that the guards have abandoned their posts and they are free to venture into the city.</p>
<p>Society has fallen as the entire population is blind amid a city devastated and overrun with filth and dead bodies. The doctor's wife leads her husband and others in search of food and shelter. The doctor and his wife arrive in a supermarket filled with stumbling blind people and they find food in a basement storeroom. As she prepares to leave and meet her husband outside, she is attacked by the starving people who smell the food she is carrying. Her husband, now used to his blindness, saves her and they manage to return to their friends.</p>
<p>The doctor and his wife with their new "family" make their way back to the doctor's house, where they establish a permanent home. Just as suddenly as his sight had been lost, the Japanese man recovers his sight. As the friends all celebrate, the doctor's wife stands out on the porch, staring up into a white overcast sky and appears to be going blind until the camera shifts downwards, revealing that she sees the cityscape.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast">Cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Julianne_Moore" title="Julianne Moore">Julianne Moore</a> as the doctor's wife, the only person immune to the epidemic of blindness. Her sight is kept a secret by her husband and others, though as time goes on, she feels isolated in being the only one with sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanity_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sanity-2">[2]</a></sup> Moore described her character's responsibility: "Her biggest concern in the beginning is simply her husband. But her ability to see ultimately both isolates her and makes her into a leader." The director also gave Moore's character a wardrobe that would match the actor's skin and dyed blond hair, giving her the appearance of a "pale angel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanity_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sanity-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mark_Ruffalo" title="Mark Ruffalo">Mark Ruffalo</a> as Doctor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sight_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-sight-3">[3]</a></sup> The doctor also becomes something of a leader; in an early scene, he reveals that he has been elected as his ward's official representative to the rest of the community. Meirelles originally sought to cast actor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Craig" title="Daniel Craig">Daniel Craig</a> as Doctor, but negotiations were not finalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Ruffalo said that his character loses the illusion of his self-perspective and perceives his wife as being a person he could aspire to. Ruffalo said, "That's a very difficult moment for anybody, to have all their perceptions completely shattered, but I think the Doctor finally comes to a peace about his inabilities and his downfall, and admits to an admiration for his wife's strengths."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> The actor wore a layer of makeup to appear older and also wore contact lenses to be blind while having his eyes open. The actor said of the experience as a blind character, "At first it's terrifying and then it's frustrating and then it gets quiet... we're tormented by our eyesight... you don't know this until you go blind... As an actor I suddenly felt free."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ills_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ills-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Danny_Glover" title="Danny Glover">Danny Glover</a> as Man with Black Eye Patch. Glover described his character: "The Man with the Black Eye Patch comes into this new world of blindness already half blind, so I think he understands where he is within his own truth, within himself. I did feel like this character was very much like Saramago because he is completely unapologetic—he is who he is and he accepts who he is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-savage_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-savage-7">[7]</a></sup> Glover explained his involvement with the role, "When you are blind you try to adopt another kind of sensitivity, so this role is definitely a challenge from a physical point of view."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gael_Garc%C3%ADa_Bernal" title="Gael García Bernal">Gael García Bernal</a> as Bartender/King of Ward 3,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> one of the film's villains. In defiance of the doctor's <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> election as leader of Ward 1, the bartender declares himself "King of Ward 3" and gains immediate popularity from his "subjects" by prioritizing food over his ward's community responsibilities such as burying the dead. He somehow obtains a revolver and uses it to bully the other wards by controlling the food supply. Meirelles followed the advice of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> stage director Antunes Filho and changed the character from the novel by making him more ambiguous, explaining, "In the book, he is really a mean guy, terribly evil from the beginning... but I thought it was more interesting to have him be not evil but more like a child with a gun."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foresight_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-foresight-9">[9]</a></sup> Bernal described the result of his character, "I think the King is just very practical, very pragmatic. He appears cold because he is not an idealist and does not see hope, but he is a survivor, the same as all the others."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> The doctor's wife kills him with a pair of medical scissors to the neck. His death marks the point when Ward 1 takes back control, with the doctor's wife's threat to kill one of the men from Ward 3 for every day her ward goes without food.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maury_Chaykin" title="Maury Chaykin">Maury Chaykin</a> as Accountant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> who helps the King of Ward 3 bully the members of the other wards. Because he has been blind since birth, the Accountant is much more used to relying on his other senses, which gives him a major advantage over the other prisoners; he assumes control over Ward 3 and the food supplies for the community after the King is murdered.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alice_Braga" title="Alice Braga">Alice Braga</a> as Woman with Dark Glasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> Braga described her character as mysterious, believing, "While she does sleep with men because it is easy money, I did not want to treat her purely as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostitute" title="Prostitute">prostitute</a>. She starts out quite tough, but she develops very strong maternal feelings." Meirelles explained that the character's glasses and cascading hair gave her a cold appearance, but through her scenes with the orphaned Boy with the Squint, she develops warmth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Secondary characters include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Don_McKellar" title="Don McKellar">Don McKellar</a> as Thief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> McKellar, who wrote the screenplay for the film, had also acted in the past and was cast as the character. The screenwriter described the Thief, "I like the trick where you think the Thief is a bad guy. He's a pathetic character you first believe is the villain of the piece and then you realize that, no, he's not even close to that. There's something charming about his desperation because, after a point, you meet the King of Ward Three and learn what real desperation is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sandra_Oh" title="Sandra Oh">Sandra Oh</a> as Minister of Health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanity_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-sanity-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yusuke_Iseya" title="Yusuke Iseya">Yusuke Iseya</a> as First Blind Man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yoshino_Kimura" title="Yoshino Kimura">Yoshino Kimura</a> as First Blind Man's Wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Nye" title="Mitchell Nye">Mitchell Nye</a> as Boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Susan_Coyne" title="Susan Coyne">Susan Coyne</a> as Receptionist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanity_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-sanity-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Martha_Burns" title="Martha Burns">Martha Burns</a> as Woman with Insomnia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanity_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-sanity-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joe_Pingue" title="Joe Pingue">Joe Pingue</a> as The Taxi Driver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanity_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-sanity-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Douglas_Silva" title="Douglas Silva">Douglas Silva</a> as Onlooker #1 in the opening scenes. Silva has previously acted in many Meirelles films: he starred in Meirelles' short film <i>Golden Gate (Palace II)</i> in 2000; the 2002 film <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(2002_film)" title="City of God (2002 film)">City of God</a></i>; its 2002 to 2005 spin-off television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cidade_dos_Homens" title="Cidade dos Homens">Cidade dos Homens</a></i> (English translation: <i>City of Men</i>); and the movie <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_Men" title="City of Men">City of Men</a></i> in 2007.</li>
</ul>
<p>Meirelles chose an international cast. Producer Niv Fichman explained Meirelles' intent: "He was inspired by [Saramago's] great masterwork to create a microcosm of the world. He wanted it cast in a way to represent all of humanity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indie_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-indie-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rights to the 1995 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Blindness_(novel)" title="Blindness (novel)">Blindness</a></i> were closely guarded by author <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Saramago" title="José Saramago">José Saramago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ills_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ills-6">[6]</a></sup> Saramago explained, "I always resisted because it's a violent book about social degradation, rape, and I didn't want it to fall into the wrong hands." Director <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Meirelles" title="Fernando Meirelles">Fernando Meirelles</a> had wanted to direct a film adaptation in 1997, perceiving it as "an allegory about the fragility of civilization". Saramago originally refused to sell the rights to Meirelles, <a href="/wiki/Whoopi_Goldberg" title="Whoopi Goldberg">Whoopi Goldberg</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gael_Garc%C3%ADa_Bernal" title="Gael García Bernal">Gael García Bernal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1999, producer Niv Fichman and Canadian screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Don_McKellar" title="Don McKellar">Don McKellar</a> visited Saramago in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>; Saramago allowed their visit on condition that they not discuss buying the rights. McKellar explained the changes he intended to make from the novel and what the focus would be, and two days later he and Fichman left Saramago's home with the rights. McKellar believed they had succeeded where others had failed because they properly researched Saramago; he was suspicious of the film industry and had therefore resisted other studios' efforts to obtain the rights through large sums of money alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-creative_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-creative-12">[12]</a></sup> Conditions set by Saramago were for the film to be set in a country that would not be recognizable to audiences,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and that the canine in the novel, the Dog of Tears, should be a big dog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cannes_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cannes-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meirelles originally envisioned doing the film in Portuguese similar to the novel's original language, but instead directed the film in English, saying, "If you do it in English you can sell it to the whole world and have a bigger audience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foresight_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-foresight-9">[9]</a></sup> Meirelles set the film in a contemporary large city, seemingly under a totalitarian government, as opposed to the novel that he believed took place in the 1940s (actually, the book is more likely to take place in the 80s or later, as evident by the fact that the characters stumble upon a store with modern appliances like microwave ovens and dishwashers, and referral to AIDS as a feared disease). Meirelles chose to make a contemporary film so audiences could relate to the characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cannes_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-cannes-14">[14]</a></sup> The director also sought a different allegorical approach. He described the novel as "very allegorical, like a fantasy outside of space, outside the world", and he instead took a naturalistic direction in engaging audiences to make the film less "cold."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifc_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifc-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Don McKellar said about adapting the story, "None of the characters even have names or a history, which is very untraditional for a <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> story. The film, like the novel, directly addresses sight and point of view and asks you to see things from a different perspective." McKellar wrote the script so audiences would see the world through the eyes of the protagonist, the doctor's wife. He sought to have them question the humanity of how she observes but does not act in various situations, including a rape scene. He consulted Saramago about why the wife took so long to act. McKellar noted, "He said she became aware of the responsibility that comes with seeing gradually, first to herself, then to her husband, then to her small family, then her ward, and finally to the world where she has to create a new civilization." The screenwriter wrote out the "actions and circumstances" that would allow the wife to find her responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup> While the completed script was mostly faithful to the novel, McKellar went through several drafts that were not. One such saw him veer away from the novel by creating names and backstories for all the characters. Another significantly changed the chronology. Only after these abortive attempts did McKellar decide to cut the backstories and focus primarily on the doctor and his wife. He attempted to reconnect with what originally drew him to the novel: what he called its "<a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existential</a> simplicity". The novel defines its characters by little more than their present actions; doing the same for the adaptation became "an interesting exercise" for McKellar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-creative_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-creative-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>McKellar attended a summer camp for the blind as part of his research. He wanted to observe how blind people interacted in groups. He discovered that excessive expositional dialogue, usually frowned upon by writers, was essential for the groups. McKellar cut one of the last lines in the novel from his screenplay: "I don't think we did go blind, I think we are blind. Blind but seeing. Blind people who can see, but do not see." McKellar believed viewers would by that point have already grasped the symbolism and didn't want the script to seem heavy-handed. He also toned down the visual cues in his screenplay, such as the "brilliant milky whiteness" of blindness described in the novel. McKellar knew he wanted a stylistically adept director and didn't want to be too prescriptive, preferring only to hint at an approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-creative_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-creative-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming_and_casting">Filming and casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Filming and casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Meirelles chose <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a> as the primary backdrop for <i>Blindness</i>, though scenes were also filmed in <a href="/wiki/Osasco" title="Osasco">Osasco</a>, Brazil; <a href="/wiki/Guelph" title="Guelph">Guelph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada; and <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>, Uruguay. With all the characters aside from Julianne Moore's character being blind, the cast was trained to simulate blindness. The director also stylized the film to reflect the lack of point of view that the characters would experience. Meirelles said several actors he talked to were intimidated by the concept of playing characters without names: "I offered the film to some actors who said, 'I can't play a character with no name, with no history, with no past. With Gael (García Bernal), he said, 'I never think about the past. I just think what my character wants.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_Fall_Preview_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW_Fall_Preview-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>By September 2006, Fernando Meirelles was attached to <i>Blindness</i>, with the script being adapted by Don McKellar. <i>Blindness</i>, budgeted at $25 million as part of a Brazilian and Canadian co-production, was slated to begin filming in summer 2007 in the towns of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guelph" title="Guelph">Guelph</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Filming began in early July in São Paulo and Guelph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-succumb_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-succumb-18">[18]</a></sup> Filming also took place in <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guelph_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guelph-19">[19]</a></sup> São Paulo served as the primary backdrop for <i>Blindness</i>, being a city mostly unfamiliar to U.S. and European audiences. With its relative obscurity, the director sought São Paulo as the film's generic location. Filming continued through autumn of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ills_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ills-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cast and crew included 700 extras who had to be trained to simulate blindness. Actor Christian Duurvoort from Meirelles' <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(2002_film)" title="City of God (2002 film)">City of God</a></i> led a series of workshops to coach the cast members. Duurvoort had researched the mannerisms of blind people to understand how they perceive the world and how they make their way through space. Duurvoort not only taught the extras mannerisms, but also to convey the emotional and psychological states of blind people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ills_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-ills-6">[6]</a></sup> One technique was reacting to others as a blind person, whose reactions are usually different from those of a sighted person. Meirelles described, "When you're talking to someone, you see a reaction. When you're blind, the response is much flatter. What's the point [in reacting]?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faith_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-faith-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmmaking_style">Filmmaking style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Filmmaking style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1816" data-file-width="3200" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg/220px-Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg/330px-Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg/440px-Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pieter_Bruegel_d._%C3%84._025.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Director Fernando Meirelles alludes to <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder" title="Pieter Bruegel the Elder">Pieter Bruegel the Elder</a>'s 1568 painting <i>The Parable of the Blind</i> in the film <i>Blindness</i>.</div>
</div>

<p>Meirelles acknowledged the challenge of making a film that would simulate the experience of blindness to the audience. He explained, "When you do a film, everything is related to point of view, to vision. When you have two characters in a dialogue, emotion is expressed by the way people look at each other, through the eyes. Especially in the cut, the edit. You usually cut when someone looks over. Film is all about point of view, and in this film there is none."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faith_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-faith-20">[20]</a></sup> Similar to the book, blindness in the film serves as a metaphor for human nature's dark side: "prejudice, selfishness, violence and willful indifference."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ills_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-ills-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>With only one character's point of view available, Meirelles sought to switch the points-of-view throughout the film, seeing three distinct stylistic sections. The director began with an omniscient vantage point, transited to the intact viewpoint of the doctor's wife, and changed again to the Man with the Black Eye Patch, who connects the quarantined to the outside world with stories. The director concluded the switching with the combination of the perspective of the Doctor's Wife and the narrative of the Man with the Black Eye Patch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-notes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film also contains visual cues, such as the 1568 painting <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Parable_of_the_Blind" title="The Parable of the Blind">The Parable of the Blind</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder" title="Pieter Bruegel the Elder">Pieter Bruegel the Elder</a>. Allusions to other famous artworks are also made. Meirelles described the intent: "It's about image, the film, and vision, so I thought it makes sense to create, not a history of painting, because it's not, but having different ways of seeing things, from Rembrandt to these very contemporary artists. But it's a very subtle thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ills_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-ills-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatrical_run">Theatrical run</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Theatrical run">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to public release, Meirelles screened <i>Blindness</i> to test audiences. He described the impact of test screenings: "If you know how to use it, how to ask the right questions, it can be really useful." A test screening of Meirelles' first cut in Toronto resulted in ten percent of the audience, nearly 50 people, walking out of the film early. Meirelles ascribed the problem to a rape scene that takes place partway through the film, and edited the scene to be much shorter in the final cut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-21">[21]</a></sup> Meirelles explained his goal, "When I shot and edited these scenes, I did it in a very technical way, I worried about how to light it and so on, and I lost the sense of their brutality. Some women were really angry with the film, and I thought, 'Wow, maybe I crossed the line.' I went back not to please the audience but so they would stay involved until the end of the story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foresight_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-foresight-9">[9]</a></sup> He also found that a <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> test screening expressed concern about a victim in the film failing to take revenge. Meirelles believed this concern to reflect what Americans have learned to expect in their cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-test-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Focus_Features" title="Focus Features">Focus Features</a> acquired the right to handle international sales for <i>Blindness</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Path%C3%A9" title="Pathé">Pathé</a> acquired UK and French rights to distribute the film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miramax_Films" title="Miramax Films">Miramax Films</a> won U.S. distribution rights with its $5 million bid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Blindness</i> premiered as the opening film at the <a href="/wiki/2008_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2008 Cannes Film Festival">61st Cannes Film Festival</a> on May 14, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> where it received a "tepid reception."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Straw polls of critics were "unkind" to the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Blindness</i> was screened at the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="Toronto International Film Festival">Toronto International Film Festival</a> in September 2008 as a Special Presentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The film also opened at the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Film_Festival" title="Atlantic Film Festival">Atlantic Film Festival</a> on September 11, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and had its North American theatrical release on October 3, 2008.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was on some critics' top ten lists of 2008 films but has received very mixed, predominantly negative reviews. With only 66 of 153 (43%) reviews on the film review site <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> being positive <i>Blindness</i> is considered "rotten". The film has an average rating of 5.2 out of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Screen_International" title="Screen International">Screen International</a>'</i>s Cannes screen jury which annually polls a panel of international film critics gave the film a 1.3 average out of 4, placing the film on the lower-tier of all the films screened at competition in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-screen_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-screen-31">[31]</a></sup> Of the film critics from the <i>Screen International</i> Cannes critics jury, Alberto Crespi of the Italian publication <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Unit%C3%A0" title="L'Unità">L'Unità</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Ciment" title="Michel Ciment">Michel Ciment</a> of French film magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Positif_(magazine)" title="Positif (magazine)">Positif</a></i> and Dohoon Kim of South Korean film publication <i>Cine21</i>, all gave the film zero points (out of four).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-screen_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-screen-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kirk Honeycutt of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> described <i>Blindness</i> as "provocative but predictable cinema", startling but failing to surprise. Honeycutt criticized the film's two viewpoints: Julianne Moore's character, the only one who can see, is slow to act against atrocities, and the behavior of Danny Glover's character comes off as "slightly pompous". Honeycutt explained, "This philosophical coolness is what most undermines the emotional response to Meirelles' film. His fictional calculations are all so precise and a tone of deadly seriousness swamps the grim action."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Justin Chang of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> described the film: "<i>Blindness</i> emerges onscreen both overdressed and undermotivated, scrupulously hitting the novel's beats yet barely approximating, so to speak, its vision." Chang thought that Julianne Moore gave a strong performance but did not feel that the film captured the impact of Saramago's novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> called <i>Blindness</i> "one of the most unpleasant, not to say unendurable, films I've ever seen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">A. O. Scott</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> stated that, although it "is not a great film, ... it is, nonetheless, full of examples of what good filmmaking looks like."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stephen Garrett of <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i> complimented Meirelles' unconventional style: "Meirelles [honors] the material by using elegant, artful camera compositions, beguiling sound design and deft touches of digital effects to accentuate the authenticity of his cataclysmic landscape." Despite the praise, Garrett wrote that Meirelles' talent at portraying real-life injustice in <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(2002_film)" title="City of God (2002 film)">City of God</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Constant_Gardener" title="The Constant Gardener">The Constant Gardener</a></i> did not suit him for directing the "heightened reality" of Saramago's social commentary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> called it "an intelligent, tightly constructed, supremely confident adaptation": "Meirelles, along with screenwriter Don McKellar and cinematographer Cesar Charlone, have created an elegant, gripping and visually outstanding film. It responds to the novel's notes of apocalypse and dystopia, and its disclosure of a spiritual desert within the modern city, but also to its persistent qualities of fable, paradox and even whimsy." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> "Blindness is a drum-tight drama, with superb, hallucinatory, images of urban collapse. It has a real coil of horror at its centre, yet is lightened with gentleness and humour. It reminded me of George A Romero's <i>Night of the Living Dead</i>, and Peter Shaffer's absurdist stage-play <i>Black Comedy</i>. This is bold, masterly, film-making."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Wesley_Morris" title="Wesley Morris">Wesley Morris</a> raved about the leading actress: "Julianne Moore is a star for these terrible times. She tends to be at her best when the world is at its worst. And things are pretty bad in "Blindness," a perversely enjoyable, occasionally harrowing adaptation of José Saramago's 1995 disaster allegory. [...] "Blindness" is a movie whose sense of crisis feels right on time, even if the happy ending feels like a gratuitous emotional bailout. Meirelles ensures that the obviousness of the symbolism (in the global village the blind need guidance!) doesn't negate the story's power, nor the power of Moore's performance. The more dehumanizing things get, the fiercer she becomes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film appeared on some critics' top ten lists of the best films of 2008. Bill White of the <i><a href="/wiki/Seattle_Post-Intelligencer" title="Seattle Post-Intelligencer">Seattle Post-Intelligencer</a></i> named it the 5th best film of 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mctop08_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-mctop08-40">[40]</a></sup> and Marc Savlov of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Austin_Chronicle" title="The Austin Chronicle">The Austin Chronicle</a></i> named it the 8th best film of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mctop08_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-mctop08-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests">Protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film has been strongly criticized by several organizations representing the blind community. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Marc_Maurer" title="Marc Maurer">Marc Maurer</a>, President of the <a href="/wiki/National_Federation_of_the_Blind" title="National Federation of the Blind">National Federation of the Blind</a>, said: "The National Federation of the Blind condemns and deplores this film, which will do substantial harm to the blind of America and the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> A press release from the <a href="/wiki/American_Council_of_the_Blind" title="American Council of the Blind">American Council of the Blind</a> said "...it is quite obvious why blind people would be outraged over this movie. Blind people do not behave like uncivilized, animalized creatures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The National Federation of the Blind announced plans to picket theaters in at least 21 states, in the largest protest in the organization's 68-year history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> José Saramago has described his novel as allegorically depicting "a blindness of rationality". He dismissed the protests, stating that "stupidity doesn't choose between the blind and the non-blind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jos.C3.A9_Saramago.27s_reaction_to_the_movie">José Saramago's reaction to the movie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blindness_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: José Saramago's reaction to the movie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a closed section, José Saramago watched the movie together with Fernando Meirelles. When the movie ended, Saramago was in tears. He turned to Fernando Meirelles and said: "Fernando, I am so happy to have seen this movie as I was the day I finished the book." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
