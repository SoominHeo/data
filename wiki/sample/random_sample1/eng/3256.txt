<p>The <b>Nùng</b> (<i>pronounced as noong</i> <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[nuːŋ]</a>) are an ethnic minority in Vietnam whose language belongs to <a href="/wiki/Central_Tai_languages" title="Central Tai languages">the Central Tai branch</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tai%E2%80%93Kadai_languages" title="Tai–Kadai languages">the Tai-Kadai language family</a>. The Nùng sometimes call themselves as Tho (<i><a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: Thổ</i>, a shared name between <a href="/wiki/Tay_people" title="Tay people">the Tày</a> and <a href="/wiki/Th%E1%BB%95_people" title="Thổ people">the Cuối</a>, literally means <i>Natives</i>). The Nùng's ethnic name is often mingled with the Tày as Tày-Nùng.</p>
<p>The Nùng reside primarily in the northern Vietnamese provinces of <a href="/wiki/Cao_B%E1%BA%B1ng_Province" title="Cao Bằng Province">Cao Bằng</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1ng_S%C6%A1n_Province" title="Lạng Sơn Province">Lạng Sơn</a>, with considerable numbers in <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%AFc_Giang_Province" title="Bắc Giang Province">Bắc Giang</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%AFc_K%E1%BA%A1n_Province" title="Bắc Kạn Province">Bắc Kạn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A1i_Nguy%C3%AAn_Province" title="Thái Nguyên Province">Thái Nguyên</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qu%E1%BA%A3ng_Ninh_Province" title="Quảng Ninh Province">Quảng Ninh</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A0o_Cai_Province" title="Lào Cai Province">Lào Cai</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_Giang_Province" title="Hà Giang Province">Hà Giang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuy%C3%AAn_Quang_Province" title="Tuyên Quang Province">Tuyên Quang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%AAn_B%C3%A1i_Province" title="Yên Bái Province">Yên Bái</a> and they can also be found in <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A2m_%C4%90%E1%BB%93ng_Province" title="Lâm Đồng Province">Lâm Đồng</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BA%AFc_L%E1%BA%AFc_Province" title="Đắc Lắc Province">Đắc Lắc</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%E1%BB%93_Ch%C3%AD_Minh_City" title="Hồ Chí Minh City">Hồ Chí Minh City</a>.</p>
<p>According to the Vietnam census, the population of the Nùng numbered about 856,412 by 1999 and 968,800 by 2009. In <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, the Nùng are the third largest Tai-speaking group, preceded by the Tày and the Thái (<a href="/wiki/Tai_Dam_people" title="Tai Dam people">Black Tai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tai_D%C3%B3n_people" title="Tai Dón people">White Tai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tai_Daeng_people" title="Tai Daeng people">Red Tai</a> groups), and sixth overall among national minority groups.</p>
<p>They are closely related to <a href="/wiki/Tay_people" title="Tay people">the Tày</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_people" title="Zhuang people">the Zhuang</a>. In China, the Nùng, together with <a href="/wiki/Tay_people" title="Tay people">the Tày</a>, are classified as <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_people" title="Zhuang people">Zhuang people</a>.</p>
<p>The Nùng are divided into several sub-groups such as: Nùng Xuồng, Nùng Giang, Nùng An, Nùng Phàn Slình, Nùng Lòi, Nùng Cháo, Nùng Quý Rỉn, Nùng Dín, Nùng Inh, Nùng Tùng Slìn etc.</p>
<p>Many of the Nùng's sub-group names correspond to the geographic regions of the Nùng homeland. Hoàng Nam (2008:11) lists the following Nùng subgroups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHo.C3.A0ng_Nam200811_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHo.C3.A0ng_Nam200811-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>

<li><i>Nùng Phàn Slình</i>: migrated from Wan Cheng
<ul>

<li><i>Nùng Phàn Slình cúm cọt</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Nùng An</i>: migrated from An Jie</li>
<li><i>Nùng Dín</i></li>
<li><i>Nùng Lòi</i>: migrated from Xia Lei</li>
<li><i>Nùng Tùng Slìn</i>: migrated from Cong Shan</li>
<li><i>Nùng Quý Rỉn</i>: migrated from Gui Shun</li>
<li><i>Nùng Cháo</i>: migrated from Long Zhou</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_confusion">Name confusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B9ng_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name confusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Vietnam, there was a group of ethnic Chinese called the <a href="/wiki/Hoa_people" title="Hoa people">Chinese Nùng</a> <i>(<a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: Hoa Nùng or Tàu Nùng)</i>. These Chinese Nùng composed 72%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETr.E1.BA.A7n_.C4.90.E1.BB.A9c_Lai201342_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETr.E1.BA.A7n_.C4.90.E1.BB.A9c_Lai201342-4">[4]</a></sup> to 78%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXiaorong_Han20091.E2.80.9336_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXiaorong_Han20091.E2.80.9336-5">[5]</a></sup>of the population of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nung_Autonomous_Territory_of_Hai_Ninh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nung Autonomous Territory of Hai Ninh (page does not exist)">Nung Autonomous Territory of Hai Ninh</a> (1947-1954) located in the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_(Vietnam)" title="Northeast (Vietnam)">northeast corner of Vietnam</a>, covering parts of present-day <a href="/wiki/Qu%E1%BA%A3ng_Ninh_Province" title="Quảng Ninh Province">Quảng Ninh</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1ng_S%C6%A1n_Province" title="Lạng Sơn Province">Lạng Sơn</a> provinces. The Chinese Nùng's name originated from the fact that almost all of them were farmers (<i>nong nhan</i> in <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETr.E1.BA.A7n_.C4.90.E1.BB.A9c_Lai20133_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETr.E1.BA.A7n_.C4.90.E1.BB.A9c_Lai20133-6">[6]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tientsin_(1885)" title="Treaty of Tientsin (1885)">Treaty of Tientsin (1885)</a>, the French refused to recognize this group as Chinese due to political and territorial issues on Vietnam's northern frontier border, therefore the French classified them as Nùng based on their main occupation. The most widely used languages of the Chinese Nùng were <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%A1i_people" title="Ngái people">Ngai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hakka_Chinese" title="Hakka Chinese">Hakka</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Diu_people" title="San Diu people">San Diu</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETr.E1.BA.A7n_.C4.90.E1.BB.A9c_Lai20137_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETr.E1.BA.A7n_.C4.90.E1.BB.A9c_Lai20137-7">[7]</a></sup> as they descended from people speaking these dialects. After 1954, more than 50,000 Nùng led by Colonel <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vong_A_Sang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vong A Sang (page does not exist)">Vong A Sang</a> (or Swong A Sang) fled as refugees, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Passage_to_Freedom" title="Operation Passage to Freedom">joining the 1 million northern Vietnamese who fled south</a> and resettled in <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnam</a>. During the Vietnam War, the Chinese Nung soldiers were best-known for their loyalty to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Special_Forces" title="US Special Forces">US Special Forces</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IVKim2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IVKim2006-8">[8]</a></sup> and had a reputation as the most-feared fighters of all the minority groups trained by the Americans. After the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">Fall of Saigon</a> in 1975, many of the Nungs fled Vietnam as <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_boat_people" title="Vietnamese boat people">Boat People</a> political refugees, many to Hong Kong's and Malaysia's refugee camps. Most were resettled in the US, Canada, France, Australia etc.</p>
<p>Unlike the Chinese Nung, the Nùng (Tai-Kadai) migrated from southern <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a> and southeast <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> to northern Vietnam since the 1700s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWangsgard200912_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWangsgard200912-9">[9]</a></sup> A Vietnamese scholar, Nguyen Van Loi of the Vietnam Institute of Linguistics, has argued that the Nùng Dín sub-group arrived in Vietnam between the eighth and tenth centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrownGanguly2003217.2C_note_8:_Le_Si_Giao_.22A_Glimpse_of_the_Tay-Thai_speaking_groups_in_Vietnam.22_.27.27Vietnamese_studies.27.27_No.4_.281999.29.2Cpp_21-31._Nguyen_Van_Loi_of_the_Vietnam_Institute_of_Linguistics_has_argued.2C_however.2C_that_the_Nung_Din_were_the_first_Nung_to_arrive_in_Vietnam_between_the_eighth_and_tenth_centuries._Interview_with_author.2C_Hanoi.2C_November_2000._10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrownGanguly2003217.2C_note_8:_Le_Si_Giao_.22A_Glimpse_of_the_Tay-Thai_speaking_groups_in_Vietnam.22_.27.27Vietnamese_studies.27.27_No.4_.281999.29.2Cpp_21-31._Nguyen_Van_Loi_of_the_Vietnam_Institute_of_Linguistics_has_argued.2C_however.2C_that_the_Nung_Din_were_the_first_Nung_to_arrive_in_Vietnam_between_the_eighth_and_tenth_centuries._Interview_with_author.2C_Hanoi.2C_November_2000.-10">[10]</a></sup> Michael Howard cites Be, Nguyen and Chu (1992:48) as claiming that: though the clan named Nong / Nùng existed earlier, the term “did not refer to a separate ethnic name until the fifteenth century.” Along with Luu (1986: 170), Howard argues that those now officially categorized as Nùng in Vietnam have arrived there much more recently than their Tày cousins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson201024_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson201024-11">[11]</a></sup> This migration was not a single mass exodus, but rather as a series of small migrations as individual families and clans fled the political turmoil, civil war, and bloodshed that often occur in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_China" title="Southern China">Southern China</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a> in the nineteenth century, and went in search of new agricultural land. Their name Nùng derives from the clan name Nông, one of the four most powerful clans of an ethno-linguistic group of Central Tai-speakers, presently known as <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_people" title="Zhuang people">Zhuang</a> in southern China, and <a href="/wiki/Tay_people" title="Tay people">Tay</a>, Nung in Vietnam, who have historically and prehistorically inhabited the present-day Sino-Vietnamese borderlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWangsgard200914_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWangsgard200914-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1954 several thousand Nung Phàn Slình moved to South Vietnam as refugees and settled in the Tuyen Duc province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SILcitation_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SILcitation-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fugong_County" title="Fugong County">Fugong County</a> in China and <a href="/wiki/Kachin_State" title="Kachin State">Kachin State</a> in Myanmar, there is another ethnic group named Nung, speaking a <a href="/wiki/Tibeto-Burman_languages" title="Tibeto-Burman languages">Tibeto-Burman language</a> also called <a href="/wiki/Nung_language_(Sino-Tibetan)" title="Nung language (Sino-Tibetan)">Nung</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B9ng_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nùng's history may date back to the eleventh century with the rise of the Nùng (Nông 儂) clan, one of the four most powerful Central Tai speaking clans in the Sino-Vietnamese frontier region. In 1038, <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B9ng_T%E1%BB%93n_Ph%C3%BAc" title="Nùng Tồn Phúc">Nùng Tồn Phúc</a> <i>(Nong Quanfu, Chinese: 儂全福)</i>, a Nùng chieftain, proclaimed the founding of a state, <i>Chang Qi Guo (長其國 )</i>. The king of the Viet kingdom Dai Co Viet led an army into the region in the third month of 1039, captured Nùng Tồn Phúc and most of his family, and returned them to Dai Co Viet for execution. His 14-year-old son, <a href="/wiki/Nong_Zhigao" title="Nong Zhigao">Nùng Trí Cao</a> <i>(Nong Zhigao, Chinese: 儂智高)</i>, evaded capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nong_Zhigao" title="Nong Zhigao">Nùng Trí Cao</a> then rose up three times in 1041, 1048, 1052. But finally he was defeated by <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">the Song</a>, a Chinese dynasty at that times. After the defeat of Nùng Trí Cao, Many of the Nùng rebels fled to Vietnam, concentrating around <a href="/wiki/Cao_B%E1%BA%B1ng_Province" title="Cao Bằng Province">Cao Bằng</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1ng_S%C6%A1n_Province" title="Lạng Sơn Province">Lạng Sơn</a> provinces and became known as the Nùng. Barlow (2005) suggests that many of the original 11th-century rebels who fled to Vietnam were absorbed by the related Tày peoples of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nùng, although lacking a leader of the stature of <a href="/wiki/Nong_Zhigao" title="Nong Zhigao">Nùng Trí Cao</a>, rose up in 1352, 1430, 1434<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> and many other unrecorded rebellions.</p>
<p>In the 16th century the Zhuang, principally from <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a> and perhaps from southeast <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a>, began migrating into Vietnam. This movement was quickened by the acceleration of the cycle of disasters, and by the political events of the seventeenth century which brought larger numbers of Chinese immigrants into the region, such as the fall of the Ming, the rebellion of Wu Sangui, the Qing occupation, and the Muslim revolts in Yunnan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> This migration was a peaceful one which occurred family by family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> French administrators later identified a number of Nùng clans in the course of their ethnographic surveys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> These had incorporated Chinese place names in their clan names and hence indicate the place of their origin in China, such as the "Nùng Inh" clan, from Long Ying in the southwest of Guangxi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> Such other names as can be correlated with locations indicate that the migrants were primarily from the immediate frontier region of the southwest of Guangxi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The Nùng became increasingly numerous in the region, and were spread out through a long stretch of the Vietnamese northern border from <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1ng_S%C6%A1n_Province" title="Lạng Sơn Province">Lạng Sơn</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cao_B%E1%BA%B1ng_Province" title="Cao Bằng Province">Cao Bằng</a>, and about That Khe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/M%E1%BA%A1c_dynasty" title="Mạc dynasty">Mạc dynasty</a>, a Vietnamese dynasty ruled over the Vietnamese northeast highlands, profited from the migration in that they were able to draw upon Nùng manpower for their own forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1833, <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B4ng_V%C4%83n_V%C3%A2n" title="Nông Văn Vân">Nông Văn Vân</a>, a Nùng chieftain, led a rebellion against Vietnamese rule. He quickly took control of <a href="/wiki/Cao_B%E1%BA%B1ng_Province" title="Cao Bằng Province">Cao Bằng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuy%C3%AAn_Quang_Province" title="Tuyên Quang Province">Tuyên Quang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A1i_Nguy%C3%AAn_Province" title="Thái Nguyên Province">Thái Nguyên</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BA%A1ng_S%C6%A1n_Province" title="Lạng Sơn Province">Lạng Sơn</a> provinces, aiming to create a separate Tày-Nùng state in the northern region of Vietnam. His rising was eventually suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_dynasty" title="Nguyễn dynasty">Nguyễn dynasty</a> in 1835.</p>
<p>In the 1860s, the Nùng sided with Sioung (Xiong), a self-proclaimed <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a> king. Sioung's armies raided gold from Buddhist temples and seized large tracts of land from other people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The period from the <a href="/wiki/Taiping_Rebellion" title="Taiping Rebellion">Taiping Rebellion</a> (1850–64) to the early twentieth century was marked by continual waves of immigration by Zhuang/Nùng peoples from China into Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> These waves were a result of the continuous drought of Guangxi which made the thinly occupied lands of northern Vietnam an attractive alternative habitation. The immigration process was generally a peaceful one as the Nùng purchased land from the "Tho" owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-11"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The Nùng were superior to the Tho in cultivating wet rice and transformed poor lands, facilitating later migrations into adjoining areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-12"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The repeated violent incursions of the Taiping era and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Flag_Army" title="Black Flag Army">Black Flag</a> occupation accelerated the outflow of Tho as the bands from China were largely Zhuang who favored the Nùng at the expense of the Tho.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-13"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The Tho who remained became alienated from the Vietnamese government which could not offer protection and became clients of the Chinese and the Nùng.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-14"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nùng dominance became so pronounced that when <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen" title="Sun Yat-sen">Sun Yat-sen</a> wished to raise fighters in the region, he could recruit them from Nùng villages such as Na Cen and Na Mo, both on the Vietnamese side of the border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-15"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The French colonists, who began to invade Vietnam from 1858, saw this Nùng predominance as a threat, and found it convenient at that time to re-assert the primacy of the Vietnamese administrative system in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-16"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French, however, perhaps having less choice, tended to support the Tho and other minorities, often undifferentiated as "Man" in their reports—usually a reference to <a href="/wiki/Yao_people" title="Yao people">Yao</a>—as a counterweight against the Nùng.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-17"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1908, for example, following an incident in which <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen" title="Sun Yat-sen">Sun Yat-sen</a>'s mercenary Nung warriors had killed several French officers, the French offered a bounty of eight dollars for each head brought in by the "Man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-18"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup> The bounty was paid 150 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005_14-19"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarlow2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French colonial government took advantage of ancient tensions between the highland ethnic groups and the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Kinh</a> majority in Vietnam. In the northwest mountains, they set up a semi-autonomous minority federation called <a href="/wiki/Sip_Song_Chau_Tai" title="Sip Song Chau Tai">Sip Song Chau Tai</a> (<i>French:</i> <b>Pays Taï</b>), complete with armed militias and border guards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup> When war broke out in 1946, groups of Thai, H’mong and Muong in the northwest sided with the French and against the Vietnamese and even provided battalions to fight with the French troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup> But The Nùng and <a href="/wiki/Tay_people" title="Tay people">Tày</a> supported the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">Viet Minh</a> and provided the Vietnamese leader, <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>, with a safe base for his guerrilla armies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinahan2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinahan2012-17">[17]</a></sup> After defeating the French at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu" title="Battle of Dien Bien Phu">Dien Bien Phu in 1954</a>, the Viet Minh tried to win the allegiance of all of the northern ethnic minorities by creating two autonomous zones, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thai%E2%80%93Meo_Autonomous_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thai–Meo Autonomous Zone (page does not exist)">Thai–Meo Autonomous Zone</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Viet_Bac_Autonomous_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Viet Bac Autonomous Zone (page does not exist)">Viet Bac Autonomous Zone</a> respectively, allowing limited self-government within a “unified multi-national state”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, many Nùng fought alongside the North Vietnamese Army (NVA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Unification of Vietnam in 1975, Viet Bac Autonomous Zone in which the Nùng and Tày were most numerous was revoked by <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_Du%E1%BA%A9n" title="Lê Duẩn">Lê Duẩn</a>. the new government pursued a policy of forced assimilation of the minorities into the Vietnamese culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup> All education was conducted in the Vietnamese language, traditional customs were discouraged or outlawed, and minority people were moved from their villages into government settlements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup> At the same time the government created <i>“<a href="/wiki/New_Economic_Zones_program" title="New Economic Zones program">New Economic Zones</a>”</i> along the Chinese border and in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Highlands_(Vietnam)" title="Central Highlands (Vietnam)">Central Highlands</a>. Frequently this involved taking the best land in order to resettle thousands of people from the overcrowded lowlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manchester2002_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-manchester2002-16">[16]</a></sup> During the 1980s, an estimated 250,000 <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">ethic Vietnamese</a> were settled in the mountainous regions along the Chinese border, leading to a shortage of food in the region and much suffering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinahan2012_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinahan2012-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As tension arose between Vietnam and China in 1975, Hanoi feared the loyalty of the Nùng and <a href="/wiki/Hoa_people" title="Hoa people">the Chinese-Vietnamese</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> concerning that they would side with China. This comes from the fact that many of the Nùng just migrated into the Vietnamese border side and many of their relatives still lived in the other side of the border.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doi_Moi" title="Doi Moi">Doi Moi program</a></i> brought a shift in policy, including the creation of a government department responsible for minority affairs. Many of the changes and the liberalization that preserving the heritage of the Nung and other ethnic groups has a great appeal to tourists a source of significant income for Vietnam. Nonetheless, in many areas the minorities’ traditional lifestyles are fast being eroded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinahan2012_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinahan2012-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the territorial disputes between Vietnam and China in recent years, the ethnic minorities living in the borderland, including the Nùng, have been under the watchful eye of the Vietnamese government.</p>

<p>The Nùng support themselves through <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, such as farming on terraced hillsides, tending <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_paddies" title="Rice paddies">rice paddies</a>, and growing <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchard</a> products. They produce <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tangerine" title="Tangerine">tangerines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persimmon" title="Persimmon">persimmons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anise" title="Anise">anise</a>. They are also known for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handicrafts" title="Handicrafts">handicrafts</a>, making items from <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rattan" title="Rattan">rattan</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a>. They engage in <a href="/wiki/Carpentry" title="Carpentry">carpentry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">forging</a> also.</p>
<p>Prominent Nùng persons include <a href="/wiki/Kim_%C4%90%E1%BB%93ng" title="Kim Đồng">Kim Đồng</a> of the <a href="/wiki/August_Revolution" title="August Revolution">August Revolution</a> in 1945.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B9ng_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nung_language_(Tai)" title="Nung language (Tai)">Nùng language</a> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Tai_languages" title="Tai languages">Tai language family</a>; its written script was developed around the 17th century. It is close to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Zhuang" title="Standard Zhuang">Zhuang language</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B9ng_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Nùng practice an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_religion" title="Indigenous religion">indigenous religion</a> with <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animistic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totemism" title="Totemism">totemic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shamanism" title="Shamanism">shamanic</a> features similarly to other Tai ethnic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguy.E1.BB.85n_Th.E1.BB.8B_Y.C3.AAn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nguy.E1.BB.85n_Th.E1.BB.8B_Y.C3.AAn-2">[2]</a></sup> Others have adopted <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customs">Customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B9ng_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When drinking <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>, partakers cross hands and drink from the opposite glass to demonstrate trust. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" title="Fairy tales">Fairy tales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, and adherence to tradition and ethnic identity are strong characteristics of Nùng people.</p>
<p>The Nùng's traditional <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a> clothing, symbolising faithfulness, was made famous by <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Hồ Chí Minh</a>, worn when he returned to Vietnam in 1941.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%B9ng_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/N%C3%B4ng_Th%E1%BB%8B_Xu%C3%A2n" title="Nông Thị Xuân">Nông Thị Xuân</a> was a mistress of North Vietnamese communist leader <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a> in the 1950s, who was later murdered in 1957 by government agents.</p>

More significant differences developed between the Tay and Nung in the highlands to the north after the border between China and Vietnam was established in the eleventh century with the Nung living to the north of the border and most Tay living to the south.
