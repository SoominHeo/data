<p><b>The New School</b> is a private <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university">research university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">Lower Manhattan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, located mostly in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a>. From its founding in 1919 by <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a> New York educators, and for most of its history, the university was known as <b>The New School for Social Research</b>. Between 1997 and 2005 it was known as <b>New School University</b>. The university and each of its colleges were renamed in 2005.</p>
<p>The New School established the <b>University in Exile</b> in 1933 as a graduate division to serve as an academic haven for scholars escaping from <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> among other <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">adversarial regimes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-online-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1934, the University in Exile was chartered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">New York State</a> and its name was changed to the Graduate Faculty of Political and Social Science. In 2005, it adopted what had initially been the name of the whole institution, the New School for Social Research, while the larger institution was renamed The New School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssrhistory_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nssrhistory-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The New School has launched or housed a range of institutions such as the international research institute <a href="/wiki/World_Policy_Institute" title="World Policy Institute">World Policy Institute</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Vera_List_Center_for_Art_and_Politics" title="The Vera List Center for Art and Politics">the Vera List Center for Art and Politics</a>, the India China Institute, the Observatory on Latin America, and the Center for New York City Affairs. <a href="/wiki/Parsons_School_of_Design" title="Parsons School of Design">Parsons School of Design</a> is the New School's art school.</p>
<p>Approximately 9,300 students are enrolled in <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate degree</a> programs, organized into seven schools which teach a variety of disciplines, including <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a>, humanities, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_arts" title="Fine arts">fine arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, music, drama, finance, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The New School for Social Research was founded by a group of university professors and intellectuals in 1919 as a modern, progressive, free school where adult students could "seek an unbiased understanding of the existing order, its genesis, growth and present working."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Founders included economist and literary scholar <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Saunders_Johnson" title="Alvin Saunders Johnson">Alvin Johnson</a>, historian <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Beard" title="Charles A. Beard">Charles A. Beard</a>, economists <a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen" title="Thorstein Veblen">Thorstein Veblen</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Harvey_Robinson" title="James Harvey Robinson">James Harvey Robinson</a>, and philosophers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horace_M._Kallen" title="Horace M. Kallen">Horace M. Kallen</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Dewey" title="John Dewey">John Dewey</a>. Several founders were former professors at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>.</p>
<p>In October 1917, after Columbia University imposed a <a href="/wiki/Loyalty_oath" title="Loyalty oath">loyalty oath</a> to the United States upon the entire faculty and student body, it fired several professors. <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Beard" title="Charles A. Beard">Charles A. Beard</a>, Professor of Political Science, resigned his professorship at Columbia in protest. His colleague <a href="/wiki/James_Harvey_Robinson" title="James Harvey Robinson">James Harvey Robinson</a> resigned in 1919 to join the faculty at the New School.</p>
<p>The New School plan was to offer the rigorousness of postgraduate education without degree matriculation or degree prerequisites. It was theoretically open to anyone, as the adult division today called Schools of Public Engagement remains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The first classes at the New School took the form of lectures followed by discussions, for larger groups, or as smaller conferences, for "those equipped for specific research." In the first semester, 100 courses, mostly in economics and politics, were offered by an ad hoc faculty that included <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Sewall_Adams" title="Thomas Sewall Adams">Thomas Sewall Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Beard" title="Charles A. Beard">Charles A. Beard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horace_M._Kallen" title="Horace M. Kallen">Horace M. Kallen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Laski" title="Harold Laski">Harold Laski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wesley_Clair_Mitchell" title="Wesley Clair Mitchell">Wesley Clair Mitchell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen" title="Thorstein Veblen">Thorstein Veblen</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Harvey_Robinson" title="James Harvey Robinson">James Harvey Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Wallas" title="Graham Wallas">Graham Wallas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_B._Davenport" title="Charles B. Davenport">Charles B. Davenport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elsie_Clews_Parsons" title="Elsie Clews Parsons">Elsie Clews Parsons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roscoe_Pound" title="Roscoe Pound">Roscoe Pound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a> later pioneered the subject of Experimental Composition at the school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Motto">Motto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Motto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The New School uses "To the Living Spirit" as its motto. In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a> remarked that a plaque bearing the inscription 'be the Living Spirit' had been torn down by the Nazis from a building at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">University of Heidelberg</a>. He suggested that the University in Exile adopt that inscription as its motto, to indicate that the 'living spirit,' mortally threatened in Europe, would have a home in this country. <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Saunders_Johnson" title="Alvin Saunders Johnson">Alvin Johnson</a> adopted that idea, and the motto continues to guide the division in its present-day endeavors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="University_in_Exile">University in Exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: University in Exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Graduate Faculty of Political and Social Science was founded in 1933 as the University in Exile, to be a haven for scholars who had been dismissed from teaching positions by the Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fascist" title="Fascist">fascists</a> or had to flee <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-online-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The University in Exile was initially founded by the director of the New School, <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Saunders_Johnson" title="Alvin Saunders Johnson">Alvin Johnson</a>, through the generous financial contributions of <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Halle" title="Hiram Halle">Hiram Halle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a>. The University in Exile and its subsequent incarnations have been the intellectual heart of the New School. Notable scholars associated with the University in Exile include psychologists <a href="/wiki/Erich_Fromm" title="Erich Fromm">Erich Fromm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Wertheimer" title="Max Wertheimer">Max Wertheimer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aron_Gurwitsch" title="Aron Gurwitsch">Aron Gurwitsch</a>, political philosophers <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Arendt" title="Hannah Arendt">Hannah Arendt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leo_Strauss" title="Leo Strauss">Leo Strauss</a>, and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hans_Jonas" title="Hans Jonas">Hans Jonas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssrhistory_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nssrhistory-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1934, the University in Exile was chartered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">New York State</a> and its name was changed to the Graduate Faculty of Political and Social Science. In 2005 the Graduate Faculty was again renamed, this time taking the original name of the university, The New School for Social Research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssrhistory_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-nssrhistory-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".C3.89cole_libre_des_hautes_.C3.A9tudes">École libre des hautes études</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: École libre des hautes études">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The New School played a similar role with the founding of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_libre_des_hautes_%C3%A9tudes" title="École libre des hautes études">École Libre des Hautes Études</a> after the Nazi invasion of France. Receiving a charter from <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">de Gaulle</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_French" title="Free French">Free French</a> government in exile, the École attracted refugee scholars who taught in French, including philosopher <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Maritain" title="Jacques Maritain">Jacques Maritain</a>, anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a>, and linguist <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a>. The <i>École Libre</i> gradually evolved into one of the leading institutions of research in Paris, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes_en_Sciences_Sociales" title="École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales">École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales</a></i>, with which the New School maintains close ties.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dramatic_Workshop_.2F_School_of_Drama">Dramatic Workshop / School of Drama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dramatic Workshop / School of Drama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Between 1940 and 1949, the New School was host to the "<a href="/wiki/Dramatic_Workshop" title="Dramatic Workshop">Dramatic Workshop</a>," a theatre workshop and predecessor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_School_for_Drama" title="The New School for Drama">School of Drama</a> that was founded by German emigrant theatre director <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Piscator" title="Erwin Piscator">Erwin Piscator</a>. Important acting teachers during this period were <a href="/wiki/Stella_Adler" title="Stella Adler">Stella Adler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elia_Kazan" title="Elia Kazan">Elia Kazan</a>. Among the famous students of the Dramatic Workshop were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beatrice_Arthur" title="Beatrice Arthur">Beatrice Arthur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Belafonte" title="Harry Belafonte">Harry Belafonte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Curtis" title="Tony Curtis">Tony Curtis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Gazzara" title="Ben Gazzara">Ben Gazzara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_V._Gazzo" title="Michael V. Gazzo">Michael V. Gazzo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rod_Steiger" title="Rod Steiger">Rod Steiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Stritch" title="Elaine Stritch">Elaine Stritch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shelley_Winters" title="Shelley Winters">Shelley Winters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I attended The New School for Social Research for only a year, but what a year it was. The school and New York itself had become a sanctuary for hundreds of extraordinary European Jews who had fled Germany and other countries before and during World War II, and they were enriching the city's intellectual life with an intensity that has probably never been equaled anywhere during a comparable period of time.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_tradition">Philosophical tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Philosophical tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The New School for Social Research continues the Graduate Faculty's tradition of synthesizing <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">leftist</a> American intellectual thought and critical European philosophy. True to its origin and its firm roots within the University in Exile, The New School for Social Research, particularly its Department of Philosophy, is in the minority in the United States in offering students thorough training in the modern continental European philosophical tradition known as "<a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">Continental philosophy</a>." Thus, it stresses the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Parmenides" title="Parmenides">Parmenides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Leibniz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spinoza" title="Spinoza">Spinoza</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">Hume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Hegel</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard" title="Søren Kierkegaard">Kierkegaard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Marx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Nietzsche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Husserl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Heidegger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Arendt" title="Hannah Arendt">Arendt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Benjamin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Wittgenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Foucault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Derrida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Deleuze" title="Gilles Deleuze">Deleuze</a>, et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The thought of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_Theory" title="Critical Theory">Critical Theory</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_School" title="Frankfurt School">Frankfurt School</a>: <a href="/wiki/Max_Horkheimer" title="Max Horkheimer">Max Horkheimer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodor_Adorno" title="Theodor Adorno">Theodor Adorno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Marcuse" title="Herbert Marcuse">Herbert Marcuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jürgen Habermas">Jürgen Habermas</a>, et al. holds an especially strong influence on all divisions of the school. After the death of Hannah Arendt in 1975, the philosophy department revolved around <a href="/wiki/Reiner_Sch%C3%BCrmann" title="Reiner Schürmann">Reiner Schürmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%81gnes_Heller" title="Ágnes Heller">Ágnes Heller</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_rebranding">2005 rebranding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2005 rebranding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2005, the university was rebranded with a new <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> and all schools were officially renamed to include "The New School" within their formal names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW2007_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BW2007-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some faculty, students, and alumni expressed concern over the rebranding of the university, and especially the dramatic redesign of the logo from a six-sided <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shield</a> against a green background to a spray-painted <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> mark reading simply, in capital letters, "THE NEW SCHOOL" with, in smaller letters beneath, "A UNIVERSITY." They claimed that the university's new identity campaign, while maintaining a slick urban edge, did little to suggest academic rigor or collegiate legacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW2007_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-BW2007-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_rebranding">2015 rebranding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2015 rebranding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015 the New School rebranded using elements designed by Paula Scher of <a href="/wiki/Pentagram_(design_studio)" title="Pentagram (design studio)">Pentagram</a> using a bespoke font called "Neue".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the new logo, the school announced that it was combining Mannes, Jazz, and Drama into a College of Performing Arts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> in fall 2015, relocating most of the performing arts to Arnhold Hall at 55 West 13th St., where the School of Jazz had occupied two floors since 1986.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curriculum">Curriculum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Curriculum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike most U.S. universities, The New School has a "student-directed curriculum", which does not require its undergraduates to take general education courses. Instead, students are encouraged to explore before focusing on a major, selecting topics that are of interest to them. Although all "New Schoolers" are required to complete rigorous core training—usually of a literary, conservatory, or artistic nature—students are expected to be the primary designer of their own individualized and eclectic education.</p>
<p>The New School's curriculum is highly experimental and avant-garde, offering classes such as: "Heterodox Identities," "Games 101," "NYC: Graphic Gotham," "Punk &amp; Noise," "Masculinity in Asia," "Queer Culture," and "Play and Toil in the Digital Sweatshop."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The New School also offers a course titled "Social Media: The Power to Speak the Truth." The course explores the transformative history of the Internet and provides a working knowledge of the tools it offers for journalism and public action.</p>
<p>The university offers 81 degree/diploma programs and majors, with a student-to-faculty ratio of 9:1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">[24]</a></sup> This small class size allows The New School to teach most of its classes in the seminar style—especially at Eugene Lang College, which consistently ranks at the top of The Princeton Review's "class discussions encouraged" national listing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_New_School_Institutes_and_Research_Centers">The New School Institutes and Research Centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The New School Institutes and Research Centers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several important institutes and research centers at The New School which are focused on various study fields. Their work is concentrated in the following areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>International Affairs and Global Perspectives</li>
<li>Philosophy and Intellectual Culture</li>
<li>The Humanities Action Lab</li>
<li>Politics, Policy, and Society</li>
<li>Art, Design, and Theory</li>
<li>Environment</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enrollment_demographics">Enrollment demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Enrollment demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thirty Three percent of New School students are international,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colleges.usnews.rankingsandreviews.com_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-colleges.usnews.rankingsandreviews.com-26">[26]</a></sup> with 112 foreign countries being represented at the university. U.S. students come from all 50 states and the District of Columbia. 43% of them are people of color, and 5% of American students identify as more than one race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">[24]</a></sup> Of the entire student population, 63% receive financial aid, and 17% study abroad before graduating.</p>

<p>The New School's campus is composed of many buildings, most of which are minutes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Square_(New_York_City)" title="Union Square (New York City)">Union Square</a>.</p>
<p>The university's Parsons division also has affiliations with schools that operate independently but embrace Parsons' philosophy and teaching methodology, including <a href="/wiki/Parsons_Paris_(2013)" title="Parsons Paris (2013)">Parsons Paris</a> (France), India School of Design and Innovation (Mumbai, India), Parsons Shanghai (China), La Escuela de Diseño at <a href="/wiki/Altos_de_Chav%C3%B3n" title="Altos de Chavón">Altos de Chavón</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Romana,_Dominican_Republic" title="La Romana, Dominican Republic">La Romana, Dominican Republic</a> and Kanazawa International Design Institute, Kanazawa (Japan).</p>
<p>The New School opened the 16-story <a href="/wiki/The_New_School_University_Center" title="The New School University Center">University Center</a> at 65 5th Avenue in January 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The tower, which was designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skidmore,_Owings_and_Merrill" title="Skidmore, Owings and Merrill">Skidmore, Owings and Merrill</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Roger_Duffy" title="Roger Duffy">Roger Duffy</a>, is the biggest capital project the university has ever undertaken. The building, added classrooms, new residences, and event facilities to the downtown New York City campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> in addition to a library, and lecture hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> While the 65 Fifth Avenue plans were initially controversial among students and Village residents (spurring in 2009 a major student occupation was held at The New School's previous building on that site), plans for the University Center were adjusted in response to community concerns and have since been well received. In a review of the University Center's final design, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> architecture critic <a href="/wiki/Nicolai_Ouroussoff" title="Nicolai Ouroussoff">Nicolai Ouroussoff</a> called the building "a celebration of the cosmopolitan city."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_significance">Historical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Historical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several of the university buildings are certified by New York City as historical landmarks. Prominent among these is the egg-shaped Tishman Auditorium, considered by many to be the first building to employ modern architecture. It was designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Urban" title="Joseph Urban">Joseph Urban</a>, along with the entirety of The New School's historic 66 West 12th Street building. Thousands of writer's forums, author visits, political debates, award ceremonies, academic lectures, performances, and public hearings are held for both the academic community and general public throughout the year in Tishman.</p>
<p>Newer buildings have garnered a multitude of awards. Among these is The Sheila Johnson Design Center, which attracted media attention for its revolutionary design. In 2009, it won the SCUP's Excellence in Architecture Renovation/Adaptive Reuse Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chronicle.com_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-chronicle.com-34">[34]</a></sup> In addition to being a Parsons core academic building, the Center also serves as a public art gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The New School Welcome Center, located on 13th Street and Fifth Avenue, won the American Institute of Architects, New York Chapter's Interiors Merit Award in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Residence_halls">Residence halls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Residence halls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The university operates four residence halls:</p>
<ul>
<li>13th Street Residence Hall: A facility primarily serving first-year students, 13th Street Residence is popular for its close proximity to academic buildings and location just seconds away from the university's flagship library, Fogelman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>Loeb Hall: Loeb Hall is a co-ed residence hall located on 12th Street in the East Village. After the relocation of Student Health Services (which was once housed in Loeb), the lobby has been renovated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Stuyvesant Park Residence: Located in the East Village near Stuyvesant Town. It overlooks Stuyvesant Park and the Manhattan skyline. The residence consists of suite-style rooms and houses mostly first-year students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>Kerrey Hall at the University Center Residence: The New School's newest dormitory, "located on floors 8-16 of [the] new University Center at 5th Avenue and 14th Street, is a LEED-certified state-of-the-art residence that will house 617 [primarily undergraduate] students."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Former residence halls:</p>
<ul>
<li>20th Street Residence Hall: Located in Chelsea, 20th Street Residence offered some of the university's largest suites and housed mostly returning students and graduate students. It closed at end of the Spring 2014 semester.</li>
<li>William Street Residence: Located in Lower Manhattan near <a href="/wiki/South_Street_Seaport" title="South Street Seaport">South Street Seaport</a>. William Street was one of The New School's largest residence halls. It closed at the end of the Spring 2013 semester.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_collection">Art collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Art collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1931 the New School commissioned two mural cycles: José Clemente Orozco's "A Call for Revolution" and "Universal Brotherhood" and Thomas Hart Benton's epic America Today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The New School Art Collection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> was established in 1960 with a grant from the Albert A. List Foundation. The collection, now grown to approximately 1,800 postwar and contemporary works of art, includes examples in almost all media. Parts of it are exhibited throughout the campus. Notable artists such as <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kara_Walker" title="Kara Walker">Kara Walker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Serra" title="Richard Serra">Richard Serra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sol_LeWitt" title="Sol LeWitt">Sol LeWitt</a> all have pieces displayed in New School's academic buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_government">Student government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Student government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several student government and leadership councils at The New School. Among them are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.newschoolsenate.org" rel="nofollow">University Student Senate</a> (USS)</li>
</ul>
<p>The University Student Senate is composed of students from every division. Its composition is Parsons - 5, Lang - 3, New School for Public Engagement - 3, New School Social Research - 2, Jazz - 1, and Mannes - 1. The USS acts as the connection between students and administration, serving the needs and concerns of the students. The duties of the USS include giving a political statement regarding macro issues within the school (gender neutral bathrooms, divestment, etc.), as well as micro issues. Additionally, the USS provide a cap of $5,000 funding for initiatives that serves the entire New School.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_organizations">Student organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Student organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The New School houses over 50 recognized student organizations, most of which are geared towards artistic endeavors or civic engagement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Notable among these are The Theatre Collective, which stages numerous dramatic productions throughout the year, Students for a Democratic Society (SDS), Debate Team, ReNew School (sustainability and environmental advocacy group) Moxie (feminist alliance), the New Urban Grilling Society (NUGS), and The Radical Student Union (RSU).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student-run_media">Student-run media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Student-run media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Print</dt>
</dl>
<p>A noted student newspaper, <i>The New School Free Press</i>, also known as <i>NSFP</i> is widely distributed throughout the campus. Hard print copies are available in most academic buildings, while an online edition is available as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Students at Eugene Lang College publishes <i>Release</i>, a student-run literary magazine.</p>
<dl>

<p>WNSR, a student-run, faculty-advised online-only radio station, also operates at the university. Programming is currently delivered in the form of streamable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mp3" title="Mp3">mp3s</a> and, in the near future, subscribable <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcasts</a>. It is a station for all divisions of The New School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics_and_recreation">Athletics and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Athletics and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current Athletics and Recreation Director Diane Yee joined The New School in August 2012. On October 25, 2012 a school-wide election was held to select a mascot, where The New School Narwhals were born. On January 25, 2013 the athletics logo was launched, designed by Parsons’ student Matthew Wolff (G '14).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The department began in December 2008 under its original name Recreation and Intramural sports. The initial director, Michael McQuarrie, held the position for four years. He built a relationship with the McBurney YMCA where intramurals continue to be held on Wednesday night's and created the ongoing New School Olympics and charitable 5K Turkey Trot.</p>
<p>The Narwhals feature several intercollegiate teams: basketball (2009), cross country (2010), cycling (2013), soccer (2013), tennis (2014), ultimate Frisbee (2014). The New School Narwhals are an independent school, unaffiliated with the NCAA, but regularly compete against NCAA Division III schools.</p>
<p><b>Basketball</b> – competes regularly against Cooper Union, Culinary Institute of America, Pratt Institute, and Vaughn College</p>
<p><b>Cross Country</b> – competes in CUNYAC and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVIAC" title="HVIAC">HVIAC</a> conference invitationals as an unaffiliated school</p>
<p><b>Cycling</b> – a member of the Eastern Collegiate Cycling Conference</p>
<p><b>Soccer</b> – competes against Cooper Union, Culinary Institute of America, St. Joseph’s College, and Vaughn College</p>
<p>In addition to sports, the recreation department offers a myriad of free fitness classes to its community including boxing, dance, <a href="/wiki/High-intensity_interval_training" title="High-intensity interval training">HIIT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pilates" title="Pilates">Pilates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tai_chi" title="Tai chi">tai chi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">yoga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zumba" title="Zumba">Zumba</a>. Personal training is also offered at an affordable rate ranging from $16.50-$40/session.</p>
<p>Outdoor Adventure trips are offered several times/week and what started to be wilderness in nature (camping, hiking, rafting) has expanded to include excursions such as archery, biking, horseback riding, skiing/snowboarding, surfing, rock climbing and trapeze.</p>
<p>Yee has increased programming to include a second charitable race that takes place annually in April called the 5K Rabbit Run. She has also started the Urban Hunt (a scavenger hunt around campus and the Village) and Club New (a dance party for first-year students the weekend before first day of classes). Her biggest accomplishment is creating Narwhal Nation. The students now have a sense of community and a way to bring all seven schools together.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Each August, community residents, the university, and local businesses stop traffic on the 12th street block for one afternoon. A massive block party is then thrown, celebrating the return of New School students to <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a>. Another block party is held in the spring, usually during one of the first warm weeks of the semester.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fusion_Fashion_Show&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Fusion Fashion Show (page does not exist)">The Fusion Fashion Show</a> is one of The New School's, and New York City's, biggest industry events. Each year, first and second-year undergraduates from Parsons compete directly with the fashion program's fiercest rival—<a href="/wiki/Fashion_Institute_of_Technology" title="Fashion Institute of Technology">Fashion Institute of Technology</a> (FIT)—in the show, vying for awards and the title of best overall school.</li>
<li>The New School annually hosts the Parsons Fashion Benefit, which is consistently attended by celebrities and industry moguls. It showcases the work of the current graduating class and raises money to fund scholarships for the fashion design program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>Every April, the university celebrates <a href="/wiki/V-Day_(movement)" title="V-Day (movement)">V-Day</a> for two weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Originally started by the student feminist organization Moxie (which has since disbanded), "V-Day at The New School" has been adopted by the community and become a major part of campus life. In conjunction with V-Day, the university also recognizes each April as Sexual Assault and Prevention Awareness Month. During this time, T-shirts painted with feminist art and statements against sexual and domestic violence are hung on a clothesline in the windows of every major academic building. This is done as part of the Clothesline Project, and seeks to engage the public with New School's activist community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>New School's annual Take Back the Night March is also held during April. In this event, students lead the university community through the streets of Greenwich Village in a public demonstration against sexual violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activist_culture_and_social_change">Activist culture and social change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Activist culture and social change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, The New School has been associated with leftist politics, campus activism, civic engagement, and social change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> It is a "Periclean University", or member <a href="/wiki/Project_Pericles" title="Project Pericles">Project Pericles</a>, meaning that it teaches "education for social responsibility and participatory citizenship as an essential part of their educational programs, in the classroom, on the campus, and in the community."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The New School is one of nine American universities to be inducted into Ashoka's "Changemaker" consortium for social entrepreneurship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, NYC Service awarded New School special recognition in The College Challenge, a volunteer initiative, for the "widest array of [civic] service events both on and off campus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Miriam Weinstein also cites the Eugene Lang division in her book, <i>Making a Difference Colleges: Distinctive Colleges to Make a Better World</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kerrey_presidency_and_opposition">Kerrey presidency and opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Kerrey presidency and opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kerrey" title="Bob Kerrey">Bob Kerrey</a> became president of The New School in 2000. Kerrey drew praise and criticism for his streamlining of the university, as well as <a href="/wiki/Censure" title="Censure">censure</a> for his support of the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>, generally opposed by the university's faculty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, Kerrey appointed <a href="/wiki/Arjun_Appadurai" title="Arjun Appadurai">Arjun Appadurai</a> as <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">provost</a>. Appadurai resigned as provost in early 2006, but retained a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenured" title="Tenured">tenured</a> faculty position. He was succeeded by Joseph Westphal, yet on December 8, 2008 Kerrey announced that Westphal was stepping down to accept a position in President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> transition team. Kerrey then took the highly unorthodox step of appointing himself to the provost position while remaining president. This decision was strongly criticised by faculty and other members of the university community as a power-grab involving potential conflicts of interest. This was seen as a threat to scholarly integrity since the role of provost in overseeing the academic functions of a university has traditionally been insulated from fundraising and other responsibilities of a college president. After a series of rifts including protests involving student occupations of university buildings, Kerrey later appointed Tim Marshall, Dean of <a href="/wiki/Parsons_School_of_Design" title="Parsons School of Design">Parsons School of Design</a>, as Interim Provost through June 2011. Marshall has since been reappointed in this role.</p>
<p>On December 10, 2008, 74 of the New School's senior professors gave a vote of no confidence for the New School's former president, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kerrey" title="Bob Kerrey">Bob Kerrey</a>. By December 15, 98% of the university's full-time faculty had voted no confidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 17, over 100 students <a href="/wiki/Occupation_(protest)" title="Occupation (protest)">barricaded themselves</a> in at a dining hall on the campus while hundreds more waited on the streets outside. They considered the current school administration opaque and harmful. Their chief demand, among others, was that Bob Kerrey resign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The students soon enlarged their occupied area, blocking security and police from entering the building. At 3 AM the next morning, the students left the building after Kerrey agreed to some of their demands (the most important elements on their first list of demands were not agreed to), including increased study space and amnesty from any actions performed during the protest. He did not, however, concede to resignation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In total, the occupation lasted 30 hours.</p>
<p>In January 2009, a student organization called The New School In Exile issued a public threat to shut down the university on April 1, unless the President and Chief Operating Officer were removed. They subsequently stole an entire edition of the student newspaper, after the paper published an article revealing their plans and names, and defaced the university's presidential residence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On April 10, 2009, students, mostly from New School but also from other New York colleges, reoccupied the building at 65 Fifth Avenue, this time holding the entire building for about six hours. Once again, the students demanded the resignation of Bob Kerrey. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Police_Department" title="New York Police Department">New York Police Department</a> arrested the occupiers; the New School students involved were then suspended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 7, 2009, Kerrey announced he would fulfill his presidency at the University through the end of his term and expressed his intent to leave office in June 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> However, he ended up resigning a semester early, on January 1, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 26, 2010, a letter was sent out stating that the board of trustees had approved the appointment of <a href="/wiki/David_E._Van_Zandt" title="David E. Van Zandt">Dr. David E. Van Zandt</a>, who succeeded Bob Kerrey and become the 8th president of the New School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_sustainability">Environmental sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Environmental sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, <a href="/wiki/The_Princeton_Review" title="The Princeton Review">The Princeton Review</a> gives the university a sustainability rating of 94 out of 99.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-66">[66]</a></sup> In 2010, the organization also named The New School one of America's "286 Green Colleges."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The New School has a student-led environment and sustainability group, called Renew School, as well as full-time employees devoted to the school's sustainability. The university signed the Presidents' Climate Commitment and <a href="/wiki/PlaNYC" title="PlaNYC">PlaNYC</a>. The institution's sustainability website outlines many goals and projects for the future which will hopefully help The New School receive a good rating in the 2010 College Sustainability Report Card.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_College_Sustainability_Report_Card_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_College_Sustainability_Report_Card-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_movement">Labor movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Labor movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">adjunct</a> faculty in several divisions of the New School began to form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_union" title="Labor union">labor union</a> chapter under the auspices of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Auto_Workers" title="United Auto Workers">United Auto Workers</a>. Though the university at first tried to contest the unionization, after several rulings against it by regional and national panels of the <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Board" title="National Labor Relations Board">National Labor Relations Board</a> the university recognized the local chapter, ACT-UAW, as the bargaining agent for the faculty. As a result of a near strike in November 2005 on the part of the adjunct faculty, the ACT-UAW union negotiated its first contract which included the acknowledgment of previously unrecognized part-time faculty at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mannes_College_of_Music" title="Mannes College of Music">Mannes College The New School for Music</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_McCain_protests">The McCain protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: The McCain protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a>'s speech at the graduation ceremony of 2006 generated a large amount of media attention, due to vocal student opposition in print,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> radio,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> and television<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> media, and the speech of Jean Rohe, a graduating senior who spoke before McCain and directly confronted the controversy, saying that the senator "does not reflect the values upon which the university was founded."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, New School <a href="/wiki/Trustee" title="Trustee">trustee</a> and long-time Clinton fundraiser <a href="/wiki/Norman_Hsu" title="Norman Hsu">Norman Hsu</a> was arrested after being found to fleeing from the authorities after a felony theft conviction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In 2008, he was convicted and sentenced to three years prison for defrauding millions of dollars of investors' money in an intricate <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi scheme</a>. In response, the Hillary Clinton campaign returned $850,000 of his campaign contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leo_Hindery" title="Leo Hindery">Leo Hindery</a>, a New School trustee, had donated nearly $270,000 to the John Edwards campaign by late 2007. Other politically involved New School trustees include <a href="/wiki/Howard_Gittis" title="Howard Gittis">Howard Gittis</a>, who was a "bundler" for the John McCain campaign, and George Haywood, who was part of Senator Barack Obama's inner fund-raising circle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_P._Hochberg" title="Fred P. Hochberg">Fred P. Hochberg</a>, former Dean of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milano_The_New_School_for_Management_and_Urban_Policy" title="Milano The New School for Management and Urban Policy">Milano The New School for Management and Urban Policy</a>, was a fundraiser for Hillary Clinton and liaison to the gay community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Commission_on_Terrorist_Attacks_Upon_the_United_States" title="National Commission on Terrorist Attacks Upon the United States">National Commission on Terrorist Attacks Upon the United States</a> held its eleventh public hearing on May 18–19, 2004, at The New School. The two-day hearing examined the response of local and federal emergency response departments on September 11, 2001, and considered how to improve these critical functions in the event of future terrorist attacks. The Commission heard from the current and former top-level officials in the fire, police, and emergency management departments of New York City, of the <a href="/wiki/Port_Authority_of_New_York_and_New_Jersey" title="Port Authority of New York and New Jersey">Port Authority of New York and New Jersey</a>, and of <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia" title="Arlington County, Virginia">Arlington County in Virginia</a>. Secretary of Homeland Security Thomas J. Ridge and former New York City Mayor Rudolph W. Giuliani also testified. Two staff statements were presented during the course of the hearing. "At this hearing, the Commission turns its attention to the day of September 11, 2001. We will focus on what confronted civilians and first responders during the attacks, how they made decisions under adverse conditions, and what first responders communicated to civilians and to each other," said Commission Chair Thomas H. Kean. “The Commission also will explore the state of the emergency preparedness and response today,” said Commission Vice Chair Lee H. Hamilton. "We will examine what steps have been taken since 9-11 to improve our preparedness against terrorist attacks and other emergencies, and whether we need national standards of preparedness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearances_in_media">Appearances in media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Appearances in media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In 1986, P.M. Rutkoff and W.B. Scott wrote <i>New School: A History of The New School For Social Research</i>, a book about the university's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li>Claus-Dieter Krohn's <i>Intellectuals In Exile: Refugee Scholars at The New School for Social Research</i> was translated into English and released by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Press" title="University of Massachusetts Press">University of Massachusetts Press</a> in 1993. The book is an in-depth examination of The New School, the University in Exile, and the work of scholars who worked within these institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li>After Eugene Lang College ranked #1 nationally in the Princeton Review's "Intramural Sports Unpopular or Nonexistent" category, <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> featured "In Search of the Worst Sports College In America", an article about The New School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
