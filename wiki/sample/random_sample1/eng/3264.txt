<p>The <b>New Wave</b> is a movement in <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> produced in the 1960s and 1970s and characterized by a high degree of experimentation, both in form and in content, a "literary" or artistic sensibility, and a focus on "<a href="/wiki/Soft_science_fiction" title="Soft science fiction">soft</a>" as opposed to hard science. New Wave writers often saw themselves as part of the <a href="/wiki/Literary_modernism" title="Literary modernism">modernist</a> tradition and sometimes mocked the traditions of <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp</a> science fiction, which some of them regarded as stodgy, adolescent and poorly written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Wave_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The New Wave science fiction of the 1960s emphasized stylistic experimentation and literary merit over scientific accuracy or prediction. It was conceived as a deliberate break from the traditions of pulp SF, which many of the writers involved considered irrelevant and unambitious. The most prominent source of New Wave science fiction was the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/New_Worlds_(magazine)" title="New Worlds (magazine)">New Worlds</a></i> under the editorship of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Moorcock" title="Michael Moorcock">Michael Moorcock</a>, who assumed the position in 1964. Moorcock sought to use the magazine to "define a new avant-garde role" for science fiction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by the use of "new literary techniques and modes of expression."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was also a period marked by the emergence of a greater variety of voices in science fiction, most notably the rise in the number of female writers, including <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Russ" title="Joanna Russ">Joanna Russ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ursula_K._Le_Guin" title="Ursula K. Le Guin">Ursula K. Le Guin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Tiptree,_Jr." title="James Tiptree, Jr.">James Tiptree, Jr.</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Wave_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "New Wave" is borrowed from the French film movement the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouvelle_vague" title="Nouvelle vague">nouvelle vague</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wolf_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wolf-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gary_K._Wolfe" title="Gary K. Wolfe">Gary K. Wolfe</a>, professor of humanities and English at <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_University" title="Roosevelt University">Roosevelt University</a>, identifies the introduction of the term New Wave to SF<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wolf_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-wolf-4">[4]</a></sup> as occurring in 1966 in an essay<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magazine_of_Fantasy_%26_Science_Fiction" title="The Magazine of Fantasy &amp; Science Fiction">The Magazine of Fantasy &amp; Science Fiction</a></i> written by <a href="/wiki/Judith_Merril" title="Judith Merril">Judith Merril</a>, who was indirectly yet it seems unambiguously referring to that term in order to comment on the experimental fiction that had begun to appear in the English magazine <i>New Worlds</i>, after <a href="/wiki/Michael_Moorcock" title="Michael Moorcock">Michael Moorcock</a> assumed editorship in 1964. However, Judith Merril denies she ever used that term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Merril later popularized this fiction in the United States through her edited anthology <i>England Swings SF: Stories of Speculative Fiction</i> (Doubleday 1968), although an earlier anthology (<a href="/wiki/Harlan_Ellison" title="Harlan Ellison">Harlan Ellison</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Visions" title="Dangerous Visions">Dangerous Visions</a></i> [Doubleday 1967]) has also come to be referred to as a key work of New Wave science fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_and_predecessors">Influences and predecessors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Wave_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influences and predecessors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though the New Wave is generally considered to have begun in the 1960s, some of its tenets can be found in <a href="/wiki/H._L._Gold" title="H. L. Gold">H. L. Gold</a>'s editorship of <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Science_Fiction" title="Galaxy Science Fiction">Galaxy</a></i>, a science fiction magazine which began publication in 1950. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gunn_(author)" title="James Gunn (author)">James Gunn</a> described Gold's focus as being "<i>not on the adventurer, the inventor, the engineer, or the scientist, but on the average citizen</i>,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and according to SF historian David Kyle, Gold's work was to lead inevitably to the New Wave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The New Wave did not define itself as a development from the science fiction which came before it, but rather reacted against it. The New Wave writers believed that the tropes of the pulp and Golden Age periods had become worn out, and should be abandoned: <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a> stated in 1962 that "science fiction should turn its back on space, on interstellar travel, extra-terrestrial life forms, (and) galactic wars",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and Brian Aldiss said in <i>Trillion Year Spree: The History of Science Fiction</i> that "the props of SF are few: rocket ships, telepathy, robots, time travel...like coins, they become debased by over-circulation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harry_Harrison_(writer)" title="Harry Harrison (writer)">Harry Harrison</a> summarised the period by saying "old barriers were coming down, pulp taboos were being forgotten, new themes and new manners of writing were being explored".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>New Wave writers began to look outside the traditional scope of science fiction for influence, and many looked to the example of beat writer <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a>, to the point where New Wave authors <a href="/wiki/Philip_Jos%C3%A9_Farmer" title="Philip José Farmer">Philip José Farmer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barrington_J._Bayley" title="Barrington J. Bayley">Barrington J. Bayley</a> wrote pastiches of his work ("The Jungle Rot Kid on the Nod" and "The Four Colour Problem", respectively) and <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a> published an admiring essay in an issue of New Worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Burroughs' use of experimentation such as the cut-up technique and his appropriation of science fiction tropes in radical ways proved the extent to which prose fiction could prove revolutionary, and many New Wave writers sought to emulate this style.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ursula_K._Le_Guin" title="Ursula K. Le Guin">Ursula K. Le Guin</a>, one of the women writers to emerge in the 1960s, describes the transition to the New Wave era thus:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Without in the least dismissing or belittling earlier writers and work, I think it is fair to say that science fiction changed around 1960, and that the change tended toward an increase in the number of writers and readers, the breadth of subject, the depth of treatment, the sophistication of language and technique, and the political and literary consciousness of the writing. The sixties in science fiction were an exciting period for both established and new writers and readers. All the doors seemed to be opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This leaves the question as to why "science fiction changed around 1960", why "all the doors seemed to be opening." Rob Latham writing in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Review_of_Science_Fiction" title="New York Review of Science Fiction">New York Review of Science Fiction</a></i> identifies three trends which he argues are to prevail immediately prior to the advent of any new genre movement. His argument is illustrated by reference to the 1960s New Wave and the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> in the 1980s. In both cases the preliminary conditions would be related to changes in technology as well as economic conditions impacting the publication and dissemination of science fiction writing, seen as generating a "widespread" sense of malaise among writers and fans, coincidental or coherent with a generational phenomenon, the retirement or obvious decline in productivity of a number of major authors whose output had dominated the previous decade; finally the perceptible emergence of fresh thematic material which might previously have been inhibited by prevailing orthodoxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movement">Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Wave_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is no consensus on a precise starting point of the New Wave – <a href="/wiki/Adam_Roberts_(British_writer)" title="Adam Roberts (British writer)">Adam Roberts</a> refers to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Bester" title="Alfred Bester">Alfred Bester</a> as having singlehandedly invented the genre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and in the introduction to a collection of <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Brackett" title="Leigh Brackett">Leigh Brackett</a>'s short fiction, Michael Moorcock referred to her as one of the genre's "true godmothers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, it is widely accepted among critics that the New Wave began in England with the SF magazine <i>New Worlds</i> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Moorcock" title="Michael Moorcock">Michael Moorcock</a> who was appointed editor in 1964 (first issue number 142, May and June<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:251</sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[note 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the American magazines <i><a href="/wiki/Amazing_Stories" title="Amazing Stories">Amazing Stories</a></i>, with Cele Goldsmith as editor, and the respected <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magazine_of_Fantasy_%26_Science_Fiction" title="The Magazine of Fantasy &amp; Science Fiction">Magazine of Fantasy &amp; Science Fiction</a></i> had from the very start had a leaning towards unusually literary stories, Moorcock turned that into a concerted policy. No other science fiction magazine sought as consistently to distance itself from traditional science fiction as much as <i>New Worlds</i>. By the time it ceased regular publication it had backed away from the science fiction genre itself, styling itself as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental_fiction" title="Experimental fiction">experimental</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_journal" title="Literary journal">literary journal</a>.</p>
<p>Under Moorcock's editorship "galactic wars went out; drugs came in; there were fewer encounters with aliens, more in the bedroom. Experimentation in prose styles became one of the orders of the day, and the baleful influence of William Burroughs often threatened to gain the upper hand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldiss1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-aldiss1-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup> <a href="/wiki/Judith_Merril" title="Judith Merril">Judith Merril</a> observed:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>this magazine [<i>New Worlds</i>] was the publishing thermometer of the trend that was dubbed "the New Wave". In the United States the trend created an intense, incredible controversy. In Britain people either found it of interest or they didn't, but in the States it was heresy on the one hand and wonderful revolution on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merril1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-merril1-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:162–163</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As an anthologist and speaker Merril with other authors had been advocating a reestablishment of sf within the literary mainstream and higher literary standards before. Her "incredible controversy" is characterized by David Hartwell in the opening sentence of a book chapter entitled "New Wave: The Great War of the 1960s": "Conflict and argument are an enduring presence in the SF world, but literary politics has yielded to open warfare on the largest scale only once."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hart1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-hart1-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:141</sup> The heresy was beyond the experimental and explicitly provocative as inspired by Burroughs. In all coherence with the literary <i>nouvelle vague</i> although not in close association to it, and addressing a much less restricted pool of readers, the New Wave was reversing the standard hero's attitude toward action and science. It illustrated egotism - by depriving the plot of all motivation toward a rational explanation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>David Hartwell<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hart1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-hart1-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:146</sup> commenting on Algis J. Budrys writing in the review column of <i>Galaxy</i> magazine notes the "ringing scorn and righteous indignation" with which Budrys' discussion vibrates in "one of the classic diatribes against Ballard and the new mode of SF then emergent":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A story by J. G. Ballard, as you know, calls for people who don't think. One begins with characters who regard the physical universe as a mysterious and arbitrary place, and who would not dream of trying to understand its actual laws. Furthermore, in order to be the protagonist of a J. G. Ballard novel, or anything more than a very minor character therein, you must have cut yourself off from the entire body of scientific education. In this way, when the world disaster – be it wind or water – comes upon you, you are under absolutely no obligation to do anything about it but sit and worship it. Even more further, some force has acted to remove from the face of the world all people who might impose good sense or rational behavior on you, so that the disaster proceeds unchecked and unopposed except by the almost inevitable thumb-rule engineer type who for his individual comfort builds a huge pyramid (without huge footings) to resist high winds, or trains a herd of alligators and renegade divers to help him out in dealing with deep water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i>New Worlds</i> author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Disch" title="Thomas Disch">Thomas Disch</a> would not earn better quotes from Budrys in the December 1966 review for his "ineptly written" <i>The Genocide</i>, a work of "unrelieved trash" which was filled with a horde of "dumb, resigned victims."</p>
<p>Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dreams_Our_Stuff_Is_Made_Of" title="The Dreams Our Stuff Is Made Of">The Dreams Our Stuff Is Made Of</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Disch" title="Thomas Disch">Thomas Disch</a> observed that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Literary movements tend to be compounded, in various proportions, of the genius of two or three genuinely original talents, some few other capable or established writers who have been co-opted or gone along for the ride, the apprentice work of epigones and wannabes, and a great deal of hype. My sense of the New Wave, with twenty-five years of hindsight, is that its irreducible nucleus was the dyad of J. G. Ballard and Michael Moorcock, ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disch1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-disch1-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:105</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ballard and Moorcock engendered much animosity from the established SF community.</p>
<p>In 1963 Michael Moorcock wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Let's have a quick look at what a lot of science fiction lacks. Briefly, these are some of the qualities I miss on the whole – passion, subtlety, irony, original characterization, original and good style, a sense of involvement in human affairs, colour, density, depth, and, on the whole, real feeling from the writer...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1962 Ballard had been writing:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I've often wondered why s-f shows so little of the experimental enthusiasm which has characterized painting, music and the cinema during the last four or five decades, particularly as these have become wholeheartly speculative, more and more concerned with the creation of new states of mind, constructing fresh symbols and languages where the old cease to be valid. …</p>
<p>The biggest developments of the immediate future will take place, not on the Moon or Mars, but on Earth, and it is <i>inner space</i>, not outer, that need to be explored. The only truly alien planet is Earth. In the past the scientific bias of s-f has been towards the physical sciences – rocketry, electronics, cybernetics – and the emphasis should switch to the biological sciences. Accuracy, that last refuge of the unimaginative, doesn't matter a hoot. …</p>
<p>It is that <i>inner</i> space-suit which is still needed, and it is up to science fiction to build it!<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ballard1-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:197</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Roger Luckhurst points out that Ballard's essay "Which Way to Inner Space?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ballard1-28">[27]</a></sup> "showed the influence of media theorist <a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan" title="Marshall McLuhan">Marshall McLuhan</a> and the 'anti-psychiatry' of <a href="/wiki/R._D._Laing" title="R. D. Laing">R. D. Laing</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luck_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-luck-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:148</sup> Luckhurst traces the influence of both these thinkers in Ballard's fiction, in particular <i>The Atrocity Exhibition</i> (1970)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luck_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-luck-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:152</sup> Another central concern of the New Wave was a fascination with <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> – that the world (and the universe) must tend to disorder, to eventually run down to 'heat death'. Ballard provided</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>an explicitly cosmological vision of entropic decline of the universe in his magisterial story "The Voices of Time", which appeared in 1960. It provided a matrix of ideas that subsequent New Wave writing teased out in various contexts. Perhaps the best instance of this elaboration was <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Zoline" title="Pamela Zoline">Pamela Zoline</a>'s "The Heat Death of the Universe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard1_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-ballard1-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:158</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Like other writers for <i>New Worlds</i> Zoline uses "science-fictional and scientific language and imagery to describe perfectly 'ordinary' scenes of life", and by doing so produces "altered perceptions of reality in the reader."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-james_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-james-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:172</sup></p>
<p>Judith Merril, "whose annual anthologies were the first heralds of the coming of the [New Wave] cult,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:105</sup> writing in 1967 in <i>The Magazine of Fantasy and Science Fiction</i> contrasts the SF New Wave (which she here terms 'The New Thing') in England and the United States:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>They call it the New Thing.</p>
<p>The people who call it that mostly don't like it, and the only general agreements they seem to have are that Ballard is its Demon and I am its prophetess – and that it is what is wrong with Tom Disch, and with British s-f in general. …</p>
<p>The American counterpart is less cohesive as a "school" or "movement": it has had no single publication in which to concentrate its development, and was, in fact, till recently, all but excluded from the regular s-f magazines. But for the same reasons, it is more diffuse and perhaps more widespread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:28</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Judith Merril's annual anthologies (1957–1968<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>), Damon Knight's <i><a href="/wiki/Orbit_(anthology_series)" title="Orbit (anthology series)">Orbit</a></i> series, and Harlan Ellison's <i>Dangerous Visions</i> featured American writers inspired by British writers (although some of the writers anthologized were British).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landon_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-landon-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:156</sup> Brooks Landon, professor of English at the University of Iowa, says of <i>Dangerous Visions</i> that it</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>was innovative and influential before it had any readers simply because it was the first big original anthology of SF, offering prices to its writers that were competitive with the magazines. The readers soon followed, however, attracted by 33 stories by SF writers both well-established and relatively unheard of. These writers responded to editor Harlan Ellison's call for stories that could not be published elsewhere or had never been written in the face of almost certain censorship by SF editors. Among the stories Ellison received were the almost <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">Joycean</a> "<a href="/wiki/Riders_of_the_Purple_Wage" title="Riders of the Purple Wage">Riders of the Purple Wage</a>," by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_Jose_Farmer" title="Philip Jose Farmer">Philip Jose Farmer</a>, "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma_Angels&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carcinoma Angels (page does not exist)">Carcinoma Angels</a>", by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Spinrad" title="Norman Spinrad">Norman Spinrad</a>, and "<a href="/wiki/Aye,_and_Gomorrah" title="Aye, and Gomorrah">Aye, and Gomorrah</a>…" by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_R._Delany" title="Samuel R. Delany">Samuel R. Delany</a>, as well as stories by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Aldiss" title="Brian Aldiss">Brian Aldiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a>, J. G. Ballard, <a href="/wiki/John_Brunner_(novelist)" title="John Brunner (novelist)">John Brunner</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Sladek" title="John Sladek">John Sladek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">Roger Zelazny</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_R._Bunch" title="David R. Bunch">David R. Bunch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Sturgeon" title="Theodore Sturgeon">Theodore Sturgeon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carol_Emshwiller" title="Carol Emshwiller">Carol Emshwiller</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sonya_Dorman" title="Sonya Dorman">Sonya Dorman</a>. [T]o SF readers, especially in the United States, <i>Dangerous Visions</i> certainly felt like a revolution ... <i>Dangerous Visions</i> marks an emblematic turning point for American SF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landon_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-landon-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:157</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The New Wave also had a political subtext.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Most of the 'classic' writers had begun writing before the Second World War, and were reaching middle age by the early 1960s; the writers of the so-called New Wave were mostly born during or after the war, and were not only reacting against the sf writers of the past, but playing their part in the general youth revolution of the 1960s which had such profound effects upon Western culture. It is no accident that the New Wave began in Britain at the time of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, and took off in the United States at the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hippies" title="Hippies">hippies</a> – both, therefore at a time of cultural innovation and generational shake-up …<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-james_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-james-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:167</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eric_S._Raymond" title="Eric S. Raymond">Eric S. Raymond</a>, looking at the New Wave with an even narrower political focus, observes:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The New Wave's inventors (notably Michael Moorcock, J. G. Ballard and Brian Aldiss) were British socialists and Marxists who rejected individualism, linear exposition, happy endings, scientific rigor and the U.S.'s <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural hegemony</a> over the SF field in one fell swoop. The New Wave's later American exponents were strongly associated with the New Left and opposition to the Vietnam War, leading to some rancorous public disputes in which politics was tangled together with definitional questions about the nature of SF and the direction of the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>For example, Judith Merril, "one of the most visible -- and voluble -- apostles of the New Wave in 1960s sf"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latham2_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-latham2-36">[35]</a></sup> remembers her return from England to the United States:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>So I went home ardently looking for a revolution. I kept searching until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1968_Democratic_Convention" title="1968 Democratic Convention">Chicago Democratic Convention in 1968</a>. I went to Chicago partly to seek out a revolution, if there was one happening, and partly because my seventeen-year-old daughter … wanted to go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merril1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-merril1-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:167</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Merril records later "At the end of the Convention week, the taste of America was sour in all our mouths,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merril1_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-merril1-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:169</sup> and "by the end of the Sixties, Merril was a political refugee living in Canada."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hart1_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-hart1-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:142</sup></p>
<p>Roger Luckhurst disagrees with those critics (he gives the example of Thomas Clareson) who perceive the New Wave in terms of rupture, suggesting that such a model</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>doesn't quite seem to map onto the American scene, even though the wider conflicts of the 1960s liberalization in universities, the civil rights movement and the cultural contradictions inherent in consumer society were starker and certainly more violent than in Britain. The young turks within SF also had an ossified 'ancient regime' to topple: John Campbell's intolerant right-wing editorials for 'Astounding Science Fiction' (which he renamed 'Analog' in 1960) teetered on the self parody. In 1970, when the campus revolt against American involvement in Vietnam reached its height and resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Kent_State_shootings" title="Kent State shootings">National Guard shooting four students dead in Kent State University</a>, Campbell editorialized that the 'punishment was due', and rioters should expect to be met with lethal force. Vietnam famously divided the SF community to the extent that, in 1968, 'Galaxy' magazine carried two adverts, one signed by writers in favour and one by those against the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luck_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-luck-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:160</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>But caution is needed when assessing any literary movement, for example science fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Sterling" title="Bruce Sterling">Bruce Sterling</a>, reacting to his association with another SF movement in the 1980s remarked:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When did the New Wave SF end? Who was the last New Wave SF writer? You can't be a New Wave SF writer today. You can recite the numbers of them: Ballard, Ellison, Spinrad, Delaney, blah, blah, blah. What about a transitional figure like Zelazny? A literary movement isn't an army. You don't wear a uniform and swear allegiance. It's just a group of people trying to develop a sensibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Similarly Rob Latham observes:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>... indeed, one of the central ways the New Wave was experienced, in the US and Britain, was as a "liberated" outburst of erotic expression, often counterpoised, by advocates of the "New Thing"(as Merril called it), with the priggish Puritanism of the Golden Age. Yet this stark contrast, while not unreasonable, tends ultimately, as do most of the historical distinctions drawn between the New Wave and its predecessors, to overemphasize rupture at the expense of continuity, effectively "disappearing" some of the pioneering trends in 1950s sf that paved the way for the New Wave's innovations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latham2_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-latham2-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:252</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Bearing this proviso in mind it is still possible to sum up the New Wave in terms of rupture as is done for example by Darren Harris-Fain of <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_State_University" title="Shawnee State University">Shawnee State University</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The split between the New Wave and everyone else in American SF during the late 1960s was nearly as dramatic as the division at the same time between young protesters and what they called "the establishment," and in fact, the political views of the younger writers, often prominent in their work, reflect many contemporary concerns. New Wave accused what became de facto the old wave of being old-fashioned, patriarchal, imperialistic, and obsessed with technology; many of the more established writers thought the New Wave shallow, said that its literary innovations were not innovations at all (which in fact, outside of SF, they were not), and accused it of betraying SF's grand view of humanity's role in the universe. Both assertions were largely exaggerations, of course, and in the next decade both trends would merge into a synthesis of styles and concerns. However, in 1970 the issue was far from settled and would remain a source of contention for the next few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harfain_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-harfain-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13–14</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_and_lasting_influence">Decline and lasting influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Wave_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Decline and lasting influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the opening paragraph of an essay<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latham3_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-latham3-39">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:296</sup> on the New Wave Rob Latham relates that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the August 1970 issue of the SFWA Forum, a publication circulated to members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_Fiction_Writers_of_America" title="Science Fiction Writers of America">Science Fiction Writers of America</a>, Harlan Ellison remarked that the controversy over the New Wave, which had consumed the field during the late 1960s, seemed to have been "blissfully laid to rest." There never was, he claimed,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>any real conflict among the writers. It was all a manufactured controversy, staged by fans to hype their own participation in the genre. Their total misunderstanding of what was happening (not unusual for fans, as history … shows us) managed to stir up a great deal of pointless animosity and if it had any real effect I suspect it was in the unfortunate area of causing certain writers to feel they were unable to keep up and consequently they slowed their writing output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>
<p>Latham remarks that this analysis by Harlan Ellison "obscures Ellison's own prominent role – and that of other professional authors and editors such as Judith Merril, Michael Moorcock, Lester Del Rey, Frederik Pohl, and Donald A. Wollheim – in fomenting the conflict, …"</p>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latham3_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-latham3-39">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:296</sup></p>
<p>In the early 1970s a number of writers and readers pointed out that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>winners of the <a href="/wiki/Nebula_Award" title="Nebula Award">Nebula Awards</a>, created by the Science Fiction Writers of America (SWFA) in 1965, tended to be very different from recipients of the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">Hugo Awards</a>, given by fans at the annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Science_Fiction_Convention" title="World Science Fiction Convention">World Science Fiction Convention</a> (the Worldcon), arguing that this highlighted the fact that many writers had departed from readers' tastes into self-indulgence. …</p>
<p>While some writers and fans continued to argue about the New Wave until the end of the 1970s – in <i>The World of Science Fiction, 1926–1976: The History of a Subculture</i>, for instance, Lester Del Ray devotes several pages to castigating the movement – for the most part the controversy died down as the decade wore on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harfain_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-harfain-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:20</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The closing of New Worlds magazine in 1970 "marked the containment of New Wave experiment with the rest of the counter-culture. The various limping manifestations of New World across the 1970s … demonstrated the posthumous nature of its avant-gardism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luck_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-luck-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:168</sup></p>
<p>In an essay <i>The Alien Encounter</i> Professor <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Parrinder" title="Patrick Parrinder">Patrick Parrinder</a>'s states that "any meaningful act of defamiliarization can only be relative, since it is not possible for man to imagine what is utterly alien to him; the utterly alien would also be meaningless."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parrinder_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-parrinder-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:48</sup> He continues later:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Within SF, however, it is not necessary to break with the wider conventions of prose narrative in order to produce work that is validly experimental. The "New Wave" writing of the 1960s, with its fragmented and surrealistic forms, has not made a lasting impact, because it cast its net too wide. To reform SF one must challenge the conventions of the genre on their own terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parrinder_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-parrinder-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:55–56</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Veteran science fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Jack_Williamson" title="Jack Williamson">Jack Williamson</a> (1908–2006) when asked in 1991: "Did the [New] Wave's emphasis on experimentalizm and its conscious efforts to make SF more 'literary' have any kind of permanent effects on the field?" replied:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>After it subsided -- it's old hat now -- it probably left us with a sharpened awareness of language and a keener interest in literary experiment. It did wash up occasional bits of beauty and power. For example, it helped launch the careers of such writers as [Samuel R] Chip Delany, Brian Aldiss, and Harlan Ellison, all of whom seem to have gone on their own highly individualistic directions. But the key point here is that New Wave SF failed to move people. I'm not sure if this failure was due to its pessimistic themes or to people feeling the stuff was too pretentious. But it never really grabbed hold of people's imaginations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It has been observed that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>there is something efficacious in sf's marginality and always tenuous self-identity -- its ambiguous generic distinction from other literary categories -- and, perhaps more importantly, in its distinction from what has variously been called realist, mainstream, or mundane fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:289</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>David Hartwell's maintains that after the New Wave, science fiction had still managed to retain this "marginality and tenuous self-identity":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The British and American New Wave in common would have denied the genre status of SF entirely and ended the continual development of new specialized words and phrases common to the body of SF, without which SF would be indistinguishable from mundane fiction in its entirety (rather than only out on the borders of experimental SF, which is properly indistinguishable from any other experimental literature). The denial of special or genre status is ultimately the cause of the failure of the New Wave to achieve popularity, which, if it had become truly dominant, would have destroyed SF as a separate field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hart1_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-hart1-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:153</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>, a science fiction writer who began his career with John W, Campbell in Astounding magazine in the 1940s, said of the New Wave: "I hope that when the New Wave has deposited its froth and receded, the vast and solid shore of <i>science fiction</i> will appear once more."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:388</sup></p>
<p>Asimov himself was to illustrate just how that "SF shore" did indeed re-emerged, vast, solid—but changed. A biographer of Asimov noted that during the 1960s</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>stories and novels that Asimov must not have liked and must have felt were not part of the science fiction he had helped to shape were winning acclaim and awards. He also must have felt that science fiction no longer needed him. His science fiction writing, … became even more desultory and casual.</p>
<p>Asimov's return to serious writing in 1971 with <i>The Gods Themselves</i> (when much of the debate about the New Wave had dissipated) was an act of courage …<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:105</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Darren Harris-Fain observes on this return to writing SF by Asimov that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the novel [<i>The Gods Themselves</i>] is noteworthy for how it both shows that Asimov was indeed the same writer in the 1970s that he had been in the 1950s and that he nonetheless had been affected by the New Wave even if he was never part of it. His depiction of an alien ménage a trois, complete with homoerotic scenes between the two males, marks an interesting departure from his earlier fiction, in which sex of any sort is conspicuously absent. Also there is some minor experimentation with structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harfain_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-harfain-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:43</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Other themes dealt with in the novel are concerns for the environment and "human stupidity and the delusional belief in human superiority", both frequent topics in New Wave SF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harfain_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-harfain-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44</sup></p>
<p>Commenting in 2002 on the publication of the 35th Anniversary edition of the <i>Dangerous Visions</i> anthology edited by Harlan Ellison, the critic Greg L. Johnson remarked that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>if the New Wave did not entirely revolutionize the way SF was written, (the exploration of an invented world through the use of an adventure plot remains the prototypical SF story outline), they did succeed in pushing the boundaries of what could be considered SF, and their use of stylistic innovations from outside SF helped raise standards. It became less easy for writers to get away with stock characters spouting wooden dialogue laced with technical jargon. Such stories still exist, and are still published, but are no longer typical of the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Isaac Asimov agrees that "on the whole, the New Wave was a good thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-47">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:137</sup> He describes several "interesting side effects" of the New Wave. Firstly that it brought non-American SF to the fore and that afterwards SF was an international phenomenon. Other changes noted were that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the New Wave encouraged more and more women to begin reading and writing science fiction…. The broadening of science fiction meant that it was approaching the 'mainstream' … in style and content. It also meant that increasing numbers of mainstream novelists were recognizing the importance of changing technology and the popularity of science fiction, and were incorporating science fiction motifs into their own novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-47">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138–139</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The noted academic writer on science fiction <a href="/wiki/Edward_James_(historian)" title="Edward James (historian)">Edward James</a> sums up the New Wave and its impact as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The American New Wave was, on the whole, quite unlike the British. The latter was effectively a group of people associated with a magazine that had a particular programme …, whereas even those American writers who gathered in London at the time, like [Samuel R] Delaney, [Thomas M] Disch, and [John] Sladek, were individuals pursuing their <i>own</i> ends, not those of Ballard or Moorcock. As a 'movement', the American New Wave was even less real than the British; it was no more than a concatenation of talent flourishing at the same time and bringing new ideas and new standards to the writing of sf. The British New Wave had few lasting effects, even in Britain; the American New Wave ushered in a great expansion of the field and of its readership. No doubt the writers did not achieve this success on their own. It may be noted, for instance, that this burst of originality occurred at almost exactly the same times as the three seasons of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Original_Series" title="Star Trek: The Original Series">Star Trek</a></i>, which certainly contributed to the expansion of sf's readership. Whether or not much of this boom can be attributed to the American New Wave, it is clear that the rise in literary and imaginative standards associated with the late 1960s contributed a great deal to some of the most original writers of the 1970s, including <a href="/wiki/John_Crowley" title="John Crowley">John Crowley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Haldeman" title="Joe Haldeman">Joe Haldeman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ursula_K._Le_Guin" title="Ursula K. Le Guin">Ursula K. Le Guin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Tiptree,_Jr." title="James Tiptree, Jr.">James Tiptree, Jr.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Varley_(author)" title="John Varley (author)">John Varley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-james_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-james-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:176</sup></p>
</blockquote>
