<p>The <b>new wave of American heavy metal</b> (commonly abbreviated as <b>NWOAHM</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal music</a> movement that originated in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> during the early to mid-1990s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOTFP-TGOGMP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-LOTFP-TGOGMP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> and expanded most in the early to mid-2000s. Some of the bands considered part of the movement had formed as early as the late 1980s, but did not become influential or reach popular standing until the following decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOTFP-TGOGMP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-LOTFP-TGOGMP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> The term itself borrows from the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">new wave of British heavy metal</a> dating to 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> NWOAHM includes a wide variety of styles, including <a href="/wiki/Alternative_metal" title="Alternative metal">alternative metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Groove_metal" title="Groove metal">groove metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_metal" title="Industrial metal">industrial metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nu_metal" title="Nu metal">nu metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metalcore" title="Metalcore">metalcore</a>.</p>
<p>Although the term is used by the media with increasing frequency, the definition has not been finished completely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> This is due in part to the growing addition of bands that assimilate to common styles in NWOAHM (as defined below), yet have not differentiated greatly enough as to garner a new genre moniker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe-Young_2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-Young_2007-3">[3]</a></sup> One description by longtime metal author Garry Sharpe-Young helps classify the NWOAHM as a "marriage of European-style riffing and throaty vocals"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe-Young_2007_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-Young_2007-3">[3]</a></sup> Several of the bands within the NWOAHM are credited with bringing heavy metal back into the mainstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOTFP-TGOGMP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-LOTFP-TGOGMP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_wave_of_American_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The new wave of American heavy metal has its origins in a group of <a href="/wiki/Post-grunge" title="Post-grunge">post-grunge</a> acts from the 1990s that brought heavy metal "back to its core brutality" and drawing not from the traditional <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> formula but from <a href="/wiki/Thrash_metal" title="Thrash metal">thrash metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> In the book <i>The Next Generation of Rock &amp; Punk</i>, <a href="/wiki/Joel_McIver" title="Joel McIver">Joel McIver</a> acknowledged <a href="/wiki/Korn" title="Korn">Korn</a> as the pioneers of the new wave of American heavy metal, and also credits them as the first band labeled as <a href="/wiki/Nu_metal" title="Nu metal">nu metal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nu-Metal_pp._10-12_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nu-Metal_pp._10-12-5">[5]</a></sup> The nu metal genre was popular in the late 1990s and early 2000s. Other roots of NWOAHM are attributed to bands such as <a href="/wiki/Pantera" title="Pantera">Pantera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biohazard_(band)" title="Biohazard (band)">Biohazard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Machine_Head_(band)" title="Machine Head (band)">Machine Head</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamb_of_God_09.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Lamb_of_God_09.jpg/220px-Lamb_of_God_09.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Lamb_of_God_09.jpg/330px-Lamb_of_God_09.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Lamb_of_God_09.jpg/440px-Lamb_of_God_09.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The producers behind the 2005 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Metal:_A_Headbanger%27s_Journey" title="Metal: A Headbanger's Journey">Metal: A Headbanger's Journey</a></i> have written of the NWOAHM: "In essence, NWOAHM can embody the seething aggression of the 'hardcore' hormone, but play a type of acrobatic, precise, technical thrash/<a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a> synthesis regularly touched by the melody of traditional metal, but often just briefly. Vocally, these bands huddle around Pantera-derived roar, leaning toward a death metal bark, but often with 'clean' or 'sung' vocals as ear candy, sometimes from a member of the band who is not the front man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metal_2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metal_2005-6">[6]</a></sup> They also reference <a href="/wiki/Unearth" title="Unearth">Unearth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shadows_Fall" title="Shadows Fall">Shadows Fall</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God_(band)" title="Lamb of God (band)">Lamb of God</a> as "leaders of the pack".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metal_2005_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metal_2005-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the book <i>New Wave of American Heavy Metal</i>, when listing the wave's most popular contributors, Garry Sharpe-Young stated: "...the groups that broke the metal scene into new territory after <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> [were] <a href="/wiki/Pantera" title="Pantera">Pantera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biohazard_(band)" title="Biohazard (band)">Biohazard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Machine_Head_(band)" title="Machine Head (band)">Machine Head</a>. From there it gets really diverse, crossing the spectrum from <a href="/wiki/Melodic_death_metal" title="Melodic death metal">melodic death metal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Progressive_metal" title="Progressive metal">progressive metal</a> and everything in between."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> Sharpe-Young described bands such as Pantera, Biohazard and Machine Head as <i>neo-metal</i>, writing that the band Pantera started a new time period of heavy metal that involved both Biohazard and Machine Head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe-Young_2007_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-Young_2007-3">[3]</a></sup> Sharpe-Young lists the broad range of styles in the new wave of American heavy metal movement as ranging from the <a href="/wiki/Christian_metal" title="Christian metal">Christian metalcore</a> scene, the 70's-inspired <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coheed_and_Cambria" title="Coheed and Cambria">Coheed and Cambria</a>, melodic death metal, and the <a href="/wiki/Screamo" title="Screamo">screamo</a> and "sub-Gothique" <a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">emocore</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_Trio" title="Alkaline Trio">Alkaline Trio</a> and <a href="/wiki/My_Chemical_Romance" title="My Chemical Romance">My Chemical Romance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup> Beyond this, the genre encompasses a number of different styles including <a href="/wiki/Alternative_metal" title="Alternative metal">alternative metal</a>, groove metal, hardcore punk and <a href="/wiki/Metalcore" title="Metalcore">metalcore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWOAHM_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-NWOAHM-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maximummetal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-maximummetal-7">[7]</a></sup> despite the fact that metalcore and hardcore punk pre-date NWOAHM by almost twenty years.</p>

<p>A list of notable bands who emerged during the NWOAHM era of music:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-3" style="-moz-column-count: 3; -webkit-column-count: 3; column-count: 3;">
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="0-9">0-9</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_wave_of_American_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 0-9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
