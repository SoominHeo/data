<p><b>New Britain</b> is a city in <a href="/wiki/Hartford_County,_Connecticut" title="Hartford County, Connecticut">Hartford County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is located approximately 9 miles (14 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a>. According to 2010 Census, the population of the city is 73,206.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popest2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-popest2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the southernmost of the communities encompassed within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartford-Springfield_Knowledge_Corridor" title="Hartford-Springfield Knowledge Corridor">Hartford-Springfield Knowledge Corridor</a> metropolitan region, New Britain is home to <a href="/wiki/Central_Connecticut_State_University" title="Central Connecticut State University">Central Connecticut State University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charter_Oak_State_College" title="Charter Oak State College">Charter Oak State College</a>.</p>
<p>The city's official nickname is the "Hardware City" because of its history as a manufacturing center and as the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Black_%26_Decker" title="Stanley Black &amp; Decker">Stanley Black &amp; Decker</a>. Because of its large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_American" title="Polish American">Polish population</a>, the city is often playfully referred to as "New Britski."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New Britain was settled in 1687 and then was incorporated as a new parish under the name New Britain Society in 1754. Chartered in 1850 as a township and in 1871 as a city, New Britain had separated from the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington</a>, Connecticut. A consolidation charter was adopted in 1905.</p>
<p>During the early part of the 20th century, New Britain was known as the "Hardware Capital of the World", as well as "Hardware City". Major manufacturers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanley_Works" title="Stanley Works">The Stanley Works</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philip_Corbin_(manufacturer)" title="Philip Corbin (manufacturer)">P&amp;F Corbin Company</a> (later Corbin Locks), and North &amp; Judd, were headquartered in the city.</p>

<p>In 1843 <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Trent_Stanley" title="Frederick Trent Stanley">Frederick Trent Stanley</a> established Stanley's Bolt Manufactory in New Britain to make door bolts and other wrought-iron hardware. In 1857 his cousin <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Stanley_(industrialist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Stanley (industrialist) (page does not exist)">Henry Stanley</a> founded The Stanley Rule and Level Company in the city. Planes invented by <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bailey_(inventor)" title="Leonard Bailey (inventor)">Leonard Bailey</a> and manufactured by the Stanley Rule and Level Company, known as "Stanley/Bailey" planes, were prized by woodworkers of the late 19th and early 20th centuries and remain popular among wood craftsmen today. The two companies merged in 1920, and the Stanley Rule and Level Company became the Hand Tools Division of Stanley Works.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Clothes_hanger" title="Clothes hanger">wire coat hanger</a> was invented in 1869 by O. A. North of New Britain. In 1895, the <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> technique of <a href="/wiki/Dribbling" title="Dribbling">dribbling</a> was developed at the New Britain <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a>. In 1938, New Britain High School competed in the high school football national championship game in Baton Rouge, Louisiana. 1954 saw the development of <a href="/wiki/Racquetball" title="Racquetball">racquetball</a>, also at the YMCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="City_motto">City motto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: City motto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New Britain's motto, <i><span lang="la">Industria implet alveare et melle fruitur</span></i> – translated from Latin – means "Industry fills the hive and enjoys the honey." This phrase was coined by <a href="/wiki/Elihu_Burritt" title="Elihu Burritt">Elihu Burritt</a>, a 19th-century New Britain resident, diplomat, philanthropist and social activist.</p>
<p>In 2007 it was reported that the Latin word for "honey" in the motto had been a typo for decades; it should be <i><span lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/melle#Latin" title="wikt:melle">melle</a></span></i>, but it had long been misspelled as <i>mele</i>. Former mayor William McNamara, who unsuccessfully tried to fix it during his term, suggested "to either fix the spelling immediately" or "switch to the English version of the motto."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As controversy arose from the matter, the word was superseded with the correct spelling, <i><span lang="la">melle</span></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_topography">Geography and topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography and topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 13.4 square miles (34.7 km²), of which, 13.3 square miles (34.6 km²) of it is land and 0.1 square miles (0.2 km²) of it (0.52%) is water.</p>
<p>New Britain's terrain is mostly made up of soft, rolling hills and young Connecticut forest. The many parks are populated with trees, and in small, undeveloped areas, there is also brushy woods. New Britain's streets also have many trees lining the sides of the roads. Many front yards in the northern half of the city have at least one tree. One or two streams flow through New Britain, undisturbed by the development.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polish_community">Polish community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Polish community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Britain has the largest <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> population of any city in Connecticut, and by 1930 a quarter of the city was ethnically Polish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Also referred to as "Little Poland", the city's Broad Street neighborhood has been home to a considerable number of Polish businesses and families since 1890. On September 23, 2008, through the urging of the Polonia Business Association, the New Britain City Council unanimously passed a resolution officially designating New Britain's Broad Street area as "Little Poland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In recent years, the Polish community has been credited with revitalizing the area both culturally and economically. Media is served by three Polish language newspapers and a television station, and many businesses and civil agencies are bilingual. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Post" title="US Post">post office</a> branch in Little Poland is the only one in the nation with the word "post" written in Polish to welcome visitors. Each year, a Little Poland festival is held on the last Sunday of April.</p>
<p>Notable visitors to the Polish district have included <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">Presidents</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> on July 8, 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1969, as then-Cardinal Karol Wojtyla, the future Pope <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">John Paul II</a> gave a mass at Sacred Heart Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A statue was erected in his honor in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Dubbed the city's "Polish heart" by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i>, Little Poland caught the attention of Polish Ambassador to the US <a href="/wiki/Ryszard_Schnepf" title="Ryszard Schnepf">Ryszard Schnepf</a>, who toured the area with US Senators <a href="/wiki/Chris_Murphy_(Connecticut_politician)" title="Chris Murphy (Connecticut politician)">Chris Murphy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Blumenthal" title="Richard Blumenthal">Richard Blumenthal</a>, US Congresswoman <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Esty" title="Elizabeth Esty">Elizabeth Esty</a>, as well as several members of the Polish <a href="/wiki/Sejm" title="Sejm">Sejm</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accent">Accent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Polish influence could be in part responsible for some vowel qualities of the distinctive <a href="/wiki/New_England_English#Western_New_England" title="New England English">New Britain accent</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> such as nasalization of reduced vowels before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n/</span>. A more characteristic feature of the central Connecticut dialect is distinguished by systematic substitution of the <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> in place of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t]</span> for an unreleased <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> word-finally and before syllabic consonants (e.g. "eight" is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛɪʔ]</span> instead of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[eɪt]</span>), leading to the <a href="/wiki/Shibboleth" title="Shibboleth">shibboleth</a> pronunciation of <i>New Britain</i> for many locals being <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nuˈbɹɪʔɨː̃n]</span>, instead of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nuˈbɹɪtn̩]</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New Britain is home to the global headquarters of the Fortune 500 manufacturing conglomerate <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Black_%26_Decker" title="Stanley Black &amp; Decker">Stanley Black &amp; Decker</a>. Other notable companies headquartered in New Britain include Creed Monarch, Guida's Dairy and Polamer Precision.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_institutions">Cultural institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New Britain is the home of the New Britain Symphony Orchestra, which was formed in 1948 by several faculty members at the former Teacher’s College, now Central Connecticut State University. The orchestra performed its first concert under the direction of Dr. Etzel Willhoit, President of the College. The orchestra was official organized as the New Britain Symphony Society, Inc. in 1952 by Helen Kilduff, who was at the time the supervisor of music in New Britain’s public schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Maestro Ertan Seyyar Sener is the current conductor and musical director of the New Britain Symphony Orchestra, a role he assumed during the 2014–2015 concert season.</p>
<p>New Britain is also home to the historic Repertory Theatre. The theatre was originally the Norden Street Lodge. In 1955, a theater group known as the Repertory Theatre of New Britain acquired the Norden Street Lodge. The lodge became known as The Repertory Theatre, as it is to this day. Members of the Repertory Theatre of New Britain sold bonds to theatre members and friends in order to acquire the property. Each year, bondholders received interest on their investment, sometimes reaching as much as 65 cents! Since 1955, at least four theatrical productions have been produced each year in the space. Since 1999, the theatre has also been home to numerous cultural events, children’s theatre and playwriting competitions. There have been notable names to grace the stage, such as Meryl Streep, who was active in children’s theatre at the Repertory Theatre during her time as a student at Yale University.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Britain,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Route_9" title="Connecticut Route 9">Connecticut Route 9</a> is the city's main expressway connecting traffic between <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Interstate_84_in_Connecticut" title="Interstate 84 in Connecticut">I-84</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_91_in_Connecticut" title="Interstate 91 in Connecticut">I-91</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Old_Saybrook,_Connecticut" title="Old Saybrook, Connecticut">Old Saybrook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middletown,_Connecticut" title="Middletown, Connecticut">Middletown</a>. <a href="/wiki/Interstate_84_in_Connecticut" title="Interstate 84 in Connecticut">I-84</a> itself clips the northwestern corner of the city. Public transportation is provided by <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Transit" title="Connecticut Transit">Connecticut Transit</a>.</p>
<p>Downtown New Britain serves as the southern terminus of <a href="/wiki/CTfastrak" title="CTfastrak">CTfastrak</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a> line. Operated by <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Transit" title="Connecticut Transit">Connecticut Transit</a>, the project officially broke ground in May 2012, and became operational in March 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CTfastrak_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-CTfastrak-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The route's northern terminus is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Hartford)" title="Union Station (Hartford)">Union Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a>. There are also CTfastrak stations on <a href="/wiki/East_Main_Street_(CDOT_station)" title="East Main Street (CDOT station)">East Main Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Street_(CDOT_station)" title="East Street (CDOT station)">East Street</a>, the latter near Central Connecticut State University. New Britain is served by <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Transit_New_Britain_and_Bristol" title="Connecticut Transit New Britain and Bristol">Connecticut Transit New Britain</a>.</p>
<p>New Britain has a nearby <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> station in adjacent Berlin. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vermonter_(Amtrak)" title="Vermonter (Amtrak)">Vermonter</a></i> (once daily) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shuttle_(Amtrak)" title="Shuttle (Amtrak)">Shuttle</a></i> (multiple daily arrivals/departures) provide service to destinations throughout the northeastern United States. There are also plans underway for a Springfield–Hartford–New Haven commuter rail, which would have Berlin as one of its stations.</p>
