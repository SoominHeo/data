<p><i><b>The New York Review of Books</b></i> (or <i><b>NYREV</b></i> or <i><b>NYRB</b></i>) is a semi-monthly magazine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with articles on literature, culture, economics, science and current affairs. Published in New York City, it is inspired by the idea that the discussion of important books is an indispensable literary activity. <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i> called it "the premier literary-intellectual magazine in the English language."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postobit_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Postobit-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1970 writer <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a> described it as "the chief theoretical organ of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical_Chic" title="Radical Chic">Radical Chic</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolfe_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfe-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Review</i> publishes long-form reviews and essays, often by well-known writers, original poetry, and has letters and personals advertising sections that had attracted critical comment. In 1979 the magazine founded the <i><a href="/wiki/London_Review_of_Books" title="London Review of Books">London Review of Books</a></i>, which soon became independent. In 1990 it founded an Italian edition, <i>la Rivista dei Libri</i>, published until 2010. <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Silvers" title="Robert B. Silvers">Robert B. Silvers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Epstein" title="Barbara Epstein">Barbara Epstein</a> edited the paper together from its founding in 1963, until her death in 2006. Since then, Silvers has been the sole editor. The <i>Review</i> has a book publishing division, established in 1999, called <a href="/wiki/New_York_Review_Books" title="New York Review Books">New York Review Books</a>, which publishes classics, collections and children's books. Since 2010, the journal has hosted an online blog written by its contributors.</p>
<p>The <i>Review</i> celebrated its 50th anniversary in 2013, and a <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> film called <i><a href="/wiki/The_50_Year_Argument" title="The 50 Year Argument">The 50 Year Argument</a></i> documents the history and influence of the paper.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_description">History and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_York_Review_of_Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_York_Review_of_Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The New York Review</i> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Silvers" title="Robert B. Silvers">Robert B. Silvers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Epstein" title="Barbara Epstein">Barbara Epstein</a>, together with publisher <a href="/wiki/A._Whitney_Ellsworth" title="A. Whitney Ellsworth">A. Whitney Ellsworth</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and writer <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Hardwick_(writer)" title="Elizabeth Hardwick (writer)">Elizabeth Hardwick</a>. They were backed and encouraged by Epstein's husband, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Epstein" title="Jason Epstein">Jason Epstein</a>, a vice president at <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a> and editor of <a href="/wiki/Vintage_Books" title="Vintage Books">Vintage Books</a>, and Hardwick's husband, poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lowell" title="Robert Lowell">Robert Lowell</a>. In 1959 Hardwick had published an essay, "The Decline of Book Reviewing", in <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper's Magazine">Harper's</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decline_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Decline-6">[6]</a></sup> where Silvers was then an editor, in a special issue that he edited called "Writing in America".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gevisser_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gevisser-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup> Her essay was an indictment of American book reviews of the time, "light, little article[s]" that she decried as "lobotomized", passionless praise and denounced as "blandly, respectfully denying whatever vivacious interest there might be in books or in literary matters generally."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The group was inspired to found a new magazine to publish thoughtful, probing, lively reviews<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> featuring what Hardwick called "the unusual, the difficult, the lengthy, the intransigent, and above all, the <i>interesting</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decline_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Decline-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooke2014_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke2014-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the New York printers' strike of 1963, when <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and six other newspapers suspended publication, Hardwick, Lowell and the Epsteins seized the chance to establish the sort of vigorous book review that Hardwick had imagined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EpsteinBlog_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-EpsteinBlog-12">[12]</a></sup> Jason Epstein knew that book publishers would advertise their books in the new publication, since they had no other outlet for promoting new books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The group turned to the Epsteins' friend Silvers, who had been an editor at <i><a href="/wiki/The_Paris_Review" title="The Paris Review">The Paris Review</a></i> and was still at <i>Harper's</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-14">[14]</a></sup> to edit the publication, and Silvers asked Barbara Epstein to co-edit with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EpsteinBlog_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-EpsteinBlog-12">[12]</a></sup> She was known as the editor at <a href="/wiki/Doubleday_(publisher)" title="Doubleday (publisher)">Doubleday</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anne_Frank" title="Anne Frank">Anne Frank</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diary_of_a_Young_Girl" title="Diary of a Young Girl">Diary of a Young Girl</a></i>, among other books, and then worked at Dutton, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McGraw-Hill" title="McGraw-Hill">McGraw-Hill</a> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Partisan_Review" title="The Partisan Review">The Partisan Review</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarbaraTimesObit_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BarbaraTimesObit-15">[15]</a></sup> Silvers and Epstein sent books to "the writers we knew and admired most. ... We asked for three thousand words in three weeks in order to show what a book review should be, and practically everyone came through. No one mentioned money."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup> The first issue of the <i>Review</i> was published on February 1, 1963 and sold out its printing of 100,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postobit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Postobit-3">[3]</a></sup> It prompted nearly 1,000 letters to the editors asking for the <i>Review</i> to continue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> called it "surely the best first issue of any magazine ever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> After the success of the first issue, the editors assembled a second issue to demonstrate that "the <i>Review</i> was not a one-shot affair".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup> The founders then collected investments from a circle of friends and acquaintances, and Ellsworth joined as publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <i>Review</i> began regular biweekly publication in November 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBM_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBM-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:35%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #fdffe7;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">
<p><i>The New York Review</i> does not pretend to cover all the books of the season or even all the important ones. Neither time nor space, however, have been spent on books which are trivial in their intentions or venal in their effects, except occasionally to reduce a temporarily inflated reputation or to call attention to a fraud. ... The hope of the editors is to suggest, however imperfectly, some of the qualities which a responsible literary journal should have and to discover whether there is, in America, not only the need for such a review but the demand for one.</p>
</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;"><b>From the only editorial ever in the <i>Review</i></b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Silvers said of the editors' philosophy, that "there was no subject we couldn't deal with. And if there was no book [on a subject], we would deal with it anyway. We tried hard to avoid books that were simply competent rehearsals of familiar subjects, and we hoped to find books that would establish something fresh, something original."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup> In particular, "We felt you had to have a political analysis of the nature of power in America – who had it, who was affected". The editors also shared an "intense admiration for wonderful writers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFC_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFC-20">[20]</a></sup> Well-known writers were willing to contribute articles for the initial issues of the <i>Review</i> without pay because it offered them a chance to write a new kind of book review. As <a href="/wiki/Mark_Gevisser" title="Mark Gevisser">Mark Gevisser</a> explained: "The essays ... made the book review form not just a report on the book and a judgment of the book, but an essay in itself. And that, I think, startled everyone – that a book review could be exciting in that way, could be provocative in that way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gevisser_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gevisser-7">[7]</a></sup> Early issues included articles by such writers as Hardwick, Lowell, Jason Epstein, <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Arendt" title="Hannah Arendt">Hannah Arendt</a>, <a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saul_Bellow" title="Saul Bellow">Saul Bellow</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Berryman" title="John Berryman">John Berryman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Goodman_(writer)" title="Paul Goodman (writer)">Paul Goodman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lillian_Hellman" title="Lillian Hellman">Lillian Hellman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irving_Howe" title="Irving Howe">Irving Howe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kazin" title="Alfred Kazin">Alfred Kazin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Macdonald" title="Dwight Macdonald">Dwight Macdonald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_McCarthy_(author)" title="Mary McCarthy (author)">Mary McCarthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Podhoretz" title="Norman Podhoretz">Norman Podhoretz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Rahv" title="Philip Rahv">Philip Rahv</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adrienne_Rich" title="Adrienne Rich">Adrienne Rich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Styron" title="William Styron">William Styron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gore_Vidal" title="Gore Vidal">Gore Vidal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Penn_Warren" title="Robert Penn Warren">Robert Penn Warren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Wilson" title="Edmund Wilson">Edmund Wilson</a>. The <i>Review</i> pointedly published interviews with European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_dissidents" title="Political dissidents">political dissidents</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Solzhenitsyn" title="Alexander Solzhenitsyn">Alexander Solzhenitsyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov" title="Andrei Sakharov">Andrei Sakharov</a> and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Havel" title="Václav Havel">Václav Havel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFC_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFC-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-22">[22]</a></sup> But, Silvers notes, it is a mystery whether "reviews have a calculable political and social impact" or will even gain attention: "You mustn't think too much about influence – if you find something interesting yourself, that should be enough."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Salon_(website)" title="Salon (website)">Salon</a></i> later commented that the list of contributors "represented a '<a href="/wiki/Shock_and_awe" title="Shock and awe">shock and awe</a>' demonstration of the intellectual firepower available for deployment in mid-century America, and, almost equally impressive, of the art of editorial networking and jawboning. This was the party everyone who was anyone wanted to attend, the <a href="/wiki/Black_and_White_Ball" title="Black and White Ball">Black and White Ball</a> of the critical elite."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-23">[23]</a></sup> The <i>Review</i> "announced the arrival of a particular sensibility ... the engaged, literary, post-war progressive intellectual, who was concerned with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> as well as fiction and poetry and theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-22">[22]</a></sup> The first issue projected "a confidence in the unquestioned rightness of the liberal consensus, in the centrality of literature and its power to convey meaning, in the solubility of our problems through the application of intelligence and good will, and in the coherence and clear hierarchy of the intellectual world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_1979">Since 1979</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_York_Review_of_Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Since 1979">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the year-long lock-out at <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> in London in 1979, the <i>Review</i> founded a daughter publication, the <i><a href="/wiki/London_Review_of_Books" title="London Review of Books">London Review of Books</a></i>. For the first six months, this journal appeared as an insert in the <i>New York Review of Books</i>, but it became an independent publication in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1990 the <i>Review</i> founded an Italian edition, <i>la Rivista dei Libri.</i> It was published for two decades until May 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>For over 40 years, Silvers and Epstein edited the <i>Review</i> together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postobit_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Postobit-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1984, Silvers, Epstein and their partners sold the <i>Review</i> to publisher <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rea_S._Hederman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rea S. Hederman (page does not exist)">Rea S. Hederman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> who still owns the paper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GJ_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-GJ-27">[27]</a></sup> but the two continued as its editors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2006, Epstein died of cancer at the age of 77.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In awarding to Epstein and Silvers its 2006 Literarian Award for Outstanding Service to the American Literary Community, the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Foundation" title="National Book Foundation">National Book Foundation</a> stated: "With <i>The New York Review of Books</i>, Robert Silvers and Barbara Epstein raised book reviewing to an art and made the discussion of books a lively, provocative and intellectual activity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-National-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since Epstein's death, Silvers has been the sole editor. Asked in December 2007 about who might succeed him as editor, Silvers demurred, "It's not a question that's posing itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observer_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-30">[30]</a></sup> When <i>The New York Times</i> renewed the question in 2012, Silvers said, "I can think of several people who would be marvelous editors. Some of them work here, some used to work here, and some are just people we know. I think they would put out a terrific paper, but it would be different."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrath_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGrath-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2008, the <i>Review</i> celebrated its 45th anniversary with a panel discussion at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a>, moderated by Silvers, discussing "What Happens Now" in the United States after the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">2008 election</a> of <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> as president. Panelists included <i>Review</i> contributors such as Didion, Wills, novelist and literary critic <a href="/wiki/Darryl_Pinckney" title="Darryl Pinckney">Darryl Pinckney</a>, political commentator <a href="/wiki/Michael_Tomasky" title="Michael Tomasky">Michael Tomasky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> professor and contributor <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Delbanco" title="Andrew Delbanco">Andrew Delbanco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The 45th anniversary edition of the <i>Review</i> (November 20, 2008) began with a posthumous piece by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Wilson" title="Edmund Wilson">Edmund Wilson</a>, who wrote for the paper's first issue in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFC_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-SFC-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2008, the paper moved its headquarters from <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown Manhattan</a> to 435 Hudson Street, located in the <a href="/wiki/West_Village" title="West Village">West Village</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observer_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2010, it launched a blog section of its website<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> that <i>The New York Times</i> called "lively and opinionated",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrath_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-McGrath-31">[31]</a></sup> and it hosts podcasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohan_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mohan-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Asked in 2013 how <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> might affect the subject matter of the <i>Review</i>, Silvers commented: "I might imagine [a] witty, aphoristic, almost <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wildean</a> [anthology of] remarks, drawn from the millions and millions of tweets. Or from comments that follow on blogs. ... <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> is a medium in which privacy is, or at least is thought to be, in some way crucial. ... And so there seems a resistance to intrusive criticism. We seem at the edge of a vast, expanding ocean of words ... growing without any critical perspective whatever being brought to bear on it. To me, as an editor, that seems an enormous absence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DannerNYMag2013_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-DannerNYMag2013-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2016, Hederman said that he sees the <i>Review</i> continuing to operate unchanged in five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GJ_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-GJ-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Review</i> began a year-long celebration of its 50th anniversary with a presentation by Silvers and several contributors at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Town_Hall" title="The Town Hall">The Town Hall</a> in New York City in February 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Other events included a program at the New York Public Library in April, called "Literary Journalism: A Discussion", focusing on the editorial process at the <i>Review</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and a reception in November at the <a href="/wiki/Frick_Collection" title="Frick Collection">Frick Collection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> During the year, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> filmed a documentary about the history and influence of the <i>Review</i>, and the debates that it has spawned, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_50_Year_Argument" title="The 50 Year Argument">The 50 Year Argument</a></i>, which premiered in June 2014 at the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Doc/Fest" title="Sheffield Doc/Fest">Sheffield Doc/Fest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> It was later seen at various film festivals, on <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television and on <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic50_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic50-8">[8]</a></sup> Asked how he maintains his "level of meticulousness and determination" after 50 years, Silvers said that the <i>Review</i> "was and is a unique opportunity ... to do what one wants on anything in the world. Now, that is given to hardly any editor, anywhere, anytime. There are no strictures, no limits. Nobody saying you can't do something. No subject, no theme, no idea that can’t be addressed in-depth. ... Whatever work is involved is minor compared to the opportunity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DannerNYMag2013_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-DannerNYMag2013-36">[36]</a></sup> A special 50th anniversary issue was dated November 7, 2013. Silvers said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"An independent, critical voice on politics, literature, science, and the arts seems as much needed today as it was when Barbara Epstein and I put out the first edition of the <i>New York Review</i> fifty years ago – perhaps even more so. Electronic forms of communication grow rapidly in every field of life but many of their effects on culture remain obscure and in need of new kinds of critical scrutiny. That will be a central concern of the <i>Review</i> for the years to come."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBM_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBM-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_York_Review_of_Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Review</i> has been described as a "kind of magazine ... in which the most interesting and qualified minds of our time would discuss current books and issues in depth ... a literary and critical journal based on the assumption that the discussion of important books was itself an indispensable literary activity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Each issue includes a broad range of subject matter, including "articles on art, science, politics and literature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrath_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-McGrath-31">[31]</a></sup> Early on, the editors decided that the <i>Review</i> would "be interested in everything ... no subject would be excluded. Someone is writing a piece about Nascar racing for us; another is working on Veronese."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooke2014_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke2014-11">[11]</a></sup> The <i>Review</i> has focused, however, on political topics; as Silvers commented in 2004: "The pieces we have published by such writers as <a href="/wiki/Brian_Urquhart" title="Brian Urquhart">Brian Urquhart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Powers" title="Thomas Powers">Thomas Powers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Danner" title="Mark Danner">Mark Danner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Dworkin" title="Ronald Dworkin">Ronald Dworkin</a> have been reactions to a genuine crisis concerning American destructiveness, American relations with its allies, American protections of its traditions of liberties. ... The aura of patriotic defiance cultivated by the [Bush] Administration, in a fearful atmosphere, had the effect of muffling dissent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Editor Bob Silvers told <i>The New York Times</i>: "The great political issues of power and its abuses have always been natural questions for us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrath_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-McGrath-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i> gave its view of the political focus of the <i>New York Review of Books</i> in 2004:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[T]he Review took a vocal role in contesting the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. ... Around 1970, a sturdy liberalism began to supplant left-wing radicalism at the paper. As <a href="/wiki/Philip_Nobile" title="Philip Nobile">Philip Nobile</a> observed in ... 1974 ... the <i>Review</i> returned to its roots and became "a literary magazine on the British nineteenth-century model, which would mix politics and literature in a tough but gentlemanly fashion." ... The publication has always been erudite and authoritative – and because of its analytical rigor and seriousness, frequently essential – but it hasn't always been lively, pungent and readable. ... But the election of <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, combined with the furies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9/11" title="9/11">9/11</a>, jolted the editors. Since 2001, the Review's temperature has risen and its political outlook has sharpened. ... Prominent [writers for] the Review ... charged into battle not only against the White House but against the lethargic press corps and the "liberal hawk" intellectuals. ... In stark contrast to <i>The New Yorker</i> ... or <i>The New York Times Magazine</i> ..., the <i>Review</i> opposed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraq_war" title="Iraq war">Iraq war</a> in a voice that was remarkably consistent and unified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Over the years, the <i>Review</i> has featured reviews and articles by such international writers and intellectuals, in addition to those already noted, as <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Garton_Ash" title="Timothy Garton Ash">Timothy Garton Ash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Atwood" title="Margaret Atwood">Margaret Atwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russell_Baker" title="Russell Baker">Russell Baker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saul_Bellow" title="Saul Bellow">Saul Bellow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_Berlin" title="Isaiah Berlin">Isaiah Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bloom" title="Harold Bloom">Harold Bloom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Brodsky" title="Joseph Brodsky">Joseph Brodsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._M._Coetzee" title="J. M. Coetzee">J. M. Coetzee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Crews" title="Frederick Crews">Frederick Crews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Dworkin" title="Ronald Dworkin">Ronald Dworkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Kenneth_Galbraith" title="John Kenneth Galbraith">John Kenneth Galbraith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nadine_Gordimer" title="Nadine Gordimer">Nadine Gordimer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hitchens" title="Christopher Hitchens">Christopher Hitchens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Judah" title="Tim Judah">Tim Judah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murray_Kempton" title="Murray Kempton">Murray Kempton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Krugman" title="Paul Krugman">Paul Krugman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lewontin" title="Richard Lewontin">Richard Lewontin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perry_Link" title="Perry Link">Perry Link</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alison_Lurie" title="Alison Lurie">Alison Lurie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Medawar" title="Peter Medawar">Peter Medawar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Mendelsohn" title="Daniel Mendelsohn">Daniel Mendelsohn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Moyers" title="Bill Moyers">Bill Moyers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>, <a href="/wiki/V._S._Naipaul" title="V. S. Naipaul">V. S. Naipaul</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_G._Peterson" title="Peter G. Peterson">Peter G. Peterson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samantha_Power" title="Samantha Power">Samantha Power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Rich_(novelist)" title="Nathaniel Rich (novelist)">Nathaniel Rich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Rohatyn" title="Felix Rohatyn">Felix Rohatyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Searle" title="John Searle">John Searle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zadie_Smith" title="Zadie Smith">Zadie Smith</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timothy_Snyder" title="Timothy Snyder">Timothy Snyder</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Soros" title="George Soros">George Soros</a>, <a href="/wiki/I._F._Stone" title="I. F. Stone">I. F. Stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Tutu" title="Desmond Tutu">Desmond Tutu</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Walcott" title="Derek Walcott">Derek Walcott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Weinberg" title="Steven Weinberg">Steven Weinberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garry_Wills" title="Garry Wills">Garry Wills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Judt" title="Tony Judt">Tony Judt</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Foundation" title="National Book Foundation">National Book Foundation</a>: "From Mary McCarthy and Edmund Wilson to Gore Vidal and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Didion" title="Joan Didion">Joan Didion</a>, <i>The New York Review of Books</i> has consistently employed the liveliest minds in America to think about, write about, and debate books and the issues they raise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-National-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Review</i> also devotes space in most issues to poetry, and has featured the work of such poets as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lowell" title="Robert Lowell">Robert Lowell</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Berryman" title="John Berryman">John Berryman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Hughes" title="Ted Hughes">Ted Hughes</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Ashbery" title="John Ashbery">John Ashbery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wilbur" title="Richard Wilbur">Richard Wilbur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seamus_Heaney" title="Seamus Heaney">Seamus Heaney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Paz" title="Octavio Paz">Octavio Paz</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czeslaw_Milosz" title="Czeslaw Milosz">Czeslaw Milosz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> For writers, the "depth [of the articles], and the quality of the people writing for it, has made a <i>Review</i> byline a résumé definer. If one wishes to be thought of as a certain type of writer – of heft, style and a certain gravitas – a <i>Review</i> byline is pretty much the gold standard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-50">[50]</a></sup> In editing a piece, Silvers has said that he asks himself "if [the point in any sentence could] be clearer, while also respecting the writer’s voice and tone. You have to listen carefully to the tone of the writer’s prose and try to adapt to it, but only up to a point. [No change is ever made without the writers' permission.] ... Writers deserve the final word about their prose."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DannerNYMag2013_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-DannerNYMag2013-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to domestic matters, the <i>Review</i> covers issues of international concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In the 1980s, a British commentator noted: "In the 1960s [the <i>Review</i>] opposed American involvement in Vietnam; more recently it has taken a line mildly Keynesian in economics, pro-Israeli but Anti-Zionist, sceptical of <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Reagan</a>'s Latin-American policy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The British newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> has described the <i>Review</i> as "the only mainstream American publication to speak out consistently against the war in Iraq."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> On Middle East coverage, Silvers said, "any serious criticism of Israeli policy will be seen by some as heresy, a form of betrayal. ... [M]uch of what we've published has come from some of the most respected and brilliant Israeli writers ... <a href="/wiki/Amos_Elon" title="Amos Elon">Amos Elon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avishai_Margalit" title="Avishai Margalit">Avishai Margalit</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Grossman" title="David Grossman">David Grossman</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Dean_Shulman" title="David Dean Shulman">David Shulman</a>, among them. What emerges from them is a sense that occupying land and people year after year can only lead to a sad and bad result."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DannerNYMag2013_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-DannerNYMag2013-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Caricaturist <a href="/wiki/David_Levine" title="David Levine">David Levine</a> illustrated <i>The New York Review of Books</i> from 1963 to 2007, giving the paper a distinctive visual image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observer_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-30">[30]</a></sup> Levine died in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levineobit_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levineobit-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>, whom Levine drew many times, wrote: "Besides offering us the delight of recognition, his drawings comfort us, in an exacerbated and potentially desperate age, with the sense of a watching presence, an eye informed by an intelligence that has not panicked, a comic art ready to encapsulate the latest apparitions of publicity as well as those historical devils who haunt our unease."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gallery_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-gallery-55">[55]</a></sup> Levine contributed more than 3,800 pen-and-ink caricatures of famous writers, artists and politicians for the publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gallery_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-gallery-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Silvers said: "David combined acute political commentary with a certain kind of joke about the person. He was immensely sensitive to the smallest details – people’s shoulders, their feet, their elbows. He was able to find character in these details."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> described Levine's illustrations as "macro-headed, somberly expressive, astringently probing and hardly ever flattering caricatures of intellectuals and athletes, politicians and potentates" that were "replete with exaggeratedly bad haircuts, 5 o'clock shadows, ill-conceived mustaches and other grooming foibles ... to make the famous seem peculiar-looking in order to take them down a peg".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levineobit_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levineobit-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Washington Post</i> described the "lively literary disputes" conducted in the 'letters to the editor' column of the <i>Review</i> as "the closest thing the intellectual world has to bare-knuckle boxing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postobit_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Postobit-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition to reviews, interviews and articles, the paper features extensive advertising from publishers promoting newly published books. It includes a popular "personals" section that "share[s] a cultivated writing style" with its articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohan_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mohan-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> One lonely heart, author <a href="/wiki/Jane_Juska" title="Jane Juska">Jane Juska</a>, documented the 63 replies to her personal ad in the <i>Review</i> with a 2003 memoir, <i>A Round-Heeled Woman</i>, that became a <a href="/wiki/A_Round-Heeled_Woman:_the_play" title="A Round-Heeled Woman: the play">West End play</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several of the magazine's editorial assistants have become prominent in journalism, academia and literature, including <a href="/wiki/Jean_Strouse" title="Jean Strouse">Jean Strouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Eisenberg" title="Deborah Eisenberg">Deborah Eisenberg</a>, Mark Danner and <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">A. O. Scott</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Another former intern and a contributor to the <i>Review</i>, author <a href="/wiki/Claire_Messud" title="Claire Messud">Claire Messud</a>, says: "They’re incredibly generous about taking the time to go through things. So much of [business today] is about people doing things quickly, with haste. One of the first things to go out the window is a type of graciousness. ... There’s a whole sort of rhythm and tone of how they deal with people. I’m sure it was always rare. But it feels incredibly precious now."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reaction">Critical reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_York_Review_of_Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> calls the <i>Review</i> "a journal of ideas that has helped define intellectual discourse in the English-speaking world for the past four decades. ... By publishing long, thoughtful articles on politics, books and culture, [the editors] defied trends toward glibness, superficiality and the cult of celebrity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postobit_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Postobit-3">[3]</a></sup> Similarly, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> praised the paper as "one of the few venues in American life that takes ideas seriously. And it pays readers the ultimate compliment of assuming that we do too."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In a 2006 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Magazine" title="New York Magazine"><i>New York</i> magazine</a> feature, <a href="/wiki/James_Atlas" title="James Atlas">James Atlas</a> stated: "It's an eclectic but impressive mix [of articles] that has made <i>The New York Review of Books</i> the premier journal of the American intellectual elite".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYmag_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYmag-63">[63]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i> commented in 2011 that the <i>Review</i> is written with "a freshness of perspective", and "much of it shapes our most sophisticated public discourse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In celebrating the 35th birthday of the <i>Review</i> in 1998, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> commented, "The N.Y.R. gives off rogue intimations of being fun to put out. It hasn't lost its sneaky nip of mischief".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, Britain's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> deemed the <i>Review</i> "scholarly without being pedantic, scrupulous without being dry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The same newspaper wrote in 2004:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The ... issues of the <i>Review</i> to date provide a history of the cultural life of the east coast since 1963. It manages to be ... serious with a fierce democratic edge. ... It is one of the last places in the English-speaking world that will publish long essays ... and possibly the very last to combine academic rigour – even the letters to the editor are footnoted – with great clarity of language."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In <i>New York</i> magazine, in February 2011, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Sacks" title="Oliver Sacks">Oliver Sacks</a> stated that the <i>Review</i> is "one of the great institutions of intellectual life here or anywhere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In 2012, <i>The New York Times</i> described the <i>Review</i> as "elegant, well mannered, immensely learned, a little formal at times, obsessive about clarity and factual correctness and passionately interested in human rights and the way governments violate them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrath_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-McGrath-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Known throughout its history as a left-liberal journal, what <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a> called "the chief theoretical organ of <a href="/wiki/Radical_chic" title="Radical chic">radical chic</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolfe_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfe-4">[4]</a></sup> the <i>Review</i> has, perhaps, had its most effective voice in wartime. According to a 2004 feature in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i>,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"One suspects they yearn for the day when they can return to their normal publishing routine – that gentlemanly <a href="/wiki/Pastiche" title="Pastiche">pastiche</a> of philosophy, art, classical music, photography, German and Russian history, East European politics, literary fiction – unencumbered by political duties of a confrontational or oppositional nature. That day has not yet arrived. If and when it does, let it be said that the editors met the challenges of the post-9/11 era in a way that most other leading American publications did not, and that <i>The New York Review of Books</i> ... was there when we needed it most."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Sometimes accused of insularity, the <i>Review</i> has been called "The New York Review of Each Other's Books".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philip_Nobile" title="Philip Nobile">Philip Nobile</a> expressed a mordant criticism along these lines in his book <i>Intellectual Skywriting: Literary Politics and the New York Review of Books</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYmag_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYmag-63">[63]</a></sup> <i>The Guardian</i> characterized such accusations as "sour grapes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2008, the <i>San Francisco Chronicle</i> wrote, "the pages of the 45th anniversary issue, in fact, reveal the actuality of [the paper's] willfully panoramic view".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFC_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-SFC-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Washington Post</i> called the 2013 50th Anniversary issue "gaudy with intellectual firepower. Four Nobel Laureates have bylines. U.S. Supreme Court Justice <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Breyer" title="Stephen Breyer">Stephen Breyer</a> muses on reading Proust. There's the transcript of a long-lost lecture by <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-50">[50]</a></sup> In 2014, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Cooke" title="Rachel Cooke">Rachel Cooke</a> wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> of a recent issue: "The offer of such an embarrassment of riches is wholly amazing in a world where print journalism increasingly operates in the most threadbare of circumstances".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooke2014_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke2014-11">[11]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/America_(Jesuit_magazine)" title="America (Jesuit magazine)">America</a></i> magazine echoed <a href="/wiki/Zo%C3%AB_Heller" title="Zoë Heller">Zoë Heller</a>'s words about the <i>Review</i>: "I like it because it educates me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_publications.3B_archives">Other publications; archives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_York_Review_of_Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other publications; archives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book publishing arm of the <i>Review</i>, established in 1999, is <a href="/wiki/New_York_Review_Books" title="New York Review Books">New York Review Books</a>, which has three imprints, "NYRB Classics", "NYRB Collections" and "NYR Children's Collection". The NYRB Classics imprint reissues books that have gone out of print in the United States and translations of classics. It has been called "a marvellous literary imprint ... that has put hundreds of wonderful books back on our shelves."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooke2014_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke2014-11">[11]</a></sup> NYRB Collections publishes collections of articles from frequent <i>Review</i> contributors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a> purchased the NYRB archives in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
