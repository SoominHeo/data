<p>The <b>Museum of the City of New York</b> (MCNY) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_museum" title="History museum">history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Art_museum" title="Art museum">art</a> museum in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. It was founded by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Collins_Brown" title="Henry Collins Brown">Henry Collins Brown</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray2005-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrone2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morrone2008-2">[2]</a></sup> in 1923<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup> to preserve and present the <a href="/wiki/History_of_New_York_City" title="History of New York City">history of New York City</a>, and its people. It is located at 1220-1227 <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue" title="Fifth Avenue">Fifth Avenue</a> from East <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/103rd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="103rd Street (Manhattan)">103rd</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/104th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="104th Street (Manhattan)">104th</a> Streets, across from <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, at the northern end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_Mile,_New_York_City" title="Museum Mile, New York City">Museum Mile</a> section of Fifth Avenue.</p>
<p>The red brick with marble trim<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup> museum was built in 1929-30<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup> and was designed by Joseph H. Freedlander in the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">neo-Georgian style</a>, with statues of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a> and <a href="/wiki/DeWitt_Clinton" title="DeWitt Clinton">DeWitt Clinton</a> by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Adolph_Alexander_Weinman" title="Adolph Alexander Weinman">Adolph Alexander Weinman</a> facing Central Park from niches in the facade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aia_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-aia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum is a private non-profit organization which receives government support as a member of New York City's <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Institutions_Group" title="Cultural Institutions Group">Cultural Institutions Group</a>, commonly known as "CIG"s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its other sources of income are endowments, admission fees, and contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fednyc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fednyc-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum was originally located in <a href="/wiki/Gracie_Mansion" title="Gracie Mansion">Gracie Mansion</a>, where available space was limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup> One of its first major exhibits was "Old New York", presented in the Fine Arts Building on <a href="/wiki/57th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="57th Street (Manhattan)">West 57th Street</a> in 1926. The success of the project led to a search for a new, permanent headquarters for the museum. A design competition was held between five invited architects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aia_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-aia-5">[5]</a></sup> and the Colonial Revival design by Freedlander was selected. The city donated a site on Fifth Avenue, and funds for construction of the museum building were raised by public subscription.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nycland_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nycland-8">[8]</a></sup> The original plans for the museum's building were scaled back as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">Wall Street Crash of 1929</a>, nevertheless, the building was dedicated on January 11, 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 24, 1967 the museum building was designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_landmark" title="New York City landmark">New York City landmark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1982, the Museum received <a href="/wiki/The_Hundred_Year_Association_of_New_York" title="The Hundred Year Association of New York">The Hundred Year Association of New York</a>'s Gold Medal Award "in recognition of outstanding contributions to the City of New York."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_move">Proposed move</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proposed move">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_Giuliani" title="Rudolph Giuliani">Giuliani administration</a> told the museum that it could relocate to the historic <a href="/wiki/Tweed_Courthouse" title="Tweed Courthouse">Tweed Courthouse</a> near <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Hall" title="New York City Hall">City Hall</a> in Lower Manhattan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/El_Museo_del_Barrio" title="El Museo del Barrio">El Museo del Barrio</a> would then have moved across the street to occupy the current Museum of the City of New York building. This decision was overturned by the incoming administration of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a>, which decided to use the Courthouse as the headquarters for the new <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Education" title="New York City Department of Education">New York City Department of Education</a>, causing MCNY's then-director Robert R. McDonald to tender his resignation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-9">[9]</a></sup> McDonald was replaced in 2002 by Susan Henshaw Jones, who was at the time the president of the <a href="/wiki/National_Building_Museum" title="National Building Museum">National Building Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was also an attempt to merge the museum with the <a href="/wiki/New-York_Historical_Society" title="New-York Historical Society">New-York Historical Society</a>, which did not come to fruition, and the museum was passed over for space at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_Zero" title="Ground Zero">Ground Zero</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2006-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Museum's new director, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Henshaw_Jones" title="Susan Henshaw Jones">Susan Henshaw Jones</a>, recommitted MCNY to its <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> neighborhood by planning an extension to the Museum. The groundbreaking for this extension, which included renovation of existing gallery space, as well as a new pavilion, took place on August 2, 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2006_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2006-10">[10]</a></sup> and it was completed in February 2008 with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribbon_cutting" title="Ribbon cutting">ribbon cutting</a> later that same year.</p>
<p>The pavilion gallery, designed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polshek_Partnership" title="Polshek Partnership">Polshek Partnership</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aia_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-aia-5">[5]</a></sup> is 3,000-square-foot (280 m<sup>2</sup>) glass addition, which has two levels for which to display <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifacts</a>. The original 1932 Georgian Revival building was also restored during this project, as well as additions including a vault for the museum's silver collection, a research room and a room for the handling of artifacts. The total costs for the first phase of refurbishments came to $28 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2011, the Museum temporarily took over operation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Street_Seaport_Museum" title="South Street Seaport Museum">South Street Seaport Museum</a> which reopened in January 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum's collection of over 1.5 million items<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-9">[9]</a></sup> – which is particularly strong in objects dating from the 19th and early 20th centuries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup> – include paintings, drawings, prints, including over 3000 by <a href="/wiki/Currier_and_Ives" title="Currier and Ives">Currier and Ives</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup> and photographs featuring New York City and its residents, as well as costumes, decorative objects and furniture, antique toys– the museum was the first in the United States to establish a curatorial department for toys<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup> – ship models, rare books and manuscripts, marine and military collections, police and fire collections, and a theater collection which documents the golden age of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadway_theater" title="Broadway theater">Broadway theater</a>. There are also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioramas" title="Dioramas">dioramas</a> about the city's history as well as its physical environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aia_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-aia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the rare items in the museum's collection is a chair that once belonged to <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Rapelje" title="Sarah Rapelje">Sarah Rapelje</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Joris_Jansen_Rapelje" title="Joris Jansen Rapelje">Joris Jansen Rapelje</a> of Nieuw Amsterdam, and said to be the first child born in New York State of European parentage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The chair was donated by her Brinckerhoff descendants.</p>
<p>The museum is known for its comprehensive collection of photographic images, which includes works by noted photographers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percy_Byron" title="Percy Byron">Percy Byron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Riis" title="Jacob Riis">Jacob Riis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berenice_Abbott" title="Berenice Abbott">Berenice Abbott</a>, as well as many <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Depression</a>-era <a href="/wiki/Federal_Art_Project" title="Federal Art Project">Federal Art Project</a> photographs. The collection also includes still photography by film director <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>MCNY is also home to several recreations of furnished rooms from the house of <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a>, donated by the Rockefeller family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encnyc_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-encnyc-3">[3]</a></sup> Notable as well is a model of <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Castello_Plan" title="Castello Plan">Castello Plan</a> of 1660.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aia_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-aia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_exhibits">Notable exhibits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notable exhibits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1864" data-file-width="1714" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg/175px-Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg/263px-Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg/350px-Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York_1220_Fifth_Avenue_signs.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>

<p>From October 2004 through July 2009, <i>Perform</i> was the only permanent exhibition in New York City focused on theater in New York. It included objects ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_%22Bojangles%22_Robinson" title='Bill "Bojangles" Robinson'>Bill "Bojangles" Robinson</a>'s tap shoes to advertising materials from <i><a href="/wiki/Avenue_Q" title="Avenue Q">Avenue Q</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2007, the museum opened its temporary "The Glory Days, 1947–1957" exhibit, an in-depth photographic look at the history of professional <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> in New York City. The exhibit included clips of <a href="/wiki/Ed_Sullivan" title="Ed Sullivan">Ed Sullivan</a>, a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> television entertainment, interviewing many of the players of the era, including <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phil_Rizzuto" title="Phil Rizzuto">Phil Rizzuto</a>. A section was devoted to <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson" title="Jackie Robinson">Jackie Robinson</a>, who broke the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_color_line" title="Baseball color line">color line</a> in 1947 when he joined the former <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="History of the Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> and became a leading figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> movement both <a href="/wiki/African-American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1896%E2%80%931954)" title="African-American Civil Rights Movement (1896–1954)">during</a>.and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1955%E2%80%931968)" title="African-American Civil Rights Movement (1955–1968)">after</a> his playing career.</p>
<p>In May 2012, the Museum opened a new space devoted to the <a href="/wiki/Puffin_Foundation" title="Puffin Foundation">Puffin Foundation</a> and its collection. The inaugural exhibit focused on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_activism" title="Social activism">social activism</a> and New York City's activist roots.</p>
