<p><b>The City University of New York</b> (<b>CUNY</b>; pron.: <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/juː/ long 'u' in 'cute'">juː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/University_system" title="University system">university system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Education_in_New_York_City" title="Education in New York City">New York City</a>, and the largest urban university in the United States. CUNY and the <a href="/wiki/State_University_of_New_York" title="State University of New York">State University of New York</a> (SUNY) are separate and independent university systems, although both are public institutions that receive funding from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">New York State</a>. CUNY, however, is additionally funded by the City of New York.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enrollment_and_demographics">Enrollment and demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Enrollment and demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CUNY is the third-largest university system in the United States, in terms of enrollment, behind the <a href="/wiki/State_University_of_New_York" title="State University of New York">State University of New York</a> (SUNY), and the <a href="/wiki/California_State_University" title="California State University">California State University</a> system. More than 270,000-degree-credit students and 273,000 continuing and professional education students are enrolled at campuses located in all five New York City boroughs.</p>
<p>The university has one of the most diverse student bodies in the United States, with students hailing from 208 countries. The black, white and Hispanic undergraduate populations each comprise more than a quarter of the student body, and Asian undergraduates make up 18 percent. Fifty-eight percent are female, and 28 percent are 25 or older.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_City_University_of_New_York_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_City_University_of_New_York-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CUNY was created in 1961, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">New York State</a> legislation, signed into law by Governor <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Rockefeller" title="Nelson Rockefeller">Nelson Rockefeller</a>. The legislation integrated existing institutions and a new graduate school into a coordinated system of higher education for the city, under the control of the "Board of Higher Education of the City of New York", which had been created by New York State legislation in 1926. By 1979, the Board of Higher Education had become the "Board of Trustees of the CUNY".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzp_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fitzp-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The institutions that were merged in order to create CUNY were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzp_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-fitzp-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The Free Academy – Founded in 1847 by <a href="/wiki/Townsend_Harris" title="Townsend Harris">Townsend Harris</a>, it was fashioned as "a Free Academy for the purpose of extending the benefits of education gratuitously to persons who have been pupils in the common schools of the city and county of New York." The Free Academy later became the <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_New_York" title="City College of New York">City College of New York</a>.</li>
<li>The Female Normal and High School – Founded in 1870, and later renamed <a href="/wiki/Normal_school" title="Normal school">the Normal College</a>. It would be renamed again in 1914 to <a href="/wiki/Hunter_College" title="Hunter College">Hunter College</a>. During the early 20th century, Hunter College expanded into the Bronx, with what became <a href="/wiki/Lehman_College" title="Lehman College">Herbert Lehman College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzp_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-fitzp-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_College" title="Brooklyn College">Brooklyn College</a> – Founded in 1930.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queens_College" title="Queens College">Queens College</a> – Founded in 1937.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessible_education">Accessible education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accessible education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CUNY has served a diverse student body, especially those excluded from or unable to afford private universities. Its four-year colleges offered a high quality, tuition-free education to the poor, the working class and the immigrants of New York City who met the grade requirements for matriculated status. During the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> era, when some <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> universities, such as <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, discriminated against <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, many Jewish academics and intellectuals studied and taught at CUNY.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The City College of New York developed a reputation of being "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> of the proletariat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the city's population—and public college enrollment—grew during the early 20th century and the city struggled for resources, the municipal colleges slowly began adopting selective tuition, also known as instructional fees, for a handful of courses and programs. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, with funding for the public colleges severely constrained, limits were imposed on the size of the colleges' free Day Session, and tuition was imposed upon students deemed "competent" but not academically qualified for the day program. Most of these "limited matriculation" students enrolled in the Evening Session, and paid tuition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demand in the United States for higher education rapidly grew after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and during the mid-1940s a movement began to create <a href="/wiki/Community_college" title="Community college">community colleges</a> to provide accessible education and training. In New York City, however, the community-college movement was constrained by many factors including "financial problems, narrow perceptions of responsibility, organizational weaknesses, adverse political factors, and another competing priorities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_1975_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_1975-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Community colleges would have drawn from the same city coffers that were funding the senior colleges, and city higher education officials were of the view that the state should finance them. It wasn't until 1955, under a shared-funding arrangement with New York State, that New York City established its first community college, on Staten Island. Unlike the day college students attending the city's public baccalaureate colleges for free, the community college students had to pay tuition fees under the state-city funding formula. Community college students paid tuition fees for approximately 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_1975_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_1975-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over time, tuition fees for limited-matriculated students became an important source of system revenues. In fall 1957, for example, nearly 36,000 attended Hunter, Brooklyn, Queens and City Colleges for free, but another 24,000 paid tuition fees of up to $300 a year – the equivalent of $2,413 in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Undergraduate tuition and other student fees in 1957 comprised 17 percent of the colleges' $46.8 million in revenues, about $7.74 million — a figure equivalent to $62.4 million in 2011 buying power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three community colleges had been established by early 1961, when the city's public colleges were codified by the state as a single university with a chancellor at the helm and an infusion of state funds. But the city's slowness in creating the community colleges as demand for college seats was intensifying, had resulted in mounting frustration, particularly on the part of minorities, that college opportunities were not available to them.</p>
<p>In 1964, as the city's Board of Higher Education moved to take full responsibility for the community colleges, city officials extended the senior colleges' free tuition policy to them, a change that was included by Mayor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wagner" title="Robert Wagner">Robert Wagner</a> in his budget plans and took effect with the 1964–65 academic year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1969, a group of <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">Black</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Rican</a> students occupied City College demanding the <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">racial integration</a> of CUNY, which at the time had an overwhelmingly <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">white</a> student body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_1975_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_1975-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_protests">Student protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Student protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Students at some campuses became increasingly frustrated with the university's and Board of Higher Education's handling of university administration. At <a href="/wiki/Baruch_College" title="Baruch College">Baruch College</a> in 1967, over a thousand students protested the plan to make the college an upper-division school limited to junior, senior, and graduate students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_College" title="Brooklyn College">Brooklyn College</a> in 1968, students attempted a sit-in to demand the admission of more <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Rican</a> students and additional black studies curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Students at <a href="/wiki/Hunter_College" title="Hunter College">Hunter College</a> also demanded a <a href="/wiki/Africana_studies" title="Africana studies">Black studies</a> program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Members of the SEEK program, which provided academic support for underprepared and underprivileged students, staged a building takeover at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queens_College" title="Queens College">Queens College</a> in 1969 to protest the decisions of the program's director, who would later be replaced by a black professor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans_in_the_United_States" title="Puerto Ricans in the United States">Puerto Rican</a> students at <a href="/wiki/Bronx_Community_College" title="Bronx Community College">Bronx Community College</a> filed a report with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Division_of_Human_Rights" title="New York State Division of Human Rights">New York State Division of Human Rights</a> in 1970, contending that the intellectual level of the college was inferior and discriminatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hunter_College" title="Hunter College">Hunter College</a> was crippled for several days by a protest of 2,000 students who had a list of demands focusing on more student representation in college administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Across CUNY, students boycotted their campuses in 1970 to protest a rise in student fees and other issues, including the proposed (and later implemented) open admissions plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like many college campuses in 1970, CUNY faced a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Student_Strike_of_1970" title="Student Strike of 1970">protests and demonstrations</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Kent_State_shootings" title="Kent State shootings">Kent State shootings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodian_Campaign" title="Cambodian Campaign">Cambodian Campaign</a>. The Administrative Council of the City University of New York sent U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> a telegram in 1970 stating, "No nation can long endure the alienation of the best of its young people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Some colleges, including <a href="/wiki/John_Jay_College_of_Criminal_Justice" title="John Jay College of Criminal Justice">John Jay College of Criminal Justice</a>, historically the "college for cops," held teach-ins in addition to student and faculty protests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_admissions">Open admissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Open admissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1969, the Board of Trustees implemented a new admissions policy. The doors to CUNY were opened wide to all those demanding entrance, assuring all high school graduates entrance to the university without having to fulfill traditional requirements such as exams or grades. This policy was known as <a href="/wiki/Open_admissions" title="Open admissions">open admissions</a> and nearly doubled the number of students enrolling in the CUNY system to 35,000 (compared to 20,000 the year before). With greater numbers came more diversity: Black and Hispanic student enrollment increased threefold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Remedial_education" title="Remedial education">Remedial education</a>, to supplement the training of under-prepared students, became a significant part of CUNY's offerings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_crisis_of_1976">Financial crisis of 1976</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Financial crisis of 1976">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In fall 1976, during New York City's fiscal crisis, the free tuition policy was discontinued under pressure from the federal government, the financial community that had a role in rescuing the city from bankruptcy, and New York State, which would take over the funding of CUNY's senior colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Tuition, which had been in place in the State University of New York system since 1963, was instituted at all CUNY colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, CUNY students were added to the state's need-based Tuition Assistance Program (TAP), which had been created to help private colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www1.cuny.edu_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-www1.cuny.edu-29">[29]</a></sup> Full-time students who met the income eligibility criteria were permitted to receive TAP, ensuring for the first time that financial hardship would deprive no CUNY student of a college education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www1.cuny.edu_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-www1.cuny.edu-29">[29]</a></sup> Within a few years, the federal government would create its own need-based program, known as <a href="/wiki/Pell_Grant" title="Pell Grant">Pell Grants</a>, providing the neediest students with a tuition-free college education. By 2011, nearly six of ten full- time undergraduates qualified for a tuition-free education at CUNY due in large measure to state, federal and CUNY financial aid programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> CUNY's enrollment dipped after tuition was re-established, and there were further enrollment declines through the 1980s and into the 1990s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_crisis_of_1995">Financial crisis of 1995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Financial crisis of 1995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, CUNY suffered another fiscal crisis when Governor <a href="/wiki/George_Pataki" title="George Pataki">George Pataki</a> proposed a drastic cut in state financing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Faculty cancelled classes and students staged protests. By May, CUNY adopted deep cuts to college budgets and class offerings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> By June, in order to save money spent on remedial programs, CUNY adopted a stricter admissions policy for its senior colleges: students deemed unprepared for college would not be admitted, this a departure from the 1970 <a href="#Open_admissions">Open Admissions</a> program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> That year's final state budget cut funding by $102 million, which CUNY absorbed by increasing tuition by $750 and offering a retirement incentive plan for faculty.</p>
<p>In 1999, a task force appointed by Mayor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_Giuliani" title="Rudolph Giuliani">Rudolph Giuliani</a> issued a report that described CUNY as "an institution adrift" and called for an improved, more cohesive university structure and management, as well as more consistent academic standards. Following the report, Matthew Goldstein, a mathematician and City College graduate who had led CUNY's Baruch College and briefly, <a href="/wiki/Adelphi_University" title="Adelphi University">Adelphi University</a>, was appointed chancellor. CUNY ended its policy of open admissions to its four-year colleges, raised its admissions standards its most selective four-year colleges (Baruch, Brooklyn, City, Hunter and Queens), and required new-enrollees who needed remediation, to begin their studies at a CUNY open-admissions community colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaminer_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaminer-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_onwards">2010 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2010 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CUNY's enrollment of degree-credit students reached 220,727 in 2005 and 262,321 in 2010 as the university broadened its academic offerings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The university added more than 2,000 full-time faculty positions, opened new schools and programs, and expanded the university's fundraising efforts to help pay for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaminer_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaminer-34">[34]</a></sup> Fundraising increased from $35 million in 2000 to more than $200 million in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of Autumn 2013, all CUNY undergraduates are required to take an administration-dictated common core of courses which have been claimed to meet specific "learning outcomes" or standards. Since the courses are accepted University wide, the administration claims it will be easier for students to transfer course credits between CUNY colleges. It also reduced the number of core courses some CUNY colleges had required, to a level below national norms, particularly in the sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The program is the target of several lawsuits by students and faculty, and was the subject of a "no confidence" vote by the faculty, who rejected it by an overwhelming 92% margin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chancellor Goldstein retired on July 1, 2013, and was replaced on June 1, 2014 by James Milliken, president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska" title="University of Nebraska">University of Nebraska</a>, and a graduate of University of Nebraska and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_University_Law_School" title="New York University Law School">New York University Law School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>The forerunner of today's City University of New York was governed by the Board of Education of New York City. Members of the Board of Education, chaired by the President of the board, served as <i>ex officio</i> trustees. For the next four decades, the board members continued to serve as <i>ex officio</i> trustees of the College of the City of New York and the city's other municipal college, the Normal College of the City of New York.</p>
<p>In 1900, the New York State Legislature created separate boards of trustees for the College of the City of New York and the Normal College, which became Hunter College in 1914. In 1926, the Legislature established the Board of Higher Education of the City of New York, which assumed supervision of both municipal colleges.</p>
<p>In 1961, the New York State Legislature established the City University of New York, uniting what had become seven municipal colleges at the time: the City College of New York, Hunter College, Brooklyn College, Queens College, Staten Island Community College, Bronx Community College and Queensborough Community College. In 1979, the CUNY Financing and Governance Act was adopted by the State and the Board of Higher Education became the City University of New York Board of Trustees.</p>
<p>Today, the City University is governed by the Board of Trustees composed of 17 members, ten of whom are appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a> "with the advice and consent of the senate," and five by the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York_City" title="Mayor of New York City">Mayor of New York City</a> "with the advice and consent of the senate." The final two trustees are <i>ex officio</i> members. One is the chair of the university's student senate, and the other is non-voting and is the chair of the university's faculty senate. Both the mayoral and gubernatorial appointments to the CUNY Board are required to include at least one resident of each of New York City's five boroughs. Trustees serve seven-year terms, which are renewable for another seven years. The Chancellor is elected by the Board of Trustees, and is the "chief educational and administrative officer" of the City University.</p>
<p>The administrative offices are in mid-town <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Safety_Department">Public Safety Department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Public Safety Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/City_University_of_New_York_Public_Safety_Department" title="City University of New York Public Safety Department">City University of New York Public Safety Department</a></div>
<p>CUNY has its own public safety force whose duties are to protect and serve all students and faculty members, and enforce all state and city laws at all of CUNY's universities. The force has more than 1000 officers, making it one of the largest public safety forces in New York City.</p>
<p>The Public Safety Department came under heavy criticism, from student groups, after several students protesting tuition increases tried to occupy the lobby of the Baruch College. The occupiers were forcibly removed from the area and several were arrested on November 21, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="City_University_Television_.28CUNY_TV.29">City University Television (CUNY TV)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: City University Television (CUNY TV)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CUNY also has a broadcast TV service, <a href="/wiki/CUNY_TV" title="CUNY TV">CUNY TV</a> (channel 75 on <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Cable" title="Time Warner Cable">Time Warner</a> cable, digital HD broadcast channel 25.3), which airs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_learning" title="Distance learning">telecourses</a>, classic and foreign films, magazine shows and panel discussions in foreign languages.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="City_University_Film_Festival_.28CUFF.29">City University Film Festival (CUFF)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_University_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: City University Film Festival (CUFF)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
