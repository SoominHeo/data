<p>The <b>Federal Reserve Bank of New York</b> is one of the 12 <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Bank" title="Federal Reserve Bank">Federal Reserve Banks</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33_Liberty_Street" title="33 Liberty Street">33 Liberty Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">NY</a>. It is responsible for the Second District of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve System</a>, which encompasses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York State</a>, the 12 northern counties of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairfield_County,_Connecticut" title="Fairfield County, Connecticut">Fairfield County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>. Working within the Federal Reserve System, the New York Federal Reserve Bank implements monetary policy, supervises and regulates financial institutions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS01_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS01-1">[1]</a></sup> and helps maintain the nation's payment systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the other regional banks, New York Federal Reserve Bank and its president are considered first among equals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Its current president is <a href="/wiki/William_C._Dudley" title="William C. Dudley">William C. Dudley</a>. It is by far the largest (by assets), most active (by volume) and most influential of the 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The New York Fed publishes a <a class="external text" href="http://www.newyorkfed.org/research/capital_markets/ycfaq.html" rel="nofollow">monthly recession probability prediction</a> derived from the yield curve and based on the work by Dr. Arturo Estrella &amp; Dr. Tobias Adrian.</p>
<p>Their models show that when the difference between short-term interest rates (using three-month T-bills) and long-term interest rates (using ten-year Treasury bonds) at the end of a Federal Reserve tightening cycle is negative or less than 93 basis points positive that a rise in unemployment usually occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_regional_Federal_Reserve_Bank">Largest regional Federal Reserve Bank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Largest regional Federal Reserve Bank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Since the founding of the Federal Reserve banking system, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>'s Financial District has been the place where monetary policy in the United States is implemented, although policy is decided in Washington, D.C. by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Board_of_Governors" title="Federal Reserve Board of Governors">Federal Reserve Board of Governors</a>. The New York Fed is the largest in terms of assets of the twelve regional banks. Operating in the financial capital of the U.S., the New York Fed is responsible for conducting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_market_operations" title="Open market operations">open market operations</a>, the buying and selling of outstanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_security" title="Treasury security">U.S. Treasury securities</a>. The <b>Trading Desk</b> is the office at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York that manages the FOMC Directive to sell or buy bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Note that the responsibility for <i>issuing</i> new U.S. Treasury securities lies with the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_the_Public_Debt" title="Bureau of the Public Debt">Bureau of the Public Debt</a>. In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Fedwire" title="Fedwire">Fedwire</a>, the Federal Reserve's system for transferring balances between it and other banks, transferred $1.8 trillion a day in funds, of which about $1.1 trillion originated in the Second District. It transferred an additional $1.3 trillion a day in securities, of which $1.2 trillion originated in the Second District. The New York Fed is also responsible for carrying out <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a> policy by buying and selling dollars at the discretion of the United States Treasury Department. The New York Federal Reserve is the only regional bank with a permanent vote on the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Open_Market_Committee" title="Federal Open Market Committee">Federal Open Market Committee</a> and its president is traditionally selected as the Committee's vice chairman.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_33_Liberty_Street">Move to 33 Liberty Street</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Move to 33 Liberty Street">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33_Liberty_Street" title="33 Liberty Street">33 Liberty Street</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goldvault_nyc.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="344" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Goldvault_nyc.jpg/220px-Goldvault_nyc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Goldvault_nyc.jpg/330px-Goldvault_nyc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/98/Goldvault_nyc.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goldvault_nyc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Vast quantities of gold were shipped to the U.S. during World War I and stored in the U.S. Federal Reserve Banks. Today gold is still stored at the bank.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A public <a href="/wiki/Architectural_design_competition" title="Architectural design competition">competition for the design of the building</a> was held and the architectural firm of <a href="/wiki/York_and_Sawyer" title="York and Sawyer">York and Sawyer</a> submitted the winning design. The bank moved to its current location in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FRB04_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FRB04-7">[7]</a></sup> The Federal Reserve Bank of New York maintains a vault that lies 80 feet (24 m) below street level and 50 feet (15 m) below sea level,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fed2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fed2-8">[8]</a></sup> resting on Manhattan bedrock. By 1927, the vault contained 10% of the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_gold_reserves" title="Official gold reserves">official gold reserves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FRB04_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FRB04-7">[7]</a></sup> Currently, it is reputedly the largest <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> repository in the world (though this cannot be confirmed as <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> banks do not report their gold stocks) and holds approximately 7,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> (7,700 <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short tons</a>) of gold bullion ($415 billion as of October 2011), more than <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bullion_Depository" title="United States Bullion Depository">Fort Knox</a>. Nearly 98% of the gold at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York is owned by the central banks of foreign nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The rest is owned by the United States and international organizations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMF" title="IMF">IMF</a>. The Federal Reserve Bank does not own the gold but serves as guardian of the precious metal, which it stores at no charge to the owners, but charging a $1.75 fee (in 2008) per bar to move the gold. Moving the bars requires special footwear for the staff, to protect their feet in case they drop one of the gold bars weighing 28 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pounds</a> (13 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>). The vault is open to tourists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrorism_attempt">Terrorism attempt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Terrorism attempt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 17, 2012, Quazi Mohammad Rezwanul Ahsan Nafis, 21, was arrested while attempting to detonate what he believed to be an 800-pound bomb. A joint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYPD" title="NYPD">NYPD</a> operation flagged the suspect on the Internet and set up the sting with fake explosives. In 2013, Nafis pleaded guilty and was sentenced to 30 years in federal prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">Branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Branches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York_Buffalo_Branch" title="Federal Reserve Bank of New York Buffalo Branch">Federal Reserve Bank of New York Buffalo Branch</a> used to be the only branch of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, but it was closed on October 31, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benjamin_Strong.2C_1914-1928">Benjamin Strong, 1914-1928</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Benjamin Strong, 1914-1928">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The New York Fed opened for business on November 16, 1914, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Strong_Jr." title="Benjamin Strong Jr.">Benjamin Strong Jr.</a>, who had previously been president of the Bankers Trust Company. He led the Bank until his death in 1928. Strong became the executive officer (then called the "governor"—today, the term would be "president"). As the leader of the Federal Reserve’s largest and most powerful district bank, Strong became a dominant force in U.S. monetary and banking affairs. One biographer has termed him the "de facto leader of the entire Federal Reserve System."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This was not only because of Strong's abilities, but also because the central board's powers were ambiguous and, for the most part, limited to supervisory and regulatory functions under the 1913 <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Act" title="Federal Reserve Act">Federal Reserve Act</a> because many Americans were antagonistic to centralized control.</p>
<p>When the United States entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Strong was a major force behind the campaigns to fund the war effort via bonds owned primarily by U.S. citizens. This enabled the United States to avoid many of the post-war financial problems of the European belligerents. Strong gradually recognized the importance of <a href="/wiki/Open_market_operation" title="Open market operation">open market operation</a>, or purchases and sales of government <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a>, as a means of managing the quantity of money in the U.S. economy and thus affecting interest rates. This was particularly important at the time because gold had flooded into the United States during and after World War I. Thus, its gold-backed currency was well-protected, but prices had been pushed up substantially by the currency expansion due to the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>-imposed expansion of currency. In 1922, Strong unofficially scrapped the gold standard and instead began aggressively pursuing open market operations as a means of stabilizing domestic prices and thus internal economic stability. Thus, he began the Federal Reserve’s practice of buying and selling government securities as monetary policy. <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>, a prominent British economist who had previously not questioned the gold standard, used Strong's activities as an example of how a central bank could manage a nation's economy without the gold standard in his book "A Tract on Monetary Reform" (1923). To quote one authority:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"It was Strong more than anyone else who invented the modern central banker. When we watch... [central bankers of today] describe how they are seeking to strike the right balance between economic growth and price stability, it is the ghost of Benjamin Strong who hovers above him. It all sounds quite prosaically obvious now, but in 1922 it was a radical departure from more than two hundred years of central banking history."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Strong's policy of maintaining price levels during the 1920s through open market operation and his willingness to maintain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquidity" title="Liquidity">liquidity</a> of banks during panics have been praised by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monetarist" title="Monetarist">monetarists</a> and harshly criticized by <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" title="Austrian School">Austrian economists</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the European economic turmoil of the 1920s, Strong's influence became worldwide. He was a strong supporter of European efforts to return to the gold standard and economic stability. Strong's new monetary policies not only stabilized U.S. prices, they encouraged both U.S. and world trade by helping to stabilize European currencies and finances. However, with virtually no inflation, interest rates were low and the U.S. economy and corporate profits surged, fueling the stock market increases of the late 1920s. This worried him, but he also felt he had no choice because the low interest rates were helping Europeans (particularly Great Britain) in their effort to return to the gold standard.</p>
<p>Economic historian <a href="/wiki/Charles_P._Kindleberger" title="Charles P. Kindleberger">Charles P. Kindleberger</a> states that Strong was one of the few U.S. policymakers interested in the troubled financial affairs of Europe in the 1920s, and that had he not died in 1928, just a year before the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, he might have been able to maintain stability in the international financial system;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Strong died in 1928, the New York Bank lost its energetic leadership. In the 1930s some of its power was transferred to the Federal Reserve System headquarters in Washington.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2009, the New York Federal Reserve Bank commissioned a probe into its own practices. David Beim, finance professor at Columbia Business School submitted a report in 2009, released by the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Crisis_Inquiry_Commission" title="Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission">Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission</a> in 2011, saying "that a number of people he interviewed at the reserve bank believe that supervisors paid excessive deference to banks and, as a result, they were less aggressive in finding issues or in following up on them in a forceful way.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloom_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloom-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Friedman_(PFIAB)" title="Stephen Friedman (PFIAB)">Stephen Friedman</a>'s resignation on 7 May 2009, Denis Hughes, formerly Deputy Chair, was designated as Interim Chair. The current Chair (for 2013) is Emily Rafferty, President of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>.</p>
<p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Segarra" title="Carmen Segarra">Carmen Segarra</a>, then New York Fed bank examiner, told her superiors that <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> had no policy governing conflicts of interest. She was "fired in May 2012 after refusing to change her findings on the conflict-of-interest policy and sued the New York Fed October 2013".</p>
<p>In April 2014 the U.S. District court in Manhattan dismissed the case, "ruling that Segarra failed to make a legally sufficient claim under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whistle-blower" title="Whistle-blower">whistle-blower</a> protections of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Act" title="Federal Deposit Insurance Act">Federal Deposit Insurance Act</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloom_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloom-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2014, Segarra´s secretly recorded conversations were aired by <i><a href="/wiki/This_American_Life" title="This American Life">This American Life</a></i> and the New York Fed was accused of political corruption by being a "captured agency", <i>i.e.</i>, subject to <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_capture" title="Regulatory capture">regulatory capture</a>, of the banks which it supervises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the first Godfather movie, the "meeting of the Dons" scene uses the Federal Reserve building exterior, even though the interior of another New York building (the Penn Central railway boardroom) was also used for filming.</p>
<p>The Federal Reserve Bank of New York plays a prominent role in the 1995 film <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Hard_with_a_Vengeance" title="Die Hard with a Vengeance">Die Hard with a Vengeance</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Willis" title="Bruce Willis">Bruce Willis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a>. The Federal Reserve Bank is the setting for a major heist of the gold by Jeremy Irons' character, Simon Gruber. The vault is penetrated under the pretense of construction work and the gold bullion transported via dump trucks to a location outside the city.</p>
