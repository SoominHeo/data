<p>The <i><b>New York World</b></i> was a <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> published in New York City from 1860 until 1931. The paper played a major role in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_American_newspapers" title="History of American newspapers">history of American newspapers</a>. It was a leading national voice of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="History of the Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>. From 1883 to 1911 under publisher <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pulitzer" title="Joseph Pulitzer">Joseph Pulitzer</a>, it became a pioneer in <a href="/wiki/Yellow_journalism" title="Yellow journalism">yellow journalism</a>, capturing readers' attention and pushing its daily circulation to the one-million mark.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>World</i> was formed in 1860. From 1862 to 1876, it was edited by <a href="/wiki/Manton_Marble" title="Manton Marble">Manton Marble</a>, who was also its proprietor. In 1864, the <i>World</i> was shut down for three days after it published forged documents purportedly from <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Marble, disgusted by the defeat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Tilden" title="Samuel Tilden">Samuel Tilden</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1876" title="U.S. presidential election, 1876">1876 presidential election</a>, sold the paper after the election to a group headed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Scott" title="Thomas A. Scott">Thomas A. Scott</a>, the president of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>, who used the paper "as a propaganda vehicle for his stock enterprises."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESwanberg196767_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwanberg196767-3">[3]</a></sup> But Scott was unable to meet the newspaper's growing losses, and in 1879 he sold the newspaper to financier <a href="/wiki/Jay_Gould" title="Jay Gould">Jay Gould</a> as part of a deal that also included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_%26_Pacific_Railroad" title="Texas &amp; Pacific Railroad">Texas &amp; Pacific Railroad</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESwanberg196767_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwanberg196767-3">[3]</a></sup> Gould, like Scott, used the paper for his own purposes, employing it to help him take over <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a>. But Gould could not turn the financial state of the newspaper around, and by the 1880s, it was losing $40,000 a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESwanberg196767_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwanberg196767-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joseph_Pulitzer_years">Joseph Pulitzer years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Joseph Pulitzer years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pulitzer" title="Joseph Pulitzer">Joseph Pulitzer</a> bought the World in 1883 and began an aggressive era of circulation building. Reporter <a href="/wiki/Nellie_Bly" title="Nellie Bly">Nellie Bly</a> became one of America's first <a href="/wiki/Investigative_journalism" title="Investigative journalism">investigative journalists</a>, often working undercover. As a <a href="/wiki/Publicity_stunt" title="Publicity stunt">publicity stunt</a> for the paper, inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Around_the_World_in_Eighty_Days_(novel)" title="Around the World in Eighty Days (novel)">Around the World in Eighty Days</a></i>, she traveled around the planet in 72 days in 1889-1890. In 1890, Pulitzer built the <a href="/wiki/New_York_World_Building" title="New York World Building">New York World Building</a>, the tallest office building in the world at the time.</p>
<p>In 1889, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Chambers" title="Julius Chambers">Julius Chambers</a> was appointed by Pulitzer as managing editor of the <i>New York World;</i> he served until 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1896, the <i>World</i> began using a four-color printing press; it was the first newspaper to launch a color <a href="/wiki/Supplement_(publishing)" title="Supplement (publishing)">supplement</a>, which featured the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_Kid" title="Yellow Kid">Yellow Kid</a> cartoon <i>Hogan's Alley</i>. It joined a circulation battle with <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Journal_American" title="New York Journal American">New York Journal American</a></i>.</p>
<p>The <i>World</i> was attacked for being "sensational", and its circulation battles with Hearst's <i>Journal American</i> gave rise to the term <a href="/wiki/Yellow_journalism" title="Yellow journalism">yellow journalism</a>. The charges of sensationalism were most frequently leveled at the paper by more established publishers, who resented Pulitzer's courting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant">immigrant</a> classes. And while the <i>World</i> presented its fair share of crime stories, it also published damning exposés of <a href="/wiki/Tenement" title="Tenement">tenement</a> abuses. After a heat wave in 1883 killed a disproportionate number of poor children, the <i>World</i> published stories about it, featuring such headlines as "Lines of Little Hearses." Its coverage spurred action in the city for reform. Hearst reproduced Pulitzer's approach in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Examiner" title="San Francisco Examiner">San Francisco Examiner</a></i> and later in the <i>Journal American</i>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_I._Cobb" title="Frank I. Cobb">Frank Irving Cobb</a> was employed on a trial basis as the editor of the <i>World</i> in 1904 by publisher Pulitzer. Cobb was a fiercely independent Kansan who resisted Pulitzer's attempts to "run the office" from his home. The elder man was so invested in the paper that he continually meddled with Cobb's work. The two found common ground in their support of <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, but they had many other areas of disagreement.</p>
<p>When Pulitzer's son took over administrative responsibility of <i>The World</i> in 1907, his father wrote a precisely worded resignation. Cobb had it printed in every New York paper—except the <i>World</i>. Pulitzer raged at the insult, but slowly began to respect Cobb's editorials and independent spirit. Exchanges, commentaries, and messages between them increased. The good rapport between the two was based largely on Cobb's flexibility. In May 1908, Cobb and Pulitzer met to outline plans for a consistent editorial policy.</p>
<p>Pulitzer's demands for editorials on contemporary breaking news led to overwork by Cobb. The publisher sent his managing editor on a six-week tour of Europe to restore his spirit. Shortly after Cobb's return, Pulitzer died. Cobb then published Pulitzer's resignation. Cobb retained the editorial policies he had shared with Pulitzer until he died of cancer in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Pulitzer died in 1911, he passed control of the <i>World</i> to his sons Ralph, Joseph and Herbert. The <i>World</i> continued to grow under its executive editor <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Bayard_Swope" title="Herbert Bayard Swope">Herbert Bayard Swope</a>, who hired writers such as <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sullivan_(writer)" title="Frank Sullivan (writer)">Frank Sullivan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deems_Taylor" title="Deems Taylor">Deems Taylor</a>. Among the <i>World'</i>s noted journalists were columnists <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Pierce_Adams" title="Franklin Pierce Adams">Franklin Pierce Adams</a> (F.P.A.) who wrote "The Conning Tower," <a href="/wiki/Heywood_Broun" title="Heywood Broun">Heywood Broun</a> who penned "It Seems To Me" on the editorial page, and <a href="/wiki/Hardboiled" title="Hardboiled">hardboiled</a> writer <a href="/wiki/James_M._Cain" title="James M. Cain">James M. Cain</a>. <a href="/wiki/C._M._Payne" title="C. M. Payne">C. M. Payne</a> created several comic strips for the newspaper.</p>
<p>The paper published the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossword_puzzle" title="Crossword puzzle">crossword puzzle</a> in December 1913. The annual <a href="/wiki/Reference_work" title="Reference work">reference book</a>, called <i><a href="/wiki/World_Almanac" title="World Almanac">The World Almanac</a>,</i> was founded by the newspaper, and its name, <i>World</i> Almanac, is directly descended from the newspaper.</p>
<p>The paper ran a twenty-article series that was an <a href="/wiki/New_York_World_Expos%C3%A9_of_the_Ku_Klux_Klan" title="New York World Exposé of the Ku Klux Klan">exposé</a> on the 20th-century revival of the <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a>, starting September 6, 1921.</p>
<p>In 1931, Pulitzer's heirs went to court to sell the <i>World</i>. A surrogate court judge decided in the Pulitzer sons' favor; <a href="/wiki/Roy_W._Howard" title="Roy W. Howard">Roy W. Howard</a> purchased the newspaper for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scripps-Howard" title="Scripps-Howard">Scripps-Howard</a> chain. He closed the <i>World</i> and laid off the staff of 3,000 after the final issue was printed on February 27, 1931. Howard added the <i>World</i> name to his afternoon paper, the <i>Evening Telegram</i>, and called it the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_World-Telegram" title="New York World-Telegram">New York World-Telegram</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Janet E. Steele argues that Pulitzer put a stamp on his age when he brought his brand of journalism from <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> to New York in 1883. In his <i>New York World</i>, Pulitzer emphasized illustrations, advertising, and a culture of consumption for working men. He believed they saved money to enjoy life with their families when they could, at <a href="/wiki/Coney_Island" title="Coney Island">Coney Island</a>, for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steele_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-steele-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By contrast, the long-established editor <a href="/wiki/Charles_Anderson_Dana" title="Charles Anderson Dana">Charles A. Dana</a>, of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(New_York)" title="The Sun (New York)">The Sun</a></i>, held to a traditional view of the working man as one engaged in a struggle to better his working conditions and to improve himself. Dana thought that readers in the 20th century followed fewer faddish illustrations and wished newspapers did not need advertising. Dana resisted buying a <a href="/wiki/Linotype_machine" title="Linotype machine">Linotype</a>. These two editors, and their newspapers, reflected two worlds—one old, one new—and Pulitzer won.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steele_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-steele-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 16, 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_Graduate_School_of_Journalism" title="Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism">Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism</a> announced that it was launching an online publication named <i>The New York World,</i> in honor of the original newspaper published by Pulitzer, who founded the graduate school. The university said the mission of the publication would be "to provide New York City citizens with accountability journalism about government operations that affect their lives." It is to be staffed mainly by those who have completed master's or doctoral degrees, and other affiliates of the school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
