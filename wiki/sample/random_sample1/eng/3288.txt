<p>The <i><b>New York Herald</b></i> was a large distribution newspaper based in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> that existed between May 6, 1835, and 1924.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first issue of the paper was published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gordon_Bennett,_Sr." title="James Gordon Bennett, Sr.">James Gordon Bennett, Sr.</a>, on May 6, 1835. By 1845, it was the most popular and profitable daily newspaper in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crouthamel_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-crouthamel-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1861, it circulated 84,000 copies and called itself "the most largely circulated journal in the world." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandburg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sandburg-2">[2]</a></sup> Bennett stated that the function of a newspaper "is not to instruct but to startle and amuse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roeder2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roeder2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His politics tended to be anti-Catholic and he had tended to favor the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Know-Nothing" title="Know-Nothing">Know-Nothing</a> faction, though he was not particularly anti-immigrant as the Know-Nothing party were. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, his policy as expressed by the newspaper was to staunchly support the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)#Civil_War_and_Reconstruction:_1854-1877" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Hudson" title="Frederic Hudson">Frederic Hudson</a> served as managing editor of the paper from 1846–1866.</p>
<p>Bennett turned over control of the paper to his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gordon_Bennett,_Jr." title="James Gordon Bennett, Jr.">James Gordon Bennett, Jr.</a> in 1866. Under Gordon Bennett Jr., the paper financed <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley" title="Henry Morton Stanley">Henry Morton Stanley</a>'s expeditions into Africa to find <a href="/wiki/David_Livingstone" title="David Livingstone">David Livingstone</a>, where they met on November 10, 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-man_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-man-5">[5]</a></sup> The paper also supported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morton_Stanley%27s_first_trans-Africa_exploration" title="Morton Stanley's first trans-Africa exploration">Stanley's trans-Africa exploration</a>, and in 1879 supported the ill-fated expedition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._DeLong" title="George W. DeLong">George W. DeLong</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> region.</p>
<p>In 1874, the <i>Herald</i> ran the infamous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_York_Zoo_hoax" title="The New York Zoo hoax">New York Zoo hoax</a>, where the front page of the newspaper was devoted entirely to a fabricated story of wild animals getting loose at the <a href="/wiki/Central_Park_Zoo" title="Central Park Zoo">Central Park Zoo</a> and attacking numerous people.</p>
<p>On October 4, 1887, Bennett Jr. sent <a href="/wiki/Julius_Chambers" title="Julius Chambers">Julius Chambers</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">Paris, France</a> to launch a European edition. Bennett himself later moved to Paris, but the <i>New York Herald</i> suffered from his attempt to manage its operation in New York by telegram. In 1924, after Bennett Jr.'s death, the <i>New York Herald</i> was acquired by its smaller rival the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Tribune" title="New York Tribune">New York Tribune</a></i>, to form the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald_Tribune" title="New York Herald Tribune">New York Herald Tribune</a></i>. In 1959, the <i>New York Herald Tribune</i> and its European edition were sold to <a href="/wiki/John_Hay_Whitney" title="John Hay Whitney">John Hay Whitney</a>, then the U.S. ambassador to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">Britain</a>. In 1966, the New York paper ceased publication. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> acquired joint control of the European edition, renaming it the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Herald_Tribune" title="International Herald Tribune">International Herald Tribune</a></i>. Today, the <i>IHT</i>, renamed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Herald_Tribune" title="International Herald Tribune">The New York Times International Edition</a></i>, is owned entirely by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> and remains an <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> paper, printed at 35 sites around the world and for sale in more than 180 countries.</p>
<p>When the <i>Herald</i> was still under the authority of its original publisher Bennett, it was considered to be the most invasive and sensationalist of the leading New York papers. Its ability to entertain the public with timely daily news made it the leading circulation paper of its time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evening_Telegram"><i>Evening Telegram</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evening Telegram">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Evening_Telegram" title="New York Evening Telegram">New York Evening Telegram</a></i> was founded in 1867 by the junior Bennett, and was considered by many to be an evening edition of the <i>Herald</i>. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Munsey" title="Frank Munsey">Frank Munsey</a> acquired the <i>Telegram</i> in 1920, which ceased its connection to the <i>Herald</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-times1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemorated">Commemorated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commemorated">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New York's <a href="/wiki/Herald_Square" title="Herald Square">Herald Square</a> is named after the <i>New York Herald</i> newspaper; in the north side of the square there is a sculpture commemorating the Bennetts. The statue of Minerva, the Bellringers, and Owls by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonin_Carles" title="Antonin Carles">Antonin Carles</a> originally graced the New York Herald building and rang every hour until it was moved to Herald Square. The chorus of <i><a href="/wiki/Give_My_Regards_to_Broadway" title="Give My Regards to Broadway">Give My Regards to Broadway</a></i> includes the phrase, "[R]emember me to Herald Square." North of Herald Square is <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a>, which is named after rival <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.</p>
