<div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/National_Park,_New_Zealand" title="National Park, New Zealand">National Park, New Zealand</a>, a village and railway station near Tongariro National Park.</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> are 13 protected areas administered by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Conservation_(New_Zealand)" title="Department of Conservation (New Zealand)">Department of Conservation</a>. Although the national parks contain some of New Zealand's most beautiful scenery, the first few established were all focused on mountain scenery. Since the 1980s the focus has been on developing a more diverse representation of New Zealand landscapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The parks are all culturally significant; many also contain historic features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tongariro_National_Park" title="Tongariro National Park">Tongariro National Park</a>, in particular, is one of 27 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Sites</a> that is of both cultural and natural significance, while four of the <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a> national parks form <a href="/wiki/Te_Wahipounamu" title="Te Wahipounamu">Te Wahipounamu</a>, another World Heritage Site. A 14th national park, <a href="/wiki/Te_Urewera_National_Park" title="Te Urewera National Park">Te Urewera National Park</a>, was disestablished in 2014.</p>
<p>The national parks are administered by the Department of Conservation "for the benefit, use, and enjoyment of the public".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They are popular tourist destinations, with three-tenths of overseas tourists visiting at least one national park during their stay in New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Parks_Act">National Parks Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_parks_of_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: National Parks Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Parks Act of 1980 was established in order to codify the purpose, governance and selection of national parks. It begins by establishing the definition of a national park:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is hereby declared that the provisions of this Act shall have</p>
<p>effect for the purpose of preserving in perpetuity as national parks, for their intrinsic worth and for the benefit, use, and enjoyment of the public, areas of New Zealand that contain scenery of such distinctive quality, ecological systems, or natural features so beautiful, unique, or scientifically important</p>
that their preservation is in the national interest.
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/National_Parks_Act_1980_(New_Zealand)" title="National Parks Act 1980 (New Zealand)">National Parks Act 1980</a>, Part 1, section 4, subsection 1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The National Parks Act goes on to state that the public will have freedom of entry and access to the parks, though this is subject to restrictions to ensure the preservation of native plants and animals and the welfare of the parks in general. Access to specially protected areas (550 km²) constituted under the act is by permit only. Under the Act, national parks are to be maintained in their natural state as far as possible to retain their value as soil, water and forest conservation areas. Native plants and animals are to be preserved and introduced plants and animals removed if their presence interferes with the natural wildlife. Development in wilderness areas established under the act is restricted to foot tracks and huts used for wild animal control or scientific research.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Services_available_for_public_use">Services available for public use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_parks_of_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Services available for public use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Act allows the Department of Conservation to provide hostels, huts, camping grounds, ski tows and similar facilities, parking areas, roading and tracks within the parks. In addition to these, the department also provides some accommodation, transport and other services at entry points to the parks, but these are also offered by other government agencies, voluntary organisations and private firms. More comprehensive services within the parks, such as guided walks and skiing tutorials, are privately provided with concessions from the department.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_national_park">Proposed national park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_parks_of_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed national park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area centred on <a href="/wiki/Waipoua_Forest" title="Waipoua Forest">Waipoua Forest</a>, north of Dargaville, has been proposed as a possible Kauri National Park. The area contains most of New Zealand's remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kauri" title="Kauri">kauri</a>, including the largest known kauri, <a href="/wiki/T%C4%81ne_Mahuta" title="Tāne Mahuta">Tāne Mahuta</a>. These stands of kauri are also valuable as havens for endangered species including the <a href="/wiki/North_Island_brown_kiwi" title="North Island brown kiwi">North Island brown kiwi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This proposal is currently being investigated by the Department of Conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to a DoC proposal to upgrade the protection of <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Island" title="Great Barrier Island">Great Barrier Island</a> (Aotea), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_and_Bird" title="Forest and Bird">Forest and Bird</a> launched a campaign in 2014 to designate it as a National Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining_concerns">Mining concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_parks_of_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mining concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2010 the New Zealand Government proposed removing some national park and conservation areas from Schedule 4 protection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Minerals_Act" title="Crown Minerals Act">Crown Minerals Act</a> which prohibits mining in those areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GREENZONE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-GREENZONE-9">[9]</a></sup> In July the government abandoned the proposal after receiving a large number of submissions, most of which opposed mining.</p>
