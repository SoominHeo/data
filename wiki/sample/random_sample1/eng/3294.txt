<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of diplomatic missions of New Zealand</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>This is a <b>list of diplomatic missions of New Zealand</b>, excluding honorary consulates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>'s Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade (NZMFAT) is responsible for nearly fifty embassies and consulates globally. The country is particularly active in the <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">South Pacific</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> where New Zealand is expanding its commercial reach. There is also an extensive number of honorary consulates who represent <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Independence_of_New_Zealand" title="Independence of New Zealand">New Zealand independence</a> was a gradual process, and the establishment of an independent New Zealand diplomatic service was similarly gradual. At first, New Zealand's foreign affairs were handled by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the only diplomacy conducted by the colonial government in New Zealand were negotiations with the British authorities. Relations between New Zealand and Britain were handled by an Agent-General in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, with the first being appointed in 1871. The title was changed to High Commissioner in 1905, reflecting the increasing autonomy of New Zealand.</p>
<p>It was not until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, however, that New Zealand sent permanent diplomatic missions to other countries. To facilitate co-ordination of the war effort New Zealand established several posts in countries with which it was allied—the first was a <a href="/wiki/Legation" title="Legation">legation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in 1941. In 1942 and 1943, high commissions were opened in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a> respectively, and in 1944, a legation was established in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. The latter was considered a striking departure from New Zealand's previous diplomatic activities—enthusiasm for the post was strongest in the governing <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour Party</a>, and the opposition <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">National Party</a> later made its closure one of their campaign policies.</p>
<p>The opening of these posts prompted New Zealand to establish its own foreign ministry, the Department of External Affairs. Created by the <i>External Affairs Act</i> in June 1943, the new Department incorporated an older office of the same name (dealing with island territories) and those sections of the Prime Minister's office which had previously co-ordinated diplomacy.</p>
<p>In 1947, a resident trade representative was appointed in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, followed by the establishment of legations in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> (1949) and <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a> (1950). By the late 1950s, these three posts, along with Washington, had been upgraded to embassies —. However, fulfilling its promise the Moscow post was closed in 1950 by the new National Party government. From 1955 to 1961, a string of new missions opened in Asia—<a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a> obtained its independence from New Zealand in 1962, it became the first Pacific Island state to host a New Zealand diplomatic post.</p>
<p>The mid-1960s saw a cluster of new posts opening in Europe, with new missions in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. A post was also opened in <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnam</a> due to the ongoing conflict there, although this did not last beyond the end of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. A post was established in the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a> after they obtained self-government from New Zealand, becoming New Zealand's second mission in the south Pacific.</p>
<p>The 1970s and 1980s were a time of continued expansion for NZMFAT. In 1973, the Labour government of <a href="/wiki/Norman_Kirk" title="Norman Kirk">Norman Kirk</a> reopened its embassy in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and opened an embassy in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>. However it was in the Pacific where New Zealand's representation rapidly expanded as several countries gained independence, including <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niue" title="Niue">Niue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>. Frequently New Zealand was the first country to establish a mission in these states. New Zealand also expanded into the Middle East, Latin America, Africa and elsewhere in Asia.</p>
<p>The early 1990s were a time of substantial reorganisation of New Zealand's diplomatic missions, and saw a number of closures. A substantial redistribution of New Zealand's European resources took place—posts in Greece and Austria were closed, while a new post was established in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. New Zealand's post in Iraq was closed due to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, and the post in Bahrain was shut shortly afterwards—the post in Saudi Arabia assumed most of their duties, aided by a new post in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. The end of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> in resulted in a decision to move New Zealand's post in Zimbabwe to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. In South America New Zealand's embassy in Peru was also closed, but missions were opened in Brazil and Argentina. New Zealand's most recent embassy openings have been in <a href="/wiki/Dili" title="Dili">Dili</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>.</p>
<p>In April 2008 it was announced that the budget for NZMFAT would be dramatically increased, and would include the opening of new missions in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_Herald_10504591_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NZ_Herald_10504591-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 4 May 2010 the Foreign Affairs Minister Murray McCully announced that New Zealand's diplomatic relations with Afghanistan will be formally represented by the first resident Ambassador of New Zealand to Kabul, retired Army Brigadier Neville Reilly. In addition the first Civil Director of the Provincial Reconstruction Team (PRT) in the central Afghan Province of Bamyan based in the Provincial Capital of Bamyan will be led by retired Air Commodore Dick Newlands of the Air Force. The Civil Director will replace the military aspects of the PRT with greater emphasis on civilian provided resources and services for the people of Bamyan. The former brass leaders Brigadier Reilly and Air Commodore Newlands will lead and represent New Zealand's national, diplomatic, security, cultural and other relevant issues of importance in Afghanistan, in addition to the nation's vested interest in providing an Afghanistan that can provide for itself and its people in a number of areas.</p>
