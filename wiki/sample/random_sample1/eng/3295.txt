<p><b>New Zealanders</b>, colloquially known as <b><a href="/wiki/Kiwi_(people)" title="Kiwi (people)">Kiwis</a></b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> are <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_nationality_law" title="New Zealand nationality law">citizens</a> of New Zealand. New Zealand is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiethnic_society" title="Multiethnic society">multiethnic society</a>, and home to people of many national origins. Originally composed solely of the indigenous <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_New_Zealand" title="Demographics of New Zealand">ethnic makeup of the population</a> has been dominated since the 19th century by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_European" title="New Zealand European">New Zealanders of European descent</a>, mainly of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> ancestry, with smaller percentages of other European ancestries such as <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">South Slavic</a>. New Zealand had an estimated resident population of around 4.47 million as of June 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> although around 220,000 of those have been resident in the country for less than five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, the ethnic makeup of the New Zealand population is undergoing a process of change, with new waves of <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_New_Zealand" title="Immigration to New Zealand">immigration</a>, higher birth rates and increasing <a href="/wiki/Interracial_marriage" title="Interracial marriage">interracial marriage</a> resulting in the New Zealand population of <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_people" title="Asian people">Asian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander" title="Pacific Islander">Pacific Islander</a> and <a href="/wiki/Multiracial" title="Multiracial">multiracial</a> descent growing at a higher rate than those of solely European descent, with such groups projected to make up a larger proportion of the population in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While most New Zealanders live in New Zealand, there is also a significant <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a>, estimated in 2001 at over 460,000 or 14% of the international total of New Zealand-born people. Of these, 360,000, over three-quarters of the New Zealand-born population residing outside of New Zealand, live in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Other communities of New Zealanders abroad are predominantly concentrated in other <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>-speaking countries, specifically the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, with smaller numbers located elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_diaspora_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-The_diaspora-2">[2]</a></sup> This diaspora has reportedly surged as of 2010, with well over 650,000 New Zealanders living abroad. According to the Australian Department of Immigration and Border Protection, an estimated 640,770 New Zealanders lived in Australia on 30 June 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Australia_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Australia-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_New_Zealand" title="Immigration to New Zealand">Immigration to New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_New_Zealand" title="Demographics of New Zealand">Demographics of New Zealand</a></div>
<p>The majority of New Zealanders or their <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_New_Zealand" title="Immigration to New Zealand">ancestors immigrated</a> within the past three centuries, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> population who settled New Zealand from Eastern <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a>. Despite its multi-ethnic composition, the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_New_Zealand" title="Culture of New Zealand">culture of New Zealand</a> held in common by most New Zealanders can also be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a> "New Zealand culture", a <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a> largely derived from the traditions of <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern European</a> colonists, settlers, and immigrants. It also includes influences of <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_culture" title="Māori culture">Māori culture</a>. Large-scale immigration in the 20th and 21st centuries from <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Zealander" title="Chinese New Zealander">Chinese</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_New_Zealander" title="Indian New Zealander">Indians</a> introduced a variety of elements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polynesian_settlers">Polynesian settlers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Polynesian settlers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori people</a> are most likely descended from people who emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a> and then travelled east through to the <a href="/wiki/Society_Islands" title="Society Islands">Society Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After a pause of 70 to 265 years, a new wave of exploration led to the discovery and settlement of New Zealand in about AD 1250–1300,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> making New Zealand one of the most recently settled major landmasses. Some researchers have suggested an earlier wave of arrivals dating to as early as AD 50–150; these people then either died out or left the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeinSmith6_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-MeinSmith6-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton1-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the following centuries the Polynesian settlers developed into a distinct culture now known as <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_culture" title="Māori culture">Māori</a>. The population was divided into <a href="/wiki/Iwi" title="Iwi">iwi</a> (tribes) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hapu" title="Hapu">hapū</a> (subtribes) which would cooperate, compete and sometimes fight with each other. At some point a group of Māori migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Islands" title="Chatham Islands">Chatham Islands</a> where they developed their distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moriori_people" title="Moriori people">Moriori</a> culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark123_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark123-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to New Zealand's geographic isolation, 500 years passed before the next phase of settlement, the arrival of Europeans. Only then did the indigenous inhabitants need to distinguish themselves from the new arrivals, using the term "Māori" which means "normal" or "ordinary".</p>
<p>The establishment of British colonies in Australia from 1788 and the boom in whaling and sealing in the Southern Ocean brought many Europeans and Americans to the vicinity of New Zealand. Some settled—for economic, religious or personal reasons.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European">European</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: European">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/European_New_Zealanders" title="European New Zealanders">European New Zealanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_census" title="New Zealand census">New Zealand census</a></div>
<p>People of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">European</a> descent constitute the majority of the 4.2 million people living in New Zealand, with 2,969,391 or 74.0% of the population in the 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Census" title="New Zealand Census">New Zealand Census</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_Census_results:_New_Zealand_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-2013_Census_results:_New_Zealand-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The table above shows the broad ethnic composition of New Zealand population at the 1961 and the most recent 2013 census. Europeans include all persons of European - Maori — quarter castes, while the Maori population consists of all persons with half or more Maori ancestry. The residual "others" ethnic group consists largely of Asians and Pacific Islanders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cicred.org_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-cicred.org-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most European New Zealanders are of <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> ancestry, with smaller percentages of other European ancestries such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croatians" title="Croatians">Croatians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a> (historically noted as German due to <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a>), <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">South Slav</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In the 1961 census, over 90% self-identified as people of European descent.</p>
<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">Europeans</a> known to have reached New Zealand were Dutch explorer <a href="/wiki/Abel_Tasman" title="Abel Tasman">Abel Janszoon Tasman</a> and his crew in 1642.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeinSmith23_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MeinSmith23-26">[26]</a></sup> Māori killed several of the crew and no more Europeans went to New Zealand until British explorer <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a>'s voyage of 1768–71.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeinSmith23_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-MeinSmith23-26">[26]</a></sup> Cook reached New Zealand in 1769 and mapped almost the entire coastline. Following Cook, New Zealand was visited by numerous European and North American <a href="/wiki/History_of_whaling" title="History of whaling">whaling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">sealing</a> and trading ships. They traded European food and goods, especially metal tools and weapons, for Māori timber, food, artefacts and water. On occasion, Europeans and Māori traded goods for sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King122_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-King122-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The potato and the <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">musket</a> transformed Māori agriculture and warfare, although the resulting <a href="/wiki/Musket_Wars" title="Musket Wars">Musket Wars</a> died out once the tribal imbalance of arms had been rectified. From the early nineteenth century, Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missionaries" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a> began to settle New Zealand, eventually converting most of the Māori population, although their initial inroads were mainly among the more disaffected elements of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Becoming aware of the lawless nature of European settlement and of increasing French interest in the territory, the British government appointed <a href="/wiki/James_Busby" title="James Busby">James Busby</a> as British Resident to New Zealand in 1832. Busby failed to bring law and order to European settlement, but did oversee the introduction of the first national flag on 20 March 1834, after an unregistered New Zealand ship was seized in Australia. The nebulous <a href="/wiki/United_Tribes_of_New_Zealand" title="United Tribes of New Zealand">United Tribes of New Zealand</a> later, in October 1835, sent the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Independence_of_New_Zealand" title="Declaration of the Independence of New Zealand">Declaration of the Independence of New Zealand</a> to King <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="William IV of the United Kingdom">William IV of the United Kingdom</a>, asking him for protection. Ongoing unrest and the legal standing of the Declaration of Independence prompted the Colonial Office to send Captain <a href="/wiki/William_Hobson" title="William Hobson">William Hobson</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">RN</a> to New Zealand to claim sovereignty for the British Crown and negotiate a treaty with the Māori.<sup class="reference" id="ref_Notei"><a href="#endnote_Notei">[i]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi" title="Treaty of Waitangi">Treaty of Waitangi</a> was first signed in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Islands" title="Bay of Islands">Bay of Islands</a> on 6 February 1840.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-29">[29]</a></sup> The drafting was done hastily and confusion and disagreement continue to surround the translation. The Treaty however remains regarded as New Zealand's foundation as a nation and is revered by Māori as a guarantee of their rights.</p>
<p>In response to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Company" title="New Zealand Company">New Zealand Company</a> attempts to establish a separate colony in Wellington, and French claims in Akaroa, Hobson, now Lieutenant-Governor, declared British sovereignty over all of New Zealand on 21 May 1840. The two proclamations published in the <i>New Zealand Advertiser and Bay Of Islands Gazette</i> issue of 19 June 1840 "assert[s] on the grounds of Discovery, the Sovereign Rights of Her Majesty over the Southern Islands of New Zealand, commonly called 'The Middle Island' (<a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a>) and 'Stewart's Island' (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stewart_Island/Rakiura" title="Stewart Island/Rakiura">Stewart Island/Rakiura</a>); and the Island, commonly called 'The Northern Island', having been ceded Sovereignty to Her Majesty." The second proclamation expanded on how sovereignty over the "Northern Island" had been ceded under the treaty signed that February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the formalising of sovereignty, the organised and structured flow of migrants from Great Britain and Ireland began, and by 1860 more than 100,000 British and Irish settlers lived throughout New Zealand. The <a href="/wiki/Otago_Association" title="Otago Association">Otago Association</a> actively recruited settlers from Scotland, creating a definite Scottish influence in that region, while the <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Association" title="Canterbury Association">Canterbury Association</a> recruited settlers from the south of England, creating a definite English influence over that region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> By 1870 the non-Māori population reached over 250,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other settlers came from Germany, Scandinavia, and other parts of Europe as well as from China and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, but British and Irish settlers made up the vast majority, and did so for the next 150 years.</p>

<p>Between 1881 and the 1920s, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Parliament" title="New Zealand Parliament">New Zealand Parliament</a> passed legislation that intended to limit Asiatic migration to New Zealand, and prevented Asians from naturalising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In particular, the New Zealand government levied a poll tax on Chinese immigrants up until the 1930s, when Japan went to war with China. New Zealand finally abolished the poll tax in 1944. An influx of Jewish refugees from central Europe came in the 1930s. Many of the persons of Polish origin in New Zealand arrived as orphans from Eastern Poland via Siberia and Iran in 1944 during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Second_World_War_European_immigration">Post-Second World War European immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-Second World War European immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the agencies of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> dealing with humanitarian efforts following the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, New Zealand accepted about 5,000 refugees and displaced persons from Europe, and more than 1,100 <a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarians</a> between 1956 and 1959 (see <a href="/wiki/Refugee_migration_into_New_Zealand" title="Refugee migration into New Zealand">Refugee migration into New Zealand</a>). The post-WWII immigration included more persons from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greeks_in_New_Zealand" title="Greeks in New Zealand">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>.</p>
<p>New Zealand limited immigration to those who would meet a labour shortage in New Zealand. To encourage those to come, the Government introduced free and assisted passages in 1947, a schema expanded by the National Party administration in 1950. However, when it became clear that not enough skilled migrants would come from the British Isles alone, recruitment began in Northern European countries. New Zealand signed a bilateral agreement for skilled migrants with the Netherlands, and a large number of Dutch immigrants arrived in New Zealand. Others came in the 1950s from Denmark, Germany, Switzerland and Austria to meet needs in specialised occupations. By the 1960s, the policy of excluding people based on nationality yielded a population overwhelmingly European in origin. By the mid-1960s, a desire for cheap unskilled labour led to ethnic diversification.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="M.C4.81ori">Māori</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Māori">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori people</a></div>
<p>In the 2013 Census, 598,602 people identified as being part of the Māori ethnic group, accounting for 14.9%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats.govt.nz_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-stats.govt.nz-22">[22]</a></sup> of the New Zealand population, while 668,724 people (17.5%) claimed Māori descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats.govt.nz_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-stats.govt.nz-22">[22]</a></sup> 278,199 people identified as of sole Māori ethnicity, while 291,015 identified as of both European and Māori ethnicity (with or without a third ethnicity), due to a high rate of <a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">intermarriage</a> between the two cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Under the Maori Affairs Amendment Act 1974, a Māori is defined as “a person of the Māori race of New Zealand; and includes any descendant of such a Māori."</p>
<p>According to the 2006 Census, the largest iwi by population is <a href="/wiki/Ng%C4%81puhi" title="Ngāpuhi">Ngāpuhi</a> (125,601), followed by <a href="/wiki/Ng%C4%81ti_Porou" title="Ngāti Porou">Ngāti Porou</a> (71,049), <a href="/wiki/Ng%C4%81i_Tahu" title="Ngāi Tahu">Ngāi Tahu</a> (54,819) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waikato_(iwi)" title="Waikato (iwi)">Waikato</a> (40,083). However, over 110,000 people of Māori descent in the 2013 Census could not identify their iwi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats.govt.nz_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-stats.govt.nz-22">[22]</a></sup> Outside of New Zealand, a large Māori population exists in Australia, estimated at 155,000 in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamer_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamer-36">[36]</a></sup> The Māori Party has suggested a special seat should be created in the New Zealand parliament representing Māori in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Smaller communities also exist in the United Kingdom (approx. 8,000), the United States (up to 3,500) and Canada (approx. 1,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walrond_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walrond-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uscb_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-uscb-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most common region this group lived in was <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Region" title="Auckland Region">Auckland Region</a> (23.9 percent or 142,770 people). They are the second-largest ethnic group in New Zealand, after European New Zealanders ("<a href="/wiki/P%C4%81keh%C4%81" title="Pākehā">Pākehā</a>"). In addition, more than 120,000 Māori live in Australia. The <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori language</a> (known as <i>Te Reo Māori</i>) is still spoken to some extent by about a fifth of all Māori, representing 3% of the total population. Many New Zealanders regularly use Māori words and expressions, such as "<i><a href="/wiki/Kia_ora" title="Kia ora">kia ora</a></i>", while speaking English. Māori are active in all spheres of New Zealand culture and society, with independent representation in areas such as media, politics and sport.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian">Asian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Asian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 2013 Census, Asian ancestries total was 11.8% of the population, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Zealander" title="Chinese New Zealander">Chinese</a> remained the largest Asian ethnic group in 2013, with 171,411 people while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_New_Zealander" title="Indian New Zealander">Indian</a> was the second-largest Asian ethnic group in 2013, with 155,178 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_New_Zealander" title="Filipino New Zealander">Filipino</a> a distant third with 40,350 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats.govt.nz_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-stats.govt.nz-22">[22]</a></sup> The Asian component actually predates the Pacific component. There had been people of Asian ethnicity living in New Zealand from the early days of European settlement, albeit in very small numbers. During the period of gold rushes later in the nineteenth century the number of Chinese temporary settlers both from China and from Australia and America increased sharply. This was an interlude in many respects, though there was a small population which remained and settled permanently. However, a century later in the 1980s and 1990s the number of people of Asian ethnicities grew rapidly, and they are likely to exceed the Pacific population within the next few years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pacific_Islander">Pacific Islander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pacific Islander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1950s and 1960s, New Zealand encouraged migrants from the South Pacific. The country had a large demand for unskilled labour in the manufacturing sector. As long as this demand continued, migrants were encouraged by the government to come from the South Pacific, and many overstayed. However, when the boom times stopped, some blamed the migrants for the economic downturn affecting the country, and many of those people suffered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dawn_raid#In_New_Zealand" title="Dawn raid">dawn raids</a> from 1974.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MELAA">MELAA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: MELAA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern" title="Middle Eastern">Middle Eastern</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American" title="Latin American">Latin American</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_immigrants_to_New_Zealand" title="African immigrants to New Zealand">African immigrants to New Zealand</a></div>
<p>This component was 1.2% of the total population in the 2013 census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats.govt.nz_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-stats.govt.nz-22">[22]</a></sup> The Latin American ethnic group almost doubled in size between the 2006 and 2013 Censuses, increasing from 6,654 people to 13,182. A more recent component comprises refugees and other settlers from Africa and the Middle East, most recently from Somalia. While there had been previous settlers from the Middle East, such as Syrians, people from Equatorial Africa have been very few in the past.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".27Other.27_ethnic_group">'Other' ethnic group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 'Other' ethnic group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, 67,752 people or 1.7% self-identified with one or more ethnicities <b>other than</b> European, Māori, Pacific, Asian, Middle Eastern, Latin American, and African. The vast majority, 65,973 people, identified as ‘New Zealander’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats.govt.nz_22-6"><a href="#cite_note-stats.govt.nz-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_personification">National personification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: National personification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3872" data-file-width="2592" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG/200px-Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG/300px-Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG/400px-Palmerston_NZ_Boer_War_Memorial.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/National_personification" title="National personification">national personification</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphism</a> of a nation or its people; it can appear in both <a href="/wiki/Editorial_cartoon" title="Editorial cartoon">editorial cartoons</a> and propaganda.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Zealandia_(personification)" title="Zealandia (personification)">Zealandia</a> is a <a href="/wiki/National_personification" title="National personification">national personification</a> of New Zealand. In her stereotypical form, Zealandia appears as a woman of European descent who is similar in dress and appearance to <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>, who is said to be the mother of Zealandia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glover_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glover-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Zealandia appeared on <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a>, posters, cartoons, war memorials, and New Zealand government publications most commonly during the first half of the 20th century. Zealandia was a commonly used symbol of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Centennial_Exhibition" title="New Zealand Centennial Exhibition">New Zealand Centennial Exhibition</a>, which was held in <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> in 1939 and 1940. Three large Zealandia statues exist in New Zealand towns or cities; one is in <a href="/wiki/Waimate" title="Waimate">Waimate</a>, one is in <a href="/wiki/Palmerston,_New_Zealand" title="Palmerston, New Zealand">Palmerston</a>, and one in <a href="/wiki/Newton,_New_Zealand#Symonds_Street" title="Newton, New Zealand">Symonds Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>. The first two (in stone) are <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a> memorials and the latter one (in bronze) is a <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Wars" title="New Zealand Wars">New Zealand Wars</a> memorial. Some smaller statues exist in museums and in private hands. The woman who appears on the left side of the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_New_Zealand" title="Coat of arms of New Zealand">coat of arms of New Zealand</a> is Zealandia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glover_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glover-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>Just under half of the population at the 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Census_of_Population_and_Dwellings" title="New Zealand Census of Population and Dwellings">New Zealand Census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013table28_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-2013table28-43">[43]</a></sup> declared an affiliation to Christianity. However, regular <a href="/wiki/Church_attendance" title="Church attendance">church attendance</a> is probably closer to 15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleengagement_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-bibleengagement-44">[44]</a></sup> Before <a href="/wiki/History_of_New_Zealand" title="History of New Zealand">European colonisation</a> the religion of the indigenous Māori population was <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_religion" title="Māori religion">animistic</a>, but the subsequent efforts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missionaries" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Marsden" title="Samuel Marsden">Samuel Marsden</a> resulted in most Māori converting to Christianity.</p>
<p>Religious affiliation has been collected in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Census_of_Population_and_Dwellings" title="New Zealand Census of Population and Dwellings">New Zealand Census of Population and Dwellings</a> since 1851. One of the many complications in interpreting religious affiliation data in New Zealand is the large proportion who object to answering the question, roughly 173,000 in 2013. Most reporting of percentages is based on the total number of responses, rather than the total population.</p>
<p>In the early 20th century New Zealand census data indicates that the vast majority of New Zealanders affiliated with Christianity. The total percentages in the 1921 non-Māori census were 45% Anglicans, 19.9% Presbyterians, 13.6% Catholics, 9.5% Methodists and 11.2% Others. Statistics for Māori were only available from 1936, with 35.8% Anglicans, 19.9% Ratana, 13.9% Catholics, 7.2% Ringatu, 7.1% Methodists, 6.5% Latter Day Saints, 1.3% Methodists and 8.3% Others recorded at this census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZHA70_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NZHA70-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
