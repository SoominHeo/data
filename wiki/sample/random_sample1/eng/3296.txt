<p><b>Newtown</b> is a <a href="/wiki/New_England_town" title="New England town">town</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fairfield_County,_Connecticut" title="Fairfield County, Connecticut">Fairfield County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Danbury" title="Greater Danbury">greater Danbury</a> metropolitan area as well as the New York metropolitan area. Newtown was founded in 1705 and later incorporated in 1711. As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>, its population was 27,560.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FactFinder_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FactFinder-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Newtown,_Connecticut" title="History of Newtown, Connecticut">History of Newtown, Connecticut</a></div>
<p>In 1705, English colonists purchased the Town site from the <a href="/wiki/Potatuck" title="Potatuck">Pohtatuck</a> Indians, a branch of the Pasgussett. It was originally known as <b>Quanneapague</b>. Settled by migrants from <a href="/wiki/Stratford,_Connecticut" title="Stratford, Connecticut">Stratford</a> and incorporated in 1711, Newtown residents had many business and trading ties with the English. It was a stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Tory</a> sentiment during the early <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a>. Late in the war, French General <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Donatien_de_Vimeur,_comte_de_Rochambeau" title="Jean-Baptiste Donatien de Vimeur, comte de Rochambeau">Rochambeau</a> and his troops encamped here in 1781 during their <a href="/wiki/Washington%E2%80%93Rochambeau_Revolutionary_Route" title="Washington–Rochambeau Revolutionary Route">celebrated march</a> on their way to the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">siege of Yorktown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, which ended the Revolution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An important crossroads throughout its early history, the village of Hawleyville briefly emerged as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroad</a> center. The town's population grew to over 4,000 circa 1881. In the following decades, the population dwindled to a low of 2,635 in 1930 before again growing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Local industry has included the manufacture of furniture, tea bags, combs, fire hoses, folding boxes, buttons, and hats, as well as farming, and mica and feldspar mining. The game of "<a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a>" was developed here by James Brunot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the period of highway development and suburbanization following World War II, the town has developed as a suburb of Danbury, with many people also commuting to Norwalk, Stamford, and Bridgeport.</p>
<p>In November 1986, Helle Crafts was killed by her husband Richard Crafts in the infamous <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Helle_Crafts" title="Murder of Helle Crafts">"Woodchipper Murder"</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook_Elementary_School_shooting" title="Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting">Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting</a></div>
<p>On December 14, 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Adam Lanza shot his mother at home and then went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook_Elementary_School" title="Sandy Hook Elementary School">Sandy Hook Elementary School</a> where he killed 26 people (20 children and 6 faculty and staff) before killing himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ID_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ID-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northeastern border of the town is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_border" title="Natural border">natural border</a> that follows the <a href="/wiki/Housatonic_River" title="Housatonic River">Housatonic River</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the town has a total area of 59.1 square miles (153 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 57.8 square miles (150 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 1.3 square miles (3.4 km<sup>2</sup>), or 2.22%, is water. Newtown is located in northern <a href="/wiki/Fairfield_County,_Connecticut" title="Fairfield County, Connecticut">Fairfield County</a>, about 45 miles (72 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a> and about 42 miles (68 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The state's fifth largest town in area, it is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Bethel,_Connecticut" title="Bethel, Connecticut">Bethel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridgewater,_Connecticut" title="Bridgewater, Connecticut">Bridgewater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brookfield,_Connecticut" title="Brookfield, Connecticut">Brookfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Easton,_Connecticut" title="Easton, Connecticut">Easton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monroe,_Connecticut" title="Monroe, Connecticut">Monroe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_Connecticut" title="Oxford, Connecticut">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redding,_Connecticut" title="Redding, Connecticut">Redding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southbury,_Connecticut" title="Southbury, Connecticut">Southbury</a>.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> of 2000, there were 25,031 people, 8,325 households, and 6,776 families residing in the town. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 433.4 people per square mile (167.3/km²). There were 8,601 housing units at an average density of 148.9 per square mile (57.5/km²). The racial makeup of the town was 95.14% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 1.75% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">Black</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 0.14% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 1.40% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.04% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.64% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.89% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 2.36% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 8,325 households out of which 44.7% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 73.3% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 5.8% had a female householder with no husband present, and 18.6% were non-families. 14.8% of all households were made up of individuals and 5.8% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.90 and the average family size was 3.24.</p>
<p>In the town the population was spread out with 29.3% under the age of 18, 4.4% from 18 to 24, 32.5% from 25 to 44, 25.1% from 45 to 64, and 8.7% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 38 years. For every 100 females there were 104.9 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 103.5 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the town was $90,193, and the median income for a family was $99,192 (these figures had risen to $101,937 and $119,175 respectively as of a 2007 estimate). Males had a median income of $68,965 versus $42,217 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the town was $37,786. About 2.2% of families and 3.1% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 3.0% of those under age 18 and 3.9% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_recreation">Parks and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Parks and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town of Newtown offers many programs for area residents. Numerous parks and fields offer playgrounds, swimming, tennis, softball, baseball, volleyball, lacrosse, soccer, as well as a nature center and trails. Prominent Newtown parks include Treadwell Park, Dickinson Park, and <a href="/wiki/Collis_P._Huntington_State_Park" title="Collis P. Huntington State Park">Collis P. Huntington State Park</a>. Treadwell Park, named after former selectman Timothy Treadwell, contains recreation facilities and the town pool. Dickinson park used to contain a swimming pool, which was a large asphalt-lined bowl-shaped depression surrounded by a grass "beach". It was a uniquely safe design for children because there was no "deep end"; however, it lacked a formal filtration system and required attendants to periodically row out and manually add chlorine to the water. The asphalt was removed and the pond pool filled with earth in 2006.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Connecticut politics, the town of Newtown is required to have both a Democratic and a Republican Town Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Town Committee members vote on which candidates to endorse for public elections. Elected to a two-year term, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Selectmen" title="Board of Selectmen">Board of Selectmen</a> supervise the administration of the affairs of the town, except those matters which by the General Statute or Town Charter are exclusively committed to the Board of Education or other departments. They are led by a First Selectman, who is the Chief Executive and Administrative Officer of the town. The Board of Selectmen with the assistance of the departments and boards and commission prepares the annual budget in February. The Legislative Council of 12 members (elected to the same two year terms) acts as the Board of Finance and passes ordinances. The Council recommends the annual education and general government budget to the towns people. The old fashion town meeting and referendum are used to pass the budget. These procedures are set forth in the Town Charter adopted and reviewed by the citizens.</p>
<p>The Borough of Newtown occupies about 1,252 acres (5.07 km<sup>2</sup>) (or roughly two square miles) in the central part of town. Incorporated in 1824 by an act of the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_General_Assembly" title="Connecticut General Assembly">Connecticut General Assembly</a>, it is one of only nine boroughs in the state. The borough adopted zoning for the town center long before the rest of the community. The lot sizes are smaller than the minimum 1-acre (4,000 m<sup>2</sup>) lots of the rest of the community. The borough also has running public water provided by a small town water company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Much of the borough is sewered, whereas most of the rest of the town has wells and septic systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement">Law enforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Law enforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Newtown Police Department has existed since 1970. It is located on 3 Main Street near Rams Pasture, the park used for town celebrations. The police chief is James Viadero and the commander of the department is Joe Rios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newtownpd1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-newtownpd1-10">[10]</a></sup> The department has 45 sworn officers in uniform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newtownpd2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-newtownpd2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Newtown has a number of local landmarks. The flagpole, first erected in 1876, now stands in the center of Main Street. The <i>Newtown Bee</i>, the local weekly newspaper, has been serving the area since 1877, and has been owned and operated by the Smith family since 1881. Across from the flagpole is Newtown Meeting House, which served as the town's <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregational church</a> for many years. The rooster <a href="/wiki/Weather_vane" title="Weather vane">weather vane</a> (a town symbol), located atop the meeting house, is said to have been used as a target by French soldiers encamped here in 1781 during the Revolutionary War.</p>
<p>Hawley School is a landmark constructed in the 1920s. It has been used as a whole-town school, a high school, and an elementary school. It is currently used as an elementary school. Though it has served many different school functions, its original section has remained much the same. Two additions have been added.</p>
<p>Newtown is the site of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fairfield_Hills_State_Hospital&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fairfield Hills State Hospital (page does not exist)">Fairfield Hills State Hospital</a>, an asylum constructed in the 1930s and closed in 1995. The hospital was used as the set of the juvenile facility in the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sleepers_(film)" title="Sleepers (film)">Sleepers</a></i> in 1995.</p>
<p>In the 21st century, Newtown purchased the property and, as of 2007, was considering a controversial plan for redevelopment. In 2008, the NYA (Newtown Youth Academy) began to operate there; extracurricular amenities include a fitness section, basketball courts, and a turf field.</p>

<p>Constructed in 1930 by a private benefactress for the community, the Town Hall is used for public-private purposes. Offices for the town are located there. The facility also has available for private rental the Alexandria Room for weddings, parties and recitals; and smaller meeting rooms that can be reserved by community groups. A gymnasium is used for community sports events, as well as private parties, and art or craft shows.</p>
<p>The Edmond Town hall is notable for its cinema. The theater shows popular films shortly after they leave mainstream theaters. It is the only $3 film theater in Connecticut. It is a popular spot for middle school and high school students. The "Live at the Edmond Town Hall" concert series was created by Newtown resident Hayden Bates in 2009. All proceeds are put towards aiding the Edmond's theater upgrades. Headliners have included The Low Anthem, The Bill Frisell and Sam Amidon Duo, Brown Bird and Phosphorescent.</p>
<p>The Board of Managers of the Hall is composed of six members serving six-year terms. At each regular Town Election, two members are elected, who must represent different political parties. According to Town Charter, the Board "shall have the exclusive care and maintenance of Edmond Town Hall and all grounds and buildings appurtenant thereto, together with all powers and duties prescribed for said Board by Special Act No. 98 of the 1931 session by which it was created, as amended by Special Act No. 517 of the 1953 session".</p>
<p>The architect was Philip Sutherland, who also designed Cyrenius H. Booth Library. The Town Hall was constructed for the community by a local benefactress Mary Elizabeth Hawley and dedicated in 1930. The building was named for Miss Hawley’s maternal great-grandfather Judge William Edmond.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyrenius_H._Booth_Library">Cyrenius H. Booth Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cyrenius H. Booth Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newtown's public library was opened December 17, 1932, with a capacity for 25,000 volumes. The library is a posthumous gift of Mary Elizabeth Hawley. She named it for her maternal grandfather, a doctor in town from 1820 until his death in 1871. Hawley's gift paid for construction of the building, and an endowment, a trust fund of about $250,000. As a result, the town did not have to provide any financial support to the library until the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chbhist_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-chbhist-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Designed by Philip Sutherland, the building was considered one of the most modern libraries of its time, with several innovative features. The building was fireproof, had cork floors and acoustic ceiling tiles to deaden sound, and had a built-in humidifying unit and a centralized vacuum cleaner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chbhist_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-chbhist-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1998 an addition to the rear of the building was completed and officially opened. The expansion doubled the available floor space. It provides areas for meetings and displays of art and local historical artifacts from the library's large collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chbhist_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-chbhist-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newtown,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Newtown_Public_Schools" title="Newtown Public Schools">Newtown Public Schools</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Newtown_Public_Schools" title="Newtown Public Schools">Newtown Public Schools</a> district operates four elementary schools (Hawley Elementary School, Head O'Meadow Elementary School, Middle Gate Elementary School, and Sandy Hook Elementary School) that serve grades K-4, Reed Intermediate School serving grades 5–6, Newtown Middle School serving grades 7–8, and <a href="/wiki/Newtown_High_School_(Connecticut)" title="Newtown High School (Connecticut)">Newtown High School</a> serving grades 9–12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newtown also has several private and parochial schools, including St. Rose of Lima Roman Catholic School, the Fraser-Woods Montessori School, and the Housatonic Valley Waldorf School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2010, six educators made the top salary list in Newtown, Connecticut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
