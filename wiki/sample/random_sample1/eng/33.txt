<p><b>1. Fußball-Club Kaiserslautern e. V.</b>, also known as <b>1. FCK</b>, <b>FCK</b> or simply <b>1. FC Kaiserslautern</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ʔɛf t͡seː kaɪ̯zɐsˈlaʊ̯tɐn]</a></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Germany" title="Football in Germany">German football club</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Kaiserslautern" title="Kaiserslautern">Kaiserslautern</a>, Rhineland-Palatinate. On 2 June 1900, Germania 1896 and FG Kaiserslautern merged to create FC 1900. In 1909, the club went on to join FC Palatia (founded in 1901) and FC Bavaria (founded in 1902) to form FV 1900 Kaiserslautern. In 1929, they merged with SV Phönix to become FV Phönix-Kaiserslautern before finally taking on their current name three years later.</p>
<p>Kaiserslautern have won four <a href="/wiki/List_of_German_football_champions" title="List of German football champions">German championships</a>, two <a href="/wiki/DFB-Pokal" title="DFB-Pokal">DFB-Pokals</a>, and one <a href="/wiki/DFL-Supercup" title="DFL-Supercup">DFL-Supercup</a>. Since 1920, Kaiserslautern's stadium is the <a href="/wiki/Fritz-Walter-Stadion" title="Fritz-Walter-Stadion">Fritz-Walter-Stadion</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_to_World_War_II">Early years to World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years to World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two of the clubs predecessors, Bavaria and FC 1900 Kaiserslautern, were part of the <a href="/wiki/Westkreis-Liga" title="Westkreis-Liga">Westkreis-Liga</a> (I) when this league was formed in 1908, with the latter winning the first league. From 1909 through 1918, the new FV Kaiserslautern performed well, finishing runners-up in 1910 and 1912. The team reached tier-one in the new <a href="/wiki/Kreisliga_Saar" title="Kreisliga Saar">Kreisliga Saar</a> in 1919, the <a href="/wiki/Kreisliga_Pfalz" title="Kreisliga Pfalz">Kreisliga Pfalz</a> in 1920 and the <a href="/wiki/Bezirksliga_Rhein-Saar" title="Bezirksliga Rhein-Saar">Bezirksliga Rhein-Saar</a> in 1931 and spent the rest of the 1930s bouncing up and down between the Bezirksliga and the upper level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauliga_S%C3%BCdwest" title="Gauliga Südwest">Gauliga Südwest</a>, one of sixteen top flight divisions formed in the re-organization of German football under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyll_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hyll-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The club's performance was unremarkable in the years leading up to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, but improved after 1939. They captured the Gauliga Südwest/Staffel Saarpfalz title, but lost the overall division title to Staffel Mainhessen winners <a href="/wiki/Kickers_Offenbach" title="Kickers Offenbach">Kickers Offenbach</a>. In the 1941–42 season the Gauliga Südwest was split into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauliga_Hessen-Nassau" title="Gauliga Hessen-Nassau">Gauliga Hessen-Nassau</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauliga_Westmark" title="Gauliga Westmark">Gauliga Westmark</a>, and Kaiserslautern took the Westmark title, going on to play for the first time in the national final rounds. They were decisively put out 3–9 by eventual champions <a href="/wiki/FC_Schalke_04" title="FC Schalke 04">Schalke 04</a>, the dominant side in this era of German football.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyll_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hyll-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The performance of the team slipped and they finished last in their division in 1944. The following year saw the collapse of league play in this part of Germany as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> crumbled under the advance of Allied armies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyll_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hyll-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_play">Postwar play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Postwar play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the war, Southwestern Germany was part of the occupation zone held by the French. Teams there were organized into northern and southern divisions and played to determine which of them would join the new Oberliga being put together. French authorities were slow to loosen their control over play in their zones of occupation – and in the <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a> in particular – Teams in the French areas took longer to join the re-established German national league than teams for other parts of the country. 1. FC Kaiserslautern resumed play in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oberliga_S%C3%BCdwest_(1945-63)" title="Oberliga Südwest (1945-63)">Oberliga Südwest</a> in 1945 and finished the season just one point behind <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Saarbr%C3%BCcken" title="1. FC Saarbrücken">1. FC Saarbrücken</a>. The next season, they easily won the Gruppe Nord in 1947 due in large part due to the play of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fritz_Walter_(1920%E2%80%932002)" title="Fritz Walter (1920–2002)">Fritz Walter</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Ottmar_Walter" title="Ottmar Walter">Ottmar</a> – the duo scored 46 goals between them, more than any other entire team.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Success_in_the_1950s_and_entry_to_the_Bundesliga">Success in the 1950s and entry to the Bundesliga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Success in the 1950s and entry to the Bundesliga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This marked the beginning of the club's dominance of the Oberliga Südwest as they went on to capture the division title eleven times over the next twelve seasons. FCK advanced to Germany's first post-war national final in 1948, but lost 1–2 to <a href="/wiki/1._FC_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="1. FC Nürnberg">1. FC Nürnberg</a>.</p>
<p>Kaiserslautern became a presence on the national scene through the early 1950s, capturing their first German championship in 1951 with a 2–1 victory of their own, this time over <a href="/wiki/SC_Preu%C3%9Fen_M%C3%BCnster" title="SC Preußen Münster">Preußen Münster</a>. They won a second title in 1953, followed by two losing final appearances in 1954 and 1955. The club also sent five players to the national side for the <a href="/wiki/1954_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1954 FIFA World Cup">1954 FIFA World Cup</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Germany_national_football_team" title="Germany national football team">West Germany</a> won in what became popularly known as "<a href="/wiki/The_Miracle_of_Bern" title="The Miracle of Bern">The Miracle of Bern</a>".</p>
<p>Kaiserslautern's performance fell off late in the decade and into the early 1960s, highlighted only by an advance to the 1961 <a href="/wiki/DFB-Pokal" title="DFB-Pokal">DFB-Pokal</a> final, where they lost 0–2 to <a href="/wiki/SV_Werder_Bremen" title="SV Werder Bremen">Werder Bremen</a>. The side recovered its form in time to again win their division on the eve of the formation in 1963 of the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga" title="Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a>, Germany's new professional football league. This secured them 1 of 16 places in the new top flight circuit. However, the club's next honours would be some time in coming: they made failed German Cup final appearances in 1972, 1976, and 1981 before finally winning in the Cup in 1990. They followed up the next season with their first Bundesliga championship.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_from_the_top_flight">Fall from the top flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fall from the top flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1. FCK won a second German Cup in 1996, but that victory was soured since the team was relegated to <a href="/wiki/2._Bundesliga" title="2. Bundesliga">2. Bundesliga</a> with a 16th-place finish just one week before the Cup final. At the time, Kaiserslautern was one of only 4 of the original 16 teams that had played in each Bundesliga season since the inception of the league, having never been relegated. This group included Kaiserslautern, <a href="/wiki/Eintracht_Frankfurt" title="Eintracht Frankfurt">Eintracht Frankfurt</a> (who went down in the same season), <a href="/wiki/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln" title="1. FC Köln">1. FC Köln</a> (down in 1998), and "the Dinosaur", unrelegated <a href="/wiki/Hamburger_SV" title="Hamburger SV">Hamburger SV</a>.</p>
<p>The <i>Red Devils</i> came storming back in 1998 with an accomplishment unique in Bundesliga history by winning re-promotion to the first division from the 2. Bundesliga, and immediately going on to win the national championship under famous coach <a href="/wiki/Otto_Rehhagel" title="Otto Rehhagel">Otto Rehhagel</a>. They also played in <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_UEFA_Champions_League" title="1998–99 UEFA Champions League">1998–99 UEFA Champions League</a>, where they were grouped with <a href="/wiki/PSV_Eindhoven" title="PSV Eindhoven">PSV</a>, <a href="/wiki/S.L._Benfica" title="S.L. Benfica">Benfica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helsingin_Jalkapalloklubi" title="Helsingin Jalkapalloklubi">HJK Helsinki</a>.</p>
<p>The club, however, found itself in serious trouble soon after. The construction work necessary for the Fritz-Walter-Stadium to be a <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006 World Cup</a> venue, incompetent management and financial misdeeds resulted in a heavy debt load and the fielding of incapable sides built out of mercenary players. In 2002, Kaiserslautern found itself on the brink of bankruptcy and at the centre of controversy being played out publicly. The club's management – <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Friedrich" title="Jürgen Friedrich">Jürgen Friedrich</a>, Robert Wieschemann and Gerhard Herzog – were forced out. A new team president, Rene C. Jäggi, sold the debt-ridden Fritz-Walter-Stadion to an entity owned by the Land Rheinland-Pfalz and the city of Kaiserslautern, thus saving the club from financial disaster, while a new coach, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erik_Gerets" title="Erik Gerets">Erik Gerets</a>, led a run after the winter break that moved the footballers out of last place and saved them from relegation.</p>
<p>The club started the 2003–04 season under the burden of a three-point penalty imposed by the <a href="/wiki/German_Football_Association" title="German Football Association">German Football Association</a> for its financial misdeeds. After a faltering start to the season, Gerets was fired and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Jara" title="Kurt Jara">Kurt Jara</a>. Jara was unpopular with the FCK faithful for his defensive football philosophy, but with him at the helm, the club had a safe season. Jara, however, quit the position before the season ended, citing irreconcilable differences with club management.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005.E2.80.93present">2005–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2005–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Henke" title="Michael Henke">Michael Henke</a>, who served as long-time assistant to Germany's most successful coach <a href="/wiki/Ottmar_Hitzfeld" title="Ottmar Hitzfeld">Ottmar Hitzfeld</a>, became coach. FCK was initially successful, but then suffered a string of reverses and crashed to the bottom of the table. Henke was fired, and FCK alumnus <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Wolf" title="Wolfgang Wolf">Wolfgang Wolf</a> took up the trainer's role. Wolf brought in many young, home-grown players, but despite winning over fans and experts alike, the 2005–06 season ended in failure as FCK was once again relegated to the 2. Bundesliga after a nine-year stay at the top flight. They finished the 2006–07 season in sixth place in the 2. Bundesliga, seven points out of the promotion places.</p>
<p>On 20 May 2007, the club announced the Norwegian manager <a href="/wiki/Kjetil_Rekdal" title="Kjetil Rekdal">Kjetil Rekdal</a>, formerly with Belgian side <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lierse_SK" title="Lierse SK">Lierse</a>, as their new head coach. Rekdal took over the reins on 1 July. Due to very bad results (the club being in 16th place in the standings with only three wins in 19 games), Rekdal was sacked and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Milan_%C5%A0a%C5%A1i%C4%87" title="Milan Šašić">Milan Šašić</a> in February 2008. In April 2008, the club hired <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Kuntz" title="Stefan Kuntz">Stefan Kuntz</a> as chairman, and with new leadership at the helm, managed to save themselves from relegation to the new <a href="/wiki/3._Liga" title="3. Liga">3. Liga</a> with a win over already promoted 1. FC Köln on the final day of the 2007–08 season.</p>

<p>Šašić lasted almost the entire <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_2._Bundesliga" title="2008–09 2. Bundesliga">2008–09 season</a> but was dismissed on 4 May 2009 after run of poor results in the second half of the season, and three days after a 1–5 defeat by <a href="/wiki/F.C._Hansa_Rostock" title="F.C. Hansa Rostock">Hansa Rostock</a>. Alois Schwartz was named interim coach and he managed the club to a seventh-place finish on the season. The club eventually hired <a href="/wiki/Marco_Kurz" title="Marco Kurz">Marco Kurz</a> as head coach.</p>
<p>Under Kurz, the club secured promotion to the 1. Bundesliga on 25 April 2010 after four years in the second league. At the start of the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Bundesliga" title="2010–11 Bundesliga">2010–11 season</a>, newly promoted 1. FCK had a promising two-straight wins, including a 2–0 victory over the previous year's Bundesliga champions, <a href="/wiki/FC_Bayern_Munich" title="FC Bayern Munich">Bayern Munich</a>. However, after a hard fought 2–1 defeat at <a href="/wiki/1._FSV_Mainz_05" title="1. FSV Mainz 05">Mainz 05</a> and a 5–0 drubbing at eventual season champions, <a href="/wiki/Borussia_Dortmund" title="Borussia Dortmund">Borussia Dortmund</a>, the club began to struggle and fell back to just ahead of the relegation zone. The club then had a poor start to the second half of the season – dropping into the relegation zone for several weeks – but managed to coalesce and eventually earned seven victories in their last ten matches, recording only two defeats and a single draw. They ended this run with four straight victories to finish the season at the seventh place. The following season, <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_Bundesliga" title="2011–12 Bundesliga">2011–12</a>, the club finished in the bottom 18th place and after only two seasons in the top flight, were relegated to the 2. Bundesliga, where they remain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reserve_team">Reserve team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reserve team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Kaiserslautern_II" title="1. FC Kaiserslautern II">1. FC Kaiserslautern II</a></div>
<p>The club's reserve team, <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Kaiserslautern_II" title="1. FC Kaiserslautern II">1. FC Kaiserslautern II</a>, played as 1. FC Kaiserslautern Amateure until 2005. It made a first appearance in the tier three <a href="/wiki/Amateurliga_S%C3%BCdwest" title="Amateurliga Südwest">Amateurliga Südwest</a> in 1957. It won a league championship in 1960 and 1968 but was not entitled to promotion to professional level. In 1978, when the <a href="/wiki/Oberliga_Rheinland-Pfalz/Saar" title="Oberliga Rheinland-Pfalz/Saar">Oberliga Südwest</a> was introduced the team qualified for this new league which it would belong to, with the exception of the 1982–83 season, until 1992. It won promotion back to the Oberliga in 1994 and became a yo-yo team between this league and the <a href="/wiki/Regionalliga" title="Regionalliga">Regionalliga</a> above, a league newly introduced in 1994. The team was relegated from the latter in 1996, 2000, 2004 and 2007 but each time won promotion back to the league. Since 2012, it has belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Regionalliga_S%C3%BCdwest" title="Regionalliga Südwest">Regionalliga Südwest</a>.</p>
<p>The team has also won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_West_Cup" title="South West Cup">South West Cup</a> on three occasions, in 1979, 1997 and 2008. Through this competition, 1. FC Kaiserslautern II qualified for the first round of the DFB-Pokal on those three occasions, reaching the second round twice and being drawn against their own first team in 1997–98 where they lost 0–5.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stadium">Stadium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Stadium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fritz_Walter_Stadion" title="Fritz Walter Stadion">Fritz Walter Stadion</a></div>

<p>FCK plays its home fixtures in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fritz_Walter_Stadion" title="Fritz Walter Stadion">Fritz Walter Stadion</a> first built in 1920. The stadium and the adjacent street are named for the player who brought the club to prominence after the war. The facility is built on the Betzenberg, literally "Mount Betze", a steep sandstone hill.</p>
<p>The stadium has a capacity of 49,780 and was a <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006 World Cup</a> venue, hosting four preliminary round and one group of 16 round matches. The facility underwent a major refurbishment for the tournament with addition of new grandstands and a roof.</p>

<p>Kaiserslautern's Fritz-Walter-Stadion has long been a feared away venue given the rabid ferocity of Kaiserslautern fans: the most faithful of these supporters are located in the stadium's "Westkurve" (Westside, literally "West Curve", since the stands used to be shaped in a semicircle behind the goals). Most famously, <a href="/wiki/FC_Bayern_Munich" title="FC Bayern Munich">Bayern Munich</a> once lost a match here in a charged atmosphere by a score of 7–4 after leading 4–1 at the 58th minute.</p>
<p>The club has friendly ties to <a href="/wiki/TSV_1860_Munich" title="TSV 1860 Munich">1860 Munich</a> and <a href="/wiki/SV_Werder_Bremen" title="SV Werder Bremen">Werder Bremen</a> and are bitter rivals of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waldhof_Mannheim" title="Waldhof Mannheim">Waldhof Mannheim</a> and Bayern Munich. They also have lesser local rivalries with <a href="/wiki/Eintracht_Frankfurt" title="Eintracht Frankfurt">Eintracht Frankfurt</a> and, more recently, with <a href="/wiki/1._FSV_Mainz_05" title="1. FSV Mainz 05">Mainz 05</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruher_SC" title="Karlsruher SC">Karlsruher SC</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basketball">Basketball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Basketball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The basketball department was founded in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The seniors team played in the second German Basketball league from 2002 until 2007. In the 2014–15 season, both the male and female senior teams play in the fourth division.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boxing">Boxing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Boxing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The boxing department exists since the times of FV Kaiserslautern. Most prominent athletes are <a href="/wiki/Silver_medal" title="Silver medal">Silver medalist</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">1964 Summer Olympics</a> <a href="/wiki/Emil_Schulz" title="Emil Schulz">Emil Schulz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronze_medal" title="Bronze medal">Bronze medalist</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Boxing_at_the_1988_Summer_Olympics" title="Boxing at the 1988 Summer Olympics">1988 Summer Olympics</a> <a href="/wiki/Reiner_Gies" title="Reiner Gies">Reiner Gies</a> and, before his professional career, later European heavyweight champion <a href="/wiki/Karl_Mildenberger" title="Karl Mildenberger">Karl Mildenberger</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_departments">Former departments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Former departments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wheelchair_basketball">Wheelchair basketball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_Kaiserslautern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Wheelchair basketball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair_basketball" title="Wheelchair basketball">wheelchair basketball</a> team <a href="/wiki/FCK_Rolling_Devils" title="FCK Rolling Devils">FCK Rolling Devils</a> was founded in 2009 as a part of the club's basketball department and turned into a separate department in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since 2014, the Rolling Devils play in the <a href="/wiki/Rollstuhlbasketball-Bundesliga" title="Rollstuhlbasketball-Bundesliga">1st German Wheelchair Basketball Federal League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In July 2015, the outsourcing of Rolling Devils into an independent club with 1.FC Kaiserslautern as name sponsor took place and the FCK department was suspended at the annual meeting of 1.FC Kaiserslautern in December 2015.</p>
