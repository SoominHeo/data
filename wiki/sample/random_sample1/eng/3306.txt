<p>The <b>Chelydridae</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a> that has seven extinct and two extant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genera" title="Genera">genera</a>. The extant genera are the snapping turtles <i><a href="/wiki/Chelydra" title="Chelydra">Chelydra</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Macrochelys" title="Macrochelys">Macrochelys</a></i>. Both are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>. The extinct genera are <i><a href="/wiki/Acherontemys" title="Acherontemys">Acherontemys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelydrops" title="Chelydrops">Chelydrops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelydropsis" title="Chelydropsis">Chelydropsis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emarginachelys" title="Emarginachelys">Emarginachelys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macrocephalochelys" title="Macrocephalochelys">Macrocephalochelys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planiplastron" title="Planiplastron">Planiplastron</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Protochelydra" title="Protochelydra">Protochelydra</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_history">Fossil history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelydridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fossil history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chelydridae have a long <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> history, with <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a> species reported from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, all over Asia and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, far outside their present range. The earliest described chelydrid is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emarginachelys_cretacea" title="Emarginachelys cretacea">Emarginachelys cretacea</a></i>, known from well-preserved fossils from the <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> stage of the Late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PB_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-PB-1">[1]</a></sup> Another well-preserved fossil chelydrid is the Late <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protochelydra_zangerli" title="Protochelydra zangerli">Protochelydra zangerli</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> of <i>P. zangerli</i> is higher domed than that of the recent <i>Chelydra</i>, a trait conjectured to be associated with the coexistence of large, chelonivorous (i.e., turtle-eating) <a href="/wiki/Crocodilia" title="Crocodilia">crocodilians</a>. Another genus, <i>Chelydropsis</i>, contains several well-known <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> chelydrid <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> that existed from the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
