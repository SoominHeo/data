<p><b>Neunggasa</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Korea" title="Buddhism in Korea">Korean Buddhist</a> temple located in Yeongnam-myeon township, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goheung" title="Goheung">Goheung-gun County</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Jeolla" title="South Jeolla">Jeollanam-do</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.</p>
<p>This small temple complex rests at the base of Palyeong Mountain (팔영산, 八影山) and Recreational Forest area, also known as Eight Peak Mountain, in Goheung county.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neunggasa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neunggasa was established in 419 and originally called Bohyeonsa by Monk Adohwasang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monument1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monument1-1">[1]</a></sup> The temple was destroyed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_(1592%E2%80%931598)" title="Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)">Japanese Invasion in 1592</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monument3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-monument3-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1644, the temple was rebuilt as Neunggasa by Monk Jeonghyeon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monument3_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-monument3-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temple_Treasures">Temple Treasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neunggasa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Temple Treasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are five Tangible Cultural Properties and Treasures found at the Neungga Temple complex.</p>

<p><b>Bronze Bell of Neunggasa.</b></p>
<p>This large bronze bell weighs 900 kg/1984 lb and was cast in 1698 during <a href="/wiki/Sukjong_of_Joseon" title="Sukjong of Joseon">King Sukjong</a>'s reign. Two dragons holding beads, called <a href="/wiki/Cintamani" title="Cintamani">Yeouiju</a>, are engraved on the hook suspending the bell (Yongnyu).</p>
<p>On the upper part of the bell are engraved 'Beomja' (an ancient Indian letter in Sanskrit), found along with standing Bodhisattvas.</p>
<p>The lower part of bell is decorated with Dangchomun, a pattern of flowers and long spreading vines. A Dangchomun symbolizes longevity, everlastingness, and the carrying on of a family line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dangchomun_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dangchomun-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design of <a href="/wiki/I_Ching#Trigrams" title="I Ching">eight divination symbols</a> in the center of the bell is a unique design which has never been found on any other of the temple bells from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bell_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bell-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Neunggasasajeokbi (Neungga Temple monument).</b></p>
<p>This monument was erected in 1690, by Monk Mokdeok to record and commemorate the activities of Monk Adohwasang.</p>
<p>Monk Adohwasang established Neunggasa in 419, the reign under <a href="/wiki/Nulji_of_Silla" title="Nulji of Silla">King Nulji</a> (417-458), of the <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> (57 BCE - 935 CE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monument1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Monument1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monument2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monument2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The turtle shaped base of the monument supports an oblong natural stone pedestal on which an epitaph is engraved. The epitaph states that Neungga Temple was originally called Bohyeon Temple before being rebuilt as Neunggasa in 1644.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monument3_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-monument3-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The monument is topped off with an ornately carved capstone.</p>

<p><b>The Wooden Four-Deva Statues of Neunggasa.</b></p>
<p>Cheongwangmun or <a href="/wiki/Four_Heavenly_Kings" title="Four Heavenly Kings">Four Guardians</a> gate, traditionally the second gate into the temple, enshrines the Four Heavenly Kings. The Four Heavenly Kings vow to protect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dharma_(Buddhism)#The_Buddha.27s_teachings" title="Dharma (Buddhism)">Dharma Truths</a> and all who worship in the temple.</p>
<p>Their ferocious looks reflect their duty to force unruly spirits into submission, or at least focus the minds of temple visitors.</p>
<p>Due to inscriptions found on the ridge beam of gate the statues of the Four Heavenly Kings found here are believed to have been created in 1666.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tangible_Cultural_Property_.23264">Tangible Cultural Property #264</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neunggasa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tangible Cultural Property #264">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG"><img alt="South Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa 5875-07 stupas.JPG" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="766" data-file-width="1280" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG/220px-South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG/330px-South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG/440px-South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5875-07_stupas.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><b>Neunggasa Tomb of Chugyedang and Sayeongdang.</b></p>
<p>Monks Chugyedang and Sayeongdang were teaching monks, revered by their students, whose ashes are interred in the Neunggasa Tomb of Chugyedang and Sayeongdang.</p>
<p>Each of the tombs differs significantly from the other. The tomb of Chugyedang, on the right, has the appearance of a stone lantern where the tomb of Sayeongdang, to the left, has the appearance of a bronze temple bell.</p>
<p>Both tombs are adorned with engravings of lotus blossoms, a very important symbol in Buddhism, exemplifying the concepts of complete purification of the body, mind and speech, and the blossoming of wholesome acts .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lotus_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lotus-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chugyedang was active during the middle of the 17th century and Sayeongdang active during the late 17th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treasure_.231307">Treasure #1307</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neunggasa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Treasure #1307">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Neunggasadaeungjeon (Daeng Hall of Neunggasa Temple).</b></p>
<p>The Hall of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakyamuni" title="Sakyamuni">Sakyamuni</a> in Neugasa Temple, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daeungjeon" title="Daeungjeon">Daeungjeon</a>, from the Late Joseon Period was rebuilt in the mid 18th century, as a result of being destroyed during the Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monument3_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-monument3-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neunggasadaeungjeon covers an area of 190 square meters/2045 square feet, comprising five bays, each about 180 cm/5.9 feet wide.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG"><img alt="South Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa 5850-07 Daeungjeon.JPG" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="854" data-file-width="1280" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG/200px-South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG/300px-South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG/400px-South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:South_Korea-Goheunggun-Neunggasa_5850-07_Daeungjeon.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other abundantly bracketed buildings, this structure also has a roof-supporting bracket system with three outward arms (oechulmok) and four inward arms (naechulmok). Bracket arms carved in relief on the wall, between the bracket system, is rarely found in temple structures.</p>
<p>Carvings on the column-top blocks (anchogong), and the exquisitely decorated interior employing the lotus-bud designs, are similar to those of the Halls of Sakyamuni in Bulgapsa (Buddhist) temple in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeonggwang" title="Yeonggwang">Yeonggwang</a> and Gaeamsa (Buddhist temple) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buan" title="Buan">Buan</a>. Both of these halls are designated as national treasures.</p>
<p>Neunggasadaeungjeon has both academical and historical significance, rendering valuable data on the temple construction methods employed during the mid/late Joseon Dynasty in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeolla" title="Jeolla">Jeolla</a> Province(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daeungjeon_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daeungjeon-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
