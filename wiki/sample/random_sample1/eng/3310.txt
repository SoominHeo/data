<p>The <b>marsh deer</b> (<i>Blastocerus dichotomus</i>) is the largest <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> reaching a length of 2 m (6.6 ft) and a shoulder height of 1.2 m (3.9 ft). It is found in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>. Formerly found in much of tropical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> South America, it ranged east of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, south from the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a>, west of the Brazilian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_rainforest" title="Atlantic rainforest">Atlantic rainforest</a> and north of the Argentinian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pampa" title="Pampa">Pampa</a>. Today it is largely reduced to isolated populations at marsh and lagoon zones in the <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_River" title="Paraná River">Paraná</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay_River" title="Paraguay River">Paraguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Araguaia_River" title="Araguaia River">Araguaia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guapor%C3%A9_River" title="Guaporé River">Guapore</a> river basins. Small populations also occur in the southern Amazon, including Peru where protected in <a href="/wiki/Bahuaja-Sonene_National_Park" title="Bahuaja-Sonene National Park">Bahuaja-Sonene National Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornback_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thornback-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cabrera_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cabrera-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas1997_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas1997-4">[4]</a></sup> It is listed as a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable species</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> and on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CITES_Appendix_I" title="CITES Appendix I">CITES Appendix I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The latter half of its scientific name refers to the forked antlers. Marsh deer resemble the North American <a href="/wiki/Mule_deer" title="Mule deer">mule deer</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blacktail_deer" title="Blacktail deer">blacktail deer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The marsh deer lives only in marsh areas, notably the <a href="/wiki/Pantanal" title="Pantanal">Pantanal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Chaco</a>, in which the level of water is less than 70 cm (28 in) deep. They are swift swimmers. The marshes with their high vegetation density protect them from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a> and provide them with food. These deer also have a small migratory pattern, they follow the water levels between the dry season and flooding season. With the fluctuation in water levels, they are able to find new food sources that the water uncovers during the dry season. Some freshwater ponds on the Pantanal Wetland, Brazil reported low densities of individuals dictating that those ponds are not able to support large populations of marsh deer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2001_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2001-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since marsh deer live near aquatic habitats, they eat a majority of their diet in aquatic plants. A study was conducted and they found 40 different species of plants in which they ate. The main food component was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graminae" title="Graminae">Graminae</a> which took up 22% of their diet, <a href="/wiki/Pontederiaceae" title="Pontederiaceae">Pontederiaceae</a> took up 12%, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leguminosae" title="Leguminosae">Leguminosae</a> was about 11%, and the rest was filled in with <a href="/wiki/Nymphaeaceae" title="Nymphaeaceae">Nymphaeaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alismataceae" title="Alismataceae">Alismataceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marantaceae" title="Marantaceae">Marantaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onagraceae" title="Onagraceae">Onagraceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">Cyperaceae</a>. They also enjoy eating aquatic flowers and shrubs that grow in the swamps and the floating mats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2000-6">[6]</a></sup> They can be best classed as a grazer-browser for food. Their diet also changes between the dry season and the flood season.</p>

<p>They possess very large ears lined with white hairs, reddish brown colored body and long dark legs. The hair turns darker during winter. There are also white marks on the hips and around the eyes. The legs are black below the tarsal as is the muzzle. The <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is of a paler reddish tone than the rest of the body on its upper part and black on the under part. The head-and-body length is 153 to 200 cm (5.02 to 6.56 ft), while the tail adds a further 12–16 cm (4.7–6.3 in). The height at the shoulder can range from 100 to 127 cm (3.28 to 4.17 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hoof" title="Hoof">hoof</a>, which is large in relation to the body, has elastic interdigital membranes which are useful for swimming and walking on marshy surfaces. Only the males possess antlers which are ramified and reach a length of 60 cm (23 inches). An adult typically grows to a weight of 80 to 125 kg (176 to 276 lb), although an occasional big male can weigh up to 150 kg (330 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are solitary animals or living in groups with less than 6 individuals with only an adult male. Their main predators are the <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">jaguar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">puma</a>.</p>
<p>Usually the rutting season coincides with the dry season but can change from animal to animal. They may use this to their advantage for breeding or finding mates because the densities of marsh deer are significantly higher on the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Negro_(Mato_Grosso_do_Sul)" title="Rio Negro (Mato Grosso do Sul)">Rio Negro</a> marshland boundary during the dry season compared to the less dense, more distributed population during the flooded season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2001_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2001-5">[5]</a></sup> Gestation lasts approximately 271 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The offspring (normally one per female, though occasionally twins are born) are born between October and November. The infant deer are whitish which becomes more adult-like after a year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The natural predators of the marsh deer – the jaguar (locally called onça or yaguaraté) (<i>Panthera onca</i>) and the puma (<i>Puma concolor</i>)— have almost completely disappeared from its habitat. The former major threat was poaching for its antlers, but this is somewhat under control. Destruction of its habitat presents nowadays the major threat to marsh deer. The <a href="/wiki/Yacyret%C3%A1_Dam" title="Yacyretá Dam">Yacyretá Dam</a> altered an area in which several hundred individuals lived and the draining of marshes for farmland and cattle threaten hundreds of hectares every year in Argentina and Brazil. Contagious diseases from cattle are also a problem, though it has been shown that the deer is not affected by <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>.</p>
