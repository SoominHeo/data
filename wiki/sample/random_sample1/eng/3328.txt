<p><b>Nishiki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">錦町</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Nishiki-machi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_towns_in_Japan" title="List of towns in Japan">town</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Kuma_District,_Kumamoto" title="Kuma District, Kumamoto">Kuma District</a> (also known as <i>Kuma County</i>), <a href="/wiki/Kumamoto_Prefecture" title="Kumamoto Prefecture">Kumamoto Prefecture</a>, Japan. The town's name is the Japanese word for "<a href="/wiki/Brocade" title="Brocade">brocade</a>", and is used as a name for <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Nishiki_(disambiguation)" title="Nishiki (disambiguation)">more than one town in Japan</a>. The town can trace its origin back to the merger of three villages in 1884. Two more villages (<i>Ichibu</i> and <i>Kijo</i>) were merged with Nishiki, then known as <i>Nishi-mura</i> or "West village", in 1955. It formally gained its status as a town (町; chō or machi) on April 1, 1965. At one time, between 1985 and 2006, Nishiki was <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with the (now defunct) town of <a href="/wiki/Nishiki,_Yamaguchi" title="Nishiki, Yamaguchi">Nishiki, Yamaguchi</a>.</p>
<p>In 2003 the town had an estimated <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of 11,904 and a <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">density</a> of 140.26 persons per km². The total area is 84.87 km<sup>2</sup> (32.77 sq mi). Nishiki is served by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Higo-Nishinomura_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Higo-Nishinomura Station (page does not exist)">Higo-Nishinomura Station</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Yunomae_Line" title="Yunomae Line">Kumagawa Railroad Yunomae Line</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flag">Flag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nishiki,_Kumamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Flag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current town flag was adopted in 1956, however there is no formal flag law codifying this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crwflags_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-crwflags-2">[1]</a></sup> The flag is green with a town emblem in the center. The emblem is a stylized <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a> ニNi of Nishiki. The circle "symbolizes a reconciliation and the mountain, the river and the sun do the town located in the center of Kuma Basin which is expanding to the future".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crwflags_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-crwflags-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nishiki,_Kumamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nishiki is located in the southern part of Kumamoto prefecture. 70 kilometers (43.5 miles) South-southeast of the town is the <a href="/wiki/Kumamoto" title="Kumamoto">prefectural capital</a>. The northern portion of the town lies in flat terrain that forms part of the <i>Hitoyoshi Basin</i>; the <a href="/wiki/Kuma_River_(Japan)" title="Kuma River (Japan)">Kuma River</a> borders the town to the East and West. The southern reaches of the <i>Kuju <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a></i> (九重山, Kujūsan)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[b]</a></sup> adjoins one corner of the town limits. The southern most corner of the townsland meets the prefectural border with <a href="/wiki/Miyazaki_Prefecture" title="Miyazaki Prefecture">Miyazaki Prefecture</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nishiki,_Kumamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town's economy is primarily based around agriculture (primarily <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">cultivation</a>) and leisure tourism activities such as <a href="/wiki/Sports_tourism" title="Sports tourism">golf holidays</a>, though the Nishiki plant of <a href="/wiki/Renesas_Electronics" title="Renesas Electronics">Renesas Semiconductor</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/NEC" title="NEC">NEC</a> Kumamoto) is also a major contributor. The Nishiki plant specializes in <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit#LSI" title="Integrated circuit">LSI</a> <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device_fabrication" title="Semiconductor device fabrication">manufacturing</a>. The total economic output of Nishiki for <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> 2004 was <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%A5" title="¥">¥</a></b>40.7 billion <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">yen</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nishiki,_Kumamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the closing years of the Second World War, a major <a href="/wiki/Aerodrome" title="Aerodrome">base</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hitoyoshi_Naval_Air_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hitoyoshi Naval Air Group (page does not exist)">Hitoyoshi Naval Air Group</a> was located near the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ajw_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ajw-4">[2]</a></sup> Relatively little is currently known about the base and its operations due to a loss of records and <a href="/wiki/Institutional_memory" title="Institutional memory">institutional memory</a> both during and after the war. The remains of the base extend over 36,000 m<sup>2</sup> (390,000 sq ft) of land, and houses underground facilities in more than 10 locations, including an <a href="/wiki/Control_room" title="Control room">operations room</a> and a large <a href="/wiki/Type_91_torpedo" title="Type 91 torpedo">torpedo</a> preparation area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ajw_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ajw-4">[2]</a></sup> In 2015, a full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological_field_survey" title="Archaeological field survey">archeological survey</a> of the site was initiated by the municipal government via the Nishiki <a href="/wiki/Board_of_education" title="Board of education">board of education</a> to discover more about the base and its past, with a view to eventually opening it up to tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ajw_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ajw-4">[2]</a></sup> This followed the discovery by the local government of a copy of a <a href="/wiki/Floor_plan" title="Floor plan">floor plan</a> for the base.</p>
