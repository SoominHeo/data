<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Niobe</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <b>Niobe</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Νιόβη</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">[ni.óbɛː]</a></span>) was a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a> and of either <a href="/wiki/Dione_(mythology)" title="Dione (mythology)">Dione</a>, the most frequently cited, or of <a href="/wiki/Eurythemista" title="Eurythemista">Eurythemista</a> or <a href="/wiki/Euryanassa" title="Euryanassa">Euryanassa</a>, and the sister of <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">Pelops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broteas" title="Broteas">Broteas</a>.</p>
<p>Her father was the ruler of a city located near <a href="/wiki/Manisa" title="Manisa">Manisa</a> in today's Aegean Turkey that was called "Tantalis"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or "the city of <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>", or "Sipylus". The city was located at the foot of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sipylus" title="Mount Sipylus">Mount Sipylus</a> and its ruins were reported to be still visible in the beginning of the 1st century AD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> although few traces remain today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> reports that Tantalis was destroyed by an earthquake and the city of Sipylus (<a href="/wiki/Magnesia_ad_Sipylum" title="Magnesia ad Sipylum">Magnesia ad Sipylum</a>) was built in its place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Niobe's father is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Phrygians" title="Phrygians">Phrygian</a>" and sometimes even as "King of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-myth_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-myth-5">[5]</a></sup> although his city was located in the western extremity of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> where <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a> was to emerge as a state before the beginning of the first millennium BC, and not in the traditional heartland of Phrygia, situated more inland. References to his son and Niobe's brother as "Pelops the Lydian" led some scholars to the conclusion that there would be good grounds for believing that she belonged to a primordial house of <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>.</p>
<p>She was already mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> which relates her proud <i><a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubris</a></i>, for which she was punished by <a href="/wiki/Leto" title="Leto">Leto</a>, who sent <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a> to slay all of her children, after which her children lay unburied for nine days while she abstained from food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Once the gods interred them, she retreated to her native Sipylus, "where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nymphs" title="Nymphs">Nymphs</a> dance around the River Acheloos,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and although being a stone, she broods over the sorrows sent from the Gods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Later writers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> asserted that Niobe was wedded to <a href="/wiki/Amphion" title="Amphion">Amphion</a>, one of the twin founders of <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">Thebes</a>, where there was a single sanctuary where the twin founders were venerated, but in fact no shrine to Niobe.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_theme">Central theme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Central theme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe,_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2255" data-file-width="3031" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe%2C_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg/250px-Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe%2C_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe%2C_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg/375px-Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe%2C_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe%2C_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg/500px-Woodcut_illustration_of_Niobe%2C_Amphion_and_their_dead_sons_-_Penn_Provenance_Project.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>Niobe boasted of her fourteen children, seven male and seven female (the <a href="/wiki/Niobids" title="Niobids">Niobids</a>), to <a href="/wiki/Leto" title="Leto">Leto</a> who only had two children, the twins Apollo and Artemis. The number varies in different sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Her speech which caused the indignation of the goddess was rendered in the following manner:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>It was on occasion of the annual celebration in honor of Latona and her offspring, Apollo and Diana</i> [i.e Artemis] <i>when the people of Thebes were assembled, their brows crowned with laurel, bearing frankincense to the altars and paying their vows, that Niobe appeared among the crowd. Her attire was splendid with gold and gems, and her face as beautiful as the face of an angry woman can be. She stood and surveyed the people with haughty looks. "What folly," said she, "is this! to prefer beings whom you never saw to those who stand before your eyes! Why should Latona be honored with worship rather than I? My father was Tantalus, who was received as a guest at the table of the gods; my mother was a goddess. My husband built and rules this city, Thebes; and Phrygia is my paternal inheritance. Wherever I turn my eyes I survey the elements of my power; nor is my form and presence unworthy of a goddess. To all this let me add, I have seven sons and seven daughters, and look for sons-in-law and daughters-in-law of pretensions worthy of my alliance. Have I not cause for pride? Will you prefer to me this Latona, the Titan's daughter, with her two children? I have seven times as many. Fortunate indeed am I, and fortunate I shall remain! Will any one deny this?</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-myth_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-myth-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="266" data-file-width="400" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg/250px-NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg/375px-NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NiobeWeepingRock_AglayanKaya_MountSipylus_ManisaTurkey.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Weeping Rock in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sipylus" title="Mount Sipylus">Mount Sipylus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manisa" title="Manisa">Manisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, has been associated with Niobe's legend since Antiquity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div>

<p>Using arrows, <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a> killed Niobe's daughters and <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> killed Niobe's sons. According to some versions, at least one Niobid (usually <a href="/wiki/Chloris" title="Chloris">Meliboea</a>) was spared. Their father, Amphion, at the sight of his dead sons, either killed himself or was killed by Apollo for having sworn revenge. Devastated, Niobe fled back to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sipylus" title="Mount Sipylus">Mount Sipylus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and was turned into stone, and, as she wept unceasingly, waters started to pour from her petrified complexion. Mount Sipylus indeed has a natural rock formation which resembles a female face, and it has been associated with Niobe since ancient times and described by <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>. The rock formation is also known as the "Weeping Rock" (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Ağlayan Kaya</i></span>), since rainwater seeps through its <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porous</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>.</p>
<p>The only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobid" title="Niobid">Niobid</a> spared stayed greenish pale from horror for the rest of her life, and for that reason she was called <a href="/wiki/Chloris" title="Chloris">Chloris</a> (the pale one).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Niobe_within_Greek_culture">Niobe within Greek culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Niobe within Greek culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his archaic role as bringer of diseases and death, Apollo with his poison arrows killed Niobe's sons and Artemis with her poison arrows killed Niobe's daughters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This is related to the myth of the seven youths and seven maidens who were sent every year to the king <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a> of Crete as an offering sacrifice to the <a href="/wiki/Minotaur" title="Minotaur">Minotaur</a>. Niobe was transformed into a stone on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sipylus" title="Mount Sipylus">Mount Sipylus</a> in her homeland of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>, where she brooded over the sorrows sent by the gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In Sophocles' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigone_(Sophocles)" title="Antigone (Sophocles)">Antigone</a></i> the heroine believes that she will have a similar death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The iconic number "seven" often appears in Greek legends, and represents an ancient tradition because it appears as a <a href="/wiki/Lyre" title="Lyre">lyre</a> with seven strings in the <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Triada" title="Hagia Triada">Hagia Triada sarcophagus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenean_Greece" title="Mycenean Greece">Mycenean age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Apollo's lyre had also seven strings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Niobe_in_literature_and_fine_arts">Niobe in literature and fine arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Niobe in literature and fine arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lineage_Tantalus.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="444" data-file-width="521" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Lineage_Tantalus.svg/250px-Lineage_Tantalus.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Lineage_Tantalus.svg/375px-Lineage_Tantalus.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Lineage_Tantalus.svg/500px-Lineage_Tantalus.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>The story of Niobe, and especially her sorrows, is an ancient one. The context in which she is mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a> to <a href="/wiki/Priam" title="Priam">Priam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> is as a stock type for mourning. Priam is not unlike Niobe in the sense that he was also grieving for his son <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>, who was killed and not buried for several days.</p>
<p>Niobe is also mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Antigone" title="Antigone">Antigone</a></i> where, as Antigone is marched toward her death, she compares her own loneliness to that of Niobe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a> is said to have also contributed a play titled <i>Niobe</i> that is lost.</p>
<p>The <i>Niobe</i> of <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>, set in Thebes, survives in fragmentary quotes that were supplemented by a papyrus sheet containing twenty-one lines of text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> From the fragments it appears that for the first part of the tragedy the grieving Niobe sits veiled and silent.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the conflict between Niobe and Leto is mentioned in one of <a href="/wiki/Sappho" title="Sappho">Sappho</a>'s poetic fragments (<i>"Before they were mothers, Leto and Niobe had been the most devoted of friends."</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin language</a> sources, Niobe's account is first told by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyginus" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a> in his collection of stories in brief and plain Fabulae.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Parthenius_of_Nicaea" title="Parthenius of Nicaea">Parthenius of Nicaea</a> records a rare version of the story of Niobe, in which her father is called Assaon and her husband Philottus. The circumstances in which Niobe loses her children are also different, see <a href="/wiki/Niobids#Variant_myth" title="Niobids">Niobids#Variant myth</a>.</p>
<p>Niobe's iconic tears were also mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Soliloquy" title="Soliloquy">soliloquy</a> (Act 1, Scene 2), in which he contrasts his mother's grief over the dead King, Hamlet's father – <i>"like Niobe, all tears"</i> – to her unseemly hasty marriage to Claudius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The quotation from <a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a> is also used in <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_L._Sayers" title="Dorothy L. Sayers">Dorothy L. Sayers</a>' novel <i>Murder Must Advertise</i>, in which an advertising agency's client turns down an advertisement using the quotation as a caption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among works of modern literature which have Niobe as a central theme, Kate Daniels' <i>"Niobe Poems"</i> can be cited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts">Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The subject of Niobe and the destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Niobids" title="Niobids">Niobids</a> was part of the repertory of Attic vase-painters and inspired sculpture groups and wall frescoes as well as relief carvings on Roman <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagi</a>.</p>
<p>The subject of the Attic calyx-krater from <a href="/wiki/Orvieto" title="Orvieto">Orvieto</a> conserved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre" title="Musée du Louvre">Musée du Louvre</a> has provided the name for the <a href="/wiki/Niobid_Painter" title="Niobid Painter">Niobid Painter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A lifesize group of marble <a href="/wiki/Niobids" title="Niobids">Niobids</a>, including one of Niobe sheltering one of her daughters, found in Rome in 1583 at the same time as the <i><a href="/wiki/Wrestlers_(sculpture)" title="Wrestlers (sculpture)">Wrestlers</a></i>, were taken in 1775 to the <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> where, in a gallery devoted to them, they remain some of the most prominent surviving sculptures of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical antiquity</a> (<i>see below</i>). New instances come to light from time to time, like one headless statue found in early 2005 among the ruins of a villa in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villa_dei_Quintili" title="Villa dei Quintili">Villa dei Quintili</a> just outside <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In painting, Niobe was painted by post-Renaissance artists from varied traditions (<i>see below</i>). An early appearance, "The Death of Niobe's Children" by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Bloemaert" title="Abraham Bloemaert">Abraham Bloemaert</a>, was painted in 1591 towards the start of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age" title="Dutch Golden Age">Dutch Golden Age</a>. The English artist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wilson_(painter)" title="Richard Wilson (painter)">Richard Wilson</a> gained great acclaim for his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Destruction_of_the_Children_of_Niobe" title="The Destruction of the Children of Niobe">The Destruction of the Children of Niobe</a></i>, painted in 1760. Three notable works, all dating from the 1770s, "Apollo and Diana Attacking Niobe and her Children" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anicet-Charles-Gabriel_Lemonnier" title="Anicet-Charles-Gabriel Lemonnier">Anicet-Charles-Gabriel Lemonnier</a>, "The Children of Niobe Killed by Apollo and Diana" by <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Charles_Jombert" title="Pierre-Charles Jombert">Pierre-Charles Jombert</a> and "Diana and Apollo Piercing Niobe’s Children with their Arrows" by <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a> belong to the tradition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Baroque_and_Classicism" title="French Baroque and Classicism">French Baroque and Classicism</a>.</p>
<p>"Niobe" is an abstract painting by <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1roly_Patk%C3%B3" title="Károly Patkó">Károly Patkó</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In classical music <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a> based one of his <i><a href="/wiki/Six_Metamorphoses_after_Ovid" title="Six Metamorphoses after Ovid">Six Metamorphoses after Ovid</a></i> on Niobe.</p>
<p>In modern music, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribou_(musician)" title="Caribou (musician)">Caribou</a> called the last track on his 2007 album <a href="/wiki/Andorra_(album)" title="Andorra (album)">Andorra</a> "Niobe".</p>
<p>In modern dance, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Lim%C3%B3n" title="José Limón">José Limón</a> named a section of his dance theater work "Dances for Isadora" as "Niobe". The section is a solo for a woman mourning the loss of her children.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terms">Related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The choice of "Niobe" simply as a name in works of art and literature is not uncommon either. Two minor characters of Greek mythology have the same name (<i>see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Niobe_(disambiguation)" title="Niobe (disambiguation)">Niobe (disambiguation)</a></i>) and the name occurs in several works of the 19th century. More recently, one of the characters in the films <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix_Reloaded" title="The Matrix Reloaded">The Matrix Reloaded</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix_Revolutions" title="The Matrix Revolutions">The Matrix Revolutions</a></i> was also named Niobe. A character named <a href="/wiki/Niobe_of_the_Voreni" title="Niobe of the Voreni">Niobe</a> also appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Rome_(TV_series)" title="Rome (TV series)">Rome</a> TV series.</p>
<p>The element <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a> was so named as an extension of the inspiration which had led earlier to the naming of the element <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Gustaf_Ekeberg" title="Anders Gustaf Ekeberg">Anders Gustaf Ekeberg</a>. On the basis of his argument according to which there were two different elements in the tantalite sample, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rose" title="Heinrich Rose">Heinrich Rose</a> named them after children of <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>—niobium and <a href="/wiki/Pelopium" title="Pelopium">pelopium</a>—although the argument was later contested as far as pelopium was concerned.</p>
<p>A mountain in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> is named Mount Niobe.</p>
