<p><b>Ebilun</b> (<a href="/wiki/Manchu_language" title="Manchu language">Manchu</a>: <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ebilun.png"><img alt="Ebilun.png" data-file-height="81" data-file-width="42" height="27" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Ebilun.png/14px-Ebilun.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Ebilun.png/21px-Ebilun.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Ebilun.png/28px-Ebilun.png 2x" width="14"/></a>; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">遏必隆</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Èbìlóng</span></i>; died 1673) was a <a href="/wiki/Manchu_people" title="Manchu people">Manchu</a> noble and warrior of the <a href="/wiki/Niohuru" title="Niohuru">Niohuru</a> clan, most famous for being one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_Regents" title="Four Regents">Four Regents</a> assisting the young <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a> from 1661 to 1667, during the early <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> (1644–1912). A largely passive figure during the regency, Ebilun was disgraced following the ouster of the far more powerful regent <a href="/wiki/Oboi" title="Oboi">Oboi</a> and considered a political supporter of the latter. He was stripped of his positions by the emperor but later regained his noble rank. Many of his descendants became influential figures in the Qing imperial government.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ebilun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ebilun was from the <a href="/wiki/Niohuru" title="Niohuru">Niohuru</a> clan, which lived north of the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Korean</a> border and belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Eight_Banners" title="Eight Banners">Bordered Yellow Banner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-george_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-george-2">[2]</a></sup> He was the youngest of the sixteen sons of <a href="/wiki/Eidu" title="Eidu">Eidu</a> (1562–1621), who had been a close associate of Manchu patriarch <a href="/wiki/Nurhaci" title="Nurhaci">Nurhaci</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ebilun's mother was herself a sister (or according to some sources, a cousin) of Nurhaci.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1634, the second Qing emperor <a href="/wiki/Hong_Taiji" title="Hong Taiji">Hong Taiji</a> (r. 1626–1643) gave Eidu a posthumous rank of viscount, which Ebilun immediately inherited but lost in 1637 after he tried to interfere in a trial involving his niece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_219_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_219-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1643 Ebilun followed Nurhaci's seventh son <a href="/wiki/Abatai" title="Abatai">Abatai</a> in forays inside North China and was credited with the capture of several towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1645 and 1646, after the Qing had defeated the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> and made <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> their capital, Ebilun served under Lekedehun in campaigns to dislodge Ming loyalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=He_Tengjiao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="He Tengjiao (page does not exist)">He Tengjiao</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E4%BD%95%E9%A8%B0%E8%9B%9F" title="zh:何騰蛟">何騰蛟</a>; 1592–1649) from <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a> and was rewarded with a minor hereditary rank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Yet his position was not assured. Because he belonged to the Yellow Banners, Ebilun was treated with suspicion by <a href="/wiki/Dorgon" title="Dorgon">Dorgon</a> (the Prince Regent of the young <a href="/wiki/Shunzhi_Emperor" title="Shunzhi Emperor">Shunzhi Emperor</a>), whose power base was in the White Banners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_219_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_219-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1648, during the persecution of <a href="/wiki/Hooge_(prince)" title="Hooge (prince)">Hooge</a>, Dorgon's main rival, Ebilun's nephew accused Ebilun of having opposed Dorgon during the 1643 succession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_219_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_219-5">[5]</a></sup> Ebilun was sentenced to death, but his penalty was commuted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Half of his property was nonetheless confiscated and his minor nobility title was revoked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_219_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_219-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Shunzhi emperor restored Ebilun's titles after Dorgon's death, and eventually entrusted Ebilun with three others to assist the rule of his son (the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a>), who ascended the throne in 1661 at the age of seven. Of the four regents, Ebilun was ranked third, after <a href="/wiki/He%C5%A1eri_Sonin" title="Hešeri Sonin">Sonin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suksaha" title="Suksaha">Suksaha</a>, and before <a href="/wiki/Oboi" title="Oboi">Oboi</a>. In practice Ebilun acquiesced to Oboi on nearly all decisions, as the latter gained increasing power. Ebilun also played a role in the ouster of <a href="/wiki/Suksaha" title="Suksaha">Suksaha</a>, which, after the infirm Sonin died, left Oboi the unchallenged top political figure at court. In 1667, after the Kangxi Emperor assumed personal rule, Ebilun was given the title of a first-class duke. In 1669, Manchu noble <a href="/wiki/Giyesu" title="Giyesu">Giyesu</a> memorialized the Kangxi Emperor listing 21 crimes supposedly committed by Ebilun shortly after the emperor had moved against Oboi. Ebilun was then sentenced to death. The sentence was later commuted, and Ebilun retained his title, which could be inherited by his descendants.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_descendants">Family and descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ebilun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family and descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ebilun belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Eidu" title="Eidu">Eidu</a> line of Niohuru clan nobles, many of whom would go on to serve with distinction in the imperial service. He had five sons. The eldest, Faka, inherited Ebilun's title of duke in 1667, only to lose it several years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-george_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-george-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ebilun's fourth son, Yende, served as an official under the <a href="/wiki/Yongzheng_Emperor" title="Yongzheng Emperor">Yongzheng Emperor</a> (r. 1722–1735), and in turn Yende's own son, Tsereng, served as <a href="/wiki/Viceroy_of_Huguang" title="Viceroy of Huguang">Viceroy of Huguang</a>; another of Yende's sons, Nochin, served on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Council_(Qing_dynasty)" title="Grand Council (Qing dynasty)">Grand Council</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong Emperor</a> (r. 1735–1796).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-george_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-george-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ebilun's fifth son, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alingga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alingga (page does not exist)">Alingga</a>, was a main figure in the succession battle among the sons of the Kangxi Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-george_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-george-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
