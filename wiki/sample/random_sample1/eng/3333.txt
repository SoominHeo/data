<p><b>Nyon</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Switzerland" title="Municipalities of Switzerland">municipality</a> in the district of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyon_(district)" title="Nyon (district)">Nyon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">canton</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">Vaud</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. It is located some 25 kilometers north east of <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>'s city centre, and since the 1970s it has become part of the Geneva <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a>. It lies on the shores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Leman" title="Lake Leman">Lake Leman</a> and is the seat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyon_(district)" title="Nyon (district)">district of Nyon</a>. The town has (as of December 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) a population of 20,088.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is connected to the rest of Switzerland by way of the Route Suisse, the <a href="/wiki/A1_motorway_(Switzerland)" title="A1 motorway (Switzerland)">A1 Motorway</a> and the railways of the <i>Arc Lémanique</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nyon derives from one of the names used by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> for the town, <b><a href="/wiki/Noviodunum_(Switzerland)" title="Noviodunum (Switzerland)">Noviodunum</a></b> or <b>Noiodunum</b>. Other names for the town, particularly of colonies placed there, are <b>Colonia Iulia Equestris</b> or <b>Colonia Julia Equestris</b>, <b>Colonia Equestris Noiodunum</b>, <b>Equestris</b>, <b>Civitas Equestrium</b>, and <b>Civitas Equestrium Noiodunum</b>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nyon is first mentioned around 367-407 as <i>civitas Equestrium id est Noiodunus</i> (in the "Notitia Galliarum"). In 1236 it was mentioned as <i>Neveduni</i> and in 1292 as <i>Nyons</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Roman_settlements">Pre-Roman settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-Roman settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few, scattered <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> items were discovered in the 19th century. North of the city, some bronze rings and the ruins of a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> settlement were discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noviodunum">Noviodunum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Noviodunum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Noviodunum_(Switzerland)" title="Noviodunum (Switzerland)">Noviodunum (Switzerland)</a></div>
<p>It was founded by the Romans between 50 and 44 BC under the name of Colonia Iulia Equestris or Colonia Equestris Noiodunum, the urban center of which was called Noviodunum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Colonia_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Colonia-5">[5]</a></sup> It grew to be one of the most important Roman colonies in modern-day Switzerland, with a <a href="/wiki/Forum_(Roman)" title="Forum (Roman)">forum</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphitheater" title="Amphitheater">amphitheater</a> that was discovered only recently, in 1996, when digging for the construction of a new building.</p>
<p>At Roman contact, the country round the town was held by the <a href="/wiki/Helvetii" title="Helvetii">Helvetii</a>. The town's importance is reflected in its numerous mentions in ancient sources. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonine_Itineraries" title="Antonine Itineraries">Antonine Itineraries</a> place the town on the road from Geneva to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacus_Lausonius" title="Lacus Lausonius">Lacus Lausonius</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>). It is first mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> (<i>H.N.</i>, iv. 7), and then by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> (ii. 9), who assigns it to the <a href="/wiki/Sequani" title="Sequani">Sequani</a>. Pliny and Ptolemy simply name it Equestris; and so it is named in the Itineraries. On some inscriptions it is called <i>Civ. Equestrium</i> (short for Civitas Equestrium), and <i>Col. Julia Equ.</i> (short for Colonia Julia Equestris) from which some have concluded that it was founded by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Notitia_Dignitatum" title="Notitia Dignitatum">Notitia</a> it is called <i>Civ. Equestrium Noiodunum</i> (short for Civitas Equestrium Noiodunum). The district in which Nyon stands is called <i>Pagus Equestricus</i> in a document of the year 1011; and it is said that the people of the country as of the 18th century still called this district <i>Enquestre</i>. (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%27Anville" title="D'Anville">D'Anville</a>, Notice, &amp;c.; Walckenaer, Géographie, &amp;c., des Gaules, vol. ii. p. 316.)</p>
<p>Noviodunum was part of a loose network of settlements that radiated out from <a href="/wiki/Lugdunum" title="Lugdunum">Lugdunum</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>) and helped to control the Rhone Valley. It served, along with other Roman colonies in the area, to control the Helvetii who were settled in the area against their will after their defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bibracte" title="Battle of Bibracte">Battle of Bibracte</a> in 58 BC.</p>
<p>A rectangular grid pattern divided the area of the wall-less city. A monumental center, housing everything needed for the economic, religious and social life of the colony, was established. Only portions of this first forum have been discovered. At its east end was a two-story basilica. Grid-like residential streets radiated out from the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Colonia_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Colonia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a>, the forum was expanded and redesigned into a familiar pattern for the provinces. The sacred area was surrounded on three sides by <a href="/wiki/Colonnade" title="Colonnade">colonnades</a>, which were built on half-sunken <a href="/wiki/Cryptoporticus" title="Cryptoporticus">Cryptoporticus</a>. Two outbuildings, including most likely the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Curia" title="Curia">Curia</a>, flanked the building. A market building (<a href="/wiki/Macellum" title="Macellum">macellum</a>) with a central courtyard around which were the sales rooms, and the baths (<a href="/wiki/Tepidarium" title="Tepidarium">tepidarium</a> with geometric shapes and <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaics</a>) were renovated. The forum witnessed further transformations, particularly the establishment of another large building. During the same building phase a large mosaic on the central part of the north portico was built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Colonia_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Colonia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The amphitheater, which was discovered in 1996, was probably built in the early 2nd Century AD. Its <a href="/wiki/Arena" title="Arena">arena</a>, which was flanked by two prisons and provided with sewers, is about 50 by 36 metres (164 by 118 ft). The ruins of the theater, that should have been in the Colonia, have not been discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Colonia_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Colonia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The residential quarters consisted of modest homes, in addition to some <a href="/wiki/Domus" title="Domus">domi</a> with beautiful gardens and pools. The buildings were originally made of wood and clay, but after the mid-1st Century AD were built from masonry. Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villa_suburbana" title="Villa suburbana">villa suburbana</a> stood in the west of the village, while the artisan and merchant quarter, presumably, developed in the southwest. A 10 km (6.2 mi) long <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueduct" title="Roman aqueduct">aqueduct</a> which ran from the Divonne area to the colony, provided the water supply. Sewage canals, that followed the road networks, dumped sewage into the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Colonia_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Colonia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_the_Roman_Colonia">Decline of the Roman Colonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline of the Roman Colonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After a long period of peace and prosperity, signs of crisis and general insecurity were increasing in the early 3rd Century. As a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> invasions of 259 or 260 AD, the forum and the public buildings in the city were razed. The stone blocks were scattered all over the Lake Geneva region. The stones were re-used as building material, especially in Geneva, where about 300 were used in the construction of the wall. But the settlement was not abandoned. Nyon-Noviodunum, which had already lost much of its prestige and reputation was as a regional capital, now separated from Geneva. Geneva became the center and seat of the diocese which initially fought to administer the territory that had been part of the Colonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Colonia_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Colonia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian</a> era, Nyon belonged to the county of Geneva. In a 926 charter, <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_II_of_Burgundy" title="Rudolph II of Burgundy">Rudolph II of Burgundy</a> mentioned that this area was under a <i>comes de pago Equestrico</i>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Kingdom_of_Burgundy" title="Second Kingdom of Burgundy">Second Kingdom of Burgundy</a>, Nyon became independent from Geneva. In 1032, <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_III_of_Burgundy" title="Rudolph III of Burgundy">Rudolf III</a> granted Nyon to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Besan%C3%A7on" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Besançon">Archbishop of Besançon</a>. The bishop granted Nyon to the Lord of Prangins as a <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fief</a>. After 1130, Humbert de Cossonay, the Lord of Prangins, held his court in Nyon. A market was built in 1211. In 1272, the Archbishop of Besançon confirmed <a href="/wiki/Philip_I,_Count_of_Savoy" title="Philip I, Count of Savoy">Philip of Savoy</a>'s right to administer <a href="/wiki/High,_middle_and_low_justice" title="High, middle and low justice">high justice</a> in Nyon. In 1279, Aymon of Prangins unsuccessfully rebelled against the counts of Savoy, but was forced to acknowledge Savoy authority over him and Nyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Mid_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Mid-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Castle is first mentioned in 1272, but probably dates back to the Lords of Prangins. It was rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Louis,_Duke_of_Savoy" title="Louis, Duke of Savoy">Louis I of Savoy</a> in 1463. The rectangular edifice was built in a variety of styles. Around the same time, the square César Tower or Tour de Rive (now a residence), was built to defend the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Mid_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Mid-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1293, <a href="/wiki/Amadeus_V,_Count_of_Savoy" title="Amadeus V, Count of Savoy">Amadeus V, Count of Savoy</a>, and his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_I,_Baron_of_Vaud" title="Louis I, Baron of Vaud">Louis I, Baron of Vaud</a>, conquered the city by besieging it from both the land and the lake. They confirmed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_rights" title="Town rights">town rights</a> which had been granted to Nyon by Aymon of Prangins, and extended further rights and freedoms. It became one of the four <i>bonnes villes</i> of Canton Vaud. In 1294, Louis I began to expand Nyon as a center of his power after Amadeus granted his share of the conquest to Louis. He created a court and a mint, which minted coins for the lords of Vaud between 1286 and 1350. In 1323 Louis II, granted the so-called mountains of Nyon, i.e. pastures and forests in the area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arzier" title="Arzier">Arzier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Cergue" title="Saint-Cergue">Saint-Cergue</a>, to Nyon. In 1359 Nyon lost importance after <a href="/wiki/Amadeus_VI,_Count_of_Savoy" title="Amadeus VI, Count of Savoy">Amadeus VI</a> acquired rights over the entire Vaud. In 1364 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_charter" title="Town charter">town charter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Morges" title="Morges">Morges</a> replaced the charter in Nyon. This change was reconfirmed in 1439. Under the new charter, the town gained greater self-sufficiency. The mint in Nyon reopened in 1430. In 1530 the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Swiss Confederation</a> invaded Vaud and acquired Nyon. Then in 1536, Nyon surrendered again to <a href="/wiki/Bern" title="Bern">Bern</a> without a fight as Bernese troops marched through to support Geneva.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Mid_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Mid-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in 1323, the municipal government was composed of eight procurators and a mayor. A little later the government was replaced by a community meeting which was headed by two mayors (Syndics). The Town Hall is first mentioned in 1508.</p>

<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">priory</a> was founded in the first half of the 12th Century in Nyon. In 1244 it was given to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustinian" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> order. The last <a href="/wiki/Prior" title="Prior">prior</a>, before the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> in 1535 was Aymon de Gingins, who was also the abbot of Bonmont and the selected <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Lausanne,_Geneva_and_Fribourg" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Lausanne, Geneva and Fribourg">Bishop of Geneva</a>.</p>
<p>In 1295–96, <a href="/wiki/Louis,_Duke_of_Savoy" title="Louis, Duke of Savoy">Louis I, Duke of Savoy</a>, built a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> monastery under the patronage of <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">St. Francis</a>, in which several members of the House of Savoy were buried. In 1530, Bern and <a href="/wiki/Fribourg" title="Fribourg">Fribourg</a> plundered the monastery for the first time, and Bern destroyed all the pictures of saints. In 1536, it was burned by the withdrawing Savoy garrison.</p>
<p>In 1110 Geneva granted authority over the church of Notre-Dame to the Benedictine abbey of Saint-Oyend (<a href="/wiki/Saint-Claude,_Jura" title="Saint-Claude, Jura">Saint-Claude</a>). The church was built with material from the Roman period and replaced an earlier Christian church. The <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a> dates from the 12th Century and the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> was built in 1448. The vault and the side chapels were built in 1470-81.</p>
<p>Situated outside the city walls was the church of Saint-Jean-Baptiste, also known as Corps-Saints. The church is first mentioned in 1346. By 1412, it was administered by the Augustinian Priory. Until the Reformation it was a popular <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> site where the <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a> of the martyrs of the <a href="/wiki/Theban_Legion" title="Theban Legion">Theban Legion</a> were revered. Bern ordered the church destroyed in 1537.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Mid_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Mid-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_Nyon">Early Modern Nyon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early Modern Nyon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Nyon was raised to the status of administrative center of a <i><a href="/wiki/Vogt" title="Vogt">Vogtei</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bern" title="History of Bern">Bern</a> in 1536. It remained the seat of the vogt until the Vaud revolution of 1798. In 1568, a yearly market took place for the first time. In 1574-80 the castle was converted into the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Bailiff" title="Bailiff">bailiff</a>. It was renovated several times in the following centuries. The municipal government added a Council of Fifty in 1558 and in 1578, a inner council of Twenty-four. The inner council was made up of nobles, citizens and <i>habitants</i>. Later the councils became a Council of Twelve and a council of Twenty-Four, which was headed by a <a href="/wiki/Knight_banneret" title="Knight banneret">Knight banneret</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Early_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Early-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1570, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Beza" title="Theodore Beza">Theodore Beza</a> headed the regional assembly of Protestant refugee clergymen in Nyon. After the revocation of the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a> in 1685, many <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a> fled to Nyon. In 1688, they founded the <i>Bourse française</i> to help other refugees.</p>
<p>Nyon remained an important transhipment point for trade along Lake Geneva and from France and Italy. Grain and wood came from Burgundy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franche-Comte" title="Franche-Comte">Franche-Comte</a> through the Col de la Givrine and Saint-Cergue on to Geneva. In 1537-76, the customs post of Nyon was the most profitable in the bailiwick. Over the following centuries, trade through Nyon remained very profitable and by 1772-73, it was again the highest in the region. In the 18th Century trade and commerce grew strongly. The flow of goods allowed four (after 1738, five) yearly markets. A granary was built in the 18th Century. The Bernese rebuilt the Asse canal system to drive tanneries, sawmills and mills. The Faïencerie Baylon earthenware factory was founded in 1769. It was followed by the Jacob Dortu and Ferdinand Müller porcelain factory in 1781, both of which contributed Nyon's reputation for fine ceramics.</p>
<p>With the profits of the Early Modern era, many of the public buildings of Nyon were rebuilt or expanded. Following the suppression of the monasteries, in 1539, the town hospital moved into the offices of the Augustinian convent and received money from the closed monasteries. Hans-Ulrich Heldt rebuilt the College, the original building was from 1559, in 1786 in a <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> style. The church of Notre-Dame was rebuilt several times between 1661 and 1718. The medieval city walls and gates were demolished in 1718. The town hall was rebuilt in 1773. In 1720, Louis de Saint-Georges, a minister of the English King, built the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Changins_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Changins Castle (page does not exist)">Changins Castle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Early_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Early-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was one of the most active centers of the revolutionary movement in the Vaud region. The councils of Nyon formed an oversight committee and refused to pay homage to Bern on 10 January 1798. A few days later, they secured the support of the French General <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Philippe_M%C3%A9nard_Romain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Philippe Ménard Romain (page does not exist)">Philippe Ménard Romain</a> in support of the independence of Canton Vaud. When this proclamation of support was brought from Nyon to Lausanne, on 24 January 1798, it finally led to the Vaudois revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_Early_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Early-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1798 to 1803, it was in the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_L%C3%A9man" title="Canton of Léman">canton of Léman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Helvetic_Republic" title="Helvetic Republic">Helvetic Republic</a> as district capital.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Nyon">Modern Nyon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern Nyon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the first half of the 19th Century, the city continued with the demolition of the fortifications, but left wall remains at the Promenade des Marronniers and by the tower of Notre Dame. During the 19th Century was the Reformed <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> was rebuilt several times. The Catholic Church was consecrated in 1839, and a chapel for the <i>Eglise libre</i> (Free Church) was finished in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_modern_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_modern-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1858, a station of the railway line Lausanne-Geneva was built in north of Nyon. New housing developments sprung up around this station, and the city began to spread. The railroads Nyon-Crassier-Divonne (1905) and Nyon-Saint-Cergue-Morez (1916) connected Nyon with its hinterland. To meet the needs of the economy, the shipping industry and the emerging tourism industry, a port was built in 1838 and a quay was added in 1873. By the middle of the 19th Century, a major source of income was the sale of timber from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_commons" title="The commons">the commons</a> forest. Other industries included tanneries (closed in 1925), carpentry, saw mills (until 1935), mills (including Andre &amp; Cie.), cooperages and a soap factory. The Faïencerie Baylon closed in 1828. The porcelain factory, Müller et Dortu temporarily closed in 1813, then resumed production of ceramics in the <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> style in 1878. From then on until 1980 they produced Stoneware. Other industries in Nyon include the pasta factory Sangal SA (1860–1996), Zyma (1906, since 1996, Swiss Novartis Consumer Health), Stellram (hard metal treatment, 1940–99), Cherix et Filanosa SA (printing and graphic arts, 1932) and several tool factories. Starting in 1966, the companies stopped using the local locks and dams for hydropower and by 1974 they had disappeared from the Asse river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_modern_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_modern-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1937, it hosted the <a href="/wiki/Nyon_Conference" title="Nyon Conference">Nyon Conference</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_situation">Current situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has a high school (Gymnase de Nyon, known as CESSOuest until 1997 or 1998), a modern hospital, a movie theater, numerous hotels, restaurants, cafes, etc. The town is best known on the international stage as the home of the headquarters for <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a>, the governing body for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> in Europe. It is also the seat of the international headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Global_union_federation" title="Global union federation">global union federation</a> <a href="/wiki/UNI_Global_Union" title="UNI Global Union">UNI Global Union</a>. Nyon is also a major centre for the International Money-Broking Industry.</p>
<p>In the last week of July each year, Nyon hosts the <a href="/wiki/Pal%C3%A9o_Festival" title="Paléo Festival">Paléo Festival</a>, the second largest outdoor festival in Europe (although technically the festival is in the village of L'Asse). Nyon Rugby Club is one of the top rugby teams in Switzerland and is twinned with Ealing Rugby Club in West London. Nyon also hosts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visions_du_r%C3%A9el" title="Visions du réel">Visions du Réel</a> international film festival every April. It hosted the UEFA Champions League and UEFA Cup draws in December 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Nyon has an area, as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, of 6.8 square kilometers (2.6 sq mi). Of this area, 2.66 km<sup>2</sup> (1.03 sq mi) or 39.2% is used for agricultural purposes, while 0.53 km<sup>2</sup> (0.20 sq mi) or 7.8% is forested. Of the rest of the land, 3.67 km<sup>2</sup> (1.42 sq mi) or 54.1% is settled (buildings or roads).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFS_land_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BFS_land-10">[10]</a></sup> Of the built up area, industrial buildings made up 5.9% of the total area while housing and buildings made up 27.4% and transportation infrastructure made up 14.1%. Power and water infrastructure as well as other special developed areas made up 2.1% of the area while parks, green belts and sports fields made up 4.6%. Out of the forested land, 5.6% of the total land area is heavily forested and 2.2% is covered with orchards or small clusters of trees. Of the agricultural land, 25.9% is used for growing crops and 4.7% is pastures, while 8.5% is used for orchards or vine crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFS_land_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BFS_land-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The municipality was the capital of the old <a href="/wiki/Nyon_District" title="Nyon District">Nyon District</a> until it was dissolved on 31 August 2006, and Nyon became the capital of the new district of Nyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mutation_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mutation-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The municipality is located between the <a href="/wiki/Jura_Mountains" title="Jura Mountains">Jura Mountains</a> and Lake Geneva. The old core of Nyon, on the right bank of the Asse, is divided into the upper city (which was built on the Roman ruins on a hill) and the lower city along the water. Along the main thoroughfare a residential section developed to the east of the old city, and an industrial sector to the west.</p>

<p>Nyon has a population (as of December 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) of 20,088.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 38.0% of the population are resident foreign nationals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_superweb_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_superweb-14">[14]</a></sup> Over the last 10 years (1999–2009 ) the population has changed at a rate of 16.8%. It has changed at a rate of 9.7% due to migration and at a rate of 7.7% due to births and deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the population (as of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) speaks <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> (12,274 or 75.8%), with <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> being second most common (918 or 5.7%) and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> being third (647 or 4.0%). There are 565 people who speak <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and 9 people who speak <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The age distribution, as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, in Nyon is; 2,015 children or 11.2% of the population are between 0 and 9 years old and 1,828 teenagers or 10.1% are between 10 and 19. Of the adult population, 2,304 people or 12.8% of the population are between 20 and 29 years old. 3,150 people or 17.4% are between 30 and 39, 3,051 people or 16.9% are between 40 and 49, and 2,187 people or 12.1% are between 50 and 59. The senior population distribution is 1,757 people or 9.7% of the population are between 60 and 69 years old, 1,045 people or 5.8% are between 70 and 79, there are 595 people or 3.3% who are between 80 and 89, and there are 130 people or 0.7% who are 90 and older.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaud_Pop_Stats_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaud_Pop_Stats-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 6,796 people who were single and never married in the municipality. There were 7,538 married individuals, 768 widows or widowers and 1,080 individuals who are divorced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 7,307 private households in the municipality, and an average of 2.2 persons per household.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup> There were 2,818 households that consist of only one person and 342 households with five or more people. Out of a total of 7,450 households that answered this question, 37.8% were households made up of just one person and there were 27 adults who lived with their parents. Of the rest of the households, there are 1,785 married couples without children, 2,053 married couples with children There were 493 single parents with a child or children. There were 131 households that were made up of unrelated people and 143 households that were made up of some sort of institution or another collective housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

There were 621 multi-family buildings (38.3%), along with 235 multi-purpose buildings that were mostly used for housing (14.5%) and 115 other use buildings (commercial or industrial) that also had some housing (7.1%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housing_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housing-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_federal_election,_2011" title="Swiss federal election, 2011">federal election</a> the most popular party was the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">SP</a> which received 26.0% of the vote. The next three most popular parties were the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_People%27s_Party" title="Swiss People's Party">SVP</a> (20.9%), the <a href="/wiki/FDP.The_Liberals" title="FDP.The Liberals">FDP</a> (13.4%) and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Green Party of Switzerland">Green Party</a> (12.2%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_federal_election,_2007" title="Swiss federal election, 2007">federal election</a> the most popular party was the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">SP</a> which received 21.54% of the vote. The next three most popular parties were the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_People%27s_Party" title="Swiss People's Party">SVP</a> (18.78%), the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Green Party of Switzerland">Green Party</a> (16.29%) and the <a href="/wiki/FDP.The_Liberals" title="FDP.The Liberals">FDP</a> (12.05%). In the federal election, a total of 3,846 votes were cast, and the <a href="/wiki/Voter_turnout" title="Voter turnout">voter turnout</a> was 42.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gl%C3%A9nat_(publisher)" title="Glénat (publisher)">Glénat</a>'s Swiss subsidiary has its headquarters in Nyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cantor_Fitzgerald" title="Cantor Fitzgerald">Cantor Fitzgerald</a> has an office in Nyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Lifesciences" title="Edwards Lifesciences">Edwards Lifesciences</a> opened the regional headquarters and training center in Nyon in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Nyon had an unemployment rate of 6%. As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 49 people employed in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary economic sector</a> and about 13 businesses involved in this sector. 1,331 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary sector</a> and there were 153 businesses in this sector. 10,644 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary sector</a>, with 1,131 businesses in this sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup> There were 8,631 residents of the municipality who were employed in some capacity, of which females made up 46.0% of the workforce.</p>
<p>In 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the total number of <a href="/wiki/Full-time_equivalent" title="Full-time equivalent">full-time equivalent</a> jobs was 10,337. The number of jobs in the primary sector was 39, of which 35 were in agriculture, 2 were in forestry or lumber production and 2 were in fishing or fisheries. The number of jobs in the secondary sector was 1,264 of which 600 or (47.5%) were in manufacturing and 588 (46.5%) were in construction. The number of jobs in the tertiary sector was 9,034. In the tertiary sector; 1,592 or 17.6% were in wholesale or retail sales or the repair of motor vehicles, 281 or 3.1% were in the movement and storage of goods, 493 or 5.5% were in a hotel or restaurant, 400 or 4.4% were in the information industry, 1,414 or 15.7% were the insurance or financial industry, 1,499 or 16.6% were technical professionals or scientists, 595 or 6.6% were in education and 1,150 or 12.7% were in health care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 7,415 workers who commuted into the municipality and 5,234 workers who commuted away. The municipality is a net importer of workers, with about 1.4 workers entering the municipality for every one leaving. About 12.1% of the workforce coming into Nyon are coming from outside Switzerland, while 0.1% of the locals commute out of Switzerland for work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commuter_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-commuter-27">[27]</a></sup> Of the working population, 26.7% used public transportation to get to work, and 50.6% used a private car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the 2000 census<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 6,121 or 37.8% were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>, while 4,522 or 27.9% belonged to the Swiss Reformed Church. Of the rest of the population, there were 174 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christianity" title="Orthodox Christianity">members of an Orthodox church</a> (or about 1.08% of the population), there were 17 individuals (or about 0.11% of the population) who belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Catholic_Church_of_Switzerland" title="Christian Catholic Church of Switzerland">Christian Catholic Church</a>, and there were 936 individuals (or about 5.78% of the population) who belonged to another Christian church. There were 32 individuals (or about 0.20% of the population) who were <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a>, and 750 (or about 4.63% of the population) who were <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a>. There were 63 individuals who were <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a>, 98 individuals who were <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> and 29 individuals who belonged to another church. 2,893 (or about 17.88% of the population) belonged to no church, are <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnostic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheist</a>, and 996 individuals (or about 6.15% of the population) did not answer the question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Nyon about 5,216 or (32.2%) of the population have completed non-mandatory <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Switzerland#Secondary" title="Education in Switzerland">upper secondary education</a>, and 3,009 or (18.6%) have completed additional higher education (either <a href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Switzerland" title="List of universities in Switzerland">university</a> or a <i><a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a></i>). Of the 3,009 who completed tertiary schooling, 36.4% were Swiss men, 27.4% were Swiss women, 20.4% were non-Swiss men and 15.7% were non-Swiss women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2009/2010 school year there were a total of 1,867 students in the Nyon school district. In the Vaud cantonal school system, two years of non-obligatory pre-school are provided by the political districts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> During the school year, the political district provided pre-school care for a total of 1,249 children of which 563 children (45.1%) received subsidized pre-school care. The canton's <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">primary school</a> program requires students to attend for four years. There were 995 students in the municipal primary school program. The obligatory lower secondary school program lasts for six years and there were 806 students in those schools. There were also 66 students who were home schooled or attended another non-traditional school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaud_Education_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaud_Education-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nyon is home to three museums; the <i>Musée historique</i>, the <i>Musée du Léman</i> and the <i>Musée romain</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaud_Museum_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaud_Museum-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2009 the <i>Musée historique</i> was visited by 14,164 visitors (the average in previous years was 26,194). In 2009 the <i>Musée du Léman</i> was visited by 20,596 visitors (the average in previous years was 23,020). In 2009 the <i>Musée romain</i>, was closed for renovations butin previous years it had an average of 9,225 visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaud_Museum_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vaud_Museum-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 1,582 students in Nyon who came from another municipality, while 415 residents attended schools outside the municipality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commuter_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-commuter-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nyon is home to 2 libraries; the Bibliothèque municipale de Nyon and the <i>Ecole d'ingénieurs de Changins</i>. There was a combined total (as of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) of 53,262 books or other media in the libraries, and in the same year a total of 117,481 items were loaned out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_Champittet" title="Collège Champittet">Collège Champittet</a>, an international school, has its Nyon campus in the commune.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nyon is home to <a href="/wiki/FC_Stade_Nyonnais" title="FC Stade Nyonnais">FC Stade Nyonnais</a>, who play in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1._Liga_Promotion" title="1. Liga Promotion">1. Liga Promotion</a>, and their ground <a href="/wiki/Centre_sportif_de_Colovray_Nyon" title="Centre sportif de Colovray Nyon">Centre sportif de Colovray Nyon</a>.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_Club_Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rugby Club Nyon (page does not exist)">Rugby Club Nyon</a> are one of the most successful rugby teams in Switzerland, regularly ending top of their leagues in both the 1st and 2nd teams leagues, they also have a 3rd team, youth, ladies and veterans side and also are based at the <a href="/wiki/Centre_sportif_de_Colovray_Nyon" title="Centre sportif de Colovray Nyon">Colovray Centre</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UEFA">UEFA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: UEFA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nyon is famous for being the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">Union of European Football Associations</a> (UEFA) and the <a href="/wiki/European_Club_Association" title="European Club Association">European Club Association</a> (ECA). The draws for the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> and the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Europa League</a> are held in Nyon. The 2 main Nyon draws, December for Round of 16 and March for the last eight until final, are eagerly waited for by <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> fans whose teams are in <a href="/wiki/Category:UEFA_competitions" title="Category:UEFA competitions">UEFA competitions</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nyon is the starting station for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemin_de_Fer_Nyon-St.Cergue-Morez" title="Chemin de Fer Nyon-St.Cergue-Morez">Chemin de Fer Nyon-St.Cergue-Morez</a>, as well as being on the main Geneva to Lausanne <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Railways" title="Swiss Federal Railways">Swiss Federal Railways</a> line.</p>
<p>The A1 motorway runs nearby and Nyon is served by Junction 11.</p>
