<p><b>Joseph Hardy Neesima</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">新島 襄</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Niijima Jō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 12 February 1843 – 23 January 1890)</span> was a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Educator" title="Educator">educator</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji era</a> who founded <a href="/wiki/Doshisha_University" title="Doshisha University">Doshisha University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doshisha_Women%27s_College_of_Liberal_Arts" title="Doshisha Women's College of Liberal Arts">Doshisha Women's College of Liberal Arts</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Hardy_Neesima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neesima was born in <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>), the son of a retainer of the <a href="/wiki/Itakura_clan" title="Itakura clan">Itakura clan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Annaka_Domain" title="Annaka Domain">Annaka</a>. His childhood name was <b>Niijima Shimeta</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">新島 七五三太</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<p>In 1864, laws on <a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">national isolation</a> were still in effect in Japan, and Japanese people were not permitted to travel overseas without government permission. However, Neesima had read extensively on various <a href="/wiki/Rangaku" title="Rangaku">rangaku</a> topics, and was determined to come to America. At the age of 21, he entreated Captain William T. Savory, of <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Massachusetts" title="Salem, Massachusetts">Salem, Massachusetts</a>, commander of the brig <i>Berlin</i>, for safe passage to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, in order to further study Western science and <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. Captain Savory agreed to help him, so long as Neesima came on board at night, without assistance from the ship's crew. Knowing Neesima could be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executed" title="Executed">executed</a> if apprehended, Savory hid Neesima from customs officials in his stateroom. He then secured Neesima's passage from China to the United States on the <i>Wild Rover</i>, commanded by Captain Horace Taylor of Chatham, Massachusetts. The <i>Wild Rover</i> was owned by Alpheus Hardy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_United_States">In United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Hardy_Neesima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When he arrived in <a href="/wiki/Andover,_Massachusetts" title="Andover, Massachusetts">Andover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, he was sponsored by Alpheus and Susan Hardy, members of <a href="/wiki/Old_South_Church" title="Old South Church">Old South Church</a>, who also saw to his education. He attended <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Academy" title="Phillips Academy">Phillips Academy</a> from 1865 to 1867 and then <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">Amherst College</a>, where he was greatly influenced by professor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julius_Seelye" title="Julius Seelye">Julius Seelye</a>, from 1867 to 1870. Upon graduating from Amherst, Neesima became the first Japanese person to receive a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a>.</p>
<p>He was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptized" title="Baptized">baptized</a> in 1866 and went on to study at <a href="/wiki/Andover_Theological_Seminary" title="Andover Theological Seminary">Andover Theological Seminary</a> in 1870.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Iwakura_Mission" title="Iwakura Mission">Iwakura Mission</a> visited the United States in 1871 on its around-the-world expedition, Neesima assisted as an interpreter. He traveled with the Mission for more than a year, in Europe and the United States.</p>
<p>On his return, he completed his studies at Andover Theological Seminary, and in 1874, he became the first Japanese to be ordained by Rev. A.C. Thompson on Thursday, September 24th at Mount Vernon Church, Boston as a Protestant Minister. In the same year, Neesima attended the 65th annual meeting of the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missionaries at a Congregational church on Friday, October 9th held in Rutland, Vermont, and made an appeal for funds to start a Christian college in Japan. Hardy, Arthur Sherburne. Life and Letters of Joseph Hardy Neesima, 1891.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Japan">Return to Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Hardy_Neesima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Return to Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the support and funding he received, he returned to Japan, and in 1875 founded a school in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, which grew rapidly and became <a href="/wiki/Doshisha_University" title="Doshisha University">Doshisha University</a>. He was assisted by his wife <a href="/wiki/Yamamoto_Yaeko" title="Yamamoto Yaeko">Niijima Yae</a> and brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Yamamoto_Kakuma" title="Yamamoto Kakuma">Yamamoto Kakuma</a>, who were also active with the local Christian community in Kyoto. He died in 1890, at age 47, in <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ciso,_Kanagawa" title="Ōiso, Kanagawa">Oiso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanagawa_Prefecture" title="Kanagawa Prefecture">Kanagawa Prefecture</a>, and was buried in Kyoto.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Hardy_Neesima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1889, Amherst College honored him with an honorary doctorate, the first ever awarded to a Japanese person.</p>
<p>In 1907, he was honored as one of six great educators of the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>, before the assembly of educators of the entire nation held by the Imperial education conference, the education conference of Tokyo prefecture and the Tokyo city board of education.</p>
<p>He was honored on a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamp</a> in 1950.</p>
<p>In his honour, <b>Niijima Gakuen Junior College</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">新島学園短期大学</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Niijima gakuen tanki daigaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was founded. It is a <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private</a> <a href="/wiki/Junior_college" title="Junior college">junior college</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Takasaki" title="Takasaki">Takasaki, Gunma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Similarly, there is Niijima Gakuen Senior College, which has close links to Doshisha University</p>
