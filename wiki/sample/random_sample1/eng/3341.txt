<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Nietzsche contra Wagner</i></h1>

<p><b>"Nietzsche contra Wagner"</b> is a critical essay by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>, composed of recycled passages from his past works. It was written in his last year of lucidity (1888–1889), but was not published until 1895, six years after his mental collapse. Nietzsche describes in this short work why he parted ways with his one-time idol and friend, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>. Nietzsche attacks Wagner's views, expressing disappointment and frustration in Wagner's life choices (such as his conversion to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, perceived as a sign of weakness). Nietzsche evaluates Wagner's philosophy on <a href="/wiki/Tonality" title="Tonality">tonality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>; he admires Wagner's power to emote and express himself, but largely disdains what Nietzsche calls his religious biases.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirteen_sections">Thirteen sections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Thirteen sections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sections are as follows:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreword">Foreword</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foreword">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nietzsche explains that this book consists of selections from his previous writings. They show that he and Wagner are opposites. Nietzsche states that this book is for psychologists. He excludes Germans from his intended readers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Where_I_Admire">Where I Admire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Where I Admire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nietzsche admired Wagner's ability to express his own suffering and misery in short musical creations. He criticized Wagner's attempt to produce large works.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Where_I_Make_Objections">Where I Make Objections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Where I Make Objections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nietzsche’s objections to Wagner’s music were physical. His lungs, feet, stomach, heart, intestines, and throat were uncomfortably affected. He was disappointed to find that the music had no pleasing rhythm or melody. Nietzsche claimed that Wagner’s music was a mere means to enhance theatrical posing and gesturing. Wagner was more of an actor than a composer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intermezzo">Intermezzo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Intermezzo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nietzsche wants music to be cheerful, profound, unique, wanton, tender, roguish, and graceful. These qualities are lacking in German music, except for the works of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Frideric_Handel" title="Georg Frideric Handel">Handel</a>, and also in Wagner’s <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Idyll" title="Siegfried Idyll">Siegfried Idyll</a>. He praises <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Liszt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Chopin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_K%C3%B6selitz" title="Heinrich Köselitz">Peter Gast</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gioachino_Rossini" title="Gioachino Rossini">Rossini</a>, as well as all Venetian music. The Intermezzo ends with Nietzsche's poem “Venice.”</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wagner_as_Danger">Wagner as Danger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wagner as Danger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1. The concept of Wagner’s “unending melody” is used to designate what Nietzsche regards as the chaotic degeneration of rhythmic feeling. This tendency results in a dangerous use of music to merely produce a dramatic effect. 2. Wagner’s music tries to produce a physically jarring effect on the <a href="/wiki/Biedermeier" title="Biedermeier">Biedermeier</a> audience. Mozart’s opera ‘’Don Giovanni’’, in contrast, had a serious music that was cheerful and tender.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Music_Without_a_Future">A Music Without a Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: A Music Without a Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Music expresses the decline of a culture. Wagner’s music, in its relation to old Germanic and Scandinavian myths and sagas, is the song of a dying swan.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="We_Antipodes">We Antipodes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: We Antipodes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nietzsche admitted that he had mistakenly thought that <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Schopenhauer</a>’s philosophy was the result of a strong attachment to life that enabled him to acknowledge life’s horrors. He also thought that Wagner’s music expressed a vital life force. But Nietzsche later realized that he was only attributing his own qualities to those men’s works. Both Schopenhauer and Wagner were against life. Other such decadents were <a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicureans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Pascal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Flaubert</a>. Nietzsche, in contrast, celebrated the egoistical <a href="/wiki/Apollonian_and_Dionysian" title="Apollonian and Dionysian">Dionysian</a> Greek who affirmed life in spite of its many horrors and terrors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Where_Wagner_Belongs">Where Wagner Belongs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Where Wagner Belongs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike Germans, the sick, artistic French appreciate Schopenhauer and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heine</a> because they value pessimistic, refined culture. Wagner belongs with the French. He is more of a Parisian Romantic than a German. French art, like Wagner’s, is based on world literature. Wagner and the French have an expressive talent for producing sensational artistic effects. Both are sick artists who stupefy mass audiences with their spectacular dramatic shows.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wagner_as_Apostle_of_Chastity">Wagner as Apostle of Chastity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Wagner as Apostle of Chastity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1. Nietzsche warned, in poetic verse, that Wagner’s art is not Germanic. Instead, it is similar to Italy’s Roman Catholic religion. 2. Why did Wagner write Parsifal and present the contrast between sensuality and chastity? 3. Did Wagner, in old age, parody tragedy by freely showing a simple country boy as the ideal embodiment of ascetic chastity? If Parsifal was, however, meant seriously, then it is an expression of Wagner’s late hatred of sensuality, egotism, and life. It would then be considered to be bad art.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="How_I_was_Set_Free_from_Wagner">How I was Set Free from Wagner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: How I was Set Free from Wagner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1. By 1876, Wagner had moved to Germany and had become a decaying anti–Semitic Christian. Nietzsche expressed his disappointment and feeling of loss. 2. Nietzsche then became a solitary, courageous pessimist and he completely dedicated himself to his life’s arduous task.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Psychologist_has_a_Word">The Psychologist has a Word</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Psychologist has a Word">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1. Sympathy interferes with the psychological analysis of great, higher humans. Psychologists should be cheerfully unsympathetic. Revered, great people always eventually decay. This realization might remind the psychologist of his own decadence and may contribute to his own corruption. The great human’s work, not his own person, should be venerated. 2. Great artists and other higher humans create works in order to forget their own decadent flaws. Revering higher people with feminine sympathy is detrimental to them. 3. When a higher human knows deep, heart–breaking suffering, there develops immunity to receiving sympathy from lower humans. Noble, profound sufferers feign cheerfulness in order to ward off unwanted pity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epilogue">Epilogue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Epilogue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1. From a universal perspective, deep suffering is necessary, healthful, and beneficial, if it doesn’t kill. Great pain is useful and should be welcomed. <i>Amor fati</i> [love your fate]. It makes a philosopher deeply profound. Questions arise concerning beloved life itself. 2. After experiencing profound pain, a taste for artificial, cheering art is acquired. The horror of life is ignored. Like the ancient Dionysian Greeks, we have known the terrible truth about life and now appreciate the effects of an artist’s false, wonderful tones, fictional words, and fascinating forms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_Poverty_of_the_Richest">On the Poverty of the Richest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nietzsche_contra_Wagner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: On the Poverty of the Richest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a "Dionysian Dithyramb," Nietzsche used poetic imagery from <a href="/wiki/Thus_Spoke_Zarathustra" title="Thus Spoke Zarathustra">Thus Spoke Zarathustra</a>. This is an example of his new taste for cheerful art after having known the seriousness and horror of life. Following many years of solitude, Zarathustra now wants to share the riches of his wisdom. In order to be loved and appreciated, he will leave his lonely cave in order to generously and completely give of himself.</p>
