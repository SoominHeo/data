<p><b>Nicola Griffith</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/Éª/ short 'i' in 'bid'">Éª</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/É/ 'a' in 'about'">É</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/É/ 'a' in 'about'">É</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">É¡</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/Éª/ short 'i' in 'bid'">Éª</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/Éª/ short 'i' in 'bid'">Éª</span><span title="/Ã°/ 'th' in 'thy'">Ã°</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born 30 September 1960 in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, England) is a British-American<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> novelist, essayist, and teacher. Griffith has won the <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Book_Award" title="Washington State Book Award">Washington State Book Award</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebula_Award" title="Nebula Award">Nebula Award</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Tiptree,_Jr._Award" title="James Tiptree, Jr. Award">James Tiptree, Jr. Award</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Fantasy_Award" title="World Fantasy Award">World Fantasy Award</a> and six <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Literary_Award" title="Lambda Literary Award">Lambda Literary Awards</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicola_Griffith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Griffith was born in Leeds, UK, to Margaret Mary and Eric Percival Griffith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> Her parentsâwhom she describes in her 2007 memoir, <i>And Now We Are Going to Have a Party</i>, as wanting "to belong to the middle of the middle class â¦ to fit in" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup>âreared Griffith and her four sisters in the Catholic faith. Her earliest surviving literary efforts include an illustrated booklet she was encouraged to create to prevent her from making trouble among her fellow nursery school students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:17</sup> At age eleven she won a BBC student poetry prize and read aloud her winning work for radio broadcast.</p>
<p>As a pre-teen Griffith felt same-sex attractions, and by sometime in her thirteenth year, she knew: "I was a dyke." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hugo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hugo-3">[3]</a></sup> She also felt cautioned by her parents' punishing response after one of her sisters acted on such desires at age fifteen. Thus her conclusion that "no hint of how I felt must be allowed â¦. Not until I reached sixteen,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:2</sup> when she would no longer be a minor. To cope she began to drinkâalcohol served as a useful suppressant. She drank, smoked cigarettes on the sly, and immersed herself in reading and music in search of escape. In addition to the classics of English literature, she read the works of such novelists as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Treece" title="Henry Treece">Henry Treece</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aud_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aud-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Sutcliff" title="Rosemary Sutcliff">Rosemary Sutcliff</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94int_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-94int-6">[6]</a></sup> fantastic fiction including the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.E._Smith" title="E.E. Smith">E.E. Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>; nonfiction about life sciences and historyâGibbon's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Decline_and_Fall_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire">History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire</a> was a particular favorite;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aud_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aud-4">[4]</a></sup> and such poetry as Homer's <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Masefield" title="John Masefield">John Masefield</a>'s Cargoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Her musical choices showed a similar divide: the classical canon, traditional church compositions, and folk music offset by <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rockers</a>. During that time a visit to relatives in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, Scotlandâin particular a behind-the-scenes tour of a power station, with its efficient water recycling systemâleft Griffith feeling "terribly alert." She paid more attention thereafter to the occasional school course that interested herâchemistry, physics, and biology especiallyâand at age fourteen broadened her artistic tastes to encompass the works of William S. Burroughs, Led Zeppelin, and early Pink Floyd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3â5</sup></p>
<p>When Griffith was fifteen, she recognised her love for a female friend, Una Fitzgerald.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup> Once the two girls were of age, they embarked upon an all-consuming romance. After almost two years, they realised their differences, and Fitzgerald left Griffith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hugo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hugo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>At this point Griffith began an extended tour of Leeds' after-hours underbelly, even as her sister Helena developed a drug habit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:37â39</sup> During this phase Griffith met Carol Taylor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-8">[8]</a></sup> and the two became longtime partners. Griffith moved out of her parents' household in Leeds and relocated to Hull, where she and Taylor initially lived a marginal existence. Recreational drugs became Griffith's default setting. Nonetheless she states that in Hull, "My real education began."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:41â56</sup></p>
<p>Griffith got to know "feminists and intellectuals â¦ bikers and drug dealers, and dykes pimping out their girlfriends." She found her first women's community there, and she read "earnest feminist fiction" as part of her regular use of Hull's central library. After the 1981 founding of the band Janes Plane, Griffith began to write her own words as its lead singer and lyricist. The group, a five-woman ensemble, played its first gig at an International Women's Day celebration in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:46â58</sup> Janes Plane achieved some local notoriety and performed in several North England cities and on national TV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Griffith attempted her first fiction after the group disbanded. In 1983 Griffith wrote a diary entry detailing her dreams of becoming a "best-seller." She was writing her first (unpublished) novel, called <i>Greenstorm</i>. Griffith began studying the physical art of self-defense the next year, and in August 1984 she smoked her last cigarette. The following month she gave up hashish and amphetamines. She received rejections of her manuscript from two publishers. Elements later to appear in <i>Ammonite</i> arose in a second unpublished (this one also unsubmitted) novel, "We Are Paradise" (ca. 1985).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8â22, 74</sup></p>
<p>Griffith suffered some personal setbacks that had roots in 1985. By that time Helena had gone from addiction to also dealing heroin and amphetamines. As the year ended, Griffith (already sick with influenza) was hurt and briefly hospitalised after helping another women in a bar assault. Delayed reaction to the attack contributed to what she later characterised as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PTSD" title="PTSD">PTSD</a> in June 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Her writing and a women's self-defense course that she was teaching sustained her amid these difficulties, and Helena's counterexample helped persuade Griffith that the time to abandon all recreational drug useâincluding "magic mushrooms," which she had relied on extensivelyâhad come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:45â50</sup></p>
<p>By late 1987 Griffith had made her first professional fiction sale, of a short story, "Mirrors and Burnstone," to <a href="/wiki/Interzone_(magazine)" title="Interzone (magazine)">Interzone</a>. She was also experiencing symptoms of multiple sclerosis, though her illness remained unrecognised. Before her starting a job at the Unemployed Advice Centre, Griffith travelled with Taylor to <a href="/wiki/Whitby_Abbey" title="Whitby Abbey">Whitby Abbey</a>, which she names as the site of one her happiest days ever. But traditional life made Griffith restless. To escape, she applied for two different international courses of study: one at a womenâs martial arts camp in the Netherlands, one at the <a href="/wiki/Clarion_Workshop" title="Clarion Workshop">Clarion Workshop</a> at <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:9â13</sup></p>
<p>Clarion accepted herâwith the added inducement of a scholarship. Griffith crossed the Atlantic to attend Clarion in 1988. There, while she was studying with such authors as <a href="/wiki/Kim_Stanley_Robinson" title="Kim Stanley Robinson">Kim Stanley Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Wilhelm" title="Kate Wilhelm">Kate Wilhelm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Powers" title="Tim Powers">Tim Powers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_R._Delany" title="Samuel R. Delany">Samuel R. Delany</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Griffith met and fell in love with writer <a href="/wiki/Kelley_Eskridge" title="Kelley Eskridge">Kelley Eskridge</a>. A quarter-million-word correspondence between the two women ensued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Party1_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Party1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13â21</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicola_Griffith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Griffith was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94int_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-94int-6">[6]</a></sup> in March 1993. She lives with her wife, author <a href="/wiki/Kelley_Eskridge" title="Kelley Eskridge">Kelley Eskridge</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicola_Griffith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nicola Griffith published her first novel <i><a href="/wiki/Ammonite_(novel)" title="Ammonite (novel)">Ammonite</a></i> in 1993. It won both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Tiptree,_Jr._Award" title="James Tiptree, Jr. Award">James Tiptree, Jr. Award</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Literary_Award" title="Lambda Literary Award">Lambda Award</a>, and was short-listed for the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke_Award" title="Arthur C. Clarke Award">Arthur C. Clarke Award</a>, <a href="/wiki/BSFA_Award" title="BSFA Award">BSFA Award</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Locus_Award" title="Locus Award">Locus Award</a>. Her second novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Slow_River" title="Slow River">Slow River</a></i> (1994), won the <a href="/wiki/Nebula_Award" title="Nebula Award">Nebula Award</a>, for best novel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and another Lambda.</p>
<p>Together with Stephen Pagel, Griffith has edited a series of three anthologies, <i>Bending the Landscape: Fantasy</i> (1997), <i>Bending the Landscape: Science Fiction</i> (1998) and <i>Bending the Landscape: Horror</i> (2001).</p>
<p><i>The Blue Place</i> (1998), <i><a href="/wiki/Stay_(novel)" title="Stay (novel)">Stay</a></i> (2002), and <i>Always</i> (2007) are linked novels about the character Aud Torvingen. Griffith's collection of stories, <i>With Her Body</i> (2004), comprises three short works. Her lone book of nonfiction, <i>And Now We Are Going to Have a Party: Liner Notes to a Writer's Early Life</i> (2007), won the Lambda Literary Award in the Women's Memoir/Biography category. It is a multimedia memoir, a "do-it-yourself Nicola Griffith home assembly kit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Griffith's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Hild_(novel)" title="Hild (novel)">Hild</a></i> was published in November 2013 by <a href="/wiki/Farrar,_Straus_and_Giroux" title="Farrar, Straus and Giroux">Farrar, Straus and Giroux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It is set in seventh-century England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Jim_Duggins_Outstanding_Mid-Career_Novelists%27_Prize" title="Jim Duggins Outstanding Mid-Career Novelists' Prize">Jim Duggins Outstanding Mid-Career Novelists' Prize</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Literary_Foundation" title="Lambda Literary Foundation">Lambda Literary Foundation</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
