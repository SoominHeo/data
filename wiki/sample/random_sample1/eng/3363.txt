<p><b>Nicolas Flamel</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[nikɔla flamɛl]</a></span>; prob. <a href="/wiki/Pontoise" title="Pontoise">Pontoise</a>, ca 1330 – <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, March 22, 1418)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a successful <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Scrivener" title="Scrivener">scribe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscript</a>-seller. After his death, Flamel developed a reputation as an <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemist</a> believed to have discovered the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone" title="Philosopher's stone">Philosopher's Stone</a> and achieved <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortality</a>. These legendary accounts only appeared in the seventeenth century.</p>
<p>According to texts ascribed to Flamel almost two hundred years after his death, he had learned alchemical secrets from a <a href="/wiki/Wandering_Jew" title="Wandering Jew">Jewish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">converso</a></i> on the road to <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a>. As Deborah Harkness put it, "Others thought Flamel was the creation of 17th-century editors and publishers desperate to produce modern printed editions of supposedly ancient alchemical treatises then circulating in manuscript for an avid reading public."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He has since appeared as a legendary alchemist in various fictional works.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas_Flamel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1324" data-file-width="1000" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg/150px-Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg/225px-Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg/300px-Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pierre_tombale_de_Nicolas_Flamel.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tombstone of Nicolas Flamel, 1418, Paris, Musée de Cluny.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The historical Flamel lived in Paris in the fourteenth and fifteenth century and his life is one of the best documented in the history of medieval alchemy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvii_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvii-3">[3]</a></sup> He ran two shops as a scribe and married <a href="/wiki/Perenelle_Flamel" title="Perenelle Flamel">Perenelle</a> in 1368. She brought the wealth of two previous husbands to the marriage. The French <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> couple owned several properties, and contributed financially to churches, sometimes by commissioning sculptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvi_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvi-4">[4]</a></sup> Later in life they were noted for their wealth and philanthropy.</p>
<p>Flamel lived into his 80s, and in 1410 designed his own <a href="/wiki/Headstone" title="Headstone">tombstone</a>, which was carved with the images of Christ, St. Peter, and St. Paul. The tombstone is preserved at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_de_Cluny" title="Musée de Cluny">Musée de Cluny</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. Records show that Flamel died in 1418.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was buried in Paris at the Musée de Cluny at the end of the nave of the former <a href="/wiki/Saint-Jacques_Tower" title="Saint-Jacques Tower">Church of Saint-Jacques-de-la-Boucherie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hauck2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hauck2008-6">[6]</a></sup> His will, dated 22 November 1416, indicates that he was generous but that he did not have the extraordinary wealth of later alchemical legend. There is no indication that the real Flamel of history was involved in alchemy, pharmacy or medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvii_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvii-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Flamel was a real person, and he may have dabbled in alchemy, but his reputation as an author and immortal adept must be accepted as an invention of the seventeenth century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvii_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xvii-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicolas_Flamel_in_Paris">Nicolas Flamel in Paris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas_Flamel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nicolas Flamel in Paris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of Flamel's houses still stands in Paris, at 51 <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_Montmorency" title="Rue de Montmorency">rue de Montmorency</a>. It is the oldest stone house in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There is an old inscription on the wall: "We, ploughmen and women living at the porch of this house, built in 1407, are requested to say every day an 'Our Father' and an 'Ave Maria' praying God that His grace forgive poor and dead sinners." The ground floor currently contains a restaurant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fronmer_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fronmer-8">[8]</a></sup> A Paris street near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louvre_Museum" title="Louvre Museum">Louvre Museum</a>, the <i>rue Nicolas Flamel</i>, has been named after him; it intersects with the <i>rue Pernelle</i>, named after his wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fronmer_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-fronmer-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_reputation_as_an_alchemist">Posthumous reputation as an alchemist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas_Flamel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Posthumous reputation as an alchemist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nicholasflamel.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="349" data-file-width="234" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Nicholasflamel.png/150px-Nicholasflamel.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Nicholasflamel.png/225px-Nicholasflamel.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Nicholasflamel.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nicholasflamel.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This imaginative portrait of Nicolas Flamel dates from the nineteenth century.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Legendary accounts of Flamel's life are based on seventeenth century works, primarily <i>Livre des figures hiéroglyphiques</i>. The essence of his reputation are claims that he succeeded at the two goals of <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>: that he made the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone" title="Philosopher's stone">Philosopher's Stone</a>, which turns base metals into <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, and that he and his wife <a href="/wiki/Perenelle_Flamel" title="Perenelle Flamel">Perenelle</a> achieved <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortality</a> through the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elixir_of_Life" title="Elixir of Life">Elixir of Life</a>".</p>
<p>An alchemical book, published in Paris in 1612 as <i>Livre des figures hiéroglyphiques</i> and in London in 1624 as <i>Exposition of the Hieroglyphical Figures</i> was attributed to Flamel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is a collection of designs purportedly commissioned by Flamel for a <a href="/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)" title="Tympanum (architecture)">tympanum</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_des_Innocents" title="Cimetière des Innocents">Cimetière des Innocents</a> in Paris, long disappeared at the time the work was published. In the publisher's introduction Flamel's search for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Philosopher's Stone">Philosopher's Stone</a> was described. According to that introduction, Flamel had made it his life's work to understand the text of a mysterious 21-page book he had purchased. The introduction claims that, around 1378, he travelled to Spain for assistance with translation. On the way back, he reported that he met a sage, who identified Flamel's book as being a copy of the original <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Abramelin" title="The Book of Abramelin">Book of Abramelin the Mage</a></i>. With this knowledge, over the next few years, Flamel and his wife allegedly decoded enough of the book to successfully replicate its recipe for the Philosopher's Stone, producing first <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> in 1382, and then <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. In addition, Flamel is said to have studied some texts in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>.</p>
<p>The validity of this story was first questioned in 1761 by Etienne Villain. He claimed that the source of the Flamel legend was P. Arnauld de la Chevalerie, publisher of <i>Exposition of the Hieroglyphical Figures</i>, who wrote the book under the pseudonym Eiranaeus Orandus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xiv_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDixon1994xiv-10">[10]</a></sup> Other writers have defended the legendary account of Flamel’s life, which has been embellished by stories of sightings in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries and expanded in fictitious works ever since.</p>
<p>Flamel had achieved legendary status within the circles of alchemy by the mid 17th Century, with references in <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>'s journals to "the Caduceus, the Dragons of Flammel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keynes-MS-56_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keynes-MS-56-11">[11]</a></sup> Interest in Flamel revived in the 19th century; <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a> mentioned him in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hunchback_of_Notre_Dame" title="The Hunchback of Notre Dame">The Hunchback of Notre Dame</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Erik_Satie" title="Erik Satie">Erik Satie</a> was intrigued by Flamel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Pike" title="Albert Pike">Albert Pike</a> makes reference to Nicholas Flamel in his book <i>Morals and Dogma of the Scottish Rite of Freemasonry</i>. Flamel's reputation as an alchemist was further bolstered in the late 20th Century by his depiction as the creator of the eponymous alchemical substance in the best-selling novel <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone">Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NLM_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NLM-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas_Flamel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li>Flamel appears in the 1995 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Indiana Jones and the Philosopher's Stone">Indiana Jones and the Philosopher's Stone</a></i> as the owner of the Stone.</li>
<li>Flamel is an unseen character in the 1997 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone">Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone</a></i>, portrayed as <a href="/wiki/Albus_Dumbledore" title="Albus Dumbledore">Albus Dumbledore</a>'s friend who has used the stone to stay alive for over 660 years.</li>

<li>Flamel is a main character in <a href="/wiki/Jeri_Westerson" title="Jeri Westerson">Jeri Westerson</a>'s 2013 novel "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow_of_the_Alchemist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shadow of the Alchemist (page does not exist)">Shadow of the Alchemist</a>".</li>
<li>Flamel is mentioned frequently in the 2014 horror film <i><a href="/wiki/As_Above,_So_Below_(film)" title="As Above, So Below (film)">As Above, So Below</a></i>.</li>
<li>Flamel is mentioned in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Librarians_(2014_TV_series)" title="The Librarians (2014 TV series)">The Librarians</a></i> 2014 episode "And the Horns of a Dilemma" as one of the immortal beings who could be wounded but not killed.</li>
<li>Flamel is mentioned in the 2014 video game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assassin%27s_Creed:_Unity" title="Assassin's Creed: Unity">Assassin's Creed: Unity</a></i>, in which the protagonist must obtain an alchemical potion said to be crafted by him. He is also mentioned in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assassin%27s_Creed:_Project_Legacy" title="Assassin's Creed: Project Legacy">Assassin's Creed: Project Legacy</a></i></li>
<li>Flamel is mentioned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.J._Rose" title="M.J. Rose">M.J. Rose</a>'s novel The Witch of Painted Sorrows.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
