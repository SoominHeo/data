<p><b>Nicholas Negroponte</b> (born December 1, 1943) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_American" title="Greek American">Greek American</a> architect. He is the founder and Chairman Emeritus of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>'s <a href="/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab" title="MIT Media Lab">Media Lab</a>, and also founded the <a href="/wiki/One_Laptop_per_Child" title="One Laptop per Child">One Laptop per Child</a> Association (OLPC).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Negroponte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Negroponte was born to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dimitri_John&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dimitri John (page does not exist)">Dimitri John</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://el.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%94%CE%B7%CE%BC%CE%AE%CF%84%CF%81%CE%B9%CE%BF%CF%82_%CE%9D%CE%B5%CE%B3%CF%81%CE%B5%CF%80%CF%8C%CE%BD%CF%84%CE%B7%CF%82" title="el:Δημήτριος Νεγρεπόντης">el</a>)</span> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Νεγροπόντης</span>), a Greek shipping magnate, and grew up in New York City's <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a>. He is the younger brother of <a href="/wiki/John_Negroponte" title="John Negroponte">John Negroponte</a>, former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Deputy_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Deputy Secretary of State">United States Deputy Secretary of State</a>. Another brother <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Negroponte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Negroponte (page does not exist)">Michael Negroponte</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a>-winning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filmmaker" title="Filmmaker">filmmaker</a>, and his other brother, George Negroponte, is an <a href="/wiki/Artist" title="Artist">artist</a> and was President of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Drawing_Center" title="The Drawing Center">the Drawing Center</a> from 2002-2007.</p>
<p>He attended <a href="/wiki/Buckley_School_(New_York_City)" title="Buckley School (New York City)">Buckley School</a> in New York City, <a href="/wiki/Institut_Le_Rosey" title="Institut Le Rosey">Le Rosey</a> in Switzerland, and <a href="/wiki/Choate_Rosemary_Hall" title="Choate Rosemary Hall">The Choate School</a> (now Choate Rosemary Hall) in <a href="/wiki/Wallingford,_Connecticut" title="Wallingford, Connecticut">Wallingford, Connecticut</a>, from which he graduated in 1961. Subsequently, he studied at <a href="/wiki/MIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Planning" title="MIT School of Architecture and Planning">MIT</a> as both an undergraduate and graduate student in <a href="/wiki/MIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Planning" title="MIT School of Architecture and Planning">Architecture</a> where his research focused on issues of <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a>. He earned a master's degree in architecture from <a href="/wiki/MIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Planning" title="MIT School of Architecture and Planning">MIT</a> in 1966. Despite his accomplished academic career, Negroponte has spoken publicly about his <a href="/wiki/Dyslexia" title="Dyslexia">dyslexia</a> and his difficulty reading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Negroponte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MIT">MIT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Negroponte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: MIT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Negroponte joined the faculty of <a href="/wiki/MIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Planning" title="MIT School of Architecture and Planning">MIT</a> in 1966. For several years thereafter he divided his teaching time between <a href="/wiki/MIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Planning" title="MIT School of Architecture and Planning">MIT</a> and several visiting professorships at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">Michigan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>.</p>
<p>In 1967, Negroponte founded <a href="/wiki/MIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Planning" title="MIT School of Architecture and Planning">MIT</a>'s Architecture Machine Group, a combination lab and think tank which studied new approaches to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-computer_interaction" title="Human-computer interaction">human-computer interaction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1985, Negroponte created the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab" title="MIT Media Lab">MIT Media Lab</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerome_B._Wiesner" title="Jerome B. Wiesner">Jerome B. Wiesner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As director, he developed the lab into the pre-eminent computer science laboratory for new media and a high-tech playground for investigating the human-computer interface. Negroponte also became a proponent of <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">intelligent agents</a> and personalized <a href="/wiki/Online_newspaper" title="Online newspaper">electronic newspapers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> for which he popularized the term the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Me" title="Daily Me">Daily Me</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wired"><i>Wired</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Negroponte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wired">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, Negroponte became involved in the creation of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wired_Magazine" title="Wired Magazine">Wired Magazine</a></i> as the first investor. From 1993 to 1998, he contributed a monthly column to the magazine in which he reiterated a basic theme: "Move bits, not atoms."</p>
<p>Negroponte expanded many of the ideas from his <i>Wired</i> columns into a bestselling book <i><a href="/wiki/Being_Digital" title="Being Digital">Being Digital</a></i> (1995),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which made famous his forecasts on how the interactive world, the entertainment world and the information world would eventually merge. <i>Being Digital</i> was a bestseller and was translated into some twenty languages. Negroponte is a digital optimist who believed that computers would make life better for everyone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, critics such as <a href="/wiki/Cass_Sunstein" title="Cass Sunstein">Cass Sunstein</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> have criticised his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Techno-utopianism" title="Techno-utopianism">techno-utopian</a> ideas for failing to consider the historical, political and cultural realities with which new technologies should be viewed.</p>
<p>In the 1980s Negroponte predicted that wired technologies such as telephones would become unwired by using airwaves instead of wires or fiber optics, and that unwired technologies such as televisions would become wired—a prediction commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Negroponte_switch" title="Negroponte switch">Negroponte switch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Negroponte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, Negroponte stepped down as director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_Lab" title="Media Lab">Media Lab</a> as <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bender" title="Walter Bender">Walter Bender</a> took over as Executive Director. However, Negroponte retained the role of laboratory Chairman. When <a href="/wiki/Frank_Moss_(technologist)" title="Frank Moss (technologist)">Frank Moss</a> was appointed director of the lab in 2006, Negroponte stepped down as lab chairman to focus more fully on his work with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Children%27s_Machine" title="The Children's Machine">One Laptop Per Child</a> (OLPC) although he retains his appointment as professor at MIT.</p>

<p>In November 2005, at the <a href="/wiki/World_Summit_on_the_Information_Society" title="World Summit on the Information Society">World Summit on the Information Society</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>, Negroponte unveiled the concept of a $100 laptop computer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Children%27s_Machine" title="The Children's Machine">The Children's Machine</a>, designed for students in the developing world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The price has increased to US$180, however. The project is part of a broader program by One Laptop Per Child, a non-profit organisation started by Negroponte and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_Lab" title="Media Lab">Media Lab</a> faculty, to extend <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> access in developing countries.</p>
<p>Negroponte is an active <a href="/wiki/Angel_investor" title="Angel investor">angel investor</a> and has invested in over 30 startup companies over the last 30 years, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zagats" title="Zagats">Zagats</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wired_magazine" title="Wired magazine">Wired</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambient_Devices" title="Ambient Devices">Ambient Devices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skype" title="Skype">Skype</a> and <a href="/wiki/Velti" title="Velti">Velti</a>. He sits on several boards, including <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a> (listed on the New York Stock Exchange) and <a href="/wiki/Velti" title="Velti">Velti</a> (listed on the NASDAQ and formerly on the London Stock Exchange<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> ). He is also on the advisory board of <a href="/wiki/TTI/Vanguard" title="TTI/Vanguard">TTI/Vanguard</a>. In August 2007, he was appointed to a five-member special committee with the objective of assuring the continued journalistic and editorial integrity and independence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_%26_Company" title="Dow Jones &amp; Company">Dow Jones &amp; Company</a> publications and services. The committee was formed as part of the merger of Dow Jones with <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Negroponte's fellow founding committee members are <a href="/wiki/Louis_Boccardi" title="Louis Boccardi">Louis Boccardi</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Bray_(writer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Bray (writer) (page does not exist)">Thomas Bray</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Fuller_(publisher)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jack Fuller (publisher) (page does not exist)">Jack Fuller</a>, and the late former Congresswoman <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Dunn_(politician)" title="Jennifer Dunn (politician)">Jennifer Dunn</a>.</p>
<p>Negroponte has influenced modern day futurists, such as <a href="/wiki/David_Houle_(futurist)" title="David Houle (futurist)">David Houle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
