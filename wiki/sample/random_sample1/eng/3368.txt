<p><b>Nikolaus Harnoncourt</b> (<b>Johann Nikolaus Graf</b> (<b>Count</b>) <b>de la Fontaine und d’Harnoncourt-Unverzagt</b>; 6 December 1929 – 5 March 2016) was an Austrian <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a>, particularly known for his <a href="/wiki/Historically_informed_performance" title="Historically informed performance">historically informed performances</a> of music from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_period_(music)" title="Classical period (music)">Classical era</a> and earlier. Starting out as a classical cellist, he founded his own period instrument ensemble, <a href="/wiki/Concentus_Musicus_Wien" title="Concentus Musicus Wien">Concentus Musicus Wien</a>, in the 1950s, and became a pioneer of the Early Music movement. Around 1970, Harnoncourt started to conduct opera and concert performances, soon leading renowned international symphony orchestras, and appearing at leading concert halls, operatic venues and festivals. His repertoire then widened to include composers of the 19th and 20th centuries. In 2001 and 2003, he conducted the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_New_Year%27s_Concert" title="Vienna New Year's Concert">Vienna New Year's Concert</a>. Harnoncourt was also the author of several books, mostly on subjects of performance history and musical aesthetics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Harnoncourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harnoncourt was born Johann Nikolaus Graf de la Fontaine und d’Harnoncourt-Unverzagt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Germany</a>. His mother, Ladislaja <a href="/wiki/Franz,_Count_of_Meran" title="Franz, Count of Meran">Gräfin von Meran</a>, Freiin von Brandhoven, was the great-granddaughter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archduke_Johann" title="Archduke Johann">Archduke Johann</a>, the 13th child of the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold II, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold II</a>, making him a descendant of various <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperors</a> and other European royalty. His father, Eberhard de la Fontaine <a href="/wiki/Graf" title="Graf">Graf</a> d'Harnoncourt-Unverzagt, was an engineer working in Berlin who had two children from a previous marriage. Two years after Nikolaus's birth, his brother Philipp was born. The family eventually moved to Graz, where Eberhard had obtained a post in the state government (<i>Landesregierung</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a>.</p>
<p>Harnoncourt was raised in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, and studied music in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. During his youth, he served in the Hitler Youth under duress, where, as he noted:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oestreich_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oestreich-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If you didn't go there every Wednesday and Saturday, the Hitler Youth police would come, fetch you, cut your hair and toss you into a group with other difficult ones who were treated terribly.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna_Music_Academy" title="Vienna Music Academy">Vienna Music Academy</a>, Harnoncourt studied cello with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gr%C3%BCmmer" title="Paul Grümmer">Paul Grümmer</a> and Emanuel Brabec, and also learned viola da gamba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millington_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Millington-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Harnoncourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harnoncourt was a <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cellist</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Symphony" title="Vienna Symphony">Vienna Symphony</a> from 1952 to 1969. In 1953, he founded the period-instrument ensemble <a href="/wiki/Concentus_Musicus_Wien" title="Concentus Musicus Wien">Concentus Musicus Wien</a> with his wife, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Harnoncourt" title="Alice Harnoncourt">Alice Hoffelner</a>, whom he married that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millington_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Millington-1">[1]</a></sup> The Concentus Musicus Wien was dedicated to performances on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authentic_performance" title="Authentic performance">period instruments</a>, and by the 1970s his work with it had made him quite well known. He played the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viola_da_gamba" title="Viola da gamba">viola da gamba</a> at this time, as well as the cello. For the <a href="/wiki/Telefunken" title="Telefunken">Telefunken</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Teldec" title="Teldec">Teldec</a>) label, Harnoncourt recorded a wide variety of the Baroque repertoire, beginning with the viol music of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Purcell" title="Henry Purcell">Henry Purcell</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and extending to include works like Bach's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Musical_Offering" title="The Musical Offering">The Musical Offering</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Monteverdi's <i><a href="/wiki/L%27incoronazione_di_Poppea" title="L'incoronazione di Poppea">L'incoronazione di Poppea</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and Rameau's <i><a href="/wiki/Castor_et_Pollux" title="Castor et Pollux">Castor et Pollux</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> One of his final recordings with the Concentus Musicus Wien was of Beethoven's <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 4 (Beethoven)">Symphonies Nos. 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven)">5</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>One reason that Harnoncourt left the Vienna Symphony was to become a conductor. He made his conducting debut at <a href="/wiki/La_Scala" title="La Scala">La Scala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, in 1970, leading a production of Monteverdi's <i><a href="/wiki/Il_ritorno_d%27Ulisse_in_patria" title="Il ritorno d'Ulisse in patria">Il ritorno d'Ulisse in patria</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, Harnoncourt started a joint project with conductor <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Leonhardt" title="Gustav Leonhardt">Gustav Leonhardt</a> to record all of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Bach_cantatas" title="List of Bach cantatas">J. S. Bach's cantatas</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bach_cantatas_(Teldec)" title="Bach cantatas (Teldec)">Teldec Bach cantata project</a> was eventually completed in 1990 and was the only cantata cycle to use an all-male choir and soloist roster, with the exception of cantatas nos. <a href="/wiki/Jauchzet_Gott_in_allen_Landen,_BWV_51" title="Jauchzet Gott in allen Landen, BWV 51">51</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mein_Herze_schwimmt_im_Blut,_BWV_199" title="Mein Herze schwimmt im Blut, BWV 199">199</a>, which were intended for a female soprano voice. He also made the first recordings in historically informed performance of Bach's <a href="/wiki/Mass_in_B_minor_discography#Harnoncourt" title="Mass in B minor discography">Mass in B minor</a> (1968) and <i><a href="/wiki/St_Matthew_Passion_discography#Harnoncourt" title="St Matthew Passion discography">St Matthew Passion</a></i> (1970). In 2001 a critically acclaimed and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> winning <a href="/wiki/St_Matthew_Passion_discography#Harnoncourt_2000" title="St Matthew Passion discography">recording of the <i>St Matthew Passion</i></a> with the <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg_Choir" title="Arnold Schoenberg Choir">Arnold Schoenberg Choir</a> was released, which included the entire score of the piece in Bach's own hand on a <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a>. This was his third recording of the work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harnoncourt later performed with many other orchestras that played on modern instruments, but still with an eye on historical authenticity in terms of <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">dynamics</a>, among other things. He also expanded his repertoire, continuing to play the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">baroque</a> works, but also championing the <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Viennese</a> <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">operetta</a> repertoire. He made a benchmark recording of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphonies_(Beethoven)" title="Symphonies (Beethoven)">Beethoven's symphonies</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_Orchestra_of_Europe" title="Chamber Orchestra of Europe">Chamber Orchestra of Europe</a> (COE),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and recorded Beethoven's <a href="/wiki/Piano_concerto" title="Piano concerto">piano concertos</a> with <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Laurent_Aimard" title="Pierre-Laurent Aimard">Pierre-Laurent Aimard</a> and the COE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harnoncourt was also a guest conductor of the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Philharmonic" title="Vienna Philharmonic">Vienna Philharmonic</a> and made several recordings with the orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Between 1987 and 1991, he conducted four new productions of Mozart operas at the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_State_Opera" title="Vienna State Opera">Vienna State Opera</a> (1987–91: <i><a href="/wiki/Idomeneo" title="Idomeneo">Idomeneo</a></i>; 1988–90: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_Flute" title="The Magic Flute">Die Zauberflöte</a></i>; 1989: <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Entf%C3%BChrung_aus_dem_Serail" title="Die Entführung aus dem Serail">Die Entführung aus dem Serail</a></i>; 1989–91: <i><a href="/wiki/Cos%C3%AC_fan_tutte" title="Così fan tutte">Così fan tutte</a></i>). He directed the Vienna Philharmonic's New Year's Day concerts in 2001 and 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, Harnoncourt debuted at the <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Festival" title="Salzburg Festival">Salzburg Festival</a> conducting a concert with the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_Orchestra_of_Europe" title="Chamber Orchestra of Europe">Chamber Orchestra of Europe</a>. In the following years, he led several concerts with the Chamber Orchestra of Europe, the Vienna Philharmonic and the Concentus Musicus. Harnoncourt also served as the conductor for major opera productions of the Festival: <i>L'incoronazione di Poppea</i> (1993), Mozart's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_nozze_di_Figaro" title="Le nozze di Figaro">Le nozze di Figaro</a></i> (1995 and 2006), <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">Don Giovanni</a></i> (2002, marking also <a href="/wiki/Anna_Netrebko" title="Anna Netrebko">Anna Netrebko</a>'s international breakthrough as Donna Anna, and 2003) and <i><a href="/wiki/La_clemenza_di_Tito" title="La clemenza di Tito">La clemenza di Tito</a></i> (2003 and 2006), and Purcell's <i><a href="/wiki/King_Arthur_(opera)" title="King Arthur (opera)">King Arthur</a></i> (2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2012, Harnoncourt conducted a new production of <i>Die Zauberflöte</i> staged by <a href="/wiki/Jens-Daniel_Herzog" title="Jens-Daniel Herzog">Jens-Daniel Herzog</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harnoncourt made his guest-conducting debut with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Concertgebouw_Orchestra" title="Royal Concertgebouw Orchestra">Concertgebouw Orchestra, Amsterdam</a> in 1975. He continued as a guest conductor with the orchestra, including in several opera productions and recordings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In October 2000, the Royal Concertgebouw Orchestra (KCO) named him their <i>Honorair gastdirigent</i> (Honorary Guest Conductor). His final appearance with the KCO was in October 2013, leading Bruckner's <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Bruckner)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Bruckner)">Symphony No. 5</a>.</p>
<p>Other recordings outside of the baroque and classical era repertoire included his 2002 recording of Bruckner's <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Bruckner)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Bruckner)">Symphony No. 9</a> with the Vienna Philharmonic. An accompanying second CD contained a lecture by Harnoncourt about the symphony with musical examples, including the rarely heard fragments from the unfinished finale. In 2009, Harnoncourt recorded Gershwin's <i><a href="/wiki/Porgy_and_Bess" title="Porgy and Bess">Porgy and Bess</a></i>, taken from live performances at Graz.</p>
<p>On 5 December 2015, one day before his 86th birthday, Harnoncourt announced his retirement via his website. "My bodily strength requires me to cancel my future plans," he wrote in a hand-written letter inserted into the program on his 86th birthday of a concert by the Concentus Musicus Wien.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcobit_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcobit-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Harnoncourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harnoncourt is survived by his wife <a href="/wiki/Alice_Harnoncourt" title="Alice Harnoncourt">Alice</a>, a violinist, and three of their four children. The couple met through their mutual interest in <a href="/wiki/Historically_informed_performance" title="Historically informed performance">historically informed performances</a> of Baroque music and co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Concentus_Musicus_Wien" title="Concentus Musicus Wien">Concentus Musicus Wien</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millington_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Millington-1">[1]</a></sup> Their daughter is the mezzo-soprano <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_von_Magnus" title="Elisabeth von Magnus">Elisabeth von Magnus</a>. Their two surviving sons are Philipp and Franz. Their third son Eberhard, a violin maker, died in 1990 in an automobile accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harnoncourt died on 5 March 2016 in the village of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sankt_Georgen_im_Attergau" title="Sankt Georgen im Attergau">Sankt Georgen im Attergau</a>, north east of Salzburg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> His widow Alice, their three adult children, seven grandchildren, and three great-grandchildren survive him.</p>
