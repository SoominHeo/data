<p><b>Nikolai Aleksandrovich Tikhonov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Николай Александрович Тихонов</span>; <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a>, 14 May [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 1 May] 1905 – <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, 1 June 1997) was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_people" title="Soviet people">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians_in_Russia" title="Ukrainians in Russia">Russian-Ukrainian</a> statesman during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. He served as <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Premier of the Soviet Union">Chairman</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Soviet_Union)" title="Council of Ministers (Soviet Union)">Council of Ministers</a> from 1980 to 1985, and as a <a href="/wiki/First_Deputy_Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="First Deputy Premier of the Soviet Union">First Deputy Chairman</a> of the Council of Ministers, literally First Vice Premier, from 1976 to 1980. Tikhonov was responsible for the cultural and economic administration of the Soviet Union during the late <a href="/wiki/Era_of_Stagnation" title="Era of Stagnation">era of stagnation</a>. He was replaced as Chairman of the Council of Ministers in 1985 by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Ryzhkov" title="Nikolai Ryzhkov">Nikolai Ryzhkov</a>. In the same year, he lost his seat in the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Politburo</a>; however, he retained his seat in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee</a> until 1989.</p>
<p>He was born in the city of <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a> in 1905 to a Russian-Ukrainian <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working-class</a> family; he graduated in the 1920s and started working in the 1930s. Tikhonov began his political career in local industry, and worked his way up the hierarchy of Soviet industrial ministries. He was appointed deputy chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Gosplan" title="Gosplan">Gosplan</a> in 1963. After <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Kosygin" title="Alexei Kosygin">Alexei Kosygin</a>'s resignation Tikhonov was voted into office as Chairman of the Council of Ministers. In this position, he refrained from taking effective measures to reform the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_economy" title="Soviet economy">Soviet economy</a>, a need which was strongly evidenced during the early–mid-1980s. He retired from active politics in 1989 as a <a href="/wiki/Pensioner" title="Pensioner">pensioner</a>. Tikhonov died on 1 June 1997.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Tikhonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tikhonov was born in the Ukrainian city of <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a> on 14 May [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 1 May] 1905 to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_people" title="Russian people">Russian</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_people" title="Ukrainian people">Ukrainian</a> <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working-class</a> family; he graduated from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dnipropetrovsk_Institute_of_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dnipropetrovsk Institute of Communications (page does not exist)">St. Catherine Institute of Communications</a> in 1924. Tikhonov worked as an assistant engineer from 1924 to 1926. Four years later, in 1930, Tikhonov graduated as an <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>, earning a degree from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dnipropetrovsk_Metallurgical_Institute" title="Dnipropetrovsk Metallurgical Institute">Dnipropetrovsk Metallurgical Institute</a>. From 1930 to 1941, Tikhonov worked as an engineer at the Lenin Metallurgical Plant in <a href="/wiki/Dnipropetrovsk" title="Dnipropetrovsk">Dnipropetrovsk</a>; he was appointed as the plant's Chief Engineer in January 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was during his stay in Dnipropetrovsk that he met <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a>, a future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Leader of the Soviet Union">leader of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989119_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989119-2">[2]</a></sup> Tikhonov joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-Union_Communist_Party_(bolsheviks)" title="All-Union Communist Party (bolsheviks)">All-Union Communist Party (bolsheviks)</a> in 1940 and by the end of the decade, had secured a job as a plant director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shortbio_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-shortbio-3">[3]</a></sup> As a director, Tikhonov was able to show off his organisational skills; under his leadership the plant became the first in the region to reopen a hospital, organising dining rooms and restoring social clubs for workers caught up in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup> Tikhonov was quickly promoted, and started working for the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Ferrous_Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ministry of Ferrous Metallurgy (page does not exist)">Ministry of Ferrous Metallurgy</a> in the 1950s. Between 1955 and 1960 Tikhonov became a Deputy Minister of the Ministry of Ferrous Metallurgy, a member (and later chairman) of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scientific Council (page does not exist)">Scientific Council</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Soviet_Union)" title="Council of Ministers (Soviet Union)">Council of Ministers</a>, and finally, a deputy chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Gosplan" title="Gosplan">State Planning Committee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZemtsov198970_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZemtsov198970-4">[4]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/22nd_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="22nd Congress of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">22nd Party Congress</a> Tikhonov was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee</a> as a non-voting member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/23rd_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="23rd Congress of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">23rd party congress</a> in 1966, Tikhonov was elected a member of the Central Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup> Tikhonov was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Socialist_Labour" title="Hero of Socialist Labour">Hero of Socialist Labour</a> award for his first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shortbio_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-shortbio-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his tenure as <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Deputy Premier of the Soviet Union">Deputy Premier</a> Tikhonov was in charge of metallurgy and chemical industry; his responsibilities did not change with his ascension to the post of <a href="/wiki/First_Deputy_Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="First Deputy Premier of the Soviet Union">First Deputy Premier</a>. However, he did provide a general coordination for <a href="/wiki/Heavy_industry" title="Heavy industry">heavy industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Kosygin" title="Alexei Kosygin">Alexei Kosygin</a>, the Premier, was on sick leave in 1976 Brezhnev took advantage of his illness by appointing Tikhonov to the office of First Deputy Premier. As First Deputy Premier, Tikhonov was able to reduce Kosygin to a standby figure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989119_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989119-2">[2]</a></sup> Tikhonov was, however, one of the few who got along with both Brezhnev and Kosygin, both of them liked his candor and honesty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kommersant_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kommersant-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1978 Tikhonov was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_members_of_the_Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union_in_the_1970s" title="List of members of the Politburo of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union in the 1970s">elected a candidate member of the Politburo</a> and was made a voting member of the Politburo in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Tikhonov was not informed of the decision to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_war_in_Afghanistan" title="Soviet war in Afghanistan">intervene in Afghanistan</a>; the reason being his bad relationship with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmitriy_Ustinov" title="Dmitriy Ustinov">Dmitriy Ustinov</a>, the Minister of Defence at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kommersant_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kommersant-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Premiership_.281980.E2.80.9385.29">Premiership (1980–85)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Tikhonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Premiership (1980–85)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tikhonov%27s_First_Government" title="Tikhonov's First Government">Tikhonov's First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tikhonov%27s_Second_Government" title="Tikhonov's Second Government">Second Government</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment_and_the_26th_Congress">Appointment and the 26th Congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Tikhonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appointment and the 26th Congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Kosygin" title="Alexei Kosygin">Alexei Kosygin</a> resigned in 1980 Tikhonov, at the age of 75, was elected the new <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Premier of the Soviet Union">Chairman</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Soviet_Union)" title="Council of Ministers (Soviet Union)">Council of Ministers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> During his five-year term as premier Tikhonov refrained from reforming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_economy" title="Soviet economy">Soviet economy</a>, despite all statistics from that time showing the <a href="/wiki/Era_of_Stagnation" title="Era of Stagnation">economy was stagnating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup> Tikhonov presented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleventh_Five-Year_Plan_(Soviet_Union)" title="Eleventh Five-Year Plan (Soviet Union)">Eleventh Five-Year Plan</a> (1981–85) at the <a href="/wiki/26th_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="26th Congress of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">26th Party Congress</a>, and told the delegates that the state would allocate nine million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_rubles" title="Soviet rubles">rubles</a> for mothers who were seeking <a href="/wiki/Parental_leave" title="Parental leave">parental leave</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In his presentation to the congress, Tikhonov admitted that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_agriculture" title="Soviet agriculture">Soviet agriculture</a> was not producing enough <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a>. Tikhonov called for an improvement in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93US_relations" title="Soviet–US relations">Soviet–US relations</a>, but dismissed all speculations that the Soviet economy was in any sort of crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-10">[10]</a></sup> Despite this, Tikhonov admitted to economic "shortcomings" and acknowledged the ongoing "food problem"; other topics for discussion were the need to save energy resources, boost labour productivity and to improve the quality of Soviet produced goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Early in his term, in January 1981, Tikhonov admitted that the government's <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Demographics of the Soviet Union">demographic policy</a> was one of the weakest areas of <a href="/wiki/Tikhonov%27s_First_Government" title="Tikhonov's First Government">his cabinet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-service_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-service-12">[12]</a></sup> In reality, however, he along with many others, were beginning to worry that not enough Russians were being born. The <a href="/wiki/Era_of_Stagnation" title="Era of Stagnation">Era of Stagnation</a> reduced the <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rate</a>, and increased the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_rate" title="Death rate">death rate</a> of the Russian population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-service_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-service-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Andropov_and_Chernenko">Andropov and Chernenko</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Tikhonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Andropov and Chernenko">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a> awarded Tikhonov the <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Socialist_Labour" title="Hero of Socialist Labour">Hero of Socialist Labour</a>, after being advised to do so by <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Chernenko" title="Konstantin Chernenko">Konstantin Chernenko</a>. Upon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_and_funeral_of_Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Death and funeral of Leonid Brezhnev">Brezhnev's death</a> in 1982, Tikhonov supported Chernenko's candidacy for the <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">General Secretaryship</a>. Chernenko lost the vote, and <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a> became General Secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989131_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989131-13">[13]</a></sup> It has been suggested that Andropov had plans of replacing Tikhonov with <a href="/wiki/Heydar_Aliyev" title="Heydar Aliyev">Heydar Aliyev</a>. Historian William A. Clark noted how Aliyev, a former head of the Azerbaijani <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a>, was appointed to the <a href="/wiki/First_Deputy_Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="First Deputy Premier of the Soviet Union">First Deputy Premiership</a> of the Council of Ministers without Tikhonov's consent; however, Andropov's death in 1984 left Tikhonov secure in his office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some Western analysts speculated that the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Gromyko" title="Andrei Gromyko">Andrei Gromyko</a> to the First Deputy Premiership, again without Tikhonov's consent, was a sign that his position within the Soviet hierarchy was weakened. Tikhonov was on a state visit to <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> when Gromyko was appointed to the First Deputy Premiership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>With his health failing, Andropov used his spare times to write speeches to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee</a>. In one of these speeches Andropov told the Central Committee that <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>, and not Chernenko, would succeed him upon his death. His speech was not read out to the Central Committee plenum because of an anti-Gorbachev <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/troika" title="wikt:troika">troika</a></i> consisting of Chernenko, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmitriy_Ustinov" title="Dmitriy Ustinov">Dmitriy Ustinov</a> and Tikhonov. During Andropov's last days, Tikhonov presided over the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Politburo</a> sessions, headed the 1984 Soviet delegation to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_for_Mutual_Economic_Assistance" title="Council for Mutual Economic Assistance">Council for Mutual Economic Assistance</a> conference in <a href="/wiki/East_Berlin" title="East Berlin">East Berlin</a>, conducted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilateral_relations" title="Bilateral relations">bilateral relations</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a> states, and hosted the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Finland" title="Prime Minister of Finland">Prime Minister of Finland</a> when he visited the Soviet Union. In short, in-between Andropov's last days and Chernenko's rise to power, Tikhonov was the dominant driving figure of the Soviet Union. However, Tikhonov peacefully stepped away, and supported Chernenko's candidacy for General Secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989146_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZemtsov1989146-16">[16]</a></sup> When Chernenko died in 1985, Tikhonov tried, but failed, to find a contender to Gorbachev's candidacy to the General Secretaryship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gorbachev_and_resignation">Gorbachev and resignation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Tikhonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gorbachev and resignation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon Gorbachev's ascension to power, Tikhonov was elected chairman of the newly established Commission on Improvements of the Management System. The title of chairman was largely honorary, and its <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> head was its deputy chairman, <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Ryzhkov" title="Nikolai Ryzhkov">Nikolai Ryzhkov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On 23 May 1985 Tikhonov presented his development plan for 1985 to 1990, and up until 2000, the plan was criticised by co-workers, and Gorbachev told his colleagues that Tikhonov was "ill-equipped" for the Premiership. Tikhonov forecast estimated growth of 20–22 percent growth in Soviet national income, an increase of 21–24 percent in industrial growth and doubling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_agriculture" title="Soviet agriculture">Soviet agriculture</a> output by 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shortbio_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-shortbio-3">[3]</a></sup> As part of Gorbachev's plan of removing, and replacing, the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_ideology_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="State ideology of the Soviet Union">conservative</a> members<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> of the Politburo, Tikhonov was compelled to retire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryzhkov_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ryzhkov-20">[20]</a></sup> Ryzhkov succeeded Tikhonov in office on 27 September 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryzhkov_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-ryzhkov-20">[20]</a></sup> His resignation was made official at a Central Committee plenum in September 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It is noteworthy that by the time of his resignation, Tikhonov was the oldest member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_leadership" title="Soviet leadership">Soviet leadership</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Tikhonov was active in Soviet politics, albeit in a much less prominent role, until 1989 when he lost his seat in the Central Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Tikhonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After his forced <a href="/wiki/Resignation" title="Resignation">resignation</a> from active politics in 1989, Tikhonov wrote a letter to <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> which stated that he regretted supporting his election to the General Secretaryship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shortbio_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-shortbio-3">[3]</a></sup> This view was strengthened when the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party</a> was banned in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. After his retirement, he lived the rest of his life in seclusion at his <i><a href="/wiki/Dacha" title="Dacha">dacha</a></i>. As one of his friends noted, he lived as "a hermit" and never showed himself in public<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shortbio_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-shortbio-3">[3]</a></sup> and that his later life was very difficult as he had no children and because his wife had died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shortbio_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-shortbio-3">[3]</a></sup> Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a> Tikhonov worked as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=State_Advisor_to_the_Supreme_Soviet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="State Advisor to the Supreme Soviet (page does not exist)">State Advisor to the Supreme Soviet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Tikhonov died on 1 June 1997 and was buried at the <a href="/wiki/Novodevichy_Cemetery" title="Novodevichy Cemetery">Novodevichy Cemetery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine, Tikhonov was a "tried and tested yes man" who had very little experience in foreign and defence policy when he took over the Premiership from <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Kosygin" title="Alexei Kosygin">Alexei Kosygin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A bust dedicated to Tikhonov can be found in <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a>, his birthplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Tikhonov, when compared to other Soviet premiers, has made little impact on post-Soviet culture and his legacy is remembered by few today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kommersant_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kommersant-6">[6]</a></sup> During his lifetime Tikhonov was awarded several awards; he was awarded nine <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Lenin" title="Order of Lenin">Order of Lenins</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Banner_of_Labour" title="Order of the Red Banner of Labour">Order of the Red Banner of Labour</a>, one Red Star, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalin_Prize" title="Stalin Prize">Stalin Prizes</a> and several medals and foreign awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reallyshortbio_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-reallyshortbio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
