<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc</h1>

<p><b>Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc</b> (1 December 1580 – 24 June 1637), often known simply as <b>Peiresc</b>, or by the Latin form of his name <b>Peirescius</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquary</a> and savant, who maintained a wide correspondence with scientists, and was a successful organizer of scientific inquiry. His research included a determination of the difference in <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> of various locations in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peiresc's father was a higher magistrate and city surgeon in <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> from a wealthy noble family, who with his wife fled their home town of <a href="/wiki/Aix-en-Provence" title="Aix-en-Provence">Aix-en-Provence</a> to avoid the plague raging there, settling in <a href="/wiki/Belgentier" title="Belgentier">Belgentier</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Var_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Var (département)">Var</a>. Peiresc was born in <a href="/wiki/Belgentier" title="Belgentier">Belgentier</a> and educated in Aix-en-Provence, <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a>, and at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> college at <a href="/wiki/Tournon-sur-Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Tournon-sur-Rhône">Tournon</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a>, he first became interested in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. Studying law and becoming interested in archaeology, he travelled to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and France in 1599, and finally finished his legal studies in 1604 at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montpellier" title="University of Montpellier">University of Montpellier</a>. It was also in 1604 that he assumed the name <i>Peiresc</i> after a domain in <a href="/wiki/Alpes-de-Haute-Provence" title="Alpes-de-Haute-Provence">Alpes-de-Haute-Provence</a> (now spelled <a href="/wiki/Peyresq" title="Peyresq">Peyresq</a>) which he had inherited from his father, although he himself never visited it.</p>
<p>After receiving his degree, he travelled to Paris (in 1605-1606, with his patron <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_du_Vair" title="Guillaume du Vair">Guillaume du Vair</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/Parlement" title="Parlement">Parlement</a> of Provence), <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> before returning to Aix in 1607 to take over his uncle's position as <i>conseiller</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Parlement" title="Parlement">Parlement</a> of Provence under du Vair. He held this post until 1615.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellectual_and_collector">Intellectual and collector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Intellectual and collector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1615 until 1622, Peiresc again visited Paris with du Vair. He then returned to Provence to serve as senator of the sovereign court. He became a patron of science and art, studied <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>, and supported the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">Pierre Gassendi</a> from 1634 to 1637.</p>
<p>Peiresc's position as a great intellectual at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" title="Scientific revolution">scientific revolution</a> has led to his being called a "Prince of the Republic of Letters". He was also a noted politician in his home region, and a tireless letter-writer (10,000 of his letters survive, and he was in constant correspondence with <a href="/wiki/Malherbe" title="Malherbe">Malherbe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Dupuy_(scholar)" title="Pierre Dupuy (scholar)">brothers Dupuy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alphonse-Louis_du_Plessis_de_Richelieu" title="Alphonse-Louis du Plessis de Richelieu">Alphonse-Louis du Plessis de Richelieu</a>, and with his great friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubens" title="Rubens">Rubens</a>. His correspondence to <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Malherbe" title="François de Malherbe">François de Malherbe</a> throws light on the personality of Malherbe's troubled son Marc-Antoine Malherbe.</p>
<p>Peiresc's house in Aix-en-Provence was a veritable museum, and held a mix of antique sculptures, modern paintings, medals, books and gardens with exotic plants. He acquired the Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Barberini_ivory" title="Barberini ivory">Barberini ivory</a> (it is not known how or from whom) and offered it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Barberini_(seniore)" title="Francesco Barberini (seniore)">Francesco Barberini</a>: the work is now in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre" title="Musée du Louvre">Louvre</a>. He had the Codex Luxemburgensis, the surviving Carolingian copy of the <a href="/wiki/Chronography_of_354" title="Chronography of 354">Chronography of 354</a> in his possession for many years; after his death it disappeared. He owned over 18,000 coins and medals, and was also an archaeologist, amateur artist, historian (he demonstrated that Julius Caesar's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_invasion_of_Britain" title="Caesar's invasion of Britain">invasion of Britain</a> set out not from <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a> but from <a href="/wiki/Saint-Omer" title="Saint-Omer">St Omer</a>), Egyptologist, botanist, zoologist (studying chameleons, crocodiles, the elephant and the alzaron, a sort of <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubian</a> gazelle with a bull-like head, now disappeared), physiologist, geographer (put on the project of linking Aix to Marseilles), and ecologist.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomer">Astronomer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Astronomer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peiresc was also an astronomer. In 1610 du Vair purchased a <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>, which Peiresc and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Gaultier" title="Joseph Gaultier">Joseph Gaultier</a> used for observing the skies, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_moon" title="Galilean moon">Jupiter's moons</a>; his courtly suggestion that individual names from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> family be applied to these "Medicean stars" was not taken up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Peiresc also made detailed observations of the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a> in 1610; Gaultier became the second person to see it in the telescope. To determine <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> with greater precision, he coordinated the observation of the lunar eclipses of 28 August 1635 right across the Mediterranean; this allowed him to work out that the Mediterranean sea was in fact 1,000 km shorter than had previously been thought. Peiresc also wrote letters to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">Pierre Gassendi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_Campanella" title="Tommaso Campanella">Tommaso Campanella</a>, two of whom he defended when they were arrested by the Inquisition.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peiresc wrote an "abridged history of Provence", but died before editing it: it was only published (edited by Jacques Ferrier and Michel Feuillas) in 1982. With Gassendi's support, notably financially, he and the engraver <a href="/wiki/Claude_Mellan" title="Claude Mellan">Claude Mellan</a> began to produce a map of the Moon's surface, but again Peiresc died before completing it.</p>
<p>Peiresc died on 24 June 1637 in Aix-en-Provence.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A bronze bust of Peiresc stands on the square of the university in Aix-en-Provence, facing the cathedral of Saint Sauveur. His home near the palais de Justice was demolished to build the present Palais, and has completely disappeared.</p>
<p>The village museum in <a href="/wiki/Peyresq" title="Peyresq">Peyresq</a> near <a href="/wiki/Digne-les-Bains" title="Digne-les-Bains">Digne-les-Bains</a> is wholly given over to his work.</p>
<p>Peiresc was honored in 1935 by the naming of the lunar <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">crater</a> <a href="/wiki/Peirescius_(crater)" title="Peirescius (crater)">Peirescius</a> (46.5S, 67.6E, 61 km diameter); and in 1993 by the naming of the asteroid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19226_Peiresc" title="19226 Peiresc">19226 Peiresc</a>.</p>
