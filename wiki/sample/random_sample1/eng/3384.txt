<p><b>Emperor Ninkō</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">仁孝天皇</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ninkō-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenn%C5%8D" title="Tennō">tennō</a></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 16 March 1800 – 21 February 1846)</span> was the 120th emperor of Japan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kunaicho_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kunaicho-1">[1]</a></sup> according to the traditional <a href="/wiki/List_of_Emperors_of_Japan" title="List of Emperors of Japan">order of succession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ninkō's reign spanned the years from 1817 through 1846.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titsingh421_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-titsingh421-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genealogy">Genealogy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Nink%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Genealogy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before Ninkō's ascension to the <a href="/wiki/Chrysanthemum_Throne" title="Chrysanthemum Throne">Chrysanthemum Throne</a>, his personal name (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imina" title="Imina">imina</a></i>) was <b>Ayahito <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">恵仁</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span></b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ninkō was the fourth son of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dkaku" title="Emperor Kōkaku">Emperor Kōkaku</a>.</p>
<p>Ninkō's Imperial family lived with him in the <a href="/wiki/Heian_Palace#Inner_Palace_.28Dairi.29" title="Heian Palace">Dairi</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Heian_Palace" title="Heian Palace">Heian Palace</a>. This family included 7 sons and 8 daughters from various concubines, but only the future Emperor Komei (Komei<i>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenn%C5%8D" title="Tennō">tennō</a></i>), Princess Sumiko (Sumiko<i>-naishinnō</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Chikako,_Princess_Kazu" title="Chikako, Princess Kazu">Princess Chikako</a> (Chikako<i>-naishinnō</i>) survived beyond childhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_of_Nink.C5.8D.27s_life">Events of Ninkō's life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Nink%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Events of Ninkō's life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He reigned from 31 October 1817 to 21 February 1846.</p>
<p>Ninkō was named as crown prince in 1809, being adopted by the chief wife (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C5%ABg%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chūgū (page does not exist)">chūgū</a></i>), of his father, the Emperor, Imperial Princess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Yoshiko" title="Empress Yoshiko">Yoshiko</a> (?, 欣子内親王), also known as Shin-Seiwa-in (?, 新清和院). Ninkō was enthroned as Emperor in 1817 after his father retired from the throne. Following his father the Retired Emperor's wishes, he attempted to revive certain court rituals and practices. For example, Ninkō and all other emperors after his father have been identified as <i>tennō.</i></p>
<p>His reign saw <a href="/wiki/Edo_period#Decline_of_the_Tokugawa" title="Edo period">some deterioration</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakufu" title="Bakufu">bakufu</a> power. The bakufu encountered yet more problems during the reign of his son, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dmei" title="Emperor Kōmei">Emperor Kōmei</a> (Komei<i>-tennō</i>). The bakufu collapsed in the beginning of the reign of his grandson, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a> (Meiji<i>-tennō</i>).</p>
<p>Among Ninkō's innovations was the establishment of the <i>Gakushūsho</i> (the predecessor of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gakushuin" title="Gakushuin">Gakushūin</a></i> for the Court Nobility just outside the Imperial Palace.</p>
<p>After Ninkō's death in 1846, he was enshrined in the Imperial mausoleum, <i>Nochi no Tsukinowa no Higashiyama no misasagi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">後月輪東山陵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which is at <a href="/wiki/Senny%C5%AB-ji" title="Sennyū-ji">Sennyū-ji</a> in <a href="/wiki/Higashiyama-ku,_Kyoto" title="Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto">Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto</a>. Also enshrined in <i><a href="/wiki/Tsuki_no_wa_no_misasagi" title="Tsuki no wa no misasagi">Tsuki no wa no misasagi</a>,</i> at Sennyū-ji are this emperor's immediate Imperial predecessors since <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Mizunoo" title="Emperor Go-Mizunoo">Emperor Go-Mizunoo</a> – <a href="/wiki/Empress_Meish%C5%8D" title="Empress Meishō">Meishō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-K%C5%8Dmy%C5%8D" title="Emperor Go-Kōmyō">Go-Kōmyō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Sai" title="Emperor Go-Sai">Go-Sai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Reigen" title="Emperor Reigen">Reigen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Higashiyama" title="Emperor Higashiyama">Higashiyama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Nakamikado" title="Emperor Nakamikado">Nakamikado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Sakuramachi" title="Emperor Sakuramachi">Sakuramachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Momozono" title="Emperor Momozono">Momozono</a>, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Go-Sakuramachi" title="Empress Go-Sakuramachi">Go-Sakuramachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Momozono" title="Emperor Go-Momozono">Go-Momozono</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dkaku" title="Emperor Kōkaku">Kōkaku</a>. The shrine complex also encompasses the <i>misasagi</i> of Ninkō's immediate successor – <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dmei" title="Emperor Kōmei">Kōmei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Dowager" title="Empress Dowager">Empress Dowager</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Yoshik%C5%8D" title="Empress Yoshikō">Yoshikō</a> is also entombed at this Imperial mausoleum complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the abdication of his father, Emperor Ninkō ascended to the Chrysanthemum Throne on 31 October 1817.</p>
<p>His reign coincided with what is referred to as "the late Tokugawa Shogunate" period and in particular the beginnings of the decline of the shōgun, or bakufu (幕府). Although the final fall of the Tokugawa shogunate only came about during the Meiji Restoration and as a result of the opening of Edo-era Japan to the Western world, it was not simply their inability to cope with the situation that was their undoing.</p>
<p>In the mid-19th century a complex political struggle was already forming between the bakufu and its critics. From the outset, the Tokugawa attempted to restrict the accumulation of wealth by families and had promoted a "back to the soil" policy, making the farmer, as the ultimate producer, the ideal person within society.</p>
<p>However, as a result of a combination of a long period of peace; better crop production, transport, housing; and in increase in entertainment and leisure time, the standard of living of the average rural dweller rose, despite efforts to restrict wealth. A high rate of literacy, resulted in cultural values being redefined and infused amongst both the samurai and chōnin (merchant-class). Although the government tried to restrict the merchants, seeing them as unproductive members of society, the samurai relied on the chōnin for supplies and were thus converted to the chōnin's cause.</p>
<p>This resulted in a struggle between the shōgun and the merchant class, as a result of the political limitations that the shōgun imposed. The cumbersome government bureaucracy could not adapt to the new emerging social order, which was compounded by rapid population growth during the same period. (The 1721 census listed 26 million commoners and about 4 million members of samurai families). Droughts, peasant unrest and mass protests over taxes and food shortages became common occurrences. Previously wealthy families, now landless, were forced to become tenant farmers, whilst the cities' populations grew as rural poor moved into them. A new, wealthy farming class emerged and many samurai who had fallen on tough times were forced to work for merchants.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kugy.C5.8D">Kugyō</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Nink%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kugyō">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Kugy%C5%8D" title="Kugyō">Kugyō</a></i> (公卿) is a collective term for the very few most powerful men attached to the court of the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a> in pre-<a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji</a> eras. Even during those years in which the court's actual influence outside the palace walls was minimal, the hierarchic organization persisted.</p>
<p>In general, this elite group included only three to four men at a time. These were hereditary courtiers whose experience and background would have brought them to the pinnacle of a life's career. During Ninkō's reign, this apex of the <i><a href="/wiki/Daij%C5%8D-kan" title="Daijō-kan">Daijō-kan</a> included:</i></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kampaku" title="Kampaku">Kampaku</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Ichij%C5%8D_Tadayoshi" title="Ichijō Tadayoshi">Ichijō Tadayoshi</a>, 1814–1823</li>
<li><i>Kampaku, <a href="/wiki/Takatsukasa_Masamichi" title="Takatsukasa Masamichi">Takatsukasa Masamichi</a>, 1823–1856</i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sadaijin" title="Sadaijin">Sadaijin</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Udaijin" title="Udaijin">Udaijin</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Naidaijin" title="Naidaijin">Naidaijin</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Dainagon" title="Dainagon">Dainagon</a></i></li>
