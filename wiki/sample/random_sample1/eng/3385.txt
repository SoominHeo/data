<p>The <b>Nintendo 2DS</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Handheld_game_console" title="Handheld game console">handheld game console</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>. Announced in August 2013, the device was released in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> on 12 October 2013 to coincide with the launch of <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_X_and_Y" title="Pokémon X and Y"><i>Pokémon X</i> and <i>Y</i></a>.</p>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS is an entry-level version of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> which maintains otherwise identical hardware, similar functionality, and compatibility with software designed for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> and 3DS. However, the 2DS is differentiated by a new slate form factor rather than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clamshell_design" title="Clamshell design">clamshell design</a> used by its precursors and by lacking the Nintendo 3DS's signature <a href="/wiki/Autostereoscopy" title="Autostereoscopy">autostereoscopic</a> 3D display. The 2DS is sold concurrently with existing 3DS models as an incentive to expand the market for Nintendo 3DS games; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_of_America" title="Nintendo of America">Nintendo of America</a> president <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Fils-Aim%C3%A9" title="Reggie Fils-Aimé">Reggie Fils-Aimé</a> stated that the 2DS was primarily targeted towards younger players (such as those younger than age 7), whom they had previously advised not to use the 3D functionality on the 3DS due to potential eye health concerns.</p>
<p>Reception to the Nintendo 2DS has been mixed; while Nintendo was praised for how it priced and positioned the 2DS alongside its higher-end counterparts, much of its criticism was directed towards its regressions in comparison to the 3DS, such as a design that some considered less appealing than that of the 3DS, its lower sound quality, and its battery life. However, the 2DS's design was praised by some critics for being more robust and comfortable to hold than the 3DS, especially for its target market. Some critics also felt that the lack of 3D support was an admission by Nintendo that the concept was a <a href="/wiki/Fad" title="Fad">fad</a>; however, Nintendo has since stated that autostereoscopic 3D would remain a part of their future plans.</p>
<p>On 5 October 2016, Nintendo announced that they would be switching the color scheme of the 2DS from the colors on only the bumper to the colors on the front and back of the system. These new systems were released in November 2016.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nintendo officially unveiled the 2DS on 28 August 2013 via a press release; members of the press were given a chance to demo the device in private prior to the announcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the 2DS, the company aimed to produce a device that would be "new, unique, different, and [bring] more people into this category that we love." Part of this goal was achieved by positioning the device at a lower price point than the 3DS; in North America, the system retailed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">US$</a>129.99 on launch, in comparison to the US$169.99 price of the standard 3DS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign-announce_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-announce-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_of_America" title="Nintendo of America">Nintendo of America</a> president <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Fils-Aim%C3%A9" title="Reggie Fils-Aimé">Reggie Fils-Aimé</a>, the Nintendo 2DS is primarily aimed at a younger demographic than the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> — particularly, those younger than 7 years old; whom Nintendo had advised not to use the 3D features on the 3DS due to potential <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS#Health_concerns" title="Nintendo 3DS">eye health concerns</a>. The validity of Nintendo's claims were questioned by vision experts, however, who believed that the 3DS could actually help detect certain eye problems, and felt the warnings were for <a href="/wiki/Public_liability" title="Public liability">liability</a> reasons rather than any realistic harm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign-announce_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ign-announce-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JT-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-3dsharm_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-3dsharm-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of a promotional effort for Nintendo's late-2013 releases, the Nintendo 2DS was featured on <i>The Nintendo Experience</i> promotional tour at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simon_Malls" title="Simon Malls">Simon Malls</a> locations in the United States throughout October and into early November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joystiq-release_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-joystiq-release-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nintendo_3DS_colors_and_styles#Nintendo_2DS" title="List of Nintendo 3DS colors and styles">List of Nintendo 2DS colors and styles</a></div>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS was released in North America, Europe and Australia on 12 October 2013, the same day as <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_X_and_Y" title="Pokémon X and Y"><i>Pokémon X</i> and <i>Y</i></a>. In North America, it was available in both <i>Black + Red</i> and <i>Black + Blue</i> color variations, while in Europe and Australia it was available in <i>White + Red</i> and <i>Black + Blue</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GamesIndustry_announcement_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GamesIndustry_announcement-11">[11]</a></sup> Each console is bundled with a 4 <a href="/wiki/Gigabyte" title="Gigabyte">GB</a> <a href="/wiki/Secure_Digital" title="Secure Digital">SD card</a> and an <a href="/wiki/AC_adapter" title="AC adapter">AC adapter</a>. Unlike the first two Nintendo 3DS models, no Japanese release for the 2DS was announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNET_announcement_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNET_announcement-12">[12]</a></sup> Matching red and blue carrying case accessories were also released on launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joystiq-release_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-joystiq-release-10">[10]</a></sup> With the release of the 2DS, Nintendo of America began to phase out the original 3DS, leaving the 2DS and 3DS XL as the only models still actively sold in North America as of its release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-no3dsna_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-no3dsna-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 December 2013, the Nintendo 2DS launched in South Korea, in both <i>White + Red</i> and <i>Black + Blue</i>, with each including a digital copy of <i>Pokémon X</i> and <i>Y</i> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-korea_launch_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-korea_launch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <i>Sea Green</i> variant, with a white body and mint green accents, color was released in North America on 6 June 2014 to coincide with the North American release of <i><a href="/wiki/Tomodachi_Life" title="Tomodachi Life">Tomodachi Life</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign-seagreen2ds_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-seagreen2ds-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Crystal Red</i> and <i>Crystal Blue</i> versions, which feature a translucent front cover, were released in Europe and North America in November 2014 as a tie-in for <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Omega_Ruby_and_Alpha_Sapphire" title="Pokémon Omega Ruby and Alpha Sapphire"><i>Pokémon Omega Ruby</i> and <i>Alpha Sapphire</i></a>. In North America, the translucent models were also sold at a lower retail price of US$100 as opposed to the base US$129.99 price of the normal 2DS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurogamer-oras2ds_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurogamer-oras2ds-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet-translucentus_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet-translucentus-16">[16]</a></sup> On 20 August 2015, the U.S. price was permanently lowered to $100, and units began to be bundled with coupons for a digital copy of <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_7" title="Mario Kart 7">Mario Kart 7</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-2ds100mk7_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-2ds100mk7-17">[17]</a></sup> On 11 May 2016, Nintendo announced that the U.S. price would be lowered to $79.99 effective 20 May 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-79992ds_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-79992ds-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, it was announced that the 2DS would be released in Japan on 27 February 2016 to coincide with the Virtual Console release of the original Game Boy <i>Pokémon</i> games. They were made available in translucent <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue">red</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocket_Monsters_Green" title="Pocket Monsters Green">green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue#Pocket_Monsters_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue">blue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Yellow" title="Pokémon Yellow">yellow</a> versions with colored buttons. Each were bundled with the corresponding <i>Pokémon</i> game, a special Home Menu theme, a poster, and a code to obtain <a href="/wiki/Mew_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Mew (Pokémon)">Mew</a> on the bundled game, or the current <i>Pokémon X</i>, <i>Y</i>, <i>Omega Ruby</i>, or <i>Alpha Sapphire</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-2dsjapan_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-verge-2dsjapan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS#Hardware" title="Nintendo 3DS">Hardware of Nintendo 3DS</a></div>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS's hardware specifications are almost identical to those of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a>; retaining features such as its <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">GPU</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> and <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">memory</a>, along with compatibility with games designed for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> and Nintendo 3DS. However, its hardware still contains some slight differences. Unlike the Nintendo 3DS, which uses two display panels, with a lower <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a> panel and a top dual-layered screen panel capable of displaying <a href="/wiki/Autostereoscopy" title="Autostereoscopy">autostereoscopic</a> <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">3D</a>, the Nintendo 2DS uses a single, non-stereoscopic LCD touchscreen, which is overlaid with a frame mimicking the screen dimensions of the 3DS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Despite its inability to display 3D content, the 2DS retains the 3DS's <a href="/wiki/Stereo_camera" title="Stereo camera">dual cameras</a> for taking photographs in a 3D format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-2dstablet_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-2dstablet-20">[20]</a></sup> The Nintendo 2DS only has an internal <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">mono</a> speaker, as opposed to the internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereo" title="Stereo">stereo</a> speakers of the 3DS, although stereo sound can still be output through the headphone jack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS's design is a significant departure from that of its precursors; while it is roughly the same size as its counterpart, the 2DS uses a "slate-like" form factor instead of the <a href="/wiki/Flip_(form)" title="Flip (form)">clamshell</a> form used by the DS and 3DS. Its buttons are positioned towards the center of the device instead of near the lower screen, and its shoulder buttons are concave in shape and relatively thicker than those of the 3DS. The 2DS uses a switch for entering <a href="/wiki/Sleep_mode" title="Sleep mode">sleep mode</a> in lieu of closing the shell, and the hardware wireless switch was replaced by a software toggle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygon-handson_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-polygon-handson-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp-release_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-wp-release-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_preview_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_preview-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS contains the same 1300mAh battery present on regular Nintendo 3DS systems. Despite not supporting the 3DS's automatic brightness setting ("Power Save Mode"), the 2DS was rated by Nintendo as having slightly longer battery life than the 3DS; Nintendo rated the 2DS as being able to play from 3 to 5.5 hours of 3DS games or 5 to 9 hours of DS games on a single charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Europe_comparison_chart_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Europe_comparison_chart-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygon-comparison_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-polygon-comparison-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joystiq-3dspowersave_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-joystiq-3dspowersave-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_and_services">Software and services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Software and services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS_system_software" title="Nintendo 3DS system software">Nintendo 3DS system software</a></div>
<p>Aside from minor adjustments to reflect its hardware design differences, the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS_system_software" title="Nintendo 3DS system software">system software</a> of the Nintendo 2DS is otherwise identical to that of the 3DS, remaining compatible with all games released for the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nintendo_3DS_games" title="List of Nintendo 3DS games">3DS</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nintendo_DS_games" title="List of Nintendo DS games">DS</a> (excluding those requiring the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a> cartridge slot), and offering online features such as <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Network" title="Nintendo Network">Nintendo Network</a> for multiplayer and online gaming, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_eShop" title="Nintendo eShop">Nintendo eShop</a> for downloading and purchasing games, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StreetPass_and_SpotPass" title="StreetPass and SpotPass">StreetPass and SpotPass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joystiq-release_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-joystiq-release-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygon-handson_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-polygon-handson-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_review_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph_review-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-release">Pre-release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pre-release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS received mixed reception upon its announcement. <a href="/wiki/Kotaku" title="Kotaku">Kotaku</a> felt that the Nintendo 2DS might appeal to a certain niche of people who otherwise would not purchase a Nintendo 3DS, and that the lack of 3D support could indicate a cooling interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoscopic" title="Stereoscopic">stereoscopic</a> graphics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup> USA Today noted that some games that were designed with 3D in mind might not be as easily playable without the stereoscopic feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_preview_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_preview-23">[23]</a></sup> CNET, meanwhile, called the console an acknowledgement by Nintendo that 3D was an unnecessary feature and even a "tactical mistake".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNET_announcement_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNET_announcement-12">[12]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NBC_News" title="NBC News">NBC News</a></i> called the 2DS a "peculiar choice" to gamers satisfied with the Nintendo DS line, and feared that it might take resources away from the less successful Nintendo console, the <a href="/wiki/Wii_U" title="Wii U">Wii U</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBC_News_announcement_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBC_News_announcement-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some technology writers have expressed concern with the physical appearance of the 2DS: CNET considered the console to be significantly less aesthetically pleasing than its <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS_family" title="Nintendo 3DS family">other models</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNET_announcement_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNET_announcement-12">[12]</a></sup> though some reviewers have generally found it to be comfortable in the hand. <i>USA Today</i> referred to it as "surprisingly comfortable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_preview_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_preview-23">[23]</a></sup> Kotaku noted that while it may look bulky, it was pleasant to hold even with one hand. It also noted that buttons on the unit were easily accessible, and that their placement on the upper half of the console directs the eye toward the upper screen. Overall, the publication felt that the build quality was good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotaku_announcement_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku_announcement-6">[6]</a></sup> GamesIndustry also described the controls as considerably more comfortable than that of its predecessors. It also noted that the console had thick shell rather than the thin Nintendo 3DS, and that the lack of a hinge added durability. The publication felt that the design resembled "an unholy union of a <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wii_U_GamePad" title="Wii U GamePad">Wii U GamePad</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">DS</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GamesIndustry_announcement_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-GamesIndustry_announcement-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>USA Today</i> and CNET both had some concern over how to protect the screens, since there is no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clamshell_design" title="Clamshell design">clamshell</a> to close.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNET_announcement_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-CNET_announcement-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_preview_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_preview-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to the 2DS using one physical display divided to emulate two, Kyle Orland of <i><a href="/wiki/Ars_Technica" title="Ars Technica">Ars Technica</a></i> believed that Nintendo had missed an opportunity to produce a full-screen gaming <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet</a> using the 2DS's form factor; which would have enabled future games and apps to use the entire screen area (which he estimated to be around the 5-inch range used by <a href="/wiki/Phablet" title="Phablet">phablets</a>), still allow backwards compatibility with games designed for the folding 3DS and DS, and enhance the ability to port existing <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> games to the platform. However, Orland also noted that introducing such a product could fragment Nintendo's portable gaming ecosystem for existing 3DS users—and that it was too early in the 3DS's life for Nintendo to introduce a successor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-2dstablet_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-ars-2dstablet-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Calling it a "smart move" to redesign the Nintendo 3DS so as to lower costs, <i>Wired</i> felt that the 2DS might enable Nintendo to sell the console at a significantly lower price with a significantly higher profit margin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_announcement_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired_announcement-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-release">Post-release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nintendo 2DS has received mixed reviews. While pricing and form-factor were generally considered ideal, the console's aesthetics and battery life were widely criticized. The Telegraph noted that the lack of hinges in the console improved its robustness, and that rounding out the footprint makes it an ideal handheld for children. However, the publication criticized the console's mono speaker, which offered a lower sound quality than its predecessors. It also criticized the battery life, which it described as being the same as the original 3DS model. On the other hand, it praised the screens' brighter display and wider viewing angle, despite their not being as large as those of the 3DS XL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_review_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph_review-26">[26]</a></sup> Eurogamer reinforced the idea that the Nintendo 2DS isn't aimed at "seasoned players" or current Nintendo 3DS owners, and felt that the system wasn't produced with aesthetics in mind. The publication also criticized the <a href="/wiki/Resistive_touchscreen" title="Resistive touchscreen">resistive touchscreen</a>, which felt outdated, the mono speaker and battery life. However, Eurogamer felt that the system is much more comfortable to hold than the Nintendo 3DS and 3DS XL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurogamer_review_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurogamer_review-29">[29]</a></sup> Analyst Piers Harding-Rolls felt that the Nintendo 2DS would help Nintendo broaden its market, and believes that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third-party_developer" title="Third-party developer">third-party publishers</a> will have more commitment to the platform moving forward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the system's launch, Nintendo stated that the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Lost_World" title="Sonic Lost World">Sonic Lost World</a></i> would boost Nintendo 2DS sales, labeling it as a "must play" title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On 31 October 2013, Nintendo president <a href="/wiki/Satoru_Iwata" title="Satoru Iwata">Satoru Iwata</a> admitted that the Nintendo 2DS lacked awareness among prospective purchasers. Additionally, he went on to say that some potential consumers believed that the product was too large and heavy to carry. However, Iwata stated that the system was well received among those who had purchased it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Despite the 2DS's lack of 3D support, Satoru Iwata insisted, during Nintendo's 2013 third quarter investors Q&amp;A, that the company "will [not] abandon 3D or cease to make new propositions in 3D", as it will continue to sell the existing Nintendo 3DS and 3DS XL platforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3d_future_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-3d_future-33">[33]</a></sup> However, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_Between_Worlds" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link Between Worlds">The Legend of Zelda: A Link Between Worlds</a></i> producer <a href="/wiki/Eiji_Aonuma" title="Eiji Aonuma">Eiji Aonuma</a> revealed in an interview that the game had minor changes in development due to the lack of 3D on the 2DS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sales">Sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_2DS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sales of the Nintendo 2DS surged three weeks after launching in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, after retailers cut on its price due to poor sales. The system was available for around £110, but major retailers including <a href="/wiki/Argos_(retailer)" title="Argos (retailer)">Argos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a> cut the system's price to under £100 to coincide with the school half-term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> As a result, sales of the Nintendo 2DS increased by 64% week-on-week, making it the UK's best selling console of the month, without combining the sales of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> and its larger counterpart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> During the third quarter of 2013, video game retailer <a href="/wiki/GameStop" title="GameStop">GameStop</a> reported that worldwide hardware sales grew by 15.3%, mainly due to strong Nintendo 2DS and 3DS sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
