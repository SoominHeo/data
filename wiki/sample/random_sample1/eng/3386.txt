<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerian_mythology" title="Sumerian mythology">Sumerian mythology</a>, <b>Ninhursag</b> (<span lang="und-Xsux" style="font-size:125%;font-family:'Segoe UI Historic','Akkadian','Noto Sans Cuneiform','Noto Sans Sumero-Akkadian Cuneiform';" title="cuneiform text" xml:lang="und-Xsux">𒊩𒌆𒉺𒂅</span> Ninḫursag) or <b>Ninkharsag</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">mother goddess</a> of the mountains, and one of the seven great deities of <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a>. She is principally a fertility goddess. Temple hymn sources identify her as the 'true and great lady of heaven' (possibly in relation to her standing on the mountain) and kings of Sumer were 'nourished by Ninhursag's milk'. Her hair is sometimes depicted in an omega shape, and she at times wears a horned head-dress and tiered skirt, often with bow cases at her shoulders, and not infrequently carries a mace or baton surmounted by an <a href="/wiki/Omega" title="Omega">omega</a> motif or a derivation, sometimes accompanied by a lion cub on a leash. She is the <a href="/wiki/Tutelary_deity" title="Tutelary deity">tutelary deity</a> to several Sumerian leaders. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ninhursag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nin-hursag means "lady of the sacred mountain" (from <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ERE%C5%A0" title="EREŠ">NIN</a> "lady" and ḪAR.SAG "sacred mountain, foothill",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley326_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley326-9">[9]</a></sup> possibly a reference to the site of her temple, the E-Kur (House of mountain deeps) at <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>. She had many names including <b>Ninmah</b> ("Great Queen");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley326_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley326-9">[9]</a></sup> <b><a href="/wiki/Mami_(goddess)" title="Mami (goddess)">Nintu</a></b> ("Lady of Birth");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley326_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley326-9">[9]</a></sup> <b><a href="/wiki/Mami_(goddess)" title="Mami (goddess)">Mamma</a></b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Mami_(goddess)" title="Mami (goddess)">Mami</a></b> (mother);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley326_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley326-9">[9]</a></sup> <b>Aruru</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley326_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley326-9">[9]</a></sup> <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belet-Ili" title="Belet-Ili">Belet-Ili</a></b> (lady of the gods, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley326_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley326-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to legend her name was changed from Ninmah to Ninhursag by her son <a href="/wiki/Ninurta" title="Ninurta">Ninurta</a> in order to commemorate his creation of the mountains. As Ninmenna, according to a Babylonian investiture ritual, she placed the golden crown on the king in the <i>Eanna</i> temple.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some of the names above were once associated with independent goddesses (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninmah" title="Ninmah">Ninmah</a> and Ninmenna), who later became identified and merged with Ninhursag, and myths exist in which the name Ninhursag is not mentioned.</p>
<p>As the wife and consort of Enki she was also referred to as <b>Damgulanna</b> (great wife of heaven) or <b>Damkina</b> (faithful wife). She had many epithets including <i>shassuru</i> or 'womb goddess', <i>tabsut ili</i> 'midwife of the gods', 'mother of all children' and 'mother of the gods'. In this role she is identified with <b><a href="/wiki/Ki_(goddess)" title="Ki (goddess)">Ki</a></b> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enuma_Elish" title="Enuma Elish">Enuma Elish</a>. She had shrines in both Eridu and Kish.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ninhursag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the legend of <a href="/wiki/Enki" title="Enki">Enki</a> and Ninhursag, Ninhursag bore a daughter to Enki called <a href="/wiki/Ninsar" title="Ninsar">Ninsar</a> ("Lady Greenery"). Through Enki, Ninsar bore a daughter <a href="/wiki/Ninkurra" title="Ninkurra">Ninkurra</a>. Ninkurra, in turn, bore Enki a daughter named <a href="/wiki/Uttu" title="Uttu">Uttu</a>. Enki then pursued Uttu, who was upset because he didn't care for her. Uttu, on her ancestress Ninhursag's advice buried Enki's seed in the earth, whereupon eight plants (the very first) sprung up. Enki, seeing the plants, ate them, and became ill in eight organs of his body. Ninhursag cured him, taking the plants into her body and giving birth to eight deities: <a href="/wiki/Abu_(god)" title="Abu (god)">Abu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintulla" title="Nintulla">Nintulla</a> (Nintul), <a href="/wiki/Ninsutu" title="Ninsutu">Ninsutu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ninkasi" title="Ninkasi">Ninkasi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanshe" title="Nanshe">Nanshe</a> (Nazi), <a href="/wiki/Azimua" title="Azimua">Azimua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ninti" title="Ninti">Ninti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Enshag" title="Enshag">Enshag</a> (Enshagag).</p>
<p>In the text 'Creator of the Hoe', she completed the birth of mankind after the heads had been uncovered by Enki's hoe.</p>
<p>In creation texts, Ninmah (another name for Ninhursag) acts as a midwife whilst the mother goddess <a href="/wiki/Nammu" title="Nammu">Nammu</a> makes different kinds of human individuals from lumps of clay at a feast given by Enki to celebrate the creation of humankind.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worship">Worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ninhursag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Her symbol, resembling the Greek letter <i>omega</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%A9" title="Ω">Ω</a>, has been depicted in art from around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3000_BC" title="3000 BC">3000 BC</a>, though more generally from the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_millennium_BC" title="Second millennium BC">second millennium BC</a>. It appears on some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_stone" title="Boundary stone">boundary stones</a> — on the upper tier, indicating her importance. The omega symbol is associated with the Egyptian cow goddess <a href="/wiki/Hathor" title="Hathor">Hathor</a>, and may represent a stylized womb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Hathor is at times depicted on a mountain, so it may be that the two goddesses are connected.</p>
<p>Her temple, the <a href="/wiki/Esagila" title="Esagila">Esagila</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89_(temple)" title="É (temple)">E</a> (temple) + SAG (head) + ILA (lofty)) was located on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KUR" title="KUR">KUR</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>, although she also had a temple at <a href="/wiki/Kish_(Sumer)" title="Kish (Sumer)">Kish</a>.</p>
