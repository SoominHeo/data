<p><b>Neil Immerman</b> (24 November 1953, <a href="/wiki/Manhasset,_New_York" title="Manhasset, New York">Manhasset, New York</a>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_computer_science" title="Theoretical computer science">theoretical computer scientist</a>, a professor of computer science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-um-dir_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-um-dir-1">[1]</a></sup> He is one of the key developers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_complexity" title="Descriptive complexity">descriptive complexity</a>, an approach he is currently applying to research in model checking, database theory, and computational complexity theory.</p>
<p>Professor Immerman is an editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/SIAM_Journal_on_Computing" title="SIAM Journal on Computing">SIAM Journal on Computing</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and of <i><a href="/wiki/Logical_Methods_in_Computer_Science" title="Logical Methods in Computer Science">Logical Methods in Computer Science</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He received B.S. and M.S. degrees from <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> in 1974 and his Ph.D. from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> in 1980 under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Juris_Hartmanis" title="Juris Hartmanis">Juris Hartmanis</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turing_award" title="Turing award">Turing award</a> winner at Cornell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-um-dir_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-um-dir-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His book "Descriptive Complexity" appeared in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Immerman is the winner, jointly with <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B3bert_Szelepcs%C3%A9nyi" title="RÃ³bert SzelepcsÃ©nyi">RÃ³bert SzelepcsÃ©nyi</a>, of the 1995 <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6del_Prize" title="GÃ¶del Prize">GÃ¶del Prize</a> in theoretical computer science for proof of what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Immerman%E2%80%93Szelepcs%C3%A9nyi_theorem" title="ImmermanâSzelepcsÃ©nyi theorem">ImmermanâSzelepcsÃ©nyi theorem</a>, the result that <a href="/wiki/NSPACE" title="NSPACE">nondeterministic space</a> <a href="/wiki/Complexity_class" title="Complexity class">complexity classes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">closed</a> under <a href="/wiki/Complement_(set_theory)" title="Complement (set theory)">complementation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Immerman is an <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="List of Fellows of the Association for Computing Machinery">ACM Fellow</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellow" title="Guggenheim Fellow">Guggenheim Fellow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
