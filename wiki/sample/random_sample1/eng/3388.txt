<p><b>Niels Wilhelm Gade</b> (22 February 1817 – 21 December 1890) was a <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violinist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organist" title="Organist">organist</a> and teacher. He is considered the most important Danish musician of his day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niels_Gade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gade was born in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, the son of a joiner and instrument maker. He began his career as a violinist with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Danish_Orchestra" title="Royal Danish Orchestra">Royal Danish Orchestra</a>, and saw his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_overture" title="Concert overture">concert overture</a> <i>Efterklange af Ossian</i> ("Echoes of <a href="/wiki/Ossian" title="Ossian">Ossian</a>") premiered with them in 1841. When his first symphony was turned down for performance in Copenhagen, he sent it to <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Felix Mendelssohn</a>. Mendelssohn received the work positively, and conducted it in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> in March 1843, to enthusiastic public reaction. Supported by a fellowship from the Danish government, Gade himself moved to Leipzig, teaching at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leipzig_Conservatory" title="Leipzig Conservatory">Conservatory</a> there, working as an assistant conductor of the <a href="/wiki/Gewandhaus" title="Gewandhaus">Gewandhaus Orchestra</a>, and befriending Mendelssohn, who had an important influence on his music. In 1845 he conducted the premiere performance of Mendelssohn's <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Mendelssohn)" title="Violin Concerto (Mendelssohn)">Violin Concerto in E minor</a>. He also became friends with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Robert Schumann</a>. In Copenhagen Niels Gade became acquainted with the composer <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Gurlitt_(composer)" title="Cornelius Gurlitt (composer)">Cornelius Gurlitt</a>, and they remained friends until the latter's death.</p>
<p>At Mendelssohn’s death in 1847, Gade was appointed to his position as chief conductor but was forced to return to Copenhagen in the spring of 1848 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_War_of_Schleswig" title="First War of Schleswig">war broke out between Prussia and Denmark</a>.</p>
<p>In Copenhagen Gade became director of the <a href="/wiki/Musikforeningen" title="Musikforeningen">Copenhagen Musical Society</a> (a post he retained until his death) and, establishing a new orchestra and chorus, settled in to a career as the most prominent musician in Denmark. Under his direction, the Music Society reached its peak. He also worked as an organist; though he lost the prestigious position of organist at Our Lady, today's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Cathedral" title="Copenhagen Cathedral">Copenhagen Cathedral</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Johan_Peter_Emilius_Hartmann" title="Johan Peter Emilius Hartmann">J.P.E. Hartmann</a>, he served in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Holmen" title="Church of Holmen">Church of Holmen</a> in Copenhagen from 1850 until his death. Gade was joint director of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Danish_Academy_of_Music" title="Royal Danish Academy of Music">Copenhagen Conservatory</a> with Hartmann (whose daughter he married in 1852) and <a href="/wiki/Holger_Simon_Paulli" title="Holger Simon Paulli">Holger Simon Paulli</a>. An important influence on a number of later Scandinavian composers, he encouraged and taught both <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Grieg" title="Edvard Grieg">Edvard Grieg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Nielsen" title="Carl Nielsen">Carl Nielsen</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Elfrida_Andr%C3%A9e" title="Elfrida Andrée">Elfrida Andrée</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Malling" title="Otto Malling">Otto Malling</a>, <a href="/wiki/August_Winding" title="August Winding">August Winding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asger_Hamerik" title="Asger Hamerik">Asger Hamerik</a>. He died in Copenhagen.</p>
<p>Among Gade's works are eight <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphonies</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Violin_concerto" title="Violin concerto">violin concerto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber music</a>, organ and <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> pieces and a number of large-scale <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a>, <i>Comala</i> (1846) and <i><a href="/wiki/Elverskud" title="Elverskud">Elverskud</a></i> (1853) amongst them, which he called <i>koncertstykker</i> ("concert pieces"). These products, embraced post-1848 as works of <a href="/wiki/Romantic_nationalism" title="Romantic nationalism">Romantic nationalism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celenza.2C_Anna_Harwell_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Celenza.2C_Anna_Harwell-1">[1]</a></sup> are sometimes based on <a href="/wiki/Danish_folklore" title="Danish folklore">Danish folklore</a>. Apparently Gade never rated "Brudevalsen" (The Bridal Waltz), and assigned it to the waste paper basket from where, it is rumoured, it was rescued by August Bournonville, to become an essential part of a Danish wedding.</p>
<p>He married Emma Sophie Amalie Hartmann, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Johan_Peter_Emilius_Hartmann" title="Johan Peter Emilius Hartmann">J. P. E. Hartmann</a>, in 1852. He remarried in 1857 after her death.</p>
