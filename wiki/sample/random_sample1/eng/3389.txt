<p><b>Nimravidae</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">carnivores</a>, sometimes known as <b>false saber-toothed cats</b>, whose fossils are found in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. Not considered to belong to the true cats (family <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">Felidae</a>), the nimravids are generally considered closely related and classified as a distinct family in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suborder" title="Suborder">suborder</a> <a href="/wiki/Feliformia" title="Feliformia">Feliformia</a>. Fossils have been dated from the Middle <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> through the Late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> epochs (<a href="/wiki/Bartonian" title="Bartonian">Bartonian</a> through <a href="/wiki/Tortonian" title="Tortonian">Tortonian</a> stages, 40.4—7.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>), spanning about <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=40.4-7.2">33.2</a> million years</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The barbourofelids were once previously classified as a <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">subfamily</a> of Nimravidae, in 2004, but were reassigned to their own distinct family <a href="/wiki/Barbourofelidae" title="Barbourofelidae">Barbourofelidae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_evolution">Morphology and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimravidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Morphology and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most nimravids had muscular, low-slung, cat-like bodies, with shorter legs and tails than are typical of cats. Unlike <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> Feliformia, the nimravids had a different bone structure in the <a href="/wiki/Ossicles" title="Ossicles">small bones</a> of the ear. The <a href="/wiki/Middle_ear" title="Middle ear">middle ear</a> of true cats is housed in an external structure called an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditory_bulla" title="Auditory bulla">auditory bulla</a>, which is separated by a <a href="/wiki/Septum" title="Septum">septum</a> into two chambers. Nimravid remains show <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ossify" title="Ossify">ossified</a> bullae with no septum, or no trace at all of the entire bulla. It is assumed that they had a <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilaginous</a> housing of the ear mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although some nimravids physically resembled the <a href="/wiki/Saber-toothed_cat" title="Saber-toothed cat">sabre-toothed cats</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Smilodon" title="Smilodon">Smilodon</a></i>, they were not closely related,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> a similar form through <a href="/wiki/Parallel_evolution" title="Parallel evolution">parallel evolution</a>. They possessed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synapomorphies" title="Synapomorphies">synapomorphies</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Barbourofelidae" title="Barbourofelidae">barbourofelids</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranium" title="Cranium">cranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dentition</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postcranium" title="Postcranium">postcranium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They also had a flange on the front of the <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>, not as prominent as those on <i><a href="/wiki/Thylacosmilus" title="Thylacosmilus">Thylacosmilus</a></i>, that projected downward as long as the canine tooth.</p>
<p>The ancestors of nimravids and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felid" title="Felid">felids</a> diverged from a common ancestor soon after the <a href="/wiki/Caniformia" title="Caniformia">Caniformia</a>-<a href="/wiki/Feliformia" title="Feliformia">Feliformia</a> split, in the middle <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> about 50 million years ago (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>), with a minimum constraint of 43 mya. Recognizable nimravid <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> date from the late Eocene (37 mya), from the <a href="/wiki/Chadronian" title="Chadronian">Chadronian</a> White River Formation at Flagstaff Rim, <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, to the late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> (5 mya). Nimravid diversity appears to have peaked about 28 mya.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimravidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Nimravidae was named by American paleontologist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">Edward Drinker Cope</a> in 1880,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with the type genus as <i><a href="/wiki/Nimravus" title="Nimravus">Nimravus</a>.</i> The family was assigned to Fissipedia by Cope (1889); to Caniformia by Flynn and Galiano (1982); to Aeluroidea by Carroll (1988); to Feliformia by Bryant (1991); and to Carnivoramorpha; by Wesley-Hunt and Werdelin (2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nimravids are placed in tribes by some authors to reflect closer relationships in genera within the family. Some nimravids evolved into large toothed, cat-like forms with massive flattened upper canines and accompanying mandibular flanges. Some had dentition similar to felids, or modern cats, with smaller canines. Others had moderately increased canines in a more intermediate relationship between the saber-toothed cats and felids. The upper canines were not only shorter, but also more conical, than those of the true saber-toothed cats (<a href="/wiki/Machairodontinae" title="Machairodontinae">Machairodontinae</a>). These nimravids are referred to as "false sabre-tooths".</p>
<p>Not only did nimravids exhibit diverse dentition, but they also showed the same diversity in size and morphology as felids. Some were <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopard</a>-sized, others the size of today's <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tigers</a>, one had the short face, rounded skull, and smaller canines of the modern <a href="/wiki/Cheetah" title="Cheetah">cheetah</a>, and one, <i><a href="/wiki/Nanosmilus" title="Nanosmilus">Nanosmilus</a></i>, was only the size of a small <a href="/wiki/Bobcat" title="Bobcat">bobcat</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_history">Natural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimravidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1343" data-file-width="2000" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg/220px-Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg/330px-Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg/440px-Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dinictis_and_Protoceras.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i>Dinictis</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Protoceras" title="Protoceras">Protoceras</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Knight" title="Charles R. Knight">Charles R. Knight</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nimravids appeared in the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> epoch, about 40 mya, in North America and Asia. The global climate at this time was warm and wet, but was trending cooler and drier toward the late Eocene. The lush forests of the Eocene were transforming to scrub and open woodland. This climatic trend continued in the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>, and nimravids evidently flourished in this environment. North America and Asia were connected and shared much related fauna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-11">[11]</a></sup> Europe in the Oligocene was more of an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> than a continent, though some land bridges must have existed, for nimravids also spread there.</p>
<p>In the Miocene, the fossil record suggests that many animals suited for living in forest or woodland were replaced by grazers suited for grassland. This suggests that much of North America and Asia became dominated by savanna. Nimravids disappeared along with the woodlands, but survived in <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">relictual</a> humid forests in Europe to the late Miocene. When conditions ultimately changed there in the late Miocene, the last nimravids disappeared about 9 mya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
