<p>The <b>Nimba otter shrew</b> (<i>Micropotamogale lamottei</i>) is a dwarf otter shrew and belongs to the mammal family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenrecidae" title="Tenrecidae">Tenrecidae</a>. Tenrics have been found throughout mainland Africa and Madagascar; however, its subfamily <a href="/wiki/Potamogalinae" title="Potamogalinae">Potamogalinae</a> are the shrew-like creatures found in sub-Saharan Africa. This species belongs to the genus <i>Micropotamogale</i>, literally meaning "dwarf shrew". It is native to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Nimba" title="Mount Nimba">Mount Nimba</a> which rests along the border of <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%E2%80%99Ivoire" title="Côte d’Ivoire">Côte d’Ivoire</a> (Ivory Coast) in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimba_otter_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nimba otter shrew is a small bodied mammal. Weighing only about 125 grams (4.5 ounces) it has a body length of 6-9 inches(15–22 cm.) with 1/4 to 1/3 of its body size being its tail. It has been described as a "miniature sea otter with a rat tail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuhn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuhn-2">[2]</a></sup> Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelage" title="Pelage">pelage</a> is long, hiding its ears and eyes, and almost always universally colored (usually brown, but black and gray otter shrews have been spotted).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burnie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burnie-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_and_Life_History">Evolution and Life History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimba_otter_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution and Life History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nimba otter shrew is solely classified as a member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenrecidae" title="Tenrecidae">Tenrecidae</a>, with African hedgehogs and rodents, based on morphological structures. New breakthroughs in <a href="/wiki/Genetic_testing" title="Genetic testing">genetic testing</a> are finding that it does belong in this family and subfamily <a href="/wiki/Potamogalinae" title="Potamogalinae">Potamogalinae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Dijk_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_Dijk-4">[4]</a></sup> Unfortunately, due to heavy mining operations for iron ore in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Nimba" title="Mount Nimba">Mount Nimba</a>, the fossil record is all but destroyed in that area. It is also difficult for scientists to gain access because the mountain crosses the borders of three different countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afconf_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-afconf-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_behavior">Ecology and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimba_otter_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nimba otter shrew is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiaquatic" title="Semiaquatic">semiaquatic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burnie_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burnie-3">[3]</a></sup> It resides in soft soils around creek beds and <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>. The Nimba otter shrew is a solitary creature and has only been seen with other shrews during mating seasons and when a mother is nursing newly born young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stephan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stephan-6">[6]</a></sup> The breeding pattern of the Nimba otter shrew is also unknown, but believed to be polygamous; as there have been no witnessed accounts of breeding in the wild and the Nimba otter shrew will not mate in captivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amori_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amori-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nimba_otter_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IUCN had listed the Nimba otter shrew as endangered in 1990, and it has remained as such since the initial listing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is confined to an area less than 5,000 square km on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Nimba" title="Mount Nimba">Mount Nimba</a>, which is currently fragmented due to mining and wetland rice agriculture. The mining operations also produce <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> into the creeks and streambeds that is highly toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amori_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amori-7">[7]</a></sup> The current population is decreasing at a rate of 1 per 10 square km (almost 500 otter shrews per year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> Although an exact number is unknown at this time, there is believed to be less than several hundred in captivity and 2500-3500 in the wild. At this rate the Nimba otter shrew will be extinct between 2017 and 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amori_et_al_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amori_et_al-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
