<p><i><b>Nip/Tuck</b></i> is an American television drama series created by <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Murphy_(writer)" title="Ryan Murphy (writer)">Ryan Murphy</a>; it aired on <a href="/wiki/FX_(TV_channel)" title="FX (TV channel)">FX</a> in the United States from July 18, 2003 to March 3, 2010. The series focuses on "McNamara/Troy", a cutting-edge, controversial <a href="/wiki/Plastic_surgery" title="Plastic surgery">plastic surgery</a> center—but, particularly its founders: Drs. <a href="/wiki/Sean_McNamara_(Nip/Tuck)" title="Sean McNamara (Nip/Tuck)">Sean McNamara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Troy" title="Christian Troy">Christian Troy</a> (portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Walsh" title="Dylan Walsh">Dylan Walsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julian_McMahon" title="Julian McMahon">Julian McMahon</a>). Each episode featured graphic, partial-depictions of the plastic surgeries of one or more patients (who requested both popular [e.g., <a href="/wiki/Breast_augmentation" title="Breast augmentation">breast augmentation</a>] and exotic [e.g., "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaginal_rejuvenation" title="Vaginal rejuvenation">vaginal rejuvenation</a>"] procedures—as well as removals of obscure skin diseases). Much of the series' drama is derived from the tumultuous personal lives of its main characters (including the doctors' loved ones).</p>
<p>The show premiered on July 22, 2003, and concluded on March 3, 2010, with the 100th episode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-season_6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-season_6-2">[2]</a></sup> While the show was initially set in Miami, at the end of the fourth season it was relocated to Los Angeles and many of the characters have followed along. The show has earned 45 award nominations, winning one <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Series creator Ryan Murphy has said that the medical cases on the show are "100 percent based on fact".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nip/Tuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The drama is set in a plastic surgery center, McNamara/Troy, centering on the two doctors who own it. Sean McNamara (<a href="/wiki/Dylan_Walsh" title="Dylan Walsh">Dylan Walsh</a>) is often found having problems at home due to being seduced by beautiful women on a daily basis, and thus tries to keep his family together by patching up the rocky road he and his family are living in. On the other hand, partner Christian Troy (<a href="/wiki/Julian_McMahon" title="Julian McMahon">Julian McMahon</a>) uses his charm to bring in potential female candidates and conducts vain business deals, almost never failing and ending up with dozens of women in bed. Sean takes his job seriously; he is often found having to fix some of Christian's mistakes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nip/Tuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In its debut season, <i>Nip/Tuck</i> was the highest-<a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">rated</a> new series on American <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">basic cable</a>, and the highest rated basic cable series of all time for the 18–49 and 25–54 age demographics. The fifth season premiered on October 30, 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> though production was affected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="2007–2008 Writers Guild of America strike">2007 Writers Strike</a>. Accordingly, the second half of the fifth season was not screened until January 6, 2009 in the U.S. Another 19 episodes were picked up by FX; airing on October 14, 2009. Following a 3-week hiatus for the Christmas holidays, the show resumed in January 2010 and concluded on March 3, 2010 with its 100th episode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Nip/Tuck</i> filmed its 100th and final episode on June 12, 2009, without creator Ryan Murphy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> who was, at the time, in India scouting locations for his <a href="/wiki/Eat_Pray_Love" title="Eat Pray Love">film version</a> of the memoir <i><a href="/wiki/Eat,_Pray,_Love" title="Eat, Pray, Love">Eat, Pray, Love</a></i>.</p>
<p>The show inspired the creation of the plastic surgery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reality_show" title="Reality show">reality show</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._90210" title="Dr. 90210">Dr. 90210</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Nip/Tuck</i> became an instant cable hit from its 2003 series premiere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>For its third season, FX aired <i>Nip/Tuck</i> solely in the fall of 2005, instead of during the summer season like the two years prior. John Landgraf, president of FX, stated that such a move was a "huge risk" since it stacked up "against the full barrage of fall network competition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005finale_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-2005finale-13">[13]</a></sup> And despite some criticism on its third season, the story arc involving <a href="/wiki/Carver_(Nip/Tuck)" title="Carver (Nip/Tuck)">The Carver</a> attracted even more of an audience to the series than any of the seasons before, reaching its climax in a December 20, 2005 two-hour season finale, entitled <i>Cherry Peck / Quentin Costa</i>, which became the most-watched scripted program in the history of the FX network.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Including <i>Cherry Peck / Quentin Costa</i>, three episodes of <i>Nip/Tuck</i> rank as the three most-watched scripted programs ever on FX.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The second season finale, entitled <i>Joan Rivers</i>, which aired on October 5, 2004, drew 5.2 million viewers. It was then eclipsed on September 20, 2005, when the third season premiere, entitled <i>Momma Boone</i>, drew roughly 5.3 million viewers. Exactly three months later on December 20, 2005, the aforementioned third season finale, entitled <i>Cherry Peck / Quentin Costa</i>, drew 5.7 million viewers. Of those 5.7 million viewers, 3.9 million viewers were in the 18–49 age group demographic, "making the finale the No. 1 episode among the key advertising demographic of any cable series in 2005. It's also the largest demographic number for any single telecast in the network's history,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005finale_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-2005finale-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since October 2013">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> according to <a class="external text" href="http://www.zap2it.com" rel="nofollow">Zap2It</a>.</p>
<p>According to the September 8, 2006 <a class="external text" href="http://www.mediaweek.com" rel="nofollow">Mediaweek</a> column <i>The Programming Insider</i>, "the fourth season-premiere on Tuesday, September 5, 2006, averaged 4.8 million total viewers and 3.4 million adults 18–49, building over its season three average by 25 percent and 26 percent, respectively. <i>Nip/Tuck</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s performance among adults 18–49 ranks as basic cable’s top-rated season-premiere in the demo for 2006, as of September 8, 2006."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006premiere_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-2006premiere-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
