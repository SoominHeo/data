<p><b>Futarasan jinja</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">二荒山神社</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine" title="Shinto shrine">Shinto shrine</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Nikk%C5%8D,_Tochigi" title="Nikkō, Tochigi">Nikkō, Tochigi</a> Prefecture, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is also known as Nikkō Futarasan Shrine, to distinguish it from the shrine in nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utsunomiya,_Tochigi" title="Utsunomiya, Tochigi">Utsunomiya</a>. Futarasan enshrines three deities: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ookuninushi" title="Ookuninushi">Ōkuninushi</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tagorihime&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tagorihime (page does not exist)">Tagorihime</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ajisukitakahikone" title="Ajisukitakahikone">Ajisukitakahikone</a>. It is located between <a href="/wiki/Nikk%C5%8D_T%C5%8Dsh%C5%8D-g%C5%AB" title="Nikkō Tōshō-gū">Nikkō Tōshō-gū</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taiy%C5%AB-in_Mausoleum" title="Taiyū-in Mausoleum">Taiyū-in Mausoleum</a>. Many visitors go to all three, as well as to <a href="/wiki/Rinn%C5%8D-ji" title="Rinnō-ji">Rinnō-ji</a>.</p>
<p>Together with Nikkō Tōshō-gū and Rinnō-ji, it forms the <a href="/wiki/Shrines_and_Temples_of_Nikk%C5%8D" title="Shrines and Temples of Nikkō">Shrines and Temples of Nikkō</a> <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. The shrine possesses two swords that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_treasures_of_Japan" title="National treasures of Japan">National treasures of Japan</a>. Additionally, dozens of buildings and cultural artifacts are listed as Important Cultural Assets.</p>
<p>The Sacred Bridge <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">神橋</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>shinkyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> crossing the Daiya River belongs to the Futarasan Shrine. This beautiful vermilion lacquered structure is known as one of the three most beautiful bridges in Japan and is a perfect gateway for Nikko. The bridge was registered as a World Heritage in December 1999. Shinkyo measures 28 meters long, 7.4 meters wide, and stands 10.6 meters above the Daiya River.</p>
<p>According to legend, a priest named Shōdō and his followers climbed Mt. Nantai in the year 766 to pray for national prosperity. However, they could not cross the fast flowing Daiya River. Shōdō prayed and a 10 foot tall god named Jinja-Daiou appeared with two snakes twisted around his right arm. Jinja-Daiou released the blue and red snakes and they transformed themselves into a rainbow-like bridge covered with sedge, which Shōdō and his followers could use to cross the river. That is why this bridge is sometimes called Yamasugeno-jabashi, which means the "Snake Bridge of Sedge".</p>
<p>The Shinkyo has been rebuilt many times but has followed the same design pattern since 1636, when it could be used only by messengers of the Imperial court. It has been opened to the general public since 1973.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Futarasan_jinja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shrine was founded in 767 by Shōdō <i>shōnin</i> (勝道上人). The shrine takes its name from <a href="/wiki/Mount_Nantai" title="Mount Nantai">Mount Nantai</a>, which is also called Futarasan <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">二荒山</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yayoi_period">Yayoi period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Futarasan_jinja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Yayoi period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Archeologists affirm that during the <a href="/wiki/Yayoi_period" title="Yayoi period">Yayoi period</a> the most common go-shintai <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">御神体</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (a <i><a href="/wiki/Yorishiro" title="Yorishiro">yorishiro</a></i> housing the enshrined <i>kami</i>) in the earliest <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine" title="Shinto shrine">Shinto shrines</a> was a nearby mountain peak supplying with its streams water, and therefore life, to the plains below, where people lived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mount Nantai constitutes Futarasan Shrine's <i>go-shintai</i>, and the shrine is an important example of this ancient type of mountain cult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-1">[1]</a></sup> Significantly, the name Nantai <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">男体</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> itself means "man's body".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-1">[1]</a></sup> The mountain not only provides water to the rice paddies below, but has the shape of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phallic_symbol" title="Phallic symbol">phallic</a> stone rods found in pre-agricultural Jōmon sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heian_period">Heian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Futarasan_jinja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Heian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Futarasan was designated as the chief Shinto shrine (<i><a href="/wiki/Ichinomiya" title="Ichinomiya">ichinomiya</a></i>) for the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shimotsuke_province" title="Shimotsuke province">Shimotsuke province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meiji-Showa_periods">Meiji-Showa periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Futarasan_jinja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meiji-Showa periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1871 through 1946, Futarasan was officially designated one of the <i>Kokuhei Chūsha</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">国幣中社</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, meaning that it stood in the mid-range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_system_of_ranked_Shinto_Shrines" title="Modern system of ranked Shinto Shrines">ranked, nationally significant shrines</a>.</p>
