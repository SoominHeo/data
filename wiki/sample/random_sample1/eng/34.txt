<p>"<b>10538 Overture</b>", released in 1972, was the first single by <a href="/wiki/Electric_Light_Orchestra" title="Electric Light Orchestra">The Electric Light Orchestra</a> (ELO).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=10538_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song, written by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Lynne" title="Jeff Lynne">Jeff Lynne</a>, was first recorded as an intended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-side" title="B-side">B-side</a> on one of <a href="/wiki/The_Move" title="The Move">The Move</a>'s singles. Both <a href="/wiki/Roy_Wood" title="Roy Wood">Roy Wood</a> and Lynne sang on it, as happened later with The Move's "<a href="/wiki/California_Man_(song)" title="California Man (song)">California Man</a>". The song is about an escaped prisoner; Lynne wanted to give the character in the song a number, as opposed to a name, and he chanced upon the number 1053 while looking at the mixing console. Wood suggested adding an "8" to fit the melody better. Although intended to be a song for The Move, after <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> parts were added it became the Electric Light Orchestra's <a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Light_Orchestra_(album)" title="The Electric Light Orchestra (album)">first release</a>. It was during the single's chart run that Wood left ELO, emerging later in the year with a new band called <a href="/wiki/Wizzard" title="Wizzard">Wizzard</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=10538_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>10538 Overture was an idea that Jeff (Lynne) brought along to the studio which was originally to be a Move track. After recording the basic backing track, the other guys went home, leaving Jeff and myself to run riot with the overdubs. At the time, I was very keen on collecting instruments, and had just acquired a cheap Chinese cello. After we had finished overdubbing the guitars, I sat in the control room trying out this cello and sort of messing around with <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> type riffs. Jeff said, 'That sounds great, why don't we throw it on the track.' I ended up recording around fifteen of these, and as the instrumentation built up, it was beginning to sound like some monster heavy metal orchestra. In fact, it sounded just bloody marvellous.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Roy Wood, March 28, 2006 - <i>No Answer</i> Remaster</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I had this guitar track, like a real big riff on a guitar. I laid it down in the studio and Roy Wood got his cello, his Chinese cello, and he overdubbed about fifteen cello riffs, just double tracking all the time-- and it sounded fantastic. We thought, it was like 'Wow!' and we just sat round playing it for days.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Jeff Lynne, 31 July 2006 - <i>The Harvest Years 1970-1973</i> liner notes</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="B-side">B-side</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=10538_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: B-side">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The B-side to "10538 Overture" was "First Movement (Jumping Biz)", an instrumental by Wood. The song first appeared on the band's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Light_Orchestra_(album)" title="The Electric Light Orchestra (album)">The Electric Light Orchestra</a></i> in 1971 and became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-side" title="B-side">B-side</a> to "10538 Overture" and features Wood on <a href="/wiki/Classical_guitar" title="Classical guitar">classical guitar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a> and cello. Wood has apparently acknowledged that "First Movement (Jumping Biz)" was inspired by the 1968 song "<a href="/wiki/Classical_Gas" title="Classical Gas">Classical Gas</a>".</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>By [Roy Wood's] own admission First Movement (Jumping Biz) owed a debt to Mason Williams' transatlantic 1968 hit Classical Gas...</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Rob Caiger, March 28, 2006 - <i>No Answer</i> Remaster</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>"10538 Overture" became a B-side itself when a live version was released as the flip side of "<a href="/wiki/Evil_Woman_(Electric_Light_Orchestra_song)" title="Evil Woman (Electric Light Orchestra song)">Evil Woman</a>" in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Covers_and_other_uses">Covers and other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=10538_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Covers and other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song was covered by Bobby Sutcliff and <a href="/wiki/Mitch_Easter" title="Mitch Easter">Mitch Easter</a> in 2001 for the Jeff Lynne tribute album <i><a href="/wiki/Lynne_Me_Your_Ears" title="Lynne Me Your Ears">Lynne Me Your Ears</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Parthenon_Huxley" title="Parthenon Huxley">Parthenon Huxley</a> in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Def_Leppard" title="Def Leppard">Def Leppard</a> in 2006 on their cover album <i><a href="/wiki/Yeah!_(Def_Leppard_album)" title="Yeah! (Def Leppard album)">Yeah!</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The song's main guitar riff was also sampled by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Weller" title="Paul Weller">Paul Weller</a> for his 1995 song "<a href="/wiki/The_Changingman" title="The Changingman">The Changingman</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
