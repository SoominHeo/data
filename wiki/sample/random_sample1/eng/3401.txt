<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Polygon</h1>

<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elementary_geometry" title="Elementary geometry">elementary geometry</a>, a <b>polygon</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> <a href="/wiki/Shape" title="Shape">figure</a> that is bounded by a finite chain of straight <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segments</a> closing in a loop to form a closed <a href="/wiki/Polygonal_chain" title="Polygonal chain">polygonal chain</a> or <i>circuit</i>. These segments are called its <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a></i> or <i>sides</i>, and the points where two edges meet are the polygon's <i><a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a></i> (singular: vertex) or <i>corners</i>. The interior of the polygon is sometimes called its <i>body</i>. An <b><i>n</i>-gon</b> is a polygon with <i>n</i> sides; for example, a <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangle</a> is a 3-gon. A polygon is a 2-dimensional example of the more general <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytope</a> in any number of dimensions.</p>
<p>The basic geometrical notion of a polygon has been adapted in various ways to suit particular purposes. Mathematicians are often concerned only with the bounding closed polygonal chain and with <a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">simple polygons</a> which do not self-intersect, and they often define a polygon accordingly. A polygonal boundary may be allowed to intersect itself, creating <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygons</a> and other <a href="/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons" title="List of self-intersecting polygons">self-intersecting polygons</a>. These and other <a href="#Generalizations_of_polygons">generalizations of polygons</a> are described below.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "polygon" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> adjective πολύς (<i>polús</i>) "much", "many" and γωνία (<i>gōnía</i>) "corner" or "angle". It has been suggested that γόνυ (<i>gónu</i>) "knee" may be the origin of “gon”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_sides">Number of sides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Number of sides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Polygons are primarily classified by the number of sides. See <a href="#Naming_polygons">table below</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Convexity_and_non-convexity">Convexity and non-convexity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Convexity and non-convexity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Polygons may be characterized by their convexity or type of non-convexity:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convex_polygon" title="Convex polygon">Convex</a>: any line drawn through the polygon (and not tangent to an edge or corner) meets its boundary exactly twice. As a consequence, all its interior angles are less than 180°. Equivalently, any line segment with endpoints on the boundary passes through only interior points between its endpoints.</li>
<li>Non-convex: a line may be found which meets its boundary more than twice. Equivalently, there exists a line segment between two boundary points that passes outside the polygon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">Simple</a>: the boundary of the polygon does not cross itself. All convex polygons are simple.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Concave_polygon" title="Concave polygon">Concave</a>. Non-convex and simple. There is at least one interior angle greater than 180°.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Star-shaped_polygon" title="Star-shaped polygon">Star-shaped</a>: the whole interior is visible from at least one point, without crossing any edge. The polygon must be simple, and may be convex or concave.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons" title="List of self-intersecting polygons">Self-intersecting</a>: the boundary of the polygon crosses itself. <a href="/wiki/Branko_Gr%C3%BCnbaum" title="Branko Grünbaum">Branko Grünbaum</a> calls these <b>coptic</b>, though this term does not seem to be widely used. The term <i>complex</i> is sometimes used in contrast to <i>simple</i>, but this usage risks confusion with the idea of a <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_polytope" title="Complex polytope">complex polygon</a></i> as one which exists in the complex <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert</a> plane consisting of two <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex</a> dimensions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">Star polygon</a>: a polygon which self-intersects in a regular way. A polygon cannot be both a star and star-shaped.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equality_and_symmetry">Equality and symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equality and symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Equiangular_polygon" title="Equiangular polygon">Equiangular</a>: all corner angles are equal.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclic_polygon" title="Cyclic polygon">Cyclic</a>: all corners lie on a single <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a>, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumcircle" title="Circumcircle">circumcircle</a>.</li>
<li>Isogonal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertex-transitive" title="Vertex-transitive">vertex-transitive</a>: all corners lie within the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_orbit" title="Symmetry orbit">symmetry orbit</a>. The polygon is also cyclic and equiangular.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Equilateral_polygon" title="Equilateral polygon">Equilateral</a>: all edges are of the same length. The polygon need not be convex.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tangential_polygon" title="Tangential polygon">Tangential</a>: all sides are tangent to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inscribed_circle" title="Inscribed circle">inscribed circle</a>.</li>
<li>Isotoxal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge-transitive" title="Edge-transitive">edge-transitive</a>: all sides lie within the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_orbit" title="Symmetry orbit">symmetry orbit</a>. The polygon is also equilateral and tangential.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">Regular</a>: the polygon is both <i>isogonal</i> and <i>isotoxal</i>. Equivalently, it is both <i>cyclic</i> and <i>equilateral</i>, or both <i>equilateral</i> and <i>equiangular</i>. A non-convex regular polygon is called a <i>regular <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygon</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous">Miscellaneous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon" title="Rectilinear polygon">Rectilinear</a>: the polygon's sides meet at right angles, i.e., all its interior angles are 90 or 270 degrees.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monotone_polygon" title="Monotone polygon">Monotone</a> with respect to a given line <i>L</i>: every line <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_(geometry)" title="Orthogonal (geometry)">orthogonal</a> to L intersects the polygon not more than twice.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_formulas">Properties and formulas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Properties and formulas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a> is assumed throughout.</p>

<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exterior_angle" title="Exterior angle">Exterior angle</a></b> – The exterior angle is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supplementary_angle" title="Supplementary angle">supplementary angle</a> to the interior angle. Tracing around a convex <i>n</i>-gon, the angle "turned" at a corner is the exterior or external angle. Tracing all the way around the polygon makes one full <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">turn</a>, so the sum of the exterior angles must be 360°. This argument can be generalized to concave simple polygons, if external angles that turn in the opposite direction are subtracted from the total turned. Tracing around an <i>n</i>-gon in general, the sum of the exterior angles (the total amount one rotates at the vertices) can be any integer multiple <i>d</i> of 360°, e.g. 720° for a <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a> and 0° for an angular "eight" or <a href="/wiki/Antiparallelogram" title="Antiparallelogram">antiparallelogram</a>, where <i>d</i> is the density or starriness of the polygon. See also <a href="/wiki/Orbit_(dynamics)" title="Orbit (dynamics)">orbit (dynamics)</a>.</li>

<p>The lengths of the sides of a polygon do not in general determine the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, if the polygon is cyclic the sides <i>do</i> determine the area.</p>
<p>Of all <i>n</i>-gons with given sides, the one with the largest area is cyclic. Of all <i>n</i>-gons with a given perimeter, the one with the largest area is regular (and therefore cyclic).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-intersecting_polygons">Self-intersecting polygons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Self-intersecting polygons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The area of a <a href="/wiki/Complex_polygon" title="Complex polygon">self-intersecting polygon</a> can be defined in two different ways, each of which gives a different answer:</p>
<ul>
<li>Using the above methods for simple polygons, we allow that particular regions within the polygon may have their area multiplied by a factor which we call the <i>density</i> of the region. For example, the central convex pentagon in the center of a pentagram has density 2. The two triangular regions of a cross-quadrilateral (like a figure 8) have opposite-signed densities, and adding their areas together can give a total area of zero for the whole figure.</li>
<li>Considering the enclosed regions as point sets, we can find the area of the enclosed point set. This corresponds to the area of the plane covered by the polygon, or to the area of one or more simple polygons having the same outline as the self-intersecting one. In the case of the cross-quadrilateral, it is treated as two simple triangles.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations_of_polygons">Generalizations of polygons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Generalizations of polygons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of a polygon has been generalized in various ways. Some of the more important include:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical_polygon" title="Spherical polygon">spherical polygon</a> is a circuit of arcs of great circles (sides) and vertices on the surface of a sphere. It allows the <a href="/wiki/Digon" title="Digon">digon</a>, a polygon having only two sides and two corners, which is impossible in a flat plane. Spherical polygons play an important role in <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a> (map making) and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wythoff%27s_construction" title="Wythoff's construction">Wythoff's construction</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uniform_polyhedra" title="Uniform polyhedra">uniform polyhedra</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Skew_polygon" title="Skew polygon">skew polygon</a> does not lie in a flat plane, but zigzags in three (or more) dimensions. The <a href="/wiki/Petrie_polygon" title="Petrie polygon">Petrie polygons</a> of the regular polytopes are well known examples.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Apeirogon" title="Apeirogon">apeirogon</a> is an infinite sequence of sides and angles, which is not closed but has no ends because it extends indefinitely in both directions.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Skew_apeirogon" title="Skew apeirogon">skew apeirogon</a> is an infinite sequence of sides and angles that do not lie in a flat plane.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Complex_polytope" title="Complex polytope">complex polygon</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Configuration_(geometry)" title="Configuration (geometry)">configuration</a> analogous to an ordinary polygon, which exists in the <a href="/wiki/Complex_plane" title="Complex plane">complex plane</a> of two <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number">imaginary</a> dimensions.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Abstract_polytope" title="Abstract polytope">abstract polygon</a> is an algebraic <a href="/wiki/Partially_ordered_set" title="Partially ordered set">partially ordered set</a> representing the various elements (sides, vertices, etc.) and their connectivity. A real geometric polygon is said to be a <i>realization</i> of the associated abstract polygon. Depending on the mapping, all the generalizations described here can be realized.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> is a three-dimensional solid bounded by flat polygonal faces, analogous to a polygon in two dimensions. The corresponding shapes in four or higher dimensions are called <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytopes</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_polygons">Naming polygons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Naming polygons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "polygon" comes from <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>polygōnum</i> (a noun), from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> πολύγωνον (<i>polygōnon/polugōnon</i>), noun use of neuter of πολύγωνος (<i>polygōnos/polugōnos</i>, the masculine adjective), meaning "many-angled". Individual polygons are named (and sometimes classified) according to the number of sides, combining a <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>-derived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_prefix" title="Numerical prefix">numerical prefix</a> with the suffix <i>-gon</i>, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dodecagon" title="Dodecagon">dodecagon</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quadrilateral" title="Quadrilateral">quadrilateral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonagon" title="Nonagon">nonagon</a> are exceptions.</p>
<p>Beyond decagons (10-sided) and dodecagons (12-sided), mathematicians generally use numerical notation, for example 17-gon and 257-gon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathworld_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mathworld-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Exceptions exist for side counts that are more easily expressed in verbal form (e.g. 20 and 30), or are used by non-mathematicians. Some special polygons also have their own names; for example the <a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular</a> <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star</a> <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a> is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="564" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/220px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/330px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/440px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Historical image of polygons (1699)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Polygons have been known since ancient times. The <a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular polygons</a> were known to the ancient Greeks, with the <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a>, a non-convex regular polygon (<a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygon</a>), appearing as early as the 7th century B.C. on a <a href="/wiki/Krater" title="Krater">krater</a> by Aristonothos, found at <a href="/wiki/Caere" title="Caere">Caere</a> and now in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitoline_Museum" title="Capitoline Museum">Capitoline Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first known systematic study of non-convex polygons in general was made by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bradwardine" title="Thomas Bradwardine">Thomas Bradwardine</a> in the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1952, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Colin_Shephard" title="Geoffrey Colin Shephard">Geoffrey Colin Shephard</a> generalized the idea of polygons to the complex plane, where each <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real</a> dimension is accompanied by an <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number">imaginary</a> one, to create <a href="/wiki/Complex_polytope" title="Complex polytope">complex polygons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polygons_in_nature">Polygons in nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Polygons in nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="192" data-file-width="256" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg/220px-Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7f/Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant's Causeway">Giant's Causeway</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Polygons appear in rock formations, most commonly as the flat facets of <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>, where the angles between the sides depend on the type of mineral from which the crystal is made.</p>
<p>Regular hexagons can occur when the cooling of <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> forms areas of tightly packed columns of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>, which may be seen at the <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant's Causeway">Giant's Causeway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, or at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Postpile" title="Devil's Postpile">Devil's Postpile</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, the surface of the wax <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb" title="Honeycomb">honeycomb</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a> is an array of <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagons</a>, and the sides and base of each cell are also polygons.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polygons_in_computer_graphics">Polygons in computer graphics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Polygons in computer graphics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>

<p>A polygon in a <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">computer graphics</a> (image generation) system is a two-dimensional shape that is modelled and stored within its database. A polygon can be colored, shaded and textured, and its position in the database is defined by the coordinates of its vertices (corners).</p>
<p>Naming conventions differ from those of mathematicians:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">simple polygon</a> does not cross itself.</li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Concave_polygon" title="Concave polygon">concave polygon</a> is a simple polygon having at least one interior angle greater than 180°.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Complex_polygon" title="Complex polygon">complex polygon</a> does cross itself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="differs from standard mathematical usage (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Any surface is modelled as a tessellation called <a href="/wiki/Polygon_mesh" title="Polygon mesh">polygon mesh</a>. If a square mesh has <span class="nowrap"><i>n</i> + 1</span> points (vertices) per side, there are <i>n</i> squared squares in the mesh, or 2<i>n</i> squared triangles since there are two triangles in a square. There are <span class="nowrap">(<i>n</i> + 1)<sup>2</sup> / 2(<i>n</i><sup>2</sup>)</span> vertices per triangle. Where <i>n</i> is large, this approaches one half. Or, each vertex inside the square mesh connects four edges (lines).</p>
<p>The imaging system calls up the structure of polygons needed for the scene to be created from the database. This is transferred to active memory and finally, to the display system (screen, TV monitors etc.) so that the scene can be viewed. During this process, the imaging system renders polygons in correct perspective ready for transmission of the processed data to the display system. Although polygons are two-dimensional, through the system computer they are placed in a visual scene in the correct three-dimensional orientation.</p>
<p>In computer graphics and <a href="/wiki/Computational_geometry" title="Computational geometry">computational geometry</a>, it is often necessary to determine whether a given point <i>P</i> = (<i>x</i><sub>0</sub>,<i>y</i><sub>0</sub>) lies inside a simple polygon given by a sequence of line segments. This is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_in_polygon_test" title="Point in polygon test">Point in polygon test</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="differs from standard mathematical usage (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
