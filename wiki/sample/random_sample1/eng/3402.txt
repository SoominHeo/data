<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Multiferroics</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Multiferroics</b> are defined as materials that exhibit more than one of the primary <a href="/wiki/Ferroics" title="Ferroics">ferroic</a> order parameters</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetism</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ferroelectricity" title="Ferroelectricity">ferroelectricity</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ferroelasticity" title="Ferroelasticity">ferroelasticity</a></li>
</ul>
<p>in the same phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> While ferroelectric ferroelastics (with their associated piezoelectric and electrostrictive coupling) and ferromagnetic ferroelastics (with piezomagnetic and magnetomechanical coupling) are formally multiferroics, these days the term is usually used to describe the <i><a href="/wiki/Magnetoelectric_effect#cite_note-5" title="Magnetoelectric effect">magnetoelectric</a> multiferroics</i> that are simultaneously ferromagnetic and ferroelectric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> Sometimes the definition is expanded to include non-primary order parameters, such as <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetism" title="Antiferromagnetism">antiferromagnetism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ferrimagnetism" title="Ferrimagnetism">ferrimagnetism</a>. In addition other types of primary order, such as ferroic arrangements of magneotelectric multipoles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> of which <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_moment" title="Toroidal moment">ferrotoroidicity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is an example, have also been recently proposed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="676" data-file-width="1281" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png/220px-Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png/330px-Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png/440px-Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Multiferroics_history_use_of_terms_magnetoelectric_and_multiferroic.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
History of multiferroics: Number of papers per year on magnetoelectrics or the magnetoelectric effect (in blue), and on multiferroics (in red).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Web_of_Science" title="Web of Science">Web of Science</a> search for the term "multiferroic*" yields the year 2000 paper <i>Why are there so few magnetic ferroelectrics?</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> from N. A. <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Spaldin" title="Nicola Spaldin">Spaldin</a> (then Hill) as the earliest result. This work explained the origin of the contraindication between magnetism and ferroelectricity and proposed practical routes to circumvent it, and is widely credited with starting the modern explosion of interest in multiferroic materials. (There is a very nice article on how Spaldin arrived at the question <a class="external text" href="http://science.sciencemag.org/content/349/6243/110.e-letters" rel="nofollow">here</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>). The graph to the right shows in red the number of papers on multiferroics from a Web of Science search until 2008; the exponential increase continues today.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Magnetoelectric_effect#cite_note-5" title="Magnetoelectric effect">Magnetoelectric materials</a>, in which an electric field induces a magnetisation that is linear in the applied field and vice versa, and the corresponding magnetoelectric effect have a longer history, shown in blue in the graph to the right. (Note that while magnetoelectric materials are not necessarily multiferroic, all ferromagnetic ferroelectric multiferroics are magneto electric.) The first known mention of magnetoelectricity is in the 1959 Edition of Landau &amp; Lifshitz' <i>Electrodynamics of Continuous Media</i> which has the following comment at the end of the section on <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectricity</a>: <i>“Let us point out two more phenomena, which, in principle, could exist. One is piezomagnetism, which consists of linear coupling between a magnetic field in a solid and a deformation (analogous to piezoelectricity). The other is a linear coupling between magnetic and electric fields in a media, which would cause, for example, a magnetization proportional to an electric field. Both these phenomena could exist for certain classes of magnetocrystalline symmetry. We will not however discuss these phenomena in more detail because it seems that till present, presumably, they have not been observed in any substance.”</i> One year later, <a class="external text" href="http://www.itp.ac.ru/en/persons/dzyaloshinskii-igor-ekhielevich/" rel="nofollow">I. E. Dzyaloshinskii</a> showed using symmetry arguments that the material Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> should have linear magnetoelectric behavior,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and his prediction was rapidly verified by D. Astrov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Over the next decades, research on magnetoelectric materials continued steadily in a number of groups in Europe, in particular in the former Soviet Union and in the group of <a class="external text" href="http://physicstoday.scitation.org/do/10.1063/PT.5.6173/full/" rel="nofollow">H. Schmid</a> at U. Geneva. A series of East-West conferences entitled Magnetoelectric Interaction Phenomena in Crystals (MEIPIC) was held between 1973 (in Seattle) and <a class="external text" href="http://www.icmr.ucsb.edu/programs/MEIPIC6.html" rel="nofollow">2009 (in Santa Barbara)</a>, and indeed the term multiferroic was first used by H. Schmid in the proceedings of the 1993 MEIPIC conference (in Ascona).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The availability of practical routes to creating multiferroic materials from 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> stimulated intense activity. Particularly key early works were the discovery of large ferroelectric polarization in epitaxially grown thin films of magnetic BiFeO<sub>3</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the observation that the non-collinear magnetic ordering in orthorhombic TbMnO<sub>3</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and TbMn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> causes ferroelectricity, and the identification of unusual improper ferroelectricity that is compatible with the coexistence of magnetism in hexagonal manganite YMnO<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symmetry">Symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ferroic properties are closely linked to symmetry and can be characterized by their behavior under <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">space inversion</a> and <a href="/wiki/T-symmetry" title="T-symmetry">time reversal</a> (see table). The operation of space inversion reverses the direction of polarization P while leaving the magnetization M invariant. As a result ferromagnets and ferroelastics are invariant under space inversion whereas ferromagnets are not. The operation of time reversal, on the other hand, changes the sign of M, while the sign of P remains invariant. Therefore ferroelastics and ferroelectrics are invariant under time reversal whereas ferromagnets are not.</p>

<tr>

<p>Magnetoelectric multiferroics violate both space-inversion and time-reversal symmetries since they are both ferromagnetic and ferroelectric.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many multiferroics are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_metal_oxides" title="Transition metal oxides">transition metal oxides</a> with <a href="/wiki/Perovskite_(structure)" title="Perovskite (structure)">perovskite</a> crystal structure. They can be generally subdivided into two classes as introduced by D. Khomskii: Type-I and type-II multiferroics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Type-I multiferroics which have been known for a long time are often good ferroelectrics and antiferromagnetic. They exhibit high ferroelectric and lower magnetic ordering temperatures. Examples are <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_ferrite" title="Bismuth ferrite">BiFeO<sub>3</sub></a> (T<sub>C</sub> = 1100 K, T<sub>N</sub> = 643 K) and YMnO<sub>3</sub> (T<sub>C</sub> = 914 K, T<sub>N</sub> = 76 K) and probably BiMnO<sub>3</sub> and PbVO<sub>3</sub>. Since ferroelectricity and magnetism develop independently from another, the <a href="/wiki/Magnetoelectric_effect" title="Magnetoelectric effect">magnetoelectric coupling</a> between the two is usually weak. In a first approach, they might be regarded as ferroelectrics which happen to be (antiferro)magnetic. However, more recently there have been reports of large magnetoelectric coupling at room-temperature in type-I multiferroics such as in the "diluted" magnetic perovskite (PbZr<sub>0.53</sub>Ti<sub>0.47</sub>O<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.6</sub>–(PbFe<sub>1/2</sub>Ta<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.4</sub> (PZTFT) in certain Aurivillius phases, and in the system (BiFe<sub>0.9</sub>Co<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.4</sub>-(Bi<sub>1/2</sub>K<sub>1/2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.6</sub> (BFC-BKT). Here, strong ME coupling has been observed on a microscopic scale using <a href="/wiki/Piezoresponse_force_microscopy" title="Piezoresponse force microscopy">PFM</a> under magnetic field among other techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The latter system, appears to be the first reported core-shell type <a href="/wiki/Relaxor_ferroelectric" title="Relaxor ferroelectric">relaxor ferroelectric</a> multiferroic, where the magnetic structure in so-called "multiferroic clusters" is proposed to be due Fe-Co ferrimagnetism, which can be switched by an electric field.</p>
<p>Type-II multiferroics include rare-earth manganites such as TbMnO<sub>3</sub>, HoMn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>. Here, magnetism causes ferroelectricity and ordering temperatures are usually identical, which are, however, at very low temperatures (e.g. 28 K in case of TbMnO<sub>3</sub>). Moreover, they exhibit low net magnetization due to antiferromagnetic spin-spiral structures and low polarization of the order of 10<sup>−2</sup> μC/cm<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other, non-perovskite multiferroic oxides include LuFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and LiCu<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and non-oxides such as BaNiF<sub>4</sub> and spinel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalcogenides" title="Chalcogenides">chalcogenides</a>, e.g. ZnCr<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. These compounds show rich phase diagrams combining different ferroic orders in separate phases.</p>
<p>Apart from single phase multiferroics, composites and heterostructures exhibiting more than one ferroic order parameter are studied extensively. Some examples include magnetic thin films on piezoelectric PMN-PT substrates and Metglass/PVDF/Metglass trilayer structures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Besides scientific interest in their physical properties, multiferroics have potential for applications as actuators, switches, magnetic field sensors or new types of electronic memory devices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms_for_ferroelectricity_in_multiferroics">Mechanisms for ferroelectricity in multiferroics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mechanisms for ferroelectricity in multiferroics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A necessary but not sufficient condition for the appearance of spontaneous electric polarization is the absence of inversion symmetry. We can distinguish between proper and improper ferroelectric (FE). The difference lies in the driving force (the primary order parameter) that leads to ferroelectricity: In the case of proper FE, the primary order parameter is the ferroelectric distortion. One example of proper FE is BaTiO<sub>3</sub> where a covalent bonding between the <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> and the oxygen happens to allow a polar state. In usual perovskite-based ferroelectrics like BaTiO<sub>3</sub>, the ferroelectric distortion occurs due to the displacement of B-site cation (Ti) with respect to the oxygen octahedral cage. Here the transition metal ion (Ti in BaTiO<sub>3</sub> ) requires an empty “d” shell since the ferroelectric displacement occurs due to the hopping of electrons between Ti “d” and O “p” atoms.</p>
<p>For technological applications, it is highly desirable to combine ferroelectric and ferromagnetic order within one material, but it has become clear that usual displacive ferroelectric order, e.g. like in BaTiO<sub>3</sub>, cannot coexist together with magnetic order. Whereas the latter requires at least partially filled d-shells (e.g.-orbitals) providing a non-zero <a href="/wiki/Electron_magnetic_moment" title="Electron magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a>, usual displacive ferroelectricity requires empty d-shells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, new ferroelectric driving mechanisms must be present for electric and magnetic ferroic order to occur simultaneously. A variety of driving mechanism is described below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lone_pair_multiferroics">Lone pair multiferroics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lone pair multiferroics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In usual perovskite-based ferroelectrics like BaTiO<sub>3</sub>, the ferroelectric distortion occurs due to the displacement of B-site cation (Ti) with respect to the oxygen octahedral cage. Here the transition metal ion (Ti in BaTiO<sub>3</sub> ) requires an empty "d" shell since the ferroelectric displacement occurs due to the hopping of electrons between Ti "d" and O p atoms. This normally excludes any net magnetic moment because magnetism requires partially filled "d" shells. However, partially filled "d" shell on the B-site reduces the tendency of perovskites to display ferroelectricity.</p>
<p>In order for the coexistence of magnetism and ferroelectricity (multiferroic), one possible mechanism is lone-pair driven<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> where the A-site drives the displacement and partially filled “d” shell on the B-site contributes to the magnetism. Examples include BiFeO<sub>3</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> BiMnO<sub>3</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> PbVO<sub>3</sub>. In the above materials, the A-site cation (Bi<sup>3+</sup>, Pb<sup>2+</sup>) has a stereochemically active 6s<sup>2</sup> lone-pair which causes the Bi 6p (empty) orbital to come closer in energy to the O 2p orbitals. This leads to hybridization between the Bi 6p and O 2p orbitals and drives the off-centering of the cation towards the neighboring anion resulting in ferroelectricity. In BiFeO<sub>3</sub>, for example, off-centering of ions gives rise to an electric polarization, while at a lower temperature additional magnetic ordering breaks time-reversal symmetry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improper_geometric_ferroelectricity">Improper geometric ferroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Improper geometric ferroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In improper geometric ferroelectrics a structural phase transition at high temperatures causes the appearance of ferroelectricity. A prototypical compound is the family of hexagonal rare earth manganites (h-<i>R</i>MnO<sub>3</sub> with <i>R</i>=Ho-Lu, Y), showing a structural phase transition at around 1300 K providing the necessary symmetry lowering by tilting of the MnO<sub>5</sub> bipyramids. This allows for an electrostatically driven corrugation of the <i>R</i>-ion layers. The value of the induced polarization of several µC/cm² is comparable to that of usual displacive ferroelectrics. The multiferroic phase is entered only at cryogenic temperatures when antiferromagnetic order due to spin frustration arises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Thus only weak, indirect coupling between two disparate order parameters can occur. The exact microscopic mechanism of the ferroelectric ordering in hexagonal <i>R</i>MnO<sub>3</sub> is still questionable in the scientific community, i.e. it is still matter of debate whether only the corrugation of <i>R</i>-ions is the origin of the electric polarization or whether an off-centering of Mn ions also contributes to the polarization.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charge_ordering">Charge ordering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Charge ordering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A possible origin for a multiferroic state is charge ordering. Such an order can occur in a compound containing ions of mixed valence and with geometrical or magnetic frustration. These ions form a polar arrangement, causing improper ferroelectricity (i.e. no ionic displacement). If magnetic ions are present, a coexisting magnetic order can be established and may be coupled to ferroelectricity</p>
<p>One prominent example for a charge ordered multiferroic is LuFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, which shows improper ferroelectricity below 330 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The arrangements of the electrons arise from the charge frustration on a triangular lattice with the mixed valence state of Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> ions. Ferrimagnetic behavior occurs below 240 K.</p>
<p>In addition, charge ordered ferroelectricity is suggested in Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and (Pr,Ca)MnO<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetically_driven_ferroelectricity">Magnetically driven ferroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Magnetically driven ferroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magnetically driven multiferroics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> are insulating materials, mostly oxides, in which macroscopic electric polarization is induced by magnetic long-range order. A necessary but not sufficient condition for the appearance of spontaneous electric polarization is the absence of inversion symmetry. We can distinguish between proper and improper ferroelectricity (FE). The difference lies in the driving force (the primary order parameter) that lead to ferroelectricity: In the case of proper FE, the primary order parameter is the ferroelectric distortion. One example of proper FE is BaTiO<sub>3</sub> where a covalent bonding between the <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> and the oxygen happens to allow a polar state.</p>
<p>In the case of improper FE, the primary order parameter is not the ferroelectric distortion but another type of phase change, like magnetic ordering or a structural change. The FE distortion is a secondary order parameter in the sense that it is driven by the presence of other order parameters. One example of improper FE is when the inversion symmetry of a crystal is broken by magnetic structure like spiral magnetic ordering. This is the spin-driven ferroelectric. The microscopic mechanism of magnetoelectric (ME) coupling in spiral multiferroics involves spin-orbit coupling. The polarization is smaller than the one of proper FE. ME coupling is very strong because ferroelectricity is driven by magnetic order and do not exist without the latter. That means that any change in the magnetic order will affect the ferroelectricity.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domains">Domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Like any ferroic material, a multiferroic system is fragmented into domains. A <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_domain" title="Magnetic domain">domain</a> is a spatially extended region with a constant direction and phase of its order parameters. Neighbouring domains are separated by transition regions called domain walls.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_multiferroic_domains">Properties of multiferroic domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Properties of multiferroic domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to materials with a single ferroic order, domains in multiferroics have additional properties and functionalities. For instance, they are characterized by an assembly of at least two order parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The order parameters may be independent (typical yet not mandatory for a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Split-order-parameter_multiferroic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Split-order-parameter multiferroic (page does not exist)">split-order-parameter multiferroic</a>) or coupled (mandatory for a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joint-order-parameter_multiferroic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joint-order-parameter multiferroic (page does not exist)">joint-order-parameter multiferroic</a>).</p>
<p>Many outstanding properties that distinguish domains in multiferroics from those in materials with a single ferroic order are consequences of the coupling between the order parameters.</p>
<ul>
<li>The coupling can lead to patterns with a distribution and/or topology of domains that is exclusive to multiferroics.</li>
<li>The order-parameter coupling is usually homogeneous across a domain, i.e., gradient effects are negligible.</li>
<li>In some cases the averaged net value of the order parameter for a domain pattern is more relevant for the coupling than the value of the order parameter of an individual domain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>These issues lead to novel functionalities which explain the current interest in these materials.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_multiferroic_domain_walls">Properties of multiferroic domain walls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Properties of multiferroic domain walls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Domain walls are spatially extended regions of transition mediating the transfer of the order parameter from one domain to another. In comparison to the domains the domain walls are not homogeneous and they can have a lower symmetry. This may modify the properties of a multiferroic and the coupling of its order parameters. Multiferroic domain walls may display particular static<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and dynamic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> properties.</p>
<p>Static properties refer to stationary walls. They can result from</p>
<ul>
<li>The reduced dimensionality</li>
<li>The finite width of the wall</li>
<li>The different symmetry of the wall</li>
<li>The inherent chemical, electronic, or order-parameter inhomogeneity within the walls and the resulting gradient effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Dynamic properties refer to moving walls. In a magnetic ferroelectric, the magnetoelectric interaction is, at its roots, usually synonymous to the movement of the multiferroic domain walls. Because of the order-parameter coupling this may reflect characteristic features of both, ferroelectric and magnetic domain wall movement.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strain_driven_magnetoelectric_heterostructured_effect">Strain driven magnetoelectric heterostructured effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Strain driven magnetoelectric heterostructured effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thin film strategy also enables achievement of interfacial multiferroic coupling through a mechanical channel in heterostructures consisting of a magnetoelastic and a piezoelectric component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> This type of heterostructure is composed of an epitaxial magnetoelastic thin film grown on a piezoelectric substrate. For this system, application of a magnetic field will induce a change in the dimension of the magnetoelastic film. This process, called magnetostriction, will alter residual strain conditions in the magnetoelastic film, which can be transferred through the interface to the piezoelectric substrate. Consequently, a polarization is introduced in the substrate through the piezoelectric process. The overall effect is that the polarization of the ferroelectric substrate is manipulated by an application of a magnetic field, which is the desired magnetoelectric effect. In this case, the interface plays an important role in mediating the responses from one component to another, realizing the magnetoelectric coupling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> For an efficient coupling, a high-quality interface with optimal strain state is desired. In light of this interest, advanced deposition techniques have been applied to synthesize these types of thin film heterostructures. Molecular beam epitaxy has been demonstrated to be capable of depositing structures consisting of piezoelectric and magnetostrictive components. Materials systems studied included cobalt ferrite, magnetite, SrTiO3, BaTiO3, PMNT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flexomagnetoelectric_effect">Flexomagnetoelectric effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Flexomagnetoelectric effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnetically driven ferroelectricity is also caused by inhomogeneous<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> magnetoelectric interaction. This effect appears due to the coupling between inhomogeneous order parameters. It was also called as flexomagnetoelectric effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Usually it is describing using the <a href="/wiki/Lifshitz" title="Lifshitz">Lifshitz</a> invariant (i.e. single-constant coupling term).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It was shown that in general case of cubic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hexoctahedral&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hexoctahedral (page does not exist)">hexoctahedral</a> crystal the four phenomenological constants approach is correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The flexomagnetoelectric effect appears in spiral multiferroics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> or micromagnetic structures like <a href="/wiki/Domain_wall_(magnetism)" title="Domain wall (magnetism)">domain walls</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and magnetic vortexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Ferroelectricity developed from micromagnetic structure can appear in any magnetic material even in centrosymmetric one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Building of symmetry classification of <a href="/wiki/Domain_wall_(magnetism)" title="Domain wall (magnetism)">domain walls</a> leads to determination of the type of electric polarization rotation in volume of any magnetic <a href="/wiki/Domain_wall_(magnetism)" title="Domain wall (magnetism)">domain wall</a>. Existing symmetry classification<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> of magnetic <a href="/wiki/Domain_wall_(magnetism)" title="Domain wall (magnetism)">domain walls</a> was applied for predictions of electric polarization spatial distribution in their volumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The predictions for almost all <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry groups</a> conform with phenomenological theory in which inhomogeneous <a href="/wiki/Magnetization" title="Magnetization">magnetization</a> couples with homogeneous <a href="/wiki/Polarization_density" title="Polarization density">polarization</a>. The total <a href="/wiki/Synergy" title="Synergy">synergy</a> between symmetry and phenomenological theory appears if energy terms with electrical <a href="/wiki/Polarization_density" title="Polarization density">polarization</a> spatial derivatives are taking into account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Multiferroics properties can appear in a large variety of materials. Therefore, several routes for conventional material fabrication are being applied. Popular techniques within the multiferroic community are: <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_chemistry" title="Solid-state chemistry">solid state synthesis</a>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_synthesis" title="Hydrothermal synthesis">hydrothermal synthesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sol-gel" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_deposition" title="Vacuum deposition">vacuum based deposition</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_zone_crystal_growth" title="Floating zone crystal growth">floating zone</a>.</p>
<p>However some types of multiferroics require specific processing conditions within certain techniques. For instance:</p>
<ul>
<li>Vacuum based deposition (for instance: <a href="/wiki/Molecular_beam_epitaxy" title="Molecular beam epitaxy">MBE</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulsed_Laser_Deposition" title="Pulsed Laser Deposition">PLD</a>) for thin film deposition to exploit certain advantages that may come with 2-dimensional layered structures such as: strain mediated multiferroics, heterostructures, anisotropy.</li>
<li>High pressure solid state synthesis to stabilize metastable or highly distorted structures as for example lone pair multiferroics like Bi based multiferroics due to their low melting point.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics">Dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The study of dynamics in multiferroic systems is concerned with understanding the time evolution of the coupling between various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferroic" title="Ferroic">ferroic</a> orders, in particular under external applied fields. Current research in this field is motivated both by the promise of new types of application reliant on the coupled nature of the dynamics, and the search for new physics lying at the heart of the fundamental understanding of the elementary MF excitations.</p>
<p>An increasing number of studies of MF dynamics are concerned with the coupling between electric and magnetic order parameters in the so-called magnetoelectric (ME) multiferroics. In this class of materials, the leading research is exploring, both theoretically and experimentally, the fundamental limits (e.g. intrinsic coupling velocity, coupling strength, materials synthesis) of the dynamical ME coupling and how these may be both reached and exploited for the development of new technologies.</p>
<p>At the heart of the proposed technologies based on ME coupling are switching processes, which describe the manipulation of the material's macroscopic magnetic properties with electric field and vice versa. Much of the physics of these processes is described by the dynamics of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Domains_and_domain_walls&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Domains and domain walls (page does not exist)">domains and domain walls</a>. An important goal for current research is the minimization of the switching time, from fractions of a second ('quasi'-static regime), towards the nanosecond range and faster, the latter being the typical time scale needed for modern electronics, such as next generation memory devices.</p>
<p>Ultrafast processes operating at picosecond, femtosecond, and even attosecond scale are both driven by, and studied using, optical methods that are at the front line of modern science. The physics underpinning the observations at these short time scales is governed by non-equilibrium dynamics, and usually makes use of resonant processes. One demonstration of ultrafast processes is the switching from collinear antiferromagnetic state to spiral antiferromagnetic state in CuO under excitation by 40 fs 800 nm laser pulse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> A second example shows the possibility for the direct control of spin waves with THz radiation on antiferromagnetic NiO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> These are promising demonstrations of how the switching of electric and magnetic properties in multiferroics, mediated by the mixed character of the magnetoelectric dynamics, may lead to ultrafast data processing, communication and quantum computing devices.</p>
<p>Current research into MF dynamics aims to address various open questions; the practical realisation and demonstration of ultra-high speed domain switching, the development of further new applications based on tunable dynamics, e.g. frequency dependence of dielectric properties, the fundamental understanding of the mixed character of the excitations (e.g. in the ME case, mixed phonon-magnon modes – 'electromagnons'), and the potential discovery of new physics associated with the MF coupling.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiferroics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Multiferroic composite structures in bulk form are explored for high-sensitivity ac magnetic field sensors and electrically tunable microwave devices such as filters, oscillators and phase shifters (in which the ferri-, ferro- or antiferro-magnetic resonance is tuned electrically instead of magnetically).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nan2008_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-nan2008-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In multiferroic thin films, the coupled magnetic and ferroelectric order parameters can be exploited for developing magnetoelectronic devices. These include novel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spintronic" title="Spintronic">spintronic</a> devices such as <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_magnetoresistance" title="Tunnel magnetoresistance">tunnel magnetoresistance</a> (TMR) sensors and spin valves with electric field tunable functions. A typical TMR device consists of two layers of ferromagnetic materials separated by a thin tunnel barrier (~2 nm) made of a multiferroic thin film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In such a device, spin transport across the barrier can be electrically tuned. In another configuration, a multiferroic layer can be used as the exchange bias pinning layer. If the antiferromagnetic spin orientations in the multiferroic pinning layer can be electrically tuned, then magnetoresistance of the device can be controlled by the applied electric field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> One can also explore multiple state memory elements, where data are stored both in the electric and the magnetic polarizations.</p>
