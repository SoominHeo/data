<p><b>Dagobert I</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Dagobertus</i></span>; <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 603 – 19 January 639 AD) was the king of <a href="/wiki/Austrasia" title="Austrasia">Austrasia</a> (623–634), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_the_Franks" title="King of the Franks">king of all the Franks</a> (629–634), and king of <a href="/wiki/Neustria" title="Neustria">Neustria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundy_(region)" title="Burgundy (region)">Burgundy</a> (629–639). He was the last king of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> dynasty to wield any real royal power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Dagobert was the first of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankish_king" title="Frankish king">Frankish kings</a> to be buried in the royal tombs at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Denis_Basilica" title="Saint Denis Basilica">Saint Denis Basilica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rule_in_Austrasia">Rule in Austrasia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dagobert_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rule in Austrasia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dagobert was the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Chlothar_II" title="Chlothar II">Chlothar II</a> and Haldetrude (575–604). Chlothar had reigned alone over all the Franks since 613. In 623, Chlothar was forced to make Dagobert king of <a href="/wiki/Austrasia" title="Austrasia">Austrasia</a> by the nobility of that region, who wanted a king of their own.</p>
<p>When Chlothar granted Austrasia to Dagobert, he initially excluded <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vosges_Mountains" title="Vosges Mountains">Vosges</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a>, but shortly thereafter the Austrasian nobility forced him to concede these regions to Dagobert. The rule of a Frank from the Austrasian heartland tied Alsace more closely to the Austrasian court. Dagobert created a new duchy (the later <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Alsace" title="Duchy of Alsace">Duchy of Alsace</a>) in southwest Austrasia to guard the region from Burgundian or Alemannic encroachments and ambitions. The duchy comprised the Vosges, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundian_Gate" title="Burgundian Gate">Burgundian Gate</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transjura" title="Transjura">Transjura</a>. Dagobert made his courtier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gundoin" title="Gundoin">Gundoin</a> the first duke of this new polity that was to last until the end of the Merovingian dynasty.</p>

<p>Upon the death of his father in 629, Dagobert inherited the Neustrian and Burgundian kingdoms. His half-brother <a href="/wiki/Charibert_II" title="Charibert II">Charibert</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Sichilde" title="Sichilde">Sichilde</a>, claimed Neustria but Dagobert opposed him. Brodulf, brother of Sichilde, petitioned Dagobert on behalf of his young nephew, but Dagobert assassinated him and gave the Aquitaine to his own younger sibling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Charibert and his son <a href="/wiki/Chilperic_of_Aquitaine" title="Chilperic of Aquitaine">Chilperic</a> were assassinated in 632. Dagobert had Burgundy and Aquitaine firmly under his rule, becoming the most powerful Merovingian king in many years and the most respected ruler in the West. In 631, Dagobert led three armies against <a href="/wiki/Samo" title="Samo">Samo</a>, the ruler of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">Slavs</a>, but his Austrasian forces were defeated at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wogastisburg" title="Battle of Wogastisburg">Wogastisburg</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Also in 632, the nobles of Austrasia revolted under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayor_of_the_palace" title="Mayor of the palace">mayor of the palace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pepin_of_Landen" title="Pepin of Landen">Pepin of Landen</a>. In 634,aged 31, Dagobert appeased the rebellious nobles by putting his three-year-old son, <a href="/wiki/Sigebert_III" title="Sigebert III">Sigebert III</a>, on the throne, thereby ceding royal power in the easternmost of his realms, just as his father had done for him eleven years earlier.</p>
<p>As king, Dagobert made <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> his capital. During his reign, he built the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burg_Meersburg" title="Burg Meersburg">Altes Schloss</a> in <a href="/wiki/Meersburg" title="Meersburg">Meersburg</a> (in modern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>), which today is the oldest inhabited castle in that country. Devoutly religious, Dagobert was also responsible for the construction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Denis_Basilica" title="Saint Denis Basilica">Saint Denis Basilica</a>, at the site of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> monastery in Paris. He also appointed St. Arbogast bishop of <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dagobert died in the abbey of Saint-Denis and was the first Frankish king to be buried in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Denis_Basilica" title="Saint Denis Basilica">Saint Denis Basilica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dagobert_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_Fredegar" title="Chronicle of Fredegar">Chronicle of Fredegar</a></i> criticises the king for his loose morals in having "three queens almost simultaneously, as well as several concubines".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The chronicle names the queens, <a href="/wiki/Nanthild" title="Nanthild">Nanthild</a> and the otherwise obscure Wulfegundis and Berchildis, but none of the concubines, stating that a full list of concubines would be too long.</p>
<p>In 625/6 Dagobert married Gormatrude, a sister of his father's wife <a href="/wiki/Sichilde" title="Sichilde">Sichilde</a>; but the marriage was childless. After divorcing Gormatrude in 629/30 he made <a href="/wiki/Nanthild" title="Nanthild">Nanthild</a>, a Saxon servant (<i>puella</i>) from his personal entourage, his new queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> She gave birth to:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clovis_II" title="Clovis II">Clovis II</a> (b. 634/5) later king of Neustria and Burgundy.</li>
</ul>
<p>Shortly after his marriage to Nanthild, he took a girl called Ragnetrude to his bed, who gave birth to his youngest son:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sigebert_III" title="Sigebert III">Sigebert III</a> (b. 630/1) later king of Austrasia.</li>
</ul>
<p>It has been speculated that <a href="/wiki/Regintrud" title="Regintrud">Regintrud</a>, abbess of <a href="/wiki/Nonnberg_Abbey" title="Nonnberg Abbey">Nonnberg Abbey</a>, was also a child of Dagobert, although this theory does not fit Regintrud's supposed date of birth between 660 and 665. She married into the Bavarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agilolfing" title="Agilolfing">Agilolfing</a> family (either <a href="/wiki/Theodo_of_Bavaria" title="Theodo of Bavaria">Theodo, Duke of Bavaria</a> or his son <a href="/wiki/Theodbert_of_Bavaria" title="Theodbert of Bavaria">Duke in Salzburg</a>).</p>
