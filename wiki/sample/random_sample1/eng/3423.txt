<p><i><b>Thunnus</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of ocean-dwelling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray-finned" title="Ray-finned">ray-finned</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fish</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Scombridae" title="Scombridae">Scombridae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scombridae" title="Scombridae">Mackerel</a>) family. More specifically, <i>Thunnus</i> is one of five <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> which make up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunnini" title="Thunnini">Thunnini</a> <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribe</a> – a tribe that is collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tunas</a>. Also called the <b>true tunas</b> or <b>real tunas</b>, <i>Thunnus</i> consists of eight <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of tuna (more than half of the overall tribe), divided into two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenera" title="Subgenera">subgenera</a>. The word <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Thunnus</span></i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Latin" title="Middle Latin">Middle Latin</a> form of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">θύννος</span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>(thýnnos)</i></span> "tunny-fish" – which is in turn derived from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">θύνω</span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">(<i>thynō</i>)</span>, "to rush; to dart".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The first written use of the word was by <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Their coloring, metallic blue on top and shimmering silver-white on the bottom, helps camouflage them from above and below. <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">Atlantic bluefin tuna</a>, the largest member of this genus, can grow to 15 feet (4.6 m) long and weigh up to 1,500 pounds (680 kg). All tunas are extremely strong swimmers, and the <a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">Yellowfin tuna</a> is known to reach speeds of up to 50 miles per hour (80 km/h) when pursuing prey. As with all tunas, members of this genus are <a href="/wiki/Warm-blooded" title="Warm-blooded">warm-blooded</a>, which is a rare trait among fish; this enables them to tolerate cold waters. Bluefin tunas, for example, are found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and also in the tropical waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, where some individuals go each year to spawn.</p>
<p>Due to <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, the range of this genus has declined significantly, having been effectively removed from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genus_membership">Genus membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunnus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genus membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until recently, it was thought that there were seven <i>Thunnus</i> species, and that <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">Atlantic bluefin tuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_bluefin_tuna" title="Pacific bluefin tuna">Pacific bluefin tuna</a> were <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of a single species. In 1999 Collette established that based on both molecular and morphological considerations, they are in fact distinct species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tanaka2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tanaka2006-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The genus <i>Thunnus</i> is further classified into two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenera" title="Subgenera">subgenera</a>: <i>Thunnus (Thunnus)</i> (the bluefin group), and <i>Thunnus (Neothunnus)</i> (the yellowfin group).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overfishing">Overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunnus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The worldwide demand for <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">sushi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sashimi" title="Sashimi">sashimi</a>, coupled with increasing population growth, has resulted in global stocks of the species being overfished<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and bluefin is the most endangered and considered "a serious conservation concern".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Complicating the efforts for sustainable management of bluefin fish stocks within national exclusive economic zones (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">EEZ</a>) is bluefin migrate long distances and hunt in the mid ocean that isn't part of any country's EEZ and therefore have been vulnerable to overfishing by multiple countries' fishing fleets. International agreements and conventions are good faith agreements and are difficult to monitor or enforce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-economist-26">[26]</a></sup> Though this fish has been farmed in captivity by the Japanese and by the Australians with the help of the Japanese,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> yields are lower than other farmed fish due to the slow growth rate of Bluefin tuna, therefore keeping prices high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-economist-26">[26]</a></sup> On December 30, 2012, a 222-kilogram (489 lb) bluefin tuna caught off northeastern Japan, was sold at the <a href="/wiki/Tsukiji_fish_market" title="Tsukiji fish market">Tsukiji fish market</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> for a record 155.4 million yen ($1.76 million) – a unit price of JP¥ 1.274 million/kg (US$3,600/lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
