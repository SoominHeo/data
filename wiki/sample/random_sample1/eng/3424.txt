<p><b>Stye</b>, also known as <b>hordeolum</b>, is an infection of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_gland" title="Oil gland">oil gland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eyelid" title="Eyelid">eyelid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2010-2">[2]</a></sup> This results in a red tender bump at the edge of the eyelid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pub2016_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pub2016-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2015_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2015-3">[3]</a></sup> The outside or the inside of the eyelid can be affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lind2013_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lind2013-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cause of a stye is usually a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_infection" title="Bacterial infection">bacterial infection</a> by <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">Staphylococcus aureus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lind2013_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lind2013-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EM2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EM2013-5">[5]</a></sup> The internal ones are due to infection of the <a href="/wiki/Meibomian_gland" title="Meibomian gland">meibomian gland</a> while the external ones are due to an infection of the <a href="/wiki/Gland_of_Zeis" title="Gland of Zeis">gland of Zeis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2015_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2015-3">[3]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Chalazion" title="Chalazion">chalazion</a> on the other hand is a blocked oil gland without infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2010-2">[2]</a></sup> They are typically in the middle of the eyelid and non painful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2015_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Often a stye will go away without any specific treatment in a few days or weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lind2013_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lind2013-4">[4]</a></sup> Recommendations to speed improvement include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm_compresses" title="Warm compresses">warm compresses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2015_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2015-3">[3]</a></sup> Occasionally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> eye ointment may be recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EM2013_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-EM2013-5">[5]</a></sup> While these measures are often recommended evidence to support them is poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lind2013_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lind2013-4">[4]</a></sup> The frequency at which they occur is unclear. They may happen at any age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fer2016_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fer2016-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first sign of a stye is a small, yellowish spot at the center of the bump that develops as <a href="/wiki/Pus" title="Pus">pus</a> and expands in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Med_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Med-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other stye symptoms may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>A lump on the top or bottom <a href="/wiki/Eyelid" title="Eyelid">eyelid</a></li>
<li>Localized swelling of the eyelid</li>
<li>Localized <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a></li>
<li>Redness</li>
<li>Tenderness</li>
<li>Crusting of the eyelid margins</li>
<li>Burning in the eye</li>
<li>Droopiness of the eyelid</li>
<li>Scratchy sensation on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_eyeball" title="Human eyeball">eyeball</a> (itching)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blurred_vision" title="Blurred vision">Blurred vision</a></li>
<li>Mucous discharge in the eye</li>
<li>Irritation of the eye<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eMed_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-eMed-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Light sensitivity</li>
<li>Tearing</li>
<li>Discomfort during blinking<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MayoCli_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MayoCli-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sensation of a foreign body in the eye</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stye complications occur in very rare cases. However, the most frequent complication of styes is progression to a <a href="/wiki/Chalazion" title="Chalazion">chalazion</a> that causes cosmetic deformity, corneal irritation, and often requires surgical removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medicine_E_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medicine_E-10">[10]</a></sup> Complications may also arise from the improper surgical lancing, and mainly consist of disruption of lash growth, lid deformity or lid <a href="/wiki/Fistula" title="Fistula">fistula</a>. Styes that are too large may interfere with one's vision.</p>
<p>Eyelid <a href="/wiki/Cellulitis" title="Cellulitis">cellulitis</a> is another potential complication of eye styes, which is a generalized infection of the eyelid. Progression of a stye to a systemic infection (spreading throughout the body) is extremely rare, and only a few instances of such spread have been recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicineNet2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MedicineNet2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although styes are harmless in most cases and complications are very rare, styes often recur. They do not cause intraocular damage, meaning they do not affect the eye. Styes normally heal on their own by rupturing within a few days to a week causing the relief of symptoms, but if it does not improve or it worsens within two weeks, a doctor's opinion should be sought. Few people require surgery as part of stye treatment. With adequate treatment, styes tend to heal quickly and without complications.</p>
<p>The prognosis is better if one does not attempt to squeeze or puncture the stye, as infection may spread to adjacent tissues. Also, patients are recommended to call a doctor if they encounter problems with vision, the eyelid bump becomes very painful, the stye bleeds or reoccurs, or the eyelid or eyes becomes red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedLine_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MedLine-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Styes are most commonly caused by the blocking of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_gland" title="Oil gland">oil gland</a> at the base of the <a href="/wiki/Eyelash" title="Eyelash">eyelash</a>. Styes are experienced by people of all ages. Styes can be triggered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poor_nutrition" title="Poor nutrition">poor nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sleep_deprivation" title="Sleep deprivation">sleep deprivation</a>, lack of hygiene, lack of water, and rubbing of the eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Styes often result from a Staphylococcal infection in the eye, and can be secondary to blepharitis or a deficiency in immunoglobulin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Sharing of washcloths or face towels should be curtailed to avoid spreading the infection between individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Styes can last from one to two weeks without treatment, or as little as four days if treated properly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stye prevention is closely related to proper hygiene. Proper <a href="/wiki/Hand_washing" title="Hand washing">hand washing</a> can reduce the risks of developing not only styes, but also many other types of infections.</p>
<p>Upon awakening, application of a warm washcloth to the eyelids for one to two minutes may be beneficial in decreasing the occurrence of styes by liquefying the contents of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_glands" title="Oil glands">oil glands</a> of the eyelid and thereby preventing blockage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicineHealthE_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-MedicineHealthE-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>To prevent developing styes, it is recommended to never share cosmetics or cosmetic eye tools with other people. People should also keep their eye tools clean and generally practice proper eye hygiene. It is also recommended to remove makeup every night before going to sleep and discard old or contaminated eye makeup.</p>

<p>Most cases of styes resolve on their own, without professional care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lind2013_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lind2013-4">[4]</a></sup> The primary treatment is application of warm compresses. As a part of self-care at home, People may cleanse the affected eyelid with tap water or with a mild, nonirritating <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shampoo" title="Shampoo">shampoo</a> (such as baby shampoo) to help clean crusted discharge. Cleansing must be done gently and while the eyes are closed to prevent eye injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MediHealth_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MediHealth-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>People with styes should avoid eye makeup (e.g., eyeliner), lotions, and wearing contact lenses, since these can aggravate and spread the infection (sometimes to the cornea). People are advised not to lance the stye themselves, as serious infection can occur. Pain relievers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetaminophen" title="Acetaminophen">acetaminophen</a> may be used.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antibiotics">Antibiotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Antibiotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence to support the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> eye ointment is poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EM2013_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-EM2013-5">[5]</a></sup> Occasionally <a href="/wiki/Erythromycin" title="Erythromycin">erythromycin</a> ophthalmic ointment is recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other antibiotics, such as <a href="/wiki/Chloramphenicol" title="Chloramphenicol">chloramphenicol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Amoxicillin" title="Amoxicillin">amoxicillin</a> may also be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Chloramphenicol is used successfully in many parts of the world, but contains a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_box_warning" title="Black box warning">black box warning</a> in the United States due to concerns about <a href="/wiki/Aplastic_anemia" title="Aplastic anemia">aplastic anemia</a>, which on rare occasions can be fatal.</p>
<p>Antibiotics are normally given to people with multiple styes or with styes that do not seem to heal, and to people who have <a href="/wiki/Blepharitis" title="Blepharitis">blepharitis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rosacea" title="Rosacea">rosacea</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedures">Procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Incision and drainage is performed if resolution does not begin in the next 48 hours after warm compresses are started. Medical professionals will sometimes lance a particularly persistent or irritating stye with a needle to accelerate its draining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surgery is the last resort in stye treatment. Styes that do not respond to any type of therapies are usually surgically removed. Stye surgery is performed by an ophthalmologist, and generally under local anesthesia. The procedure consists of making a small incision on the inner or outer surface of the eyelid, depending if the stye is pointing externally or not. After the incision is made, the pus is drained out of the gland, and very small <a href="/wiki/Surgical_suture" title="Surgical suture">sutures</a> are used to close the lesion. Sometimes the removed stye is sent for a <a href="/wiki/Histopathology" title="Histopathology">histopathological examination</a> to investigate possibility of skin cancer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>sty</i> (first recorded in the 17th century) is probably a <a href="/wiki/Back-formation" title="Back-formation">back-formation</a> from <i>styany</i> (first recorded in the 15th century),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> which in turn comes from <i>styan</i> plus <i>eye</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the former of which in turn comes from the old English <i>stígend</i>, meaning riser, from the verb <i>stígan</i>, rise (in Old English G is often a Y sound). The homonym <i>sty</i> found in the combination <i>pigsty</i> has a slightly different origin, namely it comes from the Old English <i>stí-fearh</i>—<i>fearh</i> (farrow) is the Old English word for pig—where <i>stig</i> meant hall (<i>cf.</i> steward), possibly an early Old Norse loanword, which could be cognate with the word <i>stigan</i> above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The synonym late Latin expression is "Hordeolum" a modulation of the word "Hordeolus " simply related to "Horde" ( barley), in a resemblance of the growing plant from the seed, in ancient languages, for example Arabic it has the same meaning as a barley fascicle .</p>
