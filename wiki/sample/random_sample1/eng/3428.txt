<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Isotopes of darmstadtium</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b><a href="/wiki/Darmstadtium" title="Darmstadtium">Darmstadtium</a></b> (<b>Ds</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_element" title="Synthetic element">synthetic element</a>, and thus a standard <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> cannot be given. Like all synthetic elements, it has no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotopes</a>. The first <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> to be synthesized was <sup>269</sup>Ds in 1994. There are 7 or 8 known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> from <sup>267</sup>Ds (or <sup>269</sup>Ds) to <sup>281</sup>Ds (with many gaps) and 2 or 3 known <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">isomers</a>. The longest-lived isotope is <sup>281</sup>Ds with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 9.6 seconds.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes_and_nuclear_properties">Isotopes and nuclear properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes and nuclear properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleosynthesis">Nucleosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nucleosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super-heavy_element" title="Super-heavy element">Super-heavy elements</a> such as darmstadtium are produced by bombarding lighter elements in <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a> that induce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fusion_reaction" title="Fusion reaction">fusion reactions</a>. Whereas most of the isotopes of darmstadtium can be synthesized directly this way, some heavier ones have only been observed as decay products of elements with higher <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic numbers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AM89_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AM89-10">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the energies involved, the former are separated into "hot" and "cold". In hot fusion reactions, very light, high-energy projectiles are accelerated toward very heavy targets (<a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinides</a>), giving rise to compound nuclei at high excitation energy (~40–50 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">MeV</a>) that may either fission or evaporate several (3 to 5) neutrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fusion_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-fusion-11">[4]</a></sup> In cold fusion reactions, the produced fused nuclei have a relatively low excitation energy (~10–20 MeV), which decreases the probability that these products will undergo fission reactions. As the fused nuclei cool to the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a>, they require emission of only one or two neutrons, and thus, allows for the generation of more neutron-rich products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AM89_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-AM89-10">[3]</a></sup> The latter is a distinct concept from that of where nuclear fusion claimed to be achieved at room temperature conditions (see <a href="/wiki/Cold_fusion" title="Cold fusion">cold fusion</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_fusion">Cold fusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cold fusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Before the first successful synthesis of darmstadtium in 1994 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Schwerionenforschung" title="Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung">GSI</a> team, scientists at GSI also tried to synthesize darmstadtium by bombarding lead-208 with nickel-64 in 1986. No darmstadtium atoms were identified. After an upgrade of their facilities, the team at GSI successfully detected 9 atoms of <sup>271</sup>Ds in two runs of their discovery experiment in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95Ho01_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-95Ho01-13">[6]</a></sup> This reaction was successfully repeated in 2000 by GSI (4 atoms), in 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[8]</a></sup> and 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[9]</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a> (LBNL) (9 atoms in total) and in 2002 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIKEN" title="RIKEN">RIKEN</a> (14 atoms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[10]</a></sup> The GSI team studied the analogous reaction with nickel-62 instead of nickel-64 in 1994 as part of their discovery experiment. Three atoms of <sup>269</sup>Ds were detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95Ho01_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-95Ho01-13">[6]</a></sup> A fourth decay chain was measured but was subsequently retracted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20021015_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20021015-18">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the official discovery reactions, in October–November 2000, the team at GSI also studied the analogous reaction using a lead-207 target in order to synthesize the new isotope <sup>270</sup>Ds. They succeeded in synthesising 8 atoms of <sup>270</sup>Ds, relating to a ground state isomer, <sup>270</sup>Ds, and a high-<a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable_state" title="Metastable state">metastable state</a>, <sup>270m</sup>Ds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-270Ds_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-270Ds-19">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, a team at the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Institute_for_Nuclear_Research" title="Joint Institute for Nuclear Research">Joint Institute for Nuclear Research</a> (JINR) in <a href="/wiki/Dubna" title="Dubna">Dubna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, studied the reaction:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">209<br/>
83</span>Bi + <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">59<br/>
27</span>Co → <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">267<br/>
110</span>Ds + <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">1<br/>
0</span>n</dd>
</dl>
<p>They were unable to detect any darmstadtium atoms. In 1995, the team at LBNL reported that they had succeeded in detecting a single atom of <sup>267</sup>Ds using this reaction. However, several decays were not measured and further research is required to confirm this discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-267Ds_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-267Ds-20">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_fusion">Hot fusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hot fusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In September 1994, the team at Dubna detected a single atom of <sup>273</sup>Ds by bombarding a <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-244" title="Plutonium-244">plutonium-244</a> target with accelerated <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>-34 nuclei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-273Ds_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-273Ds-21">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Experiments have been performed in 2004 at the Flerov Laboratory of Nuclear Reactions in Dubna studying the fission characteristics of the compound nucleus <sup>280</sup>Ds, produced through the nuclear reaction:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/>
90</span>Th + <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">48<br/>
20</span>Ca → <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">280<br/>
110</span>Ds* → fission</dd>
</dl>
<p>The result revealed how compound nuclei such as this fission predominantly by expelling <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(physics)" title="Magic number (physics)">magic</a> and doubly magic nuclei such as <sup>132</sup>Sn (<a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">Z</a>=50, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_number" title="Neutron number">N</a>=82). No darmstadtium atoms were obtained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-280Ds_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-280Ds-22">[15]</a></sup> A compound nucleus is a loose combination of <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleons</a> that have not arranged themselves into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_shell" title="Nuclear shell">nuclear shells</a> yet. It has no internal structure and is held together only by the collision forces between the target and projectile nuclei. It is estimated that it requires around 10<sup>−14</sup> s for the nucleons to arrange themselves into nuclear shells, at which point the compound nucleus becomes a <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclide</a>, and this number is used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> as the minimum <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> a claimed isotope must have to potentially be recognised as being discovered. Thus, the isotope <sup>280</sup>Ds currently remains unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-23">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuclidetable_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuclidetable-24">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Darmstadtium has been observed as decay products of <a href="/wiki/Copernicium" title="Copernicium">copernicium</a>. Copernicium currently has six known isotopes, four of which have been shown to undergo alpha decays to become darmstadtium nuclei, with <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass numbers</a> between 273 and 281. Darmstadtium isotopes with mass numbers 277, 279 and 281 to date have only been produced by copernicium nuclei decay. Parent copernicium nuclei can be themselves decay products of <a href="/wiki/Flerovium" title="Flerovium">flerovium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Livermorium" title="Livermorium">livermorium</a>. To date, no other elements have been known to decay to darmstadtium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuclidetable_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-nuclidetable-24">[17]</a></sup> For example, in 2004, the Dubna team (<a href="/wiki/Joint_Institute_for_Nuclear_Research" title="Joint Institute for Nuclear Research">JINR</a>) identified darmstadtium-281 as a product in the decay of livermorium via an alpha decay sequence:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-04Og01_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-04Og01-28">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Retracted_isotopes">Retracted isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Retracted isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dt><sup>280</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>The first synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Flerovium" title="Flerovium">element 114</a> resulted in two atoms assigned to <sup>288</sup>Fl, decaying to the <sup>280</sup>Ds, which underwent <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_fission" title="Spontaneous fission">spontaneous fission</a>. The assignment was later changed to <sup>289</sup>Fl and the darmstadtium isotope to <sup>281</sup>Ds. Hence, <sup>280</sup>Ds is currently unknown.</p>
<dl>
<dt><sup>277</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>In the claimed synthesis of <sup>293</sup>Og in 1999, the isotope <sup>277</sup>Ds was identified as decaying by 10.18 MeV alpha emission with a half-life of 3.0 ms. This claim was retracted in 2001. This isotope was finally created in 2010 and its decay data supported the fabrication of previous data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[22]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><sup>273m</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>In the synthesis of <sup>277</sup>Cn in 1996 by GSI (see <a href="/wiki/Copernicium" title="Copernicium">copernicium</a>), one decay chain proceeded <i>via</i> <sup>273</sup>Ds, which decayed by emission of a 9.73 MeV alpha particle with a lifetime of 170 ms. This would have been assigned to an isomeric level. This data could not be confirmed and thus this isotope is currently unknown or unconfirmed.</p>
<dl>
<dt><sup>272</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>In the first attempt to synthesize darmstadtium, a 10 ms SF activity was assigned to <sup>272</sup>Ds in the reaction <sup>232</sup>Th(<sup>44</sup>Ca,4n). Given current understanding regarding stability, this isotope has been retracted from the table of isotopes.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_isomerism">Nuclear isomerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nuclear isomerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<dl>
<dt><sup>281</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>The production of <sup>281</sup>Ds by the decay of <sup>289</sup>Fl or <sup>293</sup>Lv has produced two very different decay modes. The most common and readily confirmed mode is <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_fission" title="Spontaneous fission">spontaneous fission</a> with a half-life of 11 s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A much rarer and as yet unconfirmed mode is alpha decay by emission of an alpha particle with energy 8.77 MeV with an observed half-life of around 3.7 min. This decay is associated with a unique decay pathway from the parent nuclides and must be assigned to an isomeric level. The half-life suggests that it must be assigned to an isomeric state but further research is required to confirm these reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-04Og01_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-04Og01-28">[21]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (November 2014)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><sup>271</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>Decay data from the direct synthesis of <sup>271</sup>Ds clearly indicates the presence of two nuclear isomers. The first emits alpha particles with energies 10.74 and 10.69 MeV and has a half-life of 1.63 ms. The other only emits alpha particles with an energy of 10.71 MeV and has a half-life of 69 ms. The first has been assigned to the ground state and the latter to an isomeric level. It has been suggested that the closeness of the alpha decay energies indicates that the isomeric level may decay primarily by delayed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomeric_transition" title="Isomeric transition">isomeric transition</a> to the ground state, resulting in an identical measured alpha energy and a combined half-life for the two processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98Ho01_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-98Ho01-30">[23]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><sup>270</sup>Ds</dt>
</dl>
<p>The direct production of <sup>270</sup>Ds has clearly identified two nuclear isomers. The ground state decays by alpha emission into the ground state of <sup>266</sup>Hs by emitting an alpha particle with energy 11.03 MeV and has a half-life of 0.10 ms. The metastable state decays by alpha emission, emitting alpha particles with energies of 12.15, 11.15, and 10.95 MeV, and has a half-life of 6 ms. When the metastable state emits an alpha particle of energy 12.15 MeV, it decays into the ground state of <sup>266</sup>Hs, indicating that it has 1.12 MeV of excess energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-270Ds_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-270Ds-19">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fission_of_compound_nuclei_with_Z.3D110">Fission of compound nuclei with Z=110</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_darmstadtium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fission of compound nuclei with Z=110">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Experiments have been performed in 2004 at the Flerov Laboratory of Nuclear Reactions in Dubna studying the fission characteristics of the compound nucleus<sup>280</sup>Ds. The nuclear reaction used is <sup>232</sup>Th+<sup>48</sup>Ca. The result revealed how nuclei such as this fission predominantly by expelling closed shell nuclei such as <sup>132</sup>Sn (Z=50, N=82).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[24]</a></sup></p>
