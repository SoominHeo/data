<p><b>Darmstadt</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈdaʁmʃtat]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:De-Darmstadt.ogg" title="File:De-Darmstadt.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/De-Darmstadt.ogg" title="De-Darmstadt.ogg">listen</a>)</small>) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, located in the southern part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Rhine_Main_Area" title="Frankfurt Rhine Main Area">Rhine-Main-Area (Frankfurt Metropolitan Region)</a>. Darmstadt has a population around 150,000 (2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Darmstadt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larger_Urban_Zone" title="Larger Urban Zone">Larger Urban Zone</a> has 430,993 inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darmstadt holds the official title "City of Science" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Wissenschaftsstadt</i></span>) as it is a major centre of scientific institutions, universities, and high-technology companies. The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Operations_Centre" title="European Space Operations Centre">European Space Operations Centre</a> (ESOC) is located in Darmstadt, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Schwerionenforschung" title="Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung">GSI Centre for Heavy Ion Research</a>, where several chemical elements such as <a href="/wiki/Bohrium" title="Bohrium">bohrium</a> (1981), <a href="/wiki/Meitnerium" title="Meitnerium">meitnerium</a> (1982), <a href="/wiki/Hassium" title="Hassium">hassium</a> (1984), <a href="/wiki/Darmstadtium" title="Darmstadtium">darmstadtium</a> (1994), <a href="/wiki/Roentgenium" title="Roentgenium">roentgenium</a> (1994), and <a href="/wiki/Copernicium" title="Copernicium">copernicium</a> (1996) were discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The existence of the following elements were also confirmed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Schwerionenforschung" title="Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung">GSI Centre for Heavy Ion Research</a>: <a href="/wiki/Nihonium" title="Nihonium">nihonium</a> (2012), <a href="/wiki/Flerovium" title="Flerovium">flerovium</a> (2009), <a href="/wiki/Moscovium" title="Moscovium">moscovium</a> (2012), <a href="/wiki/Livermorium" title="Livermorium">livermorium</a> (2010), and <a href="/wiki/Tennessine" title="Tennessine">tennessine</a> (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Darmstadt is also the seat of the world's oldest pharmaceutical company, <a href="/wiki/Merck_Group" title="Merck Group">Merck</a>, which is the city's largest employer.</p>
<p>Darmstadt was formerly the capital of a sovereign country, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Grand Duchy of Hesse</a> and its successor, the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_State_of_Hesse" title="People's State of Hesse">People's State of Hesse</a>, a federal state of Germany. As the capital of an increasingly prosperous <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">duchy</a>, the city gained some international prominence and remains one of the wealthiest cities in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the 20th century, industry (especially chemicals), as well as large science and electronics (later information technology) sectors became increasingly important, and are still a major part of the city's economy. It is also home to the football club <a href="/wiki/SV_Darmstadt_98" title="SV Darmstadt 98">SV Darmstadt 98</a>. <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt,_Indiana" title="Darmstadt, Indiana">Darmstadt, Indiana</a> was named after Darmstadt and for the former Royal Family.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name Darmstadt first appears towards the end of the 11th century, then as <i>Darmundestat</i>. The origins of the name are unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DARM_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-DARM-7">[7]</a></sup> 'Dar-mund' in Middle Low German is translated as "Boggy Headlands", but it could be a misspelling in local dialect of another name. Even locals often believe, incorrectly, that the name derives from the 'Darmbach' (a small stream formerly running through the city). In fact, the stream received its current name much later, after the city, not vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DARM_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-DARM-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darmstadt was chartered as a city by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Ludwig the Bavarian</a> in 1330, at which time it belonged to the counts of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Katzenelnbogen" title="County of Katzenelnbogen">Katzenelnbogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FACTS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FACTS-8">[8]</a></sup> The city, then called <i>Darmstait</i>, became a secondary residence for the counts, with a small castle established at the site of the current, much larger edifice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HISTKatz1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HISTKatz1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the house of Katzenelnbogen became extinct in 1479, the city was passed to the <a href="/wiki/Landgraviate_of_Hesse" title="Landgraviate of Hesse">Landgraviate of Hesse</a>, and was seat of the ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landgraf" title="Landgraf">landgraves</a> (1567–1806) and thereafter (to 1918) of the grand dukes of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HISTKatz2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HISTKatz2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_age">Industrial age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industrial age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:256px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="385" data-file-width="574" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg/254px-Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg/381px-Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg/508px-Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg 2x" width="254"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luisenplatz_Darmstadt_1909.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Darmstadt in 1909</div>

<p>The city grew in population during the 19th century from little over 10,000 to 72,000 inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darmstadt-history_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-darmstadt-history-11">[11]</a></sup> A polytechnical school, which later became a Technical University now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darmstadt_University_of_Technology" title="Darmstadt University of Technology">TU Darmstadt</a>, was established in 1877.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tu-darmstadt_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-tu-darmstadt-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the beginning of the 20th century, Darmstadt was an important centre for the art movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jugendstil" title="Jugendstil">Jugendstil</a>, the German variant of <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a>. Annual architectural competitions led to the building of many architectural treasures of this period. Also during this period, in 1912 the chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anton_Kollisch" title="Anton Kollisch">Anton Kollisch</a>, working for the pharmaceutical company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merck_KGaA" title="Merck KGaA">Merck</a>, first synthesised the chemical <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">MDMA</a> (ecstasy) in Darmstadt. Darmstadt's municipal area was extended in 1937 to include the neighbouring localities of Arheilgen and Eberstadt, and in 1938 the city was separated administratively from the surrounding district (<i>Kreis</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazi_Germany">Nazi Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nazi Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darmstadt was the first city in Germany to force Jewish shops to close in early 1933, shortly after the Nazis took power in Germany. The shops were only closed for one day, for "endangering communal order and tranquility".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1942, over 3,000 Jews from Darmstadt were first forced into a collection camp located in the Liebigschule, and later deported to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" title="Concentration camp">concentration camps</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HIST_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-HIST-14">[14]</a></sup> where most eventually died.</p>
<p>Several prominent members of the German resistance movement against the Nazis were citizens of Darmstadt, including <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Leuschner" title="Wilhelm Leuschner">Wilhelm Leuschner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Haubach" title="Theodor Haubach">Theodor Haubach</a>, both executed for their opposition to Hitler's regime.</p>
<p>Darmstadt was first bombed on 30 July 1940, and 34 other air raids would follow before the war's end. The old city centre was largely destroyed in a <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Darmstadt_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Darmstadt in World War II">British bombing raid</a> on 11 September 1944. This attack was an example of the <i>firestorm</i> technique, which was subsequently used against the historic city of <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a> in February 1945. To create a firestorm, a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incendiary_bombs" title="Incendiary bombs">incendiary bombs</a> are dropped around the city before the explosive blast bombs are dropped, thus beginning a self-sustaining combustion process in which winds generated by the fire ensure it continues to burn until everything possible has been consumed. Darmstadt was selected as the secondary target for the raid, but was promoted to the primary target after clouds were observed over the primary which would have hindered any <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> of the after-effects. During this fire attack an estimated 11,000 to 12,500 of the inhabitants burned to death, and 66,000 to 70,000 were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HIST_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-HIST-14">[14]</a></sup> Over three quarters of Darmstadt's inner city was destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Post-war rebuilding was done in a relatively plain architectural style, although a number of the historic buildings were rebuilt to their original appearance following the city's capture on 20 March 1945 by American <a href="/wiki/4th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Armored Division (United States)">4th Armored Division</a>.</p>

<p>Throughout the 19th and 20th centuries, Darmstadt became home to many technology companies and research institutes, and has been promoting itself as a "city of science" since 1997. It is well known as a high-tech centre in the vicinity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt Airport</a>, with important activities in spacecraft operations (the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Operations_Centre" title="European Space Operations Centre">European Space Operations Centre</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Organisation_for_the_Exploitation_of_Meteorological_Satellites" title="European Organisation for the Exploitation of Meteorological Satellites">European Organisation for the Exploitation of Meteorological Satellites</a>), chemistry, pharmacy, information technology, biotechnology, telecommunications (substantial <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Telekom" title="Deutsche Telekom">Deutsche Telekom</a> presence) and mechatronics. In 2000, its region also scored Rank 3 amongst 97 German regions in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WirtschaftsWoche" title="WirtschaftsWoche">WirtschaftsWoche</a></i> test ranking Germany's high-tech regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FACTS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FACTS-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darmstadt_University_of_Technology" title="Darmstadt University of Technology">TU Darmstadt</a> is one of the important technical institutes in Germany and is well known for its research and teaching in the Electrical, Mechanical and Civil Engineering disciplines. Together with other tertiary institutions, the TU is responsible for the large student population of the city, which stood at 33,547 in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FACTS_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-FACTS-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Darmstadt is highly connected to all means of transportation, including the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn Network</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">Intercity-Express Network</a> and a major international airport.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darmstadt is connected to a number of major roads, including two <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahnen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_5" title="Bundesautobahn 5">Bundesautobahn 5</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_67" title="Bundesautobahn 67">Bundesautobahn 67</a>). The main road passing west-east is the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_26" title="Bundesstraße 26">Bundesstraße 26</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_3" title="Bundesstraße 3">Bundesstraße 3</a> runs north-south. The rural areas east of the city in the <a href="/wiki/Odenwald" title="Odenwald">Odenwald</a> are accessed by several secondary roads.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transport_in_Darmstadt">Public transport in Darmstadt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Public transport in Darmstadt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Darmstadt" title="Trams in Darmstadt">Trams in Darmstadt</a></div>
<p>The extensive public transport system of Darmstadt is integrated in the <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Main-Verkehrsverbund" title="Rhein-Main-Verkehrsverbund">RMV</a> (the transportation authority of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Rhine_Main_Area" title="Frankfurt Rhine Main Area">Frankfurt Metropolitan Area</a>). The backbone of public transport in Darmstadt is its modern <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram system</a> with 9 lines and a local bus service serving all parts of the city. Darmstadt is furthermore connected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_S-Bahn" title="Frankfurt S-Bahn">Frankfurt S-Bahn system</a> and being served by regional bus lines. Furthermore, regional rail lines (R64, R65, R66) connect six secondary railway stations within the city.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_rail_links">Regional rail links</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Regional rail links">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darmstadt is connected to the Frankfurt <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Main_S-Bahn" title="Rhine-Main S-Bahn">rapid transit network</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-Bahn_Rhein-Main" title="S-Bahn Rhein-Main">S-Bahn</a> line S3. Besides that, a number of regional trains connect secondary railway stations within Darmstadt and the region with <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt_Hauptbahnhof" title="Darmstadt Hauptbahnhof">Darmstadt Hauptbahnhof</a> (main station), offering a net of inner city and regional train links.</p>

<p>By its main railway station "<a href="/wiki/Darmstadt_Hauptbahnhof" title="Darmstadt Hauptbahnhof">Darmstadt Hauptbahnhof</a>", which is located in the western part of the central city, Darmstadt is connected to the rest of Germany and Europe by the <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">Intercity-Express</a> network and other long-distance trains. Darmstadt Hauptbahnhof is a busy station with 12 platforms which serves as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation_hub" title="Transportation hub">transportation hub</a> for the southern Hesse/<a href="/wiki/Odenwald" title="Odenwald">Odenwald</a> region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airports">Airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a></b></li>
</ul>
<p>Darmstadt can be easily accessed from around the world via Frankfurt Airport (<i>Flughafen Frankfurt am Main</i>) which is located 20 km (12 mi) from central Darmstadt and connected to it via <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> 5, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-Bahn" title="S-Bahn">S-Bahn</a>, several bus lines and a direct express bus-link ("<i>Airliner</i>"). The airport ranks among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_busiest_airports_by_passenger_traffic" title="World's busiest airports by passenger traffic">world's busiest airports by passenger traffic</a> and is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_busiest_airports_by_cargo_traffic" title="World's busiest airports by cargo traffic">second-busiest airport by cargo traffic</a> in Europe. The airport also serves as the main hub for German <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Egelsbach_Airport" title="Frankfurt Egelsbach Airport">Frankfurt Egelsbach Airport</a></b></li>
</ul>
<p>Frankfurt Egelsbach Airport (<i>Flugplatz Frankfurt-Egelsbach</i>) is a busy <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> airport located 5 km north of Darmstadt, near the town of <a href="/wiki/Egelsbach" title="Egelsbach">Egelsbach</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Hahn_Airport" title="Frankfurt Hahn Airport">Frankfurt Hahn Airport</a></b></li>
</ul>
<p>Despite the name, Frankfurt Hahn Airport (<i>Flughafen Frankfurt-Hahn</i>) is located far outside the Frankfurt Metro Area, approximately 120 km (75 mi) away in <a href="/wiki/Lautzenhausen" title="Lautzenhausen">Lautzenhausen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>). Hahn Airport is a major base for <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">Low-cost carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>. This airport can only be reached by car or bus.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Castles_and_historical_buildings">Castles and historical buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Castles and historical buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:256px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1070" data-file-width="1622" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG/254px-Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG/381px-Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG/508px-Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG 2x" width="254"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Residenzschloss_Darmstadt_539-Gdh.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ducal Palace and Market Square</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Darmstadt was the <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a> of an independent country (the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Grand Duchy of Hesse</a>) until 1871 and the capital of the German state of <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> until 1945. It is due to its past as a capital city that it has many architectural testimonies of this period. Many of its major architectural landmarks were created by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Moller" title="Georg Moller">Georg Moller</a> who was appointed the court master builder of the Grand Duchy of Hesse. Due to the fact that the last ruling Grand Duke of Hesse, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Louis,_Grand_Duke_of_Hesse" title="Ernest Louis, Grand Duke of Hesse">Ernst Ludwig</a> was a grandson of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> and brother to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandra_of_Hesse" title="Alexandra of Hesse">Empress Alexandra</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> the architecture of Darmstadt has been influenced by British and Russian imperial architecture with many examples still existing, such as the Luisenplatz with its ducal column, the old Hessian State Theatre (at Karolinenplatz) and the Russian Chapel by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Benois" title="Leon Benois">Leon Benois</a>. The Russian church, St. Mary Magdalene Chapel, is named in honor of the patron saint of Tzar Nicholas' mother and was built of Russian stone on Russian soil brought to Darmstadt by train. It was used by the Russian royal family and court during regular visits to the Tsarina's ducal brother and family in Darmstadt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The ducal palace of Darmstadt is located in the city centre. It was the residence of the counts of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=County_of_Hesse-Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="County of Hesse-Darmstadt (page does not exist)">Hesse-Darmstadt</a>, later as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Hesse_and_by_Rhine" title="Grand Duke of Hesse and by Rhine">Grand Dukes of Hesse</a> by the grace of Napoleon. The rulers of Hesse also owned a hunting lodge in <a href="/wiki/Kranichstein" title="Kranichstein">Kranichstein</a> which is a nowadays used as a five star hotel. The most famous castle in the Darmstadt region is <a href="/wiki/Frankenstein_Castle" title="Frankenstein Castle">Frankenstein Castle</a> due to claims that the real castle may have had an influence on Mary Shelley's decision to choose the name <a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a> for her monster-creating scientist. This castle dates back to the 13th century, but it was acquired by the counts of Hesse-Darmstadt in 1662.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_architecture">Modern architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modern architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:256px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg/254px-Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg/381px-Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg/508px-Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg 2x" width="254"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Darmstadt-Waldspirale-Hundertwasser3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Waldspirale</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Darmstadt has a rich tradition in modern architecture. After 1945 several <i>"Meisterbauten"</i> (<i>Masterful Architectonic Creations</i>) were built that set standards for modern architecture. These buildings still exist and are used for various public and private purposes. In the late 1990s the <a href="/wiki/Waldspirale" title="Waldspirale">Waldspirale</a> ('Forest Spiral') was built, a residential complex by Austrian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hundertwasser" title="Hundertwasser">Friedensreich Hundertwasser</a>. As an almost <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surreal</a> building, it is internationally famous for its almost absolute rejection of rectangular forms, down to every window having a different shape, the style being a trademark of Hundertwasser's work. Hundertwasser died before the Waldspirale was finished.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_Nouveau">Art Nouveau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Art Nouveau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darmstadt was a centre of the <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau movement</a>. Surviving examples of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jugendstil" title="Jugendstil">Jugendstil</a> period include the Rosenhöhe, a landscaped English-style rose garden from the 19th century, recently renovated and replanted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathildenh%C3%B6he" title="Mathildenhöhe">Mathildenhöhe</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> with the <i>Hochzeitsturm</i> ('Wedding tower', also commonly known as the 'Five-Finger-Tower') by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Maria_Olbrich" title="Joseph Maria Olbrich">Joseph Maria Olbrich</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Chapel_in_Darmstadt" title="Russian Chapel in Darmstadt">Russian Chapel in Darmstadt</a> and large exhibition halls as well as many private villas built by Jugendstil architects who had settled in Darmstadt. German Art Nouveau is commonly known by its German name, Jugendstil. The name is taken from the artistic journal, Die Jugend, which was published in Munich and which espoused the new artistic movement. It was founded in 1896 by Georg Hirth (Hirth remained editor until his death in 1916, and the magazine continued to be published until 1940). The magazine was instrumental in promoting the style in Germany. As a result, its name was adopted as the most common German-language term for the style: Jugendstil ("young style"). Although, during the early 20th century, the word was applied to only two-dimensional examples of the graphic arts, especially the forms of organic typography and graphic design found in and influenced by German magazines like Jugend, Pan, and Simplicissimus, it is now applied to more general manifestations of Art Nouveau visual arts in Germany, the Netherlands, the Baltic states, and Nordic countries. The two main centres for Jugendstil art in Germany were Munich and Darmstadt.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Squares">Squares</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Squares">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Luisenplatz</i>, the central square of the city, forms the centre of the city and is the main public transport hub. In 1844 the <i>Ludwigsäule</i> (called <i>Langer Lui</i>, meaning <i>Long Ludwig</i>), a 33-metre column commemorating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig_I,_Grand_Duke_of_Hesse_and_by_Rhine" title="Ludwig I, Grand Duke of Hesse and by Rhine">Ludwig I</a>, first Grand Duke of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse</a>, was placed in the middle of the square. While the column still stands, the square is today surrounded by mostly modern buildings. Other important squares are the <i>Marktplatz</i> (see image) near the old city hall and the <i>Sabaisplatz</i> at the <i>Mathildenhöhe</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks">Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The city has a high density of parks. Among the most important parks are the English style <i>Herrngarten</i> in central Darmstadt. In former times it was part of the Royal Gardens used exclusively by the duches of Darmstadt. Today it is a public park, heavily used in every season of the year. Other important parks are the French style parks <i>Prinz-Georgs-Garten</i> and <i>Orangerie</i>, the modern style <i>Bürgerpark</i> ("People's Park") in northern Darmstadt and the mystical <i>Park Rosenhöhe</i> ("Rose Heights") which also serves as the cemetery for the <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">Duches of Darmstadt</a> with two impressive mausoleum buildings in its remote parts. The <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_TU_Darmstadt" title="Botanischer Garten der TU Darmstadt">Botanischer Garten</a> in eastern Darmstadt is a botanical garden maintained by the Darmstadt University of Technology with a fine collection of rare plants and trees.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany"><i>Stadtkirche</i> church</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> built in 1369, is in the pedestrian zone of the downtown city center, next to the historic Hotel Bockshaut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The church has <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">gothic</a> elements along with <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a>, it houses the <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royal</a> <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a>. Hotel Bockshaut was built in 1580 for a church presbytery. The most important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> Church is <i>St. Ludwig</i> in central Darmstadt. The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Chapel_in_Darmstadt" title="Russian Chapel in Darmstadt">Russian Chapel in Darmstadt</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_orthodox_church" title="Russian orthodox church">Russian orthodox church</a> which is still in use. It was built and used as a private chapel by the last <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> of Russia, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II</a>, whose wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandra_Fyodorovna_of_Hesse" title="Alexandra Fyodorovna of Hesse">Alexandra</a> was born in Darmstadt. Although Russian orthodox churches also exist in other cities outside Russia, the Russian Chapel in Darmstadt was the only official Russian church used by the Russian Tsar outside the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>. It is said that the chapel was built on Russian soil that was brought to Darmstadt exclusively for the purpose of building the Tsar's private chapel on it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Every year around early July the <a href="/wiki/Heinerfest" title="Heinerfest">Heinerfest</a> festival is held in the streets surrounding the old ducal palace. It is a traditional German festival with music acts, <a href="/wiki/Beer_hall" title="Beer hall">beer halls</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amusement_ride" title="Amusement ride">amusement rides</a> and booths selling trinkets and food. The similar 'Schloßgrabenfest', which is more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_music" title="Live music">live music</a>-oriented, is held in the same location every year in May. These two festivals attract 700,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and 400,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> visitors respectively.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:256px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg/254px-Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg/381px-Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg/508px-Darmstadt_Staatstheater_Zuschauerraum_vom_Rang.jpg 2x" width="254"/></a>

<p>Darmstadt has a rich cultural heritage. The <a href="/wiki/Staatstheater_Darmstadt" title="Staatstheater Darmstadt">Staatstheater Darmstadt</a> (<i>State Theatre Darmstadt</i>) dates back to the year 1711. The present building has been in use since 1972 and has three halls which can be used independently. The "Grand Hall" (<i>Großes Haus</i>) provides seats for 956  people and serves as Darmstadt's opera house. The "Small Hall" (<i>Kleines Haus</i>) is mostly used for plays and dance and has 482 seats. A separate small hall (<i>Kammerspiele</i>) with 120 seats is used for <a href="/wiki/Chamber_play" title="Chamber play">chamber plays</a>.</p>
<p>Among the museums in Darmstadt the most important are the <i>Hessisches Landesmuseum</i> (Hessian State Museum), the <i>Porcelain Museum</i> (exhibition of the ducal <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>), the <i>Schlossmuseum</i> (exhibition of the ducal residence and possessions), the <i>Kunsthalle Darmstadt</i> (exhibitions of modern art), the exhibition centre <i>Mathildenhöhe</i> and the <i>Museum Künstlerkolonie</i> (<a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> museum).</p>
<p>The Jazz-Institut Darmstadt is Germany's largest publicly accessible <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> archive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Internationales Musikinstitut Darmstadt, harboring one of the world's largest collections of <a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">post-war</a> <a href="/wiki/Sheet_music" title="Sheet music">sheet music</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> also hosts the biennial <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darmstadt_New_Music_Summer_School" title="Darmstadt New Music Summer School">Internationale Ferienkurse für Neue Musik</a></i>, a summer school in <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_classical_music" title="Contemporary classical music">contemporary classical music</a> founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfgang_Steinecke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wolfgang Steinecke (page does not exist)">Wolfgang Steinecke</a>. A large number of avant-garde composers have attended and given lectures there, including <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier Messiaen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Berio" title="Luciano Berio">Luciano Berio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milton_Babbitt" title="Milton Babbitt">Milton Babbitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luigi_Nono_(composer)" title="Luigi Nono (composer)">Luigi Nono</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="György Ligeti">György Ligeti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iannis_Xenakis" title="Iannis Xenakis">Iannis Xenakis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Stockhausen" title="Karlheinz Stockhausen">Karlheinz Stockhausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Kagel" title="Mauricio Kagel">Mauricio Kagel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Lachenmann" title="Helmut Lachenmann">Helmut Lachenmann</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Akademie_f%C3%BCr_Sprache_und_Dichtung" title="Deutsche Akademie für Sprache und Dichtung">Deutsche Akademie für Sprache und Dichtung</a> provides writers and scholars with a place to research the German language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The Academy's annual <a href="/wiki/Georg_B%C3%BCchner_Prize" title="Georg Büchner Prize">Georg Büchner Prize</a>, named in memory of <a href="/wiki/Georg_B%C3%BCchner" title="Georg Büchner">Georg Büchner</a>, is considered the most prestigious literary award for writers of German language.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Darmstadt is located in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine_Plain" title="Upper Rhine Plain">Upper Rhine Plain</a> (German: Oberrheinische Tiefebene), a major rift, about 350 km long and on average 50 km wide, between the cities of Frankfurt in the north and Basel in the south. Darmstadt's southeastern boroughs are located in the spurs of the <a href="/wiki/Odenwald" title="Odenwald">Odenwald</a>, a low mountain range in Southern Hesse between the Main and Neckar rivers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Southern Hesse is well known for its mild climate which allows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winegrowing" title="Winegrowing">winegrowing</a> on a large scale in the region south of Darmstadt. The weather is often volatile with the summers being warm and humid with frequent thunderstorms, the winters mostly relatively mild with frequent periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_fog" title="High fog">high fog</a>. Snowfall is most likely in January and February, but mild winters without considerable snowfall can occur.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The City of Darmstadt offers students a broad variety of public primary, secondary and tertiary schools. Besides them private schools exist, e.g. the catholic secondary school <i>Edith-Stein-Schule</i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adventists" title="Adventists">Adventists</a>' <i>Schulzentrum Marienhöhe</i>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthroposophic" title="Anthroposophic">anthroposophic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waldorf_school" title="Waldorf school"><i>Waldorf School</i></a>, a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comenius" title="Comenius">Comenius</a></i> <i>School</i> and other faith based private schools.</p>

<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Darmstadt" title="Technical University of Darmstadt">Technical University of Darmstadt</a></b> (German: <i>Technische Universität Darmstadt</i>), commonly referred to as TU Darmstadt, is a prestigious research university in Germany. It was founded in 1877 and received the right to award doctorates in 1899. In 1882 it was the first university in the world to set up a chair in electrical engineering, in 1883 the first faculty for electrical engineering was founded there. The University is organized in 13 departments and 5 fields of study, which all together offer about 100 courses of studies. The fields of study offer interdisciplinary degree courses in which students take lectures in multiple departments. The University, as its title suggests, offers degree courses in the fields of electrical, mechanical and civil engineering, architecture, computer science, mathematics and the natural sciences. It also offers courses in economics, law, history, politics, sociology, psychology, sport science and linguistics. It also offers degree courses for teaching positions at German vocational schools and Gymnasiums.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/Darmstadt_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Darmstadt University of Applied Sciences">Darmstadt University of Applied Sciences</a></b> (German: <i>Hochschule Darmstadt</i>) is internationally known for its achievements in the areas of engineering and computer science. The University has the highest number of industrial linkage programs, compared to the rest of the universities of applied sciences. The roots of University of Applied Sciences Darmstadt dates back to 1876. However, it has not emerged as a separate institution before 1971. Today it is the largest University of Applied Sciences in the State of Hesse with about 11,000 students offering courses in architecture, chemical engineering, civil engineering, computer science, design, economics electrical engineering and information technology, mathematics and science, mechanical engineering, media (including information science and engineering), plastics engineering, social and cultural studies, social sciences and sechatronics.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Protestant_University_of_Applied_Sciences_Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Protestant University of Applied Sciences Darmstadt (page does not exist)">Protestant University of Applied Sciences Darmstadt</a></b> (EHD) is an officially recognised and Church-sponsored University. The sponsors are the Protestant Church in Hesse and Nassau, the Protestant Church of Kurhesse-Waldeck and the social welfare organisation of both Hessian Protestant Churches, the Diakonie Hesse. The EHD has approximately 1,700 students, 40 professors and 10 scientific employees and about 100 visiting lecturers every semester.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions">Institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_(12052189474).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2832" data-file-width="4256" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_%2812052189474%29.jpg/220px-Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_%2812052189474%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_%2812052189474%29.jpg/330px-Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_%2812052189474%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_%2812052189474%29.jpg/440px-Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_%2812052189474%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Views_in_the_Main_Control_Room_(12052189474).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Operations_Centre" title="European Space Operations Centre">ESOC</a> European Space Operations Command in Darmstadt.</div>

<p>Darmstadt is home to many research institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> (Fraunhofer IGD, Fraunhofer LBF, Fraunhofer SIT) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Schwerionenforschung" title="Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung">Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung</a> (GSI, "Society for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_ion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heavy ion (page does not exist)">Heavy Ion</a> Research"), which operates a <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> in northern Darmstadt. The GSI, amongst other elements, discovered the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> <a href="/wiki/Darmstadtium" title="Darmstadtium">darmstadtium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a>: 110), named after the city in 2003. This makes Darmstadt one of only eight settlements with elements named after them (the others being <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a> in Sweden (four elements); <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> in Sweden (<a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a>); <a href="/wiki/Strontian" title="Strontian">Strontian</a> in Scotland; <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> in Denmark (whose Latin name gives <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a>); Paris (whose Latin name gives <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>); <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Dubna" title="Dubna">Dubna</a> in Russia). Various other elements, including <a href="/wiki/Meitnerium" title="Meitnerium">meitnerium</a> (atomic number: 109) (1982), <a href="/wiki/Hassium" title="Hassium">hassium</a> (atomic number: 108) (1984), <a href="/wiki/Roentgenium" title="Roentgenium">roentgenium</a> (atomic number: 111) (1994) and <a href="/wiki/Copernicium" title="Copernicium">copernicium</a> (atomic number: 112) (1996) were also synthesized in the Darmstadt facility.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Operations_Centre" title="European Space Operations Centre">European Space Operations Centre</a> (ESOC) of the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> is located in Darmstadt. From here, various deep-space exploration spacecraft and Earth-orbiting satellites are operated for the purposes of scientific research, and technology development and demonstration.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/EUMETSAT" title="EUMETSAT">EUMETSAT</a>, the European Organisation for the Exploitation of Meteorological Satellites, operates the principal European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological" title="Meteorological">meteorological</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> from its headquarters, including the first and second generations of <a href="/wiki/Meteosat" title="Meteosat">Meteosat</a> geostationary satellites, and the polar-orbiting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metop" title="Metop">Metop</a> series.</p>
<p>Darmstadt is a centre for the pharmaceutical and chemical industry, with Merck, Röhm and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schenck" title="Carl Schenck">Schenck RoTec</a> (part of The Dürr Group) having their main plants and centres here.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_military_presence">United States military presence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: United States military presence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>U.S. forces entered the city of Darmstadt on 25 March 1945. At the end of World War II, Darmstadt was among the 112 communities where U.S. Forces were stationed. Early units stationed here included elements of the U.S. Constabulary, Air Force units and a Quartermaster School.</p>
<p>Over the years, the U.S. military community Darmstadt — under a variety of designations — served as home for thousands of American soldiers and their families. It included six principal installations in Darmstadt and nearby <a href="/wiki/Babenhausen,_Hesse" title="Babenhausen, Hesse">Babenhausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Griesheim,_Hesse" title="Griesheim, Hesse">Griesheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster,_Hesse" title="Münster, Hesse">Münster</a>, plus several housing areas, an airfield and a large number of smaller facilities as far away as <a href="/wiki/Bensheim" title="Bensheim">Bensheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a>. The military newspaper European <i><a href="/wiki/Stars_and_Stripes_(newspaper)" title="Stars and Stripes (newspaper)">Stars and Stripes</a></i> also had its headquarters there. As of 1993, the Darmstadt military community also assumed responsibility for the remaining U.S. Army facilities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt-am-Main" title="Frankfurt-am-Main">Frankfurt</a> area.</p>
<p>As part of the U.S. Army's ongoing transformation in Germany, the Darmstadt military community, by then designated <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Army_Garrison_Darmstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="U.S. Army Garrison Darmstadt (page does not exist)">U.S. Army Garrison Darmstadt</a>, inactivated on 30 September 2008. Even after the garrison inactivation, however, there is still one unit active in Darmstadt: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/66th_Military_Intelligence_Brigade_(United_States)" title="66th Military Intelligence Brigade (United States)">66th Military Intelligence Group</a> at the Dagger Complex on Eberstädter Weg,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It draws its support from the nearby <a class="external text" href="http://www.wiesbaden.army.mil/" rel="nofollow">U.S. Army Garrison Wiesbaden</a>. The website of the 66th Military Intelligence Brigade claims they moved out in 2008, but Google Maps and Bing satellite imagery still show a respectively full and quarter-full parking lot, and the U.S. Army Garrison Wiesbaden's website mentions the unit still being active in Darmstadt, and a Marine Corps company being stationed there as well. With the exception of Dagger Complex, all remaining US installations are now empty and closed to the public, pending property disposal by the German authorities.</p>
