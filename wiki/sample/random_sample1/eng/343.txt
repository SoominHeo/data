<p><b>OpenType</b> is a format for scalable <a href="/wiki/Computer_font" title="Computer font">computer fonts</a>. It was built on its predecessor <a href="/wiki/TrueType" title="TrueType">TrueType</a>, retaining TrueType's basic structure and adding many intricate data structures for prescribing <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">typographic</a> behavior. OpenType is a registered trademark of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Corporation" title="Microsoft Corporation">Microsoft Corporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OFF-chiariglione_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-OFF-chiariglione-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The specification germinated at Microsoft, with <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">Adobe Systems</a> also contributing by the time of the public announcement in 1996.</p>
<p>Because of wide availability and typographic flexibility, including provisions for handling the diverse behaviors of all the world's <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing systems</a>, OpenType fonts are used commonly today on the major computer platforms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OpenType's origins date to Microsoft's attempt to license <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple</a>'s advanced typography technology <a href="/wiki/Apple_Advanced_Typography" title="Apple Advanced Typography">GX Typography</a> in the early 1990s. Those negotiations failed, motivating Microsoft to forge ahead with its own technology, dubbed "TrueType Open" in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Adobe joined Microsoft in those efforts in 1996, adding support for the glyph outline technology used in its Type 1 fonts.</p>
<p>These efforts were intended by Microsoft and Adobe to supersede both Apple's TrueType and Adobe's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_1_font" title="Type 1 font">Type 1</a> ("<a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a>") font formats. Needing a more expressive font format to handle fine typography and the complex behavior of many of the world's writing systems, the two companies combined the underlying technologies of both formats and added new extensions intended to address those formats' limitations. The name OpenType was chosen for the combined technologies, and the technology was announced later that year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_Font_Format">Open Font Format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Open Font Format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adobe and Microsoft continued to develop and refine OpenType over the next decade. Then, in late 2005, OpenType began migrating to an open standard under the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPEG" title="MPEG">MPEG</a> group, which had previously (in 2003) adopted OpenType 1.4 by reference for <a href="/wiki/MPEG-4" title="MPEG-4">MPEG-4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OFF-chiariglione_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-OFF-chiariglione-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Adoption of the new standard reached formal approval in March 2007 as ISO Standard ISO/IEC 14496-22 (MPEG-4 Part 22) called <b>Open Font Format</b> (OFF, not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Web_Open_Font_Format" title="Web Open Font Format">Web Open Font Format</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It is also sometimes referred to as "Open Font Format Specification" (OFFS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OFF-chiariglione_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-OFF-chiariglione-6">[6]</a></sup> The initial standard was technically equivalent to OpenType 1.4 specification, with appropriate language changes for ISO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The second edition of the Open Font Format was published in 2009 (ISO/IEC 14496-22:2009) and was declared "technically equivalent" to the "OpenType font format specification".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO-2nd-edition_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISO-2nd-edition-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Since then, the Open Font Format and the OpenType specification have continued to be maintained in sync. OFF is a free, publicly available standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 2001 hundreds of OpenType fonts were on the market. Adobe finished converting their entire font library to OpenType toward the end of 2002. As of early 2005<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, around 10,000 OpenType fonts had become available, with the Adobe library comprising about a third of the total. By 2006, every major <a href="/wiki/Type_foundry" title="Type foundry">font foundry</a> and many minor ones were developing fonts in OpenType format.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="uncited statistics (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenType_Collections">OpenType Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: OpenType Collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, Adobe announced the creation of OpenType Collections (OTCs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> An OTC file (.otc extension) bundles together multiple OpenType font files (.otf extension). This allows for more efficient storage. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Noto_fonts" title="Noto fonts">Noto fonts</a> <a href="/wiki/CJK_characters" title="CJK characters">CJK</a> OTC is ~10MB smaller than the sum of the four separate OTFs of which it is comprised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Additionally, OTFs can contain a maximum of 65,535 glyphs; the use of an OTC overcomes this limitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>OpenType uses the general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sfnt" title="Sfnt">sfnt</a> structure of a TrueType font, but it adds several <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Smartfont&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Smartfont (page does not exist)">smartfont</a> options that enhance the font's typographic and language support capabilities.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyph</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outline_font" title="Outline font">outline data</a> in an OpenType font may be in one of two formats: either TrueType format outlines in a 'glyf' table, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Font_Format" title="Compact Font Format">Compact Font Format</a> (CFF) outlines in a 'CFF ' table. (The table name 'CFF ' is four characters long, ending in a space character.) CFF outline data is based on the <a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a> language Type 2 font format. However, the OpenType specification does not support the use of PostScript outlines in a TrueType Collection font file.</p>
<p>For many purposes, such as layout, it doesn't matter what the outline data format is, but for some purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Rasterisation" title="Rasterisation">rasterisation</a>, it is significant. The OpenType standard does not specify the outline data format: rather, it accommodates any of several existing standards. Sometimes terms like "OpenType (PostScript flavor)", "Type 1 OpenType", "OpenType CFF", or "OpenType (TrueType flavor)" are used to indicate which outline format a particular OpenType font file contains.</p>
<p>OpenType has several distinctive characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li>Accommodates the <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">character encoding</a> (as well as others), so that it can support any writing script (or multiple scripts at once).</li>
<li>Accommodates up to 65,536 glyphs.</li>
<li>Advanced typographic "layout" features which prescribe positioning and replacement of rendered glyphs. Replacement features include <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">ligatures</a>; positioning features include <a href="/wiki/Kerning" title="Kerning">kerning</a>, mark placement, and <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(typography)" title="Baseline (typography)">baseline</a> specification.</li>
<li>Cross-platform font files, which can be used without modification on Mac OS, Windows and Unix systems.</li>
<li>If no additional glyphs or extensive typographic features are added, OpenType CFF fonts can be considerably smaller than their Type 1 counterparts.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_other_formats">Comparison to other formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparison to other formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Compared with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a>’s "GX Typography"—now called <a href="/wiki/Apple_Advanced_Typography" title="Apple Advanced Typography">Apple Advanced Typography</a> (AAT)—and with the <a href="/wiki/SIL_International" title="SIL International">SIL</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Graphite_(SIL)" title="Graphite (SIL)">Graphite</a> technology, OpenType is less flexible in typographic options, but superior in language-related options and support.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Nevertheless, OpenType has been adopted much more widely than AAT or Graphite, despite AAT being the older technology.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> Graphite technology has been packaged with the desktop publishing and document editing software, <a href="/wiki/LibreOffice" title="LibreOffice">LibreOffice</a> (it is included in both the Windows and Linux distributions, but not with Mac, as AAT is already present).</p>
<p>From a font developer’s perspective, OpenType is, for many common situations, easier to develop for than AAT or Graphite. First, the simple declarative substitutions and positioning of OpenType are more readily understood than AAT’s more complex state tables or the Graphite description language that resembles <a href="/wiki/C_syntax" title="C syntax">C syntax</a>. Second, Adobe’s strategy of licensing at no charge the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> developed for its own font development, AFDKO (Adobe Font Development Kit for OpenType), allowed third-party font editing applications such as <a href="/wiki/FontLab" title="FontLab">FontLab</a> and FontMaster to add support with relative ease. Although Adobe’s text-driven coding support is not as visual as Microsoft’s separate tool, VOLT (Visual OpenType Layout Tool), the integration with the tools being used to make the fonts has been well received.</p>
<p>Another difference is that an OpenType support framework (such as Microsoft’s <a href="/wiki/Uniscribe" title="Uniscribe">Uniscribe</a>) needs to provide a fair bit of knowledge about special language processing issues to handle (for example: Arabic). With AAT or Graphite, the font developer has to encapsulate all that expertise in the font. This means that AAT and Graphite can handle any arbitrary language, but that it requires more work and expertise from the font developers. On the other hand, OpenType fonts are easier to make, but can only support <a href="/wiki/Complex_text_layout" title="Complex text layout">complex text layout</a> if the application or operating system knows how to handle them.</p>
<p>Prior to supporting OpenType, Adobe promoted <a href="/wiki/Multiple_master_fonts" title="Multiple master fonts">multiple master fonts</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Expert_fonts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Expert fonts (page does not exist)">expert fonts</a> for high-end typography. Multiple master fonts lacked the controls for alternate glyphs and languages provided by OpenType, but provided smooth transitions between styles within a type family. Expert fonts were intended as supplementary fonts, such that all the special characters that had no place in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adobe_Standard_Encoding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adobe Standard Encoding (page does not exist)">Adobe Standard Encoding</a> character set – ligatures, fractions, small capitals, etc. – were placed in the expert font instead. Usage in applications was tricky, with, for example, typing a <i>Z</i> causing the <i>ffl</i> ligature to be generated. In modern OpenType fonts all these glyphs are encoded with their Unicode indices and selection method (i.e. under what circumstances that glyph should be used).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenType_support">OpenType support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: OpenType support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_Roman_support">Basic Roman support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Basic Roman support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OpenType support may be divided into several categories.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Virtually all applications and most modern operating systems have basic Roman support and work with OpenType fonts just as well as other, older formats. Benefits beyond basic Roman support include extended language support through <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>, support for complex writing scripts such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indic languages</a>, and advanced typographic support for <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin script</a> languages such as <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.</p>
<p>Amongst Microsoft's operating systems, OpenType TT fonts (.TTF) are backward compatible and therefore supported by all <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> versions starting with Microsoft Windows 3.1. OpenType PS fonts (.OTF) are supported in all Windows versions starting with Microsoft <a href="/wiki/Windows_2000" title="Windows 2000">Windows 2000</a>; <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Type_Manager" title="Adobe Type Manager">Adobe Type Manager</a> is required to be installed on Microsoft Windows 95/98/NT/Me for basic Roman support (only) of OpenType PS fonts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extended_language_support">Extended language support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Extended language support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Extended language support via Unicode for both OpenType and TrueType is present in most applications for Microsoft Windows<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office_Publisher" title="Microsoft Office Publisher">Microsoft Office Publisher</a>, most Adobe applications, and Microsoft Office 2003, though not Word 2002), CorelDRAW X3 and newer, and many Mac OS X applications, including Apple's own such as <a href="/wiki/TextEdit" title="TextEdit">TextEdit</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pages_(iWork)" title="Pages (iWork)">Pages</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keynote_(presentation_creation_software)" title="Keynote (presentation creation software)">Keynote</a>. It is also widely supported in free operating systems, such as Linux (e.g. in multiplatform applications like <a href="/wiki/AbiWord" title="AbiWord">AbiWord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gnumeric" title="Gnumeric">Gnumeric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calligra_Suite" title="Calligra Suite">Calligra Suite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scribus" title="Scribus">Scribus</a>, <a href="/wiki/OpenOffice.org" title="OpenOffice.org">OpenOffice.org</a> 3.2 and later versions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> etc.).</p>
<p>OpenType support for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_script" title="Complex script">complex written scripts</a> has so far mainly appeared in Microsoft applications in <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office" title="Microsoft Office">Microsoft Office</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">Microsoft Word</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Publisher" title="Microsoft Publisher">Microsoft Publisher</a>. <a href="/wiki/Adobe_InDesign" title="Adobe InDesign">Adobe InDesign</a> provides extensive OpenType capability in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> but does not directly support Middle Eastern or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmic_family" title="Brahmic family">Indic scripts</a>— though a separate version of InDesign is available that supports Middle Eastern scripts such as Arabic and Hebrew. Undocumented functionality in many Adobe Creative Suite 4 applications, including InDesign, Photoshop and Illustrator, enables Middle Eastern, Indic and other languages, but is not officially supported by Adobe, and requires third-party plug-ins to provide a user interface for the features.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_typography">Advanced typography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Advanced typography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Advanced typographic support for Latin script languages first appeared in Adobe applications such as <a href="/wiki/Adobe_InDesign" title="Adobe InDesign">Adobe InDesign</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop" title="Adobe Photoshop">Adobe Photoshop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Illustrator" title="Adobe Illustrator">Adobe Illustrator</a>. <a href="/wiki/QuarkXPress" title="QuarkXPress">QuarkXPress</a> 6.5 and below were not Unicode compliant. Hence text in these versions of QuarkXPress that contains anything other than <a href="/wiki/Code_page#Windows_.28ANSI.29_code_pages" title="Code page">WinANSI</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_Roman" title="Mac OS Roman">MacRoman</a> characters will not display correctly in an OpenType font (nor in other Unicode font formats, for that matter). However, in QuarkXPress 7, Quark offered support similar to Adobe's. Corel's <a href="/wiki/CorelDRAW" title="CorelDRAW">CorelDRAW</a> introduced support for OpenType typographic features in version X6. <a href="/wiki/Mellel" title="Mellel">Mellel</a>, a Mac OS X-only word processor from Redlers, claims parity in typographic features with InDesign, but also extends the support to right-to-left scripts; so does the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Classical_Text_Editor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Classical Text Editor (page does not exist)">Classical Text Editor</a>, a specialized word processor developed at the Austrian Academy of Sciences.</p>
<p>As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, popular word processors for Microsoft Windows did not support advanced OpenType typography features. Advanced typography features are implemented only in high-end <a href="/wiki/Desktop_publishing" title="Desktop publishing">desktop publishing</a> software. The text engine from <a href="/wiki/Windows_Presentation_Foundation" title="Windows Presentation Foundation">Windows Presentation Foundation</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Managed_code" title="Managed code">managed code</a> implementation of OpenType, is the first Microsoft Windows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Application_Programming_Interface" title="Application Programming Interface">API</a> to expose OpenType features to software developers, supporting both OpenType TrueType, and OpenType CFF (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Font_Format" title="Compact Font Format">Compact Font Format</a>) fonts. It supports advanced typographic features such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typographical_ligature" title="Typographical ligature">ligatures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Text_figures" title="Text figures">old-style numerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swash_(typography)" title="Swash (typography)">swash</a> variants, <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)" title="Fraction (mathematics)">fractions</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superscript" title="Superscript">superscript</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscript" title="Subscript">subscript</a>, <a href="/wiki/Small_caps" title="Small caps">small capitalization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Font_substitution" title="Font substitution">glyph substitution</a>, multiple <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(typography)" title="Baseline (typography)">baselines</a>, contextual and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stylistic_alternate" title="Stylistic alternate">stylistic alternate</a> character forms, kerning, line-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justification_(typesetting)" title="Justification (typesetting)">justification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruby_character" title="Ruby character">ruby characters</a> etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sysm.C3.A4l.C3.A4inen_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sysm.C3.A4l.C3.A4inen-20">[20]</a></sup> WPF applications automatically gain support for advanced typography features. OpenType ligatures are accessible in <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word#Word_2010" title="Microsoft Word">Microsoft Office Word 2010</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Windows 7 introduced <a href="/wiki/DirectWrite" title="DirectWrite">DirectWrite</a>, a hardware accelerated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_code" title="Native code">native</a> <a href="/wiki/DirectX" title="DirectX">DirectX</a> API for text rendering with support for multi-format text, resolution-independent outline fonts, <a href="/wiki/ClearType" title="ClearType">ClearType</a>, advanced OpenType typography features, full Unicode text, layout and language support and low-level glyph rendering APIs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Mac OS X, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Advanced_Typography" title="Apple Advanced Typography">AAT</a>-supporting applications running on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X_10.4" title="Mac OS X 10.4">Mac OS X 10.4</a> and later, including TextEdit and Keynote, get considerable OpenType support. Apple's support for OpenType in Mac OS X 10.4 included most advanced typographic features necessary for <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Small_caps" title="Small caps">small caps</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oldstyle_figures" title="Oldstyle figures">oldstyle figures</a>, and various sorts of ligatures, but it did not yet support contextual alternates, positional forms, nor glyph reordering as handled by Microsoft's Uniscribe library on Windows. Thus, Mac OS X 10.4 did not offer support for Arabic or Indic scripts via OpenType (though such scripts are fully supported by existing AAT fonts). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X_10.5" title="Mac OS X 10.5">Mac OS X 10.5</a> has improved support for OpenType and supports Arabic OpenType fonts. Gradually, the OpenType typography support has improved on newer Mac OS X versions (e.g. Mac OS X 10.10 can handle much better long contextual glyph substitutions).</p>
<p>Bitstream <a href="/wiki/Panorama_(Layout_Engine)" title="Panorama (Layout Engine)">Panorama</a>, a line layout and text composition engine from <a href="/wiki/Bitstream_Inc." title="Bitstream Inc.">Bitstream Inc.</a>, provides complete OpenType support for compact and standard Asian fonts, Arabic, Hebrew, Indic, Thai and over 50 other worldwide languages. The application supports key OpenType tables required for line layout, such as BASE, glyph definition (GDEF), glyph positioning (GPOS), and glyph substitution (GSUB). Panorama also offers complete support for advanced typography features, such as ligatures, swashes, small caps, ornaments, ordinals, superiors, old style, kerning, fractions, etc.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> environments such as <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>, OpenType rendering is provided by the <a href="/wiki/FreeType" title="FreeType">FreeType</a> project, included in free implementations of the <a href="/wiki/X_Window_System" title="X Window System">X Window System</a> such as <a href="/wiki/X.Org_Server" title="X.Org Server">X.org</a>. Complex text handling is provided either by <a href="/wiki/Pango" title="Pango">pango</a> (calling <a href="/wiki/HarfBuzz" title="HarfBuzz">HarfBuzz</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qt_(toolkit)" title="Qt (toolkit)">Qt</a>. The <a href="/wiki/XeTeX" title="XeTeX">XeTeX</a> and <a href="/wiki/LuaTeX" title="LuaTeX">LuaTeX</a> systems allow <a href="/wiki/TeX" title="TeX">TeX</a> documents to use OpenType fonts, along with most of their typographic features. Linux version of <a href="/wiki/LibreOffice" title="LibreOffice">LibreOffice</a> 4.1 and newer supports many OpenType typography features, because it began to use more sophisticated HarfBuzz text shaping library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenType_Feature_File_.28.fea.29">OpenType Feature File (<code>.fea</code>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: OpenType Feature File (.fea)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OpenType features are tedious to define using a GUI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Consequently, Adobe standardized<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> a text specification format for feature files, which typically have a name ending in a <code>.fea</code> extension. These files can be compiled into the binary font container (<code>.ttf</code> or <code>.otf</code>) using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adobe_FDK" title="Adobe FDK">Adobe FDK</a> (AFDKO), <a href="/wiki/FontLab" title="FontLab">FontLab</a> or <a href="/wiki/FontForge" title="FontForge">FontForge</a>. The latter program implements a few features that are documented in the Adobe standard but are not implemented by AFDKO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Layout_tags">Layout tags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Layout tags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OpenType Layout tags are 4-byte character strings that identify the scripts, language systems, features and baselines in an OpenType Layout font. Microsoft's Layout tag registry establishes conventions for naming and using these tags. OpenType features are created by using the tags in creating feature scripts that describe how characters are to be manipulated to make the desired feature. These feature scripts can be created and incorporated into OpenType fonts by advanced font editors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fontlab" title="Fontlab">FontLab Studio</a>, AsiaFont Studio, and <a href="/wiki/FontForge" title="FontForge">FontForge</a>.</p>
<p>Operating system and application support for layout tags varies widely.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Script_tags">Script tags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Script tags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Script tags identify the scripts (writing systems) represented in an OpenType typeface. Each tag corresponds to contiguous character code ranges in Unicode. A script tag can consist of 4 or fewer lowercase letters, such as <tt>arab</tt> for the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_alphabet" title="Arabic alphabet">Arabic alphabet</a>, <tt>cyrl</tt> for the <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic script</a> and <tt>latn</tt> for the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>. The <tt>math</tt> script tag, added by Microsoft for <a href="/wiki/Cambria_(typeface)" title="Cambria (typeface)">Cambria Math</a>, has been added to the specification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scipttagregistry_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-scipttagregistry-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isoiec14496222009_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-isoiec14496222009-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_system_tags">Language system tags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Language system tags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Language system tags identify the language systems supported in an OpenType typeface. Examples include <tt>ARA</tt> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <tt>ESP</tt> for <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <tt>HYE</tt> for <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>, etc. In general, the codes are not the same as <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-2" title="ISO 639-2">ISO 639-2</a> codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feature_tags">Feature tags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Feature tags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Math">Math</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Math">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A set of tables that mirrors TeX math font metrics relatively closely was added by Microsoft initially to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambria_Math" title="Cambria Math">Cambria Math</a> for supporting their new math editing and rendering engine in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_2007" title="Office 2007">Office 2007</a> and later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This extension was added to the ISO standard (ISO/IEC CD 14496-22 3rd edition) in April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Additional (usage) details are available in the Unicode technical report 25<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and technical note 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Some of the new technical features (not present in TeX), such as "cut-ins" (which allows kerning of subscripts and superscripts relative to their bases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>) and stretch stacks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illum_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-illum-36">[36]</a></sup> have been patented by Microsoft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Windows_8" title="Windows 8">Windows 8</a> supports OpenType math outside MS Office applications via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RichEdit" title="RichEdit">RichEdit</a> 8.0 component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides Microsoft products, XeTeX and <a href="/wiki/LuaTeX" title="LuaTeX">LuaTeX</a> also have some level of support for these tables; support is more limited in XeTeX because it uses the traditional TeX math rendering engine (thus is cannot fully use some of the new features in OpenType math that extend TeX), while LuaTeX takes a more flexible approach by changing some of the internals of TeX's math rendering; in the words of Ulrik Vieth (2009): "More precisely, while XeTeX only provides access to the OpenType parameters as additional \fontdimens, LuaTeX uses an internal data structure based on the combined set of OpenType and TeX parameters, making it possible to supply missing values which are not supported in either OpenType math fonts or traditional TeX math fonts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illum_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-illum-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2013, XeTeX also gained support for cut-ins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gecko_(software)" title="Gecko (software)">Gecko</a> rendering engine used by the <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> web browser also supports some OpenType math features in its <a href="/wiki/MathML" title="MathML">MathML</a> implementation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the set of fonts that supported OpenType math was fairly limited. Besides Cambria Math, three free fonts were available: <a href="/wiki/Asana-Math" title="Asana-Math">Asana-Math</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo_Euler" title="Neo Euler">Neo Euler</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XITS" title="XITS">XITS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> More recently the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_Modern" title="Latin Modern">Latin Modern</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TeX_Gyre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TeX Gyre (page does not exist)">TeX Gyre</a> fonts (a "<a href="/wiki/Computer_Modern#Latin_Modern" title="Computer Modern">LM-ization</a>" of the standard PostScript fonts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>) have also gained support for OpenType math.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the number of OpenType math fonts is still fairly limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> A more up-to-date list is maintained on <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a>'s web site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apple added a color extension in <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X_Lion" title="Mac OS X Lion">Mac OS X Lion</a> (and also to <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> 4+) for the purpose of implementing colored <a href="/wiki/Emoji" title="Emoji">Emoji</a> fonts. The codepoints for these come from Unicode 6.0. In Apple's implementation, the fonts were extended with colored <a href="/wiki/Portable_Network_Graphics" title="Portable Network Graphics"><abbr title="Portable Network Graphics">PNG</abbr></a> images using the AAT-specific ’sbix’ table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colorinfo_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-colorinfo-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Google has made a similar proposal but using a pair of CBDT/CBLC tables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nl14_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-nl14-55">[55]</a></sup> The Google version is implemented in <a href="/wiki/FreeType" title="FreeType">FreeType</a> 2.5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fontlab-col_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-fontlab-col-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Windows_8.1" title="Windows 8.1">Windows 8.1</a> Microsoft also added color support to fonts, first implemented in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Segoe_UI" title="Segoe UI">Segoe UI</a> <a href="/wiki/Emoji" title="Emoji">Emoji</a> font.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colorinfo_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-colorinfo-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Microsoft's implementation however relies entirely on <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">vector graphics</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colorinfo_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-colorinfo-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> two new OpenType tables were added in Microsoft's implementation: the COLR table allows layered glyphs and the CPAL (“Color Palette”) actually defines the colors for the layers. The multi-layer approach allows a backwards compatible implementation as well as varying the rendering depending on the color context surrounding the glyphs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colorinfo_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-colorinfo-53">[53]</a></sup> Microsoft has announced their intention to submit their color extension for standardization thorough ISO/IEC 14496-22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adam_Twardoch" title="Adam Twardoch">Adam Twardoch</a>: "At <a href="/wiki/TypeCon" title="TypeCon">TypeCon</a> [2013], <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Hitchcock&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Greg Hitchcock (page does not exist)">Greg Hitchcock</a> clarified the envisioned roles of the palettes: first palette is used by default for “dark on light” color situations while second palette is intended for use in “light on dark” situations. Additional palettes should be selectable by the user."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fontlab-col_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-fontlab-col-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> and Adobe have proposed adding full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SVG" title="SVG">SVG</a> support (including color but also animations) to OpenType fonts. Support is present since <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nl14_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-nl14-55">[55]</a></sup> Two new OpenType tables are proposed for this purpose: one called SVG and one called CPAL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="SING_gaiji_solution">SING gaiji solution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenType&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: SING gaiji solution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, Adobe shipped a new technology in their <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Creative_Suite" title="Adobe Creative Suite">Creative Suite</a> applications bundle that offers a solution for "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaiji" title="Gaiji">gaiji</a>" (外字, Japanese for "outside character"). Ideographic writing scripts such as Chinese and Japanese do not have fixed collections of characters. They use thousands of glyphs commonly and tens of thousands less commonly. Not all glyphs ever invented and used in Far Eastern literature have even been catalogued. A typical font might contain 8,000 to 15,000 of the most commonly used glyphs. From time to time, though, an author needs a glyph not present in the font of choice. Such missing characters are known in Japan as gaiji, and they often disrupt work.</p>
<p>Another aspect of the gaiji problem is that of variant glyphs for certain characters. Often certain characters have been written differently over periods of time. It is not unusual for place names or personal family names to use a historical form of a character. Thus it is possible for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End-user_(computer_science)" title="End-user (computer science)">end user</a> using standard fonts to be left unable to spell correctly either their own name or the name of the place where they live.</p>
<p>Several ways to deal with gaiji have been devised. Solutions that treat them as characters usually assign arbitrary Unicode values to them in the <a href="/wiki/Private_Use_Areas" title="Private Use Areas">Private Use Areas</a> (PUA). Such characters cannot be used outside the environment in which the association of the private Unicode to the glyph shape is known. Documents based on them are not portable. Other installations treat gaiji as graphics. This can be cumbersome because text layout and composition cannot apply to graphics. They cannot be searched for. Often their rendering looks different from surrounding characters because the machinery for rendering graphics usually is different from the machinery for rendering glyphs from fonts.</p>
<p>The SING (Smart INdependent Glyphlets)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> technology that made its debut with Adobe's Creative Suite 2 allows for the creation of glyphs, each packaged as a standalone font, after a fashion. Such a packaged glyph is called a <i>glyphlet</i>. The format, which Adobe has made public, is based on OpenType. The package consists of the glyph outline in TrueType or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CFF_fonts" title="CFF fonts">CFF</a> (PostScript style outlines) form; standard OpenType tables declaring the glyph's metrics and behavior in composition; and metadata, extra information included for identifying the glyphlet, its ownership, and perhaps pronunciation or linguistic categorization. SING glyphlets can be created using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fontlab" title="Fontlab">Fontlab</a>'s SigMaker3 application.</p>
<p>The SING specification states that glyphlets are to travel with the document they are used in. That way documents are portable, leaving no danger of characters in the document that cannot be displayed. Because glyphlets are essentially OpenType fonts, standard font machinery can render them. The SING specification also describes an <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> format that includes all the data necessary for reconstituting the glyphlet in binary form. A typical glyphlet might require one to two kilobytes to represent.</p>
