<p><b>Davao Region</b>, formerly called <b>Southern Mindanao</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano</a>: <span lang="ceb" xml:lang="ceb"><i>Habagatang Mindanao</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">Filipino</a>: <span lang="fil" xml:lang="fil"><i>Timog Mindanao</i></span>), is an <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_the_Philippines" title="Regions of the Philippines">administrative region</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, designated as <b>Region XI</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is situated at the southeastern portion of <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a>, comprising five <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_the_Philippines" title="Provinces of the Philippines">provinces</a>: <a href="/wiki/Compostela_Valley" title="Compostela Valley">Compostela Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Norte" title="Davao del Norte">Davao del Norte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Sur" title="Davao del Sur">Davao del Sur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_Oriental" title="Davao Oriental">Davao Oriental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davao_Occidental" title="Davao Occidental">Davao Occidental</a>, the newly created province.</p>
<p>The region encloses the <a href="/wiki/Davao_Gulf" title="Davao Gulf">Davao Gulf</a>, and its regional center is <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao City</a>. Davao is the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Hispanicized pronunciation</a> of <i>daba-daba</i>, the Bagobo word for "fire" (the <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano</a> translation is <i>kalayo</i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many historians believe that the name <i>Davao</i> is the mixture of the three names that three different tribes, the earliest settlers in the region, had for the <a href="/wiki/Davao_River" title="Davao River">Davao River</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manobo" title="Manobo">Manobos</a>, an aboriginal tribe, referred to the Davao River as <i>Davohoho</i>. Another tribe, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bagobo" title="Bagobo">Bagobos</a>, referred to the river as <i>Davohaha</i>, which means "fire", while another tribe, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guiangan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guiangan (page does not exist)">Guiangan</a> tribe, called the river as <i>Duhwow</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of the region dates back to the times when various tribes occupied the region. It is believed that the <a href="/wiki/Lumad_peoples#Manobo" title="Lumad peoples">Manobos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumad_peoples#Mandaya" title="Lumad peoples">Mandayas</a> and the Bagobos occupied the area. These are the same tribes that created the small settlements and communities that eventually became Mindanao.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_contact_of_the_region_by_Europeans">First contact of the region by Europeans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First contact of the region by Europeans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Davao_Gulf" title="Davao Gulf">Davao Gulf</a> area is the first region in the country that is in contact with the Europeans, with such contacts taking place as early as 16th century. The Portuguese are the ones who preceded the Spaniards, who are the ones to colonize the region albeit much later, in sighting and visiting the region. In 1512, Francisco Serrano was shipwrecked in the shallow waters and coral reefs of Cape of San Agustín, located in what is now the province of Davao Oriental. In 1538, Francisco de Castro, a Portuguese captain, was driven by strong winds to the southeastern coast of Mindanao. He baptized several chieftains in the area.</p>
<p>Around January 1546, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Xavier" title="Francis Xavier">Francis Xavier</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> priest, left Malacca and went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollucas" title="Mollucas">Molucca Islands</a>, then called the Spice Islands, where the Portuguese had some settlements, and for a year and a half he preached the Gospel to the inhabitants of Amboyna, Ternate, Baranura, and other lesser islands. It is claimed by some that during this expedition he landed on the island of Mindanao, which is confirmed by some writers of the seventeenth century, and in the Bull of canonization issued in 1623. It is also said that he is the one to have preached the Gospel in Mindanao.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_administration">Spanish administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spanish administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For centuries the tribes lived in relative peace until the Spanish, under the adventurous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> businessman Don Jose Oyanguren, arrived in the region in 1847. At that time, the Moro tribal chieftain Datu Bago was in control of the area in what is now Davao City. Don Oyanguren attempted to conquer the area which Datu Bago had ruled; although he failed at first, the Moro chieftain eventually evacuated his people to live in the areas near <a href="/wiki/Mount_Apo" title="Mount Apo">Mount Apo</a>. This is the time the town of <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao</a>, then called Nueva Vergara by the Spaniards, was established in the year 1848.</p>
<p>Don Oyanguren attempted to develop the region. Although the Spanish gained the upper hand when they finally controlled the ports of the region, the population of Davao grew very slowly until the arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Christian missionaries</a> in the area in 1890.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_administration">American administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: American administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> in 1898, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies#Colonial_government" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish rule in the region</a> ended. Americans then landed in the region and they subsequently developed the regions communications and transportation systems. During this period, private farm ownership grew in the region. <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> migration in the region began as two Japanese entrepreneurs, Kyosaburo Otta and Yoshizo Furokawa, were finding better agricultural lands for building <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abaca" title="Abaca">abaca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a> plantations in the region. The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Davao" title="Port of Davao">Port of Davao</a> was opened on 1900, becoming the first Philippine international port to be established in the south.</p>
<p>In 1903 until 1914, the region was one of the districts of the former <a href="/wiki/Moro_Province" title="Moro Province">Moro Province</a> in Mindanao. After 1914, the province was replaced by an American colonial agency called <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Mindanao_and_Sulu" title="Department of Mindanao and Sulu">Department of Mindanao and Sulu</a>, which spanned the entire Mindanao island except Lanao. The agency lasted from 1914 to 1920.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_Japanese_occupation">Wartime Japanese occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wartime Japanese occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1942, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_the_Philippines" title="Japanese occupation of the Philippines">Japanese occupation of the Philippines</a> began, the region was one of the first among the Philippine regions to be subjected by Japanese occupation. The Japanese immigrants in Davao acted as a <a href="/wiki/Fifth_column" title="Fifth column">fifth column</a>, welcoming the Japanese invaders during World War II. These Japanese were hated by the Moro Muslims and disliked by the Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Moros were judged as <a href="/wiki/Moros_during_World_War_II" title="Moros during World War II">"fully capable of dealing with Japanese fifth columnists and invaders alike."</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Moros were to fight the Japanese invaders when they landed at Davao on Mindanao.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Japanese went back to their ships at night to sleep since the Moros struck so much fear into them, even though the Moros were outnumbered by the Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The longest battle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippines_Campaign_(1944%E2%80%9345)" title="Philippines Campaign (1944–45)">Allied liberation campaign</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Davao" title="Battle of Davao">Battle of Davao</a>, took place in 1945. After the war, the region eventually passed to the American hands again for at least almost one year before the formal Philippine independence in July 4, 1946; most of the Japanese living in the region were now integrated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_people" title="Filipino people">Filipino population</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippine_administration">Philippine administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Philippine administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Davao_province">Davao province</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Davao province">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davao_(former_province)" title="Davao (former province)">Davao (former province)</a></div>
<p>Even before the Philippine independence in 1946, the entire region was already a single province called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davao_(former_province)" title="Davao (former province)">Davao Province</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao City</a> serving as its capital. The province was one of the largest provinces in the Philippines during that time, spanning more than 20,000 square kilometres (7,700 sq mi). It lasted from 1920 until 1967, when the province was split into three provinces in May 1967: <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Norte" title="Davao del Norte">Davao del Norte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Sur" title="Davao del Sur">Davao del Sur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davao_Oriental" title="Davao Oriental">Davao Oriental</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChanRobles-RA4867_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChanRobles-RA4867-19">[19]</a></sup> After the division, Davao City was officially named its regional center.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_Mindanao_.26_Davao_Region">Southern Mindanao &amp; Davao Region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Southern Mindanao &amp; Davao Region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Region XI, then known as <i>Southern Mindanao</i>, originally covered 6 provinces (<a href="/wiki/Compostela_Valley" title="Compostela Valley">Compostela Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Norte" title="Davao del Norte">Davao del Norte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Sur" title="Davao del Sur">Davao del Sur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_Oriental" title="Davao Oriental">Davao Oriental</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Cotabato" title="South Cotabato">South Cotabato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarangani" title="Sarangani">Sarangani</a>), and the cities of <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digos" title="Digos">Digos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panabo" title="Panabo">Panabo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tagum" title="Tagum">Tagum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samal,_Davao_del_Norte" title="Samal, Davao del Norte">Samal</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Santos" title="General Santos">General Santos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Koronadal" title="Koronadal">Koronadal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSCB-SouthernMindanao-Davao_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSCB-SouthernMindanao-Davao-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Surigao_del_Sur" title="Surigao del Sur">Surigao del Sur</a> was moved to the newly-created region <a href="/wiki/Caraga" title="Caraga">Caraga</a> on February 23, 1995.</p>
<p>On September 2001, <i>Executive Order No. 36</i> was enacted which reorganized the regions in Mindanao. <span class="nowrap">Region XI</span>, then known as <i>Southern Mindanao</i>, was renamed <i>Davao Region</i>, and the provinces of South Cotabato and Sarangani, and the cities of General Santos and Koronadal were moved to <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Soccsksargen" title="Soccsksargen">Region XII</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSCB-SouthernMindanao-Davao_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSCB-SouthernMindanao-Davao-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GovPH-EO36_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-GovPH-EO36-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The region has a generally uniform distribution of rainfall through the year. It lies outside the typhoon belt.</p>

<p>Davao Region is the most populous region in Mindanao and the 7th most populous in the country, with a total of 4,893,318 inhabitants in 2015. Davao City, its regional capital, is also the largest city in Mindanao, with an area of 2,444 km<sup>2</sup>, the largest in the country and one of the largest in the world, and has 1,632,991 inhabitants in 2015, making it the fourth most populous city in the country and the most populous city proper in the entire <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a>-Mindanao region. <a href="/wiki/Metro_Davao" title="Metro Davao">Davao Metropolitan Area</a>, the primary economic and urban build-up area in the region, is also the most populous in the island and the third most populous in the country, with about 2,274,913 inhabitants in that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSO_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSO-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the region's inhabitants speak <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano language</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> are also spoken in schools, business, commerce, and industry. Prominent indigenous tribes like Bagobo, Manobo, and Mansaka also speak their own languages as well. Chinese immigrants are widespread in the region with a considerable population in <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao City</a>. <a href="/wiki/Davao_Chinatown" title="Davao Chinatown">Davao Chinatown</a> is one of the two defined chinatowns in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binondo" title="Binondo">Binondo</a> being the other one. A considerable population of Japanese can also be seen in the country. Before World War II, Davao was heavily populated by Japanese immigrants. Davao City was the <a href="/wiki/Japantown" title="Japantown">Little Tokyo</a> of the Philippines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The majority of the region's population are <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christians</a>, mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Catholics</a>; however, there are also <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Muslims</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shintoists</a> living in the region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the region’s economy is predominantly agri-based, it is now developing into a center for agro-industrial business, trade and tourism. Its competitive advantage is in agri-industry as its products, papayas, mangoes, bananas, pineapples, fresh asparagus, flowers, and fish products are exported internationally. The region can be a vital link to markets in other parts of Mindanao, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei Darussalam</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. There is also a growing call center sector in the region, mostly centered in <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao City</a>.</p>
<p>There is a gradual shift to industrialization as shown with industry’s growth rate of 8.1% in 1996. Other economic activities are mining, fishery, forestry and agriculture. Due to the region's rise as the main commercial and industrial hub of Mindanao, many of its workers are oriented to urban services such as putting small-scale businesses and working in commercial industries in thriving urban areas like <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tagum" title="Tagum">Tagum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Digos" title="Digos">Digos</a>. Both private and foreign investors and businessmen are putting up huge business centers in the region, fueling up its commercial growth rate.</p>
<p>The region is also venturing to online business like <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourcing</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ports_and_Airport">Ports and Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ports and Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The region’s principal ports are the Sasa International Seaport in Sasa and Sta. Ana Pier in the Chinatown District, both in Davao City; Panabo Seaport in Davao del Norte; and Mati Seaport in Davao Oriental. The former two, both of which are located in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Davao" title="Port of Davao">Port of Davao</a> in Davao City, can service both interisland and international shipments. Sasa International Container Port, also located in the Port of Davao, is the busiest in Mindanao.</p>
<p>The international airport in Davao City, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Bangoy_International_Airport" title="Francisco Bangoy International Airport">Davao International Airport</a>, is the largest and most developed in Mindanao, has the second longest runway in the island, and the third most busiest in the country, after <a href="/wiki/Ninoy_Aquino_International_Airport" title="Ninoy Aquino International Airport">Ninoy Aquino International Airport</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mactan-Cebu_International_Airport" title="Mactan-Cebu International Airport">Mactan-Cebu International Airport</a>. Being the only airport in the island that is currently catering for international destinations, it can handle both domestic and international flights, serving several domestic flights to <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cebu_City" title="Cebu City">Cebu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacolod" title="Bacolod">Bacolod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iloilo_City" title="Iloilo City">Iloilo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_City" title="Zamboanga City">Zamboanga</a> and other major Philippine cities, and international flights to <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manado" title="Manado">Manado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Traffic_Control_Tower" title="Air Traffic Control Tower">ATC Tower</a> is the most advanced in the country.</p>
<p>The region is accessible by land, air and sea. The region has adequate communications facilities, reliable power, and an abundant water supply.</p>
<p>In December 2016, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_Duterte" title="Rodrigo Duterte">President Duerte</a> helped enter a contract with Chinese investors to create a new port in the region of Isla Verde in Davao.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This involves the creation of three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_islands" title="Artificial islands">artificial islands</a> by a set of Chinese companies including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CCCC_Dredging_Group_Co._Ltd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CCCC Dredging Group Co. Ltd (page does not exist)">CCCC Dredging Group Co. Ltd</a> for $200 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Davao_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The government provides free education at the primary (grade school) and secondary (high school) levels. Some state-run universities in the region are the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Philippines_Mindanao" title="University of the Philippines Mindanao">University of the Philippines Mindanao</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southeastern_Philippines" title="University of Southeastern Philippines">University of Southeastern Philippines</a>. The literacy rate of the country is 93.9%; Davao City has a literacy rate of 98.05%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
