<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Many-worlds interpretation</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

</div>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schroedingers_cat_film.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The quantum-mechanical "<a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a>" theorem according to the many-worlds interpretation. In this interpretation, every event is a branch point; the cat is both alive and dead, even before the box is opened, but the "alive" and "dead" cats are in different branches of the universe, both of which are equally real, but which do not interact with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt71_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt71-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>many-worlds interpretation</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Interpretations_of_quantum_mechanics" title="Interpretations of quantum mechanics">interpretation of quantum mechanics</a> that asserts the <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">objective reality</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_wavefunction" title="Universal wavefunction">universal wavefunction</a> and denies the actuality of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">wavefunction collapse</a>. Many-worlds implies that all <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">possible</a> alternate histories and futures are real, each representing an actual "world" (or "universe"). In layman's terms, the hypothesis states there is a very large—perhaps infinite<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heresy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heresy-2">[2]</a></sup>—number of universes, and everything that could possibly have happened in our past, but did not, has occurred in the past of some other universe or universes. The theory is also referred to as <b>MWI</b>, the <b>relative state formulation</b>, the <b>Everett interpretation</b>, the <b>theory of the universal wavefunction</b>, <b>many-universes interpretation</b>, or just <b>many-worlds</b>.</p>
<p>The original relative state formulation is due to <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Everett_III" title="Hugh Everett III">Hugh Everett</a> in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everett56_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-everett56-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everett57_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-everett57-4">[4]</a></sup> Later, this formulation was popularized and renamed <i>many-worlds</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryce_Seligman_DeWitt" title="Bryce Seligman DeWitt">Bryce Seligman DeWitt</a> in the 1960s and 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt71_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt71-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt67_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt67-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt72_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt72-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">decoherence</a> approaches to interpreting quantum theory have been further explored and developed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeh_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-zeh-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zurek91_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-zurek91-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zurek03_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-zurek03-10">[10]</a></sup> becoming quite popular. MWI is one of many <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">multiverse hypotheses</a> in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. It is currently considered a mainstream interpretation along with the other decoherence interpretations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collapse_theory" title="Collapse theory">collapse theories</a> (including the historical <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theories" title="Hidden variable theories">hidden variable theories</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/De_Broglie%E2%80%93Bohm_theory" title="De Broglie–Bohm theory">Bohmian mechanics</a>.</p>
<p>Before many-worlds, reality had always been viewed as a single unfolding history. Many-worlds, however, views reality as a many-branched tree, wherein every possible quantum outcome is realised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Many-worlds reconciles the observation of non-<a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">deterministic</a> events, such as <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">random radioactive decay</a>, with the fully deterministic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equations" title="Partial differential equations">equations</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>.</p>
<p>In many-worlds, the <a href="/wiki/Phenomenalism" title="Phenomenalism">subjective appearance</a> of wavefunction collapse is explained by the mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">quantum decoherence</a>, and this is supposed to resolve all of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_paradox" title="Quantum paradox">correlation paradoxes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/EPR_paradox" title="EPR paradox">EPR paradox</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73paradox_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73paradox-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everett57paradox_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-everett57paradox-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt71_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt71-1">[1]</a></sup> since every possible outcome of every event defines or exists in its own "history" or "world".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Dublin in 1952 <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Erwin Schrödinger</a> gave a lecture in which at one point he jocularly warned his audience that what he was about to say might "seem lunatic". He went on to assert that when his Nobel <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">equations</a> seem to be describing several different histories, they are "not alternatives but all really happen simultaneously". This is the earliest known reference to the many-worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outline">Outline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:190px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hugh-Everett.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="370" data-file-width="270" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Hugh-Everett.jpg/188px-Hugh-Everett.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cf/Hugh-Everett.jpg 1.5x" width="188"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hugh-Everett.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hugh Everett III (1930–1982) was the first <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> who proposed the many-worlds interpretation (MWI) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>, which he termed his "relative state" formulation.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although several versions of many-worlds have been proposed since <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Everett_III" title="Hugh Everett III">Hugh Everett</a>'s original work,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everett57_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-everett57-4">[4]</a></sup> they all contain one key idea: the equations of physics that model the time evolution of systems <i>without</i> embedded observers are sufficient for modelling systems which <i>do</i> contain observers; in particular there is no observation-triggered <a href="/wiki/Wave_function_collapse" title="Wave function collapse">wave function collapse</a> which the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> proposes. Provided the theory is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">linear</a> with respect to the wavefunction, the exact form of the <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantum</a> dynamics modelled, be it the non-relativistic <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger equation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_field_theory" title="Relativistic quantum field theory">relativistic quantum field theory</a> or some form of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">quantum gravity</a> or <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a>, does not alter the validity of MWI since MWI is a <a href="/wiki/Metatheory" title="Metatheory">metatheory</a> applicable to all linear <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theories</a>, and there is no experimental evidence for any non-linearity of the wavefunction in physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weinberglinearitydoft_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-weinberglinearitydoft-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weinberglinearityannalsop_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-weinberglinearityannalsop-18">[18]</a></sup> MWI's main conclusion is that the universe (or <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">multiverse</a> in this context) is composed of a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">quantum superposition</a> of very many, possibly even <a href="/wiki/Uncountable_set" title="Uncountable set">non-denumerably</a> <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinitely</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heresy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heresy-2">[2]</a></sup> many, increasingly divergent, non-communicating parallel universes or quantum worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The idea of MWI originated in Everett's <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> thesis "The Theory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Wavefunction" title="Universal Wavefunction">Universal Wavefunction</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73-7">[7]</a></sup> developed under his thesis advisor <a href="/wiki/John_Archibald_Wheeler" title="John Archibald Wheeler">John Archibald Wheeler</a>, a shorter summary of which was published in 1957 entitled "Relative State Formulation of Quantum Mechanics" (Wheeler contributed the title "relative state";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheeler_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-wheeler-19">[19]</a></sup> Everett originally called his approach the "Correlation Interpretation", where "correlation" refers to <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglement</a>). The phrase "many-worlds" is due to <a href="/wiki/Bryce_DeWitt" title="Bryce DeWitt">Bryce DeWitt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73-7">[7]</a></sup> who was responsible for the wider popularisation of Everett's theory, which had been largely ignored for the first decade after publication. DeWitt's phrase "many-worlds" has become so much more popular than Everett's "Universal Wavefunction" or Everett–Wheeler's "Relative State Formulation" that many forget that this is only a difference of terminology; the content of both of Everett's papers and DeWitt's popular article is the same.</p>
<p>The many-worlds interpretation shares many similarities with later, other "post-Everett" interpretations of quantum mechanics which also use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decoherence" title="Decoherence">decoherence</a> to explain the process of measurement or wavefunction collapse. MWI treats the other histories or worlds as real since it regards the universal wavefunction as the "basic physical entity"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reality_wf57_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-reality_wf57-20">[20]</a></sup> or "the fundamental entity, obeying at all times a deterministic wave equation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reality_wf73_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-reality_wf73-21">[21]</a></sup> The other decoherent interpretations, such as <a href="/wiki/Consistent_histories" title="Consistent histories">consistent histories</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existential_Interpretation" title="Existential Interpretation">Existential Interpretation</a> etc., either regard the extra quantum worlds as metaphorical in some sense, or are <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnostic</a> about their reality; it is sometimes hard to distinguish between the different varieties. MWI is distinguished by two qualities: it assumes <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">realism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reality_wf57_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-reality_wf57-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reality_wf73_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-reality_wf73-21">[21]</a></sup> which it assigns to the wavefunction, and it has the minimal formal structure possible, rejecting any <a href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory" title="Hidden variable theory">hidden variables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_potential" title="Quantum potential">quantum potential</a>, any form of a collapse postulate (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Interpretation" title="Copenhagen Interpretation">Copenhagenism</a>) or mental postulates (such as the <a href="/wiki/Many-minds_interpretation" title="Many-minds interpretation">many-minds interpretation</a> makes).</p>
<p>Decoherent interpretations of many-worlds using <a href="/wiki/Einselection" title="Einselection">einselection</a> to explain how a small number of classical pointer states can emerge from the enormous <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert space</a> of superpositions have been proposed by <a href="/wiki/Wojciech_H._Zurek" title="Wojciech H. Zurek">Wojciech H. Zurek</a>. "Under scrutiny of the environment, only pointer states remain unchanged. Other states decohere into mixtures of stable pointer states that can persist, and, in this sense, exist: They are einselected."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZurekNature2009_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZurekNature2009-22">[22]</a></sup> These ideas complement MWI and bring the interpretation in line with our perception of reality.</p>
<p>Many-worlds is often referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a>, rather than just an interpretation, by those who propose that many-worlds can make testable predictions (such as <a href="/wiki/David_Deutsch" title="David Deutsch">David Deutsch</a>) or is <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">falsifiable</a> (such as Everett) or by those who propose that all the other, non-MW interpretations, are inconsistent, illogical or unscientific in their handling of measurements; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_Everett" title="Hugh Everett">Hugh Everett</a> argued that his formulation was a <a href="/wiki/Metatheory" title="Metatheory">metatheory</a>, since it made statements about other interpretations of quantum theory; that it was the "only completely coherent approach to explaining both the contents of quantum mechanics and the appearance of the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-23">[23]</a></sup> Deutsch is dismissive that many-worlds is an "interpretation", saying that calling it an interpretation "is like talking about dinosaurs as an 'interpretation' of fossil records."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAD_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAD-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpreting_wavefunction_collapse">Interpreting wavefunction collapse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interpreting wavefunction collapse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">

<p>As with the other interpretations of quantum mechanics, the many-worlds interpretation is motivated by behavior that can be illustrated by the <a href="/wiki/Double-slit_experiment" title="Double-slit experiment">double-slit experiment</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">particles of light</a> (or anything else) are passed through the double slit, a calculation assuming wave-like behavior of light can be used to identify where the particles are likely to be observed. Yet when the particles are observed in this experiment, they appear as particles (i.e., at definite places) and not as non-localized waves.</p>
<p>Some versions of the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> of quantum mechanics proposed a process of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">collapse</a>" in which an indeterminate quantum system would probabilistically collapse down onto, or select, just one determinate outcome to "explain" this phenomenon of observation. Wavefunction collapse was widely regarded as artificial and <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, so an alternative interpretation in which the behavior of measurement could be understood from more fundamental physical principles was considered desirable.</p>
<p>Everett's Ph.D. work provided such an alternative interpretation. Everett stated that for a composite system – for example a subject (the "observer" or measuring apparatus) observing an object (the "observed" system, such as a particle) – the statement that either the observer or the observed has a well-defined state is meaningless; in modern parlance, the observer and the observed have become <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">entangled</a>; we can only specify the state of one <i>relative</i> to the other, i.e., the state of the observer and the observed are correlated <i>after</i> the observation is made. This led Everett to derive from the unitary, deterministic dynamics alone (i.e., without assuming wavefunction collapse) the notion of a <i>relativity of states</i>.</p>
<p>Everett noticed that the unitary, deterministic dynamics alone decreed that after an observation is made each element of the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">quantum superposition</a> of the combined subject–object wavefunction contains two "relative states": a "collapsed" object state and an associated observer who has observed the same collapsed outcome; what the observer sees and the state of the object have become correlated by the act of measurement or observation. The subsequent evolution of each pair of relative subject–object states proceeds with complete indifference as to the presence or absence of the other elements, <i>as if</i> wavefunction collapse has occurred, which has the consequence that later observations are always consistent with the earlier observations. Thus the <i>appearance</i> of the object's wavefunction's collapse has emerged from the unitary, deterministic theory itself. (This answered Einstein's early criticism of quantum theory, that the theory should define what is observed, not for the observables to define the theory).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einstein26_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-einstein26-25">[25]</a></sup> Since the wavefunction merely appears to have collapsed then, Everett reasoned, there was no need to actually assume that it had collapsed. And so, invoking <a href="/wiki/Occam%27s_razor" title="Occam's razor">Occam's razor</a>, he removed the postulate of wavefunction collapse from the theory.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Probability">Probability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Probability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Attempts have been made, by many-world advocates and others, over the years to <i>derive</i> the <a href="/wiki/Born_rule" title="Born rule">Born rule</a>, rather than just conventionally <i>assume</i> it, so as to reproduce all the required statistical behaviour associated with quantum mechanics. There is no consensus on whether this has been successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency-based_approaches">Frequency-based approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Frequency-based approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Everett (1957) briefly derived the Born rule by showing that the Born rule was the only possible rule, and that its derivation was as justified as the procedure for defining probability in <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>. Everett stopped doing research in theoretical physics shortly after obtaining his Ph.D., but his work on probability has been extended by a number of people. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrew_Gleason" title="Andrew Gleason">Andrew Gleason</a> (1957) and <a href="/wiki/James_Hartle" title="James Hartle">James Hartle</a> (1965) independently reproduced Everett's work<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which was later extended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> These results are closely related to <a href="/wiki/Gleason%27s_theorem" title="Gleason's theorem">Gleason's theorem</a>, a mathematical result according to which the Born probability measure is the only one on Hilbert space that can be constructed purely from the quantum state vector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bryce_DeWitt" title="Bryce DeWitt">Bryce DeWitt</a> and his doctoral student R. Neill Graham later provided alternative (and longer) derivations to Everett's derivation of the Born rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73-7">[7]</a></sup> They demonstrated that the <a href="/wiki/Normed_vector_space" title="Normed vector space">norm</a> of the worlds where the usual statistical rules of quantum theory broke down vanished, in the limit where the number of measurements went to infinity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decision_theory">Decision theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Decision theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">decision-theoretic</a> derivation of the Born rule from Everettarian assumptions, was produced by <a href="/wiki/David_Deutsch" title="David Deutsch">David Deutsch</a> (1999)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and refined by Wallace (2002–2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and Saunders (2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Deutsch's derivation is a two-stage proof: first he shows that the number of <a href="/wiki/Orthonormal_basis" title="Orthonormal basis">orthonormal</a> Everett-worlds after a branching is proportional to the conventional <a href="/wiki/Probability_density_function" title="Probability density function">probability density</a>. Then he uses game theory to show that these are all equally likely to be observed. The last step in particular has been criticised for <a href="/wiki/Circular_definition" title="Circular definition">circularity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-baker-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Some other reviews have been positive, although the status of these arguments remains highly controversial; some theoretical physicists have taken them as supporting the case for parallel universes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsci-42">[42]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> article, reviewing their presentation at a September 2007 conference,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Andy Albrecht, a physicist at the University of California at Davis, is quoted as saying "This work will go down as one of the most important developments in the history of science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-newsci-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Born rule and the collapse of the wave function have been obtained in the framework of the relative-state formulation of quantum mechanics by Armando V.D.B. Assis. He has proved that the Born rule and the collapse of the wave function follow from a game-theoretical strategy, namely the <a href="/wiki/Nash_equilibrium" title="Nash equilibrium">Nash equilibrium</a> within a von Neumann <a href="/wiki/Zero-sum_game" title="Zero-sum game">zero-sum game</a> between nature and observer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Armando-VDB-Assis-QM_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Armando-VDB-Assis-QM-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symmetries_and_invariance">Symmetries and invariance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Symmetries and invariance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wojciech_H._Zurek" title="Wojciech H. Zurek">Wojciech H. Zurek</a> (2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> has produced a derivation of the Born rule, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decoherence" title="Decoherence">decoherence</a> has replaced Deutsch's informatic assumptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Lutz Polley (2000) has produced Born rule derivations where the informatic assumptions are replaced by symmetry arguments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles Sebens and <a href="/wiki/Sean_M._Carroll" title="Sean M. Carroll">Sean M. Carroll</a>, building on work by <a href="/wiki/Lev_Vaidman" title="Lev Vaidman">Lev Vaidman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> proposed a similar approach based on self-locating uncertainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In this approach, decoherence creates multiple identical copies of observers, who can assign credences to being on different branches using the Born rule.</p>

<p>In Everett's formulation, a measuring apparatus <b>M</b> and an object system <b>S</b> form a composite system, each of which prior to measurement exists in well-defined (but time-dependent) states. Measurement is regarded as causing <b>M</b> and <b>S</b> to interact. After <b>S</b> interacts with <b>M</b>, it is no longer possible to describe either system by an independent state. According to Everett, the only meaningful descriptions of each system are relative states: for example the relative state of <b>S</b> given the state of <b>M</b> or the relative state of <b>M</b> given the state of <b>S</b>. In DeWitt's formulation, the state of <b>S</b> after a sequence of measurements is given by a quantum superposition of states, each one corresponding to an alternative measurement history of <b>S</b>.</p>

<p>For example, consider the smallest possible truly quantum system <b>S</b>, as shown in the illustration. This describes for instance, the spin-state of an electron. Considering a specific axis (say the <i>z</i>-axis) the north pole represents spin "up" and the south pole, spin "down". The superposition states of the system are described by (the surface of) a sphere called the <a href="/wiki/Bloch_sphere" title="Bloch sphere">Bloch sphere</a>. To perform a measurement on <b>S</b>, it is made to interact with another similar system <b>M</b>. After the interaction, the combined system is described by a state that ranges over a six-dimensional space (the reason for the number six is explained in the article on the Bloch sphere). This six-dimensional object can also be regarded as a quantum superposition of two "alternative histories" of the original system <b>S</b>, one in which "up" was observed and the other in which "down" was observed. Each subsequent binary measurement (that is interaction with a system <b>M</b>) causes a similar split in the history tree. Thus after three measurements, the system can be regarded as a quantum superposition of 8 = 2 × 2 × 2 copies of the original system <b>S</b>.</p>
<p>The accepted terminology is somewhat misleading because it is incorrect to regard the universe as splitting at certain times; at any given instant there is one state in one universe.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_state">Relative state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relative state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In his 1957 doctoral dissertation, Everett proposed that rather than modeling an isolated quantum system subject to external observation, one could mathematically model an object as well as its observers as purely physical systems within the mathematical framework developed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Von_Neumann" title="Von Neumann">von Neumann</a> and others, discarding altogether the <i>ad hoc</i> mechanism of wave function collapse. Since Everett's original work, there have appeared a number of similar formalisms in the literature. One such idea is discussed in the next section.</p>
<p>The relative state formulation makes two assumptions. The first is that the wavefunction is not simply a description of the object's state, but that it actually is entirely equivalent to the object, a claim it has in common with some other interpretations. The second is that observation or measurement has no special laws or mechanics, unlike in the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> which considers the wavefunction collapse as a special kind of event which occurs as a result of observation. Instead, measurement in the relative state formulation is the consequence of a configuration change in the memory of an observer described by the same basic wave physics as the object being modeled.</p>
<p>The many-worlds interpretation is DeWitt's popularisation of Everett's work, who had referred to the combined observer–object system as being split by an observation, each split corresponding to the different or multiple possible outcomes of an observation. These splits generate a possible tree as shown in the graphic below. Subsequently, DeWitt introduced the term "world" to describe a complete measurement history of an observer, which corresponds roughly to a single branch of that tree. Note that "splitting" in this sense, is hardly new or even quantum mechanical. The idea of a space of complete alternative histories had already been used in the theory of probability since the mid-1930s for instance to model <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>.</p>

<p>Under the many-worlds interpretation, the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger equation</a>, or relativistic analog, holds all the time everywhere. An observation or measurement of an object by an observer is modeled by applying the wave equation to the entire system comprising the observer <i>and</i> the object. One consequence is that every observation can be thought of as causing the combined observer–object's wavefunction to change into a quantum superposition of two or more non-interacting branches, or split into many "worlds". Since many observation-like events have happened, and are constantly happening, there are an enormous and growing number of simultaneously existing states.</p>
<p>If a system is composed of two or more subsystems, the system's state will be a superposition of products of the subsystems' states. Once the subsystems interact, their states are no longer independent. Each product of subsystem states in the overall superposition evolves over time independently of other products. The subsystems states have become correlated or <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">entangled</a> and it is no longer possible to consider them independent of one another. In Everett's terminology each subsystem state was now <i>correlated</i> with its <i>relative state</i>, since each subsystem must now be considered relative to the other subsystems with which it has interacted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_the_theory">Properties of the theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Properties of the theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MWI removes the observer-dependent role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_measurement" title="Quantum measurement">quantum measurement</a> process by replacing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">wavefunction collapse</a> with <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">quantum decoherence</a>. Since the role of the observer lies at the heart of most if not all "quantum paradoxes," this automatically resolves a number of problems; see for example <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a> <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiment</a>, the <a href="/wiki/EPR_paradox" title="EPR paradox">EPR paradox</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">von Neumann</a>'s "boundary problem" and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave-particle_duality" title="Wave-particle duality">wave-particle duality</a>. <a href="/wiki/Quantum_cosmology" title="Quantum cosmology">Quantum cosmology</a> also becomes intelligible, since there is no need anymore for an observer outside of the universe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>MWI is a <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">realist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterministic" title="Deterministic">deterministic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_realism" title="Local realism">local</a> theory, akin to <a href="/wiki/Classical_physics" title="Classical physics">classical physics</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>), at the expense of losing <a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_definiteness" title="Counterfactual definiteness">counterfactual definiteness</a>. MWI achieves this by removing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">wavefunction collapse</a>, which is indeterministic and non-local, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterministic" title="Deterministic">deterministic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_realism" title="Local realism">local</a> equations of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubinlocality_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubinlocality-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>MWI (or other, broader <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">multiverse</a> considerations) provides a context for the <a href="/wiki/Anthropic_principle" title="Anthropic principle">anthropic principle</a> which may provide an explanation for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine-tuned_universe" title="Fine-tuned universe">fine-tuned universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>MWI, being a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">decoherent</a> formulation, is axiomatically more streamlined than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Interpretation" title="Copenhagen Interpretation">Copenhagen</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">collapse</a> interpretations; and thus favoured under certain interpretations of <a href="/wiki/Occam%27s_razor" title="Occam's razor">Occam's razor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_occam_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_occam-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (March 2013)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> Of course there are other decoherent interpretations that also possess this advantage with respect to the collapse interpretations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_properties_and_possible_experimental_tests">Comparative properties and possible experimental tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Comparative properties and possible experimental tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the salient properties of the many-worlds interpretation is that it does not require an exceptional method of wave function collapse to explain it. "It seems that there is no experiment distinguishing the MWI from other no-collapse theories such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohmian_mechanics" title="Bohmian mechanics">Bohmian mechanics</a> or other variants of MWI... In most no-collapse interpretations, the evolution of the quantum state of the Universe is the same. Still, one might imagine that there is an experiment distinguishing the MWI from another no-collapse interpretation based on the difference in the correspondence between the formalism and the experience (the results of experiments)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-stanford-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, in 1985, David Deutsch published three related thought experiments which could test the theory vs the Copenhagen interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The experiments require macroscopic quantum state preparation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_erasure" title="Quantum erasure">quantum erasure</a> by a hypothetical quantum computer which is currently outside experimental possibility. Since then Lockwood (1989), Vaidman and others have made similar proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-stanford-56">[56]</a></sup> These proposals also require an advanced technology which is able to place a macroscopic object in a coherent superposition, another task which it is uncertain will ever be possible to perform. Many other controversial ideas have been put forward though, such as a recent claim that cosmological observations could test the theory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> and another claim by Rainer Plaga (1997), published in <i><a href="/wiki/Foundations_of_Physics" title="Foundations of Physics">Foundations of Physics</a></i>, that communication might be possible between worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copenhagen_interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Copenhagen interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a>, the mathematics of quantum mechanics allows one to predict <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probabilities</a> for the occurrence of various events. When an event occurs, it becomes part of the definite reality, and alternative possibilities do not. There is no necessity to say anything definite about what is not observed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_universe_decaying_to_a_new_vacuum_state">The universe decaying to a new vacuum state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The universe decaying to a new vacuum state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any event that changes the number of observers in the universe may have experimental consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quantum_tunnelling" title="Quantum tunnelling">Quantum tunnelling</a> to a new <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_state" title="Vacuum state">vacuum state</a> would reduce the number of observers to zero (i.e., kill all life).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmologists</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who? (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> argue that the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is in a <a href="/wiki/False_vacuum" title="False vacuum">false vacuum state</a> and that consequently the universe should have already experienced quantum tunnelling to a true vacuum state. This has not happened and is cited as evidence in favor of many-worlds. In some worlds, quantum tunnelling to a true vacuum state has happened but most other worlds escape this tunneling and remain viable. This can be thought of as a variation on <a href="/wiki/Quantum_suicide_and_immortality" title="Quantum suicide and immortality">quantum suicide</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Many-minds">Many-minds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Many-minds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Many-minds_interpretation" title="Many-minds interpretation">Many-minds interpretation</a></div>
<p>The <i>many-minds</i> interpretation is a multi-world interpretation that defines the splitting of reality on the level of the observers' minds. In this, it differs from Everett's many-worlds interpretation, in which there is no special role for the observer's mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_objections">Common objections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Common objections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The many-worlds interpretation is very vague about the ways to determine when splitting happens, and nowadays usually the criterion is that the two branches have <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">decohered</a>. However, present day understanding of decoherence does not allow a completely precise, self-contained way to say when the two branches have decohered/"do not interact", and hence many-worlds interpretation remains arbitrary. This objection is saying that it is not clear what is precisely meant by branching, and point to the lack of self-contained criteria specifying branching.</p>

<p>MWI states that there is no special role, or need for precise definition of measurement in MWI, yet Everett uses the word "measurement" repeatedly throughout its exposition.</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>MWI response: "measurements" are treated as a subclass of interactions, which induce subject–object correlations in the combined wavefunction. There is nothing special about measurements (such as the ability to trigger a <a href="/wiki/Wave_function_collapse" title="Wave function collapse">wave function collapse</a>), that cannot be dealt with by the usual <a href="/wiki/Unitarity_(physics)" title="Unitarity (physics)">unitary</a> time development process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everett56_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-everett56-3">[3]</a></sup> This is why there is no precise definition of measurement in Everett's formulation, although some other formulations emphasize that measurements must be effectively irreversible or create classical information.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The splitting of worlds forward in time, but not backwards in time (i.e., merging worlds), is time asymmetric and incompatible with the time symmetric nature of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_equation" title="Schrödinger's equation">Schrödinger's equation</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPT_invariance" title="CPT invariance">CPT invariance</a> in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>MWI response: The splitting is time asymmetric; this observed temporal asymmetry is due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_conditions" title="Boundary conditions">boundary conditions</a> imposed by the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>There is circularity in Everett's measurement theory. Under the assumptions made by Everett, there are no 'good observations' as defined by him, and since his analysis of the observational process depends on the latter, it is void of any meaning. The concept of a 'good observation' is the <a href="/wiki/Projection_(linear_algebra)" title="Projection (linear algebra)">projection</a> postulate in disguise and Everett's analysis simply derives this postulate by having assumed it, without any discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (March 2013)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>MWI response: Everett's treatment of observations / measurements covers <i>both</i> idealised good measurements and the more general bad or approximate cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Thus it is legitimate to analyse probability in terms of measurement; no circularity is present.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Talk of probability in Everett presumes the existence of a preferred basis to identify measurement outcomes for the probabilities to range over. But the existence of a preferred basis can only be established by the process of decoherence, which is itself probabilistic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-baker-40">[40]</a></sup> or arbitrary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stappbasis_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-stappbasis-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>MWI response: Everett analysed branching using what we now call the "measurement <a href="/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)" title="Basis (linear algebra)">basis</a>". It is fundamental theorem of quantum theory that nothing measurable or empirical is changed by adopting a different basis. Everett was therefore free to choose whatever basis he liked. The measurement basis was simply the simplest basis in which to analyse the measurement process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownWallace_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrownWallace-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubinbasis_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubinbasis-72">[72]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>We cannot be sure that the universe is a quantum multiverse until we have a <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_everything" title="Theory of everything">theory of everything</a> and, in particular, a successful theory of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">quantum gravity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penrose_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-penrose-73">[73]</a></sup> If the final theory of everything is non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">linear</a> with respect to <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wavefunctions</a> then many-worlds would be invalid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt71_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt71-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everett57_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-everett57-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt67_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt67-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt72_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt72-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt73_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt73-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>MWI response: All accepted <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theories</a> of fundamental physics are linear with respect to the wavefunction. While quantum gravity or <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a> may be non-linear in this respect there is no evidence to indicate this at the moment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weinberglinearitydoft_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-weinberglinearitydoft-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weinberglinearityannalsop_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-weinberglinearityannalsop-18">[18]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">Conservation of energy</a> is grossly violated if at every instant near-infinite amounts of new matter are generated to create the new universes.</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>MWI response: There are two responses to this objection. First, the law of conservation of energy says that energy is conserved <i>within each universe</i>. Hence, even if "new matter" were being generated to create new universes, this would not violate conservation of energy. Second, conservation of energy is not violated since the energy of each branch has to be weighted by its probability, according to the standard formula for the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy#Quantum_theory" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy in quantum theory</a>. This results in the total energy of the multiverse being conserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_energy_conservation_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_energy_conservation-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. <p>Violation of the <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">principle of locality</a>, which contradicts <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>: MWI splitting is instant and total: this may conflict with relativity, since an alien in the Andromeda galaxy can't know I collapse an electron over here before she collapses hers there: the relativity of <a href="/wiki/Simultaneity" title="Simultaneity">simultaneity</a> says we can't say which electron collapsed first – so which one splits off another universe first? This leads to a hopeless muddle with everyone splitting differently. Note: <a href="/wiki/EPR_paradox" title="EPR paradox">EPR</a> is not a get-out here, as the alien's and my electrons need never have been part of the same quantum, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">entangled</a>.</p>

<dd>MWI response: the splitting can be regarded as causal, local and relativistic, spreading at, or below, the speed of light (e.g., we are not split by <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a> until we look in the box).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_splithow_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_splithow-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (March 2013)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacelike" title="Spacelike">spacelike</a> separated splitting you can't say which occurred first — but this is true of all spacelike separated events, <a href="/wiki/Simultaneity" title="Simultaneity">simultaneity</a> is not defined for them. Splitting is no exception; many-worlds is a local theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubinlocality_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-rubinlocality-52">[52]</a></sup></dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a wide range of claims that are considered "many-worlds" interpretations. It was often claimed by those who do not believe in MWI<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> that Everett himself was not entirely clear<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> as to what he believed; however, MWI adherents (such as <a href="/wiki/Bryce_DeWitt" title="Bryce DeWitt">DeWitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Tegmark" title="Max Tegmark">Tegmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Deutsch" title="David Deutsch">Deutsch</a> and others) believe they fully understand Everett's meaning as implying the literal existence of the other worlds. Additionally, recent biographical sources make it clear that Everett believed in the literal reality of the other quantum worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAD_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-MAD-24">[24]</a></sup> Everett's son reported that Hugh Everett "never wavered in his belief over his many-worlds theory".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsciMark_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsciMark-79">[79]</a></sup> Also Everett was reported to believe "his many-worlds theory guaranteed him <a href="/wiki/Quantum_suicide_and_immortality" title="Quantum suicide and immortality">immortality</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of MWI's strongest advocates is <a href="/wiki/David_Deutsch" title="David Deutsch">David Deutsch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deutsch98_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-deutsch98-81">[81]</a></sup> According to Deutsch, the single photon interference pattern observed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_slit_experiment" title="Double slit experiment">double slit experiment</a> can be explained by interference of photons in multiple universes. Viewed in this way, the single photon interference experiment is indistinguishable from the multiple photon interference experiment. In a more practical vein, in one of the earliest papers on quantum computing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deutsch85_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-deutsch85-82">[82]</a></sup> he suggested that parallelism that results from the validity of MWI could lead to "<i>a method by which certain probabilistic tasks can be performed faster by a universal quantum computer than by any classical restriction of it</i>". Deutsch has also proposed that when <a href="/wiki/Reversible_computing" title="Reversible computing">reversible computers</a> become conscious that MWI will be testable (at least against "naive" Copenhagenism) via the reversible observation of spin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis86_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-davis86-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Asher_Peres" title="Asher Peres">Asher Peres</a> was an outspoken critic of MWI. For example, a section in his 1993 textbook had the title <i>Everett's interpretation and other bizarre theories</i>. Peres not only questioned whether MWI is really an "interpretation", but rather, if <i>any</i> interpretations of quantum mechanics are needed at all. An interpretation can be regarded as a purely formal transformation, which adds nothing to the rules of the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Peres seems to suggest<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that positing the existence of an infinite number of non-communicating <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">parallel universes</a> is highly suspect per those<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> who interpret it as a violation of <a href="/wiki/Occam%27s_razor" title="Occam's razor">Occam's razor</a>, i.e., that it does not minimize the number of hypothesized entities. However, it is understood<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the number of elementary particles are not a gross violation of Occam's Razor, one counts the types, not the tokens. <a href="/wiki/Max_Tegmark" title="Max Tegmark">Max Tegmark</a> remarks<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the alternative to many-worlds is "many words", an <a href="/wiki/Allusion" title="Allusion">allusion</a> to the complexity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Von_Neumann" title="Von Neumann">von Neumann</a>'s collapse postulate. On the other hand, the same derogatory qualification "many words" is often applied to MWI by its critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> who see it as a word game which obfuscates rather than clarifies by confounding the von Neumann branching of possible worlds with the Schrödinger parallelism of many worlds in superposition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>MWI is considered by some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unfalsifiable" title="Unfalsifiable">unfalsifiable</a> and hence unscientific because the multiple <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">parallel universes</a> are non-communicating, in the sense that no information can be passed between them. Others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis86_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-davis86-64">[64]</a></sup> claim MWI is directly testable. Everett regarded MWI as falsifiable since any test that falsifies conventional <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a> would also falsify MWI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a>, the "other" worlds of MWI have two different interpretations: real or unreal; he claims that <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Weinberg" title="Steven Weinberg">Steven Weinberg</a> both favour the unreal interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Gardner also claims that the nonreal interpretation is favoured by the majority of physicists, whereas the "realist" view is only supported by MWI experts such as Deutsch and <a href="/wiki/Bryce_DeWitt" title="Bryce DeWitt">Bryce DeWitt</a>. Hawking has said that "according to Feynman's idea", all the other histories are as "equally real" as our own,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawkingreal_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-hawkingreal-84">[84]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a> reports Hawking saying that MWI is "trivially true".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> In a 1983 interview, Hawking also said he regarded the MWI as "self-evidently correct" but was dismissive towards questions about the interpretation of quantum mechanics, saying, "When I hear of <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanns_Johst#Schlageter" title="Hanns Johst">I reach for my gun</a>." In the same interview, he also said, "But, look: All that one does, really, is to calculate conditional probabilities—in other words, the probability of A happening, given B. I think that that's all the many worlds interpretation is. Some people overlay it with a lot of mysticism about the wave function splitting into different parts. But all that you're calculating is conditional probabilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Elsewhere Hawking contrasted his attitude towards the "reality" of physical theories with that of his colleague <a href="/wiki/Roger_Penrose" title="Roger Penrose">Roger Penrose</a>, saying, "He's a <a href="/wiki/Platonic_realism" title="Platonic realism">Platonist</a> and I'm a <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">positivist</a>. He's worried that Schrödinger's cat is in a quantum state, where it is half alive and half dead. He feels that can't correspond to reality. But that doesn't bother me. I don't demand that a theory correspond to reality because I don't know what it is. Reality is not a quality you can test with litmus paper. All I'm concerned with is that the theory should predict the results of measurements. Quantum theory does this very successfully."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> For his own part, Penrose agrees with Hawking that QM applied to the universe implies MW, although he considers the current lack of a successful theory of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">quantum gravity</a> negates the claimed universality of conventional QM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penrose_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-penrose-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polls">Polls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Polls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Advocates of MWI often cite a poll of 72 "leading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmologist" title="Cosmologist">cosmologists</a> and other quantum field theorists"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> conducted by the American political scientist David Raub in 1995 showing 58% agreement with "Yes, I think MWI is true".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>The poll is controversial: for example, <a href="/wiki/Victor_J._Stenger" title="Victor J. Stenger">Victor J. Stenger</a> remarks that <a href="/wiki/Murray_Gell-Mann" title="Murray Gell-Mann">Murray Gell-Mann</a>'s published work explicitly rejects the existence of simultaneous parallel universes. Collaborating with <a href="/wiki/James_Hartle" title="James Hartle">James Hartle</a>, Gell-Mann is working toward the development a more "palatable" <i>post-Everett quantum mechanics</i>. Stenger thinks it's fair to say that most physicists dismiss the many-world interpretation as too extreme, while noting it "has merit in finding a place for the observer inside the system being analyzed and doing away with the troublesome notion of wave function collapse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stenger1995_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-stenger1995-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Max_Tegmark" title="Max Tegmark">Max Tegmark</a> also reports the result of a "highly unscientific" poll taken at a 1997 quantum mechanics workshop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> According to Tegmark, "The many worlds interpretation (MWI) scored second, comfortably ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Consistent_histories" title="Consistent histories">consistent histories</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohm_interpretation" title="Bohm interpretation">Bohm interpretations</a>." Such polls have been taken at other conferences, for example, in response to <a href="/wiki/Sean_M._Carroll" title="Sean M. Carroll">Sean Carroll</a>'s observation, "As crazy as it sounds, most working physicists buy into the many-worlds theory"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Nielsen" title="Michael Nielsen">Michael Nielsen</a> counters: "at a quantum computing conference at Cambridge in 1998, a many-worlder surveyed the audience of approximately 200 people... Many-worlds did just fine, garnering support on a level comparable to, but somewhat below, Copenhagen and decoherence." However, Nielsen notes that it seemed most attendees found it to be a waste of time: <a href="/wiki/Asher_Peres" title="Asher Peres">Asher Peres</a> "got a huge and sustained round of applause… when he got up at the end of the polling and asked 'And who here believes the laws of physics are decided by a democratic vote?'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2005 poll of fewer than 40 students and researchers taken after a course on the Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics at the Institute for Quantum Computing University of Waterloo found "Many Worlds (and decoherence)" to be the least favored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2011 poll of 33 participants at an Austrian conference found 6 endorsed MWI, 8 "Information-based/information-theoretical", and 14 Copenhagen;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> the authors remark that the results are similar to Tegmark's 1998 poll.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speculative_implications">Speculative implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Speculative implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Speculative physics deals with questions which are also discussed in science fiction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_suicide_thought_experiment">Quantum suicide thought experiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Quantum suicide thought experiment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Quantum_suicide_and_immortality" title="Quantum suicide and immortality">Quantum suicide and immortality</a></div>
<p><b>Quantum suicide</b>, as a <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiment</a>, was published independently by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Moravec" title="Hans Moravec">Hans Moravec</a> in 1987<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moravec1987_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moravec1987-97">[97]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bruno_Marchal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bruno Marchal (page does not exist)">Bruno Marchal</a> in 1988<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchal1988_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marchal1988-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchal1991_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marchal1991-99">[99]</a></sup> and was independently developed further by <a href="/wiki/Max_Tegmark" title="Max Tegmark">Max Tegmark</a> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> It attempts to distinguish between the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> of quantum mechanics and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Everett_many-worlds_interpretation" title="Everett many-worlds interpretation">Everett many-worlds interpretation</a> by means of a variation of the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a> <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiment</a>, from the cat's point of view. <b>Quantum immortality</b> refers to the subjective experience of surviving quantum suicide regardless of the odds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit_edu_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-mit_edu-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weak_coupling">Weak coupling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Many-worlds_interpretation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Weak coupling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another speculation is that the separate worlds remain weakly coupled (e.g., by gravity) permitting "communication between parallel universes". A possible test of this using quantum-optical equipment is described in a 1997 <i><a href="/wiki/Foundations_of_Physics" title="Foundations of Physics">Foundations of Physics</a></i> article by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rainer_Plaga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rainer Plaga (page does not exist)">Rainer Plaga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup> It involves an isolated <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Ion_trap" title="Ion trap">ion trap</a>, a quantum measurement that would yield two parallel worlds (their difference just being in the detection of a single photon), and the <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excitation</a> of the ion from only one of these worlds. If the excited ion can be detected from the other parallel universe, then this would constitute direct evidence in support of the many-worlds interpretation and would automatically exclude the orthodox, "logical", and "many-histories" interpretations. The reason the ion is isolated is to make it not participate immediately in the decoherence which insulates the parallel world branches, therefore allowing it to act as a gateway between the two worlds, and if the measure apparatus could perform the measurements quickly enough before the gateway ion is decoupled then the test would succeed (with electronic computers the necessary time window between the two worlds would be in a time scale of <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">milliseconds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nanosecond" title="Nanosecond">nanoseconds</a>, and if the measurements are taken by humans then a few seconds would still be enough). R. Plaga shows that macroscopic decoherence timescales are a possibility. The proposed test is based on technical equipment described in a 1993 <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a></i> article by Itano et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itano_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-itano-102">[102]</a></sup> and R. Plaga says that this level of technology is enough to realize the proposed inter-world communication experiment. The necessary technology for precision measurements of single ions already exists since the 1970s, and the ion recommended for excitation is <sup>199</sup>Hg<sup>+</sup>. The excitation methodology is described by Itano et al. and the time needed for it is given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabi_flopping" title="Rabi flopping">Rabi flopping</a> formula<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<p>Such a test as described by R. Plaga would mean that energy transfer is possible between parallel worlds. This does not violate the fundamental principles of physics because these require energy conservation only for the whole universe and not for the single parallel branches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup> Neither the excitation of the single ion (which is a <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry)" title="Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)">degree of freedom</a> of the proposed system) leads to decoherence, something which is proven by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Welcher_Weg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Welcher Weg (page does not exist)">Welcher Weg</a> detectors which can excite atoms without momentum transfer (which causes the loss of coherence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>The proposed test would allow for low-bandwidth inter-world communication, the limiting factors of bandwidth and time being dependent on the technology of the equipment. Because of the time needed to determine the state of the partially decohered isolated excited ion based on Itano et al.'s methodology, the ion would decohere by the time its state is determined during the experiment, so Plaga's proposal would pass just enough information between the two worlds to confirm their parallel existence and nothing more. The author contemplates that with increased bandwidth, one could even transfer television imagery across the parallel worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup> For example, Itano et al.'s methodology could be improved (by lowering the time needed for state determination of the excited ion) if a more efficient process were found for the detection of fluorescence radiation using 194 nm photons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-5"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1991 article by J.Polchinski also supports the view that inter-world communication is a theoretical possibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Other authors in a 1994 preprint article also contemplated similar ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reason inter-world communication seems like a possibility is because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decoherence" title="Decoherence">decoherence</a> which separates the parallel worlds is never fully complete,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeh16_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-zeh16-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> therefore weak influences from one parallel world to another can still pass between them,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeh16_107-1"><a href="#cite_note-zeh16-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> and these should be measurable with advanced technology. Deutsch proposed such an experiment in a 1985 <i><a href="/wiki/International_Journal_of_Theoretical_Physics" title="International Journal of Theoretical Physics">International Journal of Theoretical Physics</a></i> article,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> but the technology it requires involves human-level <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plaga_59-6"><a href="#cite_note-plaga-59">[59]</a></sup></p>

<p>The many-worlds interpretation has some similarity to <a href="/wiki/Modal_realism" title="Modal realism">modal realism</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, which is the view that the <a href="/wiki/Possible_world" title="Possible world">possible worlds</a> used to interpret modal claims exist and are of a kind with the actual world. Unlike the possible worlds of philosophy, however, in quantum mechanics counterfactual alternatives can influence the results of experiments, as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elitzur%E2%80%93Vaidman_bomb-testing_problem" title="Elitzur–Vaidman bomb-testing problem">Elitzur–Vaidman bomb-testing problem</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_Zeno_effect" title="Quantum Zeno effect">Quantum Zeno effect</a>. Also, while the worlds of the many-worlds interpretation all share the same physical laws, modal realism postulates a world for every way things could conceivably have been.</p>

<p>The many-worlds interpretation could be one possible way to resolve the paradoxes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deutsch98_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-deutsch98-81">[81]</a></sup> that one would expect to arise <i>if</i> <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a> turns out to be permitted by physics (permitting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_timelike_curves" title="Closed timelike curves">closed timelike curves</a> and thus violating <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">causality</a>). Entering the past would itself be a quantum event causing branching, and therefore the timeline accessed by the time traveller simply would be another timeline of many. In that sense, it would make the <a href="/wiki/Novikov_self-consistency_principle" title="Novikov self-consistency principle">Novikov self-consistency principle</a> unnecessary.</p>

<p>The many-worlds interpretation (and the somewhat related concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possible_worlds" title="Possible worlds">possible worlds</a>) has been associated to numerous themes in <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>.</p>
<p>Some of these stories or films violate fundamental principles of causality and relativity, since the <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information-theoretic</a> structure of the path space of multiple universes (that is, information flow between different paths) is very likely complex.</p>
<p>Another kind of popular illustration of many-worlds splittings, which does not involve information flow between paths, or information flow backwards in time considers alternate outcomes of historical events. According to the many-worlds interpretation, all of the historical speculations entertained within the <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate history</a> genre are realized in parallel universes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewitt71_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-dewitt71-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The many-worlds interpretation of reality was anticipated with remarkable fidelity in <a href="/wiki/Olaf_Stapledon" title="Olaf Stapledon">Olaf Stapledon</a>'s 1937 science fiction novel <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Maker" title="Star Maker">Star Maker</a></i>, in a paragraph describing one of the many universes created by the Star Maker god of the title. "In one inconceivably complex cosmos, whenever a creature was faced with several possible courses of action, it took them all, thereby creating many distinct temporal dimensions and distinct histories of the cosmos. Since in every evolutionary sequence of the cosmos there were very many creatures, and each was constantly faced with many possible courses, and the combinations of all their courses were innumerable, an infinity of distinct universes exfoliated from every moment of every temporal sequence in this cosmos."</p>
