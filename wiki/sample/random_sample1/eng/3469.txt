<p><b>Darwin's finches</b> (also known as the <b>Galápagos finches</b>) are a group of about fifteen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They are often classified as the subfamily <b>Geospizinae</b> or <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribe</a> <b>Geospizini</b>. They belong to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thraupidae" title="Thraupidae">tanager</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> and are not closely related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/True_finch" title="True finch">true finches</a>. The closest known relative of the Galápagos finches is <i><a href="/wiki/Dull-coloured_grassquit" title="Dull-coloured grassquit">Tiaris obscura</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They were first collected by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> during <a href="/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle" title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle">the second voyage of the <i>Beagle</i></a>. Apart from the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_finch" title="Cocos finch">Cocos finch</a>, which is from <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Island" title="Cocos Island">Cocos Island</a>, the others are found only on the Galápagos Islands.</p>
<p>The term "Darwin's finches" was first applied by <a href="/wiki/Percy_Lowe" title="Percy Lowe">Percy Lowe</a> in 1936, and popularised in 1947 by <a href="/wiki/David_Lack" title="David Lack">David Lack</a> in his book <i>Darwin's Finches</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> David Lack based his analysis on the large collection of museum specimens collected by the 1905–06 Galápagos expedition of the California Academy of Sciences, to whom Lack dedicated his 1947 book. The birds vary in size from 10 to 20 cm and weigh between 8 and 38 grams. The smallest are the <a href="/wiki/Warbler-finch" title="Warbler-finch">warbler-finches</a> and the largest is the <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian_finch" title="Vegetarian finch">vegetarian finch</a>. The most important differences between species are in the size and shape of their beaks, and the beaks are highly <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adapted</a> to different food sources. The birds are all dull-coloured.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Darwin.27s_theory">Darwin's theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Darwin's theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle" title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle">the survey voyage of</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS <i>Beagle</i></a>, Darwin was unaware of the significance of the birds of the Galápagos. He had learned how to preserve bird specimens while at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> and had been keen on shooting, but he had no expertise in <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithology</a> and by this stage of the voyage concentrated mainly on geology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrantEstes09_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrantEstes09-8">[8]</a></sup> In Galápagos he mostly left bird shooting to his servant <a href="/wiki/Syms_Covington" title="Syms Covington">Syms Covington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nonetheless, these birds were to play an important part in the <a href="/wiki/Inception_of_Darwin%27s_theory" title="Inception of Darwin's theory">inception of Darwin's theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>.</p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> and afterward, Darwin thought in terms of "centres of creation" and rejected ideas concerning the <a href="/wiki/Transmutation_of_species" title="Transmutation of species">transmutation of species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K2000xix_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-K2000xix-10">[10]</a></sup> From <a href="/wiki/John_Stevens_Henslow" title="John Stevens Henslow">Henslow</a>'s teaching, he was interested in the geographical distribution of species, particularly links between species on oceanic islands and on nearby continents. On <a href="/wiki/San_Crist%C3%B3bal_Island" title="San Cristóbal Island">Chatham Island</a>, he recorded that a <a href="/wiki/Mockingbird" title="Mockingbird">mockingbird</a> was similar to those he had seen in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, and after finding a different one on <a href="/wiki/Floreana_Island" title="Floreana Island">Charles Island</a> he carefully noted where mockingbirds had been caught.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrantEstes09_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-GrantEstes09-8">[8]</a></sup> In contrast, he paid little attention to the finches. When examining his specimens on the way to <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a>, Darwin noted that all of the mockingbirds on Charles Island were of one species, those from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isabela_Island_(Ecuador)" title="Isabela Island (Ecuador)">Albemarle</a> of another, and those from <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Island_(Gal%C3%A1pagos)" title="Santiago Island (Galápagos)">James</a> and Chatham Islands of a third. As they sailed home about nine months later, this, together with other facts, including what he had heard about <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_tortoise" title="Galápagos tortoise">Galápagos tortoises</a>, made him wonder about the stability of species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fieldnotesGalapagos_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-fieldnotesGalapagos-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his return from the voyage, Darwin presented the finches to the <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_London" title="Zoological Society of London">Zoological Society of London</a> on 4 January 1837, along with other mammal and bird specimens that he had collected. The bird specimens, including the finches, were given to <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a>, the famous English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist">ornithologist</a>, for identification. Gould set aside his paying work and at the next meeting, on 10 January, reported that the birds from the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> that Darwin had thought were <a href="/wiki/Icterid" title="Icterid">blackbirds</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Grosbeak" title="Grosbeak">gross-beaks</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a> were actually "a series of ground Finches which are so peculiar [as to form] an entirely new group, containing 12 species". This story made the newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s57_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-s57-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darwin had been in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> at that time. In early March, he met Gould again and for the first time got a full report on the findings, including the point that his Galápagos "<a href="/wiki/Wren" title="Wren">wren</a>" was another closely allied species of finch. The <a href="/wiki/Mockingbird" title="Mockingbird">mockingbirds</a> that Darwin had labelled by island were separate species rather than just varieties. Gould found more species than Darwin had expected,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and concluded that 25 of the 26 land birds were new and distinct forms, found nowhere else in the world but closely allied to those found on the <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s57_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-s57-14">[14]</a></sup> Darwin now saw that, if the finch species were confined to individual islands, like the mockingbirds, this would help to account for the number of species on the islands, and he sought information from others on the expedition. Specimens had also been collected by Captain <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a>, FitzRoy’s steward Harry Fuller and Darwin's servant <a href="/wiki/Syms_Covington" title="Syms Covington">Covington</a>, who had labelled them by island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-s2006-16">[16]</a></sup> From these, Darwin tried to reconstruct the locations from where he had collected his own specimens. The conclusions supported his idea of the transmutation of species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s57_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-s57-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Text_from_The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle">Text from <i>The Voyage of the Beagle</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Text from The Voyage of the Beagle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the time that he rewrote his diary for publication as <i>Journal and Remarks</i> (later <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle" title="The Voyage of the Beagle">The Voyage of the Beagle</a></i>), he described Gould's findings on the number of birds, noting that "Although the species are thus peculiar to the archipelago, yet nearly all in their general structure, habits, colour of feathers, and even tone of voice, are strictly American".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the first edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle" title="The Voyage of the Beagle">The Voyage of the Beagle</a></i>, Darwin said that "It is very remarkable that a nearly perfect gradation of structure in this one group can be traced in the form of the beak, from one exceeding in dimensions that of the largest gros-beak, to another differing but little from that of a warbler".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time the first edition was published, the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_Darwin%27s_theory" title="Development of Darwin's theory">development of Darwin's theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> was in progress. For the 1845 second edition of <i>The Voyage</i> (now titled <i>Journal of Researches</i>), Darwin added more detail about the beaks of the birds, and two closing sentences which reflected his changed ideas: "Seeing this gradation and diversity of structure in one small, intimately related group of birds, one might really fancy that from an original paucity of birds in this archipelago, one species had been taken and modified for different ends."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The remaining land-birds form a most singular group of finches, related to each other in the structure of their beaks, short tails, form of body and plumage: there are thirteen species, which Mr. Gould has divided into four subgroups. All these species are peculiar to this archipelago; and so is the whole group, with the exception of one species of the sub-group <i>Cactornis</i>, lately brought from Bow Island, in the Low Archipelago. Of <i>Cactornis</i>, the two species may be often seen climbing about the flowers of the great cactus-trees; but all the other species of this group of finches, mingled together in flocks, feed on the dry and sterile ground of the lower districts. The males of all, or certainly of the greater number, are jet black; and the females (with perhaps one or two exceptions) are brown. The most curious fact is the perfect gradation in the size of the beaks in the different species of <i>Geospiza</i>, from one as large as that of a hawfinch to that of a chaffinch, and (if Mr. Gould is right in including his sub-group, <i>Certhidea</i>, in the main group) even to that of a warbler. The largest beak in the genus <i>Geospiza</i> is shown in Fig. 1, and the smallest in Fig. 3; but instead of there being only one intermediate species, with a beak of the size shown in Fig. 2, there are no less than six species with insensibly graduated beaks. The beak of the sub-group <i>Certhidea</i>, is shown in Fig. 4. The beak of <i>Cactornis</i> is somewhat like that of a starling, and that of the fourth subgroup, <i>Camarhynchus</i>, is slightly parrot-shaped. Seeing this gradation and diversity of structure in one small, intimately related group of birds, one might really fancy that from an original paucity of birds in this archipelago, one species had been taken and modified for different ends. In a like manner it might be fancied that <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_hawk" title="Galapagos hawk">a bird originally a buzzard</a>, had been induced here to undertake the office of the carrion-feeding <a href="/wiki/Caracara" title="Caracara">Polybori</a> of the American continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Text_from_On_the_Origin_of_Species">Text from <i>On the Origin of Species</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Text from On the Origin of Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darwin discussed the divergence of species of birds in the Galápagos more explicitly in his chapter on geographical distribution in <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Origin_of_Species" title="On the Origin of Species">On the Origin of Species</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The most striking and important fact for us in regard to the inhabitants of islands, is their affinity to those of the nearest mainland, without being actually the same species. [In] the Galapagos Archipelago... almost every product of the land and water bears the unmistakable stamp of the American continent. There are twenty-six land birds, and twenty-five of these are ranked by Mr. Gould as distinct species, supposed to have been created here; yet the close affinity of most of these birds to American species in every character, in their habits, gestures, and tones of voice, was manifest.... The naturalist, looking at the inhabitants of these volcanic islands in the Pacific, distant several hundred miles from the continent, yet feels that he is standing on American land. Why should this be so? why should the species which are supposed to have been created in the Galapagos Archipelago, and nowhere else, bear so plain a stamp of affinity to those created in America? There is nothing in the conditions of life, in the geological nature of the islands, in their height or climate, or in the proportions in which the several classes are associated together, which resembles closely the conditions of the South American coast: in fact there is a considerable dissimilarity in all these respects. On the other hand, there is a considerable degree of resemblance in the volcanic nature of the soil, in climate, height, and size of the islands, between the Galapagos and Cape de Verde Archipelagos: but what an entire and absolute difference in their inhabitants! The inhabitants of the Cape de Verde Islands are related to those of Africa, like those of the Galapagos to America. I believe this grand fact can receive no sort of explanation on the ordinary view of independent creation; whereas on the view here maintained, it is obvious that the Galapagos Islands would be likely to receive colonists, whether by occasional means of transport or by formerly continuous land, from America; and the Cape de Verde Islands from Africa; and that such colonists would be liable to modification;—the principle of inheritance still betraying their original birthplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymorphism_in_Darwin.27s_finches">Polymorphism in Darwin's finches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polymorphism in Darwin's finches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whereas <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Darwin</a> spent just five weeks in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos" title="Galápagos">Galápagos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Lack" title="David Lack">David Lack</a> spent three months, <a href="/wiki/Peter_and_Rosemary_Grant" title="Peter and Rosemary Grant">Peter and Rosemary Grant</a> and their colleagues have made research trips to the Galápagos for about thirty years, particularly studying Darwin's finches. Here, we look briefly at the case of the large cactus finch <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geospiza_conirostris" title="Geospiza conirostris">Geospiza conirostris</a></i> on Isla <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genovesa" title="Genovesa">Genovesa</a> (formerly Tower Island), which is formed from a shield volcano, and is home to a variety of birds. These birds, like all well-studied groups,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> show various kinds of <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">morphism</a>.</p>
<p>Males are dimorphic in song type: songs A and B are quite distinct. Also, males with song A have shorter bills than B males. This is also a clear difference. With these beaks, males are able to feed differently on their favourite cactus, the prickly pear <i><a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">Opuntia</a></i>. Those with long beaks are able to punch holes in the cactus fruit and eat the fleshy <a href="/wiki/Aril" title="Aril">aril</a> pulp, which surrounds the seeds, whereas those with shorter beaks tear apart the cactus base and eat the pulp and any insect larvae and pupae (both groups eat flowers and buds). This dimorphism clearly maximises their feeding opportunities during the non-breeding season when food is scarce.</p>
<p>If the population is <a href="/wiki/Panmixia" title="Panmixia">panmixic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> then <i>Geospiza conirostris</i> exhibits a balanced genetic polymorphism and not, as originally supposed, a case of nascent <a href="/wiki/Sympatric_speciation" title="Sympatric speciation">sympatric speciation</a>. The selection maintaining the polymorphism maximises the species' <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">niche</a> by expanding its feeding opportunity. The genetics of this situation cannot be clarified in the absence of a detailed breeding program, but two loci with <a href="/wiki/Linkage_disequilibrium" title="Linkage disequilibrium">linkage disequilibrium</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> is a possibility.</p>
<p>Another interesting dimorphism is for the bills of young finches, which are either 'pink' or 'yellow'. All species of Darwin's finches exhibit this morphism, which lasts for two months. No interpretation of this phenomenon is known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For some decades, taxonomists have placed these birds in the family <a href="/wiki/Emberizidae" title="Emberizidae">Emberizidae</a> along with the New World sparrows and Old World buntings (Sulloway 1982). However, the <a href="/wiki/Sibley%E2%80%93Ahlquist_taxonomy_of_birds" title="Sibley–Ahlquist taxonomy of birds">Sibley–Ahlquist taxonomy</a> puts Darwin's finches with the <a href="/wiki/Tanager" title="Tanager">tanagers</a> (Monroe and Sibley 1993), and at least one recent work follows that example (Burns and Skutch 2003). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="American Ornithologists' Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a>, in its <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> check-list, places the Cocos finch in the Emberizidae but with an asterisk indicating that the placement is probably wrong (AOU 1998–2006); in its tentative <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> check-list, the Galápagos species are <i>incertae sedis</i>, of uncertain place (Remsen et al. 2007).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_basis_of_beak_evolution">Molecular basis of beak evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_finches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Molecular basis of beak evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Developmental research in 2004 found that <a href="/wiki/Bone_morphogenetic_protein_4" title="Bone morphogenetic protein 4">bone morphogenetic protein 4</a> (BMP4), and its differential expression during development, resulted in variation of beak size and shape among finches. BMP4 acts in the developing embryo to lay down skeletal features, including the beak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The same group showed that the development of the different beak shapes in Darwin's finches are also influenced by slightly <a href="/wiki/Heterochrony" title="Heterochrony">different timing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotopy" title="Heterotopy">spatial expressions</a> of a gene called calmodulin (CaM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Calmodulin" title="Calmodulin">Calmodulin</a> acts in a similar way to BMP4, affecting some of the features of beak growth. The authors suggest that changes in the temporal and spatial expression of these two factors are possible developmental controls of beak morphology. In a recent study genome sequencing revealed a 240 kilobase haplotype encompassing the <a href="/wiki/ALX1" title="ALX1">ALX1</a> gene that encodes a transcription factor affecting craniofacial development is strongly associated with beak shape diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
