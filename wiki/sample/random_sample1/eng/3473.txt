<p><i><b>Walking with Dinosaurs</b></i> is a 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_film" title="Family film">family film</a> about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinosaurs" title="Dinosaurs">dinosaurs</a> set in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> period, <span class="nowrap">70 million</span> years ago. The production features computer-animated dinosaurs in live-action settings with actors <a href="/wiki/John_Leguizamo" title="John Leguizamo">John Leguizamo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justin_Long" title="Justin Long">Justin Long</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiya_Sircar" title="Tiya Sircar">Tiya Sircar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Skyler_Stone" title="Skyler Stone">Skyler Stone</a> providing <a href="/wiki/Voice-over" title="Voice-over">voiceovers</a> for the main characters. It was directed by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Nightingale" title="Neil Nightingale">Neil Nightingale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barry_Cook" title="Barry Cook">Barry Cook</a> from a screenplay by <a href="/wiki/John_Collee" title="John Collee">John Collee</a>.</p>
<p>The film was produced by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Earth" title="BBC Earth">BBC Earth</a> and Evergreen Films and was titled after <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Dinosaurs" title="Walking with Dinosaurs">1999 television documentary miniseries of the same name</a>. The film, with a budget of <span class="nowrap">US$80 million</span>, was one of the largest independent productions to date; it was financed by <a href="/wiki/Reliance_Entertainment" title="Reliance Entertainment">Reliance Big Entertainment</a> and <a href="/wiki/IM_Global" title="IM Global">IM Global</a> instead of a major studio. The majority of distribution rights were eventually sold to <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>. The crew filmed footage on location in the U.S. state of Alaska and in New Zealand, which were chosen for their similarities to the dinosaurs' surroundings millions of years ago. <a href="/wiki/Animal_Logic" title="Animal Logic">Animal Logic</a> designed computer-animated dinosaurs and added them to the live-action backdrop. Though the film was originally going to lack narration or dialogue, 20th Century Fox executives decided to add voiceovers, believing it would connect audiences to the characters.</p>
<p><i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> premiered on <span class="nowrap">14 December</span> 2013 at the <a href="/wiki/Dubai_International_Film_Festival" title="Dubai International Film Festival">Dubai International Film Festival</a>. It was released in cinemas in 2D and 3D on <span class="nowrap">20 December</span> 2013. Critics commended the film's visual effects but found its storytelling to be subpar and derided the voiceovers as juvenile. The film grossed <span class="nowrap">US$36.1 million</span> in the United States and Canada and <span class="nowrap">US$90.5 million</span> in other territories for a worldwide total of <span class="nowrap">US$126.5 million</span>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> stated the film's global box office performance was disappointing in context of the production budget and marketing costs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologist" title="Paleontologist">paleontologist</a> named Zack takes his nephew Ricky and niece Jade on a <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> hunt. While alone, Ricky is met by a talking <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">rook</a>, who turns into an <i><a href="/wiki/Alexornis" title="Alexornis">Alexornis</a></i> named Alex. He tells him of a story set in the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period 70 million years ago. Patchi is the smallest in a litter of <i><a href="/wiki/Pachyrhinosaurus" title="Pachyrhinosaurus">Pachyrhinosaurus</a></i> hatchlings, and is often bullied by his older brother Scowler. Their father Bulldust is the leader of a <i>Pachyrhinosaurus</i> herd. Alex, who is Patchi's mentor, tries to help Patchi impress a female <i>Pachyrhinosaurus</i> named Juniper, but her herd migrates south without him.</p>
<p>Bulldust moves his herd south as well, but when they try to pass through a forest, they are forced to flee when a fire erupts. Taking advantage of the chaos, a pack of carnivores, led by a <i><a href="/wiki/Gorgosaurus" title="Gorgosaurus">Gorgosaurus</a></i> named Gorgon, attacks the scattered herd. They kill the rest of Patchi and Scowler's family, with Gorgon personally killing Bulldust. Afterwards, Patchi's herd combines with Juniper's as they continue their migration. Gorgon's pack attacks them again; in the ensuing panic, Patchi, Scowler, and Juniper fall into the river and are swept downstream to the ocean, with Alex following them from above. At a beach, Scowler follows a herd of <i><a href="/wiki/Edmontosaurus" title="Edmontosaurus">Edmontosaurus</a></i> to find food, callously leaving Patchi and Juniper behind. The two make their way through a forest and eventually are able to find their herd and Scowler.</p>
<p>After years of making the same <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration" title="Animal migration">migration</a> from north to south and vice versa, an adult Scowler becomes the herd's new leader, and forces Juniper to be his mate, much to Patchi's disappointment. Scowler recklessly leads the herd onto a thinly frozen pond, and Patchi is able to lead the majority of the herd to safety. Enraged and believing Patchi to be usurping him, Scowler goads Patchi into a battle for leadership of the herd. Scowler quickly wins and disowns Patchi, ordering the herd to leave him behind. Despondent and heartbroken, Patchi tries to allow predators to eat him, but Alex convinces him to die for something worth dying for as Bulldust did.</p>
<p>Reinvigorated by the advice, Patchi returns to the herd, only to find them under attack by Gorgon and his pack again. As Gorgon overpowers him in battle, a repentant Scowler orders Patchi to save himself and lead the herd to safety. Instead, Patchi leads them in driving off Gorgon and his pack for good, saving Scowler. Scowler reconciles with Patchi and then concedes leadership of the herd to him, and Patchi goes on to have eggs with Juniper, one of which hatches and Alex asks for it to be named after him. In the present day, moved by Alex's story, Ricky returns the <i>Gorgosaurus</i> tooth to Zack and Jade, who have unknowingly discovered Gorgon's skull.</p>

<p>In the film, the story of the dinosaurs is book-ended by live-action footage. Leguizamo, Long, Sircar, and Stone provide voiceovers for the computer-animated dinosaurs, while the book-end scenes star Urban as an uncle taking his nephew and niece, played by Rowe and Rice, to a dinosaur excavation site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams-2">[2]</a></sup> For the role of Alex, Leguizamo said he sought to conceal his own accent and create a unique voice for Alex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morfoot_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-morfoot-3">[3]</a></sup> He adopted a Spanish accent since parrots had a <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin American</a> origin. He said, "What was most difficult was finding the right pitch, because Alex is a small bird, but he's also the story’s narrator. So he also had to sound paternal and patriarchal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Leguizamo compared his accent to that of <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Montalb%C3%A1n" title="Ricardo Montalbán">Ricardo Montalbán</a>, a Mexican actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Long said he was cast based on his voicing of the chipmunk Alvin in <i><a href="/wiki/Alvin_and_the_Chipmunks_(film)" title="Alvin and the Chipmunks (film)">Alvin and the Chipmunks</a></i> (2007) and its sequels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcmonagle_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcmonagle-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i>, named after BBC's <a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Dinosaurs" title="Walking with Dinosaurs">1999 television documentary miniseries</a>, was produced by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Earth" title="BBC Earth">BBC Earth</a>, an arm of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Worldwide" title="BBC Worldwide">BBC Worldwide</a> that was launched in 2009. The feature film is directed by <a href="/wiki/Barry_Cook" title="Barry Cook">Barry Cook</a>, who was a director for <i><a href="/wiki/Mulan_(1998_film)" title="Mulan (1998 film)">Mulan</a></i> (1998) and the co-director for <i><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Christmas" title="Arthur Christmas">Arthur Christmas</a></i> (2011), and by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Nightingale" title="Neil Nightingale">Neil Nightingale</a>, creative director at BBC Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcdaniel_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcdaniel-8">[8]</a></sup> The script was written by <a href="/wiki/John_Collee" title="John Collee">John Collee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nightingale and BBC Earth's managing director Amanda Hill sought to produce film adaptations to extend the arm's brand of nature programming. The two were inspired by returns for <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Blue_(2003_film)" title="Deep Blue (2003 film)">Deep Blue</a></i> (2003) and <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_(2007_film)" title="Earth (2007 film)">Earth</a></i> (2007), which were theatrical versions cut from their respective nature documentary series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawtrey_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-dawtrey-10">[10]</a></sup> In June 2010, BBC Earth entered a deal with Evergreen Films, based in the United States, to produce a film featuring dinosaurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodderhose_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-lodderhose-11">[11]</a></sup> By the following November, BBC Earth entered a deal with <a href="/wiki/Reliance_Entertainment" title="Reliance Entertainment">Reliance Big Entertainment</a> to finance the production of three films, including <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i>. The deal had initially attached Pierre de Lespinois of Evergreen Films and Neil Nightingale to co-direct the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Barry Cook, who joined the film in March 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup> eventually replaced de Lespinois as director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcdaniel_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcdaniel-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The total production budget was <span class="nowrap">$80 million</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> About 70% of the budget was covered when <a href="/wiki/IM_Global" title="IM Global">IM Global</a>, a finance and sales company backed by Reliance, sold film rights to various distributors at the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Market" title="American Film Market">American Film Market</a> in November 2010. The budget was further covered by tax breaks provided for filming in Alaska and in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. IM Global covered only 15% of the budget directly. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbes_(magazine)" title="Forbes (magazine)">Forbes</a></i> called <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> an independent film with an unconventionally large budget since the production did not originate at a major studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pomerantz_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pomerantz-15">[15]</a></sup> Nightingale explained, "It was originally funded by... Reliance, and then we sold it pretty quickly to 20th Century Fox for most of the world, and to a range of other distributors in a few other countries, and that's not an unusual path for an independent film — in other words, a non-studio film which doesn't originate from a big studio."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamad_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamad-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1176" data-file-width="1796" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg/220px-Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg/330px-Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg/440px-Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lakes_and_mountains_on_the_Kenai_Peninsula.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The directors filmed footage at the Kenai Peninsula in Alaska to serve as a live-action backdrop for the computer-animated dinosaurs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The film features computer-animated creatures in live-action settings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawtrey_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-dawtrey-10">[10]</a></sup> Live-action footage was filmed in New Zealand and in the southern part of the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. Director Nightingale said, "[They] have that kind of temperate climate which represents the period very well. The world was a bit warmer then, so they would have had 24 hours of sunshine in the summer and 24 hours of darkness in the winter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-black-17">[17]</a></sup> Filming began in 2011 in Alaska, where Evergreen Films is headquartered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giardina_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-giardina-18">[18]</a></sup> In the second half of 2011, more than <span class="nowrap">55 people</span> were working out of Evergreen's office in the Alaskan city of <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brehmer_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-brehmer-19">[19]</a></sup> While the film's dinosaurs lived in Alaska during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> period approximately <span class="nowrap">70 million</span> years ago, they lived more in the northern part of the state due to the climate at the time. Filmmakers considered <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Alaska" title="Southeast Alaska">Southeast Alaska</a>'s rainforests below the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> close to the climate that the dinosaurs experienced, so they filmed there and in <a href="/wiki/Southcentral_Alaska" title="Southcentral Alaska">Southcentral Alaska</a>. Specific locations included Crow Creek Mine near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girdwood,_Alaska" title="Girdwood, Alaska">Girdwood, Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kenai_Peninsula" title="Kenai Peninsula">Kenai Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2012, the state government of Alaska awarded the production companies a subsidy of <span class="nowrap">US$1.7 million</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Additional filming also took place on <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a> in New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamad_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-hamad-16">[16]</a></sup> For a river chase scene, filming was performed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid" title="Rapid">rapids</a> in New Zealand using a helicopter and with a 3D camera rig in a rubber boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-black-17">[17]</a></sup> At the locations, the crew built dinosaur shapes out of <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> drain pipes to give the filmmakers a sense of the dinosaurs' scale when filming the live-action backdrop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rowat_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-rowat-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animation">Animation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Animation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Animation work was done by the Australia-based company <a href="/wiki/Animal_Logic" title="Animal Logic">Animal Logic</a>, which joined the production in January 2011. Its involvement with the production created 140 jobs in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bulbeck_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-bulbeck-23">[23]</a></sup> The company's animation director for <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> was <a href="/wiki/Marco_Marenghi" title="Marco Marenghi">Marco Marenghi</a>, who had also worked on the BBC miniseries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The company collaborated with animation producer Jinko Gotoh, who contributed to <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Nemo" title="Finding Nemo">Finding Nemo</a></i> (2003) and <i><a href="/wiki/9_(2009_animated_film)" title="9 (2009 animated film)">9</a></i> (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fusion_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-fusion-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Character designer David Krentz, who also worked on <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animation_Studios" title="Walt Disney Animation Studios">Disney Animation</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_(film)" title="Dinosaur (film)">Dinosaur</a></i> (2000), designed about <span class="nowrap">20 creatures</span> for the film and worked with 5-6 palaeontologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup> The characters were based on creatures found at fossil sites in Alaska and in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Krentz initially designed the creatures in pencil then modeled them with the software <a href="/wiki/ZBrush" title="ZBrush">ZBrush</a> to send to animators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition, palaeontologists provided Animal Logic with technical drawings of dinosaur skeletons so animators could construct the skeletons virtually. The animators collaborated with the palaeontologists to validate the basic movements of the computer-animated dinosaurs. Software was used to overlay muscle to fit the movements. Animal Logic adapted the software Quill, which they used to animate <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguin</a> feathers in their work on <i><a href="/wiki/Happy_Feet" title="Happy Feet">Happy Feet</a></i> (2006), into new software called RepTile to animate dinosaur skin and scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-black-17">[17]</a></sup> It also added feathers for some dinosaurs, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Troodon" title="Troodon">Troodons</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Hesperonychus" title="Hesperonychus">Hesperonychus</a></i>. The color palette and feather pattern of a <a href="/wiki/Golden_pheasant" title="Golden pheasant">golden pheasant</a> was used for the appearance of the <i>Hesperonychus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murphy_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-murphy-27">[27]</a></sup> The natural history unit archives were used to create a "behaviour matrix" that matched dinosaurs' anatomically correct gestures to their moods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-black-17">[17]</a></sup> Animal Logic ultimately created <span class="nowrap">800 animated</span> shots for the film, which director Cook said was a low number for an animated film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 3D effects for the animation were achieved with the <a href="/wiki/Fusion_Camera_System" title="Fusion Camera System">Fusion 3D system</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fusion_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-fusion-25">[25]</a></sup> which was used for <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> (2009), <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers:_Dark_of_the_Moon" title="Transformers: Dark of the Moon">Transformers: Dark of the Moon</a></i> (2011), and live 3D sports broadcasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giardina_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-giardina-18">[18]</a></sup> Cinematographer John Brooks worked with consultants and <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">stereographers</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Pace_Group" title="Cameron Pace Group">Cameron Pace Group</a> to use a two-camera setup and capture film in 3D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Leonard-Morgan" title="Paul Leonard-Morgan">Paul Leonard-Morgan</a> composed the film score for <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i>. He joined the production in July 2013. The <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Filmorchester_Babelsberg" title="Deutsches Filmorchester Babelsberg">German Film Orchestra Babelsberg</a> performed the score at the <a href="/wiki/Babelsberg_Studio" title="Babelsberg Studio">Babelsberg Studio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam, Germany</a>, where it was recorded. Engineer Rupert Coulson then <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixed the score</a> at <a href="/wiki/Associated_Independent_Recording" title="Associated Independent Recording">AIR Studios</a> in London, England. Leonard-Morgan then went to Los Angeles, California to <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(music)" title="Dubbing (music)">dub the music</a> for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Leonard-Morgan's score was one of <span class="nowrap">114 original</span> scores from feature films that were determined by the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences</a> to be eligible for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Academy Award for Best Original Score</a> at the <a href="/wiki/86th_Academy_Awards" title="86th Academy Awards">86th Academy Awards</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> though it was not nominated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voiceovers">Voiceovers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Voiceovers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the film, actors provide voiceovers for the main creatures. Director Barry Cook said the original plan was for the film to be without dialogue or narration. He said, "I think originally, we were looking at a film that could stand alone as a virtual silent movie... You can turn the soundtrack off and still get involved with the story and feel the emotions of the characters. In its final version, the movie has a narration and goes inside the heads of the animals, so you can hear what they're thinking."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup> Executives at <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>, one of the film's main distributors, viewed a rough cut and thought the film needed voiceovers so children in the audience could connect to the characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclintock_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcclintock-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's character designer David Krentz said, "Although the production veered away from being very realistic, the animation still plays independently. The powers that be decided to add narration and voice-over to reach a wider audience and the characters became slightly anthropomorphized to make them more attractive to younger kids."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zahed_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-zahed-13">[13]</a></sup> Palaeontologist Steve Brusatte responded to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">The Scotsman</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s prompt about the "danger of anthropomorphising" the dinosaurs, "The voiceovers are a bit of a compromise; the dinosaurs' lips aren't moving, they are not smiling and having human-like facial expressions or anything like that... They are only anthropomorphised to a small degree and that is necessary for a film like this."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rowat_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-rowat-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeontological_accuracy">Palaeontological accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Palaeontological accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeontology" title="Palaeontology">palaeontology</a>.</div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> reported, "[Director] Nightingale describes the project as 'mainstream entertainment' rather than natural history... but draws accurately on the latest discoveries in paleontology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawtrey_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-dawtrey-10">[10]</a></sup> A team of scientific and technical consultants contributed to the film. One team member was Dr. Steve Brusatte of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edinburgh_University" title="Edinburgh University">Edinburgh University</a>, who is a Chancellor's Fellow in Vertebrate Palaeontology. Brusatte said the filmmakers strove to understand the discoveries about dinosaurs since the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> in 1993, "They used so much of this information that we've learned over the past few decades, about feathered dinosaurs, about how dinosaurs lived in big herds, which dinosaurs preyed on each other, their environments, and used that to tell the story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rowat_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-rowat-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg"><img alt="The Gorgosaurus, a bipedal dinosaur depicted with iridescent scales, stands on mostly rocky terrain with deciduous trees behind it." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="717" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg/300px-Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg/450px-Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg/600px-Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Walking_with_Dinosaurs_Gorgosaurus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>Gorgosaurus</i> is one of the film's main dinosaurs and is depicted with iridescent scales. Its appearance was scrutinized for palaeontological accuracy due to findings of preserved feathers in other tyrannosaurs.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some dinosaurs in the film are feathered. Brusatte said, "Over the past 15 years, we have collected thousands of specimens of feathered dinosaurs – proper bona fide dinosaurs covered in feathers." <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> reported that "no evidence has yet been found" to suggest that the <i>Pachyrhinosaurus</i> or the <i>Gorgosaurus</i> were feathered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamzelou_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamzelou-32">[32]</a></sup> Director Cook said of the <i>Gorgosaurus</i>, "We decided that we wouldn't put feathers on that one, but we did give that dinosaur iridescent scales."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murphy_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-murphy-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i> said, "Many paleontologists and dinosaur fans are disappointed that <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> docudrama's villains, a gaggle of iridescent <i>Gorgosaurus</i>, are devoid of any fluff or fuzz." Shortly after the filmmakers designed their <i>Gorgosaurus</i> for the film, the tyrannosauroid <i><a href="/wiki/Yutyrannus" title="Yutyrannus">Yutyrannus</a></i> was discovered with direct evidence of feathers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yutyrannus_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-yutyrannus-33">[33]</a></sup> While older than <i>Gorgosaurus</i>, <i>Yutyrannus</i> provides further evidence of feathers in tyrannosauroids. <i>National Geographic</i> also noted that <i>Gorgosaurus</i> was depicted as feathered in the 2011 direct-to-video film <i><a href="/wiki/March_of_the_Dinosaurs" title="March of the Dinosaurs">March of the Dinosaurs</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Fiorillo" title="Anthony Fiorillo">Anthony Fiorillo</a> was a consultant for the film and helped determine what dinosaurs lived in Alaska at the time. Fiorillo said "the first known track" of a <a href="/wiki/Therizinosaur" title="Therizinosaur">therizinosaur</a> in Alaska was discovered in 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> which was too late to include in the film's lineup of dinosaurs. The palaeontologist said based on ongoing research of the <i>Pachyrhinosaurus perotorum</i>, which he discovered in Alaska in 2012, he would have modified his advice to the filmmakers about designing the film's <i>Pachyrhinosaurus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Don_Lessem" title="Don Lessem">Don Lessem</a>, a writer who specializes in dinosaurs, said <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> was an improvement from <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> in how the dinosaurs fit the period. He found most of the dinosaurs to be depicted correctly but also highlighted the possibility of the <i>Gorgosaurus</i> having feathers. Lessem stated the Alaskan scenery, while "a spectacular backdrop", did not exist at the time, "Conifers excepted, such scenery did not exist in dinosaur-age Alaska. Think <i>woody outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a></i> instead. Rainy and cool. No grand Rockies. Or much grass. And not much snow or ice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> marketed <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> in the territories where it distributed the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pomerantz_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-pomerantz-15">[15]</a></sup> It also held a launch event at the <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum_of_Los_Angeles_County" title="Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County">Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County</a> in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weinkauf_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-weinkauf-38">[38]</a></sup> The film is an extension of an existing franchise that started with the BBC miniseries and continued with an arena show that put animatronic dinosaurs on display. Since merchandising deals already existed, filmmakers easily began releasing merchandise for the film. <i>Forbes</i> said, "There is already merchandise (toys, lunch boxes etc.) related to the movie which is normally unheard of for a first film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pomerantz_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-pomerantz-15">[15]</a></sup> Macmillan Children's Books (under <a href="/wiki/Macmillan_Publishers" title="Macmillan Publishers">Macmillan Publishers</a>) acquired the license to publish tie-in books for the film in the United Kingdom and <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">the Commonwealth</a>. They published in November 2013 an encyclopaedic guide to the film's dinosaurs, a film handbook, and sticker books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Travelgoods.com produced <a href="/wiki/Back_to_school_(marketing)" title="Back to school (marketing)">back to school</a> merchandise based on the film, and Keldan International sold a hatching-egg toy for Christmas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supermassive Games developed the <a href="/wiki/Wonderbook" title="Wonderbook">Wonderbook</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(video_game)" title="Walking with Dinosaurs (video game)">Walking with Dinosaurs</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>. The game was released on <span class="nowrap">13 November</span> 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_sales">Distribution sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Distribution sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>IM Global, which represented rights to distribute the film, marketed it at the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Market" title="American Film Market">American Film Market</a> in November 2010. In what <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> called one of the "biggest deals in years", 20th Century Fox purchased rights to distribute the film in the United States, the United Kingdom, France, Australia, Japan, South Korea, Latin America, and other territories. <i>Variety</i> reported, "Insiders say it's safe to say that the pre-sales cover most, if not all, of the film’s budget." For other territories, <a href="/wiki/Constantin_Film" title="Constantin Film">Constantin Film</a> purchased distribution rights for Germany, <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Films" title="Alliance Films">Alliance Films</a> for Canada, Aurum for Spain, and Dutch Film Works for Belgium, the Netherlands and Luxembourg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_One" title="Entertainment One">Entertainment One</a> acquired Alliance and Aurum in January 2013, which rearranged distribution rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatrical_run">Theatrical run</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Theatrical run">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> premiered on <span class="nowrap">14 December</span> 2013 at the <a href="/wiki/Dubai_International_Film_Festival" title="Dubai International Film Festival">Dubai International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Distributor <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> also had a special screening for the film in New York City on <span class="nowrap">15 December</span> 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morfoot_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-morfoot-3">[3]</a></sup> It distributed the film in cinemas in the United States and in the United Kingdom on <span class="nowrap">20 December</span> 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It distributed the film in Australia and New Zealand on <span class="nowrap">1 January</span> 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Distributor 20th Century Fox focused advertising on young children. It also advertised the film as one for kids like <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> (2009) was for adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-raven-47">[47]</a></sup> Prior to the film's release, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbes_(magazine)" title="Forbes (magazine)">Forbes</a></i> stated that in the United States, <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> was the only "kid-friendly film" coming out in December 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> said, "<i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i>... is the only new release targeted at children and is expected to appeal primarily to young ones."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Box_Office_Mojo" title="Box Office Mojo">Box Office Mojo</a> commented in its December 2013 forecast, "It should do some solid business among families with younger children, though in a highly-competitive season this is the kind of movie that can get lost in the pack."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It predicted that <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> would gross <span class="nowrap">US$11.8 million</span> over the weekend and rank sixth at the box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-raven-47">[47]</a></sup> <i>ComingSoon.net</i> predicted that with a crowded weekend of films, <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> would gross <span class="nowrap">US$6.8 million</span> over the weekend to rank seventh at the box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <i>TheWrap</i> says that box office analysts predict a weekend opening "in the low-teen millions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cunningham_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-cunningham-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was released in <span class="nowrap">3,231 cinemas</span> in the United States with 84% of the cinemas having 3D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> On the opening weekend in the United States, it grossed <span class="nowrap">US$7.1 million</span> and ranked eighth at the box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> According to the polling firm <a href="/wiki/CinemaScore" title="CinemaScore">CinemaScore</a>, audiences gave the film <a href="/wiki/Academic_grading_in_the_United_States" title="Academic grading in the United States">a "B" grade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heat_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-heat-55">[55]</a></sup> Distributor 20th Century Fox had anticipated an opening weekend of <span class="nowrap">US$10 million</span> to <span class="nowrap">US$12 million</span>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> said, "<i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> is the first major disappointment of the Christmas season."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> It was outperformed almost 3-to-1 by the competing family film <i><a href="/wiki/Frozen_(2013_film)" title="Frozen (2013 film)">Frozen</a></i>, which ranked third at the box office and had been in theaters for a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Outside the United States, <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> was released in <span class="nowrap">40 markets</span> the same opening weekend. It grossed <span class="nowrap">US$13.8 million</span>, with <span class="nowrap">US$1.6 million</span> grossed in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heat_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-heat-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> has grossed <span class="nowrap">US$36.1 million</span> in the United States and Canada and <span class="nowrap">US$94.5 million</span> in other territories for a worldwide total of <span class="nowrap">US$130.6 million</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-bom-58">[58]</a></sup> In the United States, it is one of only twelve feature films to be released in over 3,000 theaters and still improve on <a href="/wiki/Second_weekend_in_box_office_performance" title="Second weekend in box office performance">its box office performance in its second weekend</a>, increasing 2.6% from $7,091,938 to $7,276,172.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> said in January 2014 that the film's global box office performance was disappointing and was likely to only go up to <span class="nowrap">US$125 million</span>, meaning "a potential loss in the tens of millions for the financiers". It reported the financiers' response about the anticipated loss, "They contend they will break even because of sponsorships, merchandising, tax breaks and foreign presales in territories where Fox didn't pick up the film." Fox would also avoid a loss since it did not cover any part of the production budget and would get a distribution fee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclintock_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcclintock-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported that film critics thought that <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "majestic visuals are seriously undermined by pedestrian storytelling".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> said, "Many critics have derided the juvenile tone of the voice dialogue... also noting that the animals' lips don't move."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclintock_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcclintock-31">[31]</a></sup> The film review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gave an aggregate score of 37 out of 100, which it said indicated "generally unfavorable" reviews. It surveyed <span class="nowrap">21 critics</span> and assessed 10 reviews as negative, six as mixed, and five as positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The similar website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> scored the film with 24% based on a survey of <span class="nowrap">70 reviews</span> assessed as positive or negative. It assessed 53 as negative and 17 as positive. The website said, "<i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> boasts painstaking visual brilliance, but it's unfortunately clouded by a clumsy script that's dominated by juvenile humor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> It also reported, "The pundits say the filmmakers seem to have worried that a quasi-nature documentary approach might have turned off the youngsters, but the narrative is so poorly executed that the end result isn't all that entertaining, much less educational."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mark Adams of <i><a href="/wiki/Screen_International" title="Screen International">Screen International</a></i> said <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> worked as "a kids' film for young dino fans". Adams commended the 3D special effects as "immersive and impressive" but thought audiences may be frustrated at "its rather simplistic story". He said, "The film's attempt to make its story broad and accessible at times sits uneasily alongside the spectacular computer animation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams-2">[2]</a></sup> Michael Rechtshaffen, reviewing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i>, also approved of the effects, calling them "first rate". The critic however complained that the added dialogue was "needless" and that it was "safe to assume that the overlay was added after the fact" by studio executives. Rechtshaffen said, "Although the plotting... follows a safely predictable course... It's that forced, unnecessary and largely unfunny dialogue—save for Leguizamo's spirited way with words—that comes up distractingly flat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dan Jolin at <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> panned <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> as "insipid, bloodless, pseudo-educational and offensively anthropomorphised". Jolin applauded the visual effects but said that "the decision to insert chirp-pop songs and voice the principal dinosaurs" overshadowed the effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Tom Meek, reviewing for <i><a href="/wiki/Paste_(magazine)" title="Paste (magazine)">Paste</a></i>, also praised the effects as "a visual wonderment" but disparaged "the cutesy animal voices and personas". Aside from highlights like the line "Think outside the nest" and the song "<a href="/wiki/Tusk_(song)" title="Tusk (song)">Tusk</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood_Mac" title="Fleetwood Mac">Fleetwood Mac</a>, Meek was disappointed by the dialogue and choice of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Brian Switek, reviewing for <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i>, said the voiceovers ruined the film. He said where films like <i><a href="/wiki/The_Land_Before_Time" title="The Land Before Time">The Land Before Time</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=You_Are_Umasou_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="You Are Umasou (film) (page does not exist)">You Are Umasou</a></i> (the latter based on the Japanese book <a href="/wiki/You_Are_Umasou" title="You Are Umasou">of the same name</a>) were children's films with meaningful dialogue, <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> instead had dialogue that "is vacuous and not only lacks emotional weight, but actually drains any emotions the audience might feel for the characters". Switek said the film could be salvaged if its release on home media would include an option to watch it without the dialogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> was released on DVD and Blu-ray in the United States on <span class="nowrap">25 March</span> 2014. It ranked third in both Blu-ray sales and combined DVD and Blu-ray sales. It ranked below <i><a href="/wiki/Frozen_(2013_film)" title="Frozen (2013 film)">Frozen</a></i>, which was in its second week of home media release, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wolf_of_Wall_Street_(2013_film)" title="The Wolf of Wall Street (2013 film)">The Wolf of Wall Street</a></i>, which was also in its first week. Over 35% of sales for <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> were from Blu-ray sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arnold_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-arnold-68">[68]</a></sup> Around 4% of its total sales were Blu-ray discs in 3-D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Blu-ray 3D disc for <i>Walking with Dinosaurs</i> features bonus content including a "Cretaceous Cut" of the film that omits the voiceovers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Walking_With_Dinosaurs:_Prehistoric_Planet_3D"><i>Walking With Dinosaurs: Prehistoric Planet 3D</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walking_with_Dinosaurs_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Walking With Dinosaurs: Prehistoric Planet 3D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014, a shortened 45-minute version of <i>Walking With Dinosaurs</i> was released in 3D theaters across the U.S.A. Unlike the movie, this documentary is narrated in a style similar to that of the TV series, and removes the character names and dialogue. Unlike the series though, <i>Prehistoric Planet 3D</i> has a "profile" appear whenever a new prehistoric animal species shows up; this profile shows the animal's name and diet. The <i>Gorgosaurus</i> that appeared in the movie are referred to as <i>Nanuqsaurus;</i> this is probably because other than a few possible indeterminate remains, <i>Gorgosaurus</i> is not known from Alaska, while <i>Nanuqsaurus</i> is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
