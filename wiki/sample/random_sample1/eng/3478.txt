<p><b>Emperor Taishō</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">大正天皇</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Taishō-tennō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 31 August 1879 – 25 December 1926)</span> was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Emperors_of_Japan" title="List of Emperors of Japan">123rd</a> <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a>, according to the traditional order of succession, reigning from 30 July 1912 until his death in 1926.</p>
<p>The Emperor's personal name was <b>Yoshihito</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">嘉仁</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. According to Japanese custom, during the reign the emperor is called <i>the (present) Emperor</i>. After death he is known by a <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous name</a> that, according to a practice dating to 1912, is the name of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_era_name" title="Japanese era name">era</a> coinciding with his reign. Having ruled during the <a href="/wiki/Taish%C5%8D_period" title="Taishō period">Taishō period</a>, he is posthumously known as "The Taishō Emperor" or simply "Emperor Taishō".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taish%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prince Yoshihito was born at the <a href="/wiki/Akasaka_Palace" title="Akasaka Palace">Akasaka Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yanagihara_Naruko" title="Yanagihara Naruko">Yanagihara Naruko</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a> with the official title of <i>gon-no-tenji</i> ("lady of the bedchamber"). As was common practice at the time, Emperor Meiji's consort, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Sh%C5%8Dken" title="Empress Shōken">Empress Shōken</a>, was officially regarded as his mother. He received the personal name of Yoshihito Shinnō and the title <i>Haru-no-miya</i> from the emperor on 6 September 1879. His two older siblings had died in infancy, and he too was born sickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prince Yoshihito contracted <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">cerebral meningitis</a> within three weeks of his birth, leaving him in poor health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bix.2C_Page_22_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bix.2C_Page_22-2">[2]</a></sup> (It has also been rumoured that he suffered from <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a>, supposedly contracted from the lead-based makeup his <a href="/wiki/Wet_nurse" title="Wet nurse">wet nurse</a> used.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As was the practice at the time, Prince Yoshihito was entrusted to the care of Prince <a href="/wiki/Nakayama_Tadayasu" title="Nakayama Tadayasu">Nakayama Tadayasu</a>, in whose house he lived from infancy until the age of seven. Prince Nakayama had also raised Emperor Meiji as a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From March 1885, Prince Yoshihito moved to the Aoyama Detached Palace, where he was tutored in the mornings on reading, writing, arithmetic, and morals, and in the afternoons on sports, but progress was slow due to his poor health and frequent fevers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keene.2C_page_397-398_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keene.2C_page_397-398-4">[4]</a></sup> From 1886, he was taught together with 15–20 selected classmates from the <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cke" title="Ōke">ōke</a></i> and higher ranking <i><a href="/wiki/Kazoku" title="Kazoku">kazoku</a></i> peerage at a special school, the <i>Gogakumonsho</i>, within the Aoyama Palace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keene.2C_page_397-398_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keene.2C_page_397-398-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yoshihito was officially declared <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir</a> on 31 August 1887, and had his formal investiture as <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">crown prince</a> on 3 November 1888. While crown prince, he was often referred to simply as <b>Tōgu</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">東宮</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (a long-used generic East Asian term meaning crown prince).</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="1200" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg/330px-Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg/495px-Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg/660px-Y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Chikanobu_Asukayama_Park.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_training">Education and training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taish%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1887, Yoshihito entered the elementary department of the <a href="/wiki/Gakush%C5%ABin" title="Gakushūin">Gakushūin</a>; but, due to his health problems, he was often unable to continue his studies. He spent much of his youth by the sea at the Imperial villas at <a href="/wiki/Hayama,_Kanagawa" title="Hayama, Kanagawa">Hayama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Numazu,_Shizuoka" title="Numazu, Shizuoka">Numazu</a> for health reasons. Although the prince showed skill in some areas, such as horse riding, he proved to be poor in areas requiring higher-level thought. He was finally withdrawn from Gakushuin before finishing the middle school course in 1894. However, he did appear to have an aptitude for languages and continued to receive extensive tutoring in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, and history from private tutors at the <a href="/wiki/Akasaka_Palace" title="Akasaka Palace">Akasaka Palace</a>; Emperor Meiji gave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Takehito" title="Prince Takehito">Prince Takehito</a> responsibility for taking care of Prince Yoshihito, and the two princes became friends.</p>
<p>From 1898, largely at the insistence of <a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Hirobumi" title="Itō Hirobumi">Itō Hirobumi</a>, the Prince began to attend sessions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_House_of_Peers" title="Japanese House of Peers">House of Peers</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" title="Diet of Japan">Diet of Japan</a> as a way of learning about the political and military concerns of the country. In the same year, he gave his first official receptions to foreign diplomats, with whom he was able to shake hands and converse graciously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His infatuation with western culture and tendency to sprinkle French words into his conversations was a source of irritation for Emperor Meiji.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 1898, the Prince also traveled from the Numazu Imperial Villa to <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Etajima,_Hiroshima" title="Etajima, Hiroshima">Etajima</a>, visiting sites connected with the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a>. He made another tour in 1899 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a>, visiting government offices, schools and factories (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nippon_Steel" title="Nippon Steel">Yawata Iron and Steel</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukuoka,_Fukuoka" title="Fukuoka, Fukuoka">Fukuoka</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi</a> shipyards in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagasaki,_Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki, Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taish%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2086" data-file-width="1971" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg/180px-Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg/270px-Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg/360px-Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emperor_Taisho%27s_sons_1921.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emperor Taishō's four sons in 1921: <a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Mikasa" title="Prince Mikasa">Takahito</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Takamatsu" title="Prince Takamatsu">Nobuhito</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Chichibu" title="Prince Chichibu">Yasuhito</a></div>

<p>On 10 May 1900, Crown Prince Yoshihito married the then 15-year-old Kujō Sadako (the future <a href="/wiki/Empress_Teimei" title="Empress Teimei">Empress Teimei</a>), daughter of Prince <a href="/wiki/Kuj%C5%8D_Michitaka" title="Kujō Michitaka">Kujō Michitaka</a>, the head of the five senior branches of the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_clan" title="Fujiwara clan">Fujiwara clan</a>. She had been carefully selected by Emperor Meiji for her intelligence, articulation, and pleasant disposition and dignity – to complement Prince Yoshihito in the areas where he was lacking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bix.2C_Page_22_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bix.2C_Page_22-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Akasaka_Palace" title="Akasaka Palace">Akasaka Palace</a> was constructed from 1899 to 1909 in a lavish European <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a> style, to serve as the Crown Prince's official residence. The Prince and Princess had the following children:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito, Prince Michi</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">迪宮裕仁親王</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Michi-no-miya Hirohito Shinnō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 29 April 1901 – 7 January 1989)</span>; the future Emperor Shōwa.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yasuhito,_Prince_Chichibu" title="Yasuhito, Prince Chichibu">Yasuhito, Prince Atsu</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">淳宮雍仁親王</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Atsu-no-miya Yasuhito Shinnō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 26 May 1902 – 4 January 1953)</span>; later Prince Chichibu.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nobuhito,_Prince_Takamatsu" title="Nobuhito, Prince Takamatsu">Nobuhito, Prince Teru</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">光宮宣仁親王</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Teru-no-miya Nobuhito Shinnō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 1 March 1905 – 3 February 1987)</span>; later Prince Takamatsu.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Takahito,_Prince_Mikasa" title="Takahito, Prince Mikasa">Takahito, Prince Sumi</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">澄宮崇仁親王</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sumi-no-miya Takahito Shinnō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 2 December 1915 – 27 October 2016)</span>; later Prince Mikasa.</li>
</ol>
<p>In 1902, Yoshihito continued his tours to observe the customs and geography of Japan, this time of central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>, where he visited the noted Buddhist temple of <a href="/wiki/Zenk%C5%8D-ji" title="Zenkō-ji">Zenkō-ji</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nagano,_Nagano" title="Nagano, Nagano">Nagano</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> With tensions rising between Japan and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, Yoshihito was promoted in 1903 to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Captain_(naval)" title="Captain (naval)">captain</a> in the Imperial Japanese Navy. His military duties were only ceremonial, but he traveled to inspect military facilities in <a href="/wiki/Wakayama_Prefecture" title="Wakayama Prefecture">Wakayama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ehime_Prefecture" title="Ehime Prefecture">Ehime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kagawa_Prefecture" title="Kagawa Prefecture">Kagawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Okayama_Prefecture" title="Okayama Prefecture">Okayama</a> that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 1907, the Crown Prince toured <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Korea</a>, accompanied by Admiral <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dg%C5%8D_Heihachir%C5%8D" title="Tōgō Heihachirō">Tōgō Heihachirō</a>, General <a href="/wiki/Katsura_Tar%C5%8D" title="Katsura Tarō">Katsura Tarō</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Arisugawa_Taruhito" title="Prince Arisugawa Taruhito">Prince Arisugawa Taruhito</a>. It was the first time an heir apparent to the throne had ever left Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During this period, he began studying the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a>, although he never became proficient at it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_emperor">As emperor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taish%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As emperor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taisho_enthronement.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="571" data-file-width="505" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Taisho_enthronement.jpg/220px-Taisho_enthronement.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Taisho_enthronement.jpg/330px-Taisho_enthronement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Taisho_enthronement.jpg/440px-Taisho_enthronement.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taisho_enthronement.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Takamikura,</i> 1917</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 30 July 1912, upon the death of his father, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a>, Prince Yoshihito succeeded him on the throne. The new emperor was kept out of view of the public as much as possible. Having suffered from various neurological problems throughout his life, by the late 1910s, these maladies made it increasingly impossible for him to carry out public functions. On one of the rare occasions he was seen in public, the 1913 opening of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" title="Diet of Japan">Diet of Japan</a>, he is famously reported to have rolled his prepared speech into a cylinder and stared at the assembly through it, as if through a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/spyglass" title="wiktionary:spyglass">spyglass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Although rumors attributed this to poor mental condition, others, including those who knew him well, believed that he may have been checking to make sure the speech was rolled up properly, as his manual dexterity was also handicapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>His lack of articulation and charisma (as opposed to Emperor Meiji), his disabilities and his eccentricities led to an increase in incidents of <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A8se-majest%C3%A9" title="Lèse-majesté">lèse majesté</a>. As his condition deteriorated, he had less and less interest in daily political affairs, and the ability of the <a href="/wiki/Genr%C5%8D" title="Genrō">genrō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Keeper_of_the_Privy_Seal_of_Japan" title="Lord Keeper of the Privy Seal of Japan">Keeper of the Privy Seal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Household_Agency" title="Imperial Household Agency">Imperial Household Minister</a> to manipulate his decisions came to be a matter of common knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1918, he no longer was able to attend Army or Navy maneuvers, appear at the graduation ceremonies of the military academies, perform the annual <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> ritual ceremonies, or even attend the official opening of sessions of the Diet of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1919, he undertook no official duties, and Crown Prince <a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito</a> was named <a href="/wiki/Prince_regent" title="Prince regent">prince regent</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessho_and_Kampaku" title="Sessho and Kampaku">sesshō</a></i>) on 25 November 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Kant.C5.8D_earthquake_of_1923">Great Kantō earthquake of 1923</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taish%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Great Kantō earthquake of 1923">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Taishō's reclusive life was unaffected by the <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kantō earthquake">Great Kantō Earthquake of 1923</a>. Fortuitously, he had moved by royal train to his summer palace at <a href="/wiki/Nikk%C5%8D,_Tochigi" title="Nikkō, Tochigi">Nikko</a> the week before the disaster; but his son, Crown Prince Hirohito, remained at the Imperial Palace where he was at the heart of the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_pigeon" title="Carrier pigeon">Carrier pigeons</a> kept the emperor informed as information about the extent of the devastation became known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Taish%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG/220px-Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG/330px-Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG/440px-Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Imperial_Mausoleum-Taish%C5%8DEmperor.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emperor Taishō's mausoleum in the <a href="/wiki/Musashi_Imperial_Graveyard" title="Musashi Imperial Graveyard">Musashi Imperial Graveyard</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In early December 1926, it was announced that the emperor had <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>. Taishō died of a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a> at 1:25 a.m. in the early morning of 25 December 1926, at the <a href="/wiki/Hayama_Imperial_Villa" title="Hayama Imperial Villa">Hayama Imperial Villa</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hayama,_Kanagawa" title="Hayama, Kanagawa">Hayama</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Sagami_Bay" title="Sagami Bay">Sagami Bay</a> south of Tokyo (in <a href="/wiki/Kanagawa_Prefecture" title="Kanagawa Prefecture">Kanagawa Prefecture</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The funeral was held at night and consisted of a 4-mile-long procession in which 20,000 mourners followed a herd of sacred bulls and an ox-drawn cart containing the imperial coffin. The funeral route was lit with wood fires in iron lanterns. The emperor's coffin was then transported to his mausoleum in the western suburbs of Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yates_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yates-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Taishō has been called the first Tokyo emperor because he was the first to live his entire life in or near Tokyo. Taishō's father was born and reared in Kyoto; and although he later lived and died in Tokyo, Meiji's mausoleum is located on the outskirts of Kyoto, near the tombs of his Imperial forebears; but Taishō's grave is in Tokyo, in the <a href="/wiki/Musashi_Imperial_Graveyard" title="Musashi Imperial Graveyard">Musashi Imperial Graveyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hachi%C5%8Dji,_Tokyo" title="Hachiōji, Tokyo">Hachiōji</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> His son, the Emperor Showa, is buried next to him.</p>
