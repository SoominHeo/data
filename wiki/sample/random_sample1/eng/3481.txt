<p><b>Diane Guerrero</b> (born July 21, 1986)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGrfOitNBss_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DGrfOitNBss-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is an American actress. She is known for her roles as inmate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritza_Ramos" title="Maritza Ramos">Maritza Ramos</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Orange_Is_the_New_Black" title="Orange Is the New Black">Orange Is the New Black</a></i> and Lina on <i><a href="/wiki/Jane_the_Virgin" title="Jane the Virgin">Jane the Virgin</a></i>. Among her other roles was a recurring role on <i><a href="/wiki/Are_We_There_Yet%3F_(TV_series)" title="Are We There Yet? (TV series)">Are We There Yet?</a></i> Guerrero grew up in Boston and remained there after the rest of her family was deported to Colombia. She is an advocate for <a href="/wiki/Immigration_reform" title="Immigration reform">immigration reform</a>. Her role on <i>Orange Is the New Black</i> has twice contributed to wins for the <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award_for_Outstanding_Performance_by_an_Ensemble_in_a_Comedy_Series" title="Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Comedy Series">Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Comedy Series</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diane_Guerrero&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Guerrero was born in New Jersey to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_people" title="Colombian people">Colombian</a> parents and raised in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Massachusetts. As the only member of her immediate family with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizenship_in_the_United_States" title="Citizenship in the United States">United States citizenship</a>, she remained in the U.S. when her parents and older brother were deported back to <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> when she was 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGrfOitNBss_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DGrfOitNBss-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Her parents had pursued legal citizenship, but their representation failed them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guerrero was raised in the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Plain" title="Jamaica Plain">Jamaica Plain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Boston" title="Roxbury, Boston">Roxbury</a> neighborhoods of Boston after being taken in by other Colombian families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGrfOitNBss_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-DGrfOitNBss-1">[1]</a></sup> She has had an interest in acting since a young age and took advantage of free opportunities in the neighborhood or at school. Then she attended <a href="/wiki/Boston_Arts_Academy" title="Boston Arts Academy">Boston Arts Academy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing arts</a> high school, where she was in the music department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT-5">[5]</a></sup> Among her high school activities was singing with a jazz group, but she anticipated pursuing political science and communications in college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2010, she appeared in the <i>Faces</i> <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> that was shot in <a href="/wiki/Norwood,_Massachusetts" title="Norwood, Massachusetts">Norwood, Massachusetts</a> for Louie Bello.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Her first job after college was in a law office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT-5">[5]</a></sup> At age 24, she decided to pursue a career in acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2011, she moved to New York City and studied acting at the <a href="/wiki/Susan_Batson" title="Susan Batson">Susan Batson</a> Studios where she met her manager Josh Taylor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT-5">[5]</a></sup> Guerrero's life experience compels her to advocate for <a href="/wiki/Immigration_reform" title="Immigration reform">immigration reform</a> and is an influence that she draws upon in her acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diane_Guerrero&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She auditioned for a role on <i><a href="/wiki/Devious_Maids" title="Devious Maids">Devious Maids</a></i>, but was cast in <i>Orange is the New Black</i>, where she plays a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronx" title="Bronx">Bronx</a>-bred character that she feels is likely Puerto Rican.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT-5">[5]</a></sup> For season 2, she was part of the cast that earned recognition for <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award_for_Outstanding_Performance_by_an_Ensemble_in_a_Comedy_Series" title="Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Comedy Series">Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Comedy Series</a> at the <a href="/wiki/21st_Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" title="21st Screen Actors Guild Awards">21st Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The cast earned recognition for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Comedy Series again at the <a href="/wiki/22nd_Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" title="22nd Screen Actors Guild Awards">22nd Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> She has had a recurring role on <i><a href="/wiki/Are_We_There_Yet%3F_(TV_series)" title="Are We There Yet? (TV series)">Are We There Yet?</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2014, she appeared in <i>Emoticon ;)</i>, a comedy about a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May%E2%80%93December_romance" title="May–December romance">May–December romance</a> in which her character's father is involved with a doctoral candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Her performance in this film as Amanda ("Mandy") Nevins, an adopted teenage child, elicited positive criticism such as a description of a "well-drawn smaller moment" that was "beautifully rendered" according to Frank Scheck of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> and a performance that "command sympathy" according to Inkoo Kang of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of her upcoming roles is as an 1800s Cuban <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_Army" title="Confederate States Army">confederate</a> spy, loosely based on the life of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loreta_Janeta_Velazquez" title="Loreta Janeta Velazquez">Loreta Janeta Velazquez</a>, for <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_and_John&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter and John (page does not exist)">Peter and John</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-DGoOitNBiotFLoFfMLoT-5">[5]</a></sup> She was cast in <a href="/wiki/The_CW" title="The CW">The CW</a>'s series <i><a href="/wiki/Jane_the_Virgin" title="Jane the Virgin">Jane the Virgin</a></i> in a recurring role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In February 2015, Guerrero was cast as the female lead in <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>' television pilot for <i>Super Clyde</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> but the show was not picked up for series when CBS announced its fall schedule in May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_Fall_2015_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_Fall_2015-19">[19]</a></sup> Guerrero has upcoming film roles in <i>Happy Yummy Chicken</i>, <i>Beyond Control</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godmother_(film)" title="The Godmother (film)">The Godmother</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, Guerrero released <i>In the Country We Love: My Family Divided</i> a memoir about her parents being detained and deported when she was fourteen. The book's written with Michelle Burford and published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Holt_and_Co." title="Henry Holt and Co.">Henry Holt and Co.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guerrero is the lead in a <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> drama under development for the 2017–18 United States network television season based on and sharing the title of her memoir, <i>In the Country We Love.</i> Guerrero will portray a successful corporate attorney who takes on <a href="/wiki/Pro_bono" title="Pro bono">pro bono</a> cases for undocumented immigrants after her own past as the child of <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">deported</a> parents is revealed by a family emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
