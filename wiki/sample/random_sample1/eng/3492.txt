<p><b>Myriapoda</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Subphylum" title="Subphylum">subphylum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">centipedes</a>, and others. The group contains over 13,000 species, most of which are <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berkeley_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berkeley-2">[2]</a></sup> Although their name suggests they have <a href="/wiki/Myriad" title="Myriad">myriad</a> (10,000) legs, myriapods range from having over 750 legs (the millipede <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illacme_plenipes" title="Illacme plenipes">Illacme plenipes</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Illacme_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Illacme-3">[3]</a></sup> to having fewer than ten legs.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> of myriapods reaches back into the late <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a>, although molecular evidence suggests a diversification in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambrian_Period" title="Cambrian Period">Cambrian Period</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Cambrian fossils exist which resemble myriapods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berkeley_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Berkeley-2">[2]</a></sup> The oldest unequivocal myriapod fossil is of the millipede <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumodesmus_newmani" title="Pneumodesmus newmani">Pneumodesmus newmani</a></i>, from the late Silurian (428 million years ago). <i>P. newmani</i> is also important as the earliest known terrestrial animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-myriapoda_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-myriapoda-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garwood_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garwood-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_classification" title="Phylogenetic classification">phylogenetic classification</a> of myriapods is still debated.</p>
<p>The scientific study of myriapods is <a href="/wiki/Myriapodology" title="Myriapodology">myriapodology</a>, and those who study myriapods are myriapodologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy">Anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myriapoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2493" data-file-width="3884" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg/220px-HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg/330px-HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg/440px-HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HouseCentipedeCloseup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The head of <i><a href="/wiki/Scutigera_coleoptrata" title="Scutigera coleoptrata">Scutigera coleoptrata</a></i>, showing <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_eye" title="Compound eye">compound eyes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_mouthparts" title="Arthropod mouthparts">mouthparts</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Myriapods have a single pair of <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a> and, in most cases, simple eyes. The <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_mouthparts" title="Arthropod mouthparts">mouthparts</a> lie on the underside of the head, with an "epistome" and labrum forming the upper lip, and a pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxilla_(arthropod)" title="Maxilla (arthropod)">maxillae</a> forming the lower lip. A pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandible_(arthropod)" title="Mandible (arthropod)">mandibles</a> lie inside the mouth. Myriapods breathe through <a href="/wiki/Spiracle" title="Spiracle">spiracles</a> that connect to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrate_trachea" title="Invertebrate trachea">tracheal system</a> similar to that of insects. There is a long tubular heart that extends through much of the body, but usually few, if any, blood vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IZ_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IZ-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malpighian_tubule" title="Malpighian tubule">Malpighian tubules</a> excrete nitrogenous waste into the digestive system, which typically consists of a simple tube. Although the ventral nerve cord has a <a href="/wiki/Ganglion" title="Ganglion">ganglion</a> in each segment, the brain is relatively poorly developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IZ_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-IZ-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During mating, male myriapods produce a packet of sperm, or <a href="/wiki/Spermatophore" title="Spermatophore">spermatophore</a>, which they must transfer to the female externally; this process is often complex and highly developed. The female lays eggs which hatch as much shortened versions of the adults, with only a few segments and as few as three pairs of legs. The young add additional segments and limbs as they repeatedly <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moult</a> to reach the adult form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IZ_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-IZ-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myriapoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Myriapods are most abundant in moist forests, where they fulfill an important role in breaking down decaying plant material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berkeley_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Berkeley-2">[2]</a></sup> although a few live in <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-arid" title="Semi-arid">semi-arid</a> habitats or even deserts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A very small percentage of species are littoral (found along the sea shore).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barber2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2009-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Littoral_Myriapods_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Littoral_Myriapods-11">[11]</a></sup> The majority are <a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">detritivorous</a>, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">centipedes</a>, which are chiefly nocturnal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauropodan" title="Pauropodan">Pauropodans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphylan" title="Symphylan">symphylans</a> are small, sometimes microscopic animals that resemble centipedes superficially and live in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>. Millipedes differ from the other groups in having their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_segment" title="Body segment">body segments</a> fused into pairs, giving the appearance that each segment bears two pairs of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">legs</a>, while the other three groups have a single pair of legs on each body segment.</p>
<p>Although not generally considered dangerous to humans, many millipedes produce noxious <a href="/wiki/Secretion" title="Secretion">secretions</a> (often containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzoquinones" title="Benzoquinones">benzoquinones</a>) which in rare cases can cause temporary <a href="/wiki/Blister" title="Blister">blistering</a> and discolouration of the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Large centipedes, however can bite humans, and although the bite may cause intense pain and discomfort, fatalities are extremely rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myriapoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There has been much debate as to which arthropod group is most closely related to the Myriapoda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Mandibulata" title="Mandibulata">Mandibulata</a> hypothesis, Myriapoda is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" title="Sister taxon">sister taxon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pancrustacea" title="Pancrustacea">Pancrustacea</a>, a group comprising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustacea" title="Crustacea">Crustacea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hexapoda" title="Hexapoda">Hexapoda</a> (insects and their close relatives). Under the <a href="/wiki/Atelocerata" title="Atelocerata">Atelocerata</a> hypothesis, <a href="/wiki/Hexapoda" title="Hexapoda">Hexapoda</a> is the closest, whereas under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paradoxopoda" title="Paradoxopoda">Paradoxopoda</a> hypothesis, <a href="/wiki/Chelicerata" title="Chelicerata">Chelicerata</a> is the closest. This last hypothesis, although supported by few, if any, morphological characters, is supported by a number of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> There are four classes of extant myriapods, <a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">Chilopoda</a> (centipedes), <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">Diplopoda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pauropoda" title="Pauropoda">Pauropoda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symphyla" title="Symphyla">Symphyla</a>, containing a total of around 12,000 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman-16">[16]</a></sup> While each of these groups of myriapods is believed to be <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a>, relationships among them are less certain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">Centipede</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">Centipedes</a> make up the class Chilopoda. They are fast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predatory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venomous</a>, hunting mostly at night. There are around 3,300 species,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman-16">[16]</a></sup> ranging from the diminutive <i><a href="/wiki/Nannarrup_hoffmani" title="Nannarrup hoffmani">Nannarrup hoffmani</a></i> (less than 12 mm or <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in in length)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> to the giant <i><a href="/wiki/Scolopendra_gigantea" title="Scolopendra gigantea">Scolopendra gigantea</a></i>, which may exceed 30 centimetres (12 in).</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">Millipede</a></div>
<p>Millipedes form the class Diplopoda. Most <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipedes</a> are slower than centipedes, and feed on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_litter" title="Leaf litter">leaf litter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>. They are distinguished by the fusion of each pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_segment" title="Body segment">body segments</a> into a single unit, giving the appearance of having two pairs of legs per segment. Around 8,000 species have been described, which may represent less than a tenth of the true global millipede diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman-16">[16]</a></sup> The name "millipede" is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_word" title="Compound word">compound word</a> formed from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> roots <i>millia</i> ("thousand") and <i>pes</i> (gen. <i>pedis</i>) ("foot"), although millipedes typically have between 36 and 400 legs. One species, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illacme_plenipes" title="Illacme plenipes">Illacme plenipes</a></i>, has the greatest number of legs of any animal, with 750.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Illacme_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Illacme-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pill_millipede" title="Pill millipede">Pill millipedes</a> are much shorter, and are capable of rolling up into a ball, like <a href="/wiki/Armadillidiidae" title="Armadillidiidae">pillbugs</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Symphyla" title="Symphyla">Symphyla</a></div>
<p>About 200 species of them are known worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman-16">[16]</a></sup> They resemble centipedes but are smaller and translucent. Many spend their lives as soil <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infauna" title="Infauna">infauna</a>, but some live <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreally</a>. Juveniles have six pairs of legs, but, over a lifetime of several years, add an additional pair at each <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moult</a> so that the adult <a href="/wiki/Instar" title="Instar">instar</a> has twelve pairs of legs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pauropoda" title="Pauropoda">Pauropoda</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pauropoda" title="Pauropoda">Pauropoda</a> is another small group of small myriapods. They are typically 0.5–2.0 mm long and live in the soil on all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Over 700 species have been described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman-16">[16]</a></sup> They are believed to be the <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> to millipedes, and have the dorsal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tergite" title="Tergite">tergites</a> fused across pairs of segments, similar to the more complete fusion of segments seen in millipedes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthropleuridea">Arthropleuridea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myriapoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Arthropleuridea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arthropleuridea" title="Arthropleuridea">Arthropleuridea</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthropleuridea" title="Arthropleuridea">Arthropleurideans</a> were ancient myriapods that are now extinct, known from the late <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a>. The most famous members are from the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Arthropleura" title="Arthropleura">Arthropleura</a></i>, which was a giant, probably <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivorous</a>, animal that could be up to 3 metres (10 ft) long, but the group also includes species less than 1 cm (0.39 in). Arthropleuridea was historically considered a distinct class of myriapods, but since 2000 scientific consensus has viewed the group as a subset of millipedes, although the relationship of arthropleurideans to other millipedes and to each other is debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson.26Shear2000_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson.26Shear2000-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Myriapod_relationships">Myriapod relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myriapoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Myriapod relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A variety of groupings (<a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a>) of the myriapod classes have been proposed, some of which are mutually exclusive, and all of which represent hypotheses of evolutionary relationships. Traditional relationships supported by morphological similarities (anatomical or developmental similarities) are challenged by newer relationships supported by molecular evidence (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> similarities).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edgecombe.26Giribet2002_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edgecombe.26Giribet2002-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miyazawa_et_al_2014_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miyazawa_et_al_2014-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Dignatha</b> (also called Collifera) is a clade consisting of millipedes and pauropods, and is supported by morphological similarities including the presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnathochilarium" title="Gnathochilarium">gnathochilarium</a> (a modified jaw and plate apparatus) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collum_(millipedes)" title="Collum (millipedes)">collum</a>, a legless segment behind the head.</li>
<li><b>Trignatha</b> (also called Atelopoda) is a grouping of centipedes and symphylans, united by similarities of mouthparts.</li>
<li><b>Progoneata</b> is a group encompassing Dignatha and symphylans while excluding centipedes. Shared features include reproductive openings (<a href="/wiki/Gonopore" title="Gonopore">gonopores</a>) behind the second body segment, and sensory hairs (<a href="/wiki/Trichobothria" title="Trichobothria">trichobothria</a>) with a bulb-like swelling.</li>
<li><b>Edafopoda</b> is a grouping of symphylans and pauropodans that is supported by shared genetic sequences, yet conflicts with Dignatha and Trignatha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zwick_et_al_2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zwick_et_al_2012-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
