<p><b>Tajimi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">多治見市</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tajimi-shi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Japan" title="Cities of Japan">city</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Gifu_Prefecture" title="Gifu Prefecture">Gifu Prefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The city was founded on August 1, 1940.</p>
<p>As of April 1, 2011, the city has an estimated <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of 112,165, with 40,413 <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">households</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> of 1200 persons per km². The total area is 91.24 km².</p>
<p>Tajimi is located in the Tono region of Gifu Prefecture, on the Toki River. Tajimi is well known for ceramics, especially Oribe and Seto styles. In addition to biannual pottery fairs, the city hosts the Mino International Ceramics Festival every three years.</p>
<p>Tajimi is also home to <a href="/wiki/Eih%C5%8D-ji" title="Eihō-ji">Eihō-ji</a>, which belongs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanzenji" title="Nanzenji">Nanzenji</a> faction of the <a href="/wiki/Rinzai_school" title="Rinzai school">Rinzai school</a> of Zen Buddhism. Eihoji is located on Kokeizan, in the northern section of the city. Tajimi is also home to a Catholic monastery belonging to the Society of the Divine Word. The monastery is famous for its wine.</p>
<p>Tajimi has had a strong sister-city relationship with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terre_Haute" title="Terre Haute">Terre Haute</a>, Indiana, USA since the 1960s.</p>
<p>The hottest temperature ever measured in Japan - 40.9˚ Celsius - was recorded in Tajimi on August 16, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tajimi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tajimi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tajimi falls under the <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> classification with very hot, humid summers and relatively cold winters. During the summer, Tajimi is one of the hottest cities within mainland Japan with an average daytime high during of just over 34.1˚ Celsius during the peak of the summer. Tajimi set the record for Japan's highest recorded daytime temperature of 40.9˚ Celsius, which happened in Tajimi on August 16, 2007. Contrarily, Tajimi experiences relatively cold winters with frequent frosts and occasional snow throughout the winter.</p>
