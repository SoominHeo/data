<p><a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">Viscount</a> <b>Takahashi Korekiyo</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">高橋 是清</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 27 July 1854 – 26 February 1936)</span> was a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> who served as a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Peers_(Japan)" title="House of Peers (Japan)">House of Peers</a>, as the 20th <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister of Japan</a> from 13 November 1921 to 12 June 1922, and as the head of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Japan" title="Bank of Japan">Bank of Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Finance (Japan)">Ministry of Finance</a>.</p>
<p>Takahashi made many contributions to Japan's development during the early 20th century, including introducing its first <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> system and securing foreign financing for the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>. Following the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, he introduced controversial financial policies which included abandoning the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>, lowering interest rates, and using the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Japan" title="Bank of Japan">Bank of Japan</a> to finance <a href="/wiki/Deficit_spending" title="Deficit spending">deficit spending</a> by the central government. His decision to cut government spending in 1935 led to unrest within the Japanese military, who assassinated him in February 1936. Takahashi's policies are credited for pulling Japan out of the Depression, but led to soaring inflation following his assassination, as Takahashi's successors became highly reluctant to cut off funding to the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takahashi_Korekiyo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Takahashi was born in <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> (modern-day Tokyo), while Japan was still under the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was the illegitimate son of a court painter in residence at <a href="/wiki/Edo_Castle" title="Edo Castle">Edo Castle</a>, and adopted as the son of Takahashi Kakuji, a low-ranking <i><a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a></i> in the service of the <a href="/wiki/Date_clan" title="Date clan">Date</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyō</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Sendai_Domain" title="Sendai Domain">Sendai Domain</a>. He studied <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> and American culture in a private school run by the missionary <a href="/wiki/James_Curtis_Hepburn" title="James Curtis Hepburn">James Hepburn</a> (the forerunner of <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Gakuin_University" title="Meiji Gakuin University">Meiji Gakuin University</a>). On July 25, 1867, he set sail from Japan to <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and found employment as a menial laborer. Another version of the story has it that he went to the United States to study, but was sold as a slave by his landlord and only with some difficulty was he able to return to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takahashi_Korekiyo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After his return to Japan in 1868, Takahashi taught English conversation. He later became the first master of the <i>Kyōritsu Gakkō</i> high school in Tokyo, (currently Kaisei High School), and at the same time worked as a low-ranking bureaucrat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Education (Japan)">Ministry of Education</a>, and then in the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Agriculture_and_Commerce" title="Ministry of Agriculture and Commerce">Ministry of Agriculture and Commerce</a>. He was appointed as the first chief of the Bureau of Patents, a department of the Ministry of Agriculture and Commerce, and helped organized the <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> system in Japan. At one point, he resigned his government positions and went to <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> to start a silver mining enterprise, but failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Takahashi became an employee of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Japan" title="Bank of Japan">Bank of Japan</a> in 1892, and his talents were soon recognized, as he rose to become vice-president in 1898.</p>
<p>During and after the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a> of 1904–1905, Takahashi raised foreign loans that were critical to Japan's war effort. He met personally with American financier <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Schiff#National_loans" title="Jacob Schiff">Jacob Schiff, who floated half of Japan's loans in the U.S.</a> He also raised loans from the <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_family" title="Rothschild family">Rothschild family</a> in Britain.</p>
<p>For this success, he was appointed to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Peers_(Japan)" title="House of Peers (Japan)">House of Peers</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" title="Diet of Japan">Diet of Japan</a> in 1905.</p>
<p>Takahashi was named president of the <a href="/wiki/Yokohama_Specie_Bank" title="Yokohama Specie Bank">Yokohama Specie Bank</a> in 1906. He was made a <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">baron</a> (<i>danshaku</i>) under the <i><a href="/wiki/Kazoku" title="Kazoku">kazoku</a></i> peerage system in 1907.</p>
<p>Takahashi was Governor of the Bank of Japan from June 1, 1911, through February 20, 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_offices">Political offices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takahashi_Korekiyo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political offices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1913, Takahashi was appointed <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Finance (Japan)">Minister of Finance</a> by <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Yamamoto_Gonnohy%C5%8De" title="Yamamoto Gonnohyōe">Yamamoto Gonnohyōe</a> and then joined the <i><a href="/wiki/Rikken_Seiy%C5%ABkai" title="Rikken Seiyūkai">Rikken Seiyūkai</a></i> political party. He was re-appointed by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Hara_Takashi" title="Hara Takashi">Hara Takashi</a> in 1918. In 1920, Takahashi's title was elevated to <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscount</a> (<i>shishaku</i>). After Hara was assassinated in 1921, Takahashi was appointed both Prime Minister and the <i>Rikken Seiyūkai</i> party president.</p>
<p>Takahashi was the second Christian Prime Minister in Japanese history. His term lasted less than seven months, primarily due to his inability as an outsider to control the factions in his party, and his lack of a power base in the party.</p>
<p>After resigning as Prime Minister, Takahashi still retained the position of president of the <i>Rikken Seiyūkai</i>. He resigned his seat in the House of Peers in 1924, and was elected to a seat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Japan" title="House of Representatives of Japan">Lower House</a> of the Diet of Japan in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_1924" title="Japanese general election, 1924">1924 General Election</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Kat%C5%8D_Takaaki" title="Katō Takaaki">Katō Takaaki</a> became the prime minister and set up a coalition cabinet in 1924, Takahashi accepted the post of Minister of Agriculture and Commerce. He divided the department into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Agriculture_and_Forestry_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry (Japan)">Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Commerce_and_Industry_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Commerce and Industry (Japan)">Ministry of Commerce and Industry</a>. Takahashi resigned from the <i>Rikken Seiyūkai</i> in 1925.</p>
<p>Takahashi served as Finance Minister under the administrations of <a href="/wiki/Tanaka_Giichi" title="Tanaka Giichi">Tanaka Giichi</a> (1927–1929), <a href="/wiki/Inukai_Tsuyoshi" title="Inukai Tsuyoshi">Inukai Tsuyoshi</a> (1931–1932), <a href="/wiki/Sait%C5%8D_Makoto" title="Saitō Makoto">Saitō Makoto</a> (1932–1934) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okada_Keisuke" title="Okada Keisuke">Okada Keisuke</a> (1934–1936). To bring Japan out of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Depression_of_1929" title="Great Depression of 1929">Great Depression of 1929</a>, he instituted dramatically expansionary monetary and fiscal policy, abandoning the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a> in December 1931, and running deficits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Despite considerable success, his fiscal policies involving reduction of military expenditures created many enemies within the military, and he was among those assassinated by rebelling military officers in the <a href="/wiki/February_26_Incident" title="February 26 Incident">February 26 Incident</a> of 1936. His grave is at the Tama Reien Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Fuch%C5%AB,_Tokyo" title="Fuchū, Tokyo">Fuchū, Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>Takahashi appeared on a 50 <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">Yen</a> <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknote</a> issued by the Bank of Japan in 1951. It is the only time that a former president of the Bank of Japan has appeared on one of Japan's banknotes.</li>
<li>Takahashi's Tokyo residence is now the "Takahashi Korekiyo Memorial Park" in Tokyo's <a href="/wiki/Minato,_Tokyo" title="Minato, Tokyo">Minato Ward</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akasaka,_Tokyo" title="Akasaka, Tokyo">Akasaka</a>. However, a portion of the building survives in the <a href="/wiki/Edo-Tokyo_Open_Air_Architectural_Museum" title="Edo-Tokyo Open Air Architectural Museum">Edo-Tokyo Open Air Architectural Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Koganei,_Tokyo" title="Koganei, Tokyo">Koganei</a> city, Tokyo.</li>
<li>Takahashi's fiscal and monetary policies during the Great Depression were in many ways similar to what Keynes later published just a few years later in 1936 in <a href="/wiki/The_General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money" title="The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money">The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money</a>. It is thought but not proven that Takahashi's success contributed heavily to Keynes' theories.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ben_Bernanke" title="Ben Bernanke">Ben Bernanke</a>, chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Federal_Reserve" title="United States Federal Reserve">United States Federal Reserve</a>, characterized Takahashi as a man who "brilliantly rescued Japan from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>", and Japanese prime minister <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a> cited Takahashi as an inspiration for his <a href="/wiki/Abenomics" title="Abenomics">Abenomics</a> policies. On the other hand, Bank of Japan president <a href="/wiki/Masaaki_Shirakawa" title="Masaaki Shirakawa">Masaaki Shirakawa</a> characterized Takahashi's policies of central bank support for the government as a "bitter experience", and in 1982 the Bank of Japan itself characterized Takahashi's Depression-era policies as "the bank's biggest mistake in its 100-year history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
