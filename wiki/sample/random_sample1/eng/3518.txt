<p><b>Heizō Takenaka</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">竹中 平蔵</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Takenaka Heizō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born 3 March 1951)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> economist and retired politician, last serving as <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Internal_Affairs_and_Communications" title="Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications">Minister of Internal Affairs and Communications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan_Post" title="Japan Post">Minister of State for Privatization of the Postal Services</a> in the cabinet of <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Junichiro_Koizumi" title="Junichiro Koizumi">Junichiro Koizumi</a>. As of July 2007, he is a professor at <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> and an advisor for other academic institutions and companies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_life">Academic life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heiz%C5%8D_Takenaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Academic life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Takenaka was the second son of a shoe seller in <a href="/wiki/Wakayama,_Wakayama" title="Wakayama, Wakayama">Wakayama City</a>. He attended <a href="/wiki/Hitotsubashi_University" title="Hitotsubashi University">Hitotsubashi University</a> to study under <a href="/wiki/Ichiro_Nakayama" title="Ichiro Nakayama">Ichiro Nakayama</a> and graduated with a BA in Economics in 1973. While at Hitotsubashi, he played the <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a>, and met his wife (a student at <a href="/wiki/Tsuda_College" title="Tsuda College">Tsuda College</a>) through his mandolin club.</p>
<p>In 1973, Takenaka entered the <a href="/wiki/Development_Bank_of_Japan" title="Development Bank of Japan">Development Bank of Japan</a>. He was transferred into its Institute for Capital Investment Studies in 1977.</p>
<p>In 1981, he left the DBJ to study for a year at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, where he researched capital investment in the United States. The product of his research, the 1984 book <i>Development Studies and Capital Expenditure Economics</i>, won the Suntory Liberal Arts Prize.</p>
<p>Takenaka then worked in the Ministry of Finance as a money supply researcher. He initially planned to stay for two years, but ended up working there for five years, from 1982 to 1987.</p>
<p>He later completed his Ph.D. at <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University" title="Osaka University">Osaka University</a>. He taught as an associate professor at Osaka (1987–89) and Harvard (1989–90), and received tenure in the Faculty of Policy Management of Keio University SFC (Shonan Fujisawa Campus).</p>

<p>Takenaka was picked by Koizumi to become the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minister_of_State_for_Economic_and_Fiscal_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minister of State for Economic and Fiscal Policy (page does not exist)">Minister of State for Economic and Fiscal Policy</a> in 2001. In this capacity, Takenaka has become one of the most prominent voices in the ongoing debate over the privatization and breakup of <a href="/wiki/Japan_Post" title="Japan Post">Japan Post</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002 he became <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minister_of_State_for_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minister of State for Financial Services (page does not exist)">Minister of State for Financial Services</a> as well. In this capacity he was the author of the Takenaka Plan which successfully tackled Japan's banking crisis.</p>
<p>Takenaka won his first election in 2004 and held a <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> seat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Councillors" title="House of Councillors">House of Councillors</a>.</p>
<p>After Koizumi's <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democratic_Party_(Japan)" title="Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)">Liberal Democratic Party</a> crushing victory in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_general_election,_2005" title="Japan general election, 2005">2005 General Election</a>, Takenaka assumed his last position as Minister of Internal Affairs and Communications, in charge of Japan Post privatization.</p>
<p>He further attempted to privatize the national public broadcaster <a href="/wiki/NHK" title="NHK">NHK</a> but Koizumi did not agree and the attempt was stalled. On 15 September 2005, he announced his retirement from politics. On 28 September his resignation from the House of Councillors was permitted. On 29 September, he submitted a resignation letter to the Liberal Democratic Party, which was agreed on 11 November. On the same day his return to Keio University was disclosed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Now, he is the Chairman of <a href="/wiki/Pasona" title="Pasona">Pasona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
