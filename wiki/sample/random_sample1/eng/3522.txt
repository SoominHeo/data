<p>A <i><b>dacquoise</b></i> (<small>French pronunciation:Â â</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[dakwÉz]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">dessert</a> <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cake</a> made with layers of almond and hazelnut <a href="/wiki/Meringue" title="Meringue">meringue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whipped_cream" title="Whipped cream">whipped cream</a> or <a href="/wiki/Buttercream" title="Buttercream">buttercream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dacquoise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It takes its name from the feminine form of the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word <i>dacquois</i>, meaning 'of <a href="/wiki/Dax,_Landes" title="Dax, Landes">Dax</a>', a town in southwestern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. It is usually served chilled and accompanied by fruit.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dacquoise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg/220px-Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg/330px-Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg/440px-Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dacquoise_disks_for_mousse_cake_bases.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Piping dacquoise disks for mousse cake bases</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A particular form of the dacquoise is the <i>marjolaine</i>, invented by French chef <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Point" title="Fernand Point">Fernand Point</a>, which is long and rectangular and combines <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnut</a> meringue layers with <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a> buttercream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term dacquoise can also refer to the nut meringue layer itself.</p>
