<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tataki</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Tataki</b> (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> たたき: "pounded" or "hit into pieces"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-guide-1">[1]</a></sup> also called <i>tosa-mi</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-guide-1">[1]</a></sup> refers to two methods of preparing fish or <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese cuisine</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooked_food">Cooked food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tataki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cooked food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the first "tataki" method, the meat or fish is seared very briefly over a hot flame or in a pan, and can be briefly marinated in <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>, sliced thinly and seasoned with <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a> (which is ground or pounded into a paste, hence the name).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-guide-1">[1]</a></sup> Food so prepared can also be served with soy sauce and garnishes like a <a href="/wiki/Sashimi" title="Sashimi">sashimi</a>.</p>
<p>The method originated in <a href="/wiki/Tosa_Province" title="Tosa Province">Tosa Province</a>, now part of <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dchi_Prefecture" title="Kōchi Prefecture">Kōchi Prefecture</a>. Lore has it that it was developed by <a href="/wiki/Sakamoto_Ry%C5%8Dma" title="Sakamoto Ryōma">Sakamoto Ryōma</a>, a 19th-century rebel <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>, who picked up the European technique of <a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilling</a> meat from the foreigners resident in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagasaki,_Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki, Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-guide-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uncooked_food">Uncooked food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tataki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uncooked food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the second method, it is the food that is "hit into pieces". Fish such as tuna or horse mackerel are chopped and mixed with garnishes such as garlic, ginger, green onions or <a href="/wiki/Shiso" title="Shiso">shiso</a> leaves. Soy sauce may be poured over the chopped mixture before consumption.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
