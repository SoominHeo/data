<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Multiple birth</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TripletBoys.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="312" data-file-width="562" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/TripletBoys.JPG/300px-TripletBoys.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/TripletBoys.JPG/450px-TripletBoys.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/93/TripletBoys.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TripletBoys.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Identical triplet brothers at graduation. Identical triplets are extremely rare.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>multiple birth</b> is the culmination of one multiple <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, wherein the mother delivers two or more offspring. A term most applicable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placental" title="Placental">placental</a> species, multiple births occur in most kinds of mammals, with varying frequencies. Such births are often named according to the number of offspring, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Twin" title="Twin">twins</a></i> and <i>triplets</i>. In non-humans, the whole group may also be referred to as a <i><a href="/wiki/Litter_(animal)" title="Litter (animal)">litter</a></i>, and multiple births may be more common than single births. Multiple births in humans are the exception, and can be exceptionally rare in the largest mammals.</p>
<p>Each single fertilized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">egg</a> (<a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>) may produce a single embryo, or it may split into two or more embryos, each carrying the same genetic material. Fetuses resulting from different zygotes are called <i>fraternal</i> and share only 50% of their genetic material, as ordinary full siblings from separate births do. Fetuses resulting from the same zygote share 100% of their genetic material, and are hence called <i>identical</i> and are always of the same sex (except in cases of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment" title="Sex reassignment">sex reassignment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turner_syndrome" title="Turner syndrome">Turner syndrome</a>).</p>
<p>A multiple pregnancy may be the result of the fertilization of a single egg that then splits to create identical fetuses, or it may be the result of the fertilization of multiple eggs that create fraternal fetuses, or it may be a combination of these two. A multiple pregnancy from a single zygote is called <i>monozygotic</i>, from two zygotes is called <i>dizygotic</i>, or from two or more zygotes is called <i>polyzygotic</i>.</p>
<p>Similarly, the siblings themselves from a multiple birth may be referred to as monozygotic if they are identical, or as polyzygotic if they are fraternal.</p>

<p>Terms used for multiple births or the genetic relationships of their offspring:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monozygotic – multiple (typically two) fetuses produced by the splitting of a single <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a></li>
<li>Dizygotic – multiple (typically two) fetuses produced by two zygotes</li>
<li>Polyzygotic – multiple fetuses produced by two or more zygotes</li>
</ul>
<p>Terms used for the number of offspring in a multiple birth:</p>
<ul>
<li>Two offspring - twins</li>
<li>Three offspring - triplets</li>
<li>Four offspring - quadruplets</li>
<li>Five offspring - quintuplets</li>
<li>Six offspring - sextuplets</li>
<li>Seven offspring - septuplets</li>
<li>Eight offspring - octuplets</li>
<li>Nine offspring - nonuplets</li>
<li>Ten offspring - decuplets</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_multiple_births">Human multiple births</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Human multiple births">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In humans, the average length of pregnancy (two weeks fewer than <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a>) is 38 weeks with a single fetus. This average decreases for each additional fetus: to thirty-six weeks for twin births, thirty-two weeks for triplets, and thirty weeks for quadruplets. With the decreasing gestation time, the risks from immaturity at birth and subsequent viability increase with the size of the sibling group. Only as of the twentieth century have more than four all survived infancy.</p>
<p>Recent history has also seen increasing numbers of multiple births. In the United States, it has been estimated that by 2011, 36% of twin births and 78% of triplet and higher-order births resulted from conception by <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">assisted reproductive technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twins">Twins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Twins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Twin" title="Twin">Twin</a></div>
<p>Twins are by far the most common form of multiple births in humans. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention report more than 132,000 sets of twins out of 3.9 million births of all kinds each year, about 3.4%, or 1 in 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaltimoreSun2015_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BaltimoreSun2015-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Triplets">Triplets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Triplets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TripletsGirls.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="800" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/TripletsGirls.jpg/230px-TripletsGirls.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/TripletsGirls.jpg/345px-TripletsGirls.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/TripletsGirls.jpg/460px-TripletsGirls.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TripletsGirls.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Identical triplets like these three sisters occur when a single fertilized egg splits in two and then one of the resulting two eggs splits again.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Identical triplets come from a monozygotic pregnancy, three fetuses from one egg. The most common set, strictly fraternal triplets, comes from a polyzygotic pregnancy of three eggs. Between these types, triplets that include an identical (monozygotic) pair of siblings plus a fraternal sibling are the result of a dizygotic pregnancy, where one zygote divides into two fetuses and the other doesn't.</p>
<p>Triplets are far less common than twins, according to the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, accounting for only about 4300 sets in 3.9 million births, just a little more than 0.1%, or 1 in 1000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaltimoreSun2015_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BaltimoreSun2015-2">[2]</a></sup> According to the American Society of Reproductive Medicine, only about 10% of these are identical triplets: about 1 in ten thousand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaltimoreSun2015_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-BaltimoreSun2015-2">[2]</a></sup> Nevertheless, only 4 sets of identical triplets were reported in the U.S. during 2015, about one in a million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaltimoreSun2015_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-BaltimoreSun2015-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Victor Khouzami, Chairman of Obstetrics at Greater Baltimore Medical Center, "No one really knows the incidence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaltimoreSun2015_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-BaltimoreSun2015-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Identical triplets or quadruplets are very rare and result when the original fertilized egg splits and then one of the resultant cells splits again (for triplets) or, even more rarely, a further split occurs (for quadruplets). The odds of having identical triplets is unclear. News articles and other non-scientific organizations give odds from one in 60,000 to one in 200 million pregnancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaltimoreSun2015_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-BaltimoreSun2015-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Normal_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Normal-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quadruplets">Quadruplets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quadruplets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Quadruplets are much rarer than twins or triplets. As of 2007, there were approximately 3500 sets recorded worldwide. Quadruplet births are becoming increasingly common due to fertility treatments. There are around 70 sets of all-identical quadruplets worldwide. Many sets of quadruplets contain a mixture of identical and fraternal siblings, such as three identical and one fraternal, two identical and two fraternal, or two pairs of identicals. One famous set of identical quadruplets was the <a href="/wiki/Genain_quadruplets" title="Genain quadruplets">Genain quadruplets</a>, all of whom developed <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>. Quadruplets are sometimes referred to as "quads" in Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quintuplets">Quintuplets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Quintuplets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Quintuplets occur naturally in 1 in 55,000,000 births.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The first quintuplets known to survive infancy were the all-female Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dionne_Quintuplets" title="Dionne Quintuplets">Dionne Quintuplets</a>, born in 1934. Quintuplets are sometimes referred to as "quins" in the UK and "quints" in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Very_high-order_multiple_births">Very high-order multiple births</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Very high-order multiple births">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, the <a href="/wiki/McCaughey_septuplets" title="McCaughey septuplets">McCaughey septuplets</a> were born in Carlisle, Iowa. Multiple births of as many as eight babies have been born alive, the first set on record to the <a href="/wiki/Chukwu_octuplets" title="Chukwu octuplets">Chukwu family</a> in Texas in 1998; one died and seven survived. In 2009, a second set, the <a href="/wiki/Suleman_octuplets" title="Suleman octuplets">Suleman octuplets</a>, were born in <a href="/wiki/Bellflower,_California" title="Bellflower, California">Bellflower, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The most recent report that all were still alive was shortly before their fifth birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>There have been a few sets of nonuplets (nine) in which a few babies were born alive, though none lived longer than a few days. There have been cases of human pregnancies that started out with ten, eleven, twelve or fifteen fetuses, but no instances of live births. The pregnancies of the 10, 11 and 15 fetuses have all resulted from <a href="/wiki/Fertility_medication" title="Fertility medication">fertility medications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">assisted reproductive technology (ART)</a>. However, there has been one documented case when 12 fetuses were conceived naturally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_and_frequency">Causes and frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Causes and frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The frequency of <i>N</i> multiple births from natural pregnancies has been given as approximately 1:89<sup><i>N</i>-1</sup> (<i><a href="/wiki/Hellin%27s_law" title="Hellin's law">Hellin's law</a></i>) and as about 1:80<sup><i>N</i>-1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGraw_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-15">[15]</a></sup> This gives:</p>
<ul>
<li>1:89 (= 1.1%) or 1:80 (= 1.25%) for twins</li>
<li>1:89<sup>2</sup> (= 1:7921, about 0.013%) or 1:80<sup>2</sup> (= 1:6400) for triplets</li>
<li>1:89<sup>3</sup> (= approx. 0.000142%, less than 1:700,000) or 1:80<sup>3</sup> for quadruplets</li>
</ul>
<p>North American dizygotic twinning occurs about once in 83 conceptions, and triplets about once in 8000 conceptions. US figures for 2010 were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Twins, 132,562, 3.31%</li>
<li>Triplets, 5,503, 0.14%</li>
<li>Quadruplets, 313, 0.0078%</li>
<li>Quintuplets and more, 37, 0.00092%</li>
</ul>
<p>Human multiple births can occur either naturally (the woman ovulates multiple eggs or the fertilized egg splits into two) or as the result of <a href="/wiki/Infertility" title="Infertility">infertility</a> treatments such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilization" title="In vitro fertilization">IVF</a> (several embryos are often transferred to compensate for lower quality) or fertility drugs (which can cause multiple eggs to mature in one ovulatory cycle).</p>
<p>For reasons that are not yet known, the older a woman is, the more likely she is to have a multiple birth naturally. It is theorized that this is due to the higher level of <a href="/wiki/Follicle-stimulating_hormone" title="Follicle-stimulating hormone">follicle-stimulating hormone</a> that older women sometimes have as their ovaries respond more slowly to FSH stimulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16497698_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16497698-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of multiple births has increased over the last decade. For example, in Canada between 1979 and 1999, the number of multiple birth babies increased 35%. Before the advent of ovulation-stimulating drugs, triplets were quite rare (approximately 1 in 8000 births) and higher-order births much rarer still.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Much of the increase can probably be attributed to the impact of fertility treatments, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-vitro_fertilization" title="In-vitro fertilization">in-vitro fertilization</a>. Younger patients who undergo treatment with <a href="/wiki/Fertility_medication" title="Fertility medication">fertility medication</a> containing artificial <a href="/wiki/Follicle-stimulating_hormone" title="Follicle-stimulating hormone">FSH</a>, followed by intrauterine <a href="/wiki/Insemination" title="Insemination">insemination</a>, are particularly at risk for multiple births of higher order.</p>

<p>Certain factors appear to increase the likelihood that a woman will naturally conceive multiples. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>mother's age: women over 35 are more likely to have multiples than younger women<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>mother's use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_drug" title="Fertility drug">fertility drugs</a>: approximately 35% of pregnancies arising through the use of fertility treatments such as IVF involve more than one child</li>
</ul>
<p>The increasing use of fertility drugs and consequent increased rate of multiple births has made the phenomenon of multiples more frequent and hence more visible. In 2004 the birth of sextuplets, six children, to Pennsylvania couple Kate and Jon Gosselin helped them to launch their television series, originally <i>Jon &amp; Kate Plus 8</i> and (following their divorce) <i><a href="/wiki/Kate_Plus_8" title="Kate Plus 8">Kate Plus 8</a></i>, which became the highest-rated show on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TLC_(TV_channel)" title="TLC (TV channel)">TLC</a></i> network.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks">Risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Premature_birth_and_low_birth_weight">Premature birth and low birth weight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Premature birth and low birth weight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Babies born from multiple-birth pregnancies are much more likely to result in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premature_birth" title="Premature birth">premature birth</a> than those from single pregnancies. 51% of twins and 91% of triplets are born preterm, compared to 9.4% in singletons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlexanderKoganMartinPapiernik_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlexanderKoganMartinPapiernik-20">[20]</a></sup> 14% of twins and 41% of triplets are even born <i>very preterm</i>, compared to 1.7% in singletons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlexanderKoganMartinPapiernik_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-AlexanderKoganMartinPapiernik-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>At present, there is no way to stop twins being born early.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">[21]</a></sup> In women with single pregnancies drugs called betamimetics can relax the muscles of the uterus and delay birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-22">[22]</a></sup> Giving betamimetics can give more time to give steroids, for the baby’s lung development, or to transfer the mother to a hospital with a special care unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, there is not enough evidence to say whether or not women with twin pregnancies should be given oral betamimetics to reduce the risk of preterm birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">[21]</a></sup> In some studies betamimetics have reduced the rate of preterm labour in twin pregnancies however the studies are too small to draw any solid conclusions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">[21]</a></sup> Likewise, putting a stitch in the neck of the womb (a <a href="/wiki/Cervical_cerclage" title="Cervical cerclage">cervical suture</a>) to prevent premature birth has not been shown to work in women carrying more than one baby due to the small sample sizes in the studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">preterm births</a> also result in multiples tending to have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_birth_weight" title="Lower birth weight">lower birth weight</a> compared to singletons.</p>
<p>Some evidence<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> indicates that only 1.10% of singletons are born with a very low birth weight and 10.12% twins and 31.88% triplets were found to be born with very low birth weight. This study<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> was conducted by looking at the statistics from the U.S. Natality Files (5).</p>
<p>Among the exceptions are the Kupresak triplets of <a href="/wiki/Mississauga" title="Mississauga">Mississauga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>; their combined weight at birth in 2008, of 17 lbs, 2.7 ounces, set a world record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cerebral_palsy">Cerebral palsy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cerebral palsy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">Cerebral palsy</a> is more common among multiple births than single births, being 2.3 per 1,000 survivors in singletons, 13 in twins, and 45 in triplets in <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8976682_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8976682-25">[25]</a></sup> This is likely a side effect of premature birth and low birth weight.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incomplete_separation">Incomplete separation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Incomplete separation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Twin#Degree_of_separation" title="Twin">Twin § Degree of separation</a></div>
<p>Multiples may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochorionic" title="Monochorionic">monochorionic</a>, sharing the same <a href="/wiki/Chorion" title="Chorion">chorion</a>, with resultant risk of <a href="/wiki/Twin-to-twin_transfusion_syndrome" title="Twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome">twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome</a>. Monochorionic multiples may even be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoamniotic" title="Monoamniotic">monoamniotic</a>, sharing the same <a href="/wiki/Amniotic_sac" title="Amniotic sac">amniotic sac</a>, resulting in risk of <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cord_compression" title="Umbilical cord compression">umbilical cord compression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuchal_cord" title="Nuchal cord">nuchal cord</a>. In very rare cases, there may be <a href="/wiki/Conjoined_twins" title="Conjoined twins">conjoined twins</a>, possibly impairing function of internal organs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality_rate_.28stillbirth.29">Mortality rate (stillbirth)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mortality rate (stillbirth)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Multiples are also known to have a higher mortality rate. It is more common for multiple births to be stillborn, while for singletons the risk is not as high. A literary review on multiple pregnancies shows a study done on one set each of septuplets and octuplets, two sets of sextuplets, 8 sets of quintuplets, 17 sets of quadruplets, and 228 sets of triplets. By doing this study, Hammond found that the mean gestational age (how many weeks when birthed) at birth was 33.4 weeks for triplets and 31 weeks for quadruplets. This shows that stillbirth happens usually 3–5 weeks before the woman reaches full term and also that for sextuplets or higher it almost always ends in death of the fetuses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9620827_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9620827-26">[26]</a></sup> Though multiples are at a greater risk of being stillborn, there is inconclusive evidence whether the actual mortality rate is higher in multiples than in singletons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention_in_IVF">Prevention in IVF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Prevention in IVF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today many multiple pregnancies are the result of <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilisation" title="In vitro fertilisation">in vitro fertilisation</a> (IVF). In a study in 1997 of 2,173 <a href="/wiki/Embryo_transfer" title="Embryo transfer">embryo transfers</a> performed as part of <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilisation" title="In vitro fertilisation">in vitro fertilisation</a> (IVF), 34% were successfully delivered pregnancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elsner1997_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-elsner1997-27">[27]</a></sup> The overall multiple pregnancy rate was 31.3% (24.7% twins, 5.8% triplets, and .08% quadruplets).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elsner1997_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-elsner1997-27">[27]</a></sup> Because IVFs are producing more multiples, a number of efforts are being made to reduce the risk of multiple births- specifically triplets or more. Medical practitioners are doing this by limiting the number of embryos per embryo transfer to one or two. That way, the risks for the mother and fetuses are decreased.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_of_multiple_pregnancy">Management of multiple pregnancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Management of multiple pregnancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selective_reduction">Selective reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Selective reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Selective_reduction" title="Selective reduction">Selective reduction</a></div>
<p>Selective reduction is the termination of one or more, but not all, of the fetuses in a multiple pregnancy. This is often done in pregnancies with multiple gestations to increase the likelihood that one child may live a healthy life. Armour<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15923959_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15923959-29">[29]</a></sup> reported a loss rate of 5.4% in a review of 1,000 cases of selective reduction. Fifteen percent of the losses occurred within 4 weeks of the procedures and more than 50% occurred after 8 weeks. This suggests that the reduction was successful at reducing the embryos from multiple gestations to single.</p>
<p>Though selective reduction seems to be effective, mothers of multiples who undergo this procedure are at a higher risk of miscarrying compared to that of an unreduced multiple pregnancy. A study done by looking at 158 pregnant women who underwent selective reduction from higher order multiples to twins showed that the mother had a 10.6% chance of miscarriage. Mothers of twin pregnancies without reduction only had a 9.5% chance of miscarriage. A study by Antsaklis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> showed a small increase in mortality for reduced twin pregnancies versus unreduced twin pregnancies.</p>
<p>However, there is a lack of good quality research on the risks and benefits of selective reduction procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-31">[31]</a></sup> Many parents often find the suggestion of selective termination upsetting particularly if the pregnancy is a result of IVF or they have had difficulty getting pregnant in the past. Background, religious or personal beliefs can all play a part in effecting whether parents want to go through with the procedure and could be the reason why research in this area is scarce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Competition among fertility clinics does not appear to increase rates of multiple births from fertility treatment by promoting more aggressive embryo transfer decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Care_in_pregnancy">Care in pregnancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Care in pregnancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Women with a multiple pregnancy are usually seen more regularly by midwives or doctors than those with singleton pregnancies because of the higher risks of complications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, there is currently no evidence to suggest that specialised antenatal services produce better outcomes for mother or babies than ‘normal’ antenatal care. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As preterm birth is such a risk for women with multiple pregnancies, it has been suggested that these women should be encouraged to follow a high-calorie diet to increase the birth weights of the babies. Evidence around this subject is not yet good enough to advise women to do this because the long term effects of the high-calorie diets on the mother are not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cesarean_section_or_vaginal_delivery">Cesarean section or vaginal delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cesarean section or vaginal delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study in 2013 involving 106 participating centers in 25 countries came to the conclusion that, in a twin pregnancy of a <a href="/wiki/Gestational_age" title="Gestational age">gestational age</a> between 32 weeks 0 days and 38 weeks 6 days, and the first twin is in <a href="/wiki/Cephalic_presentation" title="Cephalic presentation">cephalic presentation</a>, planned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cesarean_section" title="Cesarean section">Cesarean section</a> does not significantly decrease or increase the risk of <a href="/wiki/Perinatal_mortality" title="Perinatal mortality">fetal or neonatal death</a> or serious neonatal <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a>, as compared with planned vaginal delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In this study, 44% of the women planned for vaginal delivery still ended up having Cesarean section for unplanned reasons such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pregnancy_complication" title="Pregnancy complication">pregnancy complications</a>. In comparison, it has been estimated that 75% of twin pregnancies in the United States were delivered by Cesarean section in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Also in comparison, the rate of Cesarean section for all pregnancies in the general population varies between 40% and 14%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fetal position (the way the babies are lying in the womb) usually determines if they are delivered by caesarean section or vaginally. A review of good quality research on this subject found that if the twin that will be born first (i.e. is lowest in the womb) is head down there is no good evidence that caesarean section will be safer than a vaginal birth for the mother or babies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Monoamniotic twins (twins that form after the splitting of a fertilised egg and share the same amniotic fluid sac) are at more risk of complications than twins that have their own sacs. There is also insufficient evidence around whether to deliver the babies early by caesarean section or to wait for labour to start naturally while running checks on the babies’ wellbeing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The birth of this type of twins should therefore be decided with the mother and her family and should take into account the need for good neonatal care services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cesarean delivery is needed when first twin is in non cephalic presentation or when it is a monoamniotic twin pregnancy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neonatal_intensive_care">Neonatal intensive care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Neonatal intensive care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Multiple-birth infants are usually admitted to neonatal intensive care immediately after being born. The records for all the triplet pregnancies managed and delivered from 1992-1996 were looked over to see what the neonatal statistics were. Kaufman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9491857_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9491857-42">[42]</a></sup> found from reviewing these files that during a five-year period, 55 triplet pregnancies, which is 165 babies, were delivered. Of the 165 babies 149 were admitted to neonatal intensive care after the delivery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insurance_coverage">Insurance coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Insurance coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study by the U.S. Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality found that, in 2011, pregnant women covered by private insurance in the United States were older and more likely to have multiple gestation than women covered by Medicaid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_aspects">Cultural aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_birth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Cultural aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Certain cultures consider multiple births a portent of either good or evil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Mayan</a> culture saw twins as a blessing, and was fascinated by the idea of two bodies looking alike. The Mayans used to believe that twins were one soul that had fragmented.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Ancient Rome, the legend of the twin brothers who founded the city (<a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">Romulus and Remus</a>) made the birth of twin boys a blessing, while twin girls were seen as an unlucky burden, since both would have to be provided with an expensive <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a> at about the same time.</p>
<p>In Greek mythology, fraternal twins <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscuri" title="Dioscuri">Castor and Polydeuces</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles and Iphicles</a>, are sons of two different fathers. One of the twins (<a href="/wiki/Castor_and_Pollux" title="Castor and Pollux">Polydeuces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>) is the illegitimate son of the god <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>; his brother is the son of their mother's mortal husband. A similar pair of twin sisters are <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">Helen</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War#Elopement_of_Paris_and_Helen" title="Trojan War">Troy</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Clytemnestra" title="Clytemnestra">Clytemnestra</a> (who are also sisters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscuri" title="Dioscuri">Castor and Polydeuces</a>). The theme occurs in other mythologies as well, and is called <i><a href="/wiki/Superfecundation#Use_in_mythology" title="Superfecundation">superfecundation</a></i>.</p>
<p>In certain medieval European <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_romance" title="Chivalric romance">chivalric romances</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_France" title="Marie de France">Marie de France</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Fresne_(lai)" title="Le Fresne (lai)">Le Fresne</a></i>, a woman cites a multiple birth (often to a lower-class woman) as proof of adultery on her part; while this may reflect a widespread belief, it is invariably treated as malicious slander, to be justly punished by the accuser having a multiple birth of her own, and the events of the romance are triggered by her attempt to hide one or more of the children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> A similar effect occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Knight_of_the_Swan" title="Knight of the Swan">Knight of the Swan</a> romance, in the Beatrix variants of the Swan-Children; her taunt is punished by giving birth to seven children at once, and her wicked mother-in-law returns her taunt before <a href="/wiki/Child_abandonment#In_fiction" title="Child abandonment">exposing</a> the children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
