<p><b>Daphnis</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">DAF</span></span></b>-nis</i></a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Δάφνις</span>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">inner satellite</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>. It is also known as <b><span class="nowrap">Saturn XXXV</span></b>; its provisional designation was <b><span class="nowrap">S/2005 S 1</span></b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8524_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAUC8524-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Daphnis is about 8 kilometres in diameter, and orbits the planet in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keeler_Gap" title="Keeler Gap">Keeler Gap</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rings_of_saturn#A_Ring" title="Rings of saturn">A ring</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daphnis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The moon was named in 2006 after <a href="/wiki/Daphnis" title="Daphnis">Daphnis</a>, a shepherd, pipes player, and pastoral poet in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> he was descendant of the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titans</a>, after whom the largest moons of Saturn are named. Both Daphnis and <a href="/wiki/Pan_(moon)" title="Pan (moon)">Pan</a>, the only other known <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_moon" title="Shepherd moon">shepherd moon</a> to orbit within Saturn's main rings, are named for mythological figures associated with <a href="/wiki/Shepherd" title="Shepherd">shepherds</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daphnis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before it was photographed, the existence of a moon in Daphnis's position had already been inferred from gravitational ripples observed on the outer edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keeler_gap" title="Keeler gap">Keeler gap</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA06237.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="328" data-file-width="744" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/PIA06237.jpg/220px-PIA06237.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/PIA06237.jpg/330px-PIA06237.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/PIA06237.jpg/440px-PIA06237.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA06237.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Daphnis was resolved into a disc for the first time in this 2005 <i>Cassini</i> probe image. The gravitational waves on the edges of the rings, which had earlier hinted at the moon's presence, are clearly visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Daphnis was discovered by the <a class="external text" href="http://ciclops.org/team/iss_team.php" rel="nofollow">Cassini Imaging Science Team</a> on May 6, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8524_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAUC8524-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_images" title="Discovery images">discovery images</a> were taken by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens" title="Cassini-Huygens">Cassini</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_space_mission" title="Unmanned space mission">probe</a> over 16 min on May 1, 2005, from a time-lapse sequence of 0.180 second narrow-angle-camera exposures of the outer edge of the A ring. The moon was subsequently found in 32 low-phase images taken of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rings_of_saturn#F_Ring" title="Rings of saturn">F ring</a> on April 13, 2005 (spanning 18 min) and again in two high-resolution (3.54 km/<a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixel</a>) low-phase images taken on May 2, 2005, when its 7 km disk was resolved.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daphnis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">eccentricity</a> of Daphnis's orbit are very small, but distinguishable from zero. Daphnis's eccentricity causes its distance from Saturn to vary by ~9 km, and its inclination causes it to move up and down by ~17 km. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keeler_Gap" title="Keeler Gap">Keeler Gap</a>, within which Daphnis orbits, is about 42 km wide.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_Saturn.27s_rings">Effect on Saturn's rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daphnis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effect on Saturn's rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The waves that Daphnis induces nearby in the A ring have vertical relief (due to its orbital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a>) and cast shadows when Saturn is close to its <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Daphnis orbits within the Keeler gap in Saturn's rings. As it orbits, it creates gravitational ripples on the edges of the gap as ring particles are attracted toward the moon and then fall back down toward the ring. The waves made by the moon in the inner edge of the gap precede it in orbit, while those on the outer edge lag behind it, due to the differences in relative orbital speed. In a photograph taken on January 18, 2017, a tendril of ring particles can be seen to extend toward the moon; according to <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">JPL</a>, "this may have resulted from a moment when Daphnis drew a packet of material out of the ring, and now that packet is spreading itself out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daphnis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 18, 2017, Daphnis was photographed from a sufficiently close distance to reveal its shape. The moon was discovered to be an irregularly-shaped object with a mostly smooth surface, a few craters, and an <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_ridge" title="Equatorial ridge">equatorial ridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
