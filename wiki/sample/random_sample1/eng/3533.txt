<p><b>Dr. Martens</b> is a British <a href="/wiki/Footwear" title="Footwear">footwear</a> and clothing brand, which also makes a range of accessories – shoe care products, clothing, luggage, etc. In addition to Dr. Martens, they are also commonly known as <b>Doctor Martens</b>, <b>Doc Martens</b>, <b>Docs</b> or <b>DMs</b>. The footwear is distinguished by its air-cushioned <a href="/wiki/Sole_(foot)" title="Sole (foot)">sole</a> (dubbed <i>Bouncing Soles</i>), upper shape, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welt_(shoe)" title="Welt (shoe)">welted</a> construction and yellow stitching. The boots have been the choice of footwear among various groups in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_culture" title="British culture">British culture</a>: in the 1960s <a href="/wiki/Skinhead" title="Skinhead">skinheads</a> started to wear them, "dms" being the usual naming, and by the late 1980s, they were popular among <a href="/wiki/Scooter_(motorcycle)" title="Scooter (motorcycle)">scooter</a> riders, <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punks</a>, some <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> musicians, and members of other youth <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subcultures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr_Martens_at_50_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dr_Martens_at_50-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Griggs' 1960 Dr. Martens AirWair boot was named in the list of British design icons which included <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mini" title="Mini">Mini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_E-Type" title="Jaguar E-Type">Jaguar E-Type</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB5" title="Aston Martin DB5">Aston Martin DB5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tube_map" title="Tube map">Tube map</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and the <a href="/wiki/AEC_Routemaster" title="AEC Routemaster">AEC Routemaster</a> bus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_design_icons_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_design_icons-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Martens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Martens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Klaus Märtens was a doctor in the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">German army</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. While on leave in 1945, he injured his ankle while <a href="/wiki/Ski" title="Ski">skiing</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Alps" title="Bavarian Alps">Bavarian Alps</a>. He found that his standard-issue army <a href="/wiki/Boot" title="Boot">boots</a> were too uncomfortable on his injured foot. While recuperating, he designed improvements to the boots, with soft <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> and air-padded soles made of tires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> When the war ended and some Germans recovered valuables from their own cities, Märtens took leather from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoemaker" title="Shoemaker">cobbler</a>'s shop. With that leather he made himself a pair of boots with air-cushioned soles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Märtens did not have much success selling his shoes until he met up with an old <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> friend, Dr. Herbert Funck, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourger" title="Luxembourger">Luxembourger</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> in 1947. Funck was intrigued by the new shoe design, and the two went into business that year in <a href="/wiki/Seeshaupt" title="Seeshaupt">Seeshaupt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, using discarded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> from <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> airfields.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The comfortable soles were a big hit with housewives, with 80% of sales in the first decade going to women over the age of 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-still_an_icon_to_boot_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-still_an_icon_to_boot-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Martens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sales had grown so much by 1952 that they opened a factory in Munich. In 1959, the company had grown large enough that Märtens and Funck looked at marketing the footwear internationally. Almost immediately, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> shoe manufacturer R. Griggs Group Ltd. bought <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> rights to manufacture the shoes in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Griggs <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicised</a> the name, slightly re-shaped the heel to make them fit better, added the trademark yellow stitching, and trademarked the soles as AirWair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_design_icons_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-British_design_icons-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

Cherry Red and Black 14-hole Dr. Martens boots</div>

<p>The first Dr. Martens boots in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> came out on 1 April 1960 (known as style 1460 and still in production today), with an eight-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyelet" title="Eyelet">eyelet</a> oxblood coloured smooth leather design. Dr. Martens boots were made in their Cobbs Lane factory in <a href="/wiki/Wollaston,_Northamptonshire" title="Wollaston, Northamptonshire">Wollaston, Northamptonshire</a> (which is still operating today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-still_an_icon_to_boot_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-still_an_icon_to_boot-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, a number of shoe manufacturers in the <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> area produced DMs under licence, as long as they passed quality standards. The boots were popular among workers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postman" title="Postman">postmen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Police_officer" title="Police officer">police officers</a> and factory workers. By the later 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Skinhead" title="Skinhead">skinheads</a> started to wear them, "dms" being the usual naming, and by the late 1980s, they were popular among <a href="/wiki/Scooter_(motorcycle)" title="Scooter (motorcycle)">scooter</a> riders, <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punks</a>, some <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> musicians, and members of other youth <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subcultures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr_Martens_at_50_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dr_Martens_at_50-1">[1]</a></sup> The shoes' popularity among skinheads led to the brand gaining an association with violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr_Martens_at_50_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dr_Martens_at_50-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Sayle" title="Alexei Sayle">Alexei Sayle</a> sang the song "Dr. Martens' Boots" in a 1982 episode of the British TV comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Young_Ones_(TV_series)" title="The Young Ones (TV series)">The Young Ones</a></i>.</p>
<p>The boots and shoes became popular in the 1990s as grunge fashion arose. In late November 1994, a six-storey Dr. Martens <a href="/wiki/Department_store" title="Department store">department store</a> was opened in <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a> in London which sold food, belts, and watches, as well as shoes. At this time the R. Griggs company employed 2,700 people, expected to earn annual revenue of £170 million, and could produce up to 10 million pairs of shoes per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stomp_Into_London_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stomp_Into_London-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flagship_For_Doc_Martens_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flagship_For_Doc_Martens-8">[8]</a></sup> Dr. Martens sponsored <a href="/wiki/Rushden_%26_Diamonds_F.C." title="Rushden &amp; Diamonds F.C.">Rushden &amp; Diamonds F.C.</a> from 1998 to 2005. Diamonds approached owner and local businessman, Max Griggs to request sponsorship from his company. A new main stand was built at <a href="/wiki/Nene_Park" title="Nene Park">Nene Park</a> in 2001, named the Airwair Stand. Dr. Martens were also the principal sponsors of <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> club, <a href="/wiki/West_Ham_United_F.C." title="West Ham United F.C.">West Ham United F.C.</a>, renaming the upgraded west stand 'The Dr Martens Stand' until 2009.</p>
<p>In the 2000s, Dr. Martens were sold exclusively under the AirWair name, and came in dozens of different styles, including conventional black shoes, sandals and steel-toed boots. AirWair International's revenue fell from US $412 million in 1999 to $127 million in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antifashion_Classic_Returns_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Antifashion_Classic_Returns-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2003, the Dr. Martens company came close to bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50_Years_of_Dr_Martens_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-50_Years_of_Dr_Martens-10">[10]</a></sup> On 1 April that year, under pressure from declining sales, the company ceased making shoes in the United Kingdom,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and moved all production to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. Five factories and two shops were closed in the UK as a result of this decision, and more than 1,000 of the firm's employees lost their jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_its_uppers_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-on_its_uppers-12">[12]</a></sup> Following the closures, the R. Griggs company employed only 20 people in the UK, all of whom were located in the firm's head office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr_Martens_closure_looms_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dr_Martens_closure_looms-13">[13]</a></sup> 5 million pairs of Dr. Martens were sold during 2003, which was half the level of annual sales during the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Footwear_Darwinism_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Footwear_Darwinism-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2004 a new range of Dr. Martens was launched in an attempt to appeal to a wider market, and especially young people. The shoes and boots were intended to be more comfortable, and easier to break in, and included some new design elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Footwear_Darwinism_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Footwear_Darwinism-14">[14]</a></sup> Dr. Martens also began producing footwear again at the Cobbs Lane Factory in <a href="/wiki/Wollaston,_Northamptonshire" title="Wollaston, Northamptonshire">Wollaston</a>, England in 2004. These products are part of the "Vintage" line, which the company advertises as being made to the original specifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vintage_Dr_Martens_expansion_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-vintage_Dr_Martens_expansion-15">[15]</a></sup> Sales of these shoes are low in comparison to those made in Asia, however; in 2010, the factory was producing about 50 pairs per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-still_an_icon_to_boot_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-still_an_icon_to_boot-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2005, the R. Griggs company was given an award by the "Institute for Turnaround" for implementing a successful restructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50_Years_of_Dr_Martens_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-50_Years_of_Dr_Martens-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Worldwide sales of Dr. Martens shoes grew strongly in the early 2010s, and in 2012 it was assessed as being the eighth fastest-growing British company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Over 100 million pairs of Dr. Martens shoes have been sold from 1960 to 2010, and in 2010, the company offered 250 different models of footwear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-still_an_icon_to_boot_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-still_an_icon_to_boot-5">[5]</a></sup> The R. Griggs company opened 14 new Dr. Martens retail stores in the United Kingdom, United States and Hong Kong between 2009 and 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and also launched a line of clothing during 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition">Acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Martens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2013, the private equity company <a href="/wiki/Permira" title="Permira">Permira</a> acquired R. Griggs Group Limited (the owner of the Dr. Martens brand) for a consideration of £300m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
