<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Human branding</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Human branding</b> or <b>stigmatizing</b> is the process which a mark, usually a symbol or ornamental pattern, is burned into the skin of a living person, with the intention that the resulting scar makes it permanent. This is performed using a hot or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeze-branding" title="Freeze-branding">very cold</a> <a href="/wiki/Branding_iron" title="Branding iron">branding iron</a>. It therefore uses the physical techniques of <a href="/wiki/Livestock_branding" title="Livestock branding">livestock branding</a> on a human, either with consent as a form of <a href="/wiki/Body_modification" title="Body modification">body modification</a>; or under coercion, as a <a href="/wiki/Punishment" title="Punishment">punishment</a> or to identify an <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">enslaved</a> or otherwise oppressed person. It may also be practiced as a "rite of passage", e.g. within a tribe, or to signify membership of or acceptance into an organization.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English verb to <i>burn</i>, attested since the 12th century, is a combination of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>brenna</i> "to burn, light", and two originally distinct <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> verbs: <i>b√¶rnan</i> "to kindle" (transitive) and <i>beornan</i> "to be on fire" (intransitive), both from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> root <i>bren(wanan)</i>, perhaps from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> root <i>bhre-n-u</i>, from base root <i>bhereu-</i> "to boil forth, well up." In <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, <i>(ver)branden</i> mean "to burn", <i>brandmerk</i> a branded mark; similarly, in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <i>Brandzeichen</i> means "a brand" and <i>brandmarken</i>, "to brand".</p>
<p>Sometimes the word <i>cauterize</i> is used. This is known in English since 1541, and is derived via Medieval French <i>cauteriser</i> from <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>cauterizare</i> "to burn or brand with a hot iron", itself from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> Œ∫Œ±œÖœÑŒ∑œÅŒπŒ¨Œ∂ŒµŒπŒΩ, <i>kauteriazein</i>, from Œ∫Œ±œÖœÑŒÆœÅ <i>kauter</i> "burning or branding iron", from Œ∫Œ±ŒØŒµŒπŒΩ <i>kaiein</i> "to burn". However <i><a href="/wiki/Cauterization" title="Cauterization">cauterization</a></i> is now generally understood to mean a medical process ‚Äì specifically to stop bleeding.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_use">Historical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marking_the_rightless">Marking the rightless</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marking the rightless">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The origin may be the ancient treatment of a <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slave</a> (often without legal rights) as <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">European</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and other colonial slavers branded millions of slaves during the period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">trans-Atlantic enslavement</a>. Sometimes there were several brandings, e.g. for the Portuguese crown and the (consecutive) private owner(s), an extra cross after baptism as well as by <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> slave catchers.</li>
</ul>
<p>To a slave owner it would be logical to mark such property just like cattle, more so since humans are more able to escape.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Romans</a> marked runaway slaves with the letters <b>FGV</b> (for <i>fugitivus</i>).</li>
<li>An intermediate case between formal slavery and criminal law is when a convict is branded and legally reduced, with or without time limit, to a slave-like status, such as on the <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galleys</a> (in France branded GAL or TF <i>travaux forc√©s</i> 'forced labour' until 1832), in a <a href="/wiki/Penal_colony" title="Penal colony">penal colony</a>, or auctioned to a private owner.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_punishment">As punishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As punishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>, branding with a <a href="/wiki/Branding_iron" title="Branding iron">hot iron</a> was a mode of punishment consisting of marking the subject as if goods or animals, sometimes concurrently with their reduction of status in life.</p>
<p>Brand marks have also been used as a punishment for convicted criminals, combining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_punishment" title="Physical punishment">physical punishment</a>, as burns are very painful, with <a href="/wiki/Public_humiliation" title="Public humiliation">public humiliation</a> (greatest if marked on a normally visible part of the body) which is here the more important intention, and with the imposition of an indelible <a href="/wiki/Criminal_record" title="Criminal record">criminal record</a>. Robbers, like runaway slaves, were marked by the Romans with the letter F (<i>fur</i>); and the toilers in the mines, and convicts condemned to figure in gladiatorial shows, were branded on the forehead for identification. Under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor)" title="Constantine I (emperor)">Constantine I</a> the face was not permitted to be so disfigured, the branding being on the hand, arm or calf.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acts_of_Sharbil" title="Acts of Sharbil">Acts of Sharbil</a> record it applied, amongst other tortures, to a Christian between the eyes and on the cheeks in <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a> at the time of the Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a> on a judge's order for refusal to sacrifice.</p>
<p>In the 16th century, German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptists" title="Anabaptists">Anabaptists</a> were branded with a cross on their foreheads for refusing to recant their faith and join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the North-American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> settlements of the 17th century, men and women sentenced for <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a> were branded with an "<b>A</b>" letter on their chest, and for other crimes, such as "<b>D</b>" for drunkenness and "<b>B</b>" for blasphemy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (October 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The mark in later times was also often chosen as a code for the crime (e.g. in Canadian military prisons D for <a href="/wiki/Desertion" title="Desertion">Desertion</a>, BC for Bad Character; most branded men were shipped off to a <a href="/wiki/Penal_colony" title="Penal colony">penal colony</a>)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Branding was used for a time by the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. Surgeon and <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> contributor <a href="/wiki/William_Chester_Minor" title="William Chester Minor">William Chester Minor</a> was required to brand deserters at around the time of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Wilderness" title="Battle of the Wilderness">Battle of the Wilderness</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a> sanctioned the punishment, and in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, in royal times, various offences carried the additional infamy of being branded with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleur_de_lys" title="Fleur de lys">fleur de lys</a>, also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galley-slave" title="Galley-slave">galley-slaves</a> could be branded <b>GAL</b> or once the galleys were replaced by the <i>bagnes</i> on land, <b>TF</b> (<i>travaux forc√©s</i>, 'forced' labor, i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_labour" title="Hard labour">hard labour</a>) or <b>TFP</b> (<i>travaux forc√©s √† perpetuit√©</i>, forced labour for life) until 1832. In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> however, branding was illegal.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_of_the_Slaves" title="Conspiracy of the Slaves">Conspiracy of the Slaves</a> of 1749 in <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, some slaves were branded with the letter <b>R</b> (for <i>ribelli</i>) on their forehead and condemned to the galleys for life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Branding tended to be abolished like other judicial mutilations (with notable exceptions, such as amputation under <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">sharia</a> law), sooner and more widely than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flogging" title="Flogging">flogging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caning" title="Caning">caning</a> and similar corporal punishments, which normally aim 'only' at pain and at worst cause stripe scars, although the most severe lashings (not uncommon in penal colonies) in terms of dosage and instrument (such as the proverbial <a href="/wiki/Knout" title="Knout">knout</a>) can even turn out to be lethal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branding_in_American_slavery">Branding in American slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Branding in American slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Slave_branding_iron_(replica),_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Slave_branding_iron_%28replica%29%2C_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg/220px-Slave_branding_iron_%28replica%29%2C_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Slave_branding_iron_%28replica%29%2C_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg/330px-Slave_branding_iron_%28replica%29%2C_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Slave_branding_iron_%28replica%29%2C_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg/440px-Slave_branding_iron_%28replica%29%2C_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Slave_branding_iron_(replica),_Museum_of_Liverpool.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A replica of a slave branding iron which would have originally been used in the Atlantic slave trade. On display at The Museum of Liverpool, England.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Louisiana, there was a "black code", or <i>Code Noir</i>, which allowed the cropping of ears, shoulder branding, and the cutting of tendons above the knee as punishments for recaptured slaves. Slave owners used extreme punishments to stop flight, or escape. They would often brand the slaves' palms, shoulders, buttocks, or cheeks with a branding iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Branding was sometimes used to mark recaptured runaway slaves to help the locals easily identify the runaway. Mr. Micajah Ricks, in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>, was looking for his slave and described, "I burnt her with a hot iron, on the left side of her face, I tried to make the letter M."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most slave owners would use whipping as their main method, but at other times they would use branding to punish their slaves. Another testimony explains how a slave owner in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> around 1848 was looking for his runaway slave. He described her having "a brand mark on the breast something like L blotched."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In South Carolina, there were many laws which permitted the punishments slaves would receive. When a slave ran away, if it was the first offense, the slave would receive no more than forty lashes. Then the second offense would be branding. The slave would have been marked with the letter R on their forehead signifying that they were a criminal, and a runaway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_religious_initiation">As religious initiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: As religious initiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ceremonial Branding is an integral part of religious initiation in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaishnava" title="Vaishnava">Vaishnava</a> sects. References to this practice can be traced in texts such as Narad <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Panchratra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Panchratra (page does not exist)">Panchratra</a>, Vaikhnasagama, <a href="/wiki/Skanda_Purana" title="Skanda Purana">Skanda Purana</a> etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This practice is still in vogue among Madhava sect Brahmins of <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branding_in_Britain">Branding in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Branding in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The punishment was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a>, and the ancient law of England authorized the penalty. By the <a href="/wiki/Vagabonds_Act_1547" title="Vagabonds Act 1547">Statute of Vagabonds (1547)</a> under <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">King Edward VI</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vagabond_(person)" title="Vagabond (person)">vagabonds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Gypsies</a> were ordered to be branded with a large <b>V</b> on the breast, and brawlers with <b>F</b> for "fraymaker"; slaves who ran away were branded with <b>S</b> on the cheek or forehead. This law was repealed in England in 1550. From the time of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a>, branding was inflicted for all offences which received <a href="/wiki/Benefit_of_clergy" title="Benefit of clergy">Benefit of clergy</a> (branding of the thumbs was used around 1600 at <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a> to ensure that the accused who had successfully used the Benefit of Clergy defence, by reading a passage from the Bible, could not use it more than once), but it was abolished for such in 1822. In 1698 it was enacted that those convicted of petty theft or <a href="/wiki/Larceny" title="Larceny">larceny</a>, who were entitled to benefit of clergy, should be "burnt in the most visible part of the left cheek, nearest the nose." This special ordinance was repealed in 1707. <a href="/wiki/James_Nayler" title="James Nayler">James Nayler</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> who in the year 1655 was accused of claiming to be the <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a>, convicted of <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy" title="Blasphemy">blasphemy</a> in a highly publicized trial before the Second Protectorate Parliament and had his tongue bored through and his forehead branded <b>B</b> for "blasphemer".</p>
<p>In the Lancaster criminal court a branding iron is still preserved in the dock. It is a long bolt with a wooden handle at one end and an <b>M</b> (malefactor) at the other; close by are two iron loops for firmly securing the hands during the operation. The brander would, after examination, turn to the judge exclaiming "A fair mark, my lord." Criminals were formerly ordered to hold up their hands before sentence to show if they had been previously convicted.</p>
<p>In the 18th century, cold branding or branding with cold irons became the mode of nominally inflicting the punishment on prisoners of higher rank. "When Charles Moritz, a young German, visited England in 1782 he was much surprised at this custom, and in his diary mentioned the case of a clergyman who had fought a <a href="/wiki/Duel" title="Duel">duel</a> and killed his man in <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a>. Found guilty of <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">manslaughter</a> he was burnt in the hand, if that could be called burning which was done with a cold iron" (Markham's <i>Ancient Punishments of <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northants</a></i>, 1886).</p>
<p>Such cases led to branding becoming obsolete, and it was abolished in 1829 except in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserters" title="Deserters">deserters</a> from the army, who were marked with the letter <b>D</b>, not with hot irons but by <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattooing</a> with ink or gunpowder. Notoriously bad soldiers were also branded with <b>BC</b> (bad character). The British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutiny_Act" title="Mutiny Act">Mutiny Act</a> of 1858 provided that the <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">court-martial</a> might, in addition to any other penalty, order deserters to be marked on the left side, 2¬†inches (5¬†cm) below the armpit, with the letter <b>D</b>, such letter to be not less than an inch long. In 1879 this was abolished.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branding_in_Russia">Branding in Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Branding in Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Branding in Russia was used quite extensively in the 18th century and the first half of the 19th century. Over time, red hot iron brands were gradually replaced by tattoo boards; criminals were first branded on the forehead and cheeks, later on the back and arms. Branding was totally abolished in 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Persisting_practices">Persisting practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Persisting practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Generally voluntary, though often under severe social pressure, branding may be used as a painful form of initiation, serving both as endurance and motivation test (<a href="/wiki/Rite_of_passage" title="Rite of passage">rite of passage</a>) and a permanent membership mark, seen as <a href="/wiki/Male_bonding" title="Male bonding">male bonding</a> in violent 'macho' circles. Branding is thus practiced:
<ul>
<li>By some <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">street gangs</a></li>
<li>In organized crime as "stripes" to signify a violent crime that the person committed. Typically on the upper arm or upper torso.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prisons</a></li>
<li>Sometimes as an extreme initiation in the increasingly less common tradition of painful <a href="/wiki/Hazing" title="Hazing">hazing</a> (otherwise mostly <a href="/wiki/Paddle_(spanking)" title="Paddle (spanking)">paddling</a>).</li>
<li>Some members of college fraternities and sororities voluntarily elect to be branded with their fraternity/sorority letters. This is far less common in sororities than fraternities and is especially prevalent in some historically African-American fraternities, such as <a href="/wiki/Omega_Psi_Phi#.22Unofficial.22_practices" title="Omega Psi Phi">Omega Psi Phi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Posey_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Posey-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Branding can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Body_modification" title="Body modification">voluntary body decoration</a> ‚Äì a form of permanent <a href="/wiki/Body_art" title="Body art">body art</a> rather like many <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattoos</a>.</li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadism_and_masochism" title="Sadism and masochism">sadomasochistic</a> scene, it is practiced as a form of bodily mutilation. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_(BDSM)" title="Slave (BDSM)">Slave (BDSM)</a> accepts a branding as a mark of belonging and commitment (possibly to slavery rather than to the specific master).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests">Protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_branding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In symbolic solidarity with <a href="/wiki/Calf_269" title="Calf 269">Calf 269</a>, protesters in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> subjected themselves to branding on World Farm Animals Day (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gandhi" title="Gandhi">Gandhi</a>'s birthday): October 2, 2012. This act was emulated by others in England and the Czech Republic. An English protester who was interviewed justified the extremism as a reaction to the extreme cruelty perpetrated by the dairy industry such as shooting calves at birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vice-20130410_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vice-20130410-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
