<p><b>Forging</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacturing_process" title="Manufacturing process">manufacturing process</a> involving the shaping of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> using localized <a href="/wiki/Compression_(physics)" title="Compression (physics)">compressive</a> forces. The blows are delivered with a <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a> (often a <a href="/wiki/Power_hammer" title="Power hammer">power hammer</a>) or a <a href="/wiki/Die_(manufacturing)" title="Die (manufacturing)">die</a>. Forging is often classified according to the temperature at which it is performed: cold forging (a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_working" title="Cold working">cold working</a>), warm forging, or hot forging (a type of <a href="/wiki/Hot_working" title="Hot working">hot working</a>). For the latter two, the metal is <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heated</a>, usually in a <a href="/wiki/Forge" title="Forge">forge</a>. Forged parts can range in weight from less than a kilogram to hundreds of metric tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo389_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo389-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wnaForge_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wnaForge-2">[2]</a></sup> Forging has been done by <a href="/wiki/Metalsmith" title="Metalsmith">smiths</a> for millennia; the traditional products were <a href="/wiki/Kitchenware" title="Kitchenware">kitchenware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Household_hardware" title="Household hardware">hardware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hand_tool" title="Hand tool">hand tools</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edged_weapon" title="Edged weapon">edged weapons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cymbals" title="Cymbals">cymbals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a>. Since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, forged parts are widely used in <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering)" title="Mechanism (engineering)">mechanisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machines</a> wherever a component requires high <a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">strength</a>; such <b>forgings</b> usually require further processing (such as <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machining</a>) to achieve a finished part. Today, forging is a major worldwide industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forging is one of the oldest known <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo389_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo389-1">[1]</a></sup> Traditionally, forging was performed by a <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">smith</a> using hammer and <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a>, though introducing water power to the production and working of iron in the 12th century allowed the use of large trip hammers or power hammers that exponentially increased the amount and size of iron that could be produced and forged easily. The smithy or <a href="/wiki/Forge" title="Forge">forge</a> has evolved over centuries to become a facility with engineered processes, production equipment, tooling, raw materials and products to meet the demands of modern industry.</p>
<p>In modern times, industrial forging is done either with <a href="/wiki/Machine_press" title="Machine press">presses</a> or with hammers powered by compressed air, electricity, hydraulics or steam. These hammers may have reciprocating weights in the thousands of pounds. Smaller <a href="/wiki/Power_hammer" title="Power hammer">power hammers</a>, 500 lb (230 kg) or less reciprocating weight, and hydraulic presses are common in art smithies as well. Some steam hammers remain in use, but they became obsolete with the availability of the other, more convenient, power sources.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forging can produce a piece that is stronger than an equivalent <a href="/wiki/Casting_(metalworking)" title="Casting (metalworking)">cast</a> or <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machined</a> part. As the metal is shaped during the forging process, its internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(metal)" title="Grain (metal)">grain</a> deforms to follow the general shape of the part. As a result, the grain is continuous throughout the part, giving rise to a piece with improved strength characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo392_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo392-4">[4]</a></sup> Additionally, forgings can target a lower total cost when compared to a casting or fabrication. When you consider all the costs that are involved in a product’s lifecycle from procurement to lead time to rework, then factor in the costs of scrap, downtime and further quality issues, the long-term benefits of forgings can outweigh the short-term cost-savings that castings or fabrications might offer. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some metals may be forged cold, but <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> are almost always <a href="/wiki/Hot_working" title="Hot working">hot forged</a>. Hot forging prevents the <a href="/wiki/Work_hardening" title="Work hardening">work hardening</a> that would result from cold forging, which would increase the difficulty of performing secondary machining operations on the piece. Also, while work hardening may be desirable in some circumstances, other methods of hardening the piece, such as <a href="/wiki/Heat_treating" title="Heat treating">heat treating</a>, are generally more economical and more controllable. Alloys that are amenable to <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_hardening" title="Precipitation hardening">precipitation hardening</a>, such as most <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> alloys and <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, can be hot forged, followed by hardening.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Production forging involves significant capital expenditure for machinery, tooling, facilities and personnel. In the case of hot forging, a high-temperature furnace (sometimes referred to as the forge) is required to heat <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">ingots</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billet_(bar_stock)" title="Billet (bar stock)">billets</a>. Owing to the size of the massive forging hammers and presses and the parts they can produce, as well as the dangers inherent in working with hot metal, a special building is frequently required to house the operation. In the case of drop forging operations, provisions must be made to absorb the shock and vibration generated by the hammer. Most forging operations use metal-forming dies, which must be precisely machined and carefully heat-treated to correctly shape the workpiece, as well as to withstand the tremendous forces involved.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes">Processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ForgedConrodShowingEtchedSection-s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A cross-section of a forged <a href="/wiki/Connecting_rod" title="Connecting rod">connecting rod</a> that has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_etching" title="Industrial etching">etched</a> to show the grain flow</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are many different kinds of forging processes available; however, they can be grouped into three main classes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo389_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo389-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Drawn out: length increases, cross-section decreases</li>
<li>Upset: length decreases, cross-section increases</li>
<li>Squeezed in closed compression dies: produces multidirectional flow</li>
</ul>
<p>Common forging processes include: roll forging, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swage" title="Swage">swaging</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cogging" title="Cogging">cogging</a>, open-die forging, impression-die forging, press forging, automatic hot forging and upsetting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo389_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo389-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Hot_working" title="Hot working">Hot working</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_working" title="Cold working">Cold working</a></div>
<p>All of the following forging processes can be performed at various temperatures; however, they are generally classified by whether the metal temperature is above or below the recrystallization temperature. If the temperature is above the material's recrystallization temperature it is deemed <i>hot forging</i>; if the temperature is below the material's recrystallization temperature but above 30% of the recrystallization temperature (on an absolute scale) it is deemed <i>warm forging</i>; if below 30% of the recrystallization temperature (usually room temperature) then it is deemed <i>cold forging</i>. The main advantage of hot forging is that it can be done more quickly and precisely, and as the metal is deformed <a href="/wiki/Work_hardening" title="Work hardening">work hardening</a> effects are negated by the recrystallization process. Cold forging typically results in work hardening of the piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo373_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo373-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo375_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo375-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drop_forging">Drop forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Drop forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Drop forging is a forging process where a hammer is raised and then "dropped" onto the workpiece to deform it according to the shape of the die. There are two types of drop forging: open-die drop forging and closed-die drop forging. As the names imply, the difference is in the shape of the die, with the former not fully enclosing the workpiece, while the latter does.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-die_drop_forging">Open-die drop forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Open-die drop forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Open-die forging is also known as <i>smith forging</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo391_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo391-8">[8]</a></sup> In open-die forging, a hammer strikes and deforms the workpiece, which is placed on a stationary <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a>. Open-die forging gets its name from the fact that the dies (the surfaces that are in contact with the workpiece) do not enclose the workpiece, allowing it to flow except where contacted by the dies. The operator therefore needs to orient and position the workpiece to get the desired shape. The dies are usually flat in shape, but some have a specially shaped surface for specialized operations. For example, a die may have a round, concave, or convex surface or be a tool to form holes or be a cut-off tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo390_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo390-9">[9]</a></sup> Open-die forgings can be worked into shapes which include discs, hubs, blocks, shafts (including step shafts or with flanges), sleeves, cylinders, flats, hexes, rounds, plate, and some custom shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Open-die forging lends itself to short runs and is appropriate for art smithing and custom work. In some cases, open-die forging may be employed to rough-shape <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">ingots</a> to prepare them for subsequent operations. Open-die forging may also orient the grain to increase strength in the required direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo390_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo390-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Advantages of open-die forging</b></p>
<ul>
<li>Reduced chance of voids</li>
<li>Better fatigue resistance</li>
<li>Improved microstructure</li>
<li>Continuous grain flow</li>
<li>Finer grain size</li>
<li>Greater strength<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>"<span id="Cogging">Cogging</span>" is the successive deformation of a bar along its length using an open-die drop forge. It is commonly used to work a piece of raw material to the proper thickness. Once the proper thickness is achieved the proper width is achieved via "edging".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> "<span id="Edging">Edging</span>" is the process of concentrating material using a concave shaped open-die. The process is called "edging" because it is usually carried out on the ends of the workpiece. "<span id="Fullering">Fullering</span>" is a similar process that thins out sections of the forging using a convex shaped die. These processes prepare the workpieces for further forging processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impression-die_forging">Impression-die forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Impression-die forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Impression-die forging is also called "closed-die forging". In impression-die forging, the metal is placed in a die resembling a mold, which is attached to an anvil. Usually, the hammer die is shaped as well. The hammer is then dropped on the workpiece, causing the metal to flow and fill the die cavities. The hammer is generally in contact with the workpiece on the scale of milliseconds. Depending on the size and complexity of the part, the hammer may be dropped multiple times in quick succession. Excess metal is squeezed out of the die cavities, forming what is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Flash_(manufacturing)" title="Flash (manufacturing)">flash</a>". The flash cools more rapidly than the rest of the material; this cool metal is stronger than the metal in the die, so it helps prevent more flash from forming. This also forces the metal to completely fill the die cavity. After forging, the flash is removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo391_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo391-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo394_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo394-14">[14]</a></sup> In commercial impression-die forging, the workpiece is usually moved through a series of cavities in a die to get from an ingot to the final form. The first impression is used to distribute the metal into the rough shape in accordance to the needs of later cavities; this impression is called an "edging", "fullering", or "bending" impression. The following cavities are called "blocking" cavities, in which the piece is working into a shape that more closely resembles the final product. These stages usually impart the workpiece with generous bends and large <a href="/wiki/Fillet_(mechanics)" title="Fillet (mechanics)">fillets</a>. The final shape is forged in a "final" or "finisher" impression cavity. If there is only a short run of parts to be done, then it may be more economical for the die to lack a final impression cavity and instead machine the final features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo392_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo392-4">[4]</a></sup> Impression-die forging has been improved in recent years through increased automation which includes induction heating, mechanical feeding, positioning and manipulation, and the direct heat treatment of parts after forging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo393_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo393-15">[15]</a></sup> One variation of impression-die forging is called "flashless forging", or "true closed-die forging". In this type of forging, the die cavities are completely closed, which keeps the workpiece from forming flash. The major advantage to this process is that less metal is lost to flash. Flash can account for 20 to 45% of the starting material. The disadvantages of this process include additional cost due to a more complex die design and the need for better lubrication and workpiece placement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo392_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo392-4">[4]</a></sup> There are other variations of part formation that integrate impression-die forging. One method incorporates casting a forging preform from liquid metal. The casting is removed after it has solidified, but while still hot. It is then finished in a single cavity die. The flash is trimmed, then the part is quench hardened. Another variation follows the same process as outlined above, except the preform is produced by the spraying deposition of metal droplets into shaped collectors (similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osprey_process" title="Osprey process">Osprey process</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo393_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo393-15">[15]</a></sup> Closed-die forging has a high initial cost due to the creation of dies and required design work to make working die cavities. However, it has low recurring costs for each part, thus forgings become more economical with more volume. This is one of the major reasons closed-die forgings are often used in the automotive and tool industries. Another reason forgings are common in these industrial sectors is that forgings generally have about a 20 percent higher strength-to-weight ratio compared to cast or machined parts of the same material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo392_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo392-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_of_impression-die_forgings_and_tooling">Design of impression-die forgings and tooling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Design of impression-die forgings and tooling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Forging dies are usually made of <a href="/wiki/Alloy_steel" title="Alloy steel">high-alloy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tool_steel" title="Tool steel">tool steel</a>. Dies must be impact resistant, wear resistant, maintain strength at high temperatures, and have the ability to withstand cycles of rapid heating and cooling. In order to produce a better, more economical die the following standards are maintained:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo393_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo393-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The dies part along a single, flat plane whenever possible. If not, the parting plane follows the contour of the part.</li>
<li>The parting surface is a plane through the center of the forging and not near an upper or lower edge.</li>
<li>Adequate <a href="/wiki/Draft_(engineering)" title="Draft (engineering)">draft</a> is provided; usually at least 3° for aluminium and 5° to 7° for steel.</li>
<li>Generous fillets and radii are used.</li>
<li>Ribs are low and wide.</li>
<li>The various sections are balanced to avoid extreme difference in metal flow.</li>
<li>Full advantage is taken of fiber flow lines.</li>
<li>Dimensional tolerances are not closer than necessary.</li>
</ul>

<p>A lubricant is used when forging to reduce friction and wear. It is also used as a thermal barrier to restrict heat transfer from the workpiece to the die. Finally, the lubricant acts as a parting compound to prevent the part from sticking in the dies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo394_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo394-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Press_forging">Press forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Press forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Press forging works by slowly applying a continuous pressure or force, which differs from the near-instantaneous impact of drop-hammer forging. The amount of time the dies are in contact with the workpiece is measured in seconds (as compared to the milliseconds of drop-hammer forges). The press forging operation can be done either cold or hot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo394_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo394-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main advantage of press forging, as compared to drop-hammer forging, is its ability to deform the complete workpiece. Drop-hammer forging usually only deforms the surfaces of the work piece in contact with the hammer and anvil; the interior of the workpiece will stay relatively undeformed. Another advantage to the process includes the knowledge of the new part's strain rate. By controlling the compression rate of the press forging operation, the internal strain can be controlled.</p>
<p>There are a few disadvantages to this process, most stemming from the workpiece being in contact with the dies for such an extended period of time. The operation is a time-consuming process due to the amount and length of steps. The workpiece will cool faster because the dies are in contact with workpiece; the dies facilitate drastically more heat transfer than the surrounding atmosphere. As the workpiece cools it becomes stronger and less ductile, which may induce cracking if deformation continues. Therefore, heated dies are usually used to reduce heat loss, promote surface flow, and enable the production of finer details and closer tolerances. The workpiece may also need to be reheated.</p>
<p>When done in high productivity, press forging is more economical than hammer forging. The operation also creates closer tolerances. In hammer forging a lot of the work is absorbed by the machinery; when in press forging, the greater percentage of work is used in the work piece. Another advantage is that the operation can be used to create any size part because there is no limit to the size of the press forging machine. New press forging techniques have been able to create a higher degree of mechanical and orientation integrity. By the constraint of oxidation to the outer layers of the part, reduced levels of microcracking occur in the finished part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo394_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo394-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Press forging can be used to perform all types of forging, including open-die and impression-die forging. Impression-die press forging usually requires less draft than drop forging and has better dimensional accuracy. Also, press forgings can often be done in one closing of the dies, allowing for easy automation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo395_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo395-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upset_forging">Upset forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Upset forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upset forging increases the diameter of the workpiece by compressing its length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo395_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo395-16">[16]</a></sup> Based on number of pieces produced, this is the most widely used forging process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo395_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo395-16">[16]</a></sup> A few examples of common parts produced using the upset forging process are engine valves, couplings, bolts, screws, and other fasteners.</p>
<p>Upset forging is usually done in special high-speed machines called <i>crank presses</i>. The machines are usually set up to work in the horizontal plane, to facilitate the quick exchange of workpieces from one station to the next, but upsetting can also be done in a vertical crank press or a hydraulic press. The initial workpiece is usually wire or rod, but some machines can accept bars up to 25 cm (9.8 in) in diameter and a capacity of over 1000 tons. The standard upsetting machine employs split dies that contain multiple cavities. The dies open enough to allow the workpiece to move from one cavity to the next; the dies then close and the heading tool, or ram, then moves longitudinally against the bar, upsetting it into the cavity. If all of the cavities are utilized on every cycle, then a finished part will be produced with every cycle, which makes this process advantageous for mass production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo395_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo395-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>These rules must be followed when designing parts to be upset forged:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The length of unsupported metal that can be upset in one blow without injurious buckling should be limited to three times the diameter of the bar.</li>
<li>Lengths of stock greater than three times the diameter may be upset successfully, provided that the diameter of the upset is not more than 1.5 times the diameter of the stock.</li>
<li>In an upset requiring stock length greater than three times the diameter of the stock, and where the diameter of the cavity is not more than 1.5 times the diameter of the stock, the length of unsupported metal beyond the face of the die must not exceed the diameter of the bar.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automatic_hot_forging">Automatic hot forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Automatic hot forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The automatic hot forging process involves feeding mill-length steel bars (typically 7 m (23 ft) long) into one end of the machine at room temperature and hot forged products emerge from the other end. This all occurs rapidly; small parts can be made at a rate of 180 parts per minute (ppm) and larger can be made at a rate of 90 ppm. The parts can be solid or hollow, round or symmetrical, up to 6 kg (13 lb), and up to 18 cm (7.1 in) in diameter. The main advantages to this process are its high output rate and ability to accept low-cost materials. Little labor is required to operate the machinery.</p>
<p>There is no flash produced so material savings are between 20 and 30% over conventional forging. The final product is a consistent 1,050 °C (1,920 °F) so air cooling will result in a part that is still easily machinable (the advantage being the lack of annealing required after forging). Tolerances are usually ±0.3 mm (0.012 in), surfaces are clean, and draft angles are 0.5 to 1°. Tool life is nearly double that of conventional forging because contact times are on the order of 0.06-second. The downside is that this process is only feasible on smaller symmetric parts and cost; the initial investment can be over $10 million, so large quantities are required to justify this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The process starts by heating the bar to 1,200 to 1,300 °C (2,190 to 2,370 °F) in less than 60 seconds using high-power induction coils. It is then descaled with rollers, sheared into blanks, and transferred through several successive forming stages, during which it is upset, preformed, final forged, and pierced (if necessary). This process can also be coupled with high-speed cold-forming operations. Generally, the cold forming operation will do the finishing stage so that the advantages of cold-working can be obtained, while maintaining the high speed of automatic hot forging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of parts made by this process are: wheel hub unit bearings, transmission gears, tapered roller bearing races, stainless steel coupling flanges, and neck rings for LP gas cylinders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Manual transmission gears are an example of automatic hot forging used in conjunction with cold working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roll_forging">Roll forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Roll forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roll forging is a process where round or flat bar stock is reduced in thickness and increased in length. Roll forging is performed using two cylindrical or semi-cylindrical rolls, each containing one or more shaped grooves. A heated bar is inserted into the rolls and when it hits a spot the rolls rotate and the bar is progressively shaped as it is rolled through the machine. The piece is then transferred to the next set of grooves or turned around and reinserted into the same grooves. This continues until the desired shape and size is achieved. The advantage of this process is there is no flash and it imparts a favorable grain structure into the workpiece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of products produced using this method include <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axles</a>, tapered levers and <a href="/wiki/Leaf_spring" title="Leaf spring">leaf springs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Net-shape_and_near-net-shape_forging">Net-shape and near-net-shape forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Net-shape and near-net-shape forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-net-shape" title="Near-net-shape">Near-net-shape</a></div>
<p>This process is also known as <i>precision forging</i>. It was developed to minimize cost and waste associated with post-forging operations. Therefore, the final product from a precision forging needs little or no final machining. Cost savings are gained from the use of less material, and thus less scrap, the overall decrease in energy used, and the reduction or elimination of machining. Precision forging also requires less of a draft, 1° to 0°. The downside of this process is its cost, therefore it is only implemented if significant cost reduction can be achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_Forging">Cold Forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cold Forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Near net shape forging is most common when parts are forged without heating the slug, bar or billet. Aluminum is a common material that can be cold forged depending on final shape. Lubrication of the parts being formed is critical to increase the life of the mating dies.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_implications">Cost implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cost implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To achieve a low-cost net shape forging for demanding applications that are subject to a high degree of scrutiny, i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-destructive_testing" title="Non-destructive testing">non-destructive testing</a> by way of a dye-penetrant inspection technique, it is crucial that basic forging process disciplines be implemented. If the basic disciplines are not met, subsequent material removal operations will likely be necessary to remove material defects found at non-destructive testing inspection. Hence low-cost parts will not be achievable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Example disciplines are: die-lubricant management (Use of uncontaminated and homogeneous mixtures, amount and placement of lubricant). Tight control of die temperatures and surface finish / friction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Induction_forging">Induction forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Induction forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Induction_forging" title="Induction forging">Induction forging</a></div>
<p>Unlike the above processes, induction forging is based on the type of heating style used. Many of the above processes can be used in conjunction with this heating method.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multidirectional_forging">Multidirectional forging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Multidirectional forging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Multidirectional Forging is forming of a work piece in a single step in several directions. The multidirectional forming takes place through constructive measures of the tool. The vertical movement of the press ram is redirected using wedges which distributes and redirects the force of the forging press in horizontal directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_and_applications">Materials and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Materials and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forging_of_steel">Forging of steel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Forging of steel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Depending on the forming temperature steel forging can be divided into:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Hot forging of steel
<ul>
<li>Forging temperatures above the recrystallization temperature between 950 - 1250 °C</li>
<li>Good formability</li>
<li>Low forming forces</li>
<li>Constant tensile strength of the workpieces</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Warm forging of steel
<ul>
<li>Forging temperatures between 750 – 950 °C</li>
<li>Less or no scaling at the workpiece surface</li>
<li>Narrower tolerances achievable than in hot forging</li>
<li>Limited formability and higher forming forces than for hot forging</li>
<li>Lower forming forces than in cold forming</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Cold forging of steel
<ul>
<li>Forging temperatures at room conditions, self-heating up to 150 °C due to the forming energy</li>
<li>Narrowest tolerances achievable</li>
<li>No scaling at workpiece surface</li>
<li>Increase of strength and decrease of ductility due to strain hardening</li>
<li>Low formability and high forming forces are necessary</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>For industrial processes steel alloys are primarily forged in hot condition. Brass, bronze, copper, precious metals and their alloys are manufactured by cold forging processes, while each metal requires a different forging temperature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forging_of_aluminium">Forging of aluminium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Forging of aluminium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Aluminium forging is performed at a temperature range between 350 and 550 °C</li>
<li>Forging temperatures above 550 °C are too close to the solidus temperature of the alloys and lead in conjunction with varying effective strains to unfavorable workpiece surfaces and potentially to a partial melting as well as fold formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>Forging temperatures below 350 °C reduce formability by increasing the yield stress, which can lead to unfilled dies, cracking at the workpiece surface and increased die forces</li>
</ul>
<p>Due to the narrow temperature range and high thermal conductivity, aluminium forging can only be realized in a particular process window. To provide good forming conditions a homogeneous temperature distribution in the entire workpiece is necessary. Therefore, the control of the tool temperature has a major influence to the process. For example, by optimizing the preform geometries the local effective strains can be influenced to reduce local overheating for a more homogeneous temperature distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_of_aluminium_forged_parts">Application of aluminium forged parts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Application of aluminium forged parts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>High-strength aluminium alloys have the tensile strength of medium strong steel alloys while providing significant weight advantages. Therefore, aluminium forged parts are mainly used in aerospace, automotive industry and many other fields of engineering especially in those fields, where highest safety standards against failure by abuse, by shock or vibratory stresses are needed. Such parts are for example chassis parts, steering components and brake parts. Commonly used alloys are AlSi1MgMn (<a href="/wiki/6082_aluminium_alloy" title="6082 aluminium alloy">EN AW-6082</a>) and AlZnMgCu1,5 (<a href="/wiki/7075_aluminium_alloy" title="7075 aluminium alloy">EN AW-7075</a>). About 80% of all aluminium forged parts are made of AlSi1MgMn. The high-strength alloy AlZnMgCu1,5 is mainly used for aerospace applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The most common type of forging equipment is the hammer and anvil. Principles behind the hammer and anvil are still used today in <i>drop-hammer</i> equipment. The principle behind the machine is simple: raise the hammer and drop it or propel it into the workpiece, which rests on the anvil. The main variations between drop-hammers are in the way the hammer is powered; the most common being air and steam hammers. Drop-hammers usually operate in a vertical position. The main reason for this is excess energy (energy that isn't used to deform the workpiece) that isn't released as heat or sound needs to be transmitted to the foundation. Moreover, a large machine base is needed to absorb the impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo390_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo390-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>To overcome some shortcomings of the drop-hammer, the <i>counterblow machine</i> or <i>impactor</i> is used. In a counterblow machine both the hammer and anvil move and the workpiece is held between them. Here excess energy becomes recoil. This allows the machine to work horizontally and have a smaller base. Other advantages include less noise, heat and vibration. It also produces a distinctly different flow pattern. Both of these machines can be used for open-die or closed-die forging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forging_presses">Forging presses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Forging presses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i>forging press</i>, often just called a press, is used for press forging. There are two main types: mechanical and hydraulic presses. Mechanical presses function by using cams, cranks and/or toggles to produce a preset (a predetermined force at a certain location in the stroke) and reproducible stroke. Due to the nature of this type of system, different forces are available at different stroke positions. Mechanical presses are faster than their hydraulic counterparts (up to 50 strokes per minute). Their capacities range from 3 to 160 MN (300 to 18,000 short tons-force). Hydraulic presses use fluid pressure and a piston to generate force. The advantages of a hydraulic press over a mechanical press are its flexibility and greater capacity. The disadvantages include a slower, larger, and costlier machine to operate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degarmo394_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-Degarmo394-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The roll forging, upsetting, and automatic hot forging processes all use specialized machinery.</p>
