<p><b>The short 20th century</b>, originally proposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iv%C3%A1n_Berend" title="Iván Berend">Iván Berend</a> (Hungarian Academy of Sciences) but defined by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hobsbawm" title="Eric Hobsbawm">Eric Hobsbawm</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a British <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> historian and author, refers to the period between the years 1914 and 1991.</p>
<p>That period begins with the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and ends with the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">fall of the Soviet Union</a>. The chain of events represented such significant changes in world history as to redefine the era:</p>
<p>It started with World War I, which caused the end of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian</a> empires. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> was greatly influenced by the outcome of World War I. The <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> was a result of World War II and ended with the fall of the Soviet Union.</p>
<p>The term is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Long_nineteenth_century" title="Long nineteenth century">long 19th century</a>, also coined by Hobsbawm, denoting the period 1789 to 1914, and to the <a href="/wiki/Long_eighteenth_century" title="Long eighteenth century">long 18th century</a>, or approximately 1688 to 1815.</p>
