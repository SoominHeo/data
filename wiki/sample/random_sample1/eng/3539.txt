<p>A <i><b>tantō</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">短刀</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "short blade")</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is one of the traditionally made <a href="/wiki/Japanese_sword" title="Japanese sword">Japanese swords</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (<a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Nihonto" title="commons:Category:Nihonto"><i>nihonto</i></a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that were worn by the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> class of feudal Japan. The tantō dates to the <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a>, when it was mainly used as a weapon but evolved in design over the years to become more ornate. Tantō were used in traditional martial arts (<a href="/wiki/Tantojutsu" title="Tantojutsu">tantojutsu</a>). The term has seen a resurgence in the West since the 1980s as a point style of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactical_knife" title="Tactical knife">tactical knives</a>, designed for piercing or stabbing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>tantō</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">dagger</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">blade</a> is single or double edged with a length between 15 and 30 cm (6–12 inches, in Japanese 1 <a href="/wiki/Shaku_(unit)" title="Shaku (unit)">shaku</a>). The tantō was designed primarily as a <a href="/wiki/Stabbing" title="Stabbing">stabbing</a> <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a>, but the edge can be used for <a href="/wiki/Cutting" title="Cutting">slashing</a> as well. Tantō are generally <a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">forged</a> in <i>hira-zukuri</i> style (without ridgeline),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> meaning that their sides have no ridge line and are nearly flat, unlike the <i>shinogi-zukuri</i> structure of a <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">katana</a>. Some tantō have particularly thick cross-sections for armor-piercing duty, and are called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoroi_toshi" title="Yoroi toshi">yoroi toshi</a></i>. Tantō were mostly carried by <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>, as commoners did not generally wear them. Women sometimes carried a small tantō called a <i><a href="/wiki/Kaiken_(dagger)" title="Kaiken (dagger)">kaiken</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in their <i><a href="/wiki/Obi_(sash)" title="Obi (sash)">obi</a></i> primarily for <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a>. Tantō were sometimes worn as the <i>shōtō</i> in place of a <i><a href="/wiki/Wakizashi" title="Wakizashi">wakizashi</a></i> in a <i><a href="/wiki/Daish%C5%8D" title="Daishō">daishō</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FS2iT8QjqEC_P.68_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FS2iT8QjqEC_P.68-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> especially on the battlefield. Before the advent of the <i>wakizashi/tantō</i> combination, it was common for a samurai to carry a <i><a href="/wiki/Tachi" title="Tachi">tachi</a></i> and a tantō as opposed to a <i><a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">katana</a></i> and a <i><a href="/wiki/Wakizashi" title="Wakizashi">wakizashi</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FS2iT8QjqEC_P.68_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FS2iT8QjqEC_P.68-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been noted that the <a href="/wiki/Tachi" title="Tachi">tachi</a> would be paired with a tantō and later the <a href="/wiki/Uchigatana" title="Uchigatana">uchigatana</a> would be paired with another shorter <i>uchigatana</i>. With the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">katana</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wakizashi" title="Wakizashi">wakizashi</a> eventually was chosen by samurai as the short sword over the tantō. Kanzan Satō in his book <i>The Japanese sword</i> notes that there did not seem to be any particular need for the <i>wakizashi</i> and suggests that the <i>wakizashi</i> may have become more popular than the <i>tantō</i> due to the <i>wakizashi</i> being more suited for indoor fighting. He mentions the custom of leaving the <i>katana</i> at the door of a castle or palace when entering while continuing to wear the <i>wakizashi</i> inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Tant.C5.8D_in_Japan">History of Tantō in Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of Tantō in Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The production of swords in Japan is divided into specific time periods:</p>
<ul>
<li>Jokoto (ancient swords, until around 900 A.D.)</li>
<li>Koto (old swords from around 900–1596)</li>
<li>Shinto (new swords 1596–1780)</li>
<li>Shinshinto (new new swords 1781–1876)</li>
<li>Gendaito (modern swords 1876–1945)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Shinsakuto (newly made swords 1953–present)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heian_to_Muromachi">Heian to Muromachi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Heian to Muromachi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="756" data-file-width="1425" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG/220px-Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG/330px-Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG/440px-Tanto_Hyuga_Masamune.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Tantō by Hyūga Masamune, 24.8cm, Unsigned Masamune, Formerly in the possession of <a href="/wiki/Ishida_Mitsunari" title="Ishida Mitsunari">Ishida Mitsunari</a>, who gave it to his brother-in-law; the tantō was stolen during the Battle of Sekigahara by Mizuno Katsushige, governor of Hyūga Province, Kamakura period, Mitsui Memorial Museum, Tokyo.</div>

<p>The tantō was invented partway through the <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a>. With the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_period" title="Kamakura period">Kamakura period</a>, tantō were forged to be more aesthetically pleasing, and <i>hira</i> and <i>uchi-sori</i> tantō becoming the most popular styles. Near the middle of the Kamakura period, more tantō artisans were seen, increasing the abundance of the weapon, and the <i>kanmuri-otoshi</i> style became prevalent in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> and Yamato. Because of the style introduced by the <i>tachi</i> in the late Kamakura period, tantō began to be forged longer and wider. The introduction of the Hachiman faith became visible in the carvings in the hilts around this time. The <i>hamon</i> (line of temper) is similar to that of the <i>tachi</i>, except for the absence of <i>choji-midare</i>, which is <i>nioi</i> and <i>utsuri</i>. <i>Gunomi-midare</i> and <i>suguha</i> are found to have taken its place.</p>
<p>During the era of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Namboku-ch%C5%8D_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Namboku-chō Period (page does not exist)">Northern and Southern Courts</a>, the tantō were forged to be up to forty centimeters as opposed to the normal one <i><a href="/wiki/Shaku_(unit)" title="Shaku (unit)">shaku</a></i> (about thirty centimeters) length. The blades became thinner between the <i>uri</i> and the <i>omote</i>, and wider between the <i>ha</i> and <i>mune</i>. At this point in time, two styles of <i>hamon</i> were prevalent: the older style, which was subtle and artistic, and the newer, more popular style. With the beginning of the Muromachi period, constant fighting caused the mass production of blades, meaning that with higher demand, lower-quality blades were manufactured. Blades that were custom-forged still were of exceptional quality, but the average blade suffered greatly. As the end of the period neared, the average blade narrowed and the curvature shallowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Momoyama_to_the_Early_Edo_Period">Momoyama to the Early Edo Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Momoyama to the Early Edo Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Approximately two hundred fifty years of peace accompanied the unification of Japan, in which there was little need for blades. In this period, both the katana and <i>wakizashi</i> were invented, taking the place of the tantō and <i>tachi</i> as the most-used pair of weapons, and the number of tantō forged was severely decreased. The only tantō produced during this period of peace were copies of others from earlier eras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sat68_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sat68-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Edo_Period">Late Edo Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Late Edo Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were still few tantō being forged during this period, and the ones that were forged reflected the work of the Kamakura, Nambokucho, or Muromachi eras. Suishinshi Masahide was a main contributor towards the forging of tantō during this age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sat68_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-sat68-14">[14]</a></sup> There are now only prehistoric tantō being used in combat.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meiji_to_present">Meiji to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Meiji to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many tantō were forged before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, due to the restoration of the Emperor to power. Members of the Imperial Court began wearing the set of <i>tachi</i> and tantō once more, and the number of tantō in existence increased dramatically. After World War II, a restriction on sword forging caused tantō manufacture to fall very low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> American and European interest in Japanese martial arts since the war created a demand for the tantō outside Japan from the 1960s through the present time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steele_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-steele-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_Tant.C5.8D">Types of Tantō</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types of Tantō">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blade_types">Blade types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Blade types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tanto_blade_styles.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1140" data-file-width="900" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Tanto_blade_styles.svg/220px-Tanto_blade_styles.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Tanto_blade_styles.svg/330px-Tanto_blade_styles.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Tanto_blade_styles.svg/440px-Tanto_blade_styles.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tanto_blade_styles.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The general blade shape is approximately 25 cm long, 17 mm wide (near the tang), 8.0 mm thick (near the tang) and approximately straight. Actual historical examples would vary in length, width, thickness and curvature. (The hira and kiriha sides of the katakiriha blade have been swapped to allow the tip to point consistently to the left while still showing the chisel-like side.)</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><i>Shinogi</i>: This is the most common type of blade geometry for long swords, but tantō made in this form are very rare, usually created from cut-down blades when a longer sword has been broken. <i>Shinogi</i> means the central ridge that runs along the length of the blade between the edge bevels and the body of the blade.</li>
<li><i>Hira</i>: A very common tantō form with no <i>shinogi</i>, the edge bevels reaching all the way from the edge (<i>ha</i>) to the back (<i>mune</i>) with no separate flats in between, creating an almost triangular cross-section (the back is ridged, as on most other blade forms, so the cross-section is actually an extremely asymmetrical diamond shape; on <i>shinogi zukuri</i> blades it is hexagonal). It is extremely common due to the simplicity of its design.</li>
<li><i>Shobu</i>: A common blade type that is very similar to the <i>shinogi zukuri</i>, except that it lacks a <i>yokote</i>, the distinct angle between the long cutting edge and the point section, and instead the edge curves smoothly and uninterrupted into the point.</li>
<li><i>Kanmuri-otoshi</i>: These tantō were shaped in hira or shobu style but from about half way to the tip the back edge was sharpened though this second edge was not particularly sharp. They had a groove running halfway up the blade and were similar to the <i>unokubi</i> style tantō.</li>
<li><i>Unokubi</i>: An uncommon tantō style akin to the <i>kanmuri-otoshi</i>, with a back that grows abruptly thinner around the middle of the blade, but in the <i>unokubi zukuri</i> it regains its thickness just before the point. There is normally a short, wide groove extending to the midway point on the blade.</li>
<li><i>Kissaki-moroha</i>: A rare blade type with a double-edged point. Unlike the later kanmuri-otoshi the tip had a distinct shape unlike any other tanto: the back edge would curve slightly downwards so that the point was lower than the back of the blade whereas other tanto had the point in line with the back of the blade. Often they had a wide groove in the base half. The most well known historical blade of this type is the <i>tachi</i> <a href="/wiki/Kogarasu_Maru" title="Kogarasu Maru">Kogarasu Maru</a>, "Little Crow", one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Treasures_of_Japan" title="National Treasures of Japan">National Treasures of Japan</a>.</li>
<li><i>Osoraku</i>: <i>Osoraku zukuri</i> features an extremely long <i>o-kissaki</i> type point, over half the blade's length.</li>
<li><i>Hochogata</i>: A tantō form that is commonly described as a short, wide, <i>hira</i>. The <i>hochogata</i> was one of the blade type that the legendary swordsmith Masamune favored.</li>
<li><i>Katakiriha</i>: An asymmetric tantō form, sharpened only on one side to create a chisel-shaped cross-section.</li>
<li><i>Moroha</i>: A rare, double-edged tantō type that has a diamond-shaped cross-section. The blade tapers to a point and contains a <i>shinogi</i> that runs to the point.</li>
<li><i>Kubikiri</i>: A very rare type; the sharpened blade is on the inside curve rather than the outside. It has no sharpened point, making it difficult to use in battle and enshrouding the weapon in mystery. <i>Kubikiri</i> can be roughly translated to "head cutter". According to one myth, they were carried by attendants of samurai for cutting off the heads of fallen enemies. There are other speculations existing about the <i>kubikiri</i>’s possible uses. Perhaps they were used by doctors or carried by high-ranking officials as a badge is worn today. They could also have been used for cutting charcoal or incense, or used as an artistic tool for pruning <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">bonsai</a> trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoroi_toshi" title="Yoroi toshi"><i>Yoroi toshi</i> or <i>yoroi doshi</i></a>: tantō that have particularly thick cross-sections for armor-piercing duty.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountings_.28koshirae.29">Mountings (koshirae)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mountings (koshirae)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aikuchi" title="Aikuchi">Aikuchi</a></i>: The <i>aikuchi</i> is a <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Koshirae_(tanto)" title="commons:Category:Koshirae (tanto)"><i>tantō koshirae</i></a> where the <i>fuchi</i> is flush with the mouth of the sheath. There is no handguard. <i>Aikuchi</i> normally have plain wooden hilts, and many forms of <i>aikuchi</i> have <i>kashira</i> that are made from animal horns.</li>
<li><i>Hamidashi</i>: The <i>hamidashi</i> is a <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Koshirae_(tanto)" title="commons:Category:Koshirae (tanto)"><i>tantō koshirae</i></a> that features a small handguard.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_tant.C5.8D">Other tantō</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other tantō">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kaiken_(dagger)" title="Kaiken (dagger)">Kaiken tantō</a></i>: The <i>kuaiken</i> (also <i>kwaiken</i> or futokoro-<i>gatana</i>) is a generally short tantō that is commonly carried in <i>aikuchi</i> or <i>shirasaya</i> mounts. It was useful for self-defense indoors where the long katana and intermediate wakizashi were inconvenient. Women carried them in the obi for self-defense and rarely for jigai (<a href="/wiki/Seppuku#Female_ritual_suicide" title="Seppuku">ritual suicide</a>). A woman received a kaiken as part of her wedding gifts.</li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Shikomi_sensu_(fan_tanto)" title="commons:Category:Shikomi sensu (fan tanto)"><i>Fan Tantō</i></a>: The fan tantō is a common tantō with a blade entirely concealed within a fan-shaped scabbard. The blade was usually low quality, as this tantō was not designed to be a display piece, but rather a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concealed_weapon" title="Concealed weapon">concealed weapon</a> for self-defense.</li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Yari_tanto" title="commons:Category:Yari tanto"><i>Yari Tantō</i></a>: Japanese spearheads were often altered so that it became possible to mount them as tantō. Unlike most blades, <i>yari</i> tantō had triangular cross-sections.</li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Ken_tanto" title="commons:Category:Ken tanto"><i>Ken tanto</i></a>: This is also not truly a tantō, though it is often used and thought of as one. <i>Ken</i> were straight, double-edged blades often used for Buddhist rituals, and could be made from spearheads that were broken or cut shorter. They were often given as offerings from sword smiths when they visited a temple. The hilt of the <i>ken</i> tantō may be found made with a <i>vajra</i> (double thunderbolt related to Buddhism).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><i>Modern tantō</i>: Modern <a href="/wiki/Combat_knife" title="Combat knife">tactical knives</a> have been made by knife makers Bob Lum, <a href="/wiki/Phill_Hartsfield" title="Phill Hartsfield">Phill Hartsfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Emerson" title="Ernest Emerson">Ernest Emerson</a>, Allen Elishewitz, Bob Terzuola, <a href="/wiki/Strider_Knives" title="Strider Knives">Strider Knives</a>, Harold J. "Kit" Carson, <a href="/wiki/Benchmade" title="Benchmade">Benchmade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spyderco" title="Spyderco">Spyderco</a>, Severtech, Ka-Bar, <a href="/wiki/SOG_Specialty_Knives" title="SOG Specialty Knives">SOG Knives</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cold_Steel" title="Cold Steel">Cold Steel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GP_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-GP-18">[18]</a></sup> These "American Tantō" designs feature a thick spine on the blade that goes from the tang to the tip for increased tip strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-customtacticals_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-customtacticals-19">[19]</a></sup> The handle shape may be altered slightly to provide better ergonomics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steele_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-steele-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_martial_arts">Use in martial arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tant%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Use in martial arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li><a href="/wiki/Jujutsu" title="Jujutsu">Jujutsu</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koryu" title="Koryu">Koryu</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bujutsu" title="Bujutsu">Bujutsu</a></li>
