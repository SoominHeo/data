<p>The <b>pilcrow</b> (<b>¶</b>), also called the <b>paragraph mark</b>, <b>paragraph sign</b>, <b>paraph</b>, <b>alinea</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>a lineā</i>, "off the line"), or <b>blind P</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-style_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-style-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typographical" title="Typographical">typographical</a> <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">character</a> for individual <a href="/wiki/Paragraph" title="Paragraph">paragraphs</a>. It is present in <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> as <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+00B6</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">¶</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">PILCROW SIGN</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#182;</code> <b>·</b> <code>&amp;para;</code>).</p>
<p>The pilcrow can be used as an indent for separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paragraphs" title="Paragraphs">paragraphs</a> or to designate a new paragraph in one long piece of copy, as <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gill" title="Eric Gill">Eric Gill</a> did in his 1930s book, <i>An Essay on Typography</i>. The pilcrow was a type of <a href="/wiki/Rubrication" title="Rubrication">rubrication</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> to mark a new <a href="/wiki/Train_of_thought" title="Train of thought">train of thought</a>, before the convention of visually discrete paragraphs was commonplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pilcrow is usually drawn similar to a lowercase <a href="/wiki/Q" title="Q">q</a> reaching from <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">descender</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ascender_(typography)" title="Ascender (typography)">ascender</a> height; the loop can be filled or unfilled. It may also be drawn with the bowl stretching further downwards, resembling a backwards <a href="/wiki/D" title="D">D</a>; this is more often seen in older printing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_name">Origin and name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pilcrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The word <i>pilcrow</i> originates from the Greek word <i>paragraphos</i>. This was rendered in Old French as <i>paragraphe</i> and later changed to <i>pelagraphe</i>. The earliest reference of the modern <i>pilcrow</i> is in 1440 with the Middle English word <i>pylcrafte</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first way to divide sentences into groups in Ancient Greek was the original <i>paragraphos</i>, which was a horizontal line in the margin to the left of the main text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As the <i>paragraphos</i> became more popular, the horizontal line eventually changed into the Greek letter Gamma (Γ and γ) and later into <i>litterae notabiliores</i>, which were enlarged letters at the beginning of a paragraph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This notation soon changed to the letter K which was an abbreviation for the Latin word <i>kaput</i>, which translates as "head," i.e. it marks the head of a new thesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Eventually, to mark a new section, the Latin word <i>capitulum</i>, which translates as "little head" was used, and the letter C came to mark a new section in 300 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the 1100s, C had completely replaced K as the symbol for a new chapter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rubrication" title="Rubrication">Rubricators</a> eventually added a vertical bar to the C to stylize it; the symbol was filled in with dark ink and eventually looked like the modern pilcrow (¶).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">Modern use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pilcrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scribes would often leave space before paragraphs to allow for <a href="/wiki/Rubrication" title="Rubrication">rubricators</a> to draw the pilcrow. With the introduction of the printing press, space before paragraphs was still left for rubricators to draw by-hand; however, rubricators could not draw fast enough for printers and often would leave the beginnings of the paragraphs as blank. This is how the indent before paragraphs was created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the pilcrow remains in use in modern time in the following ways:</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Legal_writing" title="Legal writing">legal writing</a>, it is used whenever one must <a href="/wiki/Legal_citation" title="Legal citation">cite</a> a specific paragraph within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleadings" title="Pleadings">pleadings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law_review" title="Law review">law review</a> articles, <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statutes</a>, or other legal documents and materials.</p>
<p>In academic writing, it is sometimes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> used as an in-text referencing tool to make reference to a specific paragraph from a document that does not contain page numbers, allowing the reader to find where that particular idea or statistic was sourced. The pilcrow sign followed by a number indicates the paragraph number from the top of the page. It is rarely used when citing books or journal articles.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Proofreading" title="Proofreading">proofreading</a>, it indicates that one paragraph should be split into two or more separate paragraphs. The proofreader inserts the pilcrow at the point where a new paragraph should begin.</p>
<p>In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-church" title="High-church">high-church</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal</a> churches, it is used in the printed order of service to indicate that instructions follow; these indicate when the <a href="/wiki/Local_church" title="Local church">congregation</a> should stand, sit, and kneel, who participates in various portions of the service, and similar information. <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King's College, Cambridge">King's College, Cambridge</a> uses this convention in the service <a class="external text" href="http://www.kings.cam.ac.uk/files/services/festival-nine-lessons-2010.pdf" rel="nofollow">booklet</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Festival_of_Nine_Lessons_and_Carols" title="Festival of Nine Lessons and Carols">Festival of Nine Lessons and Carols</a>. This is analogous to the writing of these instructions in red in some rubrication conventions.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Online" title="Online">Online</a>, it is used in some blogs and wikis to denote <a href="/wiki/Permalink" title="Permalink">permalinks</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Purple_Numbers" title="Purple Numbers">Purple Numbers</a>).</p>
<p>The pilcrow is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desktop_publishing_software" title="Desktop publishing software">desktop publishing software</a> such as desktop <a href="/wiki/Word_processor" title="Word processor">word processors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a> programs to mark the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Carriage_return" title="Carriage return">carriage return</a> <a href="/wiki/Control_character" title="Control character">control character</a> at the end of a paragraph. It is also used as the <a href="/wiki/Icon_(computing)" title="Icon (computing)">icon</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Toolbar" title="Toolbar">toolbar</a> button that shows or hides the pilcrow and similar <i>hidden characters</i>, including <a href="/wiki/Tab_key" title="Tab key">tabs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitespace_(computer_science)" title="Whitespace (computer science)">whitespace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Page_break" title="Page break">page breaks</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Typing" title="Typing">typing</a> programs, it marks a return that one must type.</p>
<p>The pilcrow may indicate a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Footnote" title="Footnote">footnote</a> in a convention using a sequence of distinct typographic symbols in sequence to distinguish the footnotes on a given page; it is the sixth in a series of footnote symbols beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Asterisk" title="Asterisk">asterisk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-style_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-style-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Encoding">Encoding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pilcrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Encoding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pilcrow symbol is available in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Default_hardware_codepage" title="Default hardware codepage">default hardware codepage</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codepage_437" title="Codepage 437">437</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PCs</a> (and all other 8-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OEM_codepage" title="OEM codepage">OEM codepages</a> based on this) at codepoint 20 (0x14), sharing its position with the <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_code" title="Control code">control code</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DC4_(control_code)" title="DC4 (control code)">DC4</a> (to the effect that it isn't easily accessible in most software under this position).</p>
<p>The pilcrow <a href="/wiki/Character_(computing)" title="Character (computing)">character</a> was in the 1984 <a href="/wiki/Multinational_Character_Set" title="Multinational Character Set">Multinational Character Set</a> extension of <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> at 0xB6 (decimal 182), from where it was inherited by <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-1" title="ISO/IEC 8859-1">ISO/IEC 8859-1</a> (1987) and by <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> as U+00B6 ¶ <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">PILCROW SIGN</span></span>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Html_entity" title="Html entity">html entity</a> is &amp;para;, introduced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HTML_3.2" title="HTML 3.2">HTML 3.2</a> (1997). In <a href="/wiki/LaTeX" title="LaTeX">LaTeX</a>, the pilcrow glyph is invoked by <i>\P</i> or <i>\textpilcrow</i>.</p>
<p>Apart from <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+00B6</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">¶</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">PILCROW SIGN</span> (182<sub>decimal</sub>), Unicode also defines <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+204B</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">⁋</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">REVERSED PILCROW SIGN</span> (8267<sub>decimal</sub>), <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+2761</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">❡</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">CURVED STEM PARAGRAPH SIGN ORNAMENT</span> (10081<sub>decimal</sub>), and <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+2E3F</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">⸿</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">CAPITULUM</span>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paragraph_signs_in_non-Latin_writing_systems">Paragraph signs in non-Latin writing systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pilcrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paragraph signs in non-Latin writing systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, the traditional paragraph sign (rendered as 〇) is a thin <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a> about the same size as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese character</a>. This same mark also serves as a “zero” character, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Style_(fiction)" title="Style (fiction)">stylistic</a> variation of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> character for “zero”. As a paragraph sign, this mark only appears in older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Books" title="Books">books</a>, commonly found in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Union_Version" title="Chinese Union Version">Chinese Union Version</a> of the Bible. Its current use is generally as a “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero" title="Zero">zero</a>” character. However, it can also be found in some editions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mormon" title="Book of Mormon">Book of Mormon</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>, the character <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+0E5B</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">๛</span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khomut" title="Khomut"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">THAI CHARACTER KHOMUT</span></a> can mark the end of a <a href="/wiki/Chapter_(books)" title="Chapter (books)">chapter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Document" title="Document">document</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">Indian languages</a>, text blocks used to be written in stanzas. Two bars || represented a pilcrow.</p>
