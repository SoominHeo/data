<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Injective sheaf</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <b>injective sheaves</b> of <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian groups</a> are used to construct the resolutions needed to define <a href="/wiki/Sheaf_cohomology" title="Sheaf cohomology">sheaf cohomology</a> (and other <a href="/wiki/Derived_functor" title="Derived functor">derived functors</a>, such as sheaf <a href="/wiki/Ext_functor" title="Ext functor">Ext</a>).</p>
<p>There is a further group of related concepts applied to <a href="/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics)" title="Sheaf (mathematics)">sheaves</a>: <b>flabby</b> (<i>flasque</i> in French), <b>fine</b>, <b>soft</b> (<i>mou</i> in French), <b>acyclic</b>. In the history of the subject they were introduced before the 1957 "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tohoku_paper" title="Tohoku paper">Tohoku paper</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck" title="Alexander Grothendieck">Alexander Grothendieck</a>, which showed that the <a href="/wiki/Abelian_category" title="Abelian category">abelian category</a> notion of <i><a href="/wiki/Injective_object" title="Injective object">injective object</a></i> sufficed to found the theory. The other classes of sheaves are historically older notions. The abstract framework for defining cohomology and derived functors does not need them. However, in most concrete situations, resolutions by acyclic sheaves are often easier to construct. Acyclic sheaves therefore serve for computational purposes, for example the <a href="/wiki/Leray_spectral_sequence" title="Leray spectral sequence">Leray spectral sequence</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Injective_sheaves">Injective sheaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injective_sheaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Injective sheaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <b>injective sheaf</b> <i>F</i> is just a sheaf that is an injective object of the category of abelian sheaves; in other words, homomorphisms from <i>A</i> to <i>F</i> can always be lifted to any sheaf <i>B</i> containing <i>A</i>.</p>
<p>The category of abelian sheaves has enough injective objects: this means that any sheaf is a subsheaf of an injective sheaf. This result of Grothendieck follows from the existence of a <i>generator</i> of the category (it can be written down explicitly, and is related to the <a href="/wiki/Subobject_classifier" title="Subobject classifier">subobject classifier</a>). This is enough to show that right derived functors of any left exact functor exist and are unique up to canonical isomorphism.</p>
<p>For technical purposes, injective sheaves are usually superior to the other classes of sheaves mentioned above: they can do almost anything the other classes can do, and their theory is simpler and more general. In fact, injective sheaves are flabby (<i>flasque</i>), soft, and acyclic. However, there are situations where the other classes of sheaves occur naturally, and this is especially true in concrete computational situations.</p>
<p>The dual concept, <b>projective sheaves</b>, is not used much, because in a general category of sheaves there are not enough of them: not every sheaf is the quotient of a projective sheaf, and in particular projective resolutions do not always exist. This is the case, for example, when looking at the category of sheaves on <a href="/wiki/Projective_space" title="Projective space">projective space</a> in the Zariski topology. This causes problems when attempting to define left derived functors of a right exact functor (such as Tor). This can sometimes be done by ad hoc means: for example, the left derived functors of Tor can be defined using a flat resolution rather than a projective one, but it takes some work to show that this is independent of the resolution. Not all categories of sheaves run into this problem; for instance, the category of sheaves on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affine_scheme" title="Affine scheme">affine scheme</a> contains enough projectives.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acyclic_sheaves">Acyclic sheaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injective_sheaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acyclic sheaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <b>acyclic sheaf</b> <i>F</i> over <i>X</i> is one such that all higher sheaf cohomology groups vanish.</p>
<p>The cohomology groups of any sheaf can be calculated from any acyclic resolution of it (this goes by the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Rham-Weil_theorem" title="De Rham-Weil theorem">De Rham-Weil theorem</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fine_sheaves">Fine sheaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injective_sheaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fine sheaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b>fine sheaf</b> over <i>X</i> is one with "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partitions_of_unity" title="Partitions of unity">partitions of unity</a>"; more precisely for any open cover of the space <i>X</i> we can find a family of homomorphisms from the sheaf to itself with sum 1 such that each homomorphism is 0 outside some element of the open cover.</p>
<p>Fine sheaves are usually only used over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paracompact" title="Paracompact">paracompact</a> Hausdorff spaces <i>X</i>. Typical examples are the sheaf of germs of continuous real-valued functions over such a space, or smooth functions over a smooth (paracompact Hausdorff) manifold, or modules over these sheaves of rings. Also, fine sheaves over paracompact Hausdorff spaces are soft and acyclic.</p>
<p>One can find a resolution of a sheaf on a smooth manifold by fine sheaves using the Alexander-Spanier resolution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an application, consider a real <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">manifold</a> <i>X</i>. There is the following resolution of the constant sheaf ℝ by the fine sheaves of (smooth) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_forms" title="Differential forms">differential forms</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>0 → ℝ → C<sup>0</sup><sub>X</sub> → C<sup>1</sup><sub>X</sub> → ... → C<sup>dim X</sup><sub>X</sub> → 0</dd>
</dl>
<p>This is a resolution, i.e. an exact complex of sheaves by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_lemma" title="Poincaré lemma">Poincaré lemma</a>. The cohomology of <i>X</i> with values in ℝ can thus be computed as the cohomology of the complex of globally defined differential forms:</p>
<dl>
<dd>H<sup>i</sup>(X, ℝ) = H<sup>i</sup>(C<sup>·</sup><sub>X</sub>(X)).</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soft_sheaves">Soft sheaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injective_sheaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Soft sheaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b>soft sheaf</b> <i>F</i> over <i>X</i> is one such that any section over any <b>closed</b> subset of <i>X</i> can be extended to a global section.</p>
<p>Soft sheaves are acyclic over paracompact Hausdorff spaces.</p>

<p>Flasque sheaves are useful because (by definition) sections of them extend. This means that they are some of the simplest sheaves to handle in terms of <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>. <p><i>Flasque</i> is a <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word, that has sometimes been translated into English as <i>flabby</i>.</p>
<p>Flasque sheaves are soft and acyclic.</p>
