<p><i><b>Herpesviridae</b></i> is a large <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/DNA_virus" title="DNA virus">DNA viruses</a> that cause diseases in animals, including humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherris_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherris-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sobrino9_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sobrino9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sandri_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sandri-3">[3]</a></sup> The members of this family are also known as <b>herpesviruses</b>. The family name is derived from the Greek word <i>herpein</i> ("to creep"), referring to the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/latent" title="wikt:latent">latent</a>, recurring <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infections</a> typical of this group of viruses. <i>Herpesviridae</i> can cause <a href="/wiki/Virus_latency" title="Virus latency">latent</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lytic_cycle" title="Lytic cycle">lytic</a> infections.</p>
<p>At least five species of Herpesviridae – <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex" title="Herpes simplex">HSV-1</a> and HSV-2 (both of which can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orolabial_herpes" title="Orolabial herpes">orolabial herpes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex" title="Herpes simplex">genital herpes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Varicella_zoster_virus" title="Varicella zoster virus">varicella zoster virus</a> (the cause of <a href="/wiki/Chickenpox" title="Chickenpox">chickenpox</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpes_zoster" title="Herpes zoster">shingles</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epstein-Barr_virus" title="Epstein-Barr virus">Epstein-Barr virus</a> (implicated in several diseases, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mononucleosis" title="Mononucleosis">mononucleosis</a> and some cancers), and <a href="/wiki/Cytomegalovirus" title="Cytomegalovirus">cytomegalovirus</a> – are extremely widespread among humans. More than 90% of adults have been infected with at least one of these, and a latent form of the virus remains in most people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peds09_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peds09-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_.E2.80.93_Epstein.E2.80.93Barr_Virus_and_Infectious_Mononucleosis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_.E2.80.93_Epstein.E2.80.93Barr_Virus_and_Infectious_Mononucleosis-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are 9 herpesvirus types known to infect humans: herpes simplex viruses 1 and 2 (HSV-1 and HSV-2, aka. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HHV1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HHV1 (page does not exist)">HHV1</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HHV2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HHV2 (page does not exist)">HHV2</a>), varicella-zoster virus (VZV, which may also be called by its <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses" title="International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses">ICTV</a> name, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-3" title="HHV-3">HHV-3</a>), Epstein-Barr virus (EBV or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-4" title="HHV-4">HHV-4</a>), human cytomegalovirus (HCMV or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-5" title="HHV-5">HHV-5</a>), human herpesvirus 6A and 6B (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-6A" title="HHV-6A">HHV-6A</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-6B" title="HHV-6B">HHV-6B</a>), human herpesvirus 7 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-7" title="HHV-7">HHV-7</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Kaposi%27s_sarcoma-associated_herpesvirus" title="Kaposi's sarcoma-associated herpesvirus">Kaposi's sarcoma-associated herpesvirus</a> (KSHV, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV-8" title="HHV-8">HHV-8</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-John-7">[7]</a></sup> In total, there are more than 130 herpesviruses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> some of them from <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-John-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viral_structure">Viral structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Viral structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herpesviruses all share a common structure—all herpesviruses are composed of relatively large double-stranded, linear <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a> encoding 100-200 <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> encased within an <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">icosahedral</a> protein cage called the <a href="/wiki/Capsid" title="Capsid">capsid</a> which is itself wrapped in a protein layer called the tegument containing both viral proteins and viral mRNAs and a <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a> membrane called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Envelope_(biology)" title="Envelope (biology)">envelope</a>. This whole particle is known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virion" title="Virion">virion</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Herpesvirus_life_cycle">Herpesvirus life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Herpesvirus life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All herpesviruses are <i>nuclear-replicating</i>—the viral <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcribed</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> within the infected cell's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleus_(biology)" title="Nucleus (biology)">nucleus</a>.</p>
<p>Infection is initiated when a viral particle contacts a cell with specific types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmembrane_receptors" title="Transmembrane receptors">receptor molecules on the cell surface</a>. Following binding of viral envelope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycoproteins" title="Glycoproteins">glycoproteins</a> to cell membrane receptors, the virion is internalized and dismantled, allowing viral DNA to migrate to the cell nucleus. Within the nucleus, <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">replication</a> of viral DNA and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a> of viral genes occurs.</p>
<p>During symptomatic infection, infected cells transcribe <i><a href="/wiki/Lytic_cycle" title="Lytic cycle">lytic</a></i> viral genes. In some host cells, a small number of viral genes termed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HHV_LAT" title="HHV LAT">latency associated transcript</a></i> (LAT) accumulate instead. In this fashion the virus can persist in the cell (and thus the host) indefinitely. While primary infection is often accompanied by a self-limited period of clinical illness, long-term latency is symptom-free.</p>
<p>Reactivation of latent viruses has been implicated in a number of diseases (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpes_Zoster" title="Herpes Zoster">Shingles</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pityriasis_Rosea" title="Pityriasis Rosea">Pityriasis Rosea</a></i>). Following activation, transcription of viral genes transitions from latency-associated LAT to multiple <i>lytic</i> genes; these lead to enhanced replication and virus production. Often, lytic activation leads to <a href="/wiki/Cell_death" title="Cell death">cell death</a>. Clinically, lytic activation is often accompanied by emergence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-specific_symptom" title="Non-specific symptom">non-specific symptoms</a> such as low grade fever, headache, sore throat, <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">malaise</a>, and rash as well as clinical signs such as swollen or tender <a href="/wiki/Lymph_node" title="Lymph node">lymph nodes</a> and immunological findings such as reduced levels of <a href="/wiki/Natural_killer_cell" title="Natural killer cell">natural killer cells</a>.</p>

<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICTV_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICTV-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpesvirus" title="Herpesvirus">herpesvirus</a> was first isolated from the <a href="/wiki/Blue_wildebeest" title="Blue wildebeest">blue wildebeest</a> in 1960 by veterinary scientist <a href="/wiki/Walter_Plowright" title="Walter Plowright">Walter Plowright</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The genus <i>Herpesvirus</i> was established in 1971 in the first report of the International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV). This genus consisted of 23 viruses and 4 groups of viruses. In the second ICTV report in 1976 this genus was elevated to family level - the <i>Herpetoviridae</i>. Because of possible confusion with viruses derived from <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> this name was changed in the third report in 1979 to <i>Herpesviridae</i>. In this report the family <i>Herpesviridae</i> was divided into 3 subfamilies (<i>Alphaherpesvirinae</i>, <i>Betaherpesvirinae</i> and <i>Gammaherpesvirinae</i>) and 5 unnamed genera: 21 viruses were listed. In 2009 the family <i>Herpesviridae</i> was elevated to the order <i>Herpesvirales</i>. This elevation was necessitated by the discovery that the herpes viruses of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> were only distantly related to those of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. Two new families were created - the family <i>Alloherpesviridae</i> which incorporates bony fish and frog viruses and the family <i>Malacoherpesviridae</i> which contains those of molluscs.</p>
<p>This order currently has 3 families, 3 subfamilies plus 1 unassigned, 17 genera, 90 species and plus 48 as yet unassigned viruses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davison2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davison2010-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virus_naming_system">Virus naming system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Virus naming system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The system of naming herpes viruses was originated in 1973 and has been elaborated considerably since. The recommended naming system specified that each herpes virus should be named after the taxon (family or subfamily) to which its primary natural host belongs. The subfamily name is used for viruses from members of the family <i><a href="/wiki/Bovidae" title="Bovidae">Bovidae</a></i> or from <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a> (the virus name ending in –ine, e.g. bovine) and the host family name for other viruses (ending in –id, e.g. equid). Human herpes viruses have been treated as an exception (<i>human</i> rather than <i>hominid</i>). Following the host-derived term, the word <i>herpes virus</i> is added, followed by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_numeral" title="Arabic numeral">Arabic numeral</a> (1,2,3,...). These last two additions bear no implied meaning about taxonomic or biological properties of the virus.</p>
<p>Some exceptions to this system exist. A number of viruses' names (e.g. Epstein–Barr virus) are so widely used that it is impractical to attempt to insist on their replacement. This has led to a dual nomenclature in the literature for some herpes viruses. All herpes viruses described since this system was adopted have been named in accordance with it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three mammalian subfamilies - Alpha, Beta and Gamma - arose approximately <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=180">180</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span> to <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=220">220</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGeoch1995_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGeoch1995-12">[12]</a></sup> The major sublineages within these subfamilies were probably generated before the mammalian radiation of <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=80">80</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span> to <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=60">60</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>. Speciations within sublineages took place in the last 80 million years probably with a major component of cospeciation with host lineages.</p>
<p>All the currently known bird and reptile species are alphaherpesviruses. Although the branching order of the herpes viruses has not yet been resolved, because herpes viruses and their hosts tend to coevolve this is suggestive that the alphaherpesviruses may have been the earliest branch.</p>
<p>The date of evolution of the iltovirus genus has been estimated to be <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=200">200</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span> while those of the mardivirus and simplex genera have been estimated to be between <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=150">150</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span> and <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=100">100</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGeoch2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGeoch2006-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Immune_system_evasions">Immune system evasions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Immune system evasions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herpesviruses are known for their ability to establish lifelong infections. One way this is possible is through immune evasion. Herpesviruses have many different ways of evading the immune system. One such way is by encoding a protein mimicking human <a href="/wiki/Interleukin_10" title="Interleukin 10">interleukin 10</a> (hIL-10) and another is by downregulation of the <a href="/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex" title="Major histocompatibility complex">major histocompatibility complex</a> II (MHC II) in infected cells.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="cmvIL-10">cmvIL-10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: cmvIL-10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research conducted on cytomegalovirus (CMV) indicates that the viral human IL-10 homolog, cmvIL-10, is important in inhibiting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-inflammatory_cytokine" title="Pro-inflammatory cytokine">pro-inflammatory cytokine</a> synthesis. The cmvIL-10 protein has 27% identity with hIL-10 and only one conserved residue out of the nine amino acids that make up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functional_site" title="Functional site">functional site</a> for cytokine synthesis inhibition on hIL-10. There is, however, much similarity in the functions of hIL-10 and cmvIL-10. Both have been shown to down regulate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFN-%CE%B3" title="IFN-γ">IFN-γ</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IL-1%CE%B1" title="IL-1α">IL-1α</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GM-CSF" title="GM-CSF">GM-CSF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interleukin_6" title="Interleukin 6">IL-6</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TNF-%CE%B1" title="TNF-α">TNF-α</a>, which are all pro-inflammatory cytokines. They have also been shown to play a role in downregulating MHC I and MHC II and up regulating <a href="/wiki/HLA-G" title="HLA-G">HLA-G</a> (non-classical MHC I). These two events allow for immune evasion by suppressing the cell-mediated immune response and <a href="/wiki/Natural_killer_cell" title="Natural killer cell">natural killer cell</a> response, respectively. The similarities between hIL-10 and cmvIL-10 may be explained by the fact that hIL-10 and cmvIL-10 both use the same cell surface receptor, the hIL-10 receptor. One difference in the function of hIL-10 and cmvIL-10 is that hIL-10 causes human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PBMC" title="PBMC">PBMC</a>) to both increase and decrease in proliferation whereas cmvIL-10 only causes a decrease in proliferation of PBMCs. This indicates that cmvIL-10 may lack the stimulatory effects that hIL-10 has on these cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was found that cmvIL-10 functions through phosphorylation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stat3" title="Stat3">Stat3</a> protein. It was originally thought that this phosphorylation was a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JAK-STAT" title="JAK-STAT">JAK-STAT</a> pathway. However, despite evidence that JAK does indeed phosphorylate Stat3, its inhibition has no significant influence on cytokine synthesis inhibition. Another protein, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PI3K" title="PI3K">PI3K</a>, was also found to phosphorylate Stat3. PI3K inhibition, unlike JAK inhibition, did have a significant impact on cytokine synthesis. The difference between PI3K and JAK in Stat3 phosphorylation is that PI3K phosphorylates Stat3 on the S727 residue whereas JAK phosphorylates Stat3 on the Y705 residue. This difference in phosphorylation positions seems to be the key factor in Stat3 activation leading to inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokine synthesis. In fact, when a PI3K inhibitor is added to cells, the cytokine synthesis levels are significantly restored. The fact that cytokine levels are not completely restored indicates there is another pathway activated by cmvIL-10 that is inhibiting cytokine system nthesis. The proposed mechanism is that cmvIL-10 activates PI3K which in turn activates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AKT" title="AKT">PKB</a> (Akt). PKB may then activate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTOR" title="MTOR">mTOR</a>, which may target Stat3 for phosphorylation on the S727 residue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MHC_downregulation">MHC downregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: MHC downregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another one of the many ways in which herpes viruses evade the immune system is by down regulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHC_I" title="MHC I">MHC I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHC_II" title="MHC II">MHC II</a>. This is observed in almost every human herpesvirus. Down regulation of MHC I and MHC II can come about by many different mechanisms, most causing the MHC to be absent from the cell surface. As discussed above, one way is by a viral chemokine homolog such as IL-10. Another mechanism to down regulate MHCs is to encode viral proteins that detain the newly formed MHC in the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a> (ER). The MHC cannot reach the cell surface and therefore cannot activate the <a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cell</a> response. The MHCs can also be targeted for destruction in the <a href="/wiki/Proteasome" title="Proteasome">proteasome</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lysosome" title="Lysosome">lysosome</a>. The ER protein <a href="/wiki/Transporter_associated_with_antigen_processing" title="Transporter associated with antigen processing">TAP</a> also plays a role in MHC down regulation. Viral proteins inhibit TAP preventing the MHC from picking up a viral antigen peptide. This prevents proper folding of the MHC and therefore the MHC does not reach the cell surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is important to note that <a href="/wiki/HLA-G" title="HLA-G">HLA-G</a> is often up regulated in addition to downregulation of MHC I and MHC II. This prevents the natural killer cell response.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_herpesviruses">Animal herpesviruses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herpesviridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Animal herpesviruses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_virology" title="Animal virology">animal virology</a>, the most important herpesviruses belong to the subfamily <i><a href="/wiki/Alphaherpesvirinae" title="Alphaherpesvirinae">Alphaherpesvirinae</a></i>. Research on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudorabies_virus" title="Pseudorabies virus">pseudorabies virus</a> (PrV), the causative agent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aujeszky%27s_disease" title="Aujeszky's disease">Aujeszky's disease</a> in pigs, has pioneered animal disease control with genetically modified vaccines. PrV is now extensively studied as a model for basic processes during lytic herpesvirus infection, and for unraveling molecular mechanisms of herpesvirus neurotropism, whereas <a href="/wiki/Bovine_herpesvirus_1" title="Bovine herpesvirus 1">bovine herpesvirus 1</a>, the causative agent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bovine_infectious_rhinotracheitis" title="Bovine infectious rhinotracheitis">bovine infectious rhinotracheitis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pustular_vulvovaginitis" title="Pustular vulvovaginitis">pustular vulvovaginitis</a>, is analyzed to elucidate molecular mechanisms of latency. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avian_infectious_laryngotracheitis_virus" title="Avian infectious laryngotracheitis virus">avian infectious laryngotracheitis virus</a> is phylogenetically distant from these two viruses and serves to underline similarity and diversity within the <i>Alphaherpesvirinae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sobrino9_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sobrino9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sandri_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sandri-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
