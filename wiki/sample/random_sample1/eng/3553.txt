<p><i><b>Acer palmatum</b></i>, commonly known as <b>palmate maple</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>Japanese maple</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or <b>smooth Japanese-maple</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBI07_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBI07-5">[5]</a></sup> (Japanese: <i>irohamomiji</i>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ã¤ã­ãã¢ãã¸</span></span>, or <i>momiji</i>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B4%85%E8%91%89" title="wiktionary:ç´è">ç´è</a></span></span>), is a species of woody plant native to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, and southeast <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-grin-6">[6]</a></sup> Many different <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a> have been selected and they are grown worldwide for their large variety of attractive forms, leaf shapes, and spectacular colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_palmatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Acer palmatum</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or small <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> reaching heights of 6 to 10Â m (20 to 33Â ft), rarely 16 metres (52Â ft), often growing as an understory plant in shady <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>. It may have multiple trunks joining close to the ground. In <a href="/wiki/Habit_(biology)" title="Habit (biology)">habit</a>, it is often shaped like a hemisphere (especially when younger) or takes on a <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a>-like form, especially when mature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are 4â12Â cm long and wide, palmately lobed with five, seven, or nine acutely pointed lobes. The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are produced in small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyme_(botany)" title="Cyme (botany)">cymes</a>, the individual flowers with five red or purple sepals and five whitish petals. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is a pair of winged <a href="/wiki/Samara_(fruit)" title="Samara (fruit)">samaras</a>, each samara 2â3Â cm long with a 6â8Â mm seed. The seeds of <i>Acer palmatum</i> and similar <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratification_(botany)" title="Stratification (botany)">stratification</a> in order to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germinate" title="Germinate">germinate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushforth_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even in nature, <i>Acer palmatum</i> displays considerable <a href="/wiki/Genetic_variation" title="Genetic variation">genetic variation</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedlings</a> from the same parent tree typically showing differences in such traits as leaf size, shape, and color. Overall form of the tree can vary from upright to weeping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_palmatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:224px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg/222px-Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg/333px-Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg/444px-Acer_palmatum_BotGartenMuenster_Faecherahorn_6691.jpg 2x" width="222"/></a>

<p><i>Acer palmatum</i> has been cultivated in Japan for centuries and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_zone" title="Temperate zone">temperate</a> areas around the world since the 1800s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup> The first specimen of the tree reached England in 1820.</p>
<p><br/>
When Swedish doctor-botanist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Peter_Thunberg" title="Carl Peter Thunberg">Carl Peter Thunberg</a> traveled in Japan late in the eighteenth century, he secreted out drawings of a small tree that would eventually become synonymous with the high art of oriental gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He gave it the species name <i>palmatum</i> after the hand-like shape of its leaves, similar to the centuries-old Japanese names <i>kaede</i> and <i>momiji</i>, references to the 'hands' of frogs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and babies,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> respectively.</p>
<p>For centuries Japanese horticulturalists have developed cultivars from maples found in Japan and nearby Korea and China. They are a popular choice for <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">bonsai</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ma-Ke_Acer_palmatum_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ma-Ke_Acer_palmatum-12">[12]</a></sup> enthusiasts and have long been a subject in art.</p>
<p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> are currently available commercially and are a popular item at <a href="/wiki/Garden_centre" title="Garden centre">garden centres</a> and other retail stores in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Red-leafed cultivars are the most popular, followed by cascading green shrubs with deeply dissected leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Preparations from the branches and leaves are used as a treatment in traditional Chinese medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growing_conditions">Growing conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_palmatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growing conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acer_palmatum_img.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="2304" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Acer_palmatum_img.jpg/170px-Acer_palmatum_img.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Acer_palmatum_img.jpg/255px-Acer_palmatum_img.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Acer_palmatum_img.jpg/340px-Acer_palmatum_img.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acer_palmatum_img.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fall maples in Nara, Japan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Acer palmatum</i> includes hundreds of named cultivars with a variety forms, colors, leaf types, sizes, and preferred growing conditions. Heights of mature specimens range from 0.5 m to 25 m, depending on type. Some tolerate sun, but most prefer part shade, especially in hotter climates. Almost all are adaptable and blend well with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_plant" title="Companion plant">companion plants</a>. The trees are particularly suitable for borders and ornamental paths because the root systems are compact and not invasive. Many varieties of <i>Acer palmatum</i> are successfully grown in containers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Trees are prone to die during periods of drought and prefer consistent water conditions; more established trees are less prone to drought. Trees should be mulched with a thick layer of bark. Well-drained soil is essential as they will not survive in soggy waterlogged soil. Trees do not require or appreciate heavy fertilization and should only be lightly fertilized, preferably using slow-release fertilizer with a 3 to 1 ratio of nitrogen to phosphorus respectively. Nitrogen lawn fertilizer should be avoided in the immediate vicinity of these trees as excessive nitrogen can cause overly vigorous growth that is prone to pathogens.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pruning">Pruning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_palmatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pruning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">Bonsai</a></div>
<p>If space is not a constraint, no pruning is necessary except to remove any dead branches. Trees naturally self-prune foliage that doesn't receive enough light, such as internal branches which are overly shaded by its own canopy. Some growers prefer to shape their trees artistically or to thin out interior branches to better expose the graceful main branches. The form of the tree, especially without leaves in winter, can be of great interest and can be pruned to highlight this feature. Trees heal readily after pruning without needing aftercare. This species should not be pruned like a hedge, but instead methodically shaped by carefully choosing individual branches to remove. They can also be pruned just to maintain a smaller size to suit a particular location. <i>Acer palmatum</i> can also be used as <a href="/wiki/Espalier" title="Espalier">espalier</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivars">Cultivars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_palmatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultivars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> have been chosen for particular characteristics, which are <a href="/wiki/Plant_propagation" title="Plant propagation">propagated</a> by asexual reproduction most often by <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">grafting</a>, but some cultivars can also be propagated by <a href="/wiki/Shield_budding" title="Shield budding">budding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">cuttings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tissue_culture" title="Tissue culture">tissue culture</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Layering" title="Layering">layering</a>. Some cultivars are not in cultivation in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> or have been lost over the generations, but many new cultivars are developed each decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup> Cultivars are chosen for <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypical</a> aspects such as leaf shape and size (shallowly to deeply lobed, some also palmately compound), leaf color (ranging from chartreuse through dark green or from red to dark purple, others <a href="/wiki/Variegation" title="Variegation">variegated</a> with various patterns of white and pink), bark texture and color, and growth pattern. Most cultivars are less vigorous and smaller than is typical for the species, but are more interesting than the relatively mundane species. Cultivars come in a large variety of forms including upright, broom, weeping, cascading, dwarf, and shrub. Most cultivars are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_selection" title="Artificial selection">artificially selected</a> from seedlings of open-pollinated plants, purposeful breeding is not common, and less often from grafts of <a href="/wiki/Witch%27s_broom" title="Witch's broom">witch's brooms</a>.</p>

<p>In Japan, <i>iromomiji</i> is used as an accent tree in Japanese gardens, providing gentle shade next to the house in the summer and beautiful colors in autumn. Many cultivars have characteristics that come into prominence during different seasons, including the color of new or mature leaves, extraordinary autumn color, color and shape of samaras, or even bark that becomes more brightly colored during the winter. Some cultivars can scarcely be distinguished from others unless labeled. In some cases, identical cultivars go by different names, while in other cases, different cultivars may be given the same name.</p>

<p>In addition to the cultivars described above, a number of <a href="/wiki/Cultivar_group" title="Cultivar group">cultivar groups</a> have been naturally selected over time to such an extent that seedlings often resemble the parent. Many of these are sold under the same name as the cultivars, or even propagated by grafting, so there is often much ambiguity in distinguishing them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup> In particular, a number of dark-red <i>Acer palmatum</i> are sold with the names "Atropurpureum" and "Bloodgood." Many different cultivars with delicate lace-like foliage are sold under names such as "Dissectum", "Filigree" and "Laceleaf.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species">Similar species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_palmatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Similar species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Given that these maples are phenotypically variable within each species, and may <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridise</a> with one another, distinguishing between them may be a matter of <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">gradient speciation</a>. In commercial propagation, <i>A. palmatum</i> is often used as <a href="/wiki/Rootstock" title="Rootstock">rootstock</a> for many of these other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelderen_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-gelderen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
