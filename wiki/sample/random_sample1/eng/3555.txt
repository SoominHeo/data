<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Month</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>month</b> is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, used with <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendars</a>, which is approximately as long as a natural <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">period</a> related to the motion of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>; <i>month</i> and <i>Moon</i> are <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognates</a>. The traditional concept arose with the cycle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_phase" title="Moon phase">moon phases</a>; such months (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">lunations</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month" title="Lunar month">synodic months</a> and last approximately 29.53 <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a>. From excavated <a href="/wiki/Tally_stick" title="Tally stick">tally sticks</a>, researchers have deduced that people counted days in relation to the Moon's phases as early as the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> age. Synodic months, based on the Moon's <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> with respect to the Earth-Sun line, are still the basis of many calendars today, and are used to divide the <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_months_in_astronomy">Types of months in astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of months in astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">Lunar month</a></div>
<p>The following types of months are mainly of significance in astronomy, most of them (but not the distinction between sidereal and tropical months) first recognized in <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory#Babylon" title="Lunar theory">Babylonian lunar astronomy</a>.</p>
<ol>
<li>The <b>sidereal month</b> is defined as the Moon's <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> in a non-rotating frame of reference (which on average is equal to its <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">rotation period</a> in the same frame). It is about 27.32166 days (27 days, 7 hours, 43 minutes, 11.6 seconds). The exact duration of the orbital period cannot be easily determined, because the 'non-rotating frame of reference' cannot be observed directly. However, it is approximately equal to the time it takes the Moon to pass twice a "fixed" <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> (different stars give different results because all have proper motions and are not really fixed in position).</li>
<li>A <b>synodic month</b> is the most familiar lunar cycle, defined as the time interval between two consecutive occurrences of a particular phase (such as new moon or full moon) as seen by an observer on Earth. The mean length of the synodic month is 29.53059 days (29 days, 12 hours, 44 minutes, 2.8 seconds). Due to the eccentricity of the lunar orbit around Earth (and to a lesser degree, the Earth’s elliptical orbit around the Sun), the length of a synodic month can vary by up to seven hours.</li>
<li>The <b>tropical month</b> is the average time for the Moon to pass twice through the same <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a> point of the sky. It is <b>27.32158</b> days, very slightly shorter than the sidereal month (27.32166) days, because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_of_the_equinoxes" title="Precession of the equinoxes">precession of the equinoxes</a>. Unlike the sidereal month, it can be measured precisely.</li>
<li>An <b>anomalistic month</b> is the average time the Moon takes to go from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> to perigee - the point in the Moon's orbit when it is closest to Earth. An anomalistic month is about 27.55455 days on average.</li>
<li>The <b>draconic month</b>, draconitic month, or nodal month is the period in which the Moon returns to the same <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">node</a> of its orbit; the nodes are the two points where the Moon's orbit crosses the plane of the Earth's orbit. Its duration is about 27.21222 days on average.</li>
</ol>
<p>A synodic month is longer than a sidereal month because the Earth-Moon system is orbiting the Sun in the same direction as the Moon is orbiting the Earth. The Sun moves eastward with respect to the stars (as does the Moon) and it takes about 2.2 days longer for the Moon to return to the same apparent position with respect to the Sun.</p>
<p>An anomalistic month is longer than a sidereal month because the perigee moves in the same direction as the Moon is orbiting the Earth, one revolution in nine years. Therefore, the Moon takes a little longer to return to perigee than to return to the same star.</p>
<p>A draconic month is shorter than a sidereal month because the nodes move in the opposite direction as the Moon is orbiting the Earth, one revolution in 18.6 years. Therefore, the Moon returns to the same node slightly earlier than it returns to the same star.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calendrical_consequences">Calendrical consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Calendrical consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar calendar</a>.</div>
<p>At the simplest level, most well-known lunar calendars are based on the initial approximation that 2 lunations last 59 days: a 30-day <b>full month</b> followed by a 29-day <b>hollow month</b> — but this is only roughly accurate, and eventually needs correction by using larger cycles, or the equivalent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leap_day" title="Leap day">leap days</a>. Additionally, the synodic month does not fit easily into the year, which makes accurate, rule-based lunisolar calendars complicated. The most common solution to this problem is the <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">Metonic cycle</a>, which takes advantage of the fact that 235 lunations are approximately 19 <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical years</a> (which add up to not quite 6940 days). However, a Metonic calendar will drift against the seasons by about 1 day every 200 years. Metonic calendars include the calendar used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antikythera_Mechanism" title="Antikythera Mechanism">Antikythera Mechanism</a> about 2000 years ago, and the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a>.</p>
<p>The complexity required in an accurate lunisolar calendar may explain why <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">solar calendars</a> have months which no longer relate to the phase of the Moon, but are based only on the motion of the Sun relative to the equinoxes and solstices, have generally replaced lunar calendars for civil use in most societies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Months_in_various_calendars">Months in various calendars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Months in various calendars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning_of_the_lunar_month">Beginning of the lunar month</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Beginning of the lunar month">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_calendars" title="Hellenic calendars">Hellenic calendars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew Lunisolar calendar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic Lunar calendar</a> started the month with the first appearance of the thin crescent of the <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">new moon</a>.</p>
<p>However, the motion of the Moon in its <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> is very complicated and its period is not constant. The date and time of this actual observation depends on the exact geographical longitude as well as latitude, atmospheric conditions, the visual acuity of the observers, etc. Therefore, the beginning and lengths of months defined by observation cannot be accurately predicted.</p>
<p>While some like the Jewish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karaite_(Jewish_sect)" title="Karaite (Jewish sect)">Karaites</a> still rely on actual moon observations, most people use the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian solar calendar</a>.</p>

<p>The mean month length of the Gregorian calendar is 30.436875 days.</p>
<p>Months existing in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">Roman calendar</a> in the past include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercalaris" title="Intercalaris">the <i>mensis intercalaris</i></a>, an occasional month after February to realign the calendar.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quintilis" title="Quintilis">Quintilis</a>, renamed to July in honour of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sextilis" title="Sextilis">Sextilis</a>, renamed to August in honour of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The famous <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">mnemonic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Thirty_days_hath_September" title="Thirty days hath September">Thirty days hath September</a></i> is the most<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> common way of teaching the lengths of the months in the English-speaking world.</p>
<p>Also, note that any five consecutive months (not including February) contain 153 days.</p>
<p>The knuckles of the four fingers of one's hand and the spaces between them can be used to remember the lengths of the months. By making a fist, each month will be listed as one proceeds across the hand. All months landing on a knuckle are 31 days long and those landing between them are not. When the knuckle of the index finger is reached (July), go back to the first knuckle (or over to the first knuckle on the other fist, held next to the first) and continue with August. This physical mnemonic has been taught to primary school students for many decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>This cyclical pattern of month lengths matches the <a href="/wiki/Musical_keyboard" title="Musical keyboard">musical keyboard</a> alternation of white and black keys (with the note 'F' correlating to the month of January).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Calends.2C_nones.2C_and_ides">Calends, nones, and ides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Calends, nones, and ides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ides_(calendar)" title="Ides (calendar)">ides</a></b> occur on the thirteenth day in eight of the months, but in March, May, July, and October, they occur on the fifteenth. The <b><a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar#Months" title="Roman calendar">nones</a></b> always occur 8 days (one Roman week) before the ides, i.e., on the fifth or the seventh. The <b><a href="/wiki/Calends" title="Calends">calends</a></b> are always the first day of the month, and before Julius Caesar's reform fell sixteen days (two Roman weeks) after the ides (except the ides of February and the intercalary month).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD_calendar">Bahá'í calendar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bahá'í calendar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_calendar" title="Bahá'í calendar">Bahá'í calendar</a> is the calendar used by the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>. It is a solar calendar with regular years of 365 days, and <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">leap years</a> of 366 days. Years are composed of 19 months of 19 days each (361 days), plus an extra period of "<a href="/wiki/Ayy%C3%A1m-i-H%C3%A1" title="Ayyám-i-Há">Intercalary Days</a>" (4 in regular and 5 in leap years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bne-3">[3]</a></sup> The months are named after the attributes of God. Days of the year begin and end at sundown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bne-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic_calendar">Germanic calendar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Germanic calendar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Germanic_calendar" title="Germanic calendar">Germanic calendar</a></div>
<p>The old Icelandic calendar is not in official use anymore, but some Icelandic holidays and annual feasts are still calculated from it. It has 12 months, broken down into two groups of six often termed "winter months" and "summer months". The calendar is peculiar in that the months always start on the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weekday" title="Weekday">weekday</a> rather than on the same <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">date</a>. Hence Þorri always starts on a Friday sometime between January 22 and January 28 <i><small>(<a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Old style</a>: January 9 to January 15)</small></i>, Góa always starts on a Sunday between February 21 and February 27 <i><small>(<a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Old style</a>: February 8 to February 14)</small></i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_English_calendar">Old English calendar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Old English calendar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like the Old Norse calendar, the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> had their own calendar before they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christianized" title="Christianized">Christianized</a> which reflected native traditions and deities. These months were attested by <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> in his works <i>On Chronology</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Reckoning_of_Time" title="The Reckoning of Time">The Reckoning of Time</a></i> written in the 8th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His months are probably those as written in the <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_dialect" title="Northumbrian dialect">Northumbrian dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> which he was familiar with. The months were so named after the moon; the new moon marking the end of an old month and start of a new month; the <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a> occurring in the middle of the month, after which the month was named.</p>

<p>The ancient civil Egyptian calendar had a year that was 365 days long and was divided into 12 months of 30 days each, plus 5 extra days (epagomenes) at the end of the year. The months were divided into 3 "weeks" of ten days each. Because the ancient Egyptian year was almost a quarter of a day shorter than the solar year and stellar events "wandered" through the calendar, it is referred to as Annus Vagus or "Wandering Year".</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nisga%27a" title="Nisga'a">Nisga'a</a> calendar coincides with the Gregorian calendar with each month referring to the type of harvesting that is done during the month.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li>Xsaak = To Eat Oolichans - Oolichans are harvested during this month</li>
<li>Mmaal = Canoes - The river has defrosted, hence canoes are used once more</li>
<li>Yansa'alt = Leaves are Blooming - Warm weather has arrived and leaves on the trees begin to bloom</li>
<li>Miso'o = Sockeye - majority of Sockeye Salmon runs begin this month</li>
<li>Maa'y = Berries - berry picking season</li>
<li>Wii Hoon = Great Salmon - referring to the abundance of Salmon that are now running</li>
<li>Genuugwwikw = Trail of the Marmot - Marmots, Ermines and animals as such are hunted</li>
<li>Xlaaxw = To Eat Trout - trout are mostly eaten this time of year</li>
<li>Gwilatkw = To Blanket - The earth is "blanketed" with snow</li>
<li>Luut'aa = Sit In - the Sun "sits" in one spot for a period of time</li>
</ol>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Republican_calendar" title="French Republican calendar">French Republican calendar</a></div>
<p>This calendar was proposed during the French Revolution, and used by the French government for about twelve years from late 1793. There were twelve months of 30 days each, grouped into three ten-day weeks called <i>décades</i>. The five or six extra days needed to approximate the tropical year were placed after the months at the end of each year. A period of four years ending on a leap day was to be called a <i>Franciade</i>. It began at the autumn equinox:</p>
