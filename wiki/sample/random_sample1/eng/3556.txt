<p>The <b>Zeiformes</b> are a small <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> of marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray-finned_fish" title="Ray-finned fish">ray-finned fishes</a> most notable for the dories, a group of common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_fish" title="Food fish">food fish</a>. The order consists of about 33 species in seven families, mostly deep-sea types.</p>
<p>Zeiform bodies are usually thin and deep. Mouths are large, with distensible jaws, and there is no orbitosphenoid. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">Pelvic fins</a> have 5-10 soft rays and possibly a spine, 5-10 <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> spines and up to 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a> spines. They range in size from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dwarf_Dory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dwarf Dory (page does not exist)">Dwarf Dory</a> <i>(Macrurocyttus acanthopodus)</i>, at 43 millimetres (1.7 in) in length, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Dory" title="Cape Dory">Cape Dory</a> <i>(Zeus capensis)</i>, which measures up to 90 centimetres (35 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The boarfishes (<b><a href="/wiki/Caproidae" title="Caproidae">Caproidae</a></b>) have been included in this order though they are currently included in the <a href="/wiki/Perciformes" title="Perciformes">Perciformes</a>.</p>
