<p><b>Dalyan</b> is a town in <a href="/wiki/Mu%C4%9Fla_Province" title="Muğla Province">Muğla Province</a> located between the well-known districts of <a href="/wiki/Marmaris" title="Marmaris">Marmaris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fethiye" title="Fethiye">Fethiye</a> on the south-west coast of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. The town is an independent municipality, within the administrative district of Ortaca.</p>
<p>Dalyan achieved international fame in 1987 when developers wanted to build a luxury hotel on the nearby <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0ztuzu_Beach" title="İztuzu Beach">İztuzu Beach</a>, a breeding ground for the endangered <a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_sea_turtle" title="Loggerhead sea turtle">loggerhead sea turtle</a> species. The incident created major international storm when <a href="/wiki/David_Bellamy" title="David Bellamy">David Bellamy</a> championed the cause of conservationists such as <a href="/wiki/June_Haimoff" title="June Haimoff">June Haimoff</a>, Peter Günther, Nergis Yazgan, <a href="/wiki/Lily_Venizelos" title="Lily Venizelos">Lily Venizelos</a> and Keith Corbett. The development project was temporarily stopped after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Philip" title="Prince Philip">Prince Philip</a> called for a moratorium and in 1988 the beach and its hinterland were declared a protected area, viz. <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6yce%C4%9Fiz-Dalyan_Special_Environmental_Protection_Area" title="Köyceğiz-Dalyan Special Environmental Protection Area">Köyceğiz-Dalyan Special Environmental Protection Area</a>.</p>
<p>Life in Dalyan revolves around the Dalyan Çayı River which flows past the town. The boats that ply up and down the river, navigating the maze of reeds, are the preferred means of transport to all the local sites.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalyan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg/220px-River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg/330px-River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg/440px-River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:River_Dalyan_RB3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
River Dalyan</div>

<p>Dalyan means "<a href="/wiki/Fishing_weir" title="Fishing weir">fishing weir</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>. Bass, Mullet and Sea Bream swim upstream from the sea to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6yce%C4%9Fiz_Lake" title="Köyceğiz Lake">Köyceğiz Lake</a> where another large town of the region, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6yce%C4%9Fiz" title="Köyceğiz">Köyceğiz</a>, is located. The fish spawn there, and when returning to the sea they are caught in the "dalyans".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalyan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to its attraction as a tourist destination, the region around Dalyan is a highly fertile and productive agricultural zone. Cotton used to be grown intensively, but has now largely been replaced by pomegranate. In the area around Köyceğiz many other fruits (citrus fruits) and vegetables are grown, which are all on display in the market on Saturdays, the day when villagers come from miles around to sell their products.</p>

<p>Above the river's sheer cliffs are the weathered façades of <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">Lycian</a> tombs cut from rock, circa 400 BC. The ruins of the ancient trading city of <a href="/wiki/Kaunos" title="Kaunos">Kaunos</a> are a short boat trip across the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The south of Dalyan on the Mediterranean coast, lies <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0ztuzu_Beach" title="İztuzu Beach">İztuzu Beach</a>, near the village of the same name is a popular area for sunbathing and swimming. There are regular boat and minibus (<a href="/wiki/Dolmu%C5%9F" title="Dolmuş">dolmuş</a>) services to the beach. Visitors should be aware of the wooden stakes in the beach to mark nesting sites. The road route is particularly scenic, offering views of Sülüngür Lake. In 2008 İztuzu Beach was proclaimed winner in the category <i>Best Open Space</i> (Europe) by The Times because of the eco-friendly exploitation of the beach. In 2011 Dalyan and İztuzu Beach were proclaimed <i>Best Beach Destination</i> of Europe by Dutch holiday assessment website Zoover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalyan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The beach is well known for the <i>Caretta caretta</i> (<a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_sea_turtle" title="Loggerhead sea turtle">Loggerhead sea turtle</a>), an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a> that has existed for about 45 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> International animal protection organizations monitor and protect the turtles' nesting grounds in Turkey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The beach is closed between 20.00 and 8:00 hours during the period that the turtles lay their eggs and hatchlings go out to sea (May to October).</p>
