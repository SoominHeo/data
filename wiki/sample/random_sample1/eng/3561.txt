<p>The <b>lunar nodes</b> are the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">orbital nodes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, that is, the points where the <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">orbit of the Moon</a> crosses the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>. The <b>ascending</b> or <b>north node</b> is where the moon crosses from south of the ecliptic to north of the ecliptic. The <b>descending</b> or <b>south node</b> is where it crosses from north of the ecliptic to south of the ecliptic.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">Eclipses</a> occur only near the lunar nodes: <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">Solar eclipses</a> occur when the passage of the Moon through a node coincides with the <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">new moon</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipses</a> occur when passage coincides with the <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a>. A lunar eclipse may occur if there is a full moon within 11° 38' (Celestial Longitude), of a node, and a solar eclipse may occur if there is a new moon within 17° 25' of a node.</p>
<p>The plane of the lunar orbit <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">precesses</a> in space and hence the lunar nodes <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">precess</a> around the ecliptic, completing a revolution (called a <i>draconic</i> or <i>nodal period</i>) in 6798.3835 days or 18.612958 years (note that this is not the same length as a <a href="/wiki/Saros_(astronomy)" title="Saros (astronomy)">saros</a>). The same cycle, measured against an inertial frame of reference such as <a href="/wiki/International_Celestial_Reference_System" title="International Celestial Reference System">ICRS</a> (relative to the stars) is 18.599525 years.</p>
<p>The two solar eclipses in July 2000 (<a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_1,_2000" title="Solar eclipse of July 1, 2000">Solar eclipse of July 1, 2000</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_31,_2000" title="Solar eclipse of July 31, 2000">Solar eclipse of July 31, 2000</a>) were around the time that the moon was at its ascending node. Ascending-node eclipses recur after one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draconic_year" title="Draconic year">draconic year</a> on average, which is about 0.94901 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_year" title="Gregorian year">Gregorian years</a>, and the same goes for descending-node eclipses.</p>

<p>The nodes are called by different names in different areas of the world.</p>
<p>Since the ascending node is the point of intersection between the ecliptic and the plane of the lunar orbit where the Moon is ascending from the South to the North, it is sometimes called the North node. In ancient European texts, it is referred to as the <b>dragon's head</b> (<i>Caput Draconis</i>, or <i>Anabibazon</i>). The symbol of the ascending node is <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:U%2B260A.svg"><img alt="U+260A.svg" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="180" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/U%2B260A.svg/16px-U%2B260A.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/U%2B260A.svg/24px-U%2B260A.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/U%2B260A.svg/32px-U%2B260A.svg.png 2x" width="16"/></a>, the <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrological</a> symbol for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_(mythology)" title="Dragon (mythology)">Dragon</a>'s head. Similarly the descending node is the point where the Moon is descending from the North hemisphere to South hemisphere, and is sometimes referred to as South node. It is also known as the <b>dragon's tail</b> (<i>Cauda Draconis</i>, or <i>Catabibazon</i>), and its symbol is the inversion of that of the ascending node: <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:U%2B260B.svg"><img alt="U+260B.svg" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="180" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/U%2B260B.svg/16px-U%2B260B.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/U%2B260B.svg/24px-U%2B260B.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/U%2B260B.svg/32px-U%2B260B.svg.png 2x" width="16"/></a>. Note that the so-called North node may in fact lie South of the South node in the course of the nodal cycle.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu_astronomy" title="Hindu astronomy">Hindu astronomy</a>, the ascending node ☊ is called <i><a href="/wiki/Rahu" title="Rahu">Rahu</a></i> and the descending node ☋ is called <i><a href="/wiki/Ketu_(mythology)" title="Ketu (mythology)">Ketu</a></i>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetan_Astrology" title="Tibetan Astrology">Tibetan Astrology</a> (partially based on the <a href="/wiki/Kalachakra#Astrology" title="Kalachakra">Kalachakra Tantra</a>) the southern node is named Kalagni.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_declinations">Extreme declinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_node&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extreme declinations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lunar_standstill" title="Lunar standstill">Lunar standstill</a></div>
<p>The lunar orbit is inclined by about 5.1 degrees to the ecliptic: hence the Moon can be up to about 5 degrees north or south of the ecliptic. The ecliptic is inclined by about 23.4° to the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a>, the plane that is perpendicular to the rotational axis of the Earth. As a consequence, once during the 18.6-year nodal period, when the ascending node of the Moon's orbit coincides with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">vernal equinox</a>, then the Moon reaches extreme northern and southern <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declinations</a>. Then it also has its extreme northern and southern <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a> points of rising and setting on the horizon; its extreme lowest and highest <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> when crossing the <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">meridian</a>; and potentially extreme late first sightings of the <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">new moon</a>. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultations</a> by the Moon of the star group the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a>, which are over 4° North of the ecliptic, occur during a comparatively brief period once every nodal period.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrological_significance">Astrological significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_node&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Astrological significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_astrology" title="Western astrology">Western astrology</a> the ascending and descending nodes are respectively known as the "North Node" and the "South Node". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Only the north node is usually marked in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horoscopes" title="Horoscopes">horoscopes</a>, as the south node is by definition at the opposite point in the chart.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vedic_astrology" title="Vedic astrology">Vedic astrology</a>, the north and south nodes are called Rahu and Ketu respectively, and both are marked in the chart.</p>
