<p><b>Spiny lobsters</b>, also known as <b>langouste</b> or <b>rock lobsters</b>, are a family (<b>Palinuridae</b>) of about 60 species of <a href="/wiki/Achelata" title="Achelata">achelate</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">Decapoda</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reptantia" title="Reptantia">Reptantia</a>. Spiny lobsters are also, especially in Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, and the Bahamas, sometimes called <b>crayfish</b>, <b>sea crayfish</b>, or <b>crawfish</b> ("kreef" in South Africa), terms which elsewhere are reserved for <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">freshwater crayfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_lobster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Furry_lobster" title="Furry lobster">furry lobsters</a> (<i>e.g.</i> <i>Palinurellus</i>) were previously separated into a family of their own, the Synaxidae, but are usually considered members of the Palinuridae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palero_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palero-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Slipper_lobster" title="Slipper lobster">slipper lobsters</a> (Scyllaridae) are their next-closest relatives, and these two or three families make up the <a href="/wiki/Achelata" title="Achelata">Achelata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palero_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Palero-2">[2]</a></sup> Genera of spiny lobsters include <i>Palinurus</i> and a number of anagrams thereof:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Panulirus</i>, <i>Linuparus</i>, <i>etc.</i> (<a href="/wiki/Palinurus" title="Palinurus">Palinurus</a> was also a helmsman in <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%86neid" title="Æneid">Æneid</a></i>.) In total, 12 extant genera are recognised, containing around 60 living species:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahyong_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahyong-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_lobster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jasus_edwardsii.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Jasus_edwardsii.jpg/220px-Jasus_edwardsii.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Jasus_edwardsii.jpg/330px-Jasus_edwardsii.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Jasus_edwardsii.jpg/440px-Jasus_edwardsii.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jasus_edwardsii.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Jasus_edwardsii" title="Jasus edwardsii">Jasus edwardsii</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although they superficially resemble <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">true lobsters</a> in terms of overall shape and having a hard <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>, the two groups are not closely related. Spiny lobsters can be easily distinguished from true lobsters by their very long, thick, spiny <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a>, by the lack of <a href="/wiki/Chela_(organ)" title="Chela (organ)">chelae</a> (claws) on the first four pairs of walking legs, although the females of most species have a small claw on the fifth pair,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and by a particularly specialized larval phase called <a href="/wiki/Phyllosoma" title="Phyllosoma">phyllosoma</a>. True lobsters have much smaller antennae and claws on the first three pairs of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">legs</a>, with the first being particularly enlarged.</p>
<p>Spiny lobsters have typically a slightly compressed carapace, lacking any lateral ridges. Their <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)#Crustaceans" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a> lack a scaphocerite, the flattened exopod of the antenna. This is fused to the epistome (a plate between the <a href="/wiki/Labrum_(arthropod_mouthpart)" title="Labrum (arthropod mouthpart)">labrum</a> and the basis of the antenna). The flagellum, at the top of the antenna, is stout, tapering, and very long. The ambulatory legs (<a href="/wiki/Decapod_anatomy" title="Decapod anatomy">pereopods</a>) end in claws (chelae).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_lobster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> of spiny lobsters has been extended by the discovery in 1995 of a 110-million-year-old fossil near <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=El_Espi%C3%B1al&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="El Espiñal (page does not exist)">El Espiñal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Workers from the <a href="/wiki/National_Autonomous_University_of_Mexico" title="National Autonomous University of Mexico">National Autonomous University of Mexico</a> have named the fossil <i>Palinurus palaecosi</i>, and report that it is closest to members of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Palinurus_(genus)" title="Palinurus (genus)">Palinurus</a></i> currently living off the coasts of Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_lobster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Spiny lobsters are found in almost all warm seas, including the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, but are particularly common in <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, where they are referred to commonly as <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a> or sea crayfish (<i><a href="/wiki/Jasus_edwardsii" title="Jasus edwardsii">Jasus edwardsii</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Jasus_lalandii" title="Jasus lalandii">Jasus lalandii</a></i>).</p>
<p>Spiny lobsters tend to live in crevices of rocks and <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a>, only occasionally venturing out at night to seek <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clam" title="Clam">clams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aplysiomorpha" title="Aplysiomorpha">sea-hares</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derby.2C_Kicklighter_et_al_.282007.29_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Derby.2C_Kicklighter_et_al_.282007.29-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a> to eat. Sometimes, they migrate in very large groups in long files of lobsters across the sea floor. These lines may be more than 50 lobsters long. Spiny lobsters navigate using the smell and taste of natural substances in the water that change in different parts of the ocean. It was recently discovered that spiny lobsters can also navigate by detecting the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> They keep together by contact, using their long antennae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a> may be deterred from eating spiny lobsters by a loud screech made by the antennae of the spiny lobsters rubbing against a smooth part of the <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Spiny lobsters usually exhibit the social habit of being together. However recent studies indicate that healthy lobsters move away from infected ones, leaving the diseased lobsters to fend for themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like true lobsters, spiny lobsters are edible and are an economically significant food source; they are the biggest food export of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, for instance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sound">Sound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_lobster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many spiny lobsters produce <a href="/wiki/Rasp" title="Rasp">rasping</a> sounds to repel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a> by rubbing the "<a href="/wiki/Plectrum" title="Plectrum">plectrum</a>" at the base of the spiny lobster's antennae against a "<a href="/wiki/File_(tool)" title="File (tool)">file</a>". The noise is produced by frictional vibrations - sticking and slipping, similar to rubber materials sliding against hard surfaces. While a number of insects use frictional vibration mechanisms to generate sound, this particular acoustic mechanism is unique in the animal kingdom. Significantly, the system does not rely on the hardness of the exoskeleton, as many other <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a> sounds do, meaning that the spiny lobsters can continue to produce the deterrent noises even in the period following a <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moult</a> when they are most vulnerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The stridulating organ is present in all but three genera in the family (<i><a href="/wiki/Jasus" title="Jasus">Jasus</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Projasus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Projasus (page does not exist)">Projasus</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Furry_lobster" title="Furry lobster">furry lobster</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palinurellus" title="Palinurellus">Palinurellus</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and its form can distinguish different species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
