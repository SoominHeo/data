<p><b>Dango</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9B%A3%E5%AD%90" title="wikt:団子">団子</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Dumpling" title="Dumpling">dumpling</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweet" title="Sweet">sweet</a> made from <i>mochiko</i> (<a href="/wiki/Rice_flour" title="Rice flour">rice flour</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sasaki_McCabe_Iwasaki_2002_p._461_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sasaki_McCabe_Iwasaki_2002_p._461-1">[1]</a></sup> related to <i><a href="/wiki/Mochi" title="Mochi">mochi</a></i>. It is often served with <a href="/wiki/Green_tea" title="Green tea">green tea</a>. <i>Dango</i> is eaten year-round, but the different varieties are traditionally eaten in given seasons. Three to four <i>dango</i> are often served on a <a href="/wiki/Skewer" title="Skewer">skewer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dango&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many different varieties of <i>dango</i> which are usually named after the various seasonings served on or with it.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://theanimeblog.com/japanese-recipes/dango-digest-a-thorough-look-at-japanese-dumplings-part-two/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Red_bean_paste" title="Red bean paste">Anko</a></i>: Commonly known as (sweetened) red bean paste, while ingredients other than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azuki" title="Azuki">azuki</a> are used on rare occasions.</li>
<li><i>Chadango</i>: Green-tea flavored <i>dango</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Bocchan dango</i>: Dango that has three colors. One is colored by red beans, the second by eggs, and the third by green tea.</li>
<li><i>Denpun dango</i>: Variety of dango from <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Flour#Other_flours" title="Flour">potato flour</a> and baked with sweet boiled beans.</li>
<li><i>Kuri dango</i>: Dango coated in chestnut paste.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chichi_dango" title="Chichi dango">Chichi dango</a></i>: Slightly sweet light treats usually eaten as a dessert.</li>
<li><i>Hanami dango</i>: Also has three colors, Hanami dango is traditionally made during Sakura-viewing season. Hence the name <a href="/wiki/Hanami" title="Hanami">Hanami</a> (<i>Hanami</i> means "flower viewing"; <i>hana</i> meaning "flower", and <i>mi</i> meaning "to see").</li>
<li><i>Goma</i>: <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">Sesame</a> seeds. It is both sweet and salty.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kibi_dango_(millet_dumpling)" title="Kibi dango (millet dumpling)">Kibi dango</a></i>: Dango made with <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a> flour. This variety is prominently featured in the tale of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Momotaro" title="Momotaro">Momotaro</a>, a folkloric Japanese hero, who offers the rounded ball (not skewered) to three talking animals in exchange for their aid in fighting demons.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kinako" title="Kinako">Kinako</a></i>: A toasted soy flour.</li>
<li><i>Kushi dango</i>: Dango held by a <a href="/wiki/Skewer" title="Skewer">skewer</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mitarashi_dango" title="Mitarashi dango">Mitarashi</a></i>: Covered with a syrup made from <i><a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">shouyu</a></i> (soy sauce), <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Meatball" title="Meatball">Nikudango</a></i>: A type of meatball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Teppanyaki" title="Teppanyaki">Teppanyaki</a></i>: Dango on a <a href="/wiki/Skewer" title="Skewer">skewer</a> with a tangy <a href="/wiki/Teppanyaki" title="Teppanyaki">teppanyaki</a> taste.</li>
<li><i>Sasa dango</i>: Dango that is produced and eaten primarily in <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Prefecture" title="Niigata Prefecture">Niigata Prefecture</a>. Sasa dango has two varieties: <i>onna dango</i> and <i>otoko dango</i>. Onna Dango (literally "female dango") is filled with <a href="/wiki/Red_bean_paste" title="Red bean paste">anko</a>, while the otoko dango (literally "male dango") is filled with <a href="/wiki/Kinpira" title="Kinpira">kinpira</a>. The dango is wrapped in leaves of <a href="/wiki/Sasa_(plant)" title="Sasa (plant)">sasa</a> for the purpose of preservation.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derived_terms">Derived terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dango&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Derived terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A common <a href="/wiki/Japanese_proverbs" title="Japanese proverbs">Japanese proverb</a> "hana yori dango" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%8A%B1%E3%82%88%E3%82%8A%E5%9B%A3%E5%AD%90" title="wikt:花より団子">花より団子</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, literally, "dumplings rather than flowers")</span> refers to a preference for practical things rather than aesthetics.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hairstyle" title="Hairstyle">hairstyle</a> consisting of dango-like buns on either side of the head is sometimes known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odango_(hairstyle)" title="Odango (hairstyle)"><i>odango</i></a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dorodango" title="Dorodango">Dorodango</a> is a Japanese art form in which earth and water are molded to create a delicate shiny sphere, resembling a billiard ball.</p>
