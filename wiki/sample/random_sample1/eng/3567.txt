<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dangyuja</h1>

<p><b>Dangyuja</b> (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">당유자</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[taŋ.ju.dʑa]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> non-hybrid <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> that is a specialty of <a href="/wiki/Jejudo" title="Jejudo">Jeju Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Jeju_language" title="Jeju language">Jeju language</a>, it is called <b>Daengyuji</b>(<span lang="jje" xml:lang="jje">댕유지</span>; <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[tɛŋ.ju.dʑi]</a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Dangyuja has a similar shape and flavour to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuzu" title="Yuzu">yuja</a>, but is genealogically a variety of <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelo</a>.</p>
<p>Dangyuja has been included in the <a href="/wiki/Ark_of_Taste" title="Ark of Taste">Ark of Taste</a>, an international catalogue of endangered heritage foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dangyuja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Broad-leaved_tree" title="Broad-leaved tree">broad-leaved tree</a> grows to an average height of 6 metres (20 ft), with <a href="/wiki/Branch" title="Branch">branches</a> that have <a href="/wiki/Thorns,_spines,_and_prickles" title="Thorns, spines, and prickles">thorns</a> on them, and the <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are 10–13 centimetres (3.9–5.1 in) long. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is 10–12 centimetres (3.9–4.7 in) long, 9–10 centimetres (3.5–3.9 in) wide, and usually weighs 300–500 grams (11–18 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colour of ripe fruit can range from <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_yellow" title="Shades of yellow">dark yellow</a> to <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_orange" title="Shades of orange">yellow-orange</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Peel_(fruit)" title="Peel (fruit)">rind</a> is about 9 millimetres (0.35 in) thick,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> very fragrant, and slightly bitter, whil the flesh and juice is rich in sourness, and also with a unique fragrance.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary">Culinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dangyuja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, the fruit is used mainly for tea, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dangyujacha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dangyujacha (page does not exist)">dangyujacha</a></i>, whose preparation is very similar to that of <i><a href="/wiki/Yujacha" title="Yujacha">yujacha</a></i>, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuzu" title="Yuzu">yuja</a> tea". Back in the days dangyuja was often used in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">home remedies</a> to <a href="/wiki/Preventive_healthcare" title="Preventive healthcare">prevent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Therapy" title="Therapy">treat</a> <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">common cold</a>. A soup called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daengyujikkultang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daengyujikkultang (page does not exist)">daengyujikkultang</a></i> (literally "dangyuja <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> <a href="/wiki/Guk" title="Guk">tang</a>"), was made of the crushed flesh of dangyuja, <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a>. Cooked in the <a href="/wiki/Ash" title="Ash">ashes</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a>, the mixture attains a thin syrup-like consistency. People used the cooled <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daengyujikkultang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daengyujikkultang (page does not exist)">soup</a> to treat <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">cold</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicinal">Medicinal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dangyuja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medicinal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Korean_medicine" title="Traditional Korean medicine">traditional Korean medicine</a>, the fruit is used to treat various gastrointestinal ailments. <i><a href="/wiki/Dongui_Bogam" title="Dongui Bogam">Dongui Bogam</a></i>, an encyclopaedic medical book published in 1613, writes that dangyujas can help <a href="/wiki/Detoxification" title="Detoxification">detoxify</a> and purify the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, treat <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication" title="Alcohol intoxication">alcohol intoxication</a>, and stimulate a poor <a href="/wiki/Appetite" title="Appetite">appetite</a>.</p>
