<p>The <b>OLPC XO</b>, previously known as the <b>$100 Laptop</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc-2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-2007-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>Children's Machine</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Papert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Papert-3">[3]</a></sup> and <b>2B1</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Negropontism_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Negropontism-4">[4]</a></sup> is an inexpensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laptop_computer" title="Laptop computer">laptop computer</a> intended to be distributed to children in developing countries around the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ward-5">[5]</a></sup> to provide them with <a href="/wiki/Access_to_Knowledge_movement" title="Access to Knowledge movement">access to knowledge</a>, and opportunities to "explore, experiment and express themselves" (<a href="/wiki/Constructionism_(learning_theory)" title="Constructionism (learning theory)">constructionist learning</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vision_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vision-6">[6]</a></sup> The XO was developed by Nicholas Negroponte, a co-founder of MIT's Media Lab, and designed by Yves Behar's Fusebox company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> The laptop is manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Quanta_Computer" title="Quanta Computer">Quanta Computer</a> and developed by <a href="/wiki/One_Laptop_per_Child" title="One Laptop per Child">One Laptop per Child</a> (OLPC), a non-profit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)(3)" title="501(c)(3)">501(c)(3)</a> organization.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Subnotebook" title="Subnotebook">subnotebooks</a> are designed for sale to government-education systems which then give each primary school child their own laptop. Pricing was set to start at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">$</a>188 in 2006, with a stated goal to reach the $100 mark in 2008 and the 50-dollar mark by 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TED2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-TED2006-8">[8]</a></sup> When offered for sale in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Give_One_Get_One&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Give One Get One (page does not exist)">Give One Get One</a> campaigns of Q4 2006 and Q4 2007, the laptop was sold at $199.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ways_to_Give_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ways_to_Give-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>These <a href="/wiki/Rugged_computer" title="Rugged computer">rugged</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low-power_electronics" title="Low-power electronics">low-power</a> computers use <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">flash memory</a> instead of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_drive" title="Hard drive">hard drive</a>, and come with an operating system derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fedora_Linux" title="Fedora Linux">Fedora Linux</a> as their pre-installed operating system with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_(GUI)" title="Sugar (GUI)">Sugar</a> <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">GUI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLPC.27s_Software_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-OLPC.27s_Software-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mobile_ad_hoc_network" title="Mobile ad hoc network">Mobile ad hoc networking</a> via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11s" title="802.11s">802.11s</a> <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> <a href="/wiki/Mesh_networking" title="Mesh networking">mesh networking</a> is used to allow many machines to share Internet access as long as at least one of them can see and connect to a router or other access point.</p>
<p>The latest version of the OLPC XO is the XO-4 Touch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO-4_Touch_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO-4_Touch-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first early prototype was unveiled by the project's founder <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Negroponte" title="Nicholas Negroponte">Nicholas Negroponte</a> and then-United Nations Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a> on November 16, 2005, at the <a href="/wiki/World_Summit_on_the_Information_Society" title="World Summit on the Information Society">World Summit on the Information Society</a> (WSIS) in <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>, Tunisia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc20061117_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc20061117-12">[12]</a></sup> The device shown was a rough prototype using a standard development board. Negroponte estimated that the screen alone required three more months of development.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The first working prototype was demonstrated at the project's Country Task Force Meeting on May 23, 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2006 there was a major controversy because Microsoft had suddenly developed an interest in the XO project and wanted the formerly open source effort to run Windows. Negroponte agreed to provide engineer assistance to Microsoft to facilitate their efforts. During this time, the project mission statement changed to remove mentions of "open source". A number of developers, such as <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Krsti%C4%87" title="Ivan Krstić">Ivan Krstić</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bender" title="Walter Bender">Walter Bender</a>, resigned because of these changes in strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Windows_XP_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Windows_XP-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fildes_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fildes-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XP_on_XO_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-XP_on_XO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Approximately 400 developer boards (Alpha-1) were distributed in mid-2006; 875 working prototypes (Beta 1) were delivered in late 2006; 2400 Beta-2 machines were distributed at the end of February 2007;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20061130_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20061130-16">[16]</a></sup> full-scale production started November 6, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melin_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Melin-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quanta_Computer" title="Quanta Computer">Quanta Computer</a>, the project's contract manufacturer, said in February 2007 that it had confirmed orders for one million units. <a href="/wiki/Quanta_Computer" title="Quanta Computer">Quanta</a> indicated that it could ship five million to ten million units that year because seven nations had committed to buy the XO-1 for their schoolchildren: Argentina, Brazil, Libya, Nigeria, Rwanda, Thailand, and Uruguay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDG-2007_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IDG-2007-18">[18]</a></sup> Quanta plans to offer machines very similar to the XO-1 on the open market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arstechnica-2007_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arstechnica-2007-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_Laptop_Per_Child" title="One Laptop Per Child">One Laptop Per Child</a> project originally stated that a consumer version of the XO laptop was not planned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digital50_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-digital50-20">[20]</a></sup> The project later established, in 2007 the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Laptopgiving.org&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Laptopgiving.org (page does not exist)">laptopgiving.org</a> website for outright donations and for a "Give 1 Get 1" offer valid (but only to the United States, its territories, and Canadian addresses) from November 12, 2007 until December 31, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xogiving_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-xogiving-21">[21]</a></sup> For each computer purchased at a cost of $399, an XO is also sent to a child in a developing nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xogiving_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-xogiving-21">[21]</a></sup> OLPC again restarted the G1G1 program through <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> in November 2008, but has since stopped as of December 31 (2008 or 2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystedt_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nystedt-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 20, 2008, OLPC announced the next generation of XO, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OLPC_XO-2" title="OLPC XO-2">OLPC XO-2</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talbot_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talbot-23">[23]</a></sup> which was thereafter cancelled in favor of the <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet-like</a> designed <a href="/wiki/OLPC_XO-3" title="OLPC XO-3">XO-3</a>. In late 2008, the NYC Department of Education began a project to purchase large numbers of XO computers for use by New York schoolchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nysun-2008_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nysun-2008-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design received the Community category award of the 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index:_Award" title="Index: Award">Index: Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Index:_Award_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Index:_Award-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WINNERS_2007_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-WINNERS_2007-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 the XO was awarded London's Design Museum "Design of the Year," plus two gold, one silver, and one bronze award at the Industrial Design Society of America's International Design Excellence Awards (IDEAs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Computer_Aid_International" title="Computer Aid International">Computer Aid International</a>, in a test conducted in April 2009, the device was ranked as the most power saving among other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_and_communication_technologies_in_education" title="Information and communication technologies in education">information and communication technologies in education</a> solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computer_Aid_International_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Computer_Aid_International-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals">Goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OLPC_XO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaptopOLPC_b.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="567" data-file-width="640" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/LaptopOLPC_b.jpg/220px-LaptopOLPC_b.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/LaptopOLPC_b.jpg/330px-LaptopOLPC_b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/LaptopOLPC_b.jpg/440px-LaptopOLPC_b.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaptopOLPC_b.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
OLPC XO-1 <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptop</a> in e-book mode</div>

<p>The XO-1 is designed to be low-cost, small, durable, and efficient. It is shipped with a slimmed-down version of <a href="/wiki/Fedora_(operating_system)" title="Fedora (operating system)">Fedora Linux</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">GUI</a> named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_(GUI)" title="Sugar (GUI)">Sugar</a> that is intended to help young children collaborate. The XO-1 includes a video camera, a microphone, long-range Wi-Fi, and a hybrid <a href="/wiki/Stylus_(computing)" title="Stylus (computing)">stylus</a>/touch pad. In addition to a standard plug-in power supply, human power and solar power sources are available, allowing operation far from a commercial power grid. <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lou_Jepsen" title="Mary Lou Jepsen">Mary Lou Jepsen</a> has listed the design goals of the device as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_Queue_2007_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACM_Queue_2007-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Minimal power consumption, with a design target of 2–3 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a> total power consumption</li>
<li>Minimal production cost, with a target of US$100 per laptop for production runs of millions of units</li>
<li>A "cool" look, implying innovative styling in its physical appearance</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/E-book" title="E-book">e-book</a> functionality with <i>extremely</i> low power consumption</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">Open source</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> provided with the laptop</li>
</ul>
<p>Various use models had been explored by OLPC with the help of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_Continuum" title="Design Continuum">Design Continuum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuseproject" title="Fuseproject">Fuseproject</a>, including: laptop, <a href="/wiki/E-book" title="E-book">e-book</a>, theatre, simulation, tote, and tablet architectures. The current design, by <a href="/wiki/Fuseproject" title="Fuseproject">Fuseproject</a>, uses a transformer hinge to morph between laptop, e-book, and router modes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In keeping with its goals of robustness and low power consumption, the design of the laptop intentionally omits all motor-driven moving parts; it has no <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard drive</a>, no optical (CD/DVD) media, no <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy</a> drives and no fans (the device is passively cooled). An <a href="/wiki/Serial_ATA" title="Serial ATA">ATA</a> interface is unnecessary due to the lack of hard drive. Storage is via an internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SD_card" title="SD card">SD card</a> slot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanders_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders-29">[29]</a></sup> There is also no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_card" title="PC card">PC card</a> slot, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus">USB ports</a> are included.</p>
<p>A built-in hand-<a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">crank</a> generator was part of the notebook in the original design; however, it is now an optional clamp-on peripheral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slimmer_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-slimmer-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Display">Display</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OLPC_XO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Display">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="544" data-file-width="1100" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg/300px-XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg/450px-XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg/600px-XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:XO_screen_01_Pengo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Comparison of the XO-1 display (left) with a typical liquid crystal display (LCD). The images show 1×1 mm of each screen. A typical LCD addresses groups of 3 locations as pixels. The OLPC XO LCD addresses each location as a separate pixel</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>1200×900 7.5 inch (19 cm) diagonal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCD</a> (200 <a href="/wiki/Dots_per_inch" title="Dots per inch">dpi</a>) that uses 0.1 to 1.0 W depending on mode. The two modes are:
<ul>
<li>Reflective (backlight off) monochrome mode for low-power use in sunlight. This mode provides very sharp images for high-quality text</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Backlight" title="Backlight">Backlit</a> color mode, with an alternance of red, green and blue pixels</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>XO 1.75 developmental version for XO-3 has an optional touch screen</li>
</ul>
<p>The first-generation OLPC laptops have a novel low-cost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCD</a>. Later generations of the OLPC laptop are expected to use low-cost, low-power and high-resolution color displays with an appearance similar to <a href="/wiki/Electronic_paper" title="Electronic paper">electronic paper</a>.</p>
<p>The display is the most expensive component in most laptops. In April 2005, Negroponte hired <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lou_Jepsen" title="Mary Lou Jepsen">Mary Lou Jepsen</a>—who was interviewing to join the Media Arts and Sciences faculty at the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab" title="MIT Media Lab">MIT Media Lab</a> in September 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mary_Lou_Jepsen_Bio_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mary_Lou_Jepsen_Bio-31">[31]</a></sup>—as OLPC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Technology_Officer" title="Chief Technology Officer">Chief Technology Officer</a>. Jepsen developed a new display for the first-generation OLPC laptop, inspired by the design of small LCDs used in portable DVD players, which she estimated would cost about $35. In the OLPC XO-1, the screen is estimated to be the second most expensive component (after the CPU and chipset).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texyt_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-texyt-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jepsen has described the removal of the filters that color the RGB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpixel" title="Subpixel">subpixels</a> as the critical design innovation in the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCD</a>. Instead of using subtractive color filters, the display uses a plastic <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">diffraction grating</a> and lenses on the rear of the LCD to illuminate each <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixel</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2008)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:OLPC_XO#Dubious" title="Talk:OLPC XO">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> This grating pattern is stamped using the same technology used to make <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVDs</a>. The grating splits the light from the white backlight into a spectrum. The red, green and blue components are diffracted into the correct positions to illuminate the corresponding pixel with <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">R, G or B</a>. This innovation results in a much brighter display for a given amount of backlight illumination: while the color filters in a regular display typically absorb 85% of the light that hits them, this display absorbs little of that light. Most LCD screens use <a href="/wiki/Cold_cathode" title="Cold cathode">cold cathode</a> <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamp</a> backlights which are fragile, difficult or impossible to repair, require a high voltage power supply, are relatively power-hungry, and account for 50% of the screens' cost (sometimes 60%). The <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> backlight in the XO-1 is easily replaceable, rugged, and inexpensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_Negroponte_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC_Negroponte-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worldchanging_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Worldchanging-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remainder of the LCD uses existing display technology and can be made using existing manufacturing equipment. Even the masks can be made using combinations of existing materials and processes.</p>
<p>When lit primarily from the rear with the white <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> backlight, the display shows a color image composed of both RGB and grayscale information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLPC_Wiki:_Display_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-OLPC_Wiki:_Display-35">[35]</a></sup> When lit primarily from the front by ambient light, for example from the sun, the display shows a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> (black and white) image composed of just the grayscale information.</p>
<p>"Mode" change occurs by varying the relative amounts backlight and ambient light. With more backlight, a higher chrominance is available and a color image display is seen. As ambient light levels, such as sunlight, exceed the backlight, a grayscale display is seen; this can be useful when reading e-books for an extended time in bright light such as sunlight. The backlight brightness can also be adjusted to vary the level of color seen in the display and to conserve battery power.</p>
<p>In color mode (when lit primarily from the rear), the display does not use the common RGB <a href="/wiki/Pixel_geometry" title="Pixel geometry">pixel geometry</a> for liquid crystal computer displays, in which each pixel contains three tall thin rectangles of the primary colors. Instead, the XO-1 display provides one color for each pixel. The colors align along diagonals that run from upper-right to lower left (see diagram on the right). To reduce the color artifacts caused by this pixel geometry, the color component of the image is blurred by the display controller as the image is sent to the screen. Despite the color blurring, the display still has high resolution for its physical size; normal displays as of February 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=OLPC_XO&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> put about 588(H)×441(V) to 882(H)×662(V) pixels in this amount of physical area<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and support <a href="/wiki/Subpixel_rendering" title="Subpixel rendering">subpixel rendering</a> for slightly higher perceived resolution. A Philips Research study measured the XO-1 display's perceived color resolution as effectively 984(H)×738(V).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO_laptop_screen_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO_laptop_screen-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SID08_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-SID08-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livingstone_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livingstone-38">[38]</a></sup> A conventional liquid crystal display with the same number of green pixels (green carries most brightness or luminance information for human eyes) as the OLPC XO-1 would be 693×520.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike a standard RGB LCD, resolution of the XO-1 display varies with angle. Resolution is greatest from upper-right to lower left, and lowest from upper-left to lower-right. Images which approach or exceed this resolution will lose detail and gain color artifacts. The display gains resolution when in bright light; this comes at the expense of color (as the backlight is overpowered) and color resolution can never reach the full 200 dpi sharpness of grayscale mode because of the blur which is applied to images in color mode.</p>

<p>The laptop design specification goals are consumption of about 2 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a> of power during normal use, far less than the 10 W to 45 W of conventional laptops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20061130_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20061130-16">[16]</a></sup> With build 656, power consumption is between 5 and 8 watts measured on G1G1 laptop. Future software builds are expected to meet the 2-watt target.</p>
<p>In e-book mode (XO 1.5), all hardware sub-systems except the monochrome dual-touch display are powered down. When the user moves to a different page, the other systems wake up, render the new page on the display, and then go back to sleep. Power consumption in this e-book mode is estimated to be 0.3 to 0.8 W. The XO 2.0 is planned to consume even less power than earlier versions, less than 1.0 W in full color mode.</p>
<p>Power options include batteries, solar power panels, and human-powered generators, which make the XO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-powered_equipment" title="Self-powered equipment">self-powered equipment</a>. 10 batteries at once can be charged from the school building power in the XO multi-battery charger. The low power consumption combined with these power options are useful in many countries that lack a power infrastructure.</p>

<ul>
<li>Wireless networking using an "Extended Range" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11b/g" title="802.11b/g">802.11b/g</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11s" title="802.11s">802.11s</a> (mesh) <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell</a> 8388 wireless chip, chosen due to its ability to autonomously forward packets in the mesh even if the CPU is powered off. When connected in a mesh, it is run at a low bitrate (2 Mbit/s) to minimize power consumption. Despite the wireless chip's minimalism, it supports <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_Protected_Access" title="Wi-Fi Protected Access">WPA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpa-bug-report_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-wpa-bug-report-41">[41]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/ARM_architecture" title="ARM architecture">ARM</a> processor is included.</li>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11b" title="IEEE 802.11b">IEEE 802.11b</a> support will be provided using a Wi-Fi "Extended Range" chip set. Jepsen has said the wireless chip set will be run at a low bit rate, 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbit/s" title="Mbit/s">Mbit/s</a> maximum rather than the usual higher speed 5.5 Mbit/s or 11 Mbit/s to minimize power consumption. The conventional IEEE 802.11b system only handles traffic within a local cloud of wireless devices in a manner similar to an <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> network. Each node transmits and receives its own data, but it does not route packets between two nodes that cannot communicate directly. The OLPC laptop will use <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11s" title="IEEE 802.11s">IEEE 802.11s</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Wireless_mesh_network" title="Wireless mesh network">wireless mesh network</a>.</p>
<p>Whenever the laptop is powered on it can participate in a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_ad_hoc_network" title="Mobile ad hoc network">mobile ad hoc network (MANET)</a> with each node operating in a <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> fashion with other laptops it can hear, forwarding packets across the cloud<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2009)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>. If a computer in the cloud has access to the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>—either directly or indirectly—then all computers in the cloud are able to share that access. The data rate across this network will not be high; however, similar networks, such as the <a href="/wiki/Store_and_forward" title="Store and forward">store and forward</a> Motoman project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brooke_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brooke-42">[42]</a></sup> have supported email services to 1000 schoolchildren in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, according to Negroponte. The data rate should be sufficient for asynchronous network applications (such as email) to communicate outside the cloud; interactive uses, such as web browsing, or high-bandwidth applications, such as video streaming should be possible inside the cloud. The IP assignment for the meshed network is intended to be automatically configured, so no server administrator or an administration of IP addresses is needed.</p>
<p>Building a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MANET" title="MANET">MANET</a> is still untested under the OLPC's current configuration and hardware environment. Although one goal of the laptop is that all of its software be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a>, the source code for this routing protocol is currently closed source. While there are open-source alternatives such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OLSR" title="OLSR">OLSR</a> or <a href="/wiki/B.A.T.M.A.N." title="B.A.T.M.A.N.">B.A.T.M.A.N.</a>, none of these options is yet available running at the data-link layer (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Layer_2" title="Layer 2">Layer 2</a>) on the Wi-Fi subsystem's co-processor; this is critical to OLPC's power efficiency scheme. Whether <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell Technology Group</a>, the producer of the wireless chip set and owner of the current meshing protocol software, will make the firmware open source is still an unanswered question. As of 2011, it has not done so.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shell">Shell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OLPC_XO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yves_Behar" title="Yves Behar">Yves Behar</a> is the chief designer of the present XO shell. The shell of the laptop is resistant to dirt and moisture, and is constructed with 2 mm thick plastic (50% thicker than typical laptops). It contains a pivoting, reversible display, movable rubber Wi-Fi antennas, and a sealed rubber-membrane keyboard.</p>

<p>More than twenty different keyboards have been laid out, to suit local needs to match the standard keyboard for the country in which a laptop is intended. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_history">Release history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OLPC_XO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Release history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first XO prototype, displayed in 2005, had a built-in hand-crank generator for charging the battery. The XO-1 beta, released in early 2007, used a separate hand-crank generator.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The XO-1 was released in late 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardware_wiki_2007_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardware_wiki_2007-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cl1a_spec_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-cl1a_spec-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Power option: solar panel.</li>
<li>CPU: 433 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a> <a href="/wiki/IA-32" title="IA-32">x86</a> <a href="/wiki/Geode_(processor)" title="Geode (processor)">AMD Geode</a> LX-700 at 0.8 watts, with integrated graphics controller</li>
<li>256 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> of Dual (DDR266) 133 MHz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DRAM" title="DRAM">DRAM</a> (in 2006 the specification called for 128 MB of RAM)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gettysI_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-gettysI-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>1024 <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">kB</a> (1 MB) flash ROM with open-source <a href="/wiki/Open_Firmware" title="Open Firmware">Open Firmware</a></li>
<li>1024 MB of SLC NAND <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">flash memory</a> (in 2006 the specifications called for 512 MB of flash memory)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slimmer_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-slimmer-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Average battery life three hours</li>
</ul>
<p>The XO 1.5 was released in early 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardware_specification_1.5_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardware_specification_1.5-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<li>Fewer physical parts</li>
<li>Lower power consumption</li>
<li>Power option: solar panel.</li>
<li>CPU: 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a> to 1000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a> <a href="/wiki/IA-32" title="IA-32">x86</a> <a href="/wiki/VIA_C7" title="VIA C7">VIA C7</a> at 0.8 watts, with integrated graphics controller</li>
<li>512 to 1024 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> of Dual (DDR266) 133 MHz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DRAM" title="DRAM">DRAM</a></li>

<p>The XO 1.75 began development in 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO_1.75_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO_1.75-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO_1.75_A1_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO_1.75_A1-55">[55]</a></sup> with full production commencing in February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLPC_XO_1.75_C2_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-OLPC_XO_1.75_C2-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<li>Fewer physical parts, 40% lower power consumption.</li>
<li>Power option: solar panel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO-1.75_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO-1.75-57">[57]</a></sup></li>

<li>1024 to 2048 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDR3" title="DDR3">DDR3</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/TBD" title="wikt:TBD">TBD</a>)</li>

<p>The XO 2, previously scheduled for release in 2010, was canceled in favor of XO 3. With a price target $75, it had an elegant, lighter, folding dual touch-screen design. The hardware would have been open-source and sold by various manufacturers. A choice of operating system (Windows XP or Linux) was intended outside the United States. Its $150 price target in the USA includes two computers, one donated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO_2.0_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO_2.0-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/OLPC_XO-3" title="OLPC XO-3">OLPC XO-3</a> was scheduled for release in late 2012. It was canceled in favor of the XO-4. It featured one solid color multi-touch screen design, and a solar panel in the cover or carrying case.</p>
<p>The XO 4 is a refresh of the XO 1 to 1.75 with a later ARM CPU and an optional touch screen. This model will not be available for consumer sales. There is a mini HDMI port to allow connecting to a display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO-4_Touch_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-XO-4_Touch-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The XO Tablet was designed by third-party <a href="/wiki/Vivitar" title="Vivitar">Vivitar</a>, rather than OLPC, and based on the Android platform<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO_Tablet_Review_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO_Tablet_Review-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO_Tablet_Technical_Details_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO_Tablet_Technical_Details-60">[60]</a></sup> whereas all previous XO models were based on Sugar running on top of Fedora. It is commercially available<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introducing_the_XO_tablet_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Introducing_the_XO_tablet-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XO-3_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-XO-3-62">[62]</a></sup> and has been used in OLPC projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labrador_Aboriginal_Youth_Abroad_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Labrador_Aboriginal_Youth_Abroad-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OLPC_XO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_(desktop_environment)" title="Sugar (desktop environment)">Sugar (desktop environment)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OLPC-Frame.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/OLPC-Frame.png/220px-OLPC-Frame.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/OLPC-Frame.png/330px-OLPC-Frame.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/OLPC-Frame.png/440px-OLPC-Frame.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

Mock-up of the "neighborhood view" showing children collaborating on various tasks, within the <a href="/wiki/Mesh_networking" title="Mesh networking">mesh network</a>. By clicking on the icon, communication by Wi-Fi is activated</div>

<p>Countries are expected to remove and add software to best adapt the laptop to the local laws and educational needs. As supplied by OLPC, all of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_software" title="Computer software">software</a> on the laptop will be <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source_software" title="Open source software">open source</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lworld_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-lworld-47">[47]</a></sup> All core software is intended to be localized to the languages of the target countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pootle_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pootle-64">[64]</a></sup> The underlying software<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Software_components_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Software_components-65">[65]</a></sup> includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>A pared-down version of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fedora_(Linux_distribution)" title="Fedora (Linux distribution)">Fedora</a> <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a>, with students receiving <a href="/wiki/Superuser" title="Superuser">root access</a> (although not normally operating in that mode).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gettys2_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-gettys2-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Open_Firmware" title="Open Firmware">Open Firmware</a>, written in a variant of <a href="/wiki/Forth_(programming_language)" title="Forth (programming language)">Forth</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupiter_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-jupiter-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
<li>A simple custom <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a> based upon the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gecko_(layout_engine)" title="Gecko (layout engine)">Gecko engine</a> used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Word_processor" title="Word processor">word processor</a> based on <a href="/wiki/AbiWord" title="AbiWord">AbiWord</a>.</li>
<li>Email through the web-based <a href="/wiki/Gmail" title="Gmail">Gmail</a> service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20061130_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20061130-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">Online chat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VoIP" title="VoIP">VoIP</a> programs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a> 2.5 is the primary <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> used to develop Sugar "Activities". Several other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreted_programming_language" title="Interpreted programming language">interpreted programming languages</a> are included, such as <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a>, <a href="/wiki/Csound" title="Csound">Csound</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Etoys_(programming_language)" title="Etoys (programming language)">eToys</a> version of <a href="/wiki/Squeak" title="Squeak">Squeak</a>, and Turtle Art<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turtle_Art_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turtle_Art-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Music_sequencer" title="Music sequencer">music sequencer</a> with digital instruments: <a href="/wiki/Jean_Pich%C3%A9" title="Jean Piché">Jean Piché</a>'s <a class="extiw" href="http://wiki.laptop.org/go/TamTam" title="olpc:TamTam">TamTam</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_player_(software)" title="Video player (software)">Audio and video player software</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totem_(media_player)" title="Totem (media player)">Totem</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helix_(project)" title="Helix (project)">Helix</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The laptop uses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_(GUI)" title="Sugar (GUI)">Sugar</a> graphical user interface, written in <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a>, on top of the <a href="/wiki/X_Window_System" title="X Window System">X Window System</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Matchbox_(window_manager)" title="Matchbox (window manager)">Matchbox</a> <a href="/wiki/X_window_manager" title="X window manager">window manager</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupiter_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-jupiter-67">[67]</a></sup> This interface is not based on the typical <a href="/wiki/Desktop_metaphor" title="Desktop metaphor">desktop metaphor</a> but presents an iconic view of programs and documents and a map-like view of nearby connected users. The current active program is displayed in full-screen mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20061130_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20061130-16">[16]</a></sup> Much of the core Sugar interface uses icons, bypassing localization issues. Sugar is also defined as having no folders present in the UI.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> had offered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a> free of charge for use in the laptop, but according to <a href="/wiki/Seymour_Papert" title="Seymour Papert">Seymour Papert</a>, a professor emeritus at MIT who is one of the initiative's founders, the designers wanted an operating system that can be tinkered with: "We declined because it’s not open source."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ-2005_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-2005-69">[69]</a></sup> Therefore, Linux was chosen. However, after a deal with <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, the laptop will now be offered with <a href="/wiki/Windows_XP" title="Windows XP">Windows XP</a> along with an open source alternative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fildes-2_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fildes-2-70">[70]</a></sup></p>

Activity, home, friends and neighborhood software levels</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Jim_Gettys" title="Jim Gettys">Jim Gettys</a>, responsible for the laptops' system software, has called for a re-education of programmers, saying that many applications use too much memory or even <a href="/wiki/Memory_leak" title="Memory leak">leak memory</a>. "There seems to be a common fallacy among programmers that using memory is good: on current hardware it is often much faster to recompute values than to have to reference memory to get a precomputed value. A full cache miss can be hundreds of cycles, and hundreds of times the power consumption of an instruction that hits in the first level cache."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gettysI_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-gettysI-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 4, 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Wikimedia_Foundation" title="Wikimedia Foundation">Wikimedia Foundation</a> announced that static copies of selected Wikipedia articles would be included on the laptops. <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Wales" title="Jimmy Wales">Jimmy Wales</a>, chair of the Wikimedia Foundation, said that "OLPC's mission goes hand in hand with our goal of distributing encyclopedic knowledge, free of charge, to every person in the world. Not everybody in the world has access to a broadband connection."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wikimania_OLPC_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wikimania_OLPC-71">[71]</a></sup> Negroponte had earlier suggested he would like to see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> on the laptop. Wales feels that Wikipedia is one of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Killer_app" title="Killer app">killer apps</a>" for this device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-User_talk:Jimbo_Wales_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-User_talk:Jimbo_Wales-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Don_Hopkins" title="Don Hopkins">Don Hopkins</a> announced that he is creating a free and open source port of the game <a href="/wiki/SimCity_(1989_video_game)" title="SimCity (1989 video game)">SimCity</a> to the OLPC with the blessing of <a href="/wiki/Will_Wright_(game_designer)" title="Will Wright (game designer)">Will Wright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>, and demonstrated SimCity running on the OLPC at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_Developer%27s_Conference" title="Game Developer's Conference">Game Developer's Conference</a> in March 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimCity_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-SimCity-73">[73]</a></sup> The free and open source SimCity plans were confirmed at the same conference by SJ Klein, director of content for the OLPC, who also asked game developers to create "frameworks and scripting environments—tools with which children themselves could create their own content."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SJ_Klein_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-SJ_Klein-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA_SimCity_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA_SimCity-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The laptop's security architecture, known as <a href="/wiki/Bitfrost" title="Bitfrost">Bitfrost</a>, was publicly introduced in February 2007. No passwords will be required for ordinary use of the machine. Programs are assigned certain bundles of rights at install time which govern their access to resources; users can later add more rights. Optionally, the laptops can be configured to request leases from a <a href="/wiki/OLPC_XS" title="OLPC XS">central server</a> and to stop functioning when these leases expire; this is designed as a theft-prevention mechanism.</p>
<p>The pre-8.20 software versions were criticized for bad wireless connectivity and other minor issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criticism_and_Rebuttal_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Criticism_and_Rebuttal-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
