<p><i><b>Into the White</b></i> (also known as <i><b>Comrade</b></i>, <i><b>Lost in the Snow</b></i> and <i><b>Cross of Honour</b></i> in the United Kingdom) is a film set during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Petter_N%C3%A6ss" title="Petter Næss">Petter Næss</a>. It is inspired by and loosely based on real-life events that occurred in Norway during the war.</p>
<p><i>Into the White</i> was written by Ole Meldgaard, Dave Mango and Petter Næss and directed by Næss. The film stars <a href="/wiki/David_Kross" title="David Kross">David Kross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stig_Henrik_Hoff" title="Stig Henrik Hoff">Stig Henrik Hoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florian_Lukas" title="Florian Lukas">Florian Lukas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Grint" title="Rupert Grint">Rupert Grint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lachlan_Nieboer" title="Lachlan Nieboer">Lachlan Nieboer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Into_the_White&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 27 April 1940, a <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> <a href="/wiki/Heinkel_He_111" title="Heinkel He 111">Heinkel He 111</a> bomber (1H+CT) is shot down near <a href="/wiki/Grotli" title="Grotli">Grotli</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> <a href="/wiki/Blackburn_Skua" title="Blackburn Skua">Blackburn Skua</a> (L2940) fighter. Three of the German crew are survivors: pilot Leutnant Horst Schopis (Florian Lukas), Unteroffizier Josef Schwartz (David Kross) and Feldwebel Wolfgang Strunk (Stig Henrik Hoff), who set off looking for shelter. Two British survivors from the aircraft that shot them down have also crash-landed near the same location.</p>
<p>When Capt. Charles P. Davenport (Lachlan Nieboer) and his air gunner Robert Smith (Rupert Grint) meet up with the German crew, they find out that they can all share the same abandoned Norwegian cabin, despite an uneasy armed standoff. In order to survive the harsh Norwegian winter, the German and British crew members have to learn to cooperate and an unlikely friendship blossoms between the survivors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Into_the_White&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a realistic mock-up of a Heinkel He 111 bomber is used, nearly all of the production is set in a cabin with only occasional exterior scenes, prompting one reviewer to note that the film was more like a play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Filming began 28 March 2011 with three weeks of shooting in <a href="/wiki/Grotli" title="Grotli">Grotli</a>, Norway, near where the actual events occurred, with some scenes being shot in <a href="/wiki/Trollh%C3%A4ttan" title="Trollhättan">Trollhättan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%A5landa" title="Brålanda">Brålanda</a>, Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The finished film was released in March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_accuracy">Historical accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Into_the_White&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Actual_events">Actual events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Into_the_White&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Actual events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Campaign" title="Norwegian Campaign">Norwegian Campaign</a></div>
<p>The film account is loosely based on historical events, although the British characters' names are changed. Captain R.T. Partridge is renamed Charles P. Davenport and Lieutenant R.S. Bostock became Robert Smith. The German characters bear the names of their real-life counterparts. Three British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Blackburn_Skua" title="Blackburn Skua">Blackburn Skuas</a> operating from <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ark_Royal_(91)" title="HMS Ark Royal (91)">HMS <i>Ark Royal</i></a> attacked the Heinkel He 111 and knocked out the Germans' port engine. The German aircraft crashed 1,000 meters above sea level in a remote mountain area, miles from any major road. The German tail gunner Hans Hauck was dead when the bomber crashed.</p>
<p>Captain R.T. Partridge, squadron leader of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a> <a href="/wiki/800_Naval_Air_Squadron" title="800 Naval Air Squadron">800 Naval Air Squadron</a> <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> experienced a failing engine in his Skua and glided down to land on a frozen lake. He had seen a small hut nearby and he and his radio operator, Lieutenant Bostock, hiked through heavy snow to the deserted reindeer hunters' cabin. A few minutes later, they were alerted by a whistle and saw the three survivors of the German Heinkel armed with pistols and knives. Speaking broken German and English, the British managed to persuade the Germans that they were the crew of a <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Wellington" title="Vickers Wellington">Vickers Wellington</a> bomber, rather than the fighter that had shot them down. The Germans believed that they had been shot down by a <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a>.</p>
<p>In <i>Luftkampfgegner wurden Freunde</i> ("Air combat opponents became friends"), Horst Schopis wrote in his memoirs:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>As it was getting dark Captain Partridge suggested that the Germans stay in the hut. The two British officers left and found a small chalet, which turned out to be the Grotli Hotel, which was closed for the winter. The German crew arrived the next morning and shared breakfast. It was agreed that the Captain R. T. Partridge and the German Karl-Heinz Strunk would try to locate other people. They met a Norwegian ski patrol. Strunk shouted out "Ingleesh". The Norwegian patrol fired a warning shot at which Partridge fell to the ground and Strunk placed his hands on his head. Lieutenant Bostock emerged from the hotel, suspecting that the German had shot Partridge, but instead saw Strunk apparently reaching for his pistol. One of the Norwegians, seeing this, shot him.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The two Germans survivors—Hauptmann Schopis and mechanic Joseph Auchtor—were taken over the mountains to <a href="/wiki/Stryn_(village)" title="Stryn (village)">Stryn</a> as prisoners. Later they were sent to Britain and on to a prison camp in Canada, where they remained until 1947. The German tail gunner Hans Hauck was given a memorial stone which still stands near the Grotli Hotel. Strunk was initially buried in <a href="/wiki/Skj%C3%A5k" title="Skjåk">Skjåk</a> cemetery, then later transferred to the war cemetery in Trondheim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British had some difficulty in convincing the Norwegians of their nationality until they showed the tailor's label on their uniforms and found a half crown British coin. By sheer coincidence the commander of the Norwegian patrol turned out to be a brother-in-law of a friend of Captain Partridge. The two freed British airmen hiked into <a href="/wiki/%C3%85lesund" title="Ålesund">Ålesund</a>, which was being defended by Royal Marines under heavy Luftwaffe bombing. As the destroyer scheduled to evacuate the British force failed to arrive, they commandeered a car and drove to the port of <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ndalsnes" title="Åndalsnes">Åndalsnes</a>, where they were eventually returned to the United Kingdom by the cruiser <a href="/wiki/HMS_Manchester_(15)" title="HMS Manchester (15)">HMS <i>Manchester</i></a>.</p>
<p>Captain Partridge and Lieutenant Bostock took part in the attempt to sink the German battleship <a href="/wiki/German_battleship_Scharnhorst" title="German battleship Scharnhorst"><i>Scharnhorst</i></a> on 13 June 1940. Partridge was shot down near Stallvik in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trondheimsfjord" title="Trondheimsfjord">Trondheimsfjord</a> and captured by German troops. Lieutenant Bostock was killed in another Blackburn Skua on the same raid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both the German pilot Horst Schopis and the British pilot R.T. Partridge wrote books about their experiences before, during and after the war, entitled <i>Luftkampfgegner wurden Freunde</i> and <i>Operation Skua</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blackburn_Skua_II_(L2940)_(6865236093).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1648" data-file-width="2472" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Blackburn_Skua_II_%28L2940%29_%286865236093%29.jpg/220px-Blackburn_Skua_II_%28L2940%29_%286865236093%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Blackburn_Skua_II_%28L2940%29_%286865236093%29.jpg/330px-Blackburn_Skua_II_%28L2940%29_%286865236093%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Blackburn_Skua_II_%28L2940%29_%286865236093%29.jpg/440px-Blackburn_Skua_II_%28L2940%29_%286865236093%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In 1974, the original L2940 was recovered from Breidalsvatnet lake near Grotli in <a href="/wiki/Skj%C3%A5k" title="Skjåk">Skjåk</a> municipality<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the wreck is on display at the <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm_Museum" title="Fleet Air Arm Museum">Fleet Air Arm Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yeovilton" title="Yeovilton">Yeovilton</a>. The original Heinkel wreck remains in the mountains at Grotli around 1,000 metres above sea level, left untouched in the snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974 and 2004, Horst Schopis visited Grotli, but died in 2011 at 99 years of age, one year before the film's release. British captain R.T. Partridge visited Grotli in 1974, and died in 1990.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_reception">Release and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Into_the_White&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Into the White</i> premiered at the Filmfest Oslo in March 2012 and was subsequently widely released in Norway where it grossed $636,469.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the US, the film had a limited release in select theatres.</p>
<p>Critical reviews were mixed, with some reviewers noting that the atmosphere and setting dominated to the detriment of the plot. Neil Lumbard in his review for <i>DVD Talk</i>, commented: "The entirety of the film revolves around a somewhat simplistic plot element, which is based on actual historical events, but doesn't engage much beyond the central concept of the film ... The film is slow paced and for some audience members this is an obvious detriment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has a rating of 50% at <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As of 12 October 2014, the film received an approval rating of 7.1 out of 10 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMDB" title="IMDB">IMDB</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The trio of Årets lyddesign, Nikolai Linck and Andreas Kongsgaard were nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Award" title="Amanda Award">Amanda Award</a> for Best Sound Design in <i>Into the White</i>.</p>
