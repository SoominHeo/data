<p><b>Gadiformes</b> are an order of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray-finned_fish" title="Ray-finned fish">ray-finned fish</a>, also called the <b>Anacanthini</b>, that includes the <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and its allies. Many major food fish are in this order. They are found in marine waters throughout the world and the vast majority of the species are found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> or colder regions (tropical species are typically deep-water). A few species may enter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">estuaries</a> but only one, the <a href="/wiki/Burbot" title="Burbot">burbot</a> (<i>Lota lota</i>), is a freshwater fish.</p>
<p>Common characteristics include the positioning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a> (if present), below or in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral fins</a>. Gadiformes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physoclist" title="Physoclist">physoclists</a>, which means their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_bladder" title="Gas bladder">gas bladders</a> do not have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic_duct" title="Pneumatic duct">pneumatic duct</a>. The fins are spineless. Gadiform fish range in size from the <a href="/wiki/Codlet" title="Codlet">codlets</a>, which may be as small as 7 cm (2.8 in) in adult length, to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a>, <i>Gadus morhua</i>, which reaches up to 2 m (6.6 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
