<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Queueing theory</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<a href="/wiki/Queueing_theory#Queueing_networks" title="Queueing theory">Queue networks</a> are systems in which single queues are connected by a routing network. In this image, servers are represented by circles, queues by a series of rectangles and the routing network by arrows. In the study of queue networks one typically tries to obtain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrium_distribution" title="Equilibrium distribution">equilibrium distribution</a> of the network, although in many applications the study of the <a href="/wiki/Transient_state" title="Transient state">transient state</a> is fundamental.</div>

<p><b>Queueing theory</b> is the mathematical study of waiting lines, or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/queue" title="wikt:queue">queues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sun-1">[1]</a></sup> In queueing theory, a model is constructed so that queue lengths and waiting time can be predicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sun-1">[1]</a></sup> Queueing theory is generally considered a branch of <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">operations research</a> because the results are often used when making business decisions about the resources needed to provide a service.</p>
<p>Queueing theory has its origins in research by <a href="/wiki/Agner_Krarup_Erlang" title="Agner Krarup Erlang">Agner Krarup Erlang</a> when he created models to describe the Copenhagen telephone exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-sun-1">[1]</a></sup> The ideas have since seen applications including <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traffic_engineering_(transportation)" title="Traffic engineering (transportation)">traffic engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and the design of factories, shops, offices and hospitals as well as in project management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spelling "queueing" over "queuing" is typically encountered in the academic research field. In fact, one of the flagship journals of the profession is named <i><a href="/wiki/Queueing_Systems" title="Queueing Systems">Queueing Systems</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_queueing_nodes">Single queueing nodes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Single queueing nodes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Single queueing nodes are usually described using <a href="/wiki/Kendall%27s_notation" title="Kendall's notation">Kendall's notation</a> in the form <i>A</i>/<i>S</i>/<i>C</i> where <i>A</i> describes the time between arrivals to the queue, <i>S</i> the size of jobs and <i>C</i> the number of servers at the node.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tijms_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-tijms-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Many theorems in queueing theory can be proved by reducing queues to mathematical systems known as <a href="/wiki/Markov_chain" title="Markov chain">Markov chains</a>, first described by <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Markov" title="Andrey Markov">Andrey Markov</a> in his 1906 paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agner_Krarup_Erlang" title="Agner Krarup Erlang">Agner Krarup Erlang</a>, a Danish engineer who worked for the Copenhagen Telephone Exchange, published the first paper on what would now be called queueing theory in 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He modeled the number of telephone calls arriving at an exchange by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisson_process" title="Poisson process">Poisson process</a> and solved the <a href="/wiki/M/D/1_queue" title="M/D/1 queue">M/D/1 queue</a> in 1917 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M/D/k_queue" title="M/D/k queue">M/D/k queueing</a> model in 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-century_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-century-11">[11]</a></sup> In Kendall's notation:</p>
<ul>
<li>M stands for Markov or memoryless and means arrivals occur according to a Poisson process</li>
<li>D stands for deterministic and means jobs arriving at the queue require a fixed amount of service</li>
<li><i>k</i> describes the number of servers at the queueing node (<i>k</i> = 1, 2,...). If there are more jobs at the node than there are servers then jobs will queue and wait for service</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/M/M/1_queue" title="M/M/1 queue">M/M/1 queue</a> is a simple model where a single server serves jobs that arrive according to a Poisson process and have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exponentially_distributed" title="Exponentially distributed">exponentially distributed</a> service requirements. In an <a href="/wiki/M/G/1_queue" title="M/G/1 queue">M/G/1 queue</a> the G stands for general and indicates an arbitrary <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">probability distribution</a>. The M/G/1 model was solved by <a href="/wiki/Felix_Pollaczek" title="Felix Pollaczek">Felix Pollaczek</a> in 1930,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> a solution later recast in probabilistic terms by <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Khinchin" title="Aleksandr Khinchin">Aleksandr Khinchin</a> and now known as the <a href="/wiki/Pollaczek%E2%80%93Khinchine_formula" title="Pollaczek–Khinchine formula">Pollaczek–Khinchine formula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-century_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-century-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-century1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-century1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the 1940s queueing theory became an area of research interest to mathematicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-century1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-century1-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1953 <a href="/wiki/David_George_Kendall" title="David George Kendall">David George Kendall</a> solved the GI/M/k queue<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and introduced the modern notation for queues, now known as <a href="/wiki/Kendall%27s_notation" title="Kendall's notation">Kendall's notation</a>. In 1957 Pollaczek studied the GI/G/1 using an <a href="/wiki/Integral_equation" title="Integral equation">integral equation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Kingman" title="John Kingman">John Kingman</a> gave a formula for the <a href="/wiki/Mean_sojourn_time" title="Mean sojourn time">mean waiting time</a> in a <a href="/wiki/G/G/1_queue" title="G/G/1 queue">G/G/1 queue</a>: <a href="/wiki/Kingman%27s_formula" title="Kingman's formula">Kingman's formula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Matrix_geometric_method" title="Matrix geometric method">matrix geometric method</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matrix_analytic_method" title="Matrix analytic method">matrix analytic methods</a> have allowed queues with <a href="/wiki/Phase-type_distribution" title="Phase-type distribution">phase-type distributed</a> inter-arrival and service time distributions to be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Problems such as performance metrics for the <a href="/wiki/M/G/k_queue" title="M/G/k queue">M/G/k queue</a> remain an open problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-century_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-century-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-century1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-century1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_disciplines">Service disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Service disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fifo_queue.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="773" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Fifo_queue.png/350px-Fifo_queue.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Fifo_queue.png/525px-Fifo_queue.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Fifo_queue.png/700px-Fifo_queue.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fifo_queue.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
First in first out (FIFO) queue example.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Various scheduling policies can be used at queuing nodes:</p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/FIFO_(computing_and_electronics)" title="FIFO (computing and electronics)">First in first out</a></dt>
<dd>This principle states that customers are served one at a time and that the customer that has been waiting the longest is served first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penttinen_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-penttinen-18">[18]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LIFO_(computing)" title="LIFO (computing)">Last in first out</a></dt>
<dd>This principle also serves customers one at a time, but the customer with the shortest <a href="/wiki/Mean_sojourn_time" title="Mean sojourn time">waiting time</a> will be served first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penttinen_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-penttinen-18">[18]</a></sup> Also known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stack_(data_structure)" title="Stack (data structure)">stack</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Processor_sharing" title="Processor sharing">Processor sharing</a></dt>
<dd>Service capacity is shared equally between customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penttinen_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-penttinen-18">[18]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Priority</dt>
<dd>Customers with high priority are served first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penttinen_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-penttinen-18">[18]</a></sup> Priority queues can be of two types, non-preemptive (where a job in service cannot be interrupted) and preemptive (where a job in service can be interrupted by a higher-priority job). No work is lost in either model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortest_job_first" title="Shortest job first">Shortest job first</a></dt>
<dd>The next job to be served is the one with the smallest size</dd>
<dt>Preemptive shortest job first</dt>
<dd>The next job to be served is the one with the original smallest size<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortest_remaining_processing_time" title="Shortest remaining processing time">Shortest remaining processing time</a></dt>
<dd>The next job to serve is the one with the smallest remaining processing requirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Service facility</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Single server: customers line up and there is only one server</li>
<li>Parallel servers: customers line up and there are several servers</li>
<li>Tandem queue: there are many counters and customers can decide going where to queue</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Customer’s behavior of waiting</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Balking: customers deciding not to join the queue if it is too long</li>
<li>Jockeying: customers switch between queues if they think they will get served faster by so doing</li>
<li>Reneging: customers leave the queue if they have waited too long for service</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Queueing_networks">Queueing networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Queueing networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Networks of queues are systems in which a number of queues are connected by customer routing. When a customer is serviced at one node it can join another node and queue for service, or leave the network. For a network of <i>m</i> the state of the system can be described by an <i>m</i>–dimensional vector (<i>x</i><sub>1</sub>,<i>x</i><sub>2</sub>,...,<i>x</i><sub><i>m</i></sub>) where <i>x</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> represents the number of customers at each node.</p>
<p>The first significant results in this area were <a href="/wiki/Jackson_network" title="Jackson network">Jackson networks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-jackson-23">[23]</a></sup> for which an efficient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product-form_stationary_distribution" title="Product-form stationary distribution">product-form stationary distribution</a> exists and the <a href="/wiki/Mean_value_analysis" title="Mean value analysis">mean value analysis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which allows average metrics such as throughput and sojourn times to be computed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> If the total number of customers in the network remains constant the network is called a closed network and has also been shown to have a product–form stationary distribution in the <a href="/wiki/Gordon%E2%80%93Newell_theorem" title="Gordon–Newell theorem">Gordon–Newell theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This result was extended to the <a href="/wiki/BCMP_network" title="BCMP network">BCMP network</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> where a network with very general service time, regimes and customer routing is shown to also exhibit a product-form stationary distribution. The <a href="/wiki/Normalizing_constant" title="Normalizing constant">normalizing constant</a> can be calculated with the <a href="/wiki/Buzen%27s_algorithm" title="Buzen's algorithm">Buzen's algorithm</a>, proposed in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buzen-1973_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-buzen-1973-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Networks of customers have also been investigated, <a href="/wiki/Kelly_network" title="Kelly network">Kelly networks</a> where customers of different classes experience different priority levels at different service nodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Another type of network are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-networks" title="G-networks">G-networks</a> first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Erol_Gelenbe" title="Erol Gelenbe">Erol Gelenbe</a> in 1993:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> these networks do not assume exponential time distributions like the classic Jackson Network.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Routing_algorithms">Routing algorithms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Routing algorithms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In discrete time networks where there is a constraint on which service nodes can be active at any time, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Max-weight_scheduling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Max-weight scheduling (page does not exist)">max-weight scheduling</a> algorithm chooses a service policy to give optimal throughput in the case that each job visits only a single service node. In the more general case where jobs can visit more than one node, <a href="/wiki/Backpressure_routing" title="Backpressure routing">backpressure routing</a> gives optimal throughput.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Network_scheduler" title="Network scheduler">network scheduler</a> must choose a queuing algorithm, which affects the characteristics of the larger network.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mean_field_limits">Mean field limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mean field limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_field_model" title="Mean field model">Mean field models</a> consider the limiting behaviour of the <a href="/wiki/Empirical_measure" title="Empirical measure">empirical measure</a> (proportion of queues in different states) as the number of queues (<i>m</i> above) goes to infinity. The impact of other queues on any given queue in the network is approximated by a differential equation. The deterministic model converges to the same stationary distribution as the original model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluid_limits">Fluid limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fluid limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fluid_limit" title="Fluid limit">fluid limit</a></div>
<p>Fluid models are continuous deterministic analogs of queueing networks obtained by taking the limit when the process is scaled in time and space, allowing heterogeneous objects. This scaled trajectory converges to a deterministic equation which allows the stability of the system to be proven. It is known that a queueing network can be stable, but have an unstable fluid limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_traffic.2Fdiffusion_approximations">Heavy traffic/diffusion approximations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queueing_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heavy traffic/diffusion approximations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heavy_traffic_approximation" title="Heavy traffic approximation">heavy traffic approximation</a></div>
<p>In a system with high occupancy rates (utilisation near 1) a heavy traffic approximation can be used to approximate the queueing length process by a <a href="/wiki/Reflected_Brownian_motion" title="Reflected Brownian motion">reflected Brownian motion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ornstein%E2%80%93Uhlenbeck_process" title="Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process">Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process</a> or more general <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_process" title="Diffusion process">diffusion process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The number of dimensions of the RBM is equal to the number of queueing nodes and the diffusion is restricted to the non-negative <a href="/wiki/Orthant" title="Orthant">orthant</a>.</p>
