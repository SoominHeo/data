<p><b>Daniel Handler</b> (born February 28, 1970) is an American writer and journalist. He is best known for his work under the pen name <a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket" title="Lemony Snicket">Lemony Snicket</a>, having published children's series <i><a href="/wiki/A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="A Series of Unfortunate Events">A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/All_the_Wrong_Questions" title="All the Wrong Questions">All the Wrong Questions</a></i> under this pseudonym. He has also published adult novels under his real name; his first book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Basic_Eight" title="The Basic Eight">The Basic Eight</a></i> was rejected by many publishers for its dark subject matter. His most recent book is <i>We Are Pirates</i>. Handler has also played the <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a> in several bands.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Handler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Handler was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a>, the son of Sandra Handler Day (née Walpole) a retired <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_San_Francisco" title="City College of San Francisco">City College of San Francisco</a> Dean, and Louis Handler, an accountant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beweeweht_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-beweeweht-3">[3]</a></sup> His father was a Jewish refugee from Germany, and he is distantly related to British writer <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Walpole" title="Hugh Walpole">Hugh Walpole</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emrkoweeht_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-emrkoweeht-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-higi_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-higi-5">[5]</a></sup> through his mother. He has a younger sister, Rebecca Handler. Handler has been a voracious reader since childhood, and his favorite author was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Keepers_Maxwell,_Jr." title="William Keepers Maxwell, Jr.">William Keepers Maxwell, Jr.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He attended Commodore Sloat Elementary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover_Middle_School_(San_Francisco)" title="Herbert Hoover Middle School (San Francisco)">Herbert Hoover Middle School</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lowell_High_School_(San_Francisco)" title="Lowell High School (San Francisco)">Lowell High School</a>. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Wesleyan_University" title="Wesleyan University">Wesleyan University</a> in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was awarded the 1992 Connecticut Student Poet Prize which he claims was earned by ripping off <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bishop" title="Elizabeth Bishop">Elizabeth Bishop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He is an <a href="/wiki/Alumnus" title="Alumnus">alumnus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Boys_Chorus" title="San Francisco Boys Chorus">San Francisco Boys Chorus</a>.</p>
<p>He is married to <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Brown_(artist)" title="Lisa Brown (artist)">Lisa Brown</a>, a graphic artist whom he met in college. They have a son, Otto Handler, born in 2003. They live in an old <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> house in San Francisco.</p>
<p>Handler is politically active and helped form <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LitPAC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LitPAC (page does not exist)">LitPAC</a>. In the June 10, 2007 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i>, Handler revealed ambivalence toward his wealth and the expectations that it creates. He stated that he is often asked for money for charitable causes and often gives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He has supported the <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Wall_Street" title="Occupy Wall Street">Occupy Wall Street</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (April 2014)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Handler describes himself as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secular_Humanism" title="Secular Humanism">secular humanist</a> and an atheist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition, he says, "I'm not a believer in predetermined fates, being rewarded for one's efforts. I'm not a believer in karma. The reason why I try to be a good person is because I think it's the right thing to do. If I commit fewer bad acts there will be fewer bad acts, maybe other people will join in committing fewer bad acts, and in time there will be fewer and fewer of them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_work">Professional work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Handler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Handler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four of Daniel Handler's major works have been published under his name. His first, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Basic_Eight" title="The Basic Eight">The Basic Eight</a></i>, was rejected by many publishers for its subject matter and tone (a dark view of a teenage girl's life). Handler claims that the novel was rejected 37 times before finally being published.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Watch_Your_Mouth" title="Watch Your Mouth">Watch Your Mouth</a>,</i> his second novel, was completed before <i>The Basic Eight</i> was published. It follows a more operatic theme, complete with stage directions and various acts. It is described by <a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a>, the book's reprint publisher, as an "incest opera", mixing <a href="/wiki/Jewish_mythology" title="Jewish mythology">Jewish mythology</a> with modern sexuality. <i>Watch Your Mouth'</i>s second half replaces the opera troupe with the form of a 12-step recovery program, linguistically undergone by the protagonist.</p>
<p>In April 2005, Handler published <i><a href="/wiki/Adverbs_(novel)" title="Adverbs (novel)">Adverbs</a></i>, a collection of short stories that he says are "about love." In January 2011, <i><a href="/wiki/Why_We_Broke_Up" title="Why We Broke Up">Why We Broke Up</a></i> was released; it received a 2012 Michael L. Printz honor award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>He served as a judge for the <a href="/wiki/PEN/Phyllis_Naylor_Working_Writer_Fellowship" title="PEN/Phyllis Naylor Working Writer Fellowship">PEN/Phyllis Naylor Working Writer Fellowship</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Handler's next novel was entitled <i><a href="/wiki/We_Are_Pirates" title="We Are Pirates">We Are Pirates</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and is about a modern-age pirate who "wants to be an old-fashioned kind of pirate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It was released on February 3, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Handler began writing <i><a href="/wiki/A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="A Series of Unfortunate Events">A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i> under the Snicket pseudonym in 1998. The books concern three orphaned children who experience progressively terrible events following the alleged death of their parents and burning of their home (done by a man named Count Olaf and his troupe of associates), and Snicket acts as the narrator and biographer of the fictional orphans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He has also narrated the <a href="/wiki/Audiobook" title="Audiobook">audiobooks</a> for three consecutive books in the series, before deciding to quit because he found it too difficult, handing back the narrating job to the original narrator, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Curry" title="Tim Curry">Tim Curry</a>.</p>
<p>Handler has also appeared at author appearances as "Lemony Snicket's handler," as well as appearing as Snicket himself in various other books and media, including the commentary track for the film version of his books, <i><a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket%27s_A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events">Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i>. He also wrote an introduction to <i><a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket:_The_Unauthorized_Autobiography" title="Lemony Snicket: The Unauthorized Autobiography">Lemony Snicket: The Unauthorized Autobiography</a></i> under his own name.</p>
<p>The Lemony Snicket books have been international best-sellers, and the 13th and final installment of the series came out Friday, October 13, 2006. On the day the thirteenth book came out, Handler appeared on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Today_(NBC_program)" title="Today (NBC program)">Today</a></i> show as Lemony Snicket's representative.</p>
<p>Handler has also written some short fiction and picture books under the Lemony Snicket pseudonym. As part of his support of Occupy Wall Street, Handler wrote "Thirteen Observations made by Lemony Snicket while watching Occupy Wall Street from a Discreet Distance", which was published on the Occupy Writers website.</p>
<p>Handler has recently completed a series of novels, <i><a href="/wiki/All_the_Wrong_Questions" title="All the Wrong Questions">All the Wrong Questions</a></i>, which serve as prequels to <i>A Series of Unfortunate Events</i>.</p>
<p>Lemony Snicket has his own website which has a section about "The Afflicted Author". In this autobiographical section, Snicket is very vague, saying things such as: "His family has roots in a part of the country which is now underwater" and, without any previous mention of this scandal, "The aftermath of the scandal was swift, brutal and inaccurately reported in the periodicals of the day". Also on this site, Mr. Snicket discusses his "representative" to the public, Daniel Handler. He does not go in depth about Handler, but states, "Mr. Handler has had a relatively uneventful life..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HarperCollins_Publishers" title="HarperCollins Publishers">HarperCollins Publishers</a> published a short interview with Lemony Snicket on their website, asking personal questions, such as "What were some of your hobbies as a child?" to which Snicket answered, "Taxidermy and playing the harpsichord."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Handler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Handler was in two bands following college, The Edith Head Trio and Tzamboni, but it wasn't until <i><a href="/wiki/69_Love_Songs" title="69 Love Songs">69 Love Songs</a></i>, a three-album set by <a href="/wiki/The_Magnetic_Fields" title="The Magnetic Fields">The Magnetic Fields</a>, that his music attracted attention. Handler played accordion on a number of tracks in <i>69 Love Songs</i>. In the box set of the project, Handler provides a lengthy interview with band leader <a href="/wiki/Stephin_Merritt" title="Stephin Merritt">Stephin Merritt</a> about the project, as well as conversations about each song. Handler also appears in the 2009 documentary <i>Strange Powers,</i> by Kerthy Fix and <a href="/wiki/Gail_O%27Hara" title="Gail O'Hara">Gail O'Hara</a>, about Merritt and the Magnetic Fields.</p>
<p>He has gone on to play accordion in several other Merritt projects, including music by The Magnetic Fields, <a href="/wiki/The_6ths" title="The 6ths">The 6ths</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Gothic_Archies" title="The Gothic Archies">The Gothic Archies</a>, the last of which provided songs for the audiobooks in the <i>A Series of Unfortunate Events</i> children's book series. On October 10, 2006, an album by the Gothic Archies was released with all thirteen songs from the thirteen audiobooks in <i>A Series of Unfortunate Events</i>, along with two bonus songs.</p>
<p>In the audio commentary on the film adaptation <i><a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket%27s_A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events">Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i>, Handler plays a song about how depressing it is to have leeches in a film.</p>
<p>Daniel Handler wrote the lyrics to the song "Radio," performed by <a href="/wiki/One_Ring_Zero" title="One Ring Zero">One Ring Zero</a>, and the lyrics to "The Gibbons Girl" by <a href="/wiki/Figures_on_a_Beach" title="Figures on a Beach">Chris Ewen</a>'s The Hidden Variable.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Handler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Handler has also had some success in film work. He produced the screenplay for <i>Rick</i>, which was based on the <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Verdi</a> opera <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rigoletto_(opera)" title="Rigoletto (opera)">Rigoletto</a></i>, as well as <i>Kill the Poor</i>, which was based on the novel by <a href="/wiki/Joel_Rose" title="Joel Rose">Joel Rose</a>.</p>
<p>Handler was involved in the screenwriting process for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket%27s_A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events">Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i>, but was ultimately removed from the project. He had completed eight separate drafts of the film before giving up following a change in those who were producing the film. Robert Gordon (screenwriter of <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Quest" title="Galaxy Quest">Galaxy Quest</a></i>) was hired to replace Handler and eventually received credit for the film's screenplay, under Handler's request.</p>
<p>Handler did submit a commentary track for the DVD version, alongside director <a href="/wiki/Brad_Silberling" title="Brad Silberling">Brad Silberling</a>. In character as Lemony Snicket, he derides the Lemony Snicket in the film – played by <a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude Law</a> – as an impostor, as well as choosing to play accordion and sing about leeches rather than pay attention to the film. At numerous times during the track he shows great sympathy towards the Baudelaire children, and implies that he is being held captive by the director in order to do the commentary.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Handler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">National Book Awards</a> ceremony in November 2014, Handler made a controversial remark after author <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Woodson" title="Jacqueline Woodson">Jacqueline Woodson</a> was presented with an award for young people's literature. Woodson, who is black, won the award for <i>Brown Girl Dreaming</i>. During the ceremony, Handler noted that Woodson is allergic to watermelon, a reference to the racist <a href="/wiki/Watermelon_stereotype" title="Watermelon stereotype">watermelon stereotype</a>. His comments were immediately criticized;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Handler apologized via <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> and donated $10,000 to <a href="/wiki/We_Need_Diverse_Books" title="We Need Diverse Books">We Need Diverse Books</a>, and promised to match donations up to $100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In a <i>New York Times</i> <a href="/wiki/Op-ed" title="Op-ed">op-ed</a> published shortly thereafter, "The Pain of the Watermelon Joke", Jacqueline Woodson explained that "in making light of that deep and troubled history" with his joke, Daniel Handler had come from a place of ignorance, but underscored the need for her mission to "give people a sense of this country's brilliant and brutal history, so no one ever thinks they can walk onto a stage one evening and laugh at another's too often painful past".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
