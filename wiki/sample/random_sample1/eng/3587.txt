<p><b>Danica Mae McKellar</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVGuide-bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TVGuide-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> (born January 3, 1975<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVGuide-bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TVGuide-bio-1">[1]</a></sup>) is an American actor, author, <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, and education advocate. She played <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kevin_Arnold" title="Kevin Arnold">Kevin Arnold</a>'s on-off girlfriend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winnie_Cooper" title="Winnie Cooper">Winnie Cooper</a> in the television series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonder_Years" title="The Wonder Years">The Wonder Years</a></i>, and later wrote four non-fiction books: <i>Math Doesn't Suck</i>, <i>Kiss My Math</i>, <i>Hot X: Algebra Exposed</i> and <i>Girls Get Curves: Geometry Takes Shape</i>, which encourage middle-school and high-school girls to have confidence and succeed in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2015 Mckellar was cast in the <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> original series <i><a href="/wiki/Project_Mc2" title="Project Mc2">Project Mc<sup>2</sup></a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Jolla,_California" title="La Jolla, California">La Jolla, California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVGuide-bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TVGuide-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> McKellar moved with her family to Los Angeles when she was eight. Her mother Mahalia was a homemaker; her father Christopher is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_estate_developer" title="Real estate developer">real estate developer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Danica and her sister <a href="/wiki/Crystal_McKellar" title="Crystal McKellar">Crystal McKellar</a> both maintained professional acting careers as children, but with a strong emphasis on education as a priority. As a result, Crystal became a corporate lawyer, while Danica majored in mathematics. Danica and Crystal also have two half-brothers, Chris Junior and Connor McKellar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>McKellar studied at <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">UCLA</a> and earned a Bachelor of Science in Mathematics with highest honors (<i><a href="/wiki/Latin_honors" title="Latin honors">summa cum laude</a></i>) in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As an undergraduate, she coauthored a <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">scientific paper</a> with Professor Lincoln Chayes and fellow student Brandy Winn entitled "<a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">Percolation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_state" title="Gibbs state">Gibbs states</a> multiplicity for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashkin%E2%80%93Teller_model" title="Ashkin–Teller model">Ashkin–Teller models</a> on <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Wonder_Years_and_early_acting_career"><i>The Wonder Years</i> and early acting career <span id=".27.27The_Wonder_Years_and_Early_Acting_Career.27.27"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Wonder Years and early acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At age 7, McKellar enrolled in weekend acting classes for children at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Strasberg_Institute" title="Lee Strasberg Institute">Lee Strasberg Institute</a> in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In her teens, she landed a prominent role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonder_Years" title="The Wonder Years">The Wonder Years</a></i>, an American television comedy-drama that ran for six seasons on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>, from 1988 to 1993. She played Gwendolyn "Winnie" Cooper, the main love interest of Kevin Arnold (played by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Savage" title="Fred Savage">Fred Savage</a>) on the show. Her first kiss was with Fred Savage in an episode of <i>The Wonder Years</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>She later said, "My first kiss was a pretty nerve-wracking experience! But we never kissed off screen, and pretty quickly our feelings turned into brother/sister, and stayed that way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comedy_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-comedy-13">[13]</a></sup> Late-night talk show host <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a> has said that the character of Winnie Cooper "is the coolest girl in any TV show ever." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McKellar has said that she found it difficult to move from being a child actress to an adult actress."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comedy_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-comedy-13">[13]</a></sup> Since leaving <i>The Wonder Years</i>, McKellar has had several guest roles in television series (including one with former co-star <a href="/wiki/Fred_Savage" title="Fred Savage">Fred Savage</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Working_(TV_series)" title="Working (TV series)">Working</a></i>), and has written and directed two short films. She appeared in two <a href="/wiki/Lifetime_(TV_network)" title="Lifetime (TV network)">Lifetime</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_movie" title="Television movie">TV movies</a> in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moment_of_Truth_movies" title="Moment of Truth movies">Moment of Truth</a></i> series, playing Kristin Guthrie in 1994's <i><a href="/wiki/Moment_of_Truth:_Cradle_of_Conspiracy" title="Moment of Truth: Cradle of Conspiracy">Cradle of Conspiracy</a></i> and Annie Mills Carman in 1996's <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_for_Annie:_A_Moment_of_Truth_Movie" title="Justice for Annie: A Moment of Truth Movie">Justice for Annie</a></i>. She briefly returned to regular television with a recurring role in the 2002–03 season of <i><a href="/wiki/The_West_Wing" title="The West Wing">The West Wing</a>,</i> portraying Elsie Snuffin, the stepsister and assistant of Deputy White House Communications Director <a href="/wiki/Will_Bailey" title="Will Bailey">Will Bailey</a>.</p>
<p>In 1989, she appeared playing the cello at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Gibson" title="Debbie Gibson">Debbie Gibson</a> music video <i>No More Rhyme</i>.</p>
<p>McKellar appeared in <a href="/wiki/Lingerie" title="Lingerie">lingerie</a> in the July 2005 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Stuff_(magazine)" title="Stuff (magazine)">Stuff</a></i> magazine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> after readers voted her the 1990s star they would most like to see in lingerie. McKellar explained that she agreed to the shoot in part to obtain "grittier roles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comedy_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-comedy-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, McKellar starred in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lifetime_Television" title="Lifetime Television">Lifetime</a> movie and web-based series titled <i>Inspector Mom</i> about a mother who solves mysteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the August 1, 2007, edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Don_and_Mike_Show" title="Don and Mike Show">Don and Mike Show</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/WJFK-FM" title="WJFK-FM">WJFK-FM</a> radio program out of Washington, D.C., McKellar announced plans that the producers of <i><a href="/wiki/How_I_Met_Your_Mother" title="How I Met Your Mother">How I Met Your Mother</a></i> were planning to bring her back for a recurring role (she guest-starred on the show in late 2005 in "<a href="/wiki/The_Pineapple_Incident" title="The Pineapple Incident">The Pineapple Incident</a>" and again in early 2007 in "<a href="/wiki/Third_Wheel" title="Third Wheel">Third Wheel</a>"). She also made an appearance on the show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Bang_Theory" title="The Big Bang Theory">The Big Bang Theory</a></i>, in the episode "The Psychic Vortex".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, she starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Heatstroke_(film)" title="Heatstroke (film)">Heatstroke</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Syfy" title="Syfy">Sci-Fi Channel</a> original movie about searching for alien life on Earth and in 2009 she was one of the stars commenting on the occurrences of the new millennium in <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a>'s <i>I Love the New Millennium</i> and was the math correspondent for <i><a href="/wiki/Brink_(TV_series)" title="Brink (TV series)">Brink</a></i>, a program by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_Channel" title="Science Channel">Science Channel</a> about technology. In 2013, she played Ellen Plainview in Lifetime's reimagining of the 1956 Alfred Hitchcock film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wrong_Man" title="The Wrong Man">The Wrong Man</a>.</i></p>
<p>McKellar has also found work as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_actress" title="Voice actress">voice actress</a>. She is the voice of <a href="/wiki/Miss_Martian" title="Miss Martian">Miss Martian</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Justice_(TV_series)" title="Young Justice (TV series)">Young Justice</a></i>. She has also provided the voices for two characters in three video games: <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_(comics)" title="Jubilee (comics)">Jubilee</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men_Legends" title="X-Men Legends">X-Men Legends</a></i> (2004), and <a href="/wiki/Invisible_Woman" title="Invisible Woman">Invisible Woman</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel:_Ultimate_Alliance" title="Marvel: Ultimate Alliance">Marvel: Ultimate Alliance</a></i> (2006) and <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel:_Ultimate_Alliance_2" title="Marvel: Ultimate Alliance 2">Marvel: Ultimate Alliance 2</a></i> (2009).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2012, she starred in the Lifetime movie, <i>Love at the Christmas Table</i> with <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Milligan" title="Dustin Milligan">Dustin Milligan</a>.</p>
<p>In 2013, she starred in the Syfy movie <i><a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_Devils_(film)" title="Tasmanian Devils (film)">Tasmanian Devils</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Apolo_Ohno" title="Apolo Ohno">Apolo Ohno</a>.</p>
<p>On March 4, 2014, she was announced to be joining <a href="/wiki/Dancing_with_the_Stars_(U.S._season_18)" title="Dancing with the Stars (U.S. season 18)">season 18</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Dancing_with_the_Stars_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Dancing with the Stars (U.S. TV series)">Dancing with the Stars</a></i>. <p>She had a guest appearance in the <i><a href="/wiki/Impractical_Jokers" title="Impractical Jokers">Impractical Jokers</a></i> season four episode six titled "The Blunder Years"</p>
<p>In 2015 she starred in the <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> original series <i><a href="/wiki/Project_Mc2" title="Project Mc2">Project Mc<sup>2</sup></a></i> as The Quail.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She married composer Mike Verta on March 22, 2009, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Jolla,_California" title="La Jolla, California">La Jolla, California</a>; the couple had dated since 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> They had their first child, a son named Draco, in 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> McKellar filed for divorce from Verta in June 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> with the dissolution becoming final in February 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 16, 2014, she was engaged to her boyfriend Scott Sveslosky, a partner in the Los Angeles legal firm Sheppard Mullin Richter and Hampton. On November 15, 2014, the two wed in <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kaua'i</a>, Hawai'i.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Her first book, <i>Math Doesn't Suck: How to Survive Middle School Math without Losing Your Mind or Breaking a Nail</i>, was a <i>New York Times</i> bestseller,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-2">[2]</a></sup> and was favorably reviewed by Tara C. Smith, the founder of Iowa Citizens for Science and a professor of epidemiology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The book also received a review from Anthony Jones, writing for the <i>School Librarian</i> journal, who described the book as "a trouble-shooting guide to help girls overcome their biggest maths challenges," noting what he described as "real-world examples of great mathematics in action."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In an interview with Smith, McKellar said that she wrote the book "to show girls that math is accessible and relevant, and even a little glamorous" and to counteract "damaging social messages telling young girls that math and science aren't for them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>McKellar's second book, <i>Kiss My Math: Showing Pre-Algebra Who's Boss</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> was released on August 5, 2008. The book's target audience is girls in the 7th through 9th grades. Her third book, <i>Hot X: Algebra Exposed!</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> covers algebra topics, while the previous two titles were intended as "algebra-readiness books."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <i>Hot X</i> was published on August 3, 2010. Her fourth book, <i>Girls Get Curves – Geometry Takes Shape</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book-curves_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-book-curves-32">[32]</a></sup> focuses on the subject of geometry, and attempts to make the subject more accessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three of McKellar's books were listed in <i>The New York Times</i> children's bestseller list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McKellar was named <i>Person of the Week</i> on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_News_with_Charles_Gibson" title="World News with Charles Gibson">World News with Charles Gibson</a></i> for the week ending August 10, 2007. The news segment highlighted her book <i>Math Doesn't Suck</i> and her efforts to help girls develop an interest in mathematics, especially during the middle school years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In January 2014, she received the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Policy_Board_for_Mathematics" title="Joint Policy Board for Mathematics">Joint Policy Board for Mathematics</a> (JPBM) Communications Award. The citation credited her books, blog, and public appearances for encouraging "countless middle and high school students, especially girls, to be more interested in mathematics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_videos">Music videos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danica_McKellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Music videos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 20, 2013, Canadian singer <a href="/wiki/Avril_Lavigne" title="Avril Lavigne">Avril Lavigne</a> released her music video for new single "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_N_Roll_(Avril_Lavigne_song)" title="Rock N Roll (Avril Lavigne song)">Rock N Roll</a>" from her <a href="/wiki/Avril_Lavigne_(album)" title="Avril Lavigne (album)">upcoming self-titled fifth album</a>, which features McKellar as "Winnie Cooper".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>McKellar was featured in <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Gibson" title="Debbie Gibson">Debbie Gibson</a>'s 8th single from the <i><a href="/wiki/Electric_Youth_(album)" title="Electric Youth (album)">Electric Youth</a></i> album, "<a href="/wiki/No_More_Rhyme" title="No More Rhyme">No More Rhyme</a>", which was released in 1989. She plays the cello in the beginning of the video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
