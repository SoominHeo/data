<p><b>Aortic dissection</b> occurs when a tear in <a href="/wiki/Tunica_intima" title="Tunica intima">the inside</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> causes blood to flow between the layers of the wall of the aorta, <a href="/wiki/Dissection_(medical)" title="Dissection (medical)">forcing the layers apart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup> In most cases this is associated with a sudden onset of severe <a href="/wiki/Acute_aortic_syndrome" title="Acute aortic syndrome">chest or back pain</a>, often described as "tearing" in character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2013_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-White2013-3">[3]</a></sup> Also, vomiting, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaphoresis" title="Diaphoresis">sweating</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lightheadedness" title="Lightheadedness">lightheadedness</a> may occur. Other symptoms may result from decreased blood supply to other organs such as <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mesenteric_ischemia" title="Mesenteric ischemia">mesenteric ischemia</a>. Aortic dissection can quickly lead to death as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myocardial_ischemia" title="Myocardial ischemia">not enough blood flow to the heart</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aortic_rupture" title="Aortic rupture">rupture of the aorta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2013_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-White2013-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aortic dissection is more common in those with a history of <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">high blood pressure</a>, a number of <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue_disease" title="Connective tissue disease">connective tissue diseases</a> that affect blood vessel wall strength such as <a href="/wiki/Marfan_syndrome" title="Marfan syndrome">Marfan syndrome</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bicuspid_aortic_valve" title="Bicuspid aortic valve">bicuspid aortic valve</a>, and previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_surgery" title="Heart surgery">heart surgery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2013_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-White2013-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Major_trauma" title="Major trauma">Major trauma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smoking" title="Smoking">smoking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> use, <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_aortic_aneurysm" title="Thoracic aortic aneurysm">thoracic aortic aneurysm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arteritis" title="Arteritis">inflammation of arteries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dyslipidemia" title="Dyslipidemia">abnormal lipid levels</a> also increase the risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2013_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-White2013-3">[3]</a></sup> The diagnosis is confirmed with <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">medical imaging</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_computed_tomography" title="X-ray computed tomography">computed tomography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Echocardiography" title="Echocardiography">ultrasound</a>. The two main types are Stanford type A which involves the <a href="/wiki/Ascending_aorta" title="Ascending aorta">first part of the aorta</a> and type B which does not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prevention is by blood pressure control and not smoking. The treatment of aortic dissection depends on the part of the aorta involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup> Surgery is usually required for dissections that involve the first part of the aorta, while those that do not can typically be treated with blood pressure and heart-rate lowering unless complications result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2013_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-White2013-3">[3]</a></sup> Surgery may be done either by an opening in the chest or by <a href="/wiki/Endovascular_aneurysm_repair" title="Endovascular aneurysm repair">endovascular aneurysm repair</a> (carried out from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endovascular_surgery" title="Endovascular surgery">inside the blood vessels</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aortic dissection is relatively rare, occurring at an estimated rate of three per 100,000 people per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup> It is more common in males than females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup> The typical age at diagnosis is 63, with about 10% of cases occurring before 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup> Without treatment, about half of people with type A die within three days and about 10% of people with type B die within a month. The first case of aortic dissection described was in the examination of <a href="/wiki/George_II_of_Great_Britain" title="George II of Great Britain">King George II of Great Britain</a> following his death in 1760. Surgery for aortic dissection was introduced in the 1950s by <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._DeBakey" title="Michael E. DeBakey">Michael E. DeBakey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 96% of individuals with aortic dissection present with severe pain that had a sudden onset. The pain may be described as a tearing, stabbing, or sharp sensation; 17% of individuals feel the pain migrate as the dissection extends down the aorta. The location of pain is associated with the location of the dissection. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior" title="Anterior">Anterior</a> chest pain is associated with dissections involving the ascending aorta, while interscapular (back) pain is associated with descending aortic dissections. If the pain is <a href="/wiki/Pleurisy" title="Pleurisy">pleuritic</a> in nature, it may suggest acute <a href="/wiki/Pericarditis" title="Pericarditis">pericarditis</a> caused by bleeding into the <a href="/wiki/Pericardium" title="Pericardium">pericardial sac</a>. This is a particularly dangerous eventuality, suggesting that acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pericardial_tamponade" title="Pericardial tamponade">pericardial tamponade</a> may be imminent. Pericardial tamponade is the most common cause of death from aortic dissection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the pain may be confused with the pain of a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> (heart attack), aortic dissection is usually not associated with the other signs that suggest myocardial infarction, including heart failure and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECG" title="ECG">ECG</a> changes.</p>
<p>Individuals with aortic dissection who do not present with pain have a chronic dissection.</p>
<p>Less common symptoms that may be seen in the setting of aortic dissection include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congestive_heart_failure" title="Congestive heart failure">congestive heart failure</a> (7%), <a href="/wiki/Syncope_(medicine)" title="Syncope (medicine)">fainting</a> (9%), <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> (6%), ischemic <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_neuropathy" title="Peripheral neuropathy">peripheral neuropathy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraplegia" title="Paraplegia">paraplegia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-She2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-She2013-5">[5]</a></sup> If the individual had a fainting episode, about half the time it is due to bleeding into the pericardium leading to pericardial tamponade.</p>
<p>Neurological complications of aortic dissection (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a>) are due to the involvement of one or more arteries supplying portions of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>.</p>
<p>If the aortic dissection involves the abdominal aorta, compromise of the branches of the abdominal aorta is possible. In abdominal aortic dissections, compromise of one or both <a href="/wiki/Renal_artery" title="Renal artery">renal arteries</a> occurs in 5–8% of cases, while mesenteric <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemia</a> (ischemia of the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestines</a>) occurs 3–5% of the time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_pressure">Blood pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Blood pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People with an aortic dissection often have a history of <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>; the blood pressure is quite variable at presentation with acute aortic dissection, and tends to be higher in individuals with a distal dissection. In individuals with a proximal aortic dissection, 36% present with hypertension, while 25% present with <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>. Proximal aortic dissections tend to be more associated with weakening of the vascular wall due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cystic_medial_degeneration" title="Cystic medial degeneration">cystic medial degeneration</a>. In those who present with distal aortic dissections, 70% present with hypertension, while 4% present with hypotension.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Severe hypotension at presentation is a grave prognostic indicator. It is usually associated with pericardial tamponade, severe aortic insufficiency, or rupture of the aorta. Accurate measurement of the blood pressure is important. Pseudohypotension (falsely low blood-pressure measurement) may occur due to involvement of the <a href="/wiki/Brachiocephalic_artery" title="Brachiocephalic artery">brachiocephalic artery</a> (supplying the right arm) or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_subclavian_artery" title="Left subclavian artery">left subclavian artery</a> (supplying the left arm).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aortic_insufficiency">Aortic insufficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aortic insufficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aortic_insufficiency" title="Aortic insufficiency">Aortic insufficiency</a> (AI) occurs in half to two-thirds of ascending aortic dissections, and the <a href="/wiki/Heart_sounds" title="Heart sounds">murmur</a> of aortic insufficiency is audible in about 32% of proximal dissections. The intensity (loudness) of the murmur is dependent on the blood pressure and may be inaudible in the event of hypotension.</p>
<p>Multiple causes exist for AI in the setting of ascending aortic dissection. The dissection may dilate the annulus of the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_valve" title="Aortic valve">aortic valve</a>, so that the leaflets of the valve cannot coapt. Another mechanism is that the dissection may extend into the aortic root and detach the aortic valve leaflets. The third mechanism is that if there was an extensive intimal tear, the intimal flap may prolapse into the LV outflow tract, causing intimal <a href="/wiki/Intussusception_(blood_vessel_growth)" title="Intussusception (blood vessel growth)">intussusception</a> into the aortic valve preventing proper valve closure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Myocardial_infarction">Myocardial infarction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Myocardial infarction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Myocardial infarction (heart attack) occurs in 1–2% of aortic dissections. The etiology of the infarction is involvement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_artery" title="Coronary artery">coronary arteries</a>, which supply the heart, in the dissection. The right coronary artery is involved more commonly than the left coronary artery. If the myocardial infarction is treated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrombolytic" title="Thrombolytic">thrombolytic</a> therapy, the mortality increases to over 70%, mostly due to bleeding into the pericardial sac causing pericardial tamponade.</p>
<p>Because aortic dissection may present to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_room" title="Emergency room">emergency room</a> <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> similar to a myocardial infarction, the physician must be careful to make the proper diagnosis prior to initiating treatment for myocardial infarction since the treatment regimen for myocardial infarction can be lethal to an individual presenting with aortic dissection.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pleural_effusion">Pleural effusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pleural effusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Pleural_effusion" title="Pleural effusion">pleural effusion</a> (fluid collection in the space between the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> and the chest wall or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(anatomy)" title="Diaphragm (anatomy)">diaphragm</a>) can be due to either blood from a transient rupture of the aorta or fluid due to an inflammatory reaction around the aorta. If a pleural effusion were to develop due to an aortic dissection, it is more commonly in the left hemithorax rather than the right hemithorax.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aortic dissection is associated with <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> (high blood pressure) and many <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> disorders. <a href="/wiki/Vasculitis" title="Vasculitis">Vasculitis</a> (<a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> of an artery) is rarely associated with aortic dissection. It can also be the result of chest trauma. About 72 to 80% of individuals who present with an aortic dissection have a previous history of hypertension.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Bicuspid_aortic_valve" title="Bicuspid aortic valve">bicuspid aortic valve</a> (a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_heart_disease" title="Congenital heart disease">congenital heart disease</a> involving the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_valve" title="Aortic valve">aortic valve</a>) is found in 7–14% of individuals who have an aortic dissection. These individuals are prone to dissection in the ascending aorta. The risk of dissection in individuals with bicuspid aortic valve is not associated with the degree of <a href="/wiki/Aortic_stenosis" title="Aortic stenosis">stenosis</a> of the valve.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marfan_syndrome" title="Marfan syndrome">Marfan syndrome</a> is noted in 5–9% of individuals who suffer from an aortic dissection. In this subset, the incidence in young individuals is increased. Individuals with Marfan syndrome tend to have aneurysms of the aorta and are more prone to proximal dissections of the aorta.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Turner_syndrome" title="Turner syndrome">Turner syndrome</a> also increases the risk of aortic dissection, by aortic root dilatation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner2006_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner2006-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chest trauma leading to aortic dissection can be divided into two groups based on etiology: blunt chest trauma (commonly seen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_accident" title="Car accident">car accidents</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iatrogenic" title="Iatrogenic">iatrogenic</a>. Iatrogenic causes include trauma during <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_catheterization" title="Cardiac catheterization">cardiac catheterization</a> or due to an <a href="/wiki/Intra-aortic_balloon_pump" title="Intra-aortic balloon pump">intra-aortic balloon pump</a>.</p>
<p>Aortic dissection may be a late sequela of <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_surgery" title="Cardiac surgery">cardiac surgery</a>. About 18% of individuals who present with an acute aortic dissection have a history of open-heart surgery. Individuals who have undergone aortic valve replacement for <a href="/wiki/Aortic_insufficiency" title="Aortic insufficiency">aortic insufficiency</a> are at particularly high risk because aortic insufficiency causes increased blood flow in the ascending aorta. This can cause dilatation and weakening of the walls of the ascending aorta.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">Syphilis</a> only potentially causes aortic dissection in its tertiary stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kamalakannan2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kamalakannan2007-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AoDissect_Schema_01a.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="610" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/AoDissect_Schema_01a.png/220px-AoDissect_Schema_01a.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/AoDissect_Schema_01a.png/330px-AoDissect_Schema_01a.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/AoDissect_Schema_01a.png/440px-AoDissect_Schema_01a.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AoDissect_Schema_01a.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>As with all other arteries, the aorta is made up of three layers, the <a href="/wiki/Tunica_intima" title="Tunica intima">intima</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tunica_media" title="Tunica media">media</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunica_adventitia" title="Tunica adventitia">adventitia</a>. The intima is in direct contact with the blood inside the vessel, and mainly consists of a layer of <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelial cells</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Basement_membrane" title="Basement membrane">basement membrane</a>; the media contains connective and muscle tissue, and the vessel is protected on the outside by the adventitia, comprising connective tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hiratzka2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hiratzka2010-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an aortic dissection, blood penetrates the intima and enters the media layer. The high pressure rips the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissue</a> of the media apart along the laminated plane splitting the inner two-thirds and the outer one-third of the media apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seidensticker2008_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seidensticker2008-9">[9]</a></sup> This can propagate along the length of the aorta for a variable distance forward or backwards. Dissections that propagate towards the iliac bifurcation (with the flow of blood) are called anterograde dissections and those that propagate towards the aortic root (opposite of the flow of blood) are called retrograde dissections. The initial tear is usually within 100 mm of the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_valve" title="Aortic valve">aortic valve</a>, so a retrograde dissection can easily compromise the pericardium leading to a hemopericardium. Anterograde dissections may propagate all the way to the iliac bifurcation of the aorta, rupture the aortic wall, or recanalize into the intravascular lumen leading to a double-barrel aorta. The double-barrel aorta relieves the pressure of blood flow and reduces the risk of rupture. Rupture leads to hemorrhaging into a body cavity, and prognosis depends on the area of rupture. Retroperitoneal and pericardial ruptures are both possible.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aortic_dissection_(1)_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="376" data-file-width="500" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Aortic_dissection_%281%29_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg/220px-Aortic_dissection_%281%29_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Aortic_dissection_%281%29_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg/330px-Aortic_dissection_%281%29_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Aortic_dissection_%281%29_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg/440px-Aortic_dissection_%281%29_Victoria_blue-HE.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The initiating event in an aortic dissection is a tear in the intimal lining of the aorta. Due to the high pressures in the aorta, blood enters the media at the point of the tear. The force of the blood entering the media causes the tear to extend. It may extend proximally (closer to the heart) or distally (away from the heart) or both. The blood travels through the media, creating a <a href="/wiki/Pseudoaneurysm" title="Pseudoaneurysm">false lumen</a> (the true lumen is the normal conduit of blood in the aorta). Separating the false lumen from the true lumen is a layer of intimal tissue known as the intimal flap.</p>
<p>The vast majority of aortic dissections originate with an intimal tear in either the <a href="/wiki/Ascending_aorta" title="Ascending aorta">ascending aorta</a> (65%), the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_arch" title="Aortic arch">aortic arch</a> (10%), or just distal to the <a href="/wiki/Ligamentum_arteriosum" title="Ligamentum arteriosum">ligamentum arteriosum</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Descending_aorta" title="Descending aorta">descending thoracic aorta</a> (20%).</p>
<p>As blood flows down the false lumen, it may cause secondary tears in the intima. Through these secondary tears, the blood can re-enter the true lumen.</p>
<p>While it is not always clear why an intimal tear may occur, quite often it involves degeneration of the <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elastin" title="Elastin">elastin</a> that make up the media. This is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cystic_medial_necrosis" title="Cystic medial necrosis">cystic medial necrosis</a> and is most commonly associated with Marfan syndrome and is also associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ehlers-Danlos_syndrome" title="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome">Ehlers-Danlos syndrome</a>.</p>
<p>In about 13% of aortic dissections, no evidence of an intimal tear is found. In these cases, the inciting event is thought to be an intramural <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a> (caused by bleeding within the media). Since no direct connection exists between the true lumen and the false lumen in these cases, diagnosing an aortic dissection by <a href="#Aortogram">aortography</a> is difficult if the <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">etiology</a> is an intramural hematoma. An aortic dissection secondary to an intramural hematoma should be treated the same as one caused by an intimal tear.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the varying symptoms and signs of aortic dissection depending on the initial intimal tear and the extent of the dissection, the proper diagnosis is sometimes difficult to make.</p>
<p>While taking a good history from the individual may be strongly suggestive of an aortic dissection, the diagnosis cannot always be made by history and physical signs alone. Often, the diagnosis is made by visualization of the intimal flap on a diagnostic imaging test. Common tests used to diagnose an aortic dissection include a <a href="/wiki/CT_scan" title="CT scan">CT scan</a> of the chest with iodinated contrast material and a <a href="/wiki/Transesophageal_echocardiogram" title="Transesophageal echocardiogram">transesophageal echocardiogram</a>. The proximity of the aorta to the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> allows the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-frequency_ultrasound" title="High-frequency ultrasound">higher-frequency ultrasound</a> for better anatomical images. Other tests that may be used include an aortogram or magnetic resonance angiogram of the aorta. Each of these tests has pros and cons, and they do not have equal sensitivities and specificities in the diagnosis of aortic dissection.</p>
<p>In general, the imaging technique chosen is based on the pretest likelihood of the diagnosis, availability of the testing modality, patient stability, and the sensitivity and specificity of the test.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="D-dimer">D-dimer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: D-dimer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A blood <a href="/wiki/D-dimer" title="D-dimer">D-dimer</a> level less than 500 ng/ml may be able to rule out the diagnosis of aortic dissection, avoiding the need for further imaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shimony2011_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shimony2011-10">[10]</a></sup> This appears to only apply to those at low risk<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and if the test is done within 24 hours of the onset of symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a> does not advise using this test in making the diagnosis, as evidence is still tentative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chest_X-ray">Chest X-ray</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Chest X-ray">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1575" data-file-width="2052" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg/220px-AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg/330px-AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg/440px-AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AoDiss_ChestXRay.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Aortic dissection on CXR: Note is made of a wide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aortic_knob" title="Aortic knob">aortic knob</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Widening of the <a href="/wiki/Mediastinum" title="Mediastinum">mediastinum</a> on an <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> of the chest has moderate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(tests)" title="Sensitivity (tests)">sensitivity</a> in the setting of an ascending aortic dissection. However, it has low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specificity_(tests)" title="Specificity (tests)">specificity</a>, as many other conditions can cause a widening of the mediastinum on chest X-ray.</p>
<p>The calcium sign, the separation of the intimal calcification from the outer aortic soft tissue border by 10 mm, is a finding on chest X-ray that suggests aortic dissection.</p>
<p>Pleural effusions may be seen on chest X-ray, more commonly in descending aortic dissections. If seen, they are typically in the left hemithorax.</p>
<p>Other findings include obliteration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aortic_knob" title="Aortic knob">aortic knob</a>, depression of the left mainstem bronchus, loss of the paratracheal stripe, and tracheal deviation.</p>
<p>About 12 to 20% of individuals presenting with an aortic dissection have a "normal" chest X-ray; therefore, a normal chest radiograph does not rule out aortic dissection. If the clinical index of suspicion is high, ruling out dissection with another imaging modality (CT angiogram, MRA, aortogram, or transesophageal echo)is imperative.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computed_tomography">Computed tomography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Computed tomography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Computed_tomography_angiography" title="Computed tomography angiography">Computed tomography angiography</a> is a fast, noninvasive test that gives an accurate three-dimensional view of the aorta. These images are produced by taking rapid, thin-cut slices of the chest and abdomen, and combining them in the computer to create cross-sectional slices. To delineate the aorta to the accuracy necessary to make the proper diagnosis, an iodinated contrast material is injected into a peripheral vein. Contrast is injected and the scan performed using a <a href="/wiki/Bolus_tracking" title="Bolus tracking">bolus tracking</a> method. This type of scan is timed to an injection to capture the contrast as it enters the aorta. The scan then follows the contrast as it flows though the vessel. It has a sensitivity of 96 to 100% and a specificity of 96 to 100%. Disadvantages include the need for iodinated contrast material and the inability to diagnose the site of the intimal tear.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MRI">MRI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: MRI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AoDiss_MRT.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="416" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/AoDiss_MRT.jpg/220px-AoDiss_MRT.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/AoDiss_MRT.jpg/330px-AoDiss_MRT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/85/AoDiss_MRT.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AoDiss_MRT.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a> of an aortic dissection<br/>

<i>2</i> Aorta isthmus</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">Magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI) is currently the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard_(test)" title="Gold standard (test)">gold standard test</a> for the detection and assessment of aortic dissection, with a sensitivity of 98% and a specificity of 98%. An MRI examination of the aorta produces a three-dimensional reconstruction of the aorta, allowing the physician to determine the location of the intimal tear and the involvement of branch vessels, and to locate any secondary tears. It is a noninvasive test, does not require the use of iodinated contrast material, and can detect and quantitate the degree of aortic insufficiency.</p>
<p>The disadvantage of the MRI scan in the face of aortic dissection is that it has limited availability and is often located only in larger hospitals, and the scan is relatively time-consuming, which may be dangerous in people who are already very unwell. Due to the high-intensity magnetic fields used during MRI, it is contraindicated in individuals with metallic implants. In addition, many individuals experience <a href="/wiki/Claustrophobia" title="Claustrophobia">claustrophobia</a> while in the MRI scanning tube.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Transesophageal_echocardiogram" title="Transesophageal echocardiogram">transesophageal echocardiogram</a> (TEE) is a relatively good test in the diagnosis of aortic dissection, with a sensitivity up to 98% and a specificity up to 97%. It has become the preferred imaging modality for suspected aortic dissection. It is a relatively noninvasive test, requiring the individual to swallow the echocardiography probe. It is especially good in the evaluation of AI in the setting of ascending aortic dissection, and to determine whether the ostia (origins) of the coronary arteries are involved. While many institutions give sedation during transesophageal echocardiography for added patient comfort, it can be performed in cooperative individuals without the use of sedation. Disadvantages of TEE include the inability to visualize the distal ascending aorta (the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_arch" title="Aortic arch">aortic arch</a>), and the descending abdominal aorta that lies below the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>. A TEE may be technically difficult to perform in individuals with esophageal strictures or <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_varices" title="Esophageal varices">varices</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aortogram">Aortogram</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Aortogram">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aortography" title="Aortography">Aortography</a></div>
<p>An aortogram involves placement of a catheter in the aorta and injection of contrast material while taking X-rays of the aorta. The procedure is known as <a href="/wiki/Aortography" title="Aortography">aortography</a>. Previously thought to be the diagnostic <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard_(test)" title="Gold standard (test)">gold standard</a>, it has been supplanted by other, less-invasive imaging modalities.</p>

<p>Several different classification systems have been used to describe aortic dissections. The systems commonly in use are based on either the anatomy of the dissection or the duration of onset of symptoms prior to presentation. The Stanford system is used more commonly now, as it is more attuned to the management of the patient.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DeBakey">DeBakey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: DeBakey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The DeBakey system, named after cardiothoracic surgeon <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._DeBakey" title="Michael E. DeBakey">Michael E. DeBakey</a>, is an anatomical description of the aortic dissection. It categorizes the dissection based on where the original intimal tear is located and the extent of the dissection (localized to either the ascending aorta or descending aorta or involves both the ascending and descending aorta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeBakey1965_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeBakey1965-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Type I</b> – originates in ascending aorta, and propagates at least to the aortic arch and often beyond it distally. It is most often seen in patients less than 65 years of age and is the most lethal form of the disease.</li>
<li><b>Type II</b> – originates in the ascending aorta and is confined to it.</li>
<li><b>Type III</b> – originates in the descending aorta and rarely extends proximally, but will extend distally. It most often occurs in elderly patients with <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a> and hypertension.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stanford">Stanford</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Stanford">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Stanford classification is divided into two groups, A and B, depending on whether the ascending aorta is involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daily1970_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daily1970-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>A</b> – involves the ascending aorta and/or aortic arch, and possibly the descending aorta. The tear can originate in the ascending aorta, the aortic arch, or more rarely, in the descending aorta. It includes DeBakey types I and II.</li>
<li><b>B</b> – involves the descending aorta or the arch (distal to the left subclavian artery), without the involvement of the ascending aorta. It includes DeBakey type III.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Stanford classification is useful as it follows clinical practice, as type A ascending aortic dissections generally require primary surgical treatment, whereas type B dissections generally are treated medically as initial treatment with surgery reserved for any complications.</p>
<p>The reason for surgical repair of type A dissections is that ascending aortic dissections often involve the aortic valve, which, having lost its suspensory support, telescopes down into the aortic root, resulting in aortic incompetence. This needs to be resuspended to reseat the valve and repair or prevent coronary artery injury. Also, the area of dissection is removed and replaced with a Dacron graft to prevent further dissection from occurring. However, type B dissections are not improved, from a mortality point of view, by the operation, unless leaking, rupture, or compromise to other organs, e.g. kidneys, occurs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prevention is by blood pressure control and not smoking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet2016_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Repair of an enlargement of the ascending aorta from an aneurysm or previously unrecognized and untreated aortic dissections is recommended when greater than 6 cm (2.4 in) in size to decrease the risk of dissection. Repair may be recommended when greater than 4.5 cm (1.8 in) in size if the person has one of the several connective-tissue disorders or a family history of a ruptured aorta.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">

<p>In an acute dissection, treatment choice depends on its location. For Stanford type A (ascending aortic) dissection, surgical management is superior to medical management. For uncomplicated Stanford type B (distal aortic) dissections (including abdominal aortic dissections), medical management is preferred over surgical.</p>
<p>The risk of death due to aortic dissection is highest in the first few hours after the dissection begins, and decreases afterward. Because of this, the therapeutic strategies differ for the treatment of an acute dissection compared to a chronic dissection. An acute dissection is one in which the individual presents within the first two weeks. If the individual has managed to survive this window period, his prognosis is improved. About 66% of all dissections present in the acute phase. Individuals who present two weeks after the onset of the dissection are said to have chronic aortic dissections. These individuals have been self-selected as survivors of the acute episode and can be treated with medical therapy as long as they are stable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">Medication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Medication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aortic dissection generally presents as a hypertensive emergency, and the prime consideration of medical management is strict blood pressure control. The target blood pressure should be a <a href="/wiki/Mean_arterial_pressure" title="Mean arterial pressure">mean arterial pressure</a> (MAP) of 60 to 75 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MmHg" title="MmHg">mmHg</a>, or the lowest blood pressure tolerated. Initial decreases should be by about 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2013_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-White2013-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another factor is to reduce the shear-force dP/dt (force of ejection of blood from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a>). Long-term management of physical, emotional, and psychological stresses are important to controlling blood pressure.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_blockers" title="Beta blockers">Beta blockers</a> are the first-line treatment for patients with acute and chronic aortic dissection. In acute dissection, rapidly acting, titratable parenteral agents (such as <a href="/wiki/Esmolol" title="Esmolol">esmolol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propranolol" title="Propranolol">propranolol</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Labetalol" title="Labetalol">labetalol</a>) are preferred. Vasodilators such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitroprusside" title="Sodium nitroprusside">sodium nitroprusside</a> can be considered for patients with ongoing hypertension, but they should never be used alone, as they generally cause reflex tachycardia.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcium_channel_blocker" title="Calcium channel blocker">Calcium channel blockers</a> can be used in the treatment of aortic dissection, particularly a contraindication to the use of beta blockers exists. The calcium channel blockers typically used are <a href="/wiki/Verapamil" title="Verapamil">verapamil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diltiazem" title="Diltiazem">diltiazem</a>, because of their combined vasodilator and negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inotropic" title="Inotropic">inotropic</a> effects.</p>
<p>If the individual has refractory hypertension (persistent hypertension on the maximum doses of three different classes of antihypertensive agents), an involvement of the renal arteries in the aortic dissection plane should be considered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgical">Surgical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Surgical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Indications for the surgical treatment of aortic dissection include an acute proximal aortic dissection and an acute distal aortic dissection with one or more complications. Complications include compromise of a vital organ, rupture or impending rupture of the aorta, retrograde dissection into the ascending aorta, and a history of Marfan syndrome or Ehlers-Danlos syndrome.</p>
<p>The objective in the surgical management of aortic dissection is to resect (remove) the most severely damaged segments of the aorta and to obliterate the entry of blood into the false lumen (both at the initial intimal tear and any secondary tears along the vessel). While excision of the intimal tear may be performed, it does not significantly change mortality.</p>
<p>The particular treatment used depends on the segment or segments of aorta involved. Some treatments are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Open_aortic_surgery" title="Open aortic surgery">Open aortic surgery</a> with replacement of the damaged section of aorta with a tube graft (often made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dacron" title="Dacron">Dacron</a>) when no damage to the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_valve" title="Aortic valve">aortic valve</a> is seen</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bentall_procedure" title="Bentall procedure">Bentall procedure</a> — replacement of the damaged section of aorta and replacement of the aortic valve</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_procedure" title="David procedure">David procedure</a> — replacement of the damaged section of aorta and reimplantation of the aortic valve</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TEVAR" title="TEVAR">Thoracic endovascular aortic repair</a>, a minimally invasive surgical procedure usually combined with on-going medical management</li>
<li>Replacement of the damaged section of aorta with a sutureless <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vascular_ring_connector&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vascular ring connector (page does not exist)">vascular ring connector</a>-reinforced Dacron graft: The vascular ring connector is a titanic ring used as a stent in the vascular graft to achieve a quick, blood-sealed, and sutureless anastomosis. Two furrows on the surface of the ring are for fixation of the vascular graft and the aorta. The tapes used to tie against the ring provide a larger contact surface area than the traditional stitches, thus it provides stronger anastomosis and better surgical results.</li>
</ul>
<p>A number of comorbid conditions increase the surgical risk of repair of an aortic dissection. These conditions include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Prolonged preoperative evaluation (increased length of time prior to surgery)</li>
<li>Advanced age</li>
<li>Comorbid disease states (e.g.: <a href="/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease" title="Coronary artery disease">coronary artery disease</a>)</li>
<li>Aneurysm leakage</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cardiac_tamponade" title="Cardiac tamponade">Cardiac tamponade</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">Shock</a></li>
<li>Past history of myocardial infarction</li>
<li>History of <a href="/wiki/Kidney_failure" title="Kidney failure">kidney failure</a> (either acute or chronic kidney failure)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Follow-up">Follow-up</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Follow-up">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="1173" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png/220px-TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png/330px-TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png/440px-TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TypeBdisectionlumonclosed.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Closure of the lumen of a Type B aortic dissection following medical management</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The long-term follow-up in individuals who survive aortic dissection involves strict blood pressure control. The relative risk of late rupture of an <a href="/wiki/Aortic_aneurysm" title="Aortic aneurysm">aortic aneurysm</a> is 10 times higher in individuals who have uncontrolled hypertension, compared to individuals with a systolic pressure below 130 mmHg.</p>
<p>The risk of death is highest in the first two years after the acute event, and individuals should be followed closely during this time period. About 29% of late deaths following surgery are due to rupture of either a dissecting aneurysm or another aneurysm. In addition, a 17% to 25% incidence exists of new aneurysm formation, typically due to dilatation of the residual false lumen. These new aneurysms are more likely to rupture, due to their thinner walls.</p>
<p>Serial imaging of the aorta is suggested, with MRI being the preferred imaging technique.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Establishing the incidence of aortic dissection has been difficult because many cases are only diagnosed after death (which may have been attributed to another cause), and is often initially misdiagnosed. Aortic dissection affects an estimated 2.0–3.5 people per every 100,000 every year. Studies from Sweden suggest that the incidence of aortic dissection may be rising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olsson2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson2006-16">[16]</a></sup> Men are more commonly affected than women: 65% of all people with aortic dissection are male. The mean age at diagnosis is 63 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hiratzka2010_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hiratzka2010-8">[8]</a></sup> In females before the age of 40, half of all aortic dissections occur during <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> (typically in the third trimester or early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postpartum" title="Postpartum">postpartum</a> period).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ardehali2013_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ardehali2013-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Of all people with aortic dissection, 40% die immediately and do not reach a hospital in time. Of the remainder, 1% die every hour, making prompt diagnosis and treatment a priority. Even after diagnosis, 5–20% die during surgery or in the immediate postoperative period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hiratzka2010_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hiratzka2010-8">[8]</a></sup> In ascending aortic dissection, if surgery is decided to be not appropriate, 75% die within 2 weeks. With aggressive treatment, 30-day survival for thoracic dissections may be as high as 90%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woo2009_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woo2009-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest fully documented case of aortic dissection is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Frank_Nicholls" title="Frank Nicholls">Frank Nicholls</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a> report of <a href="/wiki/George_II_of_Great_Britain" title="George II of Great Britain">King George II of Great Britain</a>, who had been found dead on 25 October 1760; the report describes dissection of the aortic arch and into the pericardium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholls1761_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholls1761-19">[19]</a></sup> The term "aortic dissection" was introduced by the French physician J.P. Maunoir in 1802, and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Laennec" title="René Laennec">René Laennec</a> labelled the condition "dissecting aneurysm".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard1979_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard1979-20">[20]</a></sup> London cardiologist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bevill_Peacock" title="Thomas Bevill Peacock">Thomas Bevill Peacock</a> contributed to the understanding of the condition by publishing two series of the cases described in the literature so far: 19 cases in an 1843 review, and 80 in 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard1979_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard1979-20">[20]</a></sup> The characteristic symptom of tearing pain in the chest was recognized in 1855 when a case was diagnosed in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard1979_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard1979-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surgery for aortic dissection was first introduced and developed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._DeBakey" title="Michael E. DeBakey">Michael E. DeBakey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denton_Cooley" title="Denton Cooley">Denton Cooley</a>, and Oscar Creech, cardiac surgeons associated with the <a href="/wiki/Baylor_College_of_Medicine" title="Baylor College of Medicine">Baylor College of Medicine</a>, Houston, Texas, in 1954. DeBakey developed aortic dissection himself at age 97 in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup> and underwent surgery in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Altman2006_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Altman2006-21">[21]</a></sup> Endovascular treatment of aortic dissection was developed in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Criado2011_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Criado2011-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ritter_rules">Ritter rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aortic_dissection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Ritter rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ritter rules are a compilation of reminders, symptoms, and risk factors designed to prevent the misdiagnosis of thoracic aortic dissection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tadcoalition.36beta.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-tadcoalition.36beta.com-22">[22]</a></sup> The rules were named for actor <a href="/wiki/John_Ritter" title="John Ritter">John Ritter</a>, who died from a thoracic aortic dissection on September 11, 2003. Ritter was initially misdiagnosed and subsequently treated for a heart attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_News_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_News-23">[23]</a></sup> The Ritter rules were developed by Dianna Milewicz, of the University of Texas Medical School, Houston, and were jointly published in March 2010 by John Ritter Foundation and the Thoracic Aortic Disease Coalition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tadcoalition.36beta.com_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-tadcoalition.36beta.com-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_News_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_News-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
