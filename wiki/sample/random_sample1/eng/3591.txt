<p>A <b>weapon of mass destruction</b> (<b>WMD</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiological_weapon" title="Radiological weapon">radiological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a> that can kill and bring significant harm to a large number of life or cause great damage to human-made structures (e.g. buildings), natural structures (e.g. mountains), or the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. The scope and usage of the term has evolved and been disputed, often signifying more politically than technically. Originally coined in reference to <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing" title="Strategic bombing">aerial bombing</a> with <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material#Chemical" title="Explosive material">chemical explosives</a>, since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> it has come to refer to large-scale weaponry of other technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiological_warfare" title="Radiological warfare">radiological</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_uses_of_term">Early uses of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early uses of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first use of the term "weapon of mass destruction" on record is by <a href="/wiki/Cosmo_Gordon_Lang" title="Cosmo Gordon Lang">Cosmo Gordon Lang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, in 1937 in reference to the aerial <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Guernica" title="Bombing of Guernica">bombardment</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guernica_(town)" title="Guernica (town)">Guernica</a>, Spain:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Who can think at this present time without a sickening of the heart of the appalling slaughter, the suffering, the manifold misery brought by war <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">to Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">to China</a>? Who can think without horror of what another widespread war would mean, waged as it would be with all the new weapons of mass destruction?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the time, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> had not been developed. Japan conducted research on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_weapons" title="Biological weapons">biological weapons</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Unit_731" title="Unit 731">Unit 731</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weapons" title="Chemical weapons">chemical weapons</a> had seen wide battlefield use in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. They were outlawed by the Geneva Protocol of 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Italy used mustard gas against civilians and soldiers in Ethiopia in 1935–36.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki</a> that ended <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the term came to refer more to non-<a href="/wiki/Conventional_weapon" title="Conventional weapon">conventional weapons</a>. The application of the term to specifically nuclear and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiological_weapons" title="Radiological weapons">radiological weapons</a> is traced by <a href="/wiki/William_Safire" title="William Safire">William Safire</a> to the Russian phrase "Оружие массового поражения" – <i>oruzhiye massovogo porazheniya</i> (weapons of mass destruction).</p>
<p>He credits James Goodby (of the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Institution" title="Brookings Institution">Brookings Institution</a>) with tracing what he considers the earliest known English-language use soon after the nuclear bombing of <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagasaki,_Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki, Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a> (although it is not quite verbatim): a communique from a 15 November 1945, meeting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_Truman" title="Harry Truman">Harry Truman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mackenzie_King" title="Mackenzie King">Mackenzie King</a> (probably drafted by <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a>, as Bush claimed in 1970) referred to "weapons adaptable to mass destruction."</p>
<p>That exact phrase, says Safire, was also used by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Baruch" title="Bernard Baruch">Bernard Baruch</a> in 1946 (in a speech at the United Nations probably written by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Bayard_Swope" title="Herbert Bayard Swope">Herbert Bayard Swope</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The phrase found its way into the very first resolution adopted by the United Nations General assembly in January 1946 in London, which used the wording "the elimination from national armaments of atomic weapons and of all other weapons adaptable to mass destruction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The resolution also created the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United Nations Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a> (predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA)</a>).</p>
<p>An exact use of this term was given in a lecture "<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">Atomic Energy</a> as an Atomic Problem" by <a href="/wiki/J._Robert_Oppenheimer" title="J. Robert Oppenheimer">J. Robert Oppenheimer</a>. The lecture was delivered to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Foreign_Service" title="United States Foreign Service">Foreign Service</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Department" title="State Department">State Department</a>, on 17 September 1947. The lecture is reprinted in <i>The Open Mind</i> (New York: Simon &amp; Schuster, 1955).</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"It is a very far reaching control which would eliminate the rivalry between nations in this field, which would prevent the surreptitious arming of one nation against another, which would provide some cushion of time before atomic attack, and presumably therefore before any attack with weapons of mass destruction, and which would go a long way toward removing atomic energy at least as a source of conflict between the powers."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The term was also used in the introduction to the hugely influential U.S. government document known as <a href="/wiki/NSC-68" title="NSC-68">NSC-68</a> written in April 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During a speech at <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a> on 12 September 1962, President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> spoke of not filling space "with weapons of mass destruction, but with instruments of knowledge and understanding."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The following month, during a televised presentation about the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a> on 22 October 1962, Kennedy made reference to "offensive weapons of sudden mass destruction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>An early use of the exact phrase in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_treaty" title="International treaty">international treaty</a> was in the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a> of 1967, but no definition was provided.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_its_use">Evolution of its use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution of its use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the term "weapons of mass destruction" was primarily a reference to nuclear weapons. At the time, in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world#Modern_political" title="Western world">West</a> the euphemism "strategic weapons" was used to refer to the American nuclear arsenal, which was presented as a necessary deterrent against nuclear or conventional attack from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutual_Assured_Destruction" title="Mutual Assured Destruction">Mutual Assured Destruction</a>.</p>
<p>Subsequent to <a href="/wiki/Operation_Opera" title="Operation Opera">Operation Opera</a>, the destruction of a pre-operational nuclear reactor inside Iraq by the Israeli Air Force, the Israeli prime minister, <a href="/wiki/Menachem_Begin" title="Menachem Begin">Menachem Begin</a>, countered criticism by saying that "on no account shall we permit an enemy to develop weapons of mass destruction against the people of Israel." This policy of pre-emptive action against real or perceived weapons of mass destruction became known as the <a href="/wiki/Begin_Doctrine" title="Begin Doctrine">Begin Doctrine</a>.</p>
<p>The term "weapons of mass destruction" continued to see periodic use, usually in the context of nuclear <a href="/wiki/Arms_control" title="Arms control">arms control</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> used it during the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk_Summit" title="Reykjavík Summit">Reykjavík Summit</a>, when referring to the 1967 <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Reagan's successor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_H.W._Bush" title="George H.W. Bush">George H.W. Bush</a>, used the term in an 1989 speech to the United Nations, primarily in reference to chemical arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The end of the Cold War reduced U.S. reliance on nuclear weapons as a deterrent, causing it to shift its focus to disarmament. With the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Kuwait" title="Invasion of Kuwait">invasion of Kuwait</a> and 1991 <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, Iraq's nuclear, biological, and chemical weapons programs became a particular concern of the first <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_George_H._W._Bush" title="Presidency of George H. W. Bush">Bush Administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Following the war, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> and other western politicians and media continued to use the term, usually in reference to ongoing attempts to dismantle <a href="/wiki/Iraq_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Iraq and weapons of mass destruction">Iraq's weapons programs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/11_September_2001_attacks" title="11 September 2001 attacks">11 September 2001 attacks</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2001_anthrax_attacks" title="2001 anthrax attacks">2001 anthrax attacks</a> in the United States, an increased fear of nonconventional weapons and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetrical_warfare" title="Asymmetrical warfare">asymmetrical warfare</a> took hold in many countries. The fear reached a crescendo with the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Iraq_disarmament_crisis" title="Iraq disarmament crisis">Iraq disarmament crisis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Niger_uranium_forgeries" title="Niger uranium forgeries">alleged existence</a> of weapons of mass destruction in Iraq that became the primary justification for the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>. However, American forces found none in Iraq. (Old stockpiles of chemical munitions including <a href="/wiki/Sarin" title="Sarin">sarin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_mustard" title="Sulfur mustard">mustard agents</a> were found, but all were considered to be unusable because of corrosion.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Iraq, however, declared a chemical weapons stockpile in 2009. The stockpile contained mainly chemical precursors, but some warheads remained usable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of its prolific use and (worldwide) public profile during this period, the <a href="/wiki/American_Dialect_Society" title="American Dialect Society">American Dialect Society</a> voted "weapons of mass destruction" (and its abbreviation, "WMD") the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_of_the_Year" title="Word of the Year">word of the year</a> in 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and in 2003 <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior_State_University" title="Lake Superior State University">Lake Superior State University</a> added WMD to its list of terms banished for "<i>Mis-use, Over-use and General Uselessness</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_complaint" title="Criminal complaint">criminal complaint</a> against the main suspect of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon" title="Boston Marathon">Boston Marathon</a> bombing of 15 April 2013, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> refers to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure-cooker" title="Pressure-cooker">pressure-cooker</a> improvised <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">bomb</a> as a "weapon of mass destruction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_the_term">Definitions of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitions of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic">Strategic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Strategic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most widely used definition of "weapons of mass destruction" is that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_weapons" title="Biological weapons">biological</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weapons" title="Chemical weapons">chemical weapons</a> (NBC) although there is no <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> or customary <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> that contains an authoritative definition. Instead, international law has been used with respect to the specific categories of weapons within WMD, and not to WMD as a whole. While nuclear, chemical and biological weapons are regarded as the three major types of WMDs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMD_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMD-18">[18]</a></sup> some analysts have argued that radiological materials as well as missile technology and delivery systems such as aircraft and ballistic missiles could be labeled as WMDs as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMD_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-WMD-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The abbreviations NBC (for nuclear, biological and chemical) or CBR (chemical, biological, radiological) are used with regards to battlefield protection systems for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_vehicles" title="Armored vehicles">armored vehicles</a>, because all three involve insidious toxins that can be carried through the air and can be protected against with vehicle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_filtration" title="Air filtration">air filtration</a> systems.</p>
<p>However, there is an argument that nuclear and biological weapons do not belong in the same category as chemical and "<a href="/wiki/Dirty_bomb" title="Dirty bomb">dirty bomb</a>" radiological weapons, which have limited destructive potential (and close to none, as far as property is concerned), whereas nuclear and biological weapons have the unique ability to kill large numbers of people with very small amounts of material, and thus could be said to belong in a class by themselves.</p>
<p>The NBC definition has also been used in official U.S. documents, by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. President</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">U.S. Central Intelligence Agency</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">U.S. Government Accountability Office</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other documents expand the definition of WMD to also include radiological or <a href="/wiki/Conventional_weapon" title="Conventional weapon">conventional weapons</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_military" title="US military">U.S. military</a> refers to WMD as:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear weapons capable of a high order of destruction or causing mass casualties and exclude the means of transporting or propelling the weapon where such means is a separable and divisible part from the weapon. Also called WMD.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>This may also refer to nuclear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBMs</a> (intercontinental ballistic missiles).</p>
<p>The significance of the words <i>separable and divisible part of the weapon</i> is that missiles such as the <a href="/wiki/Pershing_II" title="Pershing II">Pershing II</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SCUD" title="SCUD">SCUD</a> are considered weapons of mass destruction, while aircraft capable of carrying bombloads are not.</p>
<p>In 2004, the United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Butler_Review" title="Butler Review">Butler Review</a> recognized the "considerable and long-standing academic debate about the proper interpretation of the phrase ‘weapons of mass destruction’". The committee set out to avoid the general term but when using it, employed the definition of <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_687" title="United Nations Security Council Resolution 687">United Nations Security Council Resolution 687</a>, which defined the systems which Iraq was required to abandon:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Nuclear weapons or nuclear-weapons-usable material or any sub-systems or components or any research, development, support or manufacturing facilities relating to [nuclear weapons].</li>
<li>Chemical and biological weapons and all stocks of agents and all related subsystems and components and all research,development,support and manufacturing facilities.</li>
<li>Ballistic missiles with a range greater than 150 kilometres and related major parts, and repair and production facilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Chemical weapons expert Gert G. Harigel considers only nuclear weapons true weapons of mass destruction, because "only nuclear weapons are completely indiscriminate by their explosive power, heat radiation and radioactivity, and only they should therefore be called a weapon of mass destruction". He prefers to call chemical and biological weapons "weapons of terror" when aimed against civilians and "weapons of intimidation" for soldiers.</p>
<p>Testimony of one such soldier expresses the same viewpoint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> For a period of several months in the winter of 2002–2003, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Deputy_Secretary_of_Defense" title="US Deputy Secretary of Defense">U.S. Deputy Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wolfowitz" title="Paul Wolfowitz">Paul Wolfowitz</a> frequently used the term "weapons of mass terror," apparently also recognizing the distinction between the psychological and the physical effects of many things currently falling into the WMD category.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustavo_Bell_Lemus" title="Gustavo Bell Lemus">Gustavo Bell Lemus</a>, the Vice President of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, at 9 July 2001 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Illicit_Trade_in_Small_Arms" title="United Nations Conference on the Illicit Trade in Small Arms">United Nations Conference on the Illicit Trade in Small Arms</a> and Light Weapons in All Its Aspects, quoted the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Millennium_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Millennium Report (page does not exist)">Millennium Report</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary General of the United Nations">UN Secretary-General</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">General Assembly</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a> said that <a href="/wiki/Small_arms" title="Small arms">small arms</a> could be described as WMD because the fatalities they cause "<i>dwarf that of all other weapons systems – and in most years greatly exceed the toll of the atomic bombs that devastated <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagasaki,_Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki, Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a></i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>An additional condition often implicitly applied to WMD is that the use of the weapons must be strategic. In other words, they would be designed to "<i>have consequences far outweighing the size and effectiveness of the weapons themselves</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The strategic nature of WMD also defines their function in the military doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Total_war" title="Total war">total war</a> as targeting the means a country would use to support and supply its war effort, specifically its population, industry, and natural resources.</p>
<p>Within U.S. <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense" title="Civil defense">civil defense</a> organizations, the category is now <b>Chemical, Biological, Radiological, Nuclear, and Explosive (CBRNE)</b>, which defines WMD as:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>(1) Any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">incendiary</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_gas" title="Poison gas">poison gas</a>, bomb, <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenade</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> having a <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellant</a> charge of more than four ounces [113 g], <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> having an explosive or incendiary charge of more than one-quarter ounce [7 g], or <a href="/wiki/Land_mine" title="Land mine">mine</a> or device similar to the above. (2) Poison gas. (3) Any weapon involving a disease organism. (4) Any weapon that is designed to release <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radiation</a> at a level dangerous to human life.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For the general purposes of national defense,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> the U.S. Code<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> defines a weapon of mass destruction as:</p>
<ul>
<li>any weapon or device that is intended, or has the capability, to cause death or serious bodily injury to a significant number of people through the release, dissemination, or impact of:
<ul>
<li>toxic or poisonous chemicals or their precursors</li>
<li>a disease organism</li>
<li>radiation or radioactivity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>For the purposes of the prevention of weapons <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon_proliferation" title="Chemical weapon proliferation">proliferation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> the U.S. Code defines weapons of mass destruction as "chemical, biological, and nuclear weapons, and chemical, biological, and nuclear materials used in the manufacture of such weapons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_.28civilian.29">Criminal (civilian)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criminal (civilian)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For the purposes of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a> concerning terrorism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> weapons of mass destruction are defined as:</p>
<ul>
<li>any "destructive device" defined as any explosive, incendiary, or poison gas – bomb, grenade, rocket having a propellant charge of more than four ounces, missile having an explosive or incendiary charge of more than one-quarter ounce, mine, or device similar to any of the devices described in the preceding clauses<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>any weapon that is designed or intended to cause death or serious bodily injury through the release, dissemination, or impact of toxic or poisonous chemicals, or their precursors</li>
<li>any weapon involving a biological agent, toxin, or vector</li>
<li>any weapon that is designed to release radiation or radioactivity at a level dangerous to human life<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a>'s definition is similar to that presented above from the terrorism statute:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>any "destructive device" as defined in Title 18 USC Section 921: any explosive, incendiary, or poison gas – bomb, grenade, rocket having a propellant charge of more than four ounces, missile having an explosive or incendiary charge of more than one-quarter ounce, mine, or device similar to any of the devices described in the preceding clauses</li>
<li>any weapon designed or intended to cause death or serious bodily injury through the release, dissemination, or impact of toxic or poisonous chemicals or their precursors</li>
<li>any weapon involving a disease organism</li>
<li>any weapon designed to release radiation or radioactivity at a level dangerous to human life</li>
<li>any device or weapon designed or intended to cause death or serious bodily injury by causing a malfunction of or destruction of an aircraft or other vehicle that carries humans or of an aircraft or other vehicle whose malfunction or destruction may cause said aircraft or other vehicle to cause death or serious bodily injury to humans who may be within range of the vector in its course of travel or the travel of its debris.</li>
</ul>
<p>Indictments and convictions for possession and use of WMD such as truck bombs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pipe_bombs" title="Pipe bombs">pipe bombs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> shoe bombs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and cactus needles coated with a biological toxin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> have been obtained under 18 USC 2332a.</p>
<p>As defined by 18 USC §2332 (a), a Weapon of Mass Destruction is:</p>
<ul>
<li>(a) any destructive device as defined in section 921 of the title;</li>
<li>(B) any weapon that is designed or intended to cause death or serious bodily injury through the release, dissemination, or impact of toxic or poisonous chemicals, or their precursors;</li>
<li>(C) any weapon involving a biological agent, toxin, or vector (as those terms are defined in section 178 of this title); or</li>
<li>(D) any weapon that is designed to release radiation or radioactivity at a level dangerous to human life;</li>
</ul>
<p>Under the same statute, conspiring, attempting, threatening, or using a Weapon of Mass Destruction may be imprisoned for any term of years or for life, and if resulting in death, be punishable by death or by imprisonment for any terms of years or for life. They can also be asked to pay a maximum fine of $250,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> reported on 30 March 2006: "Jurors asked the judge in the death penalty trial of <a href="/wiki/Zacarias_Moussaoui" title="Zacarias Moussaoui">Zacarias Moussaoui</a> today to define the term 'weapons of mass destruction' and were told it includes airplanes used as missiles". Moussaoui was indicted and tried for the use of airplanes as WMD.</p>
<p>The surviving <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon_bombing" title="Boston Marathon bombing">Boston Marathon bombing</a> perpetrator, <a href="/wiki/Dzhokhar_Tsarnaev" title="Dzhokhar Tsarnaev">Dzhokhar Tsarnaev</a>, was charged in June 2013 with the federal offense of "use of a weapon of mass destruction" after he and his brother <a href="/wiki/Tamerlan_Tsarnaev" title="Tamerlan Tsarnaev">Tamerlan Tsarnaev</a> allegedly placed crude shrapnel bombs, made from pressure cookers packed with ball bearings and nails, near the finish line of the Boston Marathon. He was convicted in April 2015. The bombing resulted in three deaths and at least 264 injuries.</p>

<p>The development and use of WMD is governed by several international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conventions_and_treaties" title="Conventions and treaties">conventions and treaties</a>, although not all countries have signed and ratified them:</p>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Test_Ban_Treaty" title="Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty</a> (CTBT, has not entered into force as of 2015)</li>

</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use.2C_possession_and_access">Use, possession and access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use, possession and access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_weapons">Nuclear weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nuclear weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_with_nuclear_weapons" title="List of countries with nuclear weapons">List of countries with nuclear weapons</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The only country to have used a nuclear weapon in war is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_United_States_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="The United States and weapons of mass destruction">United States</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">dropped two atomic bombs</a> on the Japanese cities of <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a> during World War II. There are eight countries that have declared they possess nuclear weapons and are known to have tested a nuclear weapon, only five of which are members of the NPT. The eight are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="People's Republic of China and weapons of mass destruction">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/France_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="France and weapons of mass destruction">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/India_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="India and weapons of mass destruction">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="North Korea and weapons of mass destruction">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Pakistan and weapons of mass destruction">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Russia and weapons of mass destruction">Russia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_United_Kingdom_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="The United Kingdom and weapons of mass destruction">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_and_the_United_States" title="Nuclear weapons and the United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Israel_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Israel and weapons of mass destruction">Israel</a> is considered by most analysts to have nuclear weapons numbering in the low hundreds as well, but maintains an official policy of nuclear ambiguity, neither denying nor confirming its nuclear status.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/South_Africa_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="South Africa and weapons of mass destruction">South Africa</a> developed a small nuclear arsenal in the 1980s but disassembled them in the early 1990s, making it the only country to have fully given up an independently developed nuclear weapons arsenal. <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, Kazakhstan, and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> inherited stockpiles of nuclear arms following the break-up of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, but relinquished them to the Russian Federation.</p>
<p>Countries with access to nuclear weapons through <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_sharing" title="Nuclear sharing">nuclear sharing</a> agreements include Belgium, <a href="/wiki/Germany_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Germany and weapons of mass destruction">Germany</a>, Italy, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Netherlands and weapons of mass destruction">Netherlands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_politics">United States politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: United States politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the indiscriminate impact of WMD, the fear of a WMD attack has shaped political policies and campaigns, fostered social movements, and has been the central theme of many films. Support for different levels of WMD development and control varies nationally and internationally. Yet understanding of the nature of the threats is not high, in part because of imprecise usage of the term by politicians and the media.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blueprint_abomb.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3672" data-file-width="5141" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Blueprint_abomb.JPG/210px-Blueprint_abomb.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Blueprint_abomb.JPG/315px-Blueprint_abomb.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Blueprint_abomb.JPG/420px-Blueprint_abomb.JPG 2x" width="210"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blueprint_abomb.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An atomic-bomb blueprint</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fear of WMD, or of threats diminished by the possession of WMD, has long been used to catalyze public support for various WMD policies. They include mobilization of pro- and anti-WMD campaigners alike, and generation of popular political support. The term WMD may be used as a powerful <a href="/wiki/Buzzword" title="Buzzword">buzzword</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> or to generate a <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_fear" title="Culture of fear">culture of fear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It is also used ambiguously, particularly by not distinguishing among the different types of WMD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>A television commercial called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daisy_(television_commercial)" title="Daisy (television commercial)">Daisy</a></i>, promoting Democrat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a>'s 1964 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1964" title="U.S. presidential election, 1964">presidential candidacy</a>, invoked the fear of a nuclear war and was an element in Johnson's subsequent election.</p>
<p>More recently, the threat of potential WMD in Iraq was used by President George W. Bush as justification for the 2003 invasion of Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Broad reference to Iraqi WMD in general was seen as an element of President Bush's arguments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-47">[47]</a></sup> The claim that Iraq possessed Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD) led to the invasion of Iraq in 2003 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multinational_force_in_Iraq" title="Multinational force in Iraq">Coalition forces</a>.</p>
<p>Over 500 munitions were discovered throughout Iraq since 2003 containing chemical agents mustard and Sarin gas, produced in the 1980s and no longer usable as originally intended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, Polish troops found nineteen 1980s-era rocket warheads, thwarting an attempt by militants to buy them at $5000 each. Some of the rockets contained extremely deteriorated nerve agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Heritage_Dictionary" title="American Heritage Dictionary">American Heritage Dictionary</a> defines a weapon of mass destruction as: "a weapon that can cause widespread destruction or kill large numbers of people, especially a nuclear, chemical, or biological weapon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In other words, it does not have to be nuclear, biological or chemical (NBC). For example, the terrorist for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon_bombings" title="Boston Marathon bombings">Boston Marathon bombings</a> was charged under <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> law <i>18 U.S.C. 2332A</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> for using a weapon of mass destruction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and that was a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_cooker_bomb" title="Pressure cooker bomb">pressure cooker bomb</a>. In other words, it was a weapon that caused large-scale death and destruction, without being an NBC weapon.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_coverage">Media coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Media coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004, the Center for International and Security Studies at Maryland (CISSM) released a report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> examining the media’s coverage of WMD issues during three separate periods: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_testing" title="Nuclear testing">nuclear weapons tests</a> by India and Pakistan in May 1998; the U.S. announcement of evidence of a <a href="/wiki/North_Korea_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="North Korea and weapons of mass destruction">North Korean nuclear weapons program</a> in October 2002; and revelations about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iran%27s_nuclear_program" title="Iran's nuclear program">Iran's nuclear program</a> in May 2003. The CISSM report notes that poor coverage resulted less from political <a href="/wiki/Media_bias" title="Media bias">bias among the media</a> than from tired journalistic conventions. The report’s major findings were that:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>Most media outlets represented WMD as a monolithic menace, failing to adequately distinguish between weapons programs and actual weapons or to address the real differences among chemical, biological, nuclear, and radiological weapons.</i></li>
<li><i>Most journalists accepted the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_the_George_W._Bush_administration" title="Foreign policy of the George W. Bush administration">Bush administration’s</a> formulation of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_on_Terrorism" title="War on Terrorism">War on Terror</a>" as a campaign against WMD, in contrast to coverage during the <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Clinton</a> era, when many journalists made careful distinctions between acts of terrorism and the acquisition and use of WMD.</i></li>
<li><i>Many stories stenographically reported the incumbent administration’s perspective on WMD, giving too little critical examination of the way officials framed the events, issues, threats, and policy options.</i></li>
<li><i>Too few stories proffered alternative perspectives to official line, a problem exacerbated by the <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalistic</a> prioritizing of breaking-news stories and the "<a href="/wiki/Inverted_pyramid" title="Inverted pyramid">inverted pyramid</a>" style of storytelling.</i></li>
</ol>
<p>In a separate study published in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> a group of researchers assessed the effects reports and retractions in the media had on people’s <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a> regarding the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Iraq and weapons of mass destruction">search for WMD in Iraq</a> during the 2003 Iraq War. The study focused on populations in two <a href="/wiki/Coalition_of_the_willing" title="Coalition of the willing">coalition</a> countries (Australia and the United States) and one opposed to the war (Germany). Results showed that U.S. citizens generally did not correct initial misconceptions regarding WMD, even following disconfirmation; Australian and German citizens were more responsive to retractions. Dependence on the initial source of information led to a substantial minority of Americans exhibiting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_memories" title="False memories">false memory</a> that WMD were indeed discovered, while they were not. This led to three conclusions:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>The repetition of tentative news stories, even if they are subsequently disconfirmed, can assist in the creation of false memories in a substantial proportion of people.</i></li>
<li><i>Once information is published, its subsequent correction does not alter people's beliefs unless they are suspicious about the motives underlying the events the news stories are about.</i></li>
<li><i>When people ignore corrections, they do so irrespective of how certain they are that the corrections occurred.</i></li>
</ol>
<p>A poll conducted between June and September 2003 asked people whether they thought evidence of WMD had been discovered in Iraq since the war ended. They were also asked which media sources they relied upon. Those who obtained their news primarily from Fox News were three times as likely to believe that evidence of WMD had been discovered in Iraq than those who relied on PBS and NPR for their news, and one third more likely than those who primarily watched CBS.</p>

<p>In 2006 Fox News reported the claims of two Republican lawmakers that WMDs had been found in Iraq,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> based upon unclassified portions of a report by the <a href="/wiki/National_Ground_Intelligence_Center" title="National Ground Intelligence Center">National Ground Intelligence Center</a>. Quoting from the report, Senator <a href="/wiki/Rick_Santorum" title="Rick Santorum">Rick Santorum</a> said "Since 2003, coalition forces have recovered approximately 500 weapons munitions which contain degraded mustard or sarin nerve agent". According to David Kay, who appeared before the U.S. House Armed Services Committee to discuss these badly corroded munitions, they were leftovers, many years old, improperly stored or destroyed by the Iraqis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Charles Duelfer agreed, stating on NPR's <i>Talk of the Nation</i>: "When I was running the ISG – the Iraq Survey Group – we had a couple of them that had been turned in to these IEDs, the improvised explosive devices. But they are local hazards. They are not a major, you know, weapon of mass destruction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, wikileaks would show that WMDs of these kinds continued to be found as the Iraqi occupation continued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many news agencies, including Fox News, reported the conclusions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> that, based upon the investigation of the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_Survey_Group" title="Iraq Survey Group">Iraq Survey Group</a>, WMDs are yet to be found in Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_perceptions">Public perceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Public perceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Awareness and opinions of WMD have varied during the course of their history. Their threat is a source of unease, security, and pride to different people. The anti-WMD movement is embodied most in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament" title="Nuclear disarmament">nuclear disarmament</a>, and led to the formation of the British <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a> in 1957.</p>
<p>In order to increase awareness of all kinds of WMD, in 2004 the nuclear physicist and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rotblat" title="Joseph Rotblat">Joseph Rotblat</a> inspired the creation of The WMD Awareness Programme<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> to provide trustworthy and up to date information on WMD worldwide.</p>
<p>In 1998 <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Mexico" title="University of New Mexico">University of New Mexico</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_for_Public_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute for Public Policy (page does not exist)">Institute for Public Policy</a> released their third report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> on U.S. perceptions – including the general public, politicians and scientists – of nuclear weapons since the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Risks of nuclear conflict, proliferation, and terrorism were seen as substantial.</p>
<p>While maintenance of the U.S. nuclear arsenal was considered above average in importance, there was widespread support for a reduction in the stockpile, and very little support for developing and testing new nuclear weapons.</p>
<p>Also in 1998, but after the UNM survey was conducted, nuclear weapons became an issue in India's election of March,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> in relation to political tensions with neighboring <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. Prior to the election the <a href="/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party" title="Bharatiya Janata Party">Bharatiya Janata Party</a> (BJP) announced it would "declare India a nuclear weapon state" after coming to power.</p>
<p>BJP won the elections, and on 14 May, three days after India tested nuclear weapons for the second time, a public opinion poll reported that a majority of Indians favored the country’s nuclear build-up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On 15 April 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Program_on_International_Policy_Attitudes" title="Program on International Policy Attitudes">Program on International Policy Attitudes</a> (PIPA) reported<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> that U.S. citizens showed high levels of concern regarding WMD, and that preventing the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">spread of nuclear weapons</a> should be "a very important U.S. foreign policy goal", accomplished through multilateral arms control rather than the use of military threats.</p>
<p>A majority also believed the United States should be more forthcoming with its biological research and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a> commitment of nuclear arms reduction.</p>
<p>A Russian opinion poll conducted on 5 August 2005 indicated half the population believes new nuclear powers have the right to possess nuclear weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> 39% believes the Russian stockpile should be reduced, though not fully eliminated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction_in_popular_culture" title="Weapons of mass destruction in popular culture">Weapons of mass destruction in popular culture</a></div>
<p>Weapons of mass destruction and their related impacts have been a mainstay of <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> since the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, as both political commentary and humorous outlet. The actual phrase "weapons of mass destruction" has been used similarly and as a way to characterise any powerful force or product since the Iraqi weapons crisis in the lead up to the Coalition invasion of Iraq in 2003.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioactive_weaponry.2Fhazard_symbol">Radioactive weaponry/hazard symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weapon_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Radioactive weaponry/hazard symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The international radioactivity symbol (also known as <a href="/wiki/Trefoil" title="Trefoil">trefoil</a>) first appeared in 1946, at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> Radiation Laboratory. At the time, it was rendered as <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">magenta</a>, and was set on a blue background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origin_of_the_Radiation_Warning_Symbol_.28Trefoil.29_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Origin_of_the_Radiation_Warning_Symbol_.28Trefoil.29-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is drawn with a central circle of radius <i>R</i>, the blades having an internal radius of 1.5<i>R</i> and an external radius of 5<i>R</i>, and separated from each other by 60°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biohazard_and_radioactive_Symbol.2C_design_and_proportions_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biohazard_and_radioactive_Symbol.2C_design_and_proportions-69">[69]</a></sup> It is meant to represent a radiating atom.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> found that the trefoil radiation symbol is unintuitive and can be variously interpreted by those uneducated in its meaning; therefore, its role as a hazard warning was compromised as it did not clearly indicate "danger" to many non-Westerners and children who encountered it. As a result of research, a new radiation hazard symbol was developed in 2007 to be placed near the most dangerous parts of radiation sources featuring a skull, someone running away, and using a red rather than yellow background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The red background is intended to convey urgent danger, and the sign is intended to be used on equipment where very strong radiation fields can be encountered if the device is dismantled or otherwise tampered with. The intended use of the sign is not in a place where the normal user will see it, but in a place where it will be seen by someone who has started to dismantle a radiation-emitting device or equipment. The aim of the sign is to warn people such as scrap metal workers to stop work and leave the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
