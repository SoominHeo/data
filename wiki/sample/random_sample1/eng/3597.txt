<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_entry_bookkeeping_system" title="Double entry bookkeeping system">double entry bookkeeping</a>, <b>debits</b> and <b>credits</b> (abbreviated <b>Dr</b> and <b>Cr</b>, respectively) are entries made in <a href="/wiki/Account_(accountancy)" title="Account (accountancy)">account</a> <a href="/wiki/Ledger" title="Ledger">ledgers</a> to record changes in <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> resulting from business transactions. Generally speaking, the source account for the transaction is <i>credited</i> (that is, an entry is made on the right side of the account's ledger) and the destination account is <i>debited</i> (that is, an entry is made on the left side). Total debits must equal total credits for each transaction; individual transactions may require multiple debit and credit entries to record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The difference between the total debits and total credits in a single account is the account's <i>balance</i>. If debits exceed credits, the account has a debit balance; if credits exceed debits, the account has a credit balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For the company as a whole, the totals of debit balances and credit balances must be equal as shown in the <a href="/wiki/Trial_balance" title="Trial balance">trial balance</a> report, otherwise an error has occurred.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Accountant" title="Accountant">Accountants</a> use the trial balance to prepare <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">financial statements</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Balance_sheet" title="Balance sheet">balance sheet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Income_statement" title="Income statement">income statement</a>) which communicate information about the company's financial activities in a <a href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">generally accepted</a> standard format.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known recorded use of the terms is Venetian <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pacioli" title="Luca Pacioli">Luca Pacioli</a>'s 1494 work, <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_de_arithmetica" title="Summa de arithmetica">Summa de Arithmetica, Geometria, Proportioni et Proportionalita</a></i> (translated: <i>Everything That Is Known About Arithmetic, Geometry, Proportions and Proportionality</i>). Pacioli devoted one section of his book to documenting and describing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping" title="Double-entry bookkeeping">double-entry bookkeeping</a> system in use during the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> by Venetian merchants, traders and bankers. This system is still the fundamental system in use by modern bookkeepers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>One theory is that in its original Latin, Pacioli's <i>Summa</i> used the Latin words <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/debere" title="wiktionary:debere"><i>debere</i></a></span> (to owe) and <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/credere" title="wiktionary:credere"><i>credere</i></a></span> (to entrust) to describe the two sides of a closed accounting transaction. Assets were owed to the owner and the owners' equity was entrusted to the company. At the time negative numbers were not in use. When his work was translated, the Latin words <i>debere</i> and <i>credere</i> became the English <i>debit</i> and <i>credit</i>. Under this theory, the abbreviations Dr (for debit) and Cr (for credit) derive from the original Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knol.google.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-knol.google.com-5">[5]</a></sup> However, Sherman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> casts doubt on this idea because Pacioli uses <i>Per</i> (Latin for "from") for the debtor and <i>A</i> (Latin for "to") for the creditor in the Journal entries. Sherman goes on to say that the earliest text he found that actually uses "Dr." as an abbreviation in this context was an English text, the third edition (1633) of Ralph Handson's book <i>Analysis or Resolution of Merchant Accompts</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and that Handson uses Dr. as an abbreviation for the English word "debtor." (Sherman could not locate a first edition, but speculates that it too used Dr. for debtor.) The words actually used by Pacioli for the left and right sides of the Ledger are "in dare" and "in havere" (<i>give</i> and <i>receive</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Geijsbeek the translator suggests in the preface: 'if we today would abolish the use of the words debit and credit in the ledger and substitute the ancient terms of "shall give" and "shall have" or "shall receive," the personification of accounts in the proper way would not be difficult and, with it, bookkeeping would become more intelligent to the proprietor, the layman and the student.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jackson<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> notes that "debtor" need not be a person, but can be an abstract operator (cf. "divisor" in math) "...it became the practice to extend the meanings of the terms ... beyond their original personal connotation and apply them to inanimate objects and abstract conceptions...".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects_of_transactions">Aspects of transactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aspects of transactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To determine whether one must debit or credit a specific account we use either the <i>accounting equation</i> approach which consists of five accounting rules<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pieters_2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pieters_2009-11">[11]</a></sup> or the <i>traditional approach</i> based on three rules (for Real accounts, Personal accounts, and Nominal accounts) to determine whether <i>to debit</i> or <i>to credit</i> an account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nadu_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nadu-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Real accounts</b> are the assets of a firm, which may be <i>tangible</i> (machinery, buildings etc.) or <i>intangible</i> (goodwill, patents etc.)</li>
<li><b>Personal accounts</b> relate to individuals, companies, creditors, banks etc.</li>
<li><b>Nominal accounts</b> relate to expenses, losses, incomes or gains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Whether a <i>debit</i> increases or decreases an account depends on what kind of account it is. An increase to an <i>asset</i> account is a debit. An increase to a <i>liability</i> or to an <i>equity</i> account is a credit.</p>

<p>Conversely, a decrease to an asset account is a credit. A decrease to a liability or equity account is a debit.</p>
<p>Debits and credits occur simultaneously in every <a href="/wiki/Financial_transaction" title="Financial transaction">financial transaction</a> in double-entry bookkeeping. In the accounting equation—Assets = Liabilities + Equity—if an asset account increases (a debit), then either another asset account must decrease (a credit), or a liability or equity account must increase (a credit).</p>
<p>For example, when the customer of a <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">bank</a> deposits money into his bank <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_account_(banking)#Current_accounts" title="Current account (banking)">account</a> two things change: the customer's cash-in-hand (asset) decreases and the customer's bank account balance increases. The decrease in the cash-in-hand asset is a credit while the increase in the bank account balance is a debit of equal magnitude.</p>
<p>The bank views the transaction from a different perspective but follows the same rules: the bank's vault cash (asset) increases, which is a debit; the increase in the customer's account balance (liability from the bank's perspective) is a credit. A customer's periodic bank statement generally shows transactions from the bank's perspective, with bank deposits characterized as credits and withdrawals as debits.</p>
<p>Debits are traditionally entered on the left-hand side of a <a href="/wiki/Ledger" title="Ledger">ledger</a> and credits on the right-hand side.</p>

<p>When dealing with one's own business, one must set up various accounts to record all transactions that may take place. When the owner of a business refers to their bank account, they are referring to the business's account, not to their personal account. In addition, all accounts referred to in bookkeeping belong to the business, <i>not</i> to other businesses, regardless of their title. For instance, if a business expects to receive money from another person or company and the account is labelled "Receivable A", this does <b>not</b> imply that the account in question belongs to "Receivable A". It is merely a recording of a <a href="/wiki/Current_asset" title="Current asset">current asset</a> (a receivable) of one's own business. Therefore, when assessing any transaction, the transaction is from the point of view of one's own business or the business in question.</p>
<p>All accounts must first be classified as one of the <a href="#The_five_accounting_elements">five types of accounts (accounting elements)</a>. To determine how to classify an account into one of the five elements, the definitions of the five account types must be fully understood i.e. the definition of an <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">asset</a> according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFRS" title="IFRS">IFRS</a> is as follows, "An asset is a resource controlled by the entity as a result of past events from which future economic benefits are expected to flow to the entity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRS_for_SMEs_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFRS_for_SMEs-14">[14]</a></sup> To understand this definition we can break it down into its constituent parts with an example:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Example: Classify what type of account the business "Bank account" is.</dd>
<dd>The bank account of a business is "a resource controlled by the entity" as it belongs to the business. "As a result of past events" such as the opening of the business. "From which future economic benefits are expected to flow to the entity" – a business such as a grocers can expect to make money due to the sale of their goods. This basic analogy can be applied to any asset account.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>All of the five accounting elements have their own definitions (discussed in other articles see: <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">asset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liability_(financial_accounting)" title="Liability (financial accounting)">liability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">equity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> and <a href="/wiki/Expense" title="Expense">expense</a>) that must be fully understood in order to classify an account correctly.</dd>
</dl>
<p>A business will most often have more than one asset account. An essential asset account in any business is the business's <i>bank account</i> (see: "Accounts pertaining to the five accounting elements" below for more examples) The same applies to liability accounts i.e. if I have borrowed money from two sources (called creditors or payables), then I must open two accounts to represent this present liability, called 'Creditor/Payable A' and 'Creditor/Payable B'. In this manner I may have multiple, different accounts. However all these accounts are all classified as one of the five types of accounts, therefore my entire business can be described in terms of its assets, expenses, liabilities, income and equity/capital (see extended <a href="/wiki/Accounting_equation" title="Accounting equation">accounting equation</a>). This is the extent of "my" business in relation to accounts, regardless of the business' practices (the business may be a retail franchise, furniture shop, restaurant, etc.). With respect to my business, each of the five accounting elements will have a monetary value, and this can be used to assess the financial position of my business at any time (my success, failure, or any other attributes that I might need to know).</p>
<p>Traditionally, transactions are recorded in two separate columns of numbers (known as a ledger or "T-account"): debit transactions in the left hand column and credit transactions in the right hand column. Keeping the debits and credits in separate columns allows each column to be recorded and totaled <i>independently</i>. Accounts within the <i>general ledger</i> are known colloquially as "T-accounts" due to the "T" shape that the table resembles. Each column of a ledger account lists transactions affecting that account.</p>

<p>The words <b>debit</b> and <b>credit</b> are both used differently depending on whether they are used in a bookkeeping (accounting) sense, or non-accounting sense.</p>
<p>In a non-accounting sense, "debit" is:</p>
<ul>
<li>a sum of money taken from a bank account.</li>
</ul>
<p>In a non-accounting sense, "credit" is</p>
<ul>
<li>a sum of money placed into a bank account.</li>
<li>money available to spend.</li>
<li>money available to borrow.</li>
</ul>
<p>The reason why individuals see debits and credits in the above manner, is that the bank statement presented by the bank to the customer is the bank's view of the account. The bank views money in a chequing account as money the bank owes to the customer, i.e. a liability, and in the rules of accounting, an increase to a liability account is a credit. Likewise, when a bank lends money to a customer and places the money into the customer's chequing account, the bank has increased its obligation to pay that money, which is a liability, and this increase is a credit and appears in the credit column of a bank statement.</p>
<p>When recording numbers in accounting, a debit value is placed on the left side of a ledger for a debited account and a credit value is placed on the right side of a ledger for a credited account. A debit or a credit either increases or decreases the total balance in each account, depending on what kind of accounts they are.</p>
<p>Each transaction (say, of value £100) is recorded by a debit entry of £100 in one account and a credit entry of £100 in another account. When people say, "debits must equal credits" they do <i>not</i> mean that the two columns of any ledger account must be equal. If that were the case, every account would have a zero balance (no difference between the columns) which is often not the case. The rule that <i>total debits equal the total credits</i> applies when all accounts are totaled.</p>
<p>More than two accounts may be affected by the same transaction. A transaction for £100 can be recorded as a £100 debit in one account and as multiple credits that total £100 in other accounts.</p>
<p><i>Example</i>:</p>
<p>I owe creditors A and B £100 each. Thus my liability account for Creditor A has a credit balance of £100 and the same for Creditor B.</p>

<p>When I write two £100 cheques for a total of £200, the balance in my bank account is reduced by £200. Based on the law of accounting, a decrease in my cash asset is a credit. The total credit for my asset balance is greater than the total debit. Thus, in my records, my "Bank" ledger account has an asset credit balance, which is reduced by the credit for £200. Amounts in my records for the two creditors are liabilities, which are reduced by the two debits totaling £200.</p>
<p>Therefore, for this transaction, the total amount debited = 200 and the total amount credited = 200. When all three accounts are totaled, the total debits equal the total credits.</p>
<p>At the end of any financial period (say at the end of the quarter or the year), the total debits and the total credits for each account may be different and this difference of the two sides is called the <i>balance</i>. If the sum of the debit side is greater than the sum of the credit side, then the account has a "debit balance". If the sum of the credit side is greater, then the account has a "credit balance". If the two sides do equal each other (this would be a coincidence, not as a result of the laws of accounting), then we say we have a "zero balance".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Debit_cards_and_credit_cards">Debit cards and credit cards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Debit cards and credit cards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Debit cards and credit cards are creative terms used by the banking industry to market and identify each card.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> From the cardholder's point of view, a credit card account normally contains a credit balance, a debit card account normally contains a debit balance. A debit card is used to make a purchase with one's own money. A credit card is used to make a purchase by borrowing money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the bank's point of view, when a debit card is used to pay a merchant, the payment causes a decrease in the amount of money the bank <i>owes to</i> the cardholder. From the bank's point of view, your debit card account is the bank's liability. A decrease to the bank's liability account is a debit. From the bank's point of view, when a credit card is used to pay a merchant, the payment causes an increase in the amount of money the bank <i>is owed by</i> the cardholder. From the bank's point of view, your credit card account is the bank's asset. An increase to the bank's asset account is a debit. Hence, using a debit card <i>or</i> credit card causes a debit to the cardholder's account in either situation when viewed from the bank's perspective.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_ledgers">General ledgers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: General ledgers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/General_ledger" title="General ledger">General ledger</a> is the term for the comprehensive collection of T-accounts (so called because there was a pre-printed vertical line in the middle of each ledger page and a horizontal line at the top of each ledger page, like a large letter T). Before the advent of computerised accounting, manual accounting procedure used a book (known as a ledger) for each T-account. The collection of all these books was called the general ledger.</p>
<p>"Day Books" or journals are used to list every single transaction that took place during the day, and the list is totalled at the end of the day. These daybooks are not part of the <a href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping_system" title="Double-entry bookkeeping system">double-entry bookkeeping system</a>. The information recorded in these daybooks is then transferred to the general ledgers. Modern computer software now allows for the instant update of each ledger account – for example, when recording a cash receipt in a cash receipts journal a debit is posted to a cash ledger account with a corresponding credit in the ledger account for which the cash was received. Not every single transaction need be entered into a T-account. Usually only the sum of the book transactions (a batch total) for the day is entered in the general ledger.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_five_accounting_elements">The five accounting elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The five accounting elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are five fundamental elements<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pieters_2009_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pieters_2009-11">[11]</a></sup> within accounting. These elements are as follows: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assets" title="Assets">Assets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liability_(financial_accounting)" title="Liability (financial accounting)">Liabilities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">Equity</a> (or Capital), <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">Income</a> (or Revenue) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expenses" title="Expenses">Expenses</a>. The five accounting elements are all affected in either a positive or negative way. A credit transaction does not always dictate a positive value or increase in a <a href="/wiki/Financial_transaction" title="Financial transaction">transaction</a> and similarly, a debit does not always indicate a negative value or decrease in a transaction. An <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">asset</a> account is often referred to as a "debit account" due to the account's standard <i>increasing</i> attribute on the debit side. When an asset (e.g. an espresso machine) has been acquired in a business, the transaction will affect the debit side of that asset account illustrated below:</p>

<p>Each transaction that takes place within the business will consist of at least one debit to a specific account and at least one credit to another specific account. A debit to one account can be balanced by more than one credit to other accounts, and vice versa. For all transactions, the total debits must be equal to the total credits and therefore <i>balance</i>.</p>

<p>where the relationship of the Income and Expenses accounts to Equity and profit is a bit clearer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Here Income and Expenses are regarded as temporary or nominal accounts which pertain only to the current accounting period whereas Asset, Liabilities and Equity accounts are permanent or real accounts pertaining to the lifetime of the business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The temporary accounts are closed to the Equity account at the end of the accounting period to record profit/loss for the period. Both sides of these equations must be equal (balance).</p>
<p>Each transaction is recorded in a <a href="/wiki/Ledger" title="Ledger">ledger</a> or "T" account, e.g. a ledger account named "Bank" that can be changed with either a debit or credit transaction.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounts_pertaining_to_the_five_accounting_elements">Accounts pertaining to the five accounting elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accounts pertaining to the five accounting elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Accounts are created/opened when the need arises for whatever purpose or situation the entity may have. For example, if your business is an airline company they will have to purchase airplanes, therefore even if an account is not listed below, a bookkeeper or accountant can create an account for a specific item, such as an asset account for airplanes. In order to understand how to classify an account into one of the five elements, a good understanding of the <i>definitions</i> of these accounts is required. Below are examples of some of the more common accounts that pertain to the five accounting elements:</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Asset_accounts">Asset accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Asset accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Asset accounts are economic resources which benefit the business/entity and will continue to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Cash, bank, <a href="/wiki/Accounts_receivable" title="Accounts receivable">accounts receivable</a>, inventory, land, buildings/plant, machinery, furniture, equipment, supplies, vehicles, trademarks and patents, goodwill, prepaid expenses, prepaid insurance, debtors (people who owe us money, due within one year), VAT input etc.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liability_accounts">Liability accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Liability accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Liability accounts record debts or future obligations the business/entity owes to others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Accounts_payable" title="Accounts payable">Accounts payable</a>, salaries and wages payable, income taxes, bank overdrafts, trust accounts, accrued expenses, sales taxes, advance payments (unearned revenue), debt and accrued interest on debt, customer deposits, VAT output etc.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Equity_accounts">Equity accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Equity accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Equity accounts record the claims of the owners of the business/entity to the assets of that business/entity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Capital, <a href="/wiki/Retained_earnings" title="Retained earnings">retained earnings</a>, drawings, common stock, accumulated funds, etc.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Income.2FRevenue_accounts">Income/Revenue accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Income/Revenue accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Income accounts record all increases in Equity other than that contributed by the owner/s of the business/entity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Services rendered, sales, interest income, membership fees, rent income, interest from investment, recurring receivables,donation etc.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Expense_accounts">Expense accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Expense accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Expense accounts record all decreases in the owners' equity which occur from using the assets or increasing liabilities in delivering goods or services to a customer - the costs of doing business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Telephone, water, electricity, repairs, salaries, wages, depreciation, bad debts, stationery, entertainment, <a href="/wiki/Honorarium" title="Honorarium">honorarium</a>, rent, fuel, utility, interest etc.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_examples">Further examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Further examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ol>
<li>A business pays <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">rent</a> with cash: You increase rent (expense) by recording a debit transaction, and decrease cash (asset) by recording a credit transaction.</li>
<li>A business receives cash for a sale: You increase cash (asset) by recording a debit transaction, and increase sales (income) by recording a credit transaction.</li>
<li>A business buys equipment with cash: You increase equipment (asset) by recording a debit transaction, and decrease cash (asset) by recording a credit transaction.</li>
<li>A business borrows with a cash <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loan</a>: You increase cash (asset) by recording a debit transaction, and increase loan (liability) by recording a credit transaction.</li>
<li>A business pays salaries with cash: You increase <a href="/wiki/Salary" title="Salary">salary</a> (expenses) by recording a debit transaction, and decrease cash (asset) by recording a credit transaction.</li>
<li>The totals show the net effect on the accounting equation and the double-entry principle, where the transactions are balanced.</li>
</ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="T-accounts">T-accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: T-accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The process of using debits and credits creates a ledger format that resembles the letter "T".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weygandt2009_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weygandt2009-25">[25]</a></sup> The term "T-account" is accounting jargon for a "ledger account" and is often used when discussing bookkeeping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The reason that a ledger account is often referred to as a T-account is due to the way the account is physically drawn on paper (representing a "T"). The left side (column) of the "T" for Debit (Dr) transactions and the right side (column) of the "T" for Credit (Cr) transactions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contra_account">Contra account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Contra account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All accounts have corresponding contra accounts depending on what transaction has taken place e.g., when a vehicle is purchased using cash, the asset account "Vehicles" is <i>debited</i> as the vehicle account increases, and simultaneously the asset account "Bank" is <i>credited</i> due to the payment for the vehicle using cash. Some balance sheet items have corresponding contra accounts, with negative balances, that offset them. Examples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accumulated_depreciation" title="Accumulated depreciation">accumulated depreciation</a> against equipment, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allowance_for_bad_debts" title="Allowance for bad debts">allowance for bad debts</a> (also known as allowance for doubtful accounts) against accounts receivable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> United States GAAP utilizes the term <i>contra</i> for specific accounts only and doesn't recognize the second half of a transaction as a contra, thus the term is restricted to accounts that are related. For example, sales returns and allowance and sales discounts are contra revenues with respect to sales, as the balance of each contra (a debit) is the opposite of sales (a credit). To understand the actual value of sales, one must net the contras against sales, which gives rise to the term net sales (meaning net of the contras).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accountingcoach_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-accountingcoach-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>A more specific definition in common use is an account with a balance that is the opposite of the normal balance (Dr/Cr) for that section of the <a href="/wiki/General_ledger" title="General ledger">general ledger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accountingcoach_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-accountingcoach-28">[28]</a></sup> An example is an office coffee fund: Expense "Coffee" (Dr) may be immediately followed by "Coffee - employee contributions" (Cr).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Such an account is used for clarity rather than being a necessary part of GAAP (<a href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">generally accepted accounting principles</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accountingcoach_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-accountingcoach-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real.2C_personal.2C_and_nominal_accounts">Real, personal, and nominal accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debits_and_credits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Real, personal, and nominal accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Real accounts are assets. Personal accounts are liabilities and owners' equity and represent people and entities that have invested in the business. Nominal accounts are revenue, expenses, gains, and losses. Accountants close nominal accounts at the end of each accounting period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> This method is used in the United Kingdom, where it is simply known as the <a href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping_system#Traditional_.28British.29_approach" title="Double-entry bookkeeping system">Traditional approach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nadu_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-nadu-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Transactions are recorded by a debit to one account and a credit to another account using these three "golden rules of accounting":</p>
<ol>
<li>Real account: Debit what comes in and credit what goes out</li>
<li>Personal account: Debit who receives and Credit who gives.</li>
<li>Nominal account: Debit all expenses &amp; losses and Credit all incomes &amp; gains</li>
</ol>
