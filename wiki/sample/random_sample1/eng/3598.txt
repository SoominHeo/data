<p>In the teaching of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, an <b>indulgence</b> is "a way to reduce the amount of punishment one has to undergo for sins".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_Guide_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Guide-1">[1]</a></sup> It may reduce the temporal punishment after death, in the state or process of purification called <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">Purgatory</a>. This temporal punishment would not be considered "time", as Purgatory, like Heaven and Hell, is said to exist "outside of time".</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catechism_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Catechism of the Catholic Church">Catechism of the Catholic Church</a> describes an indulgence as "a remission before God of the temporal punishment due to sins whose guilt has already been forgiven, which the faithful Christian who is duly disposed gains under certain prescribed conditions through the action of the Church which, as the minister of redemption, dispenses and applies with authority the treasury of the satisfactions of Christ and the saints".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vatican.va_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-vatican.va-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The recipient of an indulgence must perform an action to receive it. This is most often the saying (once, or many times) of a specified <a href="/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">prayer</a>, but may also include the visiting of a particular place, or the performance of specific <a href="/wiki/Good_works" title="Good works">good works</a>.</p>

<p>Indulgences were introduced to allow for the remission of the severe penances of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Church" title="Early Church">early Church</a> and granted at the intercession of Christians awaiting martyrdom or at least imprisoned for the faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup> They draw on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_of_Merit" title="Treasury of Merit">Treasury of Merit</a> accumulated by <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Christ's</a> superabundantly meritorious sacrifice on the cross and the <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penances</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_History_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_History-4">[4]</a></sup> They are granted for specific good works and prayers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_History_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-World_History-4">[4]</a></sup> in proportion to the devotion with which those good works are performed or prayers recited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the abuse of indulgences, mainly through commercialization, had become a serious problem which the Church recognized but was unable to restrain effectively. Indulgences were from the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> a target of attacks by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> and all other Protestant theologians. Eventually the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> curbed the excesses, but indulgences continue to play a role in modern Catholic religious life. Reforms in the 20th century largely abolished the quantification of indulgences, which had been expressed in terms of days or years. These days or years were meant to represent the equivalent of time spent in penance, although it was widely taken to mean time spent in Purgatory. The reforms also greatly reduced the number of indulgences granted for visiting particular churches and other locations.</p>

<p>"When a person sins, he acquires certain liabilities: the liability of guilt and the liability of punishment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Primer-6">[6]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Mortal_sin" title="Mortal sin">mortal sin</a> (one that is grave, or serious, in nature and is committed knowingly and freely) is equivalent to refusing friendship with God and <a href="/wiki/Communion_(Christian)" title="Communion (Christian)">communion</a> with the only source of eternal life. The loss of eternal life with God, and the eternal death of hell that is the effect of this rejection, is called the "eternal punishment" of sin. The Sacrament of Penance removes the guilt and the liability of eternal punishment related to <a href="/wiki/Mortal_sin" title="Mortal sin">mortal sin</a>. "Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be white as snow; though they are red like crimson, they shall become like wool."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The forgiveness of sin and restoration of communion with God entail the remission of the eternal punishment of sin, but temporal punishment of sin remains. An example of this can be seen in 2 Samuel 12 when after David repents of his sin, the prophet Nathan tells him that he is forgiven but, "Thus says the Lord God of Israel:...Now, therefore, the sword shall never depart from your house, because you have despised me and have taken the wife of Uriah to be your wife."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to this eternal punishment due to mortal sin, every sin, including <a href="/wiki/Venial_sin" title="Venial sin">venial sin</a>, is a turning away from God through what the Catechism of the Catholic Church calls an unhealthy attachment to creatures, an attachment that must be purified either here on earth, or after death in the state called <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">purgatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECCC_.C2.A71472_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECCC_.C2.A71472-9">[9]</a></sup> "The process of sanctification and interior renewal requires not only forgiveness from the guilt (<i>culpa</i>) of sin, but also purification from the harmful effects or wounds of sin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeters.2C_p.1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeters.2C_p.1-10">[10]</a></sup> This purification process gives rise to "temporal punishment", because, not involving a total rejection of God, it is not eternal and can be expiated.</p>
<p>"While patiently bearing sufferings and trials of all kinds and, when the day comes, serenely facing death, the Christian must strive to accept this temporal punishment of sin as a grace. He should strive by works of mercy and charity, as well as by prayer and the various practices of <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penance</a>, to put off completely the 'old man' and to put on the 'new man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The temporal punishment that follows sin is thus undergone either during life on earth or in purgatory. In this life, as well as by patient acceptance of sufferings and trials, the necessary cleansing from attachment to creatures may, at least in part, be achieved by turning to God in prayer and penance and by works of mercy and charity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Primer-6">[6]</a></sup> Indulgences (from the Latin verb <i>indulgere</i> meaning to forgive, to be lenient toward)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeters.2C_p.1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeters.2C_p.1-10">[10]</a></sup> are a help towards achieving this purification.</p>
<p>An indulgence does not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forgiveness_of_sin#Christianity" title="Forgiveness of sin">forgive</a> the guilt of sin, nor does it provide release from the eternal punishment associated with unforgiven <a href="/wiki/Mortal_sin" title="Mortal sin">mortal sins</a>. The Catholic Church teaches that indulgences relieve only the temporal punishment resulting from the effect of sin (the effect of rejecting God the source of good), and that a person is still required to have his grave sins <a href="/wiki/Absolution" title="Absolution">absolved</a>, ordinarily through the sacrament of <a href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Penance" title="Sacrament of Penance">Confession</a>, to receive <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a>. Similarly, an indulgence is not a permit to commit sin, a pardon of future sin, nor a guarantee of salvation for oneself or for another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CE-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Ordinarily, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forgiveness_of_sin#Christianity" title="Forgiveness of sin">forgiveness</a> of mortal sins is obtained through <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">Confession (i.e., penance or reconciliation)</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Catechism_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Catechism of the Catholic Church">Catechism of the Catholic Church</a>, "The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_of_Merit" title="Treasury of Merit">'treasury of the Church'</a> is the infinite value, which can never be exhausted, which Christ's merits have before God. They were offered so that the whole of mankind could be set free from sin and attain communion with the Father. ... In Christ, the Redeemer himself, the satisfactions and merits of his Redemption exist and find their efficacy. ...This treasury includes as well the prayers and good works of the Blessed Virgin Mary. They are truly immense, unfathomable, and even pristine in their value before God. In the treasury, too, are the prayers and good works of all the saints, all those who have followed in the footsteps of Christ the Lord and by his grace have made their lives holy and carried out the mission in the unity of the Mystical Body."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pursuant to the Church's understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Power_of_the_Keys" title="Power of the Keys">power of binding or loosing</a> granted by Christ, it administers to those under its jurisdiction the benefits of these merits in consideration of prayer or other pious works undertaken by the faithful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup> In opening for individual Christians its treasury, "the Church does not want simply to come to the aid of these Christians, but also to spur them to works of devotion, penance, and charity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>An indulgence may be plenary (remits all temporal "punishment" required to cleanse the soul from attachment to anything but God) or partial (remits only part of the temporal "punishment", i.e. cleansing, due to sin).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vatican.va_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-vatican.va-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> To gain a plenary indulgence, a person must exclude all attachment to sin of any kind, even venial sin, must perform the work or say the prayer for which the indulgence is granted, and must also fulfil the three conditions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Penance_(Catholic_Church)" title="Sacrament of Penance (Catholic Church)">sacramental confession</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharistic communion</a> and praying for the <a href="/wiki/Holy_actions#Intentions" title="Holy actions">intentions of the Pope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The minimum condition for gaining a partial indulgence is to be contrite in heart: on this condition, a Catholic who performs the work or recites the prayer in question is granted, through the Church, remission of temporal punishment equal to that obtained by the person's own action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since those who have died in the state of grace (with all mortal sins forgiven) are members of the communion of saints, the living (members of the <a href="/wiki/Churches_Militant,_Penitent,_and_Triumphant" title="Churches Militant, Penitent, and Triumphant">Churches Militant</a>) can assist those whose purification from their sins was not yet completed at the time of death through prayer but also by obtaining indulgences in their behalf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Since the Church has no jurisdiction over the dead, indulgences can be gained for them only <i>per modum suffragii</i>, i.e. by an act of <a href="/wiki/Intercession_of_saints" title="Intercession of saints">intercession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup> This is sometimes termed <i>impetration</i>, which Aquinas explains "...is not founded on God's justice, but on His goodness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_discipline">Present discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Present discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Indulgentiarum_Doctrina" title="Indulgentiarum Doctrina">Indulgentiarum Doctrina</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Socvillegas4jf.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Socvillegas4jf.JPG/220px-Socvillegas4jf.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Socvillegas4jf.JPG/330px-Socvillegas4jf.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Socvillegas4jf.JPG/440px-Socvillegas4jf.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socrates_B._Villegas" title="Socrates B. Villegas">Socrates B. Villegas</a> bestows the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" title="Easter Sunday">Easter</a> Mass Plenary Indulgence in 2012 (St. John the Evangelist Metropolitan Cathedral, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dagupan_City,_Pangasinan" title="Dagupan City, Pangasinan">Dagupan City, Pangasinan</a>).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_constitution" title="Apostolic constitution">apostolic constitution</a> <i>Indulgentiarum doctrina</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> of 1 January 1967, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a>, responding to suggestions made at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a>, substantially revised the practical application of the traditional doctrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EnChr_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-EnChr-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>He made it clear that the Church's aim was not merely to help the faithful make due satisfaction for their sins, but chiefly to bring them to greater fervour of charity. For this purpose he decreed that partial indulgences, previously granted as the equivalent of a certain number of days, months, quarantines (forty-day periods) or years of canonical penance, simply supplement, and to the same degree, the remission that those performing the indulgenced action already gain by the charity and contrition with which they do it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The abolition of the classification by years and days made it clearer than before that repentance and faith are required not only for remission of eternal punishment for mortal sin but also for remission of temporal punishment for sin. In <i>Indulgentiarum doctrina</i> Pope Paul VI wrote that indulgences cannot be gained without a sincere conversion of outlook and unity with God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Indulgentiarum_doctrina.27.27.2C_.C2.A711_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Indulgentiarum_doctrina.27.27.2C_.C2.A711-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the same bull, Pope Paul ordered that the official list of indulgenced prayers and good works, called the <i><a href="/wiki/Raccolta" title="Raccolta">Raccolta</a></i>, be revised "with a view to attaching indulgences only to the most important prayers and works of piety, charity and penance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The <i>Raccolta</i> was replaced with the <i>Enchiridion Indulgentiarum</i>. While a number of indulgenced prayers and good works were removed from the list, it now includes new general grants of partial indulgences that apply to a wide range of prayerful actions, and it indicates that the prayers that it does list as deserving veneration on account of divine inspiration or antiquity or as being in widespread use are only <i>examples</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> of those to which the first of these general grants applies: "Raising the mind to God with humble trust while performing one's duties and bearing life's difficulties, and adding, at least mentally, some pious invocation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In this way, the <i>Enchiridion Indulgentiarum</i>, in spite of its smaller size, classifies as indulgenced an immensely greater number of prayers than were treated as such in the <i>Raccolta</i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Actions_for_which_indulgences_are_granted">Actions for which indulgences are granted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Actions for which indulgences are granted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are four general grants of indulgence, which are meant to encourage the faithful to infuse a Christian spirit into the actions of their daily lives and to strive for perfection of charity. These indulgences are partial, and their worth therefore depends on the fervour with which the person performs the recommended actions:</p>
<ol>
<li>Raising the mind to God with humble trust while performing one's duties and bearing life's difficulties, and adding, at least mentally, some pious invocation.</li>
<li>Devoting oneself or one's goods compassionately in a spirit of faith to the service of one's brothers and sisters in need.</li>
<li>Freely abstaining in a spirit of penance from something licit and pleasant.</li>
<li>Freely giving open witness to one's faith before others in particular circumstances of everyday life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Among the particular grants, which, on closer inspection, will be seen to be included in one or more of the four general grants, especially the first, the <i>Enchiridion Indulgentiarum</i> draws special attention<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> to four activities for which a plenary indulgence can be gained on any day, though only once a day:</p>
<ol>
<li>Piously reading or listening to <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Sacred Scripture</a> for at least half an hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eucharistic_adoration" title="Eucharistic adoration">Adoration</a> of Jesus in the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a> for at least half an hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>The pious exercise of the <a href="/wiki/Stations_of_the_Cross" title="Stations of the Cross">Stations of the Cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Recitation of the <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">Rosary</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Akathist" title="Akathist">Akathist</a> in a church or oratory, or in a family, a religious community, an association of the faithful and, in general, when several people come together for an honourable purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>A plenary indulgence may also be gained on some occasions, which are not everyday occurrences. They include but are not limited to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Receiving, even by radio or television, the blessing given by the Pope <i><a href="/wiki/Urbi_et_Orbi" title="Urbi et Orbi">Urbi et Orbi</a></i> (to the city of Rome and to the world) or that which a bishop is authorized to give three times a year to the faithful of his diocese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>Taking part devoutly in the celebration of a day devoted on a world level to a particular religious purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Under this heading come the annual celebrations such as the World Day of Prayer for Vocations, and occasional celebrations such as <a href="/wiki/World_Youth_Day" title="World Youth Day">World Youth Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>Taking part for at least three full days in a <a href="/wiki/Retreat_(spiritual)" title="Retreat (spiritual)">spiritual retreat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>Taking part in some functions during the Week of Prayer for Christian Unity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Special indulgences are also granted on occasions of particular spiritual significance such as a <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christianity)" title="Jubilee (Christianity)">jubilee year</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> or the centenary or similar anniversary of an event such as the apparition of <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Lourdes" title="Our Lady of Lourdes">Our Lady of Lourdes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prayers specifically mentioned in the <i>Enchiridion Indulgentiarum</i> are not of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a> tradition alone, but also from the traditions of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akathistos" title="Akathistos">Akathistos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraklesis" title="Paraklesis">Paraklesis</a>, <i>Evening Prayer</i>, and <i>Prayer for the Faithful Departed</i> (Byzantine), <i>Prayer of Thanksgiving</i> (Armenian), <i>Prayer of the Shrine</i> and the <i>Lakhu Mara</i> (Chaldean), <i>Prayer of Incense</i> and <i>Prayer to Glorify Mary the Mother of God</i> (Coptic), <i>Prayer for the Remission of Sins</i> and <i>Prayer to Follow Christ</i> (Ethiopian), <i>Prayer for the Church</i>, and <i>Prayer of Leave-taking from the Altar</i> (Maronite), and <i>Intercessions for the Faithful Departed</i> (Syrian).</p>
<p>Of particular significance is the plenary indulgence attached to the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Blessing" title="Apostolic Blessing">Apostolic Blessing</a> that a priest is to impart when giving the sacraments to a person in danger of death, and which, if no priest is available, the Church grants to any rightly disposed Christian at the moment of death, on condition that that person was accustomed to say some prayers during life. In this case the Church itself makes up for the three conditions normally required for a plenary indulgence: sacramental confession, Eucharistic communion and prayer for the Pope's intentions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_medieval_beliefs">Early and medieval beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early and medieval beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early church, especially from the third century on, ecclesiastic authorities allowed a confessor or a Christian awaiting martyrdom to intercede for another Christian in order to shorten the other's canonical penance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup> During the Decian persecution, many Christians obtained signed statements (libelli) certifying that they had sacrificed to the Roman gods in order to avoid persecution or confiscation of property. When these <a href="/wiki/Lapsi_(Christianity)" title="Lapsi (Christianity)">lapsi</a> later wished to once again be admitted to the Christian community, some of the lapsi presented a second <i>libellus</i> purported to bear the signature of some martyr or confessor who, it was held, had the spiritual prestige to reaffirm individual Christians. Bishop <a href="/wiki/Cyprian" title="Cyprian">Cyprian</a> of Carthage insisted that none of the lapsi be admitted without sincere repentance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Council_of_Epaone" title="Council of Epaone">Council of Epaone</a> in 517 witnesses to the rise of the practice of replacing severe canonical penances with a new milder penance: its 29th canon reduced to two years the penance that apostates were to undergo on their return to the Church, but obliged them to fast one day in three during those two years, to come to church and take their place at the penitents' door, and to leave with the catechumens. Any who objected to the new arrangement were to observe the much longer ancient penance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sixth century saw the development in Ireland of <a href="/wiki/Penitential" title="Penitential">Penitentials</a>, handbooks for confessors in assigning penance. The Penitential of Cummean counseled a priest to take into consideration in imposing a penance, the penitent's strengths and weaknesses. Some penances could be commuted through payments or substitutions. It became customary to commute penances to less demanding works, such as prayers, alms, fasts and even the payment of fixed sums of money depending on the various kinds of offences (tariff penances). While the sanctions in early penitentials, such as that of Gildas, were primarily act of mortification or in some cases excommunication, the inclusion of fines in later compilations derive from secular law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the tenth century some penances were not replaced but merely reduced in connection with pious donations, pilgrimages and similar meritorious works. Then, in the 11th and 12th centuries, the recognition of the value of these works began to become associated not so much with canonical penance but with remission of the temporal punishment due to sin. A particular form of the commutation of penance was practised at the time of the Crusades, when the confessor required the penitent to go on a Crusade in place of some other penance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Covolo_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Covolo-45">[45]</a></sup> The earliest record of a plenary indulgence was <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_II" title="Pope Urban II">Pope Urban II</a>'s declaration at the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Clermont" title="Council of Clermont">Council of Clermont</a> (1095) that he remitted all penance incurred by <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">crusaders</a> who had confessed their sins in the <a href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Penance" title="Sacrament of Penance">Sacrament of Penance</a>, considering participation in the crusade equivalent to a complete penance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Plenary_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Plenary-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Theologians looked to God's mercy, the value of the Church's prayers, and the merits of the saints as the basis on which indulgences could be granted. Around 1230 the Dominican Hugh of St-Cher proposed the idea of a "treasury" at the Church's disposal, consisting of the infinite merits of Christ and the immeasurable abundance of the saints' merits, a thesis that was demonstrated by great scholastics such as <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> and remains the basis for the theological explanation of indulgences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Covolo_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Covolo-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indulgences were intended to offer remission of the temporal punishment due to sin equivalent to that someone might obtain by performing a canonical penance for a specific period of time. As Purgatory became more prominent in Christian thinking, the idea developed that the term of indulgences related to remission of time in Purgatory. Indeed, many Late Medieval indulgences were for terms well over a human lifetime, reflecting this belief. For several centuries it was debated by theologians whether penance or purgatory was the currency of the indulgences granted, and the church did not settle the matter definitively, for example avoiding doing so at the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The modern view of the church is that the term is penance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_medieval_abuses">Late medieval abuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late medieval abuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Pardoner" redirects here. For the character in Chaucer's Canterbury Tales, see <a href="/wiki/The_Pardoner%27s_Tale" title="The Pardoner's Tale">The Pardoner's Tale</a>.</div>

<p>Indulgences became increasingly popular in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> as a reward for displaying piety and doing good deeds, though, doctrinally speaking, the Church stated that the indulgence was only valid for temporal punishment for sins already forgiven in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Confession" title="Sacrament of Confession">Sacrament of Confession</a>. The faithful asked that indulgences be given for saying their favourite prayers, doing acts of devotion, attending places of worship, and going on <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a>; <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">confraternities</a> wanted indulgences for putting on performances and processions; associations demanded that their meetings be rewarded with indulgences. Good deeds included charitable donations of money for a good cause, and money thus raised was used for many righteous causes, both religious and civil; building projects funded by indulgences include churches, hospitals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leper_colonies" title="Leper colonies">leper colonies</a>, schools, roads, and bridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Covolo_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-Covolo-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the later Middle Ages saw the growth of considerable abuses. Greedy <a href="/wiki/Commissary" title="Commissary">commissaries</a> sought to extract the maximum amount of money for each indulgence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Professional "pardoners"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup> (<i>quaestores</i> in Latin) - who were sent to collect alms for a specific project - practiced the unrestricted sale of indulgences. Many of these <i>quaestores</i> exceeded official Church doctrine, whether in avarice or ignorant zeal, and promised rewards like salvation from eternal damnation in return for money. With the permission of the Church, indulgences also became a way for Catholic rulers to fund expensive projects, such as <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> and cathedrals, by keeping a significant portion of the money raised from indulgences in their lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Covolo_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-Covolo-45">[45]</a></sup> There was a tendency to forge documents declaring that indulgences had been granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Covolo_45-4"><a href="#cite_note-Covolo-45">[45]</a></sup> Indulgences grew to extraordinary magnitude, in terms of longevity and breadth of forgiveness.</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_Lateran_Council" title="Fourth Lateran Council">Fourth Lateran Council</a> (1215) suppressed some abuses connected with indulgences, spelling out, for example, that only a one-year indulgence would be granted for the consecration of churches and no more than a 40-days indulgence for other occasions. The Council also stated that "Catholics who have girded themselves with the cross for the extermination of the heretics, shall enjoy the indulgences and privileges granted to those who go in defense of the Holy Land."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Very soon these limits were widely exceeded. False documents were circulated with indulgences surpassing all bounds: indulgences of hundreds or even thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Covolo_45-5"><a href="#cite_note-Covolo-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1392, more than a century before <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> published the <a href="/wiki/Ninety-five_Theses" title="Ninety-five Theses">95 Theses</a>, Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boniface_IX" title="Boniface IX">Boniface IX</a> wrote to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ferrara" title="Bishop of Ferrara">Bishop of Ferrara</a> condemning the practice of certain <a href="/wiki/Religious_order#Catholic_tradition" title="Religious order">members of religious orders</a> who falsely claimed that they were authorized by the pope to forgive all sorts of sins, and obtained money from the simple-minded faithfuls by promising them perpetual happiness in this world and eternal glory in the next.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newadvent_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent-51">[51]</a></sup> The "Butter Tower" of <a href="/wiki/Rouen_Cathedral" title="Rouen Cathedral">Rouen Cathedral</a> earned its nickname because the money to build it was raised by the sale of indulgences allowing the use of butter during Lent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jensky_kodex_satan_prodava_odpustky.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Satan distributing indulgences</i>, an illumination from a Czech manuscript, 1490s; <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a> (the main leader of the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Reformation" title="Bohemian Reformation">Bohemian Reformation</a>) condemned the selling of indulgences already in 1412</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> by <a href="/wiki/Israhel_van_Meckenem" title="Israhel van Meckenem">Israhel van Meckenem</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Mass_of_Saint_Gregory" title="Mass of Saint Gregory">Mass of Saint Gregory</a></i> contained a "bootlegged" indulgence of 20,000 years; one of the copies of this plate (not the one illustrated, but also from the 1490s) was altered in a later <a href="/wiki/State_(printmaking)" title="State (printmaking)">state</a> to increase it to 45,000 years. The indulgences applied each time a specified collection of prayers - in this case seven each of the <a href="/wiki/Creed" title="Creed">Creed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Prayer" title="Lord's Prayer">Our Father</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hail_Mary" title="Hail Mary">Hail Mary</a> - were recited in front of the image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The image of the <i>Mass of Saint Gregory</i> had been especially associated with large indulgences since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christian)" title="Jubilee (Christian)">jubilee year</a> of 1350 in Rome, when it was at least widely believed that an indulgence of 14,000 years had been granted for praying in the presence of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imago_Pietatis" title="Imago Pietatis">Imago Pietatis</a></i> ("Man of Sorrows"), a popular pilgrimage destination in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Croce_in_Gerusalemme" title="Santa Croce in Gerusalemme">Santa Croce in Gerusalemme</a> in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_Reformation">Protestant Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Protestant Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The scandalous conduct of the "pardoners" was an immediate occasion of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Indulgences_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Indulgences-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1517, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a> offered indulgences for those who gave alms to rebuild <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. The aggressive <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> practices of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tetzel" title="Johann Tetzel">Johann Tetzel</a> in promoting this cause provoked <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> to write his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninety-Five_Theses" title="Ninety-Five Theses">Ninety-Five Theses</a>, condemning what he saw as the purchase and sale of <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a>. In Thesis 28 Luther objected to a saying attributed to Tetzel: "As soon as a coin in the coffer rings, a soul from purgatory springs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The Ninety-Five Theses not only denounced such transactions as worldly but denied the Pope's right to grant pardons on God's behalf in the first place: the only thing indulgences guaranteed, Luther said, was an increase in profit and greed, because the pardon of the Church was in God's power alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>This oft-quoted saying was by no means representative of the <i>official</i> Catholic teaching on indulgences, but rather, more a reflection of Tetzel’s capacity to exaggerate. Yet if Tetzel overstated the matter in regard to indulgences for the <i>dead</i>, his teaching on indulgences for the <i>living</i> was pure. German Catholic historian of the Papacy, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_von_Pastor" title="Ludwig von Pastor">Ludwig von Pastor</a> explains:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antichrist1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="543" data-file-width="441" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Antichrist1.jpg/220px-Antichrist1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Antichrist1.jpg/330px-Antichrist1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Antichrist1.jpg/440px-Antichrist1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antichrist1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Pope as the Antichrist, signing and selling indulgences, from Luther's 1521 <i>Passional Christi und Antichristi</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach the Elder</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote>
<p>Above all, a most clear distinction must be made between indulgences for the living and those for the dead.<br/></p>
<p>As regards indulgences for the living, Tetzel always taught pure doctrine. The assertion that he put forward indulgences as being not only a remission of the temporal punishment of sin, but as a remission of its guilt, is as unfounded as is that other accusation against him, that he sold the forgiveness of sin for money, without even any mention of contrition and confession, or that, for payment, he absolved from sins which might be committed in the future. His teaching was, in fact, very definite, and quite in harmony with the theology of the Church, as it was then and as it is now, i.e., that indulgences "apply only to the temporal punishment due to sins which have been already repented of and confessed"....</p>
<p>The case was very different with indulgences for the dead. As regards these there is no doubt that Tetzel did, according to what he considered his authoritative instructions, proclaim as Christian doctrine that nothing but an offering of money was required to gain the indulgence for the dead, without there being any question of contrition or confession. He also taught, in accordance with the opinion then held, that an indulgence could be applied to any given soul with unfailing effect. Starting from this assumption, there is no doubt that his doctrine was virtually that of the drastic proverb:</p>
<dl>
<dd>“As soon as money in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory's fire springs."</dd>
</dl>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Bull" title="Papal Bull">Papal Bull</a> of indulgence gave no sanction whatever to this proposition. It was a vague scholastic opinion, rejected by the Sorbonne in 1482, and again in 1518, and certainly not a doctrine of the Church, which was thus improperly put forward as dogmatic truth. The first among the theologians of the Roman court, Cardinal Cajetan, was the enemy of all such extravagances, and declared emphatically that, even if theologians and preachers taught such opinions, no faith need be given them. "Preachers," said he, "speak in the name of the Church only so long as they proclaim the doctrine of Christ and His Church; but if, for purposes of their own, they teach that about which they know nothing, and which is only their own imagination, they must not be accepted as mouthpieces of the Church. No one must be surprised if such as these fall into error."</blockquote>
<p>While Luther did not deny the Pope’s right to grant pardons for <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penance</a> imposed by the Church, he made it clear that preachers who claimed indulgences absolved those who obtained them from all punishments and granted them salvation were in error, in agreement with Catholic theology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is commonly believed that the Catholic Church was in the business of selling indulgences for money, though the Church itself says that all such transactions were officially classified as donations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Nonetheless, the Council of Trent took steps to put a stop to them.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Council_of_Trent">Council of Trent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Council of Trent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 16 July 1562, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> suppressed the office of <i>quaestores</i> and reserved the collection of alms to two <a href="/wiki/Canon_(priest)" title="Canon (priest)">canon members</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chapter_(religion)" title="Chapter (religion)">chapter</a>, who were to receive no remuneration for their work; it also reserved the publication of indulgences to the bishop of the diocese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Then on 4 December 1563, in its final session, the Council addressed the question of indulgences directly, declaring them "most salutary for the Christian people", decreeing that "all evil gains for the obtaining of them be wholly abolished", and instructing bishops to be on the watch for any abuses concerning them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few years later, in 1567, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_V" title="Pope Pius V">Pope Pius V</a> cancelled all grants of indulgences involving any fees or other financial transactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catholic.com_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-catholic.com-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Council of Trent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_VIII" title="Clement VIII">Clement VIII</a> established a commission of Cardinals to deal with indulgences according to the mind of the Council. It continued its work during the pontificate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_V" title="Paul V">Paul V</a> and published various bulls and decrees on the matter. But only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_IX" title="Clement IX">Clement IX</a> established a true <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_of_Indulgences" title="Congregation of Indulgences">Congregation of Indulgences</a> (and Relics) with a Brief of 6 July 1669. In a <i><a href="/wiki/Motu_proprio" title="Motu proprio">motu proprio</a></i> on 28 January 1904, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_X" title="Pius X">Pius X</a> joined the Congregation of Indulgences with that of Rites, but with the restructuring of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Curia" title="Roman Curia">Roman Curia</a> in 1908 all matters regarding indulgences was assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Holy Inquisition</a>. In a <i>motu proprio</i> on 25 March 1915, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedict_XV" title="Benedict XV">Benedict XV</a> transferred the Holy Inquisition's Section for Indulgences to the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Penitentiary" title="Apostolic Penitentiary">Apostolic Penitentiary</a>, but maintained the Holy Inquisition's responsibility for matters regarding the doctrine of indulgences.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodox_Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indulgence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodox Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2431" data-file-width="2992" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg/220px-Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg/330px-Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg/440px-Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orthodox_Indulgence.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An 18th-century absolution certificate granted by the Patriarch of Jerusalem and sold by Greek monks in <a href="/wiki/Wallachia" title="Wallachia">Wallachia</a> (History Museum, <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Churches</a> believe one can be absolved from sins by the Sacred Mystery of Confession. Because of differences in the theology of salvation, indulgences for the remission of temporal punishment of sin do not exist in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, but until the twentieth century there existed in some places a practice of absolution certificates (<i>συγχωροχάρτια – synchorochartia</i>).</p>
<p>While some of these certificates were connected with any patriarch's decrees lifting for the living or the dead some serious ecclesiastical penalty, including excommunication, the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Patriarch_of_Jerusalem" title="Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem">Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem</a>, with the approval of the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople">Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople</a>, had the sole privilege, because of the expense of maintaining the Holy Places and paying the many taxes levied on them, of distributing such documents in large numbers to pilgrims or sending them elsewhere, sometimes with a blank space for the name of the beneficiary, living or dead, an individual or a whole family, for whom the prayers would be read.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Patriarch_of_Jerusalem" title="Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem">Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dositheos_Notaras" title="Dositheos Notaras">Dositheos Notaras</a> (1641–1707) wrote: "It is an established custom and ancient tradition, known to all, that the Most Holy Patriarchs give the absolution certificate (<i>συγχωροχάρτιον – synchorochartion</i>) to the faithful people … they have granted them from the beginning and still do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Russian Orthodox source says that these certificates were in use among Greek Orthodox until the middle of the twentieth century, and were "certificates which absolved from sins, which anyone could obtain, often for a specified sum of money. The absolution granted by these papers, according to Christos Yannaras, had no connection with any participation of the faithful in the Mystery of Penance, nor in the Mystery of the Eucharist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>

In, <i>The reformation in Germany</i>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_Vedder" title="Henry Clay Vedder">Henry Clay Vedder</a>, 1914, Macmillon Company, p. 405. <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=JQ4QAAAAIAAJ&amp;pg=PA405&amp;dq=%22For+a+soul+to+fly+out,+is+for+it+to+obtain+the+vision+of+God%22&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=1nAnTeHnNcOblgfCmPHeAQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCMQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q=%22For%20a%20soul%20to%20fly%20out%2C%20is%20for%20it%20to%20obtain%20the%20vision%20of%20God%22&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">Books.google.com</a> <i>Animam purgatam evolare, est eam visione dei potiri, quod nulla potest intercapedine impediri. Quisquis ergo dicit, non citius posse animam volare, quam in fundo cistae denarius possit tinnire, errat</i>. In: <i>D. Martini Lutheri, Opera Latina: Varii Argumenti</i>, 1865, Henricus Schmidt, ed., Heyder and Zimmer, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt am Main</a> &amp; Erlangen, vol. 1, p. 300. (Reprinted: Nabu Press, 2010, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/1142405516">ISBN 1-142-40551-6</a> <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9781142405519">ISBN 9781142405519</a>). <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=s633jfx_uEUC&amp;pg=PA300&amp;dq=%22Quisquis+ergo+dicit,+non+citius+posse%22&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=Dx4kTfLZKcH58AaOx8TTAQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=4&amp;ved=0CDoQ6AEwAw#v=onepage&amp;q=%22Quisquis%20ergo%20dicit%2C%20non%20citius%20posse%22&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">Books.google.com</a> See also: <a class="external text" href="http://www.newadvent.org/cathen/14539a.htm" rel="nofollow">Catholic Encyclopedia: Johann Tetzel</a></span></li>
