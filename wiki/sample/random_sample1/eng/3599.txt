<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Archimandrite</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The title <i><b>archimandrite</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀρχιμανδρίτης</span> <i>archimandritis</i>), primarily used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic" title="Eastern Catholic">Eastern Catholic</a> churches, originally referred to a <a href="/wiki/Superior_(hierarchy)" title="Superior (hierarchy)">superior</a> abbot whom a <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> appointed to supervise several 'ordinary' <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">abbots</a> (each styled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegumenos" title="Hegumenos">hegumenos</a></i>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monasteries" title="Monasteries">monasteries</a>, or to the abbot of some especially great and important monastery.</p>
<p>It is also used as one purely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_of_honour" title="Title of honour">title of honour</a>, with no connection to any actual monastery, and is bestowed on clergy as a mark of respect or gratitude for service to the Church. This particular sign of respect is only given to those priests who have taken vows of celibacy, that is monks. Distinguished married clergy may receive the title of <a href="/wiki/Archpriest" title="Archpriest">archpriest</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Archimandrite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term derives from the Greek: the first element from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀρχι</span> <i>archi-</i> meaning "highest" or from <i>archon</i> "ruler"; and the second root from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μάνδρα</span> <i>mandra</i> meaning "enclosure" or "corral", "pen" and denoting a "monastery" (compare the usage of "flock" for "congregation").</p>
<p>The title has been in common use since the 5th century, but is mentioned for the first time in a letter to <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius</a>, prefixed to his <a href="/wiki/Panarion" title="Panarion"><i>Panarium</i></a> (ca. 375), but the <i><a href="/wiki/Lausiac_History" title="Lausiac History">Lausiac History</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Palladius_of_Galatia" title="Palladius of Galatia">Palladius</a> may evidence its common use in the 4th century as applied to Saint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pachomius" title="Pachomius">Pachomius</a>.</p>
<p>When the supervision of monasteries passed to another episcopal official—the Great <a href="/wiki/Sakellarios" title="Sakellarios">Sakellarios</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Sacristan" title="Sacristan">sacristan</a>")—the title of archimandrite became an honorary one for abbots of important monasteries (compared to an ordinary abbot, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegumenos" title="Hegumenos">hegumenos</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kiev_Metropolis">Kiev Metropolis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Archimandrite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kiev Metropolis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initially in some cases it served as an extra title: for example, manuscripts of 1174 mention <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hegumen_Polikarp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hegumen Polikarp (page does not exist)">Hegumen Polikarp</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiev_Cave_Monastery" title="Kiev Cave Monastery">Kiev Cave Monastery</a> as "Hegumen Archimandrite".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_usage">Russian usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Archimandrite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Russian usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Archimandrite%27s_crozier_(Kirillo-Belozersk).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2809" data-file-width="868" height="324" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Archimandrite%27s_crozier_%28Kirillo-Belozersk%29.jpg/100px-Archimandrite%27s_crozier_%28Kirillo-Belozersk%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Archimandrite%27s_crozier_%28Kirillo-Belozersk%29.jpg/150px-Archimandrite%27s_crozier_%28Kirillo-Belozersk%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Archimandrite%27s_crozier_%28Kirillo-Belozersk%29.jpg/200px-Archimandrite%27s_crozier_%28Kirillo-Belozersk%29.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Archimandrite%27s_crozier_(Kirillo-Belozersk).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Archimandrite's crozier, Armenia, 19th century</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1764 the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> secularised its monasteries and ranked them in one of three classes, awarding only the abbots at the head of monasteries of the second or first class the title of archimandrite. Abbots of third class monasteries were to be styled "hegumen".</p>
<p>The duties of both a hegumen and an archimandrite are the same; however, during the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Service_(Eastern_Orthodoxy)" title="Divine Service (Eastern Orthodoxy)">Divine Service</a> a hegumen wears a simple <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(vesture)" title="Mantle (vesture)">mantle</a>, while the mantle of an archimandrite is decorated with sacral texts; an archimandrite also wears a <a href="/wiki/Mitre" title="Mitre">mitre</a> and bears a pastoral staff (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pateritsa" title="Pateritsa">pateritsa</a></i>).</p>
<p>The Russian Orthodox Church commonly selects its <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> from the ranks of the archimandrites.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_usage">Greek usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Archimandrite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Greek usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An archimandrite is a priest who has taken monastic vows and is theoretically in line to be ordained a bishop. Churches under the spiritual jurisdiction of the four Eastern Orthodox Patriarchates generally require that such a monastic priest possess a university degree in <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> before they are elevated to the rank of archimandrite. Sometimes the requirement is waived if the priest can show outstanding achievement in other academic fields, such as the humanities or science.</p>

<p>An archimandrite who does not function as an abbot has the style "The Very Reverend Archimandrite" whilst one with abbatial duties uses the style "The Right Reverend Archimandrite".</p>
<p>The word occurs in the <i>Regula Columbani</i> (c. 7), and <a href="/wiki/Charles_du_Fresne,_sieur_du_Cange" title="Charles du Fresne, sieur du Cange">du Cange</a> gives a few other cases of its use in Latin documents, but it never came into vogue in the West; yet, owing to intercourse with Greek and Slavonic Christianity, the title sometimes appears in southern Italy and Sicily, and in Croatia, Hungary and Poland.</p>
