<p><b>Derby Museum and Art Gallery</b> was established in 1879, along with <a href="/wiki/Derby_Central_Library" title="Derby Central Library">Derby Central Library</a>, in a new building designed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Knill_Freeman" title="Richard Knill Freeman">Richard Knill Freeman</a> and given to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derby,_England" title="Derby, England">Derby</a> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Thomas_Bass" title="Michael Thomas Bass">Michael Thomas Bass</a>. The collection includes a gallery displaying many paintings by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wright_of_Derby" title="Joseph Wright of Derby">Joseph Wright of Derby</a>; there is also a large display of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Crown_Derby" title="Royal Crown Derby">Royal Crown Derby</a> and other porcelain from Derby and the surrounding area. Further displays include archaeology, natural history, geology and military collections. The Art Gallery was opened in 1882.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum can trace its start to the formation of the Derby Town and County Museum and Natural History Society on 10 February 1836.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> The society was housed by Full Street Public Baths but it was a private society funded by its members' subscriptions. Its collections were created by donations initially from Dr Forrester who had been a President of <a href="/wiki/Derby_Philosophical_Society" title="Derby Philosophical Society">Derby Philosophical Society</a>. The patron of the Museum Society was <a href="/wiki/William_Cavendish,_6th_Duke_of_Devonshire" title="William Cavendish, 6th Duke of Devonshire">William Cavendish, 6th Duke of Devonshire</a>, and the President was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_George_Harpur_Crewe,_8th_Baronet" title="Sir George Harpur Crewe, 8th Baronet">Sir George Crewe</a> who was a keen naturalist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> Col. <a href="/wiki/George_Gawler" title="George Gawler">George Gawler</a> contributed a collection of minerals and exotic stuffed birds which included an albatross from his time as governor in South Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glover_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-glover-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1839 a major exhibition was held at the Mechanics' Institute which contained many items including those from <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Strutt_(philanthropist)" title="Joseph Strutt (philanthropist)">Joseph Strutt</a>'s collection. Many of these made their way into Derby Museum's collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodeys_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-goodeys-3">[3]</a></sup> The society moved in 1840 to the Athenaeum in Victoria Street. The society's collections grew in 1856 and they were first offered for incorporation into the town by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Mundy_(Markeaton)" title="William Mundy (Markeaton)">William Mundy</a>, but the offer was rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1857, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Llewellyn_Jewitt" title="Llewellyn Jewitt">Llewellyn Jewitt</a> became secretary and the museum was opened to the general public on Saturday mornings. In 1858 the <a href="/wiki/Derby_Philosophical_Society" title="Derby Philosophical Society">Derby Philosophical Society</a> moved to a house on the Wardwick in Derby as it merged with what was called the Derby Town and County Museum and the Natural History Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> This move included the society's library of 4,000 volumes, mathematical and scientific apparatus and its collection of fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1863 the botanist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stirling_Smith_Museum_and_Art_Gallery#The_early_years" title="Stirling Smith Museum and Art Gallery">Alexander Croall</a> was appointed the first Librarian and Curator and the following year the museum and library were joined together. Croall left in 1875<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> to become the curator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stirling_Smith_Museum_and_Art_Gallery" title="Stirling Smith Museum and Art Gallery">Smith Institute in Stirling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Derby Town and County Museum was finally transferred into the ownership of Derby Corporation in 1870, but there were difficulties in finding space to display the collections. After placing all the artefacts into storage for three years, the museum was finally opened to the public on 28 June 1879.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> The Art Gallery opened in 1882 and in 1883 the museum had electricity supplied for new lighting. Today the museum is managed by Derby Museums, a registered charitable trust that manages three sites: the City Museum and Art Gallery, Pickford’s House and The Silk Mill, and all art and collections are held and curated by the Trust.</p>
<p>In 1936 the museum was given a substantial collection of paintings by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Goodey" title="Alfred E. Goodey">Alfred E. Goodey</a> who had been collecting art for 50 years. At his death in 1945 he left £13,000 to build an extension to the museum. The extension, which now houses the museum, was completed in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodeys_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-goodeys-3">[3]</a></sup> Refurbishment to parts of both the new and old buildings were undertaken in 2010–11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leap_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-leap-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derby_and_the_Enlightenment_connection">Derby and the Enlightenment connection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Derby and the Enlightenment connection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Derbymuseumlib.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="884" data-file-width="500" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Derbymuseumlib.jpg/170px-Derbymuseumlib.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Derbymuseumlib.jpg/255px-Derbymuseumlib.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Derbymuseumlib.jpg/340px-Derbymuseumlib.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Derbymuseumlib.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 1876 building mostly houses <a href="/wiki/Derby_Central_Library" title="Derby Central Library">Derby Central Library</a> today but the dividing line with newer building varies</div>

<p>Derby was significant in the eighteenth century for its role in the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>, a period in which science and philosophy challenged the <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a> to rule. The enlightenment has many strands, including the largely philosophical "Scottish enlightenment" centred around the philosopher <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, and political changes that culminated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_revolution" title="French revolution">French revolution</a>, but the English Midlands was an area where many key figures of industry and science came together. The <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Society_of_Birmingham" title="Lunar Society of Birmingham">Lunar Society</a> included <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Darwin" title="Erasmus Darwin">Erasmus Darwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Boulton" title="Matthew Boulton">Matthew Boulton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Wedgwood" title="Josiah Wedgwood">Josiah Wedgwood</a> with <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> corresponding from America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Erasmus Darwin, grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, started the <a href="/wiki/Derby_Philosophical_Society" title="Derby Philosophical Society">Derby Philosophical Society</a> when he moved to Derby in 1783. It was this society which helped to found the first library in Derby.</p>
<p>Some of the paintings by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wright_of_Derby" title="Joseph Wright of Derby">Joseph Wright of Derby</a>, which are renowned for their use of light and shade, are of Lunar Society members. The Derby Gallery possesses over 300 sketches and 34 oil paintings by Wright, and also holds a document collection. One of the paintings is entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Alchymist_in_Search_of_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="The Alchymist in Search of the Philosopher's Stone">The Alchymist in Search of the Philosopher's Stone</a></i> (1771) and it depicts the discovery of the element <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> by German alchemist <a href="/wiki/Hennig_Brand" title="Hennig Brand">Hennig Brand</a> in 1669. A flask in which a large quantity of <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a> has been boiled down is seen bursting into light as the phosphorus, which is abundant in urine, ignites spontaneously in air.</p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/A_Philosopher_Lecturing_on_the_Orrery" title="A Philosopher Lecturing on the Orrery">A Philosopher Lecturing on the Orrery</a></i> shows an early mechanism for demonstrating the movement of the planets around the sun, and an actual <a href="/wiki/Orrery" title="Orrery">orrery</a> is on display in the centre of the gallery in front of the painting. The Scottish scientist, astronomer and lecturer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Ferguson_(1710%E2%80%931776)" title="James Ferguson (1710–1776)">James Ferguson</a> undertook a series of lectures in Derby in July 1762.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Search.revolutionaryplayers.org.uk_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Search.revolutionaryplayers.org.uk-7">[7]</a></sup> They were based on his book <i>Lectures on Select Subjects in Mechanics, Hydrostatics, Pneumatics, Optics &amp;c.</i>, published in 1760. In order to illustrate his lectures he used various machines, models and instruments. Wright possibly attended Ferguson’s lecture, especially as tickets for the event were available from <a href="/wiki/John_Whitehurst" title="John Whitehurst">John Whitehurst</a>, his close neighbour, the clockmaker and scientist. The artist could also have drawn on Whitehurst's practical knowledge to find out more about the orrery and its operation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_of_Joseph_Wright.27s_paintings">Significance of Joseph Wright's paintings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Significance of Joseph Wright's paintings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These factual paintings are considered to have metaphorical meaning too, the bursting into light of the phosphorus in front of a praying figure signifying the problematic transition from faith to scientific understanding and enlightenment, and the various expressions on the figures around the bird in the airpump indicating concern over the possible inhumanity of the coming age of science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Search.revolutionaryplayers.org.uk_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Search.revolutionaryplayers.org.uk-7">[7]</a></sup> These paintings represent a high point in scientific enquiry which began the undermining of the power of religion in Western societies. Some ten years later scientists worldwide would find themselves persecuted, or even put to death in the backlash to the French Revolution of 1789, itself the culmination of enlightenment thinking. Joseph Priestley, member of the Lunar Society and discoverer of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> would flee Britain after his laboratory in Birmingham was smashed and his house burned down in the <a href="/wiki/Priestley_Riots" title="Priestley Riots">Birmingham riots</a> of 1791, by a mob objecting to his outspoken support for the French Revolution;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schofield2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schofield2004-8">[8]</a></sup> and his colleague <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lavoisier" title="Lavoisier">Lavoisier</a> in France would be executed at the <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotine</a>. The politician and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, in his famous <i>Reflections on the Revolution in France</i> (1790), tied natural philosophers, and specifically Priestley, to the French Revolution, writing that radicals who supported science in Britain "considered man in their experiments no more than they do mice in an air pump". In the light of this comment, Wright's painting of the bird in the air pump, completed over twenty years earlier, seems particularly prescient.</p>
<p>Because of this web of connections related to science, and the tensions it created which were so subtly illustrated by the art of the painter Joseph Wright of Derby, Derby Museum and Art Gallery, far from being just a collection of fine paintings as the casual visitor might imagine, is significant for being in a place that some would see as having a very significant role in the birth of modern science and industry worldwide. <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, with its science and industry, has been described as the 'silicon valley' of the eighteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Erasmus Darwin has only a small display. <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a>, friend of Charles Darwin and originator of the phase "the survival of the fittest", who was born in Derby and has been described as the founder of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> does not appear to be mentioned at all.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wright_of_Derby">Wright of Derby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wright of Derby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Derby_City_Council" title="Derby City Council">Derby City Council</a> announced that it was to use Joseph Wright of Derby to brand the city of Derby. At the same time, the Museum announced that it was "joining forces" with Wikipedia to improve the quality of its information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-green-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In February 2011 the <a href="/wiki/Museums,_Libraries_and_Archives_Council" title="Museums, Libraries and Archives Council">Museums, Libraries and Archives Council</a> (MLA) announced that it had awarded Designated status to Derby Museum and Art Gallery for its nationally-significant holdings of paintings and drawings by Joseph Wright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonnie_Prince_Charlie_Room">Bonnie Prince Charlie Room</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bonnie Prince Charlie Room">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>A replica of the room in <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> where <a href="/wiki/Charles_Edward_Stuart" title="Charles Edward Stuart">Bonnie Prince Charlie</a> held his <a href="/wiki/Council_of_war" title="Council of war">council of war</a> in 1745, while on his way south to seize the British crown. The paneling is from the original <a href="/wiki/Exeter_House" title="Exeter House">Exeter House</a>, which was demolished in 1854. At the time of demolition, the panels were brought to the museum, which then received related objects as donations. <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> provided an original letter of Bonnie Prince Charlie from her own collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_artists">Other artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_soldiers_story">The soldiers story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The soldiers story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The soldiers story<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> is dedicated to the history of the <a href="/wiki/9th/12th_Royal_Lancers" title="9th/12th Royal Lancers">9th/12th Royal Lancers</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Foresters" title="Sherwood Foresters">Sherwood Foresters</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire_Yeomanry" title="Derbyshire Yeomanry">Derbyshire Yeomanry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objects">Objects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derby_Museum_and_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Objects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1932" data-file-width="1411" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG/200px-The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG/300px-The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG/400px-The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Repton_Stone-_detail.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The mounted figure on the Repton Stone in the museum has been identified as King <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelbald_of_Mercia" title="Æthelbald of Mercia">Æthelbald of Mercia</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A fragment of a cross shaft from Repton includes on one face a carved image of a mounted man which, it has been suggested, may be a memorial to <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelbald_of_Mercia" title="Æthelbald of Mercia">Æthelbald of Mercia</a>. The figure is of a man wearing mail armour and brandishing a sword and shield, with a <a href="/wiki/Diadem" title="Diadem">diadem</a> bound around his head. In 757, Æthelbald was killed at <a href="/wiki/Seckington" title="Seckington">Seckington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>, near the royal seat of <a href="/wiki/Tamworth,_Staffordshire" title="Tamworth, Staffordshire">Tamworth</a> and buried at <a href="/wiki/Repton" title="Repton">Repton</a>, Derbyshire. If this is Æthelbald, it would make it the earliest large-scale pictorial representation of an English monarch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackwell_Repton_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blackwell_Repton-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum has a large collection of items from the <a href="/wiki/Bretby_Art_Pottery" title="Bretby Art Pottery">Bretby Art Pottery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
