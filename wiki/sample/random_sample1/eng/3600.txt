<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Algebraic topology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Algebraic topology</b> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> that uses tools from <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a> to study <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological spaces</a>. The basic goal is to find algebraic <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics)" title="Invariant (mathematics)">invariants</a> that <a href="/wiki/Classification_theorem" title="Classification theorem">classify</a> topological spaces <a href="/wiki/Up_to" title="Up to">up to</a> <a href="/wiki/Homeomorphism" title="Homeomorphism">homeomorphism</a>, though usually most classify up to <a href="/wiki/Homotopy#Homotopy_equivalence_and_null-homotopy" title="Homotopy">homotopy equivalence</a>.</p>
<p>Although algebraic topology primarily uses algebra to study topological problems, using topology to solve algebraic problems is sometimes also possible. Algebraic topology, for example, allows for a convenient proof that any <a href="/wiki/Subgroup" title="Subgroup">subgroup</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Free_group" title="Free group">free group</a> is again a free group.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_branches_of_algebraic_topology">Main branches of algebraic topology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main branches of algebraic topology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Below are some of the main areas studied in algebraic topology:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homotopy_groups">Homotopy groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Homotopy groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Homotopy_group" title="Homotopy group">Homotopy group</a></div>
<p>In mathematics, homotopy groups are used in algebraic topology to classify <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological spaces</a>. The first and simplest homotopy group is the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_group" title="Fundamental group">fundamental group</a>, which records information about loops in a space. Intuitively, homotopy groups record information about the basic shape, or holes, of a topological space.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homology">Homology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Homology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Homology_(mathematics)" title="Homology (mathematics)">Homology</a></div>
<p>In algebraic topology and <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a>, <b>homology</b> (in part from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ὁμός <i>homos</i> "identical") is a certain general procedure to associate a <a href="/wiki/Sequence" title="Sequence">sequence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian groups</a> or <a href="/wiki/Module_(mathematics)" title="Module (mathematics)">modules</a> with a given mathematical object such as a <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cohomology">Cohomology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cohomology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cohomology" title="Cohomology">Cohomology</a></div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homology_theory" title="Homology theory">homology theory</a> and algebraic topology, <b>cohomology</b> is a general term for a <a href="/wiki/Sequence" title="Sequence">sequence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian groups</a> defined from a <a href="/wiki/Chain_complex" title="Chain complex">co-chain complex</a>. That is, cohomology is defined as the abstract study of <b>cochains</b>, <a href="/wiki/Chain_complex" title="Chain complex">cocycles</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coboundary" title="Coboundary">coboundaries</a>. Cohomology can be viewed as a method of assigning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algebraic_invariant" title="Algebraic invariant">algebraic invariants</a> to a topological space that has a more refined <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structure</a> than does <a href="/wiki/Homology_(mathematics)" title="Homology (mathematics)">homology</a>. Cohomology arises from the algebraic dualization of the construction of homology. In less abstract language, cochains in the fundamental sense should assign 'quantities' to the <i><a href="/wiki/Chain_(algebraic_topology)" title="Chain (algebraic topology)">chains</a></i> of homology theory.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manifolds">Manifolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Manifolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">Manifold</a></div>
<p>A <b>manifold</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a> that near each point resembles <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a>, which can all be realized in three dimensions, but also the <a href="/wiki/Klein_bottle" title="Klein bottle">Klein bottle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Real_projective_plane" title="Real projective plane">real projective plane</a> which cannot be realized in three dimensions, but can be realized in four dimensions. Typically, results in algebraic topology focus on global, non-differentiable aspects of manifolds; for example <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_duality" title="Poincaré duality">Poincaré duality</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Knot_theory">Knot theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Knot theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Knot_theory" title="Knot theory">Knot theory</a></div>
<p><b>Knot theory</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)" title="Knot (mathematics)">mathematical knots</a>. While inspired by knots that appear in daily life in shoelaces and rope, a mathematician's knot differs in that the ends are joined together so that it cannot be undone. In precise mathematical language, a knot is an <a href="/wiki/Embedding" title="Embedding">embedding</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a> in 3-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>, <b>R</b><sup>3</sup>. Two mathematical knots are equivalent if one can be transformed into the other via a deformation of <b>R</b><sup>3</sup> upon itself (known as an <a href="/wiki/Ambient_isotopy" title="Ambient isotopy">ambient isotopy</a>); these transformations correspond to manipulations of a knotted string that do not involve cutting the string or passing the string through itself.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complexes">Complexes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Complexes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_complex" title="Simplicial complex">Simplicial complex</a> and <a href="/wiki/CW_complex" title="CW complex">CW complex</a></div>
<p>A <b>simplicial complex</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a> of a certain kind, constructed by "gluing together" <a href="/wiki/Point_(geometry)" title="Point (geometry)">points</a>, <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangles</a>, and their <a href="/wiki/Simplex" title="Simplex"><i>n</i>-dimensional counterparts</a> (see illustration). Simplicial complexes should not be confused with the more abstract notion of a <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_set" title="Simplicial set">simplicial set</a> appearing in modern simplicial homotopy theory. The purely combinatorial counterpart to a simplicial complex is an <a href="/wiki/Abstract_simplicial_complex" title="Abstract simplicial complex">abstract simplicial complex</a>.</p>
<p>A <b>CW complex</b> is a type of topological space introduced by <a href="/wiki/J._H._C._Whitehead" title="J. H. C. Whitehead">J. H. C. Whitehead</a> to meet the needs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homotopy_theory" title="Homotopy theory">homotopy theory</a>. This class of spaces is broader and has some better <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">categorical</a> properties than <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_complex" title="Simplicial complex">simplicial complexes</a>, but still retains a combinatorial nature that allows for computation (often with a much smaller complex).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_algebraic_invariants">Method of algebraic invariants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Method of algebraic invariants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An older name for the subject was <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_topology" title="Combinatorial topology">combinatorial topology</a>, implying an emphasis on how a space X was constructed from simpler ones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (the modern standard tool for such construction is the <a href="/wiki/CW_complex" title="CW complex">CW-complex</a>). In the 1920s and 1930s, there was growing emphasis on investigating topological spaces by finding correspondences from them to algebraic <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, which led to the change of name to algebraic topology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The combinatorial topology name is still sometimes used to emphasize an algorithmic approach based on decomposition of spaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the algebraic approach, one finds a correspondence between spaces and <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a> that respects the relation of <a href="/wiki/Homeomorphism" title="Homeomorphism">homeomorphism</a> (or more general <a href="/wiki/Homotopy" title="Homotopy">homotopy</a>) of spaces. This allows one to recast statements about topological spaces into statements about groups, which have a great deal of manageable structure, often making these statement easier to prove. Two major ways in which this can be done are through <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_group" title="Fundamental group">fundamental groups</a>, or more generally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homotopy_theory" title="Homotopy theory">homotopy theory</a>, and through <a href="/wiki/Homology_(mathematics)" title="Homology (mathematics)">homology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cohomology" title="Cohomology">cohomology</a> groups. The fundamental groups give us basic information about the structure of a topological space, but they are often <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">nonabelian</a> and can be difficult to work with. The fundamental group of a (finite) <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_complex" title="Simplicial complex">simplicial complex</a> does have a finite <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_a_group" title="Presentation of a group">presentation</a>.</p>
<p>Homology and cohomology groups, on the other hand, are abelian and in many important cases finitely generated. <a href="/wiki/Finitely_generated_abelian_group" title="Finitely generated abelian group">Finitely generated abelian groups</a> are completely classified and are particularly easy to work with.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting_in_category_theory">Setting in category theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Setting in category theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, all constructions of algebraic topology are <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">functorial</a>; the notions of <a href="/wiki/Category_(mathematics)" title="Category (mathematics)">category</a>, <a href="/wiki/Functor" title="Functor">functor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_transformation" title="Natural transformation">natural transformation</a> originated here. Fundamental groups and homology and cohomology groups are not only <i>invariants</i> of the underlying topological space, in the sense that two topological spaces which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeomorphic" title="Homeomorphic">homeomorphic</a> have the same associated groups, but their associated morphisms also correspond — a continuous mapping of spaces induces a <a href="/wiki/Group_homomorphism" title="Group homomorphism">group homomorphism</a> on the associated groups, and these homomorphisms can be used to show non-existence (or, much more deeply, existence) of mappings.</p>
<p>One of the first mathematicians to work with different types of cohomology was <a href="/wiki/Georges_de_Rham" title="Georges de Rham">Georges de Rham</a>. One can use the differential structure of <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">smooth manifolds</a> via <a href="/wiki/De_Rham_cohomology" title="De Rham cohomology">de Rham cohomology</a>, or <a href="/wiki/%C4%8Cech_cohomology" title="Čech cohomology">Čech</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sheaf_cohomology" title="Sheaf cohomology">sheaf cohomology</a> to investigate the solvability of <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> defined on the manifold in question. De Rham showed that all of these approaches were interrelated and that, for a closed, oriented manifold, the Betti numbers derived through simplicial homology were the same Betti numbers as those derived through de Rham cohomology. This was extended in the 1950s, when Eilenberg and Steenrod generalized this approach. They defined homology and cohomology as <a href="/wiki/Functor" title="Functor">functors</a> equipped with <a href="/wiki/Natural_transformation" title="Natural transformation">natural transformations</a> subject to certain axioms (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Weak_equivalence_(homotopy_theory)" title="Weak equivalence (homotopy theory)">weak equivalence</a> of spaces passes to an isomorphism of homology groups), verified that all existing (co)homology theories satisfied these axioms, and then proved that such an axiomatization uniquely characterized the theory.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_algebraic_topology">Applications of algebraic topology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Applications of algebraic topology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Classic applications of algebraic topology include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brouwer_fixed_point_theorem" title="Brouwer fixed point theorem">Brouwer fixed point theorem</a>: every <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">continuous</a> map from the unit <a href="/wiki/Disk_(mathematics)" title="Disk (mathematics)"><i>n</i>-disk</a> to itself has a fixed point.</li>
<li>The free rank of the <i>n</i>th homology group of a <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_complex" title="Simplicial complex">simplicial complex</a> is the <i>n</i>-th <a href="/wiki/Betti_number" title="Betti number">Betti number</a>, which allows one to calculate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler-Poincar%C3%A9_characteristic" title="Euler-Poincaré characteristic">Euler-Poincaré characteristic</a>.</li>
<li>One can use the differential structure of <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">smooth manifolds</a> via <a href="/wiki/De_Rham_cohomology" title="De Rham cohomology">de Rham cohomology</a>, or <a href="/wiki/%C4%8Cech_cohomology" title="Čech cohomology">Čech</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sheaf_cohomology" title="Sheaf cohomology">sheaf cohomology</a> to investigate the solvability of <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> defined on the manifold in question.</li>
<li>A manifold is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orientable" title="Orientable">orientable</a> when the top-dimensional integral homology group is the integers, and is non-orientable when it is 0.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/N-sphere" title="N-sphere"><i>n</i>-sphere</a> admits a nowhere-vanishing continuous unit <a href="/wiki/Vector_fields_on_spheres" title="Vector fields on spheres">vector field</a> if and only if <i>n</i> is odd. (For <i>n</i> = 2, this is sometimes called the "<a href="/wiki/Hairy_ball_theorem" title="Hairy ball theorem">hairy ball theorem</a>".)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Borsuk%E2%80%93Ulam_theorem" title="Borsuk–Ulam theorem">Borsuk–Ulam theorem</a>: any continuous map from the <i>n</i>-sphere to Euclidean <i>n</i>-space identifies at least one pair of antipodal points.</li>
<li>Any subgroup of a <a href="/wiki/Free_group" title="Free group">free group</a> is free. This result is quite interesting, because the statement is purely algebraic yet the simplest known proof is topological. Namely, any free group <i>G</i> may be realized as the fundamental group of a <a href="/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)" title="Graph (discrete mathematics)">graph</a> <i>X</i>. The main theorem on <a href="/wiki/Covering_space" title="Covering space">covering spaces</a> tells us that every subgroup <i>H</i> of <i>G</i> is the fundamental group of some covering space <i>Y</i> of <i>X</i>; but every such <i>Y</i> is again a graph. Therefore, its fundamental group <i>H</i> is free. On the other hand, this type of application is also handled more simply by the use of covering morphisms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groupoids" title="Groupoids">groupoids</a>, and that technique has yielded subgroup theorems not yet proved by methods of algebraic topology. (See the book by Higgins listed under groupoids.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Topological_combinatorics" title="Topological combinatorics">Topological combinatorics</a></li>
</ul>
