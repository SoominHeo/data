<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bandwidth (signal processing)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Bandwidth</b> is the difference between the upper and lower frequencies in a continuous set of frequencies. It is typically measured in <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">hertz</a>, and may sometimes refer to <i>passband bandwidth</i>, sometimes to <i>baseband bandwidth</i>, depending on context. <b>Passband bandwidth</b> is the difference between the upper and lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cutoff_frequencies" title="Cutoff frequencies">cutoff frequencies</a> of, for example, a <a href="/wiki/Band-pass_filter" title="Band-pass filter">band-pass filter</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_channel" title="Communication channel">communication channel</a>, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_spectrum" title="Signal spectrum">signal spectrum</a>. In the case of a <a href="/wiki/Low-pass_filter" title="Low-pass filter">low-pass filter</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseband_signal" title="Baseband signal">baseband signal</a>, the bandwidth is equal to its upper cutoff frequency.</p>
<p>Bandwidth in hertz is a central concept in many fields, including <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information theory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_communication" title="Digital communication">digital communications</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_communication" title="Radio communication">radio communications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Signal_processing" title="Signal processing">signal processing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> and is one of the determinants of the capacity of a given communication channel.</p>
<p>A key characteristic of bandwidth is that any band of a given width can carry the same amount of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, regardless of where that band is located in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">frequency spectrum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[note 1]</a></sup> For example, a 3 kHz band can carry a telephone conversation whether that band is at baseband (as in a <a href="/wiki/Plain_old_telephone_service" title="Plain old telephone service">POTS</a> telephone line) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modulated" title="Modulated">modulated</a> to some higher <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandwidth_(signal_processing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bandwidth is a key concept in many <a href="/wiki/Telephony" title="Telephony">telephony</a> applications. In <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> communications, for example, bandwidth is the frequency range occupied by a modulated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_signal" title="Carrier signal">carrier signal</a>, whereas in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a> it is the width of an individual <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral line</a> or the entire <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectral range</a>.</p>
<p>In many signal processing contexts, bandwidth is a valuable and limited resource. For example, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FM_radio" title="FM radio">FM radio</a> receiver's <a href="/wiki/Tuner_(radio)" title="Tuner (radio)">tuner</a> spans a limited range of frequencies. A government agency (such as the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> in the United States) may apportion the regionally available bandwidth to <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_license" title="Broadcast license">broadcast license</a> holders so that their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_(electronics)" title="Signal (electronics)">signals</a> do not mutually interfere. Each transmitter owns a slice of bandwidth, a valuable (albeit intangible) commodity.</p>
<p>For different applications there are different precise definitions, which are necessarily different for signals than for systems. For example, one definition of bandwidth, for a system, could be the range of frequencies beyond which the <a href="/wiki/Frequency_response" title="Frequency response">frequency response</a> is zero. This would correspond to the mathematical notion of the <a href="/wiki/Support_(mathematics)" title="Support (mathematics)">support</a> of a function (i.e., the total "length" of values for which the function is nonzero). A less strict and more practically useful definition will refer to the frequencies beyond which frequency response is <i>small</i>. Small could mean less than 3 <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a> below the maximum value, or more rarely 10 dB below, or it could mean below a certain absolute value. As with any definition of the <i>width</i> of a function, many definitions are suitable for different purposes.</p>
<p>Bandwidth typically refers to baseband bandwidth in the context of, for example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_theorem" title="Sampling theorem">sampling theorem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nyquist_rate" title="Nyquist rate">Nyquist sampling rate</a>, while it refers to passband bandwidth in the context of <a href="/wiki/Nyquist_rate" title="Nyquist rate">Nyquist symbol rate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shannon-Hartley" title="Shannon-Hartley">Shannon-Hartley</a> <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel capacity</a> for communication systems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="x_dB_bandwidth"><i>x</i> dB bandwidth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandwidth_(signal_processing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: x dB bandwidth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In some contexts, the signal bandwidth in <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">hertz</a> refers to the frequency range in which the signal's <a href="/wiki/Spectral_density" title="Spectral density">spectral density</a> (in W/Hz or V<sup>2</sup>/Hz) is nonzero or above a small threshold value. That definition is used in calculations of the lowest sampling rate that will satisfy the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_theorem" title="Sampling theorem">sampling theorem</a>. The threshold value is often defined relative to the maximum value, and is most commonly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3dB_point" title="3dB point">3dB point</a>, that is the point where the spectral density is half its maximum value (or the spectral amplitude, in V or V/Hz, is 70.7% of its maximum).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word bandwidth applies to signals as described above, but it could also apply to <i>systems</i>, for example <a href="/wiki/Electronic_filter" title="Electronic filter">filters</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_channel" title="Communication channel">communication channels</a>. To say that a system has a certain bandwidth means that the system can process signals of that bandwidth, or that the system reduces the bandwidth of a white noise input to that bandwidth.</p>
<p>The 3 dB bandwidth of an <a href="/wiki/Electronic_filter" title="Electronic filter">electronic filter</a> or communication channel is the part of the system's frequency response that lies within 3 dB of the response at its peak, which in the passband filter case is typically at or near its <a href="/wiki/Center_frequency" title="Center frequency">center frequency</a>, and in the lowpass filter is near 0 hertz. If the maximum gain is 0 dB, the 3 dB bandwidth is the frequency range where the gain is more than −3 dB, or the attenuation is less than 3 dB. This is also the range of frequencies where the amplitude gain is above 70.7% of the maximum amplitude gain, and the power gain is above half the maximum power gain. This same "half power gain" convention is also used in <a href="/wiki/Spectral_width" title="Spectral width">spectral width</a>, and more generally for extent of functions as <a href="/wiki/Full_width_at_half_maximum" title="Full width at half maximum">full width at half maximum</a> (FWHM).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Electronic_filter" title="Electronic filter">electronic filter</a> design, a filter specification may require that within the filter <a href="/wiki/Passband" title="Passband">passband</a>, the gain is nominally 0 dB ± a small number of dB, for example within the ±1 dB interval. In the <a href="/wiki/Stopband" title="Stopband">stopband</a>(s), the required attenuation in dB is above a certain level, for example &gt;100 dB. In a <a href="/wiki/Transition_band" title="Transition band">transition band</a> the gain is not specified. In this case, the filter bandwidth corresponds to the passband width, which in this example is the 1 dB-bandwidth. If the filter shows amplitude ripple within the passband, the <i>x</i> dB point refers to the point where the gain is <i>x</i> dB below the nominal passband gain rather than <i>x</i> dB below the maximum gain.</p>
<p><span id="Fractional_bandwidth"></span>A commonly used quantity is <b>fractional bandwidth</b>. This is the bandwidth of a device divided by its center frequency. E.g., a passband filter that has a bandwidth of 2 MHz with center frequency 10 MHz will have a fractional bandwidth of 2/10, or 20%.</p>
<p>In communication systems, in calculations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shannon%E2%80%93Hartley" title="Shannon–Hartley">Shannon–Hartley</a> <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel capacity</a>, bandwidth refers to the 3 dB-bandwidth. In calculations of the maximum <a href="/wiki/Symbol_rate" title="Symbol rate">symbol rate</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyquist_sampling_rate" title="Nyquist sampling rate">Nyquist sampling rate</a>, and maximum bit rate according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shannon%E2%80%93Hartley#Hartley.27s_law" title="Shannon–Hartley">Hartley formula</a>, the bandwidth refers to the frequency range within which the gain is non-zero, or the gain in dB is below a very large value.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antenna_systems">Antenna systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandwidth_(signal_processing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antenna systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)#Bandwidth" title="Antenna (radio)">Antenna (radio) § Bandwidth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antenna_measurement#Bandwidth" title="Antenna measurement">Antenna measurement § Bandwidth</a></div>
<p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a>, two different methods of expressing relative bandwidth are used for <a href="/wiki/Narrowband" title="Narrowband">narrowband</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wideband" title="Wideband">wideband</a> antennas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> For either, a set of criteria is established to define the extents of the bandwidth, such as <a href="/wiki/Input_impedance" title="Input impedance">input impedance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pattern" title="Pattern">pattern</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarization_(antenna)" title="Polarization (antenna)">polarization</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photonics">Photonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandwidth_(signal_processing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Photonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Photonics" title="Photonics">photonics</a>, the term <i>bandwidth</i> occurs in a variety of meanings:</p>
<ul>
<li>the bandwidth of the output of some light source, e.g., an ASE source or a laser; the bandwidth of ultrashort optical pulses can be particularly large</li>
<li>the width of the frequency range that can be transmitted by some element, e.g. an optical fiber</li>
<li>the gain bandwidth of an optical amplifier</li>
<li>the width of the range of some other phenomenon (e.g., a reflection, the phase matching of a nonlinear process, or some resonance)</li>
<li>the maximum modulation frequency (or range of modulation frequencies) of an optical modulator</li>
<li>the range of frequencies in which some measurement apparatus (e.g., a powermeter) can operate</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">data rate</a> (e.g., in Gbit/s) achieved in an optical communication system; see <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth (computing)</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>A related concept is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_linewidth" title="Spectral linewidth">spectral linewidth</a> of the radiation emitted by excited atoms.</p>
