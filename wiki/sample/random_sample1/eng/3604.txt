<p>The <b>giant oarfish</b> (<i>Regalecus glesne</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Oarfish" title="Oarfish">oarfish</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regalecidae" title="Regalecidae">Regalecidae</a>. It is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanodromous" title="Oceanodromous">oceanodromous</a> species with a worldwide distribution, excluding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_regions" title="Polar regions">polar regions</a>. Other common names include <b>Pacific oarfish</b>, <b>king of herrings</b>, <b>ribbonfish</b>, and <b>streamer fish</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>R. glesne</i> is the world's longest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fish</a>. Its shape is ribbon-like, narrow laterally, with a <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> along its entire length, stubby pectoral fins, and long, oar-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a>, from which its common name is derived. Its coloration is silvery with dark markings, and its fins are red. Its physical characteristics and its undulating mode of swimming have led to speculation that it might be the source of many "<a href="/wiki/Sea_serpent" title="Sea serpent">sea serpent</a>" sightings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_oarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>R. glesne</i> was first described by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ascanius" title="Peter Ascanius">Peter Ascanius</a> in 1772. The genus name, <i>Regalecus</i>, signifies "belonging to a king"; the specific epithet <i>glesne</i> is from "Glesnaes", the name of a farm at Glesvær (not far from Norway's second largest city of <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a>), where the <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type specimen</a> was found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNatlMuseum_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNatlMuseum-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its "king of <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herrings</a>" nickname may derive from its crownlike appendages and from being sighted near shoals of herring, which fishermen thought were being guided by this fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grtbkan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-grtbkan-4">[4]</a></sup> Its common name, oarfish, is probably an allusion to the shape of its pelvic fins, or else it may refer to the long slender shape of the fish itself.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_oarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The giant oarfish is found worldwide in the upper layers of the open ocean (the <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic zone</a>). It is believed to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanodromous" title="Oceanodromous">oceanodromous</a>, following its primary food source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CaribbeanJS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CaribbeanJS-5">[5]</a></sup> It has been found as far north as <a href="/wiki/72nd_parallel_north" title="72nd parallel north">72°N</a> and as far south as <a href="/wiki/52nd_parallel_south" title="52nd parallel south">52°S</a>, but is most common in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> to <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">middle latitudes</a>. It is thought to inhabit the sunlit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epipelagic" title="Epipelagic">epipelagic</a> to dimly lit <a href="/wiki/Mesopelagic_zone" title="Mesopelagic zone">mesopelagic zones</a>, ranging as deeply as 1,000 m (3,300 ft) below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olney2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olney2005-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_oarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>This species is the world's longest bony fish, reaching a record length of 11 m (36 ft); however, unconfirmed specimens of up to 17 m (56 ft) have been reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_WildlifeEncyc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_WildlifeEncyc-7">[7]</a></sup> It is commonly measured to 3 m (9.8 ft) <a href="/wiki/Fish_measurement" title="Fish measurement">in total length</a>. The maximum recorded weight of a giant oarfish is 270 kg (600 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_WildlifeEncyc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_WildlifeEncyc-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its shape is ribbonlike, narrow laterally, with a dorsal fin along its entire length from between its eyes to the tip of its tail. The fin rays are soft and may number up to 400 or more. At the head of the fish, the rays are lengthened forming a distinctive red crest. Its pectoral and pelvic fins are nearly adjacent. The pectoral fins are stubby while the pelvic fins are long, single-rayed, and reminiscent of an oar in shape, widening at the tip. Its head is small with the protrusible jaw typical of lampriformes; it has 40 to 58 <a href="/wiki/Gill_raker" title="Gill raker">gill rakers</a>, and no teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_FOTW_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson_FOTW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organs of the giant oarfish are concentrated toward the head end of the body, possibly enabling it to survive losing large portions of its tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_WildlifeEncyc_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_WildlifeEncyc-7">[7]</a></sup> It has no swim bladder. The liver of <i>R. glesne</i> is orange or red, the likely result of <a href="/wiki/Astaxanthin" title="Astaxanthin">astaxanthin</a> in its diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> begins above and behind the eye then, descending to the lower third of the body, extends to the caudal tip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruiz_Argentine_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruiz_Argentine-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The skin of <i>R. glesne</i> is scaleless but covered with <a href="/wiki/Tubercle" title="Tubercle">tubercles</a>. The skin color is silver with streaks, spots or splotches of black or dark gray, and a bluish or brownish tinge on the head. Its fins, including its long dorsal fin and crest, are red, again probably resulting from its diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_oarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="499" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png/220px-Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png/330px-Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png/440px-Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Regalecus_glesne_juvenile.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>R. glesne</i> juvenile</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Little is known about oarfish behavior. It has been observed swimming by means of its dorsal fin, and also swimming in a vertical position. In 2010, scientists filmed a giant oarfish in the Gulf of Mexico swimming in the mesopelagic layer, the first footage of a reliably identified <i>R. glesne</i> in its natural setting. The footage was caught during a survey, using an <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_underwater_vehicle" title="Remotely operated underwater vehicle">ROV</a> in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Horse_PDQ" title="Thunder Horse PDQ">Thunder Horse PDQ</a>, and shows the fish swimming in a columnar orientation, tail downward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoMsighting_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GoMsighting-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a> and other small crustaceans, as well as small fish and squid. It is known to spawn from July to December. The eggs are 2.5 mm (0.1 in) large,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSIP_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSIP-12">[12]</a></sup> and float near the surface until hatching. Its larvae are also observed near the surface during this season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_WildlifeEncyc_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_WildlifeEncyc-7">[7]</a></sup> As an adult it is believed to be solitary.</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_oarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>R. glesne</i> is not fished commercially, but is an occasional <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> in commercial nets, and as such it has been marketed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishBase_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FishBase-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_WildlifeEncyc_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_WildlifeEncyc-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because they are not often seen and because of their size, elongated bodies, and appearance, giant oarfish are presumed to be responsible for some <a href="/wiki/Sea_serpent" title="Sea serpent">sea serpent</a> sightings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis_Monsters_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis_Monsters-13">[13]</a></sup> Formerly considered rare, the species is now suspected to be comparatively common,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_WildlifeEncyc_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_WildlifeEncyc-7">[7]</a></sup> although sightings of healthy specimens in their natural habitat remain very unusual.</p>
