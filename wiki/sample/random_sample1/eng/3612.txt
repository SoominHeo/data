<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hypothetical types of biochemistry</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

"The existence of lakes of liquid hydrocarbons on Titan opens up the possibility for solvents and energy sources that are alternatives to those in our biosphere and that might support novel life forms altogether different from those on Earth."—NASA Astrobiology Roadmap 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>

<p><b>Hypothetical types of biochemistry</b> are forms of <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> speculated to be scientifically viable but not proven to exist at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKay_2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKay_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> The kinds of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living organisms currently known on Earth</a> all use <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> compounds for basic structural and <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> functions, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> or <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> to define and control their form. If <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> exists on other <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a>, it may be chemically similar; it is also possible that there are organisms with quite different chemistries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRD-201719_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-WRD-201719-3">[3]</a></sup>—for instance involving other classes of carbon compounds, compounds of another element, or another solvent in place of water.</p>
<p>The possibility of life-forms being based on "alternative" biochemistries is the topic of an ongoing scientific discussion, informed by what is known about extraterrestrial environments and about the chemical behaviour of various elements and compounds. It is also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_fiction_theme" title="Science fiction theme">common subject in science fiction</a>.</p>
<p>The element <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> has been much discussed as a hypothetical alternative to carbon. Silicon is in the same group as carbon on the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> and, like carbon, it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetravalent" title="Tetravalent">tetravalent</a>, although the silicon analogs of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> are generally less stable. Hypothetical alternatives to water include <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, which, like water, is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a> molecule, and cosmically abundant; and non-polar <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> solvents such as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, which are known to exist in liquid form on the surface of <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shadow_biosphere">Shadow biosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Shadow biosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arecibo_message.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1125" data-file-width="375" height="525" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Arecibo_message.svg/175px-Arecibo_message.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Arecibo_message.svg/263px-Arecibo_message.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Arecibo_message.svg/350px-Arecibo_message.svg.png 2x" width="175"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shadow_biosphere" title="Shadow biosphere">Shadow biosphere</a></div>
<p>Apart from the prospect of finding different forms of life on other <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> itself has been suggested as a place where a shadow biosphere of biochemically unfamiliar micro-organisms might have lived in the past, or may still exist today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative-chirality_biomolecules">Alternative-chirality biomolecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative-chirality biomolecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Perhaps the least unusual alternative biochemistry would be one with differing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-handed_protein" title="Left-handed protein">chirality</a> of its biomolecules. In known Earth-based life, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> are almost universally of the <small>L</small> form and <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> are of the <small>D</small> form. Molecules of opposite chirality have identical chemical properties to their mirrored forms, so life that used <small>D</small> amino acids or <small>L</small> sugars may be possible; molecules of such a chirality, however, would be incompatible with organisms using the opposing chirality molecules. Amino acids whose chirality is opposite to the norm are found on Earth, and these substances are generally thought to result from decay of organisms of normal chirality. However, physicist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Davies" title="Paul Davies">Paul Davies</a> speculates that some of them might be products of "anti-chiral" life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is questionable, however, whether such a biochemistry would be truly alien. Although it would certainly be an alternative <a href="/wiki/Stereochemistry" title="Stereochemistry">stereochemistry</a>, molecules that are overwhelmingly found in one <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomer</a> throughout the vast majority of organisms can nonetheless often be found in another enantiomer in different (often <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a>) organisms such as in comparisons between members of <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Domain_(biology)" title="Domain (biology)">domains</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> making it an open topic whether an alternative stereochemistry is truly novel.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-carbon-based_biochemistries">Non-carbon-based biochemistries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-carbon-based biochemistries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On Earth, all known living things have a carbon-based structure and system. Scientists have speculated about the pros and cons of using <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> other than carbon to form the molecular structures necessary for life, but no one has proposed a theory employing such atoms to form all the necessary structures. However, as <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> argued, it is very difficult to be certain whether a statement that applies to all life on Earth will turn out to apply to all life throughout the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Sagan used the term "<a href="/wiki/Carbon_chauvinism" title="Carbon chauvinism">carbon chauvinism</a>" for such an assumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan_2000page.3D46_46_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan_2000page.3D46_46-8">[8]</a></sup> Carl Sagan regarded <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> as conceivable alternatives to carbon;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan_2000page.3D46_46_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan_2000page.3D46_46-8">[8]</a></sup> but, on the other hand, he noted that carbon does seem more chemically versatile and is more abundant in the cosmos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Organosilicon" title="Organosilicon">Organosilicon</a></div>
<p>The silicon atom has been much discussed as the basis for an alternative biochemical system, because silicon has many <a href="/wiki/Chemical_property" title="Chemical property">chemical properties</a> similar to those of carbon and is in the same <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group of the periodic table</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_group" title="Carbon group">carbon group</a>. Like carbon, silicon can create molecules that are sufficiently large to carry biological information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pace_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pace-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, silicon has several drawbacks as an alternative to carbon. Silicon, unlike carbon, lacks the ability to form chemical bonds with diverse types of atoms as is necessary for the chemical versatility required for metabolism. Elements creating organic functional groups with carbon include hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur, and metals such as iron, magnesium, and zinc. Silicon, on the other hand, interacts with very few other types of atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pace_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pace-10">[10]</a></sup> Moreover, where it does interact with other atoms, silicon creates molecules that have been described as "monotonous compared with the combinatorial universe of organic macromolecules".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pace_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pace-10">[10]</a></sup> This is because silicon atoms are much bigger, having a larger <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">atomic radius</a>, and so have difficulty forming double bonds (the double bonded carbon is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group, a fundamental motif of bio-organic chemistry).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silanes" title="Silanes">Silanes</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and silicon that are analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, are highly reactive with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>, and long-chain silanes spontaneously decompose. Molecules incorporating <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> of alternating silicon and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms instead of direct bonds between silicon, known collectively as <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicones</a>, are much more stable. It has been suggested that silicone-based chemicals would be more stable than equivalent hydrocarbons in a sulfuric-acid-rich environment, as is found in some extraterrestrial locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world-building_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-world-building-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the varieties of molecules identified in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> as of 1998<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 84 are based on carbon while only 8 are based on silicon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Moreover, of those 8 compounds, four also include carbon within them. The <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">cosmic abundance</a> of carbon to silicon is roughly 10 to 1. This may suggest a greater variety of complex carbon compounds throughout the cosmos, providing less of a foundation on which to build silicon-based biologies, at least under the conditions prevalent on the surface of planets. Also, even though <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a> are exceptionally silicon-rich and carbon-poor (the relative abundance of silicon to carbon in the Earth's crust is roughly 925:1), terrestrial life is carbon-based. The fact that carbon is used instead of silicon, may be evidence that silicon is poorly suited for biochemistry on Earth-like planets. For example: silicon is less versatile than carbon in forming compounds; the compounds formed by silicon are unstable and it blocks the flow of heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BC_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BC-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even so, <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_silica" title="Biogenic silica">biogenic silica</a> is used by some Earth life, such as the <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> skeletal structure of <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis#Clay_hypothesis" title="Abiogenesis">clay hypothesis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Graham_Cairns-Smith" title="Graham Cairns-Smith">A. G. Cairns-Smith</a>, silicate minerals in water played a crucial role in abiogenesis: they replicated their crystal structures, interacted with carbon compounds, and were the precursors of carbon-based life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although not observed in nature, carbon-silicon bonds have been added to biochemistry by using directed evolution (artificial selection). A heme containing cytochrome <i>c</i> protein from <i>Rhodothermus marinus</i> has been engineered using directed evolution to catalyze the formation of new carbon silicon bonds between hydrosilanes and diazo compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Silicon compounds may possibly be biologically useful under temperatures or pressures different from the surface of a terrestrial planet, either in conjunction with or in a role less directly analogous to carbon. Polysilanols, the silicon compounds corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, are soluble in liquid nitrogen, suggesting that they could play a role in very low temperature biochemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In cinematic and literary science fiction, at a moment when man-made machines cross from nonliving to living, it is often posited, this new form would be the first example of non-carbon-based life. Since the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> in the late 1960s, these machines are often classed as <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> (or computer-guided <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a>) and filed under "silicon-based life", even though the silicon backing matrix of these processors is not nearly as fundamental to their operation as carbon is for "wet life".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_exotic_element-based_biochemistries">Other exotic element-based biochemistries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other exotic element-based biochemistries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Organoboron_chemistry" title="Organoboron chemistry">Organoboron chemistry</a></div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boranes" title="Boranes">Boranes</a> are dangerously explosive in Earth's atmosphere, but would be more stable in a reducing environment. However, boron's low cosmic abundance makes it less likely as a base for life than carbon.</li>
<li>Various metals, together with oxygen, can form very complex and thermally stable structures rivaling those of organic compounds;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/Heteropoly_acid" title="Heteropoly acid">heteropoly acids</a> are one such family. Some metal oxides are also similar to carbon in their ability to form both nanotube structures and diamond-like crystals (such as <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic zirconia</a>). <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">Titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> are all more abundant in the Earth's crust than carbon. Metal-oxide-based life could therefore be a possibility under certain conditions, including those (such as high temperatures) at which carbon-based life would be unlikely. The Cronin group at Glasgow University reported self-assembly of tungsten polyoxometallates into cell-like spheres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cronin_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cronin-19">[19]</a></sup> By modifying their metal oxide content, the spheres can acquire holes that act as porous membrane, selectively allowing chemicals in and out of the sphere according to size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cronin_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cronin-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">Sulfur</a> is also able to form long-chain molecules, but suffers from the same high-reactivity problems as phosphorus and silanes. The biological use of sulfur as an alternative to carbon is purely hypothetical, especially because sulfur usually forms only linear chains rather than branched ones. (The biological use of sulfur as an electron acceptor is widespread and can be traced back 3.5 billion years on Earth, thus predating the use of molecular oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sulfur-reducing_bacteria" title="Sulfur-reducing bacteria">Sulfur-reducing bacteria</a> can utilize elemental sulfur instead of oxygen, reducing sulfur to <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>.)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arsenic_as_an_alternative_to_phosphorus">Arsenic as an alternative to phosphorus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arsenic as an alternative to phosphorus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/GFAJ-1" title="GFAJ-1">GFAJ-1</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">Arsenic</a>, which is chemically similar to <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, while poisonous for most <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">life forms</a> on Earth, is incorporated into the biochemistry of some organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_algae" title="Marine algae">marine algae</a> incorporate arsenic into complex organic molecules such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenosugar" title="Arsenosugar">arsenosugars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arsenobetaine" title="Arsenobetaine">arsenobetaines</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">Fungi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> can produce volatile methylated arsenic compounds. Arsenate reduction and arsenite oxidation have been observed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">microbes</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Chrysiogenes_arsenatis" title="Chrysiogenes arsenatis">Chrysiogenes arsenatis</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Additionally, some <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a> can use arsenate as a terminal electron acceptor during anaerobic growth and some can utilize arsenite as an electron donor to generate energy.</p>
<p>It has been speculated that the earliest life forms on Earth may have used <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_biochemistry" title="Arsenic biochemistry">arsenic</a> in place of phosphorus in the structure of their DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A common objection to this scenario is that arsenate esters are so much less stable to <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> than corresponding <a href="/wiki/Organophosphate" title="Organophosphate">phosphate esters</a> that arsenic is poorly suited for this function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Westheimer_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Westheimer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The authors of a 2010 <a href="/wiki/Geomicrobiology" title="Geomicrobiology">geomicrobiology</a> study, supported in part by NASA, have postulated that a bacterium, named <a href="/wiki/GFAJ-1" title="GFAJ-1">GFAJ-1</a>, collected in the sediments of <a href="/wiki/Mono_Lake" title="Mono Lake">Mono Lake</a> in eastern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, can employ such 'arsenic DNA' when cultured without phosphorus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasafund_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasafund-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolfe-Simon_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfe-Simon-26">[26]</a></sup> They proposed that the bacterium may employ high levels of <a href="/wiki/Polyhydroxybutyrate" title="Polyhydroxybutyrate">poly-β-hydroxybutyrate</a> or other means to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activity_(chemistry)" title="Activity (chemistry)">effective concentration</a> of water and stabilize its arsenate esters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolfe-Simon_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfe-Simon-26">[26]</a></sup> This claim was heavily criticized almost immediately after publication for the perceived lack of appropriate controls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redfield_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Redfield-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beasties_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beasties-28">[28]</a></sup> Science writer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zimmer" title="Carl Zimmer">Carl Zimmer</a> contacted several scientists for an assessment: "I reached out to a dozen experts ... Almost unanimously, they think the NASA scientists have failed to make their case".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zimmer_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmer-29">[29]</a></sup> Other authors were unable to reproduce their results and showed that the study had issues with phosphate contamination, suggesting that the low amounts present could sustain extremophile lifeforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Alternatively, it was suggested that GFAJ-1 cells grow by recycling phosphate from degraded ribosomes, rather than by replacing it with arsenate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-water_solvents">Non-water solvents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-water solvents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1528" data-file-width="778" height="344" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG/175px-Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG/263px-Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG/350px-Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carl_Sagan_Viking.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Carl Sagan speculated alien life might use ammonia, hydrocarbons or hydrogen fluoride instead of water.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addition to carbon compounds, all currently known terrestrial life also requires water as a solvent. This has led to discussions about whether water is the only liquid capable of filling that role. The idea that an extraterrestrial life-form might be based on a solvent other than water has been taken seriously in recent scientific literature by the biochemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steven_Benner" title="Steven Benner">Steven Benner</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and by the astrobiological committee chaired by John A. Baross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.nap.edu_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.nap.edu-33">[33]</a></sup> Solvents discussed by the Baross committee include <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barossammonia_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-barossammonia-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosssulfuric_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-barosssulfuric-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Formamide" title="Formamide">formamide</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planetary_Systems_p_74_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planetary_Systems_p_74-36">[36]</a></sup> hydrocarbons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planetary_Systems_p_74_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Planetary_Systems_p_74-36">[36]</a></sup> and (at temperatures much lower than Earth's) liquid <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, or hydrogen in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosscryo_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-barosscryo-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carl Sagan once described himself as both a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_chauvinist" title="Carbon chauvinist">carbon chauvinist</a> and a water chauvinist;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> however on another occasion he said he was a carbon chauvinist but "not that much of a water chauvinist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conversations_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-conversations-39">[39]</a></sup> He speculated on hydrocarbons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conversations_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-conversations-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128-40">[40]</a></sup> and ammonia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conversations_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-conversations-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128-40">[40]</a></sup> as possible alternatives to water.</p>
<p>Some of the properties of water that are important for life processes include a large temperature range over which it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_phase" title="Liquid phase">liquid</a>, a high <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> (useful for temperature regulation), a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a>, and the ability to dissolve a wide variety of compounds. Water is also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>, meaning it can donate and accept an <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">H<sup>+</sup> ion</a>, allowing it to act as an acid or a base. This property is crucial in many organic and biochemical reactions, where water serves as a solvent, a reactant, or a product. There are other chemicals with similar properties that have sometimes been proposed as alternatives. Additionally, water has the unusual property of being less dense as a solid (ice) than as a liquid. This is why bodies of water freeze over but do not freeze solid (from the bottom up). If ice were denser than liquid water (as is true for nearly all other compounds), then large bodies of liquid would slowly freeze solid, which would not be conducive to the formation of life. Water as a compound is cosmically abundant, although much of it is in the form of vapour or ice. Subsurface liquid water is considered likely or possible on several of the outer moons: <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a> (where geysers have been observed), <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>. Earth and Titan are the only worlds currently known to have stable bodies of liquid on their surfaces.</p>
<p>Not all properties of water are necessarily advantageous for life, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosswater_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-barosswater-41">[41]</a></sup> For instance, water ice has a high <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosswater_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-barosswater-41">[41]</a></sup> meaning that it reflects a significant quantity of light and heat from the Sun. During <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>, as reflective ice builds up over the surface of the water, the effects of global cooling are increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosswater_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-barosswater-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are some properties that make certain compounds and elements much more favorable than others as solvents in a successful biosphere. The solvent must be able to exist in liquid equilibrium over a range of temperatures the planetary object would normally encounter. Because boiling points vary with the pressure, the question tends not to be <i>does</i> the prospective solvent remain liquid, but <i>at what pressure</i>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a> has a narrow liquid phase temperature range at 1 atmosphere, but in an atmosphere with the pressure of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, with 92 bars (9.2 MPa) of pressure, it can indeed exist in liquid form over a wide temperature range.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ammonia">Ammonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ammonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ammonia_World.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4414" data-file-width="4414" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Ammonia_World.jpg/320px-Ammonia_World.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Ammonia_World.jpg/480px-Ammonia_World.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Ammonia_World.jpg/640px-Ammonia_World.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ammonia_World.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Artist's conception of how a planet with ammonia-based life might look.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> molecule (NH<sub>3</sub>), like the water molecule, is abundant in the universe, being a compound of hydrogen (the simplest and most common element) with another very common element, nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-42">[42]</a></sup> The possible role of liquid ammonia as an alternative solvent for life is an idea that goes back at least to 1954, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.B.S._Haldane" title="J.B.S. Haldane">J.B.S. Haldane</a> raised the topic at a symposium about life's origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haldane_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-haldane-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous chemical reactions are possible in an ammonia solution, and liquid ammonia has chemical similarities with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddammonia_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-ddammonia-44">[44]</a></sup> Ammonia can dissolve most organic molecules at least as well as water does and, in addition, it is capable of dissolving many elemental metals. Haldane made the point that various common water-related organic compounds have ammonia-related analogs; for instance the ammonia-related <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> group (-NH<sub>2</sub>) is analogous to the water-related <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> group (-OH).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddammonia_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-ddammonia-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ammonia, like water, can either accept or donate an H<sup>+</sup> ion. When ammonia accepts an H<sup>+</sup>, it forms the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> cation (NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>), analogous to <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> (H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>). When it donates an H<sup>+</sup> ion, it forms the <a href="/wiki/Metal_amides" title="Metal amides">amide</a> anion (NH<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>), analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> anion (OH<sup>−</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barossammonia_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-barossammonia-34">[34]</a></sup> Compared to water, however, ammonia is more inclined to accept an H<sup>+</sup> ion, and less inclined to donate one; it is a stronger <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barossammonia_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-barossammonia-34">[34]</a></sup> Ammonia added to water functions as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhenius_base" title="Arrhenius base">Arrhenius base</a>: it increases the concentration of the anion hydroxide. Conversely, using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction#Solvent_system_definition" title="Acid-base reaction">solvent system definition</a> of acidity and basicity, water added to liquid ammonia functions as an acid, because it increases the concentration of the cation ammonium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddammonia_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-ddammonia-44">[44]</a></sup> The carbonyl group (C=O), which is much used in terrestrial biochemistry, would not be stable in ammonia solution, but the analogous <a href="/wiki/Imine" title="Imine">imine</a> group (C=NH) could be used instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barossammonia_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-barossammonia-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, ammonia has some problems as a basis for life. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonds" title="Hydrogen bonds">hydrogen bonds</a> between ammonia molecules are weaker than those in water, causing ammonia's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a> to be half that of water, its <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> to be a third, and reducing its ability to concentrate non-polar molecules through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> effect. Gerald Feinberg and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Shapiro_(chemist)" title="Robert Shapiro (chemist)">Robert Shapiro</a> have questioned whether ammonia could hold prebiotic molecules together well enough to allow the emergence of a self-reproducing system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Ammonia is also flammable in oxygen, and could not exist sustainably in an environment suitable for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_metabolism" title="Aerobic metabolism">aerobic metabolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dsmammonia_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-dsmammonia-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> based on ammonia would likely exist at temperatures or air pressures that are extremely unusual in relation to life on Earth. Life on Earth usually exists within the melting point and <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> of water at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_pressure" title="Normal pressure">normal pressure</a>, between 0 °C (273 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) and 100 °C (373 K); at normal pressure ammonia's melting and boiling points are between −78 °C (195 K) and −33 °C (240 K). Chemical reactions generally proceed more slowly at a lower temperature. Therefore, ammonia-based life, if it exists, might metabolize more slowly and evolve more slowly than life on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dsmammonia_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-dsmammonia-46">[46]</a></sup> On the other hand, lower temperatures could also enable living systems to use chemical species that would be too unstable at Earth temperatures to be useful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ammonia could be a liquid at Earth-like temperatures, but at much higher pressures; for example, at 60 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>, ammonia melts at −77 °C (196 K) and boils at 98 °C (371 K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barossammonia_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-barossammonia-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ammonia and ammonia–water mixtures remain liquid at temperatures far below the freezing point of pure water, so such biochemistries might be well suited to planets and moons orbiting outside the water-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitability_zone" title="Habitability zone">habitability zone</a>. Such conditions could exist, for example, under the surface of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>'s largest moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_and_other_hydrocarbons">Methane and other hydrocarbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Methane and other hydrocarbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methane (CH<sub>4</sub>) is a simple hydrocarbon: that is, a compound of two of the most common elements in the cosmos, hydrogen and carbon. It has a cosmic abundance comparable with ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-42">[42]</a></sup> Hydrocarbons could act as a solvent over a wide range of temperatures, but would lack <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polarity</a>. Isaac Asimov, the <a href="/wiki/Biochemist" title="Biochemist">biochemist</a> and science fiction writer, suggested in 1981 that poly-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a> could form a substitute for proteins in a non-polar solvent such as methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_42-4"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-42">[42]</a></sup> Lakes composed of a mixture of hydrocarbons, including methane and <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, have been detected on the surface of Titan by the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens"><i>Cassini</i> spacecraft</a>.</p>
<p>There is debate about the effectiveness of methane and other hydrocarbons as a solvent for life compared to water or ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methanesolvent_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-methanesolvent-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polyesters_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polyesters-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Water is a stronger solvent than the hydrocarbons, enabling easier transport of substances in a cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methlife_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-methlife-51">[51]</a></sup> However, water is also more chemically reactive, and can break down large organic molecules through hydrolysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methanesolvent_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-methanesolvent-48">[48]</a></sup> A life-form whose solvent was a hydrocarbon would not face the threat of its biomolecules being destroyed in this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methanesolvent_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-methanesolvent-48">[48]</a></sup> Also, the water molecule's tendency to form strong hydrogen bonds can interfere with internal hydrogen bonding in complex organic molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosswater_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-barosswater-41">[41]</a></sup> Life with a hydrocarbon solvent could make more use of hydrogen bonds within its biomolecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methanesolvent_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-methanesolvent-48">[48]</a></sup> Moreover, the strength of hydrogen bonds within biomolecules would be appropriate to a low-temperature biochemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methanesolvent_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-methanesolvent-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Astrobiologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christopher_McKay_(planetary_scientist)" title="Christopher McKay (planetary scientist)">Chris McKay</a> has argued, on thermodynamic grounds, that if life does exist on Titan's surface, using hydrocarbons as a solvent, it is likely also to use the more complex hydrocarbons as an energy source by reacting them with hydrogen, <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> ethane and <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a> to methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mckay_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-mckay-52">[52]</a></sup> Possible evidence for this form of <a href="/wiki/Life_on_Titan" title="Life on Titan">life on Titan</a> was identified in 2010 by Darrell Strobel of <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>; a greater abundance of molecular hydrogen in the upper atmospheric layers of Titan compared to the lower layers, arguing for a downward diffusion at a rate of roughly 10<sup>25</sup> molecules per second and disappearance of hydrogen near Titan's surface. As Strobel noted, his findings were in line with the effects Chris McKay had predicted if <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanogenic" title="Methanogenic">methanogenic</a> life-forms were present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methlife_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-methlife-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mckay_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-mckay-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strobel_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-strobel-53">[53]</a></sup> The same year, another study showed low levels of acetylene on Titan's surface, which were interpreted by Chris McKay as consistent with the hypothesis of organisms reducing acetylene to methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methlife_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-methlife-51">[51]</a></sup> While restating the biological hypothesis, McKay cautioned that other explanations for the hydrogen and acetylene findings are to be considered more likely: the possibilities of yet unidentified physical or chemical processes (e.g. a non-living surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> enabling acetylene to react with hydrogen), or flaws in the current models of material flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-life.3F_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-life.3F-54">[54]</a></sup> He noted that even a non-biological catalyst effective at 95 K would in itself be a startling discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-life.3F_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-life.3F-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>A hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a> capable of functioning in liquid <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> in Titan conditions was computer-modeled in February 2015. Composed of small molecules that contain carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen, it would have the same stability and flexibility as cell membranes on Earth, which are composed of <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a>, compounds of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>. This hypothetical cell membrane was termed an "<a href="/wiki/Azotosome" title="Azotosome">azotosome</a>", a <a href="/wiki/Classical_compound" title="Classical compound">classical compound</a> made of "azote", French for nitrogen, and "<a href="/wiki/Soma_(biology)" title="Soma (biology)">soma</a>", Greek for body, by analogy with "<a href="/wiki/Liposome" title="Liposome">liposome</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azotosomemodel_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-azotosomemodel-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_fluoride">Hydrogen fluoride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hydrogen fluoride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">Hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF), like water, is a polar molecule, and due to its polarity it can dissolve many ionic compounds. Its melting point is −84 °C and its boiling point is 19.54 °C (at <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>); the difference between the two is a little more than 100 K. HF also makes hydrogen bonds with its neighbor molecules, as do water and ammonia. It has been considered as a possible solvent for life by scientists such as Peter Sneath<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sneath_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-sneath-56">[56]</a></sup> and Carl Sagan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>HF is dangerous to the systems of molecules that Earth-life is made of, but certain other organic compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin waxes</a>, are stable with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan.2C_Carl_2002_128-40">[40]</a></sup> Like water and ammonia, liquid hydrogen fluoride supports an acid-base chemistry. Using a solvent system definition of acidity and basicity, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> functions as a base when it is added to liquid HF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, hydrogen fluoride, unlike water, ammonia, and methane, is cosmically rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_sulfide">Hydrogen sulfide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hydrogen sulfide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">Hydrogen sulfide</a> is the closest <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chalcogenide" title="Hydrogen chalcogenide">chemical analog to water,</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> but is less polar and a weaker inorganic solvent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Hydrogen sulfide is quite plentiful on Jupiter's moon <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>, and may be in liquid form a short distance below the surface; and astrobiologist <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Schulze-Makuch" title="Dirk Schulze-Makuch">Dirk Schulze-Makuch</a> has suggested it as a possible solvent for life there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> On a planet with hydrogen-sulfide oceans the source of the hydrogen sulfide could come from volcanos, in which case it could be mixed in with a bit of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, which could help dissolve minerals. Hydrogen sulfide life might use a mixture of carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide as their carbon source. They might produce and live off of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide" title="Sulfur monoxide">sulfur monoxide</a>, which is analogous to oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>). Hydrogen sulfide, like hydrogen cyanide and ammonia, suffers from the small temperature range where it is liquid, though that, like that of hydrogen cyanide and ammonia, increases with increasing pressure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silicon_dioxide_and_silicates">Silicon dioxide and silicates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Silicon dioxide and silicates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">Silicon dioxide</a>, also known as glass, silica, or quartz, is very abundant in the universe and has a large temperature range where it is liquid. However, its melting point is 1,600 to 1,725 °C (2,912 to 3,137 °F), so it would be impossible to make organic compounds in that temperature, because all of them would decompose. Moreover, if the pressure increases, the melting point goes down.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Silicates are similar to silicon dioxide and some could have lower boiling points than silica. <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Feinberg" title="Gerald Feinberg">Gerald Feinberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Shapiro_(chemist)" title="Robert Shapiro (chemist)">Robert Shapiro</a> have suggested that molten silicate rock could serve as a liquid medium for organisms with a chemistry based on silicon, oxygen, and other elements such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_solvents_or_cosolvents">Other solvents or cosolvents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other solvents or cosolvents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Other solvents sometimes proposed:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">Supercritical fluids</a>: <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical carbon dioxide</a> and supercritical hydrogen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Supercritical_Carbon_Dioxide_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Supercritical_Carbon_Dioxide-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>Simple hydrogen compounds: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wardsolvents_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-wardsolvents-64">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>More complex compounds: <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosssulfuric_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-barosssulfuric-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Formamide" title="Formamide">formamide</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planetary_Systems_p_74_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Planetary_Systems_p_74-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wardsolvents_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-wardsolvents-64">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>Very-low-temperature fluids: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosscryo_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-barosscryo-37">[37]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">hydrogen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosscryo_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-barosscryo-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>High-temperature liquids: <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Sulfuric acid in liquid form is strongly polar. It remains liquid at higher temperatures than water, its liquid range being 10 °C to 337 °C at a pressure of 1 atm, although above 300 °C it will slowly decompose. Sulfuric acid is known to be abundant in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">clouds of Venus</a>, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> droplets. In a biochemistry that used sulfuric acid as a solvent, the <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkene</a> group (C=C), with two carbon atoms joined by a double bond, could function analogously to the carbonyl group (C=O) in water-based biochemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barosssulfuric_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-barosssulfuric-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>A proposal has been made that life on Mars may exist and be using a mixture of water and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> as its solvent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> A 61.2% (by weight) mix of water and hydrogen peroxide has a freezing point of −56.5 °C, and also tends to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super-cool" title="Super-cool">super-cool</a> rather than crystallize. It is also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a>, an advantage in a water-scarce environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supercritical carbon dioxide has been proposed as a candidate for alternative biochemistry due to its ability to selectively dissolve organic compounds and assist the functioning of enzymes and because "super-Earth"- or "super-Venus"-type planets with dense high-pressure atmospheres may be common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Supercritical_Carbon_Dioxide_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Supercritical_Carbon_Dioxide-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_speculations">Other speculations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other speculations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-green_photosynthesizers">Non-green photosynthesizers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Non-green photosynthesizers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Physicists have noted that, although photosynthesis on Earth generally involves green plants, a variety of other-colored plants could also support photosynthesis, essential for most life on Earth, and that other colors might be preferred in places that receive a different mix of stellar radiation than Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiang_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kiang-70">[70]</a></sup> These studies indicate that, although blue photosynthetic plants would be less likely, yellow or red plants are plausible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiang_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kiang-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variable_environments">Variable environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Variable environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Earth plants and animals undergo major biochemical changes during their life cycles as a response to changing environmental conditions, for example, by having a <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spore</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">hibernation</a> state that can be sustained for years or even millennia between more active life stages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christmas_in_Yellowstone_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christmas_in_Yellowstone-71">[71]</a></sup> Thus, it would be biochemically possible to sustain life in environments that are only periodically consistent with life as we know it.</p>
<p>For example, frogs in cold climates can survive for extended periods of time with most of their body water in a frozen state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christmas_in_Yellowstone_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christmas_in_Yellowstone-71">[71]</a></sup> whereas desert frogs in Australia can become inactive and dehydrate in dry periods, losing up to 75% of their fluids, yet return to life by rapidly rehydrating in wet periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Either type of frog would appear biochemically inactive (i.e. not living) during dormant periods to anyone lacking a sensitive means of detecting low levels of metabolism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonplanetary_life">Nonplanetary life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Nonplanetary life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_and_plasma-based">Dust and plasma-based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Dust and plasma-based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vadim_N._Tsytovich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vadim N. Tsytovich (page does not exist)">Vadim N. Tsytovich</a> and colleagues proposed that lifelike behaviors could be exhibited by dust particles suspended in a <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>, under conditions that might exist in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dust1_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-dust1-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dust2_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-dust2-74">[74]</a></sup> Computer models showed that, when the dust became charged, the particles could self-organize into microscopic helical structures, and the authors offer "a rough sketch of a possible model of the helical grain structure reproduction".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientists_who_have_published_on_this_topic">Scientists who have published on this topic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Scientists who have published on this topic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scientists who have considered possible alternatives to carbon-water biochemistry include:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a></b> (1920–1992), biochemist and science fiction writer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asimov_42-5"><a href="#cite_note-asimov-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Bains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Bains (page does not exist)">William Bains</a></b>, Cambridge biologist, a contributor to the journal <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology_(journal)" title="Astrobiology (journal)">Astrobiology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bains_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-bains-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Baross&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Baross (page does not exist)">John Baross</a></b>, oceanographer and astrobiologist, who chaired a committee of scientists under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">United States National Research Council</a> that published a report on life's limiting conditions in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baross_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-baross-76">[76]</a></sup> The report addresses the concern that a space agency might conduct a well-resourced search for life on other worlds "and then fail to recognize it if it is encountered".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Gerald_Feinberg" title="Gerald Feinberg">Gerald Feinberg</a></b> (1933–1992), physicist and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Shapiro_(chemist)" title="Robert Shapiro (chemist)">Robert Shapiro</a> (1935–2011), chemist, co-authors of the book <i>Life Beyond Earth</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Axel_Firsoff" title="Axel Firsoff">V.Axel Firsoff</a></b> (1910–1981), British astronomer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Robert_Freitas" title="Robert Freitas">Robert A. Freitas Jr.</a></b> (1952–present), specialist in nano-technology and nano-medicine; author of the book <i>Xenology</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/J._B._S._Haldane" title="J. B. S. Haldane">J. B. S. Haldane</a></b> (1892–1964), a geneticist noted for his work on <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haldane_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-haldane-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Pimentel" title="George Pimentel">George Pimentel</a></b> (1922–1989), American chemist, University of California, Berkeley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shklovskii_1977_229_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shklovskii_1977_229-83">[83]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a></b> (1934–1996), astronomer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shklovskii_1977_229_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shklovskii_1977_229-83">[83]</a></sup> science popularizer, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SETI" title="SETI">SETI</a> proponent.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Peter_Sneath" title="Peter Sneath">Peter Sneath</a></b> (1923–2011), microbiologist, author of the book <i>Planets and Life</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sneath_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-sneath-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_extraterrestrials_by_form" title="List of fictional extraterrestrials by form">List of fictional extraterrestrials by form</a></div>
<ul>
<li>Alternate chirality: In <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>'s short story "<a href="/wiki/Technical_Error" title="Technical Error">Technical Error</a>", there is an example of differing <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chirality</a>.</li>
<li>The concept of reversed chirality also figured prominently in the plot of <a href="/wiki/James_Blish" title="James Blish">James Blish</a>'s <i>Star Trek</i> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Spock_Must_Die!" title="Spock Must Die!">Spock Must Die!</a></i>, where a transporter experiment gone awry ends up creating a duplicate Spock who turns out to be a perfect mirror-image of the original all the way down to the atomic level.</li>
<li>The eponymous organism in <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain" title="The Andromeda Strain">The Andromeda Strain</a></i> is described as reproducing via the direct conversion of energy into matter.</li>
<li>Silicoids: <a href="/wiki/John_Drury_Clark" title="John Drury Clark">John Clark</a>, in the introduction to the 1952 shared-world anthology <i><a href="/wiki/The_Petrified_Planet" title="The Petrified Planet">The Petrified Planet</a></i>, outlined the biologies of the planet Uller, with a mixture of siloxane and silicone life, and of Niflheim, where metabolism is based on hydrofluoric acid and carbon tetrafluoride.</li>
</ul>

This was an enormous spacefaring <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal lattice</a> that had taken thousands of lives in its quest for energy. It was destroyed before communications could be established.</li>
<li>In the 1994 <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Firewalker_(The_X-Files)" title="Firewalker (The X-Files)">Firewalker</a>", Mulder and Scully investigate a death in a remote research base and discover that a new silicon-based fungus found in the area may be affecting and killing the researchers.</li>
