<p><i><b>Ziziphus jujuba</b></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>ζίζυφον</i>, <i>zízyphon</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>), commonly called <b>jujube</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-3">[3]</a></sup> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span></span>/</a></span></span>; sometimes jujuba), <b>red date</b>, <b>Chinese date</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>Korean date</b>, or <b>Indian date</b> is a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Ziziphus" title="Ziziphus">Ziziphus</a></i> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buckthorn" title="Buckthorn">buckthorn</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rhamnaceae" title="Rhamnaceae">Rhamnaceae</a>). It is used primarily as a shade tree that also bears fruit.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2022" data-file-width="1417" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png/220px-Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png/330px-Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png/440px-Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zizyphus_jujuba_Blanco1.59.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plate from the book <i>Flora de Filipinas</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a small <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> reaching a height of 5–12 metres (16–39 ft), usually with thorny branches. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are shiny-green, ovate-acute, 2–7 centimetres (0.79–2.76 in) wide and 1–3 centimetres (0.39–1.18 in) broad, with three conspicuous veins at the base, and a finely toothed margin. The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are small, 5 millimetres (0.20 in) wide, with five inconspicuous yellowish-green petals. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is an edible oval <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupe</a> 1.5–3 centimetres (0.59–1.18 in) deep; when immature it is smooth-green, with the consistency and taste of an <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, maturing brown to purplish-black and eventually wrinkled, looking like a small <a href="/wiki/Date_palm" title="Date palm">date</a>. There is a single hard seed similar to an <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a> pit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushforth_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its precise natural distribution is uncertain due to extensive cultivation, but is thought to be in southern Asia, between Lebanon, northern India, and southern and central China, and possibly also southeastern Europe though more likely introduced there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushforth_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>This plant has been introduced in Madagascar and grows as an invasive species in the western part of the island. This plant is known as the "hinap" or "finab" in the eastern part of Bulgaria where it grows wild but is also a garden shrub, kept for its fruit. The fruit is picked in the autumn. The trees grow wild in the eastern Caribbean, and are reported to exist in Jamaica and Trinidad as well. In Antigua and Barbuda, the fruit is called "dumps" or "dums". It is also known as "pomme surete" on the French islands of the Caribbean. This fruit, more precisely known as "Indian jujube" elsewhere, is different from the "jujube" fruit that is cultivated in various parts of southern California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species has a curious nomenclatural history, due to a combination of botanical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Botanical_Nomenclature" title="International Code of Botanical Nomenclature">naming regulations</a>, and variations in spelling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was first described scientifically by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> as <i>Rhamnus zizyphus</i>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i> in 1753. Later, in 1768, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Miller" title="Philip Miller">Philip Miller</a> concluded it was sufficiently distinct from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhamnus_(plant)" title="Rhamnus (plant)">Rhamnus</a></i> to merit separation into a new genus, in which he named it <i>Ziziphus jujube</i>, using Linnaeus' species name for the genus but with a probably accidental single letter spelling difference, "i" for "y". For the species name he used a different name, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tautonymy" title="Tautonymy">tautonyms</a> (repetition of exactly the same name in the genus and species) are not permitted in botanical naming. However, because of Miller's slightly different spelling, the combination correctly using the earliest species name (from Linnaeus) with the new genus, <i>Ziziphus zizyphus</i>, is <i>not</i> a tautonym, and was therefore permitted as a botanical name. This combination was made by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Karl_Wilhelm_Hermann_Karsten" title="Gustav Karl Wilhelm Hermann Karsten">Hermann Karsten</a> in 1882.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushforth_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2006, a proposal was made to suppress the name <i>Ziziphus zizyphus</i> in favor of <i>Ziziphus jujuba</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and this proposal was accepted in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Ziziphus jujuba</i> is thus the correct scientific name for this species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vernacular_names">Vernacular names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vernacular names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_and_religious_references">Cultural and religious references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural and religious references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Arabic-speaking regions the jujube and alternatively the <a href="/wiki/Ziziphus_lotus" title="Ziziphus lotus">Ziziphus lotus</a> are closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Sidrat_al-Muntaha" title="Sidrat al-Muntaha">lote-trees</a> ("sing. "sidrah", pl. "sidr") which are mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> while in <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> it is rather the <a href="/wiki/Ziziphus_spina-christi" title="Ziziphus spina-christi">Ziziphus spina-christi</a> that is called <i>sidr</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Jujube was domesticated in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">south Asia</a> by 9000 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gupta54_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-gupta54-14">[14]</a></sup> Over 400 <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> have been selected.</p>
<p>The tree tolerates a wide range of temperatures and rainfall, though it requires hot summers and sufficient water for acceptable fruiting. Unlike most of the other species in the genus, it tolerates fairly cold winters, surviving temperatures down to about −15 °C (5 °F). This enables the jujube to grow in mountain or desert habitats, provided there is access to underground water throughout the summer. The species <i>Ziziphus jujuba</i> grows in cooler regions of Asia. Five or more other species of <i>Ziziphus</i> are widely distributed in milder climates to hot deserts of Asia and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, jujube trees grow extensively in the western part of the island, from the north all the way to the south. It is widely eaten by free ranging <a href="/wiki/Zebu" title="Zebu">zebus</a>, and its seeds grow easily in zebu feces. It is an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, threatening mostly protected areas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">Culinary use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jujube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Culinary use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The freshly harvested, as well as the candied <a href="/wiki/Dried_fruit" title="Dried fruit">dried fruit</a>, are often eaten as a snack, or with coffee. Smoked jujubes are consumed in Vietnam and are referred to as black jujubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Both China and Korea produce a sweetened tea syrup containing jujube fruit in glass jars, and canned jujube tea or jujube tea in the form of teabags. To a lesser extent, jujube fruit is made into juice and jujube <a href="/wiki/Vinegar#Fruit" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a> (called <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9E%A3" title="wikt:枣">枣</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%86%8B" title="wikt:醋">醋</a> or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BA%A2%E6%9E%A3" title="wikt:红枣">红枣</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%86%8B" title="wikt:醋">醋</a> in Chinese). They are used for making pickles (কুলের আচার) in west Bengal and Bangladesh. In China, a wine made from jujube fruit is called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_zao_jiu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hong zao jiu (page does not exist)">hong zao jiu</a></i> (红枣酒).</p>
<p>Sometimes pieces of jujube fruit are preserved by storing them in a jar filled with <i><a href="/wiki/Baijiu" title="Baijiu">baijiu</a></i> (Chinese liquor), which allows them to be kept fresh for a long time, especially through the winter. Such jujubes are called <i>jiu zao</i> (酒枣; literally "alcohol jujube"). The fruit is also a significant ingredient in a wide variety of Chinese delicacies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2014)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>In Vietnam and Taiwan, fully mature, nearly ripe fruit is harvested and sold on the local markets and also exported to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asian</a> countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LIM_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-LIM-17">[17]</a></sup> The dried fruit is used in desserts in China and Vietnam, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ching_bo_leung" title="Ching bo leung">ching bo leung</a></i>, a cold beverage that includes the dried jujube, longan, fresh seaweed, barley, and lotus seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LIM_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-LIM-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Korea, jujubes are called <i>daechu</i> (대추) and are used in <i><a href="/wiki/Daechucha" title="Daechucha">daechucha</a></i> teas and <i><a href="/wiki/Samgyetang" title="Samgyetang">samgyetang</a></i>.</p>
<p>In Croatia, especially <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>, jujubes are used in marmalades, juices, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rakija" title="Rakija">rakija</a></i> (fruit brandy).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, and other Middle Eastern countries, the fruit is eaten as snacks or alongside a dessert after a meal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On his visit to <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>, the 19th-century English explorer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Burton" title="Sir Richard Burton">Sir Richard Burton</a>, observed that the local variety of <a href="/wiki/Sidrat_al-Muntaha" title="Sidrat al-Muntaha">jujube</a> fruit was widely eaten. He describes its taste as "like a bad plum, an unrepentant cherry and an insipid apple." He gives the local names for three varieties as "Hindi (Indian), Baladi (native), Tamri (date-like)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> a hundred years ago, a close variety was common in the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Valley_(Middle_East)" title="Jordan Valley (Middle East)">Jordan valley</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">bedouin</a> valued the fruit, calling it <i>nabk</i>. It could be dried and kept for winter or made into a paste which was used as bread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_cuisine" title="Persian cuisine">Persian cuisine</a>, the dried drupes are known as <i>annab</i>, while in neighboring <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, it is commonly eaten as a snack, and is known as <i>innab</i>. These names are related, and the Turks use a similarly related name, <i>hünna</i>. <i>Z. jujuba</i> grows in northern Pakistan and is known as <i>innab</i>, commonly used in the Tibb system of medicine.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Confusion in the common name apparently is widespread. The <i>innab</i> is <i>Z. jujuba</i>: the local name <i>ber</i> is not used for <i>innab</i>. Rather, <i>ber</i> is used for three other cultivated or wild species, e.g., <i>Z. spina-christi</i>, <i>Z. mauritiana</i>, and <i>Z. nummularia</i> in Pakistan and parts of India and is eaten both fresh and dried. Often, the dry fruit (<i>ber</i>) was used as a padding in leather horse saddles in parts of Baluchistan in Pakistan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Arabic name <i>sidr</i> is used for <i>Ziziphus</i> species other than <i>Z. jujuba</i>.</p>
<p>Traditionally in India, the fruit is dried in the sun and the hard nuts are removed. Then, it is pounded with <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_chillies" title="Red chillies">red chillies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jaggery" title="Jaggery">jaggery</a>. In some parts of the Indian state of Tamil Nadu, fresh whole ripe fruit is crushed with the above ingredients and dried under the sun to make cakes called <i>ilanthai vadai</i> or <i>regi vadiyalu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Madagascar, jujube fruit is eaten fresh or dried. People also use it to make jam. A jujube honey is produced in the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> of Morocco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LIM_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-LIM-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> has an alcoholic syrup called <i>brodo di giuggiole</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In Senegal Jujube is called Sii dem and the fruit is used as snack. The fruit is turned into dried paste used by school kids.</p>

<p>The fruit and its seeds are used in Chinese and Korean <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional medicine</a>, where they are believed to alleviate stress,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and traditionally for anti-fungal, anti-bacterial, anti-ulcer, anti-inflammatory purposes and sedation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> antispastic, antifertility/contraception, hypotensive and antinephritic, cardiotonic, antioxidant, immunostimulant, and wound healing properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>A controlled clinical trial found the fruit helpful for chronic constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In another clinical trial, jujube was proved to be effective against neonatal jaundice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Persian traditional medicine it is used in combination with other herbal medicines to treat colds, flu and coughing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Research suggests jujube fruit has nootropic and neuroprotective properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carson2012_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carson2012-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ziziphin" title="Ziziphin">Ziziphin</a>, a compound in the leaves of the jujube, suppresses the ability to perceive sweet taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crfsn-32-231_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-crfsn-32-231-32">[32]</a></sup> The fruit, being <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilaginous</a>, is very soothing to the throat and decoctions of jujube have often been used in pharmacy to treat sore throats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The jujube's sweet smell is believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2014)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to make teenagers fall in love, and as a result, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karakoram" title="Karakoram">Karakoram</a> regions, boys take a stem of sweet-smelling jujube flowers with them or put it on their hats to attract girls.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the traditional Chinese wedding ceremony, the jujube was often placed in the newlyweds' bedroom as a good luck charm for fertility, along with <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longan" title="Longan">longan</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chestnuts" title="Chestnuts">chestnuts</a>, punning on an invocation to "have an honored child soon".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Bhutan, the leaves are used as a <a href="/wiki/Potpourri" title="Potpourri">potpourri</a> to help keep homes smelling fresh and clean. It is also used to keep bugs and other insects out of the house and free of infestation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Japan, the <i>natsume</i> has given its name to a style of <a href="/wiki/Chaki#Usucha-ki_.2F_natsume" title="Chaki">tea caddy</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tea_ceremony" title="Japanese tea ceremony">Japanese tea ceremony</a>, due to the similar shape,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and also to <a href="/wiki/Nightlight" title="Nightlight">nightlights</a> (ナツメ球), again due to the similarity between the shape of the bulb and the fruit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Korea, the wood is used to make the body of the <i><a href="/wiki/Taepyeongso" title="Taepyeongso">taepyeongso</a></i>, a double-reed wind instrument. The wood is also used to make <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">Go</a> bowls, beads, and violin parts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Madagascar, jujube trees are a good wood for charcoal, the second main source of cooking energy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
