<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Presidential library</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <b>presidential library</b> system is a nationwide network of 13 libraries administered by the Office of Presidential Libraries, which is part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives and Records Administration</a> (NARA). These are repositories for preserving and making available the papers, records, collections and other historical materials of every <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> since <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> (31st President, 1929-1933). In addition to the library services, museum exhibitions concerning the presidency are displayed.</p>
<p>Although recognized as having historical significance, before the middle 20th century, presidential papers and effects were generally understood to be the private property of the president. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a>, the 32nd president, proposed to leave his papers to the public in a building donated by him on his Hyde Park estate. Since then a series of laws have established the presidential library system. While not officially sanctioned and maintained by the NARA, libraries have been organized for several presidents who preceded the official start of the Presidential Library Office (i.e., those before President Hoover).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:508px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UnitedStatesPresidentialLibraries.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="313" data-file-width="506" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/UnitedStatesPresidentialLibraries.png" width="506"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UnitedStatesPresidentialLibraries.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map of all of the official (those administered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NARA" title="NARA">NARA</a>) U.S. presidential libraries by U.S. state. States denoted in yellow have 1 official (administered by NARA) U.S. presidential library, California (green) has 2, and Texas (blue) has 3.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Since the 32nd presidency of Herbert Hoover (in office, 1929-1933), presidential libraries have been established in each president's home state in which documents, artifacts, gifts of state and museum exhibits are maintained that relate to the former president's life and career both political and professional. Each library also provides an active series of public programs. When a president leaves office, the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) establishes a presidential materials project to house and index the documents until a new presidential library is built and transferred to the federal government. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_J._Clinton_Presidential_Center_and_Park" title="William J. Clinton Presidential Center and Park">William J. Clinton Presidential Library</a> became the eleventh presidential library on November 18, 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="Richard Nixon Presidential Library and Museum">Richard Nixon Presidential Library and Museum</a> became the twelfth on July 11, 2007, and the <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_Presidential_Center" title="George W. Bush Presidential Center">George W. Bush Presidential Center</a> became the thirteenth on May 1, 2013. Official fundraising for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="Barack Obama Presidential Library and Museum">Barack Obama Presidential Library and Museum</a> began in January 2014; the 14th facility in the presidential library system is then expected to be open around 2020.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_libraries_outside_of_NARA">Presidential libraries outside of NARA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Presidential libraries outside of NARA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The presidential library system is made up of thirteen presidential libraries operated by the NARA. Libraries and museums have been established for earlier presidents, but they are not part of the NARA presidential library system, and are operated by private foundations, historical societies, or state governments, including the <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rutherford_Hayes" title="Rutherford Hayes">Rutherford Hayes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> libraries. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="Abraham Lincoln Presidential Library and Museum">Abraham Lincoln Presidential Library and Museum</a> is owned and operated by the state of <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon_Library_and_Birthplace" title="Richard Nixon Library and Birthplace">Richard Nixon Library and Birthplace</a> was not originally part of the presidential library system. While the Nixon Presidential Materials Staff, which administers the Nixon presidential materials under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Recordings_and_Materials_Preservation_Act" title="Presidential Recordings and Materials Preservation Act">Presidential Recordings and Materials Preservation Act</a>, is part of NARA, a private foundation operated the Richard Nixon Library &amp; Birthplace. In January 2004, Congress passed legislation that provided for the establishment of a federally operated Richard Nixon Presidential Library in <a href="/wiki/Yorba_Linda,_California" title="Yorba Linda, California">Yorba Linda</a>. In March 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Archivist_of_the_United_States" title="Archivist of the United States">Archivist of the United States</a> and John Taylor, the director of the Richard Nixon Library &amp; Birthplace Foundation, exchanged letters on the requirements to allow the Nixon Library to become the twelfth federally funded Presidential library operated by the NARA by 2007. On October 16, 2006, Dr. Timothy Naftali began his tenure as the first federal director of the Richard Nixon Library and Birthplace, and in the winter of 2006 NARA began to transfer the 30,000 presidential gifts from the Nixon Presidential Materials Staff in College Park, Maryland to the facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NixonLib_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NixonLib-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NixonDir_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NixonDir-2">[2]</a></sup> On July 11, 2007, the Nixon Foundation deeded the Library and Birthplace to the government of the United States. On the same day, the newly renamed federal Richard Nixon Presidential Library and Museum officially opened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Open_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Open-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2012, on the fiftieth anniversary of the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">Ulysses S. Grant</a> Foundation, it selected <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_State_University#Mitchell_Memorial_Library" title="Mississippi State University">Mississippi State University</a> as the permanent location for Ulysses S. Grant's presidential library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSU-4">[4]</a></sup> Historian John Simon edited Grant's letters into a 32-volume scholarly edition published by Southern Illinois University Press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-overview-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 30, 2013, both chambers of the <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota_Legislative_Assembly" title="North Dakota Legislative Assembly">North Dakota Legislative Assembly</a> passed a bill appropriating $12 million to <a href="/wiki/Dickinson_State_University" title="Dickinson State University">Dickinson State University</a> to award a grant to the Theodore Roosevelt Center for construction of a building to be named the <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> Presidential Library. To access these funds, the Theodore Roosevelt Center must first raise $3 million from non-state sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRcenter_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRcenter-6">[6]</a></sup> Dickinson State University is also home to the <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt_Digital_Library" title="Theodore Roosevelt Digital Library">Theodore Roosevelt Digital Library</a> which has formed partnerships with the Library of Congress and Harvard University, among other institutions. They currently have over 25,000 items online.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, all presidential papers were considered the personal property of the president. Some took them at the end of their terms, others destroyed them, and many papers were scattered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunther1950_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunther1950-7">[7]</a></sup> Though many pre-Hoover collections now reside in the Manuscript Division of the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-LOC-8">[8]</a></sup> others are split among other libraries, historical societies, and private collections. However, many materials have been lost or deliberately destroyed.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucretia_Rudolph_Garfield" title="Lucretia Rudolph Garfield">Lucretia Rudolph Garfield</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/James_A._Garfield" title="James A. Garfield">James A. Garfield</a> (president from March 4, 1881, until his death on September 19, 1881) added a Memorial Library wing to their family home in <a href="/wiki/Mentor,_Ohio" title="Mentor, Ohio">Mentor, Ohio</a>, four years after his assassination. The <a href="/wiki/James_A._Garfield_National_Historic_Site" title="James A. Garfield National Historic Site">James A. Garfield National Historic Site</a> is operated by the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Reserve_Historical_Society" title="Western Reserve Historical Society">Western Reserve Historical Society</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Archives">National Archives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National Archives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1939, President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a> donated his personal and presidential papers to the federal government. At the same time, Roosevelt pledged part of his estate at <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_New_York" title="Hyde Park, New York">Hyde Park, New York</a> to the United States, and friends of the President formed a non-profit corporation to raise funds for the construction of the library and museum building. Roosevelt's decision stemmed from his belief that Presidential papers were an important part of the national heritage and should be accessible to the public. He asked the National Archives to take custody of his papers and other historical materials and to administer his library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunther1950_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gunther1950-7">[7]</a></sup> On June 30, 2013, new interactive and multimedia exhibits developed by the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) opened to the public as part of the first renovation of this library since its opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistAssoc_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistAssoc-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1950, <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> decided that he, too, would build a library to house his Presidential papers and helped to galvanize congressional action.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_Libraries_Act_of_1955">Presidential Libraries Act of 1955</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Presidential Libraries Act of 1955">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1955, Congress passed the <a class="external text" href="http://www.archives.gov/presidential-libraries/laws/1955-act.html" rel="nofollow">Presidential Libraries Act of 1955</a>, establishing a system of privately erected and federally maintained libraries. The Act encouraged other Presidents to donate their historical materials to the government and ensured the preservation of Presidential papers and their availability to the American people. Under this and subsequent acts, nine more libraries have been established. In each case, funds from private and non-federal public sources provided the funds to build the library. Once completed, the private organization turned over the libraries to the National Archives and Records Administration to operate and maintain.</p>
<p>Until 1978, Presidents, scholars, and legal professionals held the view dating back to <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> that the records created by the President or his staff while in office remained the personal property of the President and were his to take with him when he left office. The first Presidential libraries were built on this concept. NARA successfully persuaded Presidents to donate their historical materials to the federal government for housing in a Presidential library managed by NARA.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_Records_Act_of_1978">Presidential Records Act of 1978</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Presidential Records Act of 1978">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidential_Records_Act_of_1978" title="Presidential Records Act of 1978">Presidential Records Act of 1978</a> established that the Presidential records that document the constitutional, statutory, and ceremonial duties of the President are the property of the United States Government. After the President leaves office, the <a href="/wiki/Archivist_of_the_United_States" title="Archivist of the United States">Archivist of the United States</a> assumes custody of the records. The Act allowed for the continuation of Presidential libraries as the repository for Presidential records.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_Libraries_Act_of_1986">Presidential Libraries Act of 1986</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Presidential Libraries Act of 1986">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.archives.gov/presidential-libraries/laws/1986-act.html" rel="nofollow">Presidential Libraries Act of 1986</a> made additional changes to Presidential libraries, requiring private endowments linked to the size of the facility. NARA uses these endowments to offset a portion of the maintenance costs for the library.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Holdings">Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The thirteen presidential libraries maintain over 400 million pages of textual materials; nearly ten million photographs; over 15 million feet (5,000 km) of motion picture film; nearly 100,000 hours of disc, audiotape, and videotape recordings; and approximately half a million museum objects. These varied holdings make each library a valuable source of information and a center for research on the Presidency.</p>
<p>The most important textual materials in each library are those created by the President and his staff in the course of performing the official duties. Libraries also house numerous objects including family heirlooms, items collected by the President and his family, campaign memorabilia, awards, and the many gifts given to the President by American citizens and foreign dignitaries. These gifts range in type from homemade items to valuable works of art. Curators in Presidential libraries and in other museums throughout the country draw upon these collections for historical exhibits.</p>
<p>Other significant holdings include the personal papers and historical materials donated by individuals associated with the President. These individuals may include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Cabinet" title="United States Cabinet">Cabinet officials</a>, envoys to foreign governments, political party associates, and the President's family and personal friends. Several libraries have undertaken oral history programs that have produced tape-recorded memoirs. A third body of materials comprises the papers accumulated by the President prior to, and following, his Presidency. Such collections include documents relating to Roosevelt's tenure as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>'s long military career.</p>
<p>With the exception of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, and upon their own deaths, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, every American president since Hoover is or has chosen to be buried at his presidential library. Kennedy is buried at <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>; Johnson is buried at <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_National_Historical_Park" title="Lyndon B. Johnson National Historical Park">his ranch</a> in the hill country of Texas, west of Austin; Carter plans to be buried near his home in <a href="/wiki/Plains,_Georgia" title="Plains, Georgia">Plains, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carter_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carter-10">[10]</a></sup> George W. Bush has a burial plot in the <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Cemetery" title="Texas State Cemetery">Texas State Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TexasCem_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-TexasCem-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike all other Presidents whose libraries are part of the NARA system, Ford's <a href="/wiki/Gerald_R._Ford_Presidential_Library" title="Gerald R. Ford Presidential Library">library</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerald_R._Ford_Presidential_Museum" title="Gerald R. Ford Presidential Museum">museum</a> are geographically separate buildings, located in different parts of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>; Ford is buried at his museum in <a href="/wiki/Grand_Rapids,_Michigan" title="Grand Rapids, Michigan">Grand Rapids</a>, while the library is in <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations_of_other_presidents.27_papers">Locations of other presidents' papers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Presidential_library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Locations of other presidents' papers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> left their papers to Princeton University where they may be found at the <a href="/wiki/Seeley_G._Mudd_Manuscript_Library" title="Seeley G. Mudd Manuscript Library">Seeley G. Mudd Manuscript Library</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt_Association" title="Theodore Roosevelt Association">Theodore Roosevelt Association</a> failed to get enough funds to start what would be the second presidential library after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rutherford_Hayes" title="Rutherford Hayes">Rutherford Hayes</a>', they donated his papers to Harvard University in 1943. These reside at its <a class="external text" href="http://www.theodorerooseveltcenter.org/Research/Collections/Harvard.aspx" rel="nofollow">Houghton Library</a>. <a href="/wiki/James_Buchanan" title="James Buchanan">James Buchanan</a> left his papers to the <a href="/wiki/Historical_Society_of_Pennsylvania" title="Historical Society of Pennsylvania">Historical Society of Pennsylvania</a> in Philadelphia, where they may still be found. In the 1960s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Tennessee_at_Knoxville" title="University of Tennessee at Knoxville">University of Tennessee at Knoxville</a> endeavored to collect all of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>'s papers on microfilm and the results are now available <a class="external text" href="http://thepapersofandrewjackson.utk.edu" rel="nofollow">online</a>. A similar project is underway on behalf of <a href="/wiki/The_Papers_of_James_Madison" title="The Papers of James Madison">James Madison</a> by the Universities of Virginia and Chicago. For many presidents, especially before the development of the NARA system, substantial collections may be found in multiple private and public collections.</p>
