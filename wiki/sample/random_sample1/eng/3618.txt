<p><b>Daxia</b>, <b>Ta-Hsia</b>, or <b>Ta-Hia</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">大夏</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Dàxià</span></i>) was apparently the name given in antiquity by the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> to <b>Tukhara</b> or <b>Tokhara</b>: the main part of <b><a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a></b>, in what is now northern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, and parts of southern <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>.</p>
<p>The name "Daxia" first appears in Chinese accounts from the 3rd century BCE, to designate a kingdom in the far west – possibly a consequence of the first contacts with the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a> – and then is used by the explorer <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> in 126 BCE to designate Bactria.</p>
<p>It is possible that "Daxia", in part, conflated or confused Tokhara with the country of the <a href="/wiki/Dahae" title="Dahae">Dahae</a> (on the south-eastern shores of the Caspian Sea), who were usually known in classical Chinese sources as the <i>Dayi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">大益</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Dayi</span></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daxia is mentioned by, for instance, Chapter VIII (Xiao Kuang) of the <i>Guanzi</i> (7th Century BCE): "In the west [Duke Huan]... having passed through the valleys of the Taihang and Bier, took captive the chief of the Da Xia. Further to the west, he subjugated the Xi Yu of Liusha, and for the first time the Rong People of Qin were obedient." (Taihang and Bier are located along the Shanxi-Hebei border in China.)</p>
<p>The reports of Zhang Qian are preserved in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i> ("Records of the Great Historian") by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a> in the 1st century BCE.</p>
<p>They describe an important urban civilization of about one million people, living in walled cities under small city kings or <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a>. Daxia was an affluent country with rich markets, trading in an incredible variety of objects, coming as far as Southern China. By the time Zhang Qian visited Daxia, there were no longer a major king, and the Bactrian were suzerains to the nomadic <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a>, who were settled to the north of their territory beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a>. Overall, Zhang Qian depicted a rather sophisticated but demoralized people who were afraid of war.</p>
<p>Following these reports, the Chinese emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han_China" title="Emperor Wu of Han China">Wudi</a> was informed of the level of sophistication of the urban civilizations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>, Bactria and <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>, and became interested in developing commercial relationships with them:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Thus the emperor learned of <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a>, Daxia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anxi_(Parthia)" title="Anxi (Parthia)">Anxi</a>, and the others, all states rich in unusual products whose people cultivated the land and made their living in much the same way as the Chinese. All these states, he was told, were militarily weak and prized Han goods and wealth. (<i>Shiji</i> 123)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>These contacts immediately led to the dispatch of multiple embassies from the Chinese, initiating the development of the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>.</p>
