<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Academic Festival Overture</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><b>Academic Festival Overture</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i><b>Akademische Festouvertüre</b></i></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Op. 80, by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Johannes Brahms</a>, was one of a pair of contrasting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_overture" title="Concert overture">concert overtures</a> — the other being the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragic_Overture" title="Tragic Overture">Tragic Overture</a></i>, Op. 81. Brahms composed the work during the summer of 1880 as a musical "thank you" to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Breslau" title="University of Breslau">University of Breslau</a>, which had notified him that it would award him an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary doctorate</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Festival_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initially, Brahms had contented himself with sending a simple handwritten note of acknowledgment to the University, since he loathed the public fanfare of celebrity. However, the <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Scholz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bernard Scholz (page does not exist)">Bernard Scholz</a>, who had nominated him for the degree, convinced him that protocol required him to make a grander gesture of gratitude. The University expected nothing less than a musical offering from the composer. "Compose a fine symphony for us!" he wrote to Brahms. "But well orchestrated, old boy, not too uniformly thick!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_instrumentation">Structure and instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Festival_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brahms, who was known to be a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/curmudgeonly" title="wikt:curmudgeonly">curmudgeonly</a> joker, filled his quota by creating a "very boisterous <a href="/wiki/Fantasia_(music)" title="Fantasia (music)">potpourri</a> of <a href="/wiki/Commercium_song" title="Commercium song">student drinking songs</a> à la <a href="/wiki/Franz_von_Supp%C3%A9" title="Franz von Suppé">Suppé</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in an intricately designed structure made to appear loose and episodic, thus drawing on the "academic" for both his sources and their treatment.</p>
<p>The work sparkles with some of the finest virtues of Brahms's orchestral technique, sometimes applied for comic effect, such as the bassoons that inflate the light subject of "Fuchslied" (<i>Was kommt dort von der Höh?</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The inventive treatment includes tunes appropriated from the student ditties "Fuchslied", "Wir hatten gebauet ein stattliches Haus", "Hört, ich sing das Lied der Lieder", and most memorably, the broad, triumphant finale on "<a href="/wiki/Gaudeamus_igitur" title="Gaudeamus igitur">Gaudeamus igitur</a>", which succinctly engages Brahms's sophisticated mastery of <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">counterpoint</a>, further fulfilling the "Academic" aspect of his program, cheekily applied to the well-worn melody. Brahms manages to evoke ravishing euphoria without sacrificing his commitment to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_music_era" title="Classical music era">classical</a> balance.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Orchestration" title="Orchestration">blend of orchestral colors</a> is carefully planned and highlighted in the piece, which, in spite of Scholz's request, calls for one of the largest ensembles for any of his compositions: <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flutes</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a> (both doubling on B-flat and C clarinets), two <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contrabassoon" title="Contrabassoon">contrabassoon</a>, four <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">horns</a> (two in C and two in E), three C <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombones</a>, one <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cymbal" title="Cymbal">cymbals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle_(musical_instrument)" title="Triangle (musical instrument)">triangle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">strings</a>.</p>
<p>The Overture consists of four continuous sections:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Allegro</i> (C minor)</li>
<li><i>Maestoso</i> (C major)</li>
<li><i>Animato</i> (G major)</li>
<li><i>Maestoso</i> (C major).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Convocation_for_the_premiere_and_awarding_of_the_degree">Convocation for the premiere and awarding of the degree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Festival_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Convocation for the premiere and awarding of the degree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The composer himself <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conducted</a> the premiere of the overture, and received his honorary degree, at a special <a href="/wiki/Convocation" title="Convocation">convocation</a> held by the University on January 4, 1881. To the chagrin (or mischievous delight) of many of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">academics</a> in the audience, there was an "ironic" contrast between the mood of the student drinking songs and the seriousness of a ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Festival_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its easily grasped structure, its lyrical warmth, as well as its excitement and humor, the work has remained a staple of today's concert-hall repertoire. A typical performance lasts around ten minutes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References_in_Popular_Culture">References in Popular Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Festival_Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References in Popular Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cary_Grant" title="Cary Grant">Cary Grant</a> in his role as Dr. Noah Praetorius conducts the Overture at the beginning and end of the 1951 <a href="/wiki/Joseph_L._Mankiewicz" title="Joseph L. Mankiewicz">Joseph L. Mankiewicz</a>/<a href="/wiki/Darryl_F._Zanuck" title="Darryl F. Zanuck">Darryl F. Zanuck</a> film, <i><a href="/wiki/People_Will_Talk" title="People Will Talk">People Will Talk</a></i>, and part of the piece can also be heard in the opening theme of the 1978 film, <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_House" title="Animal House">National Lampoon's Animal House</a></i>. In the 1967 film "<a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Bedazzled" title="Bedazzled">Bedazzled</a>", <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Moore" title="Dudley Moore">Dudley Moore</a>, as a pretentious pseudo-intellectual Welsh student, and <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Bron" title="Eleanor Bron">Eleanor Bron</a>, lie on the floor listening to the beginning of the Overture on an LP until the record begins to skip (thanks to a scratch deliberately made in the LP by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cook" title="Peter Cook">Peter Cook</a> as the Devil earlier in the film).</p>
