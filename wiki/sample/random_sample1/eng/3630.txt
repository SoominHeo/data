<p>The <b>First Republic of South Korea</b> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">제1공화국</span>, <i>Jeil Gonghwaguk</i>, literally "the first republic") was <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>'s first independent government, ruling the country from 1948 to 1960. It succeeded <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Military_Government_in_Korea" title="United States Army Military Government in Korea">USAMGIK</a>, the United States military government, which ruled the area from 1945 to 1948. The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> recognized South Korea on 15 August 1948. The First Republic was established on August 15, 1948, with <a href="/wiki/Syngman_Rhee" title="Syngman Rhee">Syngman Rhee</a> as the first president. Like subsequent governments, it claimed sovereignty over the entire <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean Peninsula</a>, although it only had power over the area south of the <a href="/wiki/38th_parallel_north" title="38th parallel north">38th parallel</a>. The investiture of the Rhee government followed the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_Constitutional_Assembly_election,_1948" title="South Korean Constitutional Assembly election, 1948">general election</a> of May 10, 1948. The country's first constitution had been promulgated by the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_South_Korea" title="National Assembly of South Korea">National Assembly</a> on July 17. It established a system with a strong president, who was elected indirectly by the National Assembly.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rhee was supported in the elections by the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Democratic_Party" title="Korea Democratic Party">Korea Democratic Party</a>, but didn't include any of its members in his cabinet. In retaliation, the members of the party formed a united opposition Democratic Nationalist Party, and began to advocate a cabinet system which would remove power from the president. This led to a regrouping of the Rhee faction into the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nationalist_Party_(South_Korea)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nationalist Party (South Korea) (page does not exist)">Nationalist Party</a>, which later became the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(South_Korea)" title="Liberal Party (South Korea)">Liberal Party</a>, and remained Rhee's base throughout his administration. The country's second parliamentary elections were held on May 30, 1950, and gave the majority of seats to independents.</p>
<p>The South Korean government continued many of the practices of the U.S. military government. This included the brutal repression of leftist activity. The Rhee government continued the harsh military action against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeju_Uprising" title="Jeju Uprising">Jeju Uprising</a>. It also crushed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeosu%E2%80%93Suncheon_Rebellion" title="Yeosu–Suncheon Rebellion">military uprisings in Suncheon and Yeosu</a>, which were provoked by orders to sail to Jeju and participate in the crackdown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prewar">Prewar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prewar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This government also oversaw several massacres, the most notable being the <a href="/wiki/Bodo_League_massacre" title="Bodo League massacre">Bodo League massacre</a> where between 100,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and 1,140,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> were executed on suspicion of supporting communism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_War">Korean War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Korean War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a></div>
<p>On June 25, 1950, North Korean forces invaded South Korea, starting the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. Led by the United States, a 16-member coalition undertook the first collective action under the umbrella of the U.N. Command (UNC). Oscillating battle lines inflicted a high number of civilian casualties and wrought immense destruction. With the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a>'s entry on behalf of North Korea in 1951, the fighting came to a stalemate close to the original line of demarcation.</p>
<p>Armistice negotiations, initiated in July 1951, finally concluded on July 27, 1953 at <a href="/wiki/Panmunjom" title="Panmunjom">Panmunjom</a>, now in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Korean Demilitarized Zone">Demilitarized Zone</a> (DMZ). The resulting Armistice Agreement was signed by the North Korean army, Chinese People's Volunteers and the U.S.-led and South Korean-supported <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> Command. A peace treaty has not been signed up to now. Following the armistice, the South Korean government returned to Seoul on the symbolic date of August 15, 1953.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_events">Postwar events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Postwar events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the armistice, South Korea experienced political turmoil under years of autocratic leadership of <a href="/wiki/Syngman_Rhee" title="Syngman Rhee">Syngman Rhee</a>, which was ended by student revolt in 1960. Throughout his rule, Rhee sought to take additional steps to cement his control of government. These began in 1952 (shortly after being elected to a second term), when the government was still based in <a href="/wiki/Busan" title="Busan">Busan</a> due to the ongoing war. In May of that year, Rhee pushed through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Constitution of the Republic of Korea">constitutional amendments</a> which made the presidency a directly-elected position. In order to do this, he declared martial law and jailed the members of parliament whom he expected to vote against it. Rhee was subsequently elected by a wide margin. He regained control of parliament in the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_legislative_election,_1954" title="South Korean legislative election, 1954">1954 elections</a>, and thereupon pushed through an amendment to exempt himself from the eight-year <a href="/wiki/Term_limit" title="Term limit">term limit</a>.</p>
<p>Rhee's prospects for reelection during the presidential campaign of 1956 initially seemed dim. Public disillusionment regarding his attempt to seek a third term was growing, and the main opposition candidate <a href="/wiki/Shin_Ik-hee" title="Shin Ik-hee">Shin Ik-hee</a> drew immense crowds during his campaign. Shin's sudden death while on the campaign trail, however, allowed Rhee to win the presidency with ease. The runner-up of that election, <a href="/wiki/Cho_Bong-am" title="Cho Bong-am">Cho Bong-am</a> of the Progressive Party, was later charged with espionage and executed in 1959.</p>
<p>The events of 1960, known as the <a href="/wiki/April_Revolution" title="April Revolution">April Revolution</a>, were touched off by the violent repression of a student demonstration in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masan_(Korea)" title="Masan (Korea)">Masan</a> on the day of the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_presidential_election,_March_1960" title="South Korean presidential election, March 1960">presidential election</a>, March 15. Initially these protests were quelled by local police, but they broke out again after the body of a student was found floating in the harbor. Subsequently nonviolent protests spread to Seoul and throughout the country, and Rhee resigned on April 26.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This period saw explosive growth in education at all levels, even during the turmoil of the Korean War. The First Republic saw the full implementation of an educational system that had been sketched out by the Council for Korean Education under USAMGIK. This education was shaped by the ideal of <i>Hongik Ingan</i>, the person who is a benefit to all, and sought to prepare students for participation in a democratic society. Some contend that this democratic education contributed to the student protests which brought down the authoritarian Rhee government in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first Education Law came into force on December 31, 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The most important aspect of this was the introduction of universal <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_education" title="Compulsory education">compulsory education</a> at the primary level. This requirement led to widespread school construction; by the end of the First Republic, primary-school enrollment had topped 95%. In addition, the dual ladder system used by the Japanese occupation government was replaced by a single-ladder system, with 6 years of primary education, 3 of middle-school education, 3 of high-school education, and 4 of college education.</p>
<p>This period also saw the adoption of South Korea's first national curriculum.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The economy of this period was deeply troubled.</p>
<p>During 1945–1950, United States and South Korean authorities carried out a land reform that retained the institution of private property. They confiscated and redistributed all land held by the Japanese colonial government, Japanese companies, and individual Japanese colonists. The South Korean government carried out a reform whereby South Koreans with large landholdings were obliged to divest most of their land. A new class of independent, family proprietors was created.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_relations">International relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Republic_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: International relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rhee sought to align his government strongly with the United States, and against both North Korea and Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The policy of the First Republic on North Korea, before and after the Korean War, was one of "unification by force."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Although some talks towards normalization of relations with Japan took place, they achieved little.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the government took in vast sums of American aid, in amounts sometimes near the total size of the national budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 18, 1952, Rhee declared South Korean sovereignty over the waters around the Korean Peninsula, in a concept similar to that of today's <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zones</a>. The maritime demarcation thus drawn up, which Rhee called the "<a href="/wiki/Syngman_Rhee_Line" title="Syngman Rhee Line">Peace Line</a>", included <a href="/wiki/Liancourt_Rocks" title="Liancourt Rocks">Liancourt Rocks</a> as South Korean territory.</p>
