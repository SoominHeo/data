<p><b>Religion in South Korea</b> is characterized by the fact that an absolute majority of South Koreans (54.4% as of 2015) have no formal membership in a religious organisation; among those that are members of a religious organisation, there is a dominance of Protestantism, Buddhism, and Roman Catholicism. According to national census conducted in 2015, 19.7% of the population belongs to Protestantism, 15.5% to <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> (Korean Buddhism), and 7.9% to the Roman Catholic Church; in total <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> is the religion of 27.6% of the Korean population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreastats_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-koreastats-1">[1]</a></sup> Buddhism was influential in ancient times and Christianity persuaded large segments of the population already in the 18th and 19th century, yet they have grown rapidly in membership only by the mid-20th century, as part of the profound transformations that the South Korean society has gone through in the past century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> and then have shown some downhill from the 2000s onwards. Native shamanic religions (i.e. <i><a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism" title="Korean shamanism">Sindo</a></i>) remain popular and could represent a large part of the unaffiliated. Indeed, according to a 2012 survey, only 15% of the population declared to be not religious in the sense of "<a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Korea entered the 20th century with an already ingrained presence of Christianity and a vast majority of the population practicing native religion (Sindo). The latter never developed into the high status of a national religious culture comparable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_religion" title="Chinese religion">China's system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Japan's Shinto</a>; this weakness of Korean Sindo was among the reasons that left freehand to an early and thorough rooting of Christian churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogata.2C_Mamoru_Billy_1984_p._32_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ogata.2C_Mamoru_Billy_1984_p._32-4">[4]</a></sup> The population also took part in Confucianising rites and held private <a href="/wiki/Jesa" title="Jesa">ancestor worship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Organised religions and philosophies were a matter of the ruling elites, and the long patronage exerted by the Chinese empire led these elites to embrace a particularly strict Confucianism (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Korean_Confucianism" title="Korean Confucianism">Korean Confucianism</a>). <a href="/wiki/Korean_Buddhism" title="Korean Buddhism">Korean Buddhism</a>, despite an erstwhile rich tradition, at the dawn of the 20th century was virtually extinct as a religious institution, after 500 years of suppression under the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._15_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._15-5">[5]</a></sup> Christianity had antecedents in the Korean peninsula back in the 18th century, when the philosophical school of <i><a href="/wiki/Seohak" title="Seohak">Seohak</a></i> supported the religion. With the fall of the Joseon in the last decades of the 19th century, the Koreans largely embraced Christianity, since the monarchy itself and the intellectuals looked to Western models to modernise the country and endorsed the work of Catholic and Protestant missionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese colonisation</a> of the first half of the 20th century, the identification of Christianity with <a href="/wiki/Korean_nationalism" title="Korean nationalism">Korean nationalism</a> was furtherly strengthened,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._158-161_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._158-161-7">[7]</a></sup> as the Japanese tried to merge native Sindo with their <a href="/wiki/Shinto_in_Korea" title="Shinto in Korea">State Shinto</a>.</p>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Korea" title="Division of Korea">division of Korea</a> into two states after 1945, the communist north and the anti-communist south, the majority of the Korean Christian population that had been until then in the northern half of the peninsula,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158.2C_p._162_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158.2C_p._162-8">[8]</a></sup> fled to South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._163_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._163-9">[9]</a></sup> It has been estimated that Christians who emigrated to the south were more than one million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Throughout the second half of the 20th century, the South Korean state enacted <a href="/wiki/Misin_tapa_undong" title="Misin tapa undong">measures to furtherly marginalise indigenous Sindo</a> strengthening Christianity and a revival of Buddhism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> According to scholars, South Korean censuses do not count believers in indigenous Sindo and underestimate the number of adherents of <a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism#Branching" title="Korean shamanism">Sindo sects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For instance, statistics compiled by the ARDA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> estimate that as of 2010, 14.7% of South Koreans practice ethnic religion, 14.2% adhere to new movements, and 10.9% practice Confucianism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Korea">Ancient Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_North_Korea#Ancient_Korea" title="Religion in North Korea">Religion in North Korea § Ancient Korea</a></div>
<p>Prior to the introduction of Buddhism, all Koreans believed in their indigenous religion socially guided by <i>mu</i> (shamans). Buddhism was introduced from the Chinese Former Qin state in 372 to the northern Korean state of Goguryeo and developed into distinctive Korean forms. At that time, the peninsula was divided into <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">three kingdoms</a>: the aforementioned Goguryeo in the north, <a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a> in the southwest, and <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> in the southeast. Buddhism reached Silla only in the 5th century, but it was made the state religion only in that kingdom in the year 552.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korea.2C_300_to_600_CE_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Korea.2C_300_to_600_CE-21">[21]</a></sup> Buddhism became much more popular in Silla and even in Baekje (both areas comprehended in modern South Korea), while in Goguryeo the Korean indigenous religion remained dominant. In the following unified state of Goryeo (918–1392) Buddhism flourished, and even became a political force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Joseon kingdom (1392–1910), adopted an especially strict version of <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Korean_Confucianism" title="Korean Confucianism">Korean Confucianism</a>) and suppressed and marginalised Korean Buddhism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._120-138_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._120-138-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tudor.2C_2012_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tudor.2C_2012-24">[24]</a></sup> and Korean shamanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._15_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._15-5">[5]</a></sup> Buddhist monasteries were destroyed and their number dropped from several hundreds to a mere thirty-six; Buddhism was eradicated from the life of towns as monks and nuns were prohibited from entering them and were marginalised to the mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tudor.2C_2012_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tudor.2C_2012-24">[24]</a></sup> These restrictions lasted until the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 19th century, the Joseon state was politically and culturally collapsing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._155_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._155-26">[26]</a></sup> Interested parties were looking for solutions to invigorate and transform the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._155_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._155-26">[26]</a></sup> It was in this critical period that they came into contact with Western Protestant missionaries who offered a proven way to use religious beliefs and their system of fears for shaping a citizenship that is obedient to religious and other leaderships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._155_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._155-26">[26]</a></sup> Christian communities already existed in Joseon since the 17th century, however it was only by the 1880s that the government allowed a large number of Western missionaries to enter the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Protestant missionaries set up schools, hospitals and publishing agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._157-158_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._157-158-28">[28]</a></sup> The royal family supported Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">absorption of Korea into the Japanese Empire</a> (1910–1945) the already formed link of Christianity with <a href="/wiki/Korean_nationalism" title="Korean nationalism">Korean nationalism</a> was strengthened,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._158-161_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._158-161-7">[7]</a></sup> as the Japanese tried to impose <a href="/wiki/State_Shinto" title="State Shinto">State Shinto</a>, coopting within it native Korean Sindo, and Christians refused to take part in Shinto rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._158-161_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._158-161-7">[7]</a></sup> At the same time, numerous religious movements that since the 19th century had been trying to reform the Korean indigenous religion, notably <a href="/wiki/Cheondoism" title="Cheondoism">Cheondoism</a>, flourished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carl_Young_2013._pp._51-66_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carl_Young_2013._pp._51-66-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1945_onwards.E2.80.94South_Korea">1945 onwards—South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1945 onwards—South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="310" data-file-width="448" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg/150px-Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg/225px-Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg/300px-Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mudang_performing_a_ritual_placating_the_angry_spirits_of_the_dead.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <i>mudang</i> holding a <i>gut</i> to placate the angry spirits of the dead.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Korea" title="Division of Korea">division of Korea</a> into two states in 1945, the <a href="/wiki/History_of_North_Korea" title="History of North Korea">communist north</a> and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_South_Korea" title="History of South Korea">anti-communist south</a>, the majority of the Korean Christian population that had been until then in the northern half of the peninsula,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158.2C_p._162_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158.2C_p._162-8">[8]</a></sup> fled to South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._163_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._163-9">[9]</a></sup> Christians who resettled in the south were more than one million. Cheondoists, who were concentrated in the north like Christians, remained there after the partition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carl_Young_2013._pp._51-66_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carl_Young_2013._pp._51-66-30">[30]</a></sup> and South Korea now has no more than few thousands Cheondoists.</p>
<p>The so-called "<a href="/wiki/Misin_tapa_undong" title="Misin tapa undong">movement to defeat the worship of gods</a>" promoted by governments of South Korea in the 1970s and 1980s, prohibited indigenous cults and wiped out nearly all traditional shrines (<i>sadang</i> 사당) of the Confucian kinship religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall.2C_2010._p._10_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall.2C_2010._p._10-31">[31]</a></sup> This was particularly tough under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Park_Chung-hee" title="Park Chung-hee">Park Chung-hee</a> (a Buddhist).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._17_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._17-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>This measures, combined with the rapid social changes of the same period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> favoured a rapid revival of Korean Buddhism and growth of Christian churches in a trend to register as members of organised religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The number of Buddhist temples rose from 2.306 in 1962 to 11.561 in 1997, Protestant churches rose from 6.785 in 1962 to 58.046 in 1997, the Catholic Church had 313 churches in 1965 and 1.366 in 2005, <a href="/wiki/Won_Buddhism" title="Won Buddhism">Won Buddhism</a> had 131 temples in 1969 and 418 in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Daesun_Jinrihoe" title="Daesun Jinrihoe">Daesun Jinrihoe</a>'s number of temples has grown from 700 in 1983 to 1.600 in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Statistics from censuses show that the proportion of the South Korean population self-identifying as Buddhist has grown from 2.6% in 1962 to 22.8% in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> while the proportion of Christians has grown from 5% in 1962 to 29.2% in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Pyong_Gap_Min.2C_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> However, both religions have shown a downhill between the years 2005 and 2015, with Buddhism sharply declining in influence to 15.5% of the population, and a less significant decline of Christianity to 27.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2015_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2015-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_attacks_on_Buddhism_and_other_traditional_religions">Protestant attacks on Buddhism and other traditional religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protestant attacks on Buddhism and other traditional religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the 1980s and the 1990s there have been events of hostility of Protestants against Buddhists and followers of traditional religions in South Korea. This include the arson of temples, the beheading of statues of Buddha and bodhisattvas, and red Christian crosses painted on either statues or other Buddhist and other religions' properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buswell.2C_Lee._2007._p._375_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buswell.2C_Lee._2007._p._375-37">[37]</a></sup> Some of these acts have even been promoted by churches' <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buswell.2C_Lee._2007._p._375_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buswell.2C_Lee._2007._p._375-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Korea" title="Christianity in Korea">Christianity in Korea</a></div>
<p>Christianity (기독교 <i>Gidoggyo</i>) in South Korea is predominantly constituted by communities pertaining to <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> (개신교 <i>Gaesingyo</i>, "renewed teaching") and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholicism</a> (천주교 <i>Cheonjugyo</i>, "religion of the Lord of Heaven", or 가톨릭교 <i>Gatolliggyo</i>), respectively with 9.6 million and 3.8 million members as of the 2015 census. There are also small communities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Christians</a> (정교회 <i>Jeonggyohoe</i>), founded by Russian Orthodox missionary endeavors in the 19th century, and <a href="/wiki/Mormonism" title="Mormonism">Mormons</a> (모르몬교 <i>Molmongyo</i>).</p>
<p>Foreign Roman Catholic missionaries did not arrive in Korea until 1794, a decade after the return of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yi_Sung-hun" title="Yi Sung-hun">Yi Sung-hun</a>, a diplomat who was the first baptised Korean in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> He established a grassroots lay Catholic movement in Korea. However, the writings of the Jesuit missionary <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Ricci" title="Matteo Ricci">Matteo Ricci</a>, who was resident at the imperial court in Beijing, had been already brought to Korea from China in the 17th century. Scholars of the <i><a href="/wiki/Silhak" title="Silhak">Silhak</a></i> ("Practical Learning"), were attracted to Catholic doctrines, and this was a key factor for the spread of the Catholic faith in the 1790s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The penetration of Western ideas and Christianity in Korea became known as <i><a href="/wiki/Seohak" title="Seohak">Seohak</a></i> ("Western Learning"). A study of 1801 found that more than half of the families that had converted to Catholicism were linked to the Seohak school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Largely because converts refused to perform Confucian ancestral rituals, the Joseon government prohibited the proselytisation of Christianity. Some Catholics were executed during the early 19th century, but the restrictive law was not strictly enforced.</p>
<p>Protestant missionaries entered Korea during the 1880s and, along with Catholic priests, converted a remarkable number of Koreans this time with the support of the royal government which winked at Westernising forces in a period of deep internal crisis (due to the wane of the centuries-long patronage from a then-weakened China).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158-29">[29]</a></sup> The lack of a national religious system compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_religion" title="Chinese religion">those of China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">that of Japan</a> (Korean <a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism" title="Korean shamanism">Sindo</a> never developed to a high status of institutional and civic religion) gave freehand to Christian churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogata.2C_Mamoru_Billy_1984_p._32_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ogata.2C_Mamoru_Billy_1984_p._32-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterian</a> missionaries were especially successful. They established schools, universities, hospitals, and orphanages and played a significant role in the modernisation of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._157-158_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._157-158-28">[28]</a></sup> During the Japanese colonial occupation, Christians were in the front ranks of the struggle for independence. Factors contributing to the growth of Protestantism included the decayed state of Korean Buddhism, the support of the intellectual elite, and the encouragement of self-support and self-government among members of the Korean church, and finally the identification of Christianity with Korean nationalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>A large number of Christians lived in the northern part of the peninsula (it was part of the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Manchurian_revival" title="Manchurian revival">Manchurian revival</a>")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158-29">[29]</a></sup> where Confucian influence was not as strong as in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158.2C_p._162_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._158.2C_p._162-8">[8]</a></sup> Before 1948 <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a> was an important Christian center: one-sixth of its population of about 300,000 people were converts. Following the establishment of the communist regime in the north, an estimated more than one million Korean Christians resettled to South Korea to escape persecution by North Korea's anti-Christian politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._p._163_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._p._163-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christianity grew significantly in the 1970s and 1980s. In the 1990s it continued to grow, but at a slower rate, and since the 2000s it has showed some decline. Christianity is especially dominant in the west of the country including <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incheon" title="Incheon">Incheon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gyeonggi_Province" title="Gyeonggi Province">Gyeonggi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honam" title="Honam">Honam</a> regions. The Christian faith in South Korea is dominated by four denominations: Presbyterianism (장로교 pronounced <i>Jangnogyo</i>), Methodism (감리교 pronounced <i>Gamnigyo</i>), <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptists</a> (침례교 pronounced <i>Chimnyegyo</i>) and Catholicism. The <a href="/wiki/Yoido_Full_Gospel_Church" title="Yoido Full Gospel Church">Yoido Full Gospel Church</a> is the largest <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostal</a> church in the country. Some <a href="/wiki/Non-denominational" title="Non-denominational">non-denominational</a> churches also exist. Korean Catholics still observe <i><a href="/wiki/Jesa" title="Jesa">jesa</a></i> (ancestral rites); the Korean tradition is very different from the institutional religious ancestral worship that is found in <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration_in_China" title="Ancestor veneration in China">in China</a> and Japan, and can be easily integrated as ancillary to Christianity. Protestants, by contrast, have completely abandoned the practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Peace_churches" title="Peace churches">peace churches</a> have not gained a strong foothold on the peninsula. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quaker</a> thought briefly attracted a national following in the late 20th century, thanks to the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Ham_Seok-heon" title="Ham Seok-heon">Ham Seok-heon</a>. However, after Ham's death, interest in Quakerism declined. The state of <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a> is similar.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mormonism">Mormonism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mormonism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints_in_South_Korea" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in South Korea">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in South Korea</a> was established following the baptism of <a href="/wiki/Kim_Ho_Jik" title="Kim Ho Jik">Kim Ho Jik</a> in 1951. Studying nutrition in Cornell, he met a member of their faith, Oliver Wyman, and was struck with their doctrine. Upon joining the church and completing his degree, Kim traveled back to South Korea, started translations for materials, and helped the church gain official recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2012 there were 81,628 Mormons and one temple in Seoul,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> four missions (Seoul, Daejeon, Busan, and Seoul South),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> 128 congregations, and twenty-four family history centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<p>Buddhism (불교 <i>Bulgyo</i>) entered Korea from China during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">period of the three kingdoms</a> (372, or the 4th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korea.2C_300_to_600_CE_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Korea.2C_300_to_600_CE-21">[21]</a></sup> Buddhism was the dominant religious and cultural influence in the <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> (668-935) and subsequent Goryeo (918-1392) states. Confucianism was also brought to Korea from China in early centuries, and was formulated as <a href="/wiki/Korean_Confucianism" title="Korean Confucianism">Korean Confucianism</a> in Goryeo. However, it was only in the subsequent Joseon kingdom (1392–1910) that Korean Confucianism was established as the state ideology and religion, and Korean Buddhism underwent 500 years of suppression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._120-138_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayson.2C_2002._pp._120-138-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tudor.2C_2012_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tudor.2C_2012-24">[24]</a></sup> Buddhism in the contemporary state of South Korea is stronger in the east of the country, namely the <a href="/wiki/Yeongnam" title="Yeongnam">Yeongnam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangwon_Province_(South_Korea)" title="Gangwon Province (South Korea)">Gangwon</a> regions, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Jeju_Province" title="Jeju Province">Jeju</a>.</p>
<p>There are a number of different schools in Korean Buddhism (대한불교 <i>Daehanbulgyo</i>), including the <i><a href="/wiki/Korean_Seon" title="Korean Seon">Seon</a></i> (Korean Zen). The overwhelming majority of Buddhist temples in contemporary South Korea belong to the <a href="/wiki/Jogye_Order" title="Jogye Order">Jogye Order</a>, traditionally related to the Seon school. The order's headquarters are at <a href="/wiki/Jogyesa" title="Jogyesa">Jogyesa</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, and it operates most of the country's old and famous temples, such as <a href="/wiki/Bulguksa" title="Bulguksa">Bulguksa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beomeosa" title="Beomeosa">Beomeosa</a>.</p>
<p>Buddhism in South Korea is dominated by the Jogye Order, a syncretic sect traditionally linked to the <a href="/wiki/Zen#Seon_in_Korea" title="Zen">Seon</a> tradition. Most of the country's old and famous temples, such as <a href="/wiki/Bulguksa" title="Bulguksa">Bulguksa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beomeosa" title="Beomeosa">Beomeosa</a>, are operated by the Jogye Order, which is headquartered at <a href="/wiki/Jogyesa" title="Jogyesa">Jogyesa</a> in central Seoul. Other Buddhist traditions in South Korea include the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taego" title="Taego">Taego</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Cheontae" title="Cheontae">Cheontae</a>" lineages. Taego is a form of Seon (Zen), while the Choentae is a modern revival of the <a href="/wiki/Tiantai" title="Tiantai">Tiantai</a> lineage in Korea, focusing on the <i><a href="/wiki/Lotus_Sutra" title="Lotus Sutra">Lotus Sutra</a></i>. Another lineage, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jingak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jingak (page does not exist)">Jingak</a>, is a form of <a href="/wiki/Vajrayana" title="Vajrayana">Vajrayana</a> Buddhism. Both the Jogye and Cheontae orders require their monastics to be celibate, while the Taego and Jingak orders allow for married priests.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Won_Buddhism">Won Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Won Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Won Buddhism (원불교 <i>Wonbulgyo</i>) is a modernly reformed Buddhism that seeks to make enlightenment possible for everyone and applicable to regular life. The scriptures and practices are simplified so that anyone, regardless of their wealth, occupation, or other external living conditions, can understand them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_shamanism.E2.80.94Sindo">Korean shamanism—Sindo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Korean shamanism—Sindo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Korean shamanism, also known as "Muism" (무교 <i>Mugyo</i>, "<i>mu</i> [shaman] religion")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and "Sindo" (신도) or "Sinism" (신교 <i>Singyo</i> "Way of the Gods").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> is the native religion of the Koreans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL4_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL4-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[note 1]</a></sup> Although used synonymously, the two terms aren't identical:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL4_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-JYL4-50">[50]</a></sup> Jung Young Lee describes Muism as a form of Sindo - the shamanic tradition within the religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL5_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL5-52">[51]</a></sup> Particularly akin to Japan's <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a>, contrarywise to it ad to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_religion" title="Chinese religion">China's religious systems</a>, Korean Sindo never developed into a national religious culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogata.2C_Mamoru_Billy_1984_p._32_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ogata.2C_Mamoru_Billy_1984_p._32-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contemporary Korean language the shaman-priest or <i><a href="/wiki/Mu_(shaman)" title="Mu (shaman)">mu</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a>: <span lang="ko"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B7%AB" title="wiktionary:巫">巫</a></span> ) is known as a <i>mudang</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EB%AC%B4%EB%8B%B9" title="wiktionary:무당">무당</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a>: <span lang="ko"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B7%AB%E5%A0%82" title="wiktionary:巫堂">巫堂</a></span> ) if female or <i>baksu</i> if male, although other names and locutions are used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL4_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-JYL4-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[note 2]</a></sup> Korean <i>mu</i> "shaman" is synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Wu_(shaman)" title="Wu (shaman)">wu</a></i>, which defines priests both male and female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL5_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-JYL5-52">[51]</a></sup> The role of the <i>mudang</i> is to act as intermediary between the <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">spirits</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a>, and the human plain, through <i><a href="/wiki/Gut_(ritual)" title="Gut (ritual)">gut</a></i> (rituals), seeking to resolve problems in the patterns of development of human life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Central is interaction with <i>Haneullim</i> or <i>Hwanin</i>, meaning "source of all being",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL18_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL18-56">[54]</a></sup> and of all gods of nature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL5_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-JYL5-52">[51]</a></sup> the utmost god or the supreme mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL17_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL17-57">[55]</a></sup> The <i>mu</i> are mythically described as descendants of the "Heavenly King", son of the "Holy Mother [of the Heavenly King]", with investiture often passed down through female princely lineage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[56]</a></sup> However, other myths link the heritage of the traditional faith to Dangun, male son of the Heavenly King and initiator of the Korean nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL13_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL13-59">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides Japanese <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a>, Korean religion has also similarities with Chinese <a href="/wiki/Chinese_shamanism" title="Chinese shamanism">Wuism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL21_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL21-60">[58]</a></sup> and is akin to the Siberian, Mongolian, and Manchurian religious traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL21_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-JYL21-60">[58]</a></sup> Some studies trace the Korean ancestral god Dangun to the Ural-Altaic Tengri "Heaven", the shaman and the prince.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL_17-18_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-JYL_17-18-62">[60]</a></sup> In the dialects of some provinces of Korea the shaman is called <i>dangul dangul-ari</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JYL18_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-JYL18-56">[54]</a></sup> The <i>mudang</i> is similar to the Japanese <i>miko</i> and the Ryukyuan <i>yuta</i>. Muism has exerted an influence on some Korean new religions, such as <a href="/wiki/Cheondoism" title="Cheondoism">Cheondoism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeungsanism" title="Jeungsanism">Jeungsanism</a>. According to various sociological studies, Korea's type of Christianity owes much of its success to native shamanism, which provided a congenial mindset and models for the religion to take root.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1890s, the last decades of the Joseon kingdom, Protestant missionaries gained significant influence, and led a demonisation of native religion through the press, and even carried out campaigns of physical suppression of local cults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall.2C_2010._pp._4-7_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall.2C_2010._pp._4-7-64">[62]</a></sup> The Protestant discourse would have had an influence on all further attempts to uproot native religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall.2C_2010._pp._4-7_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall.2C_2010._pp._4-7-64">[62]</a></sup> The "movement to destroy Sindo" carried out in South Korea in the 1970s and 1980s, destroyed much of the physical heritage of Korean religion (temples and shrines),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall.2C_2010._p._10_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall.2C_2010._p._10-31">[31]</a></sup> especially during the regime of President <a href="/wiki/Park_Chung-hee" title="Park Chung-hee">Park Chung-hee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._17_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joon-sik_Choi.2C_2006._p._17-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[64]</a></sup> There has been of a revival of shamanism in South Korea in most recent times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cheondoism">Cheondoism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cheondoism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cheondoism (천도교 <i>Cheondogyo</i>) is a fundamentally Confucian religious tradition derived from indigenous Sinism. It is the religious dimension of the <i><a href="/wiki/Donghak" title="Donghak">Donghak</a></i> ("Eastern Learning") movement that was founded by <a href="/wiki/Choe_Je-u" title="Choe Je-u">Choe Je-u</a> (1824-1864), a member of an impoverished <a href="/wiki/Yangban" title="Yangban">yangban</a> (aristocratic) family,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[67]</a></sup> in 1860 as a counter-force to the rise of "foreign religions",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee.2C_1996._p._105_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee.2C_1996._p._105-70">[68]</a></sup> which in his view included Buddhism and Christianity (part of <i><a href="/wiki/Seohak" title="Seohak">Seohak</a></i>, the wave of Western influence that penetrated Korean life at the end of the 19th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee.2C_1996._p._105_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee.2C_1996._p._105-70">[68]</a></sup> Choe Je-u founded Cheondoism after having been allegedly healed from illness by an experience of <i>Sangje</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Haneullim" title="Haneullim">Haneullim</a></i>, the god of the universal Heaven in traditional shamanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee.2C_1996._p._105_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lee.2C_1996._p._105-70">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Donghak movement became so influential among common people that in 1864 the Joseon government sentenced Choe Je-u to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee.2C_1996._p._105_70-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lee.2C_1996._p._105-70">[68]</a></sup> The movement grew and in 1894 the members gave rise to the Donghak Peasant Revolution against the royal government. With the division of Korea in 1945, most of the Cheondoist community remained in the north, where the majority of them dwelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carl_Young_2013._pp._51-66_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carl_Young_2013._pp._51-66-30">[30]</a></sup> Only few thousands of them remain in South Korea today.</p>
<p>The social and historical significance of the Donghak movement and Cheondoism has been largely ignored in South Korea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee.2C_1996._p._110_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee.2C_1996._p._110-71">[69]</a></sup> contrarywise to <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> where Cheondoism is viewed positively as a folk (<i><a href="/wiki/Minjung" title="Minjung">minjung</a></i>) movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee.2C_1996._p._110_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee.2C_1996._p._110-71">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sects">Other sects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other sects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apart from Cheondoism, other sects based on indigenous religion were founded between the end of the 19th century and the early decades of the 20th century. They include <a href="/wiki/Daejongism" title="Daejongism">Daejongism</a> (대종교 <i>Daejonggyo</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup> which has as its central creed the worship of Dangun, legendary founder of <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a>, thought of as the first proto-Korean kingdom; and two splinter sects of Cheondoism: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suwunism" title="Suwunism">Suwunism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bocheonism" title="Bocheonism">Bocheonism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Jeungsanism (증산교 <i>Jeungsangyo</i>) defines a family of religions founded in the early 20th century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup> that emphasise magical practices and <a href="/wiki/Millenarianism" title="Millenarianism">millenarian</a> teachings. Part of it are three or possibly more "Jeungsan religions": <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeungsando" title="Jeungsando">Jeungsando</a> (증산도) that is the most popular and also proselytises overseas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup> Taegeukdo (태극도) and <a href="/wiki/Daesun_Jinrihoe" title="Daesun Jinrihoe">Daesun Jinrihoe</a> (대순진리회). They show Taoist-derived themes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are also a number of small religious sects, which have sprung up around <a href="/wiki/Gyeryongsan" title="Gyeryongsan">Gyeryongsan</a> ("Rooster-Dragon Mountain", always one of Korea's most-sacred areas) in <a href="/wiki/South_Chungcheong_Province" title="South Chungcheong Province">South Chungcheong Province</a>, the supposed future site of the founding of a new dynasty originally prophesied in the 18th century (or before). Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenriism" title="Tenriism">Tenriism</a> (천리교 <i>Cheonligyo</i>) also claims to have thousands of South Korean members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to an article, "Characteristics of Religious Life in South Korea: A Sociological Survey," there was a rise of new religious movements in the late 1900s which has been claimed to be about 10 percent of all churches within South Korea. It has been speculated that this had occurred due to the foreign invasion of countries for the land of South Korea in addition to conflicting views within the country in regards to social and political issues. Within the new religious movements, many of them are syncretic which is combination of beliefs of multiple religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup></p>

<p>Only few of contemporary South Koreans identify as adherents of Confucianism (유교 <i>Yugyo</i>). Korean intellectuals historically developed a distinct <a href="/wiki/Korean_Confucianism" title="Korean Confucianism">Korean Confucianism</a>. However, with the end of the Joseon state and the wane of Chinese influence in the 19th and 20th century, Confucianism was abandoned. The influence of Confucian ethical thought remains strong in other religious practices, and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_culture" title="Korean culture">Korean culture</a> in general. Confucian rituals are still practiced at various times of the year. The most prominent of these are the annual rites held at the Shrine of Confucius in Seoul. Other rites, for instance those in honor of clan founders, are held at shrines found throughout the country.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/History_of_Sino-Korean_relations" title="History of Sino-Korean relations">Diplomatic ties between the Chinese empire and the Korean kingdom</a> meant that Chinese officers and diplomats tried to exert influence by exporting <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folk_religion" title="Chinese folk religion">Chinese religious models and divine cults</a>, building temples to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_gods_and_immortals" title="Chinese gods and immortals">Chinese gods</a>. Chinese religious facilities were used almost exclusively by Chinese officials and their families and the Korean population remained indifferent towards such practices. The <i><a href="/wiki/Dongmyo" title="Dongmyo">Dongmyo</a></i> (Temple of <a href="/wiki/Guan_Yu" title="Guan Yu">Guan Yu</a>) in the capital Seoul is one of the few remaining examples of state Chinese religion in Korea.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_Shinto">Japanese Shinto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Japanese Shinto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shinto_in_Korea" title="Shinto in Korea">Shinto in Korea</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1038" data-file-width="1346" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg/150px-Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg/225px-Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg/300px-Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Keijyo_shrine_11.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Keijyo Shinto Shrine, prior to 1935, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During Japan's colonisation of Korea (1910-1945), given the common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altaic_peoples" title="Altaic peoples">Altaic</a> origins of the two peoples, Koreans were considered to be outright part of the Japanese population, to be wholly assimilated. The Japanese studied and coopted native <i><a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism" title="Korean shamanism">Sindo</a></i> by overlapping it with their <a href="/wiki/State_Shinto" title="State Shinto">State Shinto</a> (similar measures of assimilation were applied to Buddhism), which hinged upon the worship of Japanese high gods and the emperor's godhead. Hundreds of Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shinto_shrines" title="Shinto shrines">Shinto shrines</a> were built throughout the peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>This policy led to massive conversion of Koreans to Christian churches, which were already well ingrained in the country, representing a concern for the Japanese program, and supported Koreans' independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[76]</a></sup> After the downfall of Japanese power in the peninsula, the Shinto infrastructure was completely dismantled.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mostly_Asian_religions">Other mostly Asian religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other mostly Asian religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Korea" title="Islam in Korea">Islam in Korea</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg/150px-Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg/225px-Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg/300px-Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seoul_central_mosque_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Minaret" title="Minaret">Minarets</a> of the Seoul Central Mosque.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Islam (이슬람교 <i>Iseullamgyo</i>) in South Korea is represented by a community of roughly 40,000 <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a>, mainly composed by people who converted during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and their descendants and not including migrant workers from South and Southeast Asia. The largest mosque is the <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Central_Mosque" title="Seoul Central Mosque">Seoul Central Mosque</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Itaewon" title="Itaewon">Itaewon</a> district of Seoul; smaller mosques can be found in most of the country's major cities.</p>
<p>In addition to native Korean Muslims, there are around a hundred thousand foreign workers from Muslim countries, particularly Indonesians, Malaysians, and Pakistanis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Korea" title="Hinduism in Korea">Hinduism in Korea</a></div>
<p>Hinduism (힌두교 <i>Hindugyo</i>) is practiced among South Korea's small <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepali</a> immigrant community. However, Hindu traditions such as <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">yoga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">Vedanta</a> have attracted interest among younger South Koreans. There are two Hindu temples in the Seoul region, the <a href="/wiki/Sri_Radha_Shyamasundar_Mandir" title="Sri Radha Shyamasundar Mandir">Sri Radha Shyamasundar Mandir</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sri_Sri_Radha_Krishna_temple" title="Sri Sri Radha Krishna temple">Sri Sri Radha Krishna temple</a>, located on Seoul's outskirts.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_South_Korea" title="History of the Jews in South Korea">History of the Jews in South Korea</a></div>
<p>The Jewish existence in South Korea effectively began with the dawn of the Korean War in 1950. At this time a large number of Jewish soldiers, including the chaplain <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Potok" title="Chaim Potok">Chaim Potok</a>, came to the Korean peninsula. Today the Jewish community is very small and limited to the <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Capital_Area" title="Seoul Capital Area">Seoul Capital Area</a>. There have been very few Korean converts to Judaism (유태교 <i>Yutaegyo</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unification_Church">Unification Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Unification Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unification_Church" title="Unification Church">Unification Church</a></div>
<p>The Unification Church or Unificationism (통일교 <i>Tongilgyo</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[78]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/New_religious_movement" title="New religious movement">new religious movement</a> founded in South Korea in 1954 by <a href="/wiki/Sun_Myung_Moon" title="Sun Myung Moon">Sun Myung Moon</a>. Since its founding, the church has expanded throughout the world with most members living in East Asia.</p>
<p>Unificationist beliefs are derived from the Christian Bible and are explained in the church's textbook, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine_Principle" title="Divine Principle">Divine Principle</a></i>. It teaches that God is the creator and Heavenly Father, whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duotheism" title="Duotheism">dual nature</a> combines both masculinity and femininity and whose center is true love.</p>
<p>The Unification Church has sponsored many organizations and projects over the years; including businesses, news media, projects in education and the arts, and political and social activism. The church was led by Moon until his death on September 3, 2012. At the time of Moon's death, it was reported that his widow <a href="/wiki/Hak_Ja_Han" title="Hak Ja Han">Hak Ja Han</a> would assume the leadership of the church. Their philosophy for establishing the Kingdom of Heaven is family-centered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Korean Unification Church members started a political party in South Korea. It was named "The Party for God, Peace, Unification, and Home". In an inauguration declaration, the new party said it would focus on preparing for the reunification of the South and North Korea by educating the public about God and peace. A church official said that similar political parties would be started in Japan and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup></p>
