<p>The <b>State Council of the Republic of Korea</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">국무회의</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani" xml:lang="ko-Hani"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9C%8B%E5%8B%99" title="wikt:國務">國務</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9C%83%E8%AD%B0" title="wikt:會議">會議</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Gungmuhoeui</span></i>) is the chief <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> body and national <b>cabinet</b> of the <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Republic of Korea</a> involved in discussing "important policies that fall within the power of the Executive" as specified by the Constitution. The most influential part of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_South_Korea" title="Government of South Korea">South Korean government</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_(government_department)" title="Ministry (government department)">ministries</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Members">Members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Council_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2013, executive branch of South Korea consists of 18 ministries, two agencies and five boards. State Council includes its 18 ministers, the prime minister and the president. Any ministers must be appointed into the State Council before he or she can be confirmed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_South_Korea" title="National Assembly of South Korea">National Assembly</a>. There must be no more than thirty and no less than fifteen council members excluding the President and the Prime Minister. The President is the Chairperson of the State Council, and the Prime Minister is the Vice-Chairperson.</p>
<p>Although not the official members of the State Council, the Presidential Chief of Staff (대통령비서실장), the Minister of the Office for Government Policy Coordination (국무조정실장), the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Government_Legislation" title="Ministry of Government Legislation">Minister of Government Legislation</a> (법제처장), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Patriots_and_Veterans_Affairs" title="Ministry of Patriots and Veterans Affairs">Minister of Patriots and Veterans Affairs</a> (국가보훈처장), the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Food_and_Drug_Safety" title="Ministry of Food and Drug Safety">Minister of Food and Drug Safety</a> (식품의약품안전처장), the Chairperson of <a href="/wiki/Fair_Trade_Commission_(South_Korea)" title="Fair Trade Commission (South Korea)">Korea Fair Trade Commission</a> (공정거래위원회위원장), the Chairperson of <a href="/wiki/Financial_Services_Commission_(South_Korea)" title="Financial Services Commission (South Korea)">Financial Services Commission</a> (금융위원회위원장), the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Seoul" title="Mayor of Seoul">Mayor of Seoul Special City</a> (서울특별시장), and other officials designated by law or deemed necessary by the Chairperson of the State Council can also attend the State Council meetings and speak in front of the State Council without the right to vote on the matters discussed in the meetings <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Mayor of Seoul, although being the head of a local autonomous region in South Korea and not directly related to the central executive branch, has been allowed to attend the State Council meeting considering the special status of <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> as a Special City and its mayor as the only cabinet-level mayor in Korea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Council_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The State Council is the highest body for policy deliberation and resolution in the executive branch of the Republic of Korea. Article 89 of the South Korean constitution specifies what "important policies that fall within the power of the Executive" the State Council has to deliver: <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Article_89_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Article_89-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<ol>
<li>Basic plans for state affairs, and general policies of the Executive;</li>
<li>Declaration of war, conclusion of peace and other important matters pertaining to foreign policy;</li>
<li>Draft amendments to the Constitution, proposals for national referenda, proposed treaties, legislative bills, and proposed presidential decrees;</li>
<li>Budgets, settlement of accounts, basic plans for disposal of state properties, contracts incurring financial burden on the State, and other important financial matters;</li>
<li>Emergency orders and emergency financial and economic actions or orders by the President, and declaration and termination of martial law;</li>
<li>Important military affairs;</li>
<li>Requests for convening an extraordinary session of the National Assembly;</li>
<li>Awarding of honors;</li>
<li>Granting of amnesty, commutation and restoration of rights;</li>
<li>Demarcation of jurisdiction among the Ministries of the Executive;</li>
<li>Basic plans concerning delegation or allocation of powers within the Executive;</li>
<li>Evaluation and analysis of the administration of state affairs;</li>
<li>Formulation and coordination of important policies of each Executive Ministry;</li>
<li>Action for the dissolution of a political party;</li>
<li>Examination of petitions pertaining to executive policies submitted or referred to the Executive;</li>
<li>Appointment of the Prosecutor General, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, the Chief of Staff of each armed service, the presidents of national universities, ambassadors, and such other public officials and managers of important state-run enterprises as designated by Act; and</li>
<li>Other matters presented by the President, the Prime Minister or a member of the State Council.</li>
</ol>
</blockquote>
<p>It has to be noted that the State Council of the Republic of Korea performs somewhat different roles than those of many other nations with similar forms. As the Korean political system is basically a presidential system yet with certain aspects of parliamentary State Council system combined, the State Council of the Republic of Korea also is a combination of both systems. More specifically, the Korean State Council performs policy resolutions as well as policy consultations to the President. Reflecting that the Republic of Korea is basically a presidential republic the State Council resolutions cannot bind the president's decision, and in this regard the Korean State Council is similar to those advisory counsels in strict presidential republics. At the same time, however, the Constitution of the Republic of Korea specifies in details 17 categories including budgetary and military matters, which necessitates the resolution of the State Council in addition to the President's approval, and in this regard the Korean State Council is similar to those State Councils in strict parliamentary State Council systems. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Article_89_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Article_89-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meetings">Meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Council_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the president is the chairman of the council, the Prime Minister nevertheless frequently holds the meetings without the presence of the President as the meeting can be lawfully held as long as the majority of the State Council members are present at the meeting. Also, as many government agencies have recently been moved out of Seoul into other parts of the country, the need to hold State Council meetings without having to convene in one place at the same time has been growing, and therefore the law has been amended to allow State Council meetings in a visual teleconference format. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
