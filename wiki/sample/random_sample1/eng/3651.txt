<p>A <b>macrophyte</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">aquatic plant</a> that grows in or near water and is either emergent, submergent, or floating, and includes <b>helophytes</b> (a plant that grows in <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marsh</a>, partly submerged in water, so that it regrows from <a href="/wiki/Bud" title="Bud">buds</a> below the water surface).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In lakes and rivers macrophytes provide cover for <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(marine_biology)" title="Substrate (marine biology)">substrate</a> for <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, produce <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and act as food for some fish and wildlife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fringing stands of tall vegetation by water basins and rivers may include helophytes. Examples include stands of <i><a href="/wiki/Equisetum_fluviatile" title="Equisetum fluviatile">Equisetum fluviatile</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Glyceria_maxima" title="Glyceria maxima">Glyceria maxima</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hippuris_vulgaris" title="Hippuris vulgaris">Hippuris vulgaris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sagittaria" title="Sagittaria">Sagittaria</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carex" title="Carex">Carex</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Schoenoplectus" title="Schoenoplectus">Schoenoplectus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sparganium" title="Sparganium">Sparganium</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Acorus" title="Acorus">Acorus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Iris_pseudacorus" title="Iris pseudacorus">yellow flag</a> (<i>Iris pseudacorus</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Typha" title="Typha">Typha</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phragmites_australis" title="Phragmites australis">Phragmites australis</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergent_plants">Emergent plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macrophyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Emergent plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <b>emergent plant</b> is one which grows in water but which pierces the surface so that it is partially in air. Collectively, such plants are <b>emergent vegetation</b>.</p>
<p>This habit may have developed because the leaves can <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesize</a> more efficiently above the shade of cloudy water and competition from submerged plants but often, the main aerial feature is the flower and the related reproductive process. The emergent habit permits pollination by wind or by flying <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>.</p>
<p>There are many species of emergent plants, among them, the reed (<i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Cyperus_papyrus" title="Cyperus papyrus">Cyperus papyrus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Typha" title="Typha">Typha</a></i> species, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowering_rush" title="Flowering rush">flowering rush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wild_rice" title="Wild rice">wild rice</a> species. These may be found growing in fens but usually less well owing to competition from other plants. Some species, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purple_loosestrife" title="Purple loosestrife">purple loosestrife</a>, may grow in water as emergent plants but they are capable of flourishing in fens or simply in damp ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_watershed_health_indicators">As watershed health indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macrophyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As watershed health indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A decline in a macrophyte community may indicate water quality problems and changes in the ecological status of the water body. Such problems may be the result of excessive <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbicides" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salinization" title="Salinization">salinization</a>. Conversely, overly high nutrient levels may create an overabundance of macrophytes, which may in turn interfere with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lake processing (page does not exist)">lake processing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macrophyte levels are easy to sample, do not require laboratory analysis, and are easily used for calculating simple abundance metrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_potential_sources_of_therapeutic_agents">As potential sources of therapeutic agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macrophyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As potential sources of therapeutic agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phytochemical and pharmacological researches suggest that freshwater macrophytes, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Centella_asiatica" title="Centella asiatica">Centella asiatica</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nelumbo_nucifera" title="Nelumbo nucifera">Nelumbo nucifera</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasturtium_officinale" title="Nasturtium officinale">Nasturtium officinale</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ipomoea_aquatica" title="Ipomoea aquatica">Ipomoea aquatica</a></i> and <i>Ludwigia adscendens</i>, are promising sources of anticancer and antioxidative natural products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hot water extracts of the stem and root of <i>Ludwigia adscendens</i>, as well as those of the fruit, leaf and stem of <i>Monochoria hastata</i> were found to have <a href="/wiki/Lipoxygenase" title="Lipoxygenase">lipoxygenase</a> inhibitory activity. Hot water extract prepared from the leaf of <i>Ludwigia adscendens</i> exhibits <a href="/wiki/Alpha-glucosidase" title="Alpha-glucosidase">alpha-glucosidase</a> inhibitory activity more potent than that of <a href="/wiki/Acarbose" title="Acarbose">acarbose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
