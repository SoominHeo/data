<p><b>Daniel Gerhard "Dan" Brown</b> (born June 22, 1964) is an American author of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thriller_fiction" title="Thriller fiction">thriller fiction</a> who is best known for the 2003 bestselling novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Brown's novels are treasure hunts set in a 24-hour period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Themes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Themes-3">[3]</a></sup> and feature the recurring themes of <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a>, keys, symbols, codes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theories" title="Conspiracy theories">conspiracy theories</a>. His books have been translated into 52 languages, and as of 2012, sold over 200 million copies. Three of them, <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons" title="Angels &amp; Demons">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i> (2000), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i> (2003), and <i><a href="/wiki/Inferno_(Brown_novel)" title="Inferno (Brown novel)">Inferno</a></i> (2013), have been adapted into films.</p>
<p>Brown's novels that feature the lead character <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langdon" title="Robert Langdon">Robert Langdon</a> also include historical themes and <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> as motifs, and as a result, have generated controversy. Brown states on his website that his books are not <a href="/wiki/Anti-Christian_sentiment" title="Anti-Christian sentiment">anti-Christian</a>, though he is on a 'constant spiritual journey' himself, and says that his book <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> is simply "an entertaining story that promotes spiritual discussion and debate" and suggests that the book may be used "as a positive catalyst for introspection and exploration of our faith."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dan Brown was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Exeter,_New_Hampshire" title="Exeter, New Hampshire">Exeter, New Hampshire</a>, the eldest of three children. He grew up on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Exeter_Academy" title="Phillips Exeter Academy">Phillips Exeter Academy</a>, where his father, <a href="/wiki/Richard_G._Brown" title="Richard G. Brown">Richard G. Brown</a>, was a teacher of mathematics and wrote textbooks from 1968 until his retirement in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-5">[5]</a></sup> Brown's parents are singers and musicians, having served as church choir masters, with his mother, Constance (Gerhard), serving as church <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Brown was raised an <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopalian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-4">[4]</a></sup> but has stated he had drifted away from Christianity before finding a renewed interest in religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brown's interest in secrets and puzzles stems from their presence in his household as a child, where codes and <a href="/wiki/Cipher" title="Cipher">ciphers</a> were the linchpin tying together the mathematics, music, and languages in which his parents worked. The young Brown spent hours working out <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagrams</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossword_puzzles" title="Crossword puzzles">crossword puzzles</a>, and he and his siblings participated in elaborate treasure hunts devised by their father on birthdays and holidays. On Christmas, for example, Brown and his siblings did not find gifts under the tree, but followed a treasure map with codes and clues throughout their house and even around town to find the gifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witness_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-witness-10">[10]</a></sup> Brown's relationship with his father inspired that of <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Da_Vinci_Code_characters#Sophie_Neveu_Saint-Clair" title="List of The Da Vinci Code characters">Sophie Neveu</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Da_Vinci_Code_characters#Jacques_Sauni.C3.A8re" title="List of The Da Vinci Code characters">Jacques SauniÃ¨re</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i>, and Chapter 23 of that novel was inspired by one of his childhood treasure hunts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating from Phillips Exeter, Brown attended <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">Amherst College</a>, where he was a member of <a href="/wiki/Psi_Upsilon" title="Psi Upsilon">Psi Upsilon</a> fraternity. He played <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)" title="Squash (sport)">squash</a>, sang in the <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College_Glee_Club" title="Amherst College Glee Club">Amherst Glee Club</a>, and was a writing student of visiting novelist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lelchuk" title="Alan Lelchuk">Alan Lelchuk</a>. Brown spent the 1985 school year abroad in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, Spain, where he was enrolled in an art history course at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Seville" title="University of Seville">University of Seville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witness_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-witness-10">[10]</a></sup> Brown graduated from Amherst in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amherst8.12_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amherst8.12-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songwriter_and_pop_singer">Songwriter and pop singer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Songwriter and pop singer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from Amherst, Brown dabbled with a musical career, creating effects with a synthesizer, and self-producing a children's cassette entitled <i>SynthAnimals</i>, which included a collection of tracks such as "Happy Frogs" and "Suzuki Elephants"; it sold a few hundred copies. He then formed his own record company called Dalliance, and in 1990 self-published a CD entitled <i>Perspective</i>, targeted to the adult market, which also sold a few hundred copies. In 1991 he moved to <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> to pursue a career as singer-songwriter and pianist. To support himself, he taught classes at <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills_Preparatory_School" title="Beverly Hills Preparatory School">Beverly Hills Preparatory School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also joined the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Songwriters" title="National Academy of Songwriters">National Academy of Songwriters</a>, and participated in many of its events. It was there that he met Blythe Newlon, a woman 12 years his senior, who was the Academy's Director of Artist Development. Though it was not officially part of her job, she took on the seemingly unusual task of helping to promote Brown's projects; she wrote press releases, set up promotional events, and put him in contact with people who could be helpful to his career. She and Brown also developed a personal relationship, though this was not known to all of their associates until 1993, when Brown moved back to New Hampshire, and it was learned that Newlon would accompany him. They married in 1997, at Pea Porridge Pond, near <a href="/wiki/Conway,_New_Hampshire" title="Conway, New Hampshire">Conway, New Hampshire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walters-OKeeffe_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walters-OKeeffe-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994 Brown released a CD titled <i>Angels &amp; Demons</i>. Its artwork was the same <a href="/wiki/Ambigram" title="Ambigram">ambigram</a> by artist <a href="/wiki/John_Langdon_(typographer)" title="John Langdon (typographer)">John Langdon</a> which he later used for the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons" title="Angels &amp; Demons">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i>. The liner notes also again credited his wife for her involvement, thanking her "for being my tireless cowriter, coproducer, second engineer, significant other, and therapist." The CD included songs such as "Here in These Fields" and the religious ballad, "All I Believe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Brown and his wife, Blythe, moved to his home town in New Hampshire in 1993. Brown became an English teacher at his alma mater Phillips Exeter, and gave Spanish classes to 6th, 7th, and 8th graders at <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Akerman_School" title="Lincoln Akerman School">Lincoln Akerman School</a>, a small school for Kâ8th grade with about 250 students, in <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Falls,_New_Hampshire" title="Hampton Falls, New Hampshire">Hampton Falls</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_career">Writing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langdon_(book_series)" title="Robert Langdon (book series)">Robert Langdon (book series)</a></div>
<p>While on vacation in <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> in 1993,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witness_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-witness-10">[10]</a></sup> Brown read <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Sheldon" title="Sidney Sheldon">Sidney Sheldon</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Doomsday_Conspiracy" title="The Doomsday Conspiracy">The Doomsday Conspiracy</a></i>, and was inspired to become a writer of thrillers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witness_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-witness-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He started work on <i><a href="/wiki/Digital_Fortress" title="Digital Fortress">Digital Fortress</a></i>, setting much of it in Seville, where he had studied in 1985. He also co-wrote a humor book with his wife, <i>187 Men to Avoid: A Survival Guide for the Romantically Frustrated Woman</i>, under the pseudonym "Danielle Brown". The book's author profile reads, "Danielle Brown currently lives in New England: teaching school, writing books, and avoiding men." The copyright is attributed to Dan Brown.</p>
<p>In 1996 Brown quit teaching to become a full-time writer. <i>Digital Fortress</i> was published in 1998. His wife, Blythe, did much of the book's promotion, writing press releases, booking Brown on talk shows, and setting up press interviews. A few months later, Brown and his wife released <i>The Bald Book</i>, another humor book. It was officially credited to his wife, though a representative of the publisher said that it was primarily written by Brown. Brown subsequently wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons" title="Angels &amp; Demons">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Deception_Point" title="Deception Point">Deception Point</a></i>, released in 2000 and 2001 respectively, the former of which was the first to feature the lead character, Harvard symbology expert <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langdon" title="Robert Langdon">Robert Langdon</a>.</p>
<p>Brown's first three novels had little success, with fewer than 10,000 copies in each of their first printings. His fourth novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i>, became a bestseller, going to the top of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> Best Seller list</a> during its first week of release in 2003. It is now credited with being one of the most popular books of all time, with 81 million copies sold worldwide as of 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Its success has helped push sales of Brown's earlier books. In 2004 all four of his novels were on the <i>New York Times</i> list in the same week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and in 2005 he made <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's list of the 100 most influential people of the year. <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine placed Brown at No. 12 on their 2005 "Celebrity 100" list, and estimated his annual income at US$76.5 million. <i>The Times</i> estimated his income from <i>Da Vinci Code</i> sales as $250 million.</p>
<p>Brown's third novel featuring Robert Langdon, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Symbol" title="The Lost Symbol">The Lost Symbol</a></i>, was released on September 15, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bostonheraldannounce_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-bostonheraldannounce-23">[23]</a></sup> According to the publisher, on its first day the book sold over one million in hardcover and e-book versions in the US, the UK and Canada, prompting the printing of 600,000 hardcover copies in addition to the five million first printing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The story takes place in Washington D.C. over a period of 12 hours, and features the <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasons</a>. Brown's promotional website states that puzzles hidden in the book jacket of <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, including two references to the <i><a href="/wiki/Kryptos" title="Kryptos">Kryptos</a></i> sculpture at <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a> Headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Langley,_Virginia" title="Langley, Virginia">Langley, Virginia</a>, give hints about the sequel. This repeats a theme from some of Brown's earlier work.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Brown's fourth novel featuring Robert Langdon, <i><a href="/wiki/Inferno_(Brown_novel)" title="Inferno (Brown novel)">Inferno</a></i> is a mystery thriller novel released on May 14, 2013, by Doubleday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It immediately became a bestseller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2006 interview, Brown stated that he had ideas for about 12 future books featuring Robert Langdon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Characters in Brown's books are often named after real people in his life. Robert Langdon is named after John Langdon, the artist who created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambigrams" title="Ambigrams">ambigrams</a> used for the <i>Angels &amp; Demons</i> CD and novel. <a href="/wiki/Camerlengo" title="Camerlengo">Camerlengo</a> Carlo Ventresca is named after "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On_A_Claire_Day" title="On A Claire Day">On A Claire Day</a>" cartoonist friend <a href="/wiki/Carla_Ventresca" title="Carla Ventresca">Carla Ventresca</a>. In the Vatican archives, Langdon recalls a wedding of two people named Dick and Connie, which are the names of his parents. Robert Langdon's editor Jonas Faukman is named after Brown's real life editor Jason Kaufman. Brown also said that characters were based on a New Hampshire librarian, and a French teacher at Exeter, AndrÃ© Vernet. Cardinal Aldo Baggia, in <i>Angels &amp; Demons</i>, is named after Aldo Baggia, instructor of modern languages at Phillips Exeter Academy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In interviews, Brown has said his wife, Blythe, is an art historian and painter. When they met, she was the Director of Artistic Development at the National Academy for Songwriters in Los Angeles. During the 2006 lawsuit over alleged copyright infringement in <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, information was introduced at trial that showed that Blythe did research for the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In one article, she was described as "chief researcher."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2016, Brown has revealed that Doubleday will publish his next book, entitled <i>Origin.</i> It is going to include his famous fictional character, Robert Langdon. This is his seventh book published overall, and the fifth book in his Robert Langdon series. It is due to be published on September 27, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_and_habits">Influences and habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influences and habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to Sidney Sheldon, Brown has been quite vocal about a number of other literary influences who have inspired his writing. He has named his six favorite literary works as <i><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6del,_Escher,_Bach" title="GÃ¶del, Escher, Bach">GÃ¶del, Escher, Bach</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hofstadter" title="Douglas Hofstadter">Douglas Hofstadter</a>, <i>Codes Ciphers &amp; Other Cryptic &amp; Clandestine Communication</i> by Fred Wrixon, <i><a href="/wiki/Of_Mice_and_Men" title="Of Mice and Men">Of Mice and Men</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Steinbeck" title="John Steinbeck">John Steinbeck</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wordplay:_Ambigrams_and_Reflections_on_the_Art_of_Ambigrams" title="Wordplay: Ambigrams and Reflections on the Art of Ambigrams">Wordplay: Ambigrams and Reflections on the Art of Ambigrams</a></i> by John Langdon, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Much_Ado_About_Nothing" title="Much Ado About Nothing">Much Ado About Nothing</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Puzzle_Palace" title="The Puzzle Palace">The Puzzle Palace</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/James_Bamford" title="James Bamford">James Bamford</a>. Commenting on <i>Much Ado About Nothing</i>, Brown says, "I didn't understand how funny this play <i>Much Ado About Nothing</i> truly was until I became an English teacher and had to teach it. There is no wittier dialogue anywhere." Speaking of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ludlum" title="Robert Ludlum">Robert Ludlum</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Bourne_(novel_series)" title="Bourne (novel series)">The Bourne Trilogy</a></i>, he states, "Ludlum's early books are complex, smart, and yet still move at a lightning pace. This series got me interested in the genre of big-concept, international thrillers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes.26Noble_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes.26Noble-33">[33]</a></sup> His other favorite books include <i><a href="/wiki/Kane_and_Abel_(novel)" title="Kane and Abel (novel)">Kane and Abel</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Archer" title="Jeffrey Archer">Jeffrey Archer</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Plum_Island_(novel)" title="Plum Island (novel)">Plum Island</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Nelson_DeMille" title="Nelson DeMille">Nelson DeMille</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elements_of_Style" title="The Elements of Style">The Elements of Style</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strunk_and_White" title="Strunk and White">Strunk and White</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes.26Noble_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes.26Noble-33">[33]</a></sup> Recurring elements that Brown prefers to incorporate into his novels include a simple hero pulled out of their familiar setting and thrust into a new one with which they are unfamiliar, strong female characters, travel to interesting locations, and a 24-hour time frame in which the story takes place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Themes_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Themes-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the research-intensive nature of his novels, Brown can spend up to two years writing them. To remain focused on such projects, Brown ensures that when he chooses a theme for the novel (what he refers to as the "big idea"), and its subject, that they be those that can hold his interest. In Brown's view, the ideal topic does not have an easily defined right or wrong view, but presents a moral grey area that can lend itself to debate. Because his favorite subjects include codes, puzzles, treasure hunts, secretive organizations and academic lectures on obscure topics, he tends to incorporate those into his novels. Because Brown considers writing to be a discipline that requires constant practice, he has developed a routine to maintain his abilities. He wakes up at 4:00Â am when there are no distractions (a practice he began with <i><a href="/wiki/Digital_Fortress" title="Digital Fortress">Digital Fortress</a></i> when he had two daytime teaching jobs) and when he feels most productive, in order to give symbolic importance to the first order of business each day. He keeps an antique <a href="/wiki/Hourglass" title="Hourglass">hourglass</a> on his desk, so that he can stop briefly every hour to do push-ups, sit-ups and stretching exercises to keep his blood flowing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Brown does his writing in his loft. He has also told fans that he uses <a href="/wiki/Inversion_therapy" title="Inversion therapy">inversion therapy</a> to help with <a href="/wiki/Writer%27s_block" title="Writer's block">writer's block</a>. He uses <a href="/wiki/Gravity_boots" title="Gravity boots">gravity boots</a> and says, "hanging upside down seems to help me solve plot challenges by shifting my entire perspective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006 Brown's novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i> was released as <a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code_(film)" title="The Da Vinci Code (film)">a film</a> by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>, with director <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>. It was widely anticipated and launched the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>, though it received overall poor reviews. It currently has a 24% rating at the film review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, derived from 165 negative reviews of the 214 counted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It was later listed as one of the worst films of 2006 on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebert_%26_Roeper" title="Ebert &amp; Roeper">Ebert &amp; Roeper</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> but also the second highest-grossing film of the year, pulling in US$750 million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Brown was listed as one of the executive producers of the film <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, and also created additional codes for the film. One of his songs, "Phiano," which Brown wrote and performed, was listed as part of the film's soundtrack. In the film, Brown and his wife can be seen in the background of one of the early book signing scenes.</p>
<p>The next film, <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons_(film)" title="Angels &amp; Demons (film)">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i>, was released on May 15, 2009, with Howard and Hanks returning. It, too, garnered mostly negative reviews, though critics were kinder to it than to its predecessor. As of July 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it has a 37% meta-rating at <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Filmmakers expressed interest in adapting <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Symbol#Film" title="The Lost Symbol">The Lost Symbol</a></i> into a film as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The screenplay was being written by <a href="/wiki/Danny_Strong" title="Danny Strong">Danny Strong</a>, with pre-production expected to begin in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes1-42">[42]</a></sup> According to a January 2013 article in <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> the final draft of the screenplay was due sometime in February,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes1_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes1-42">[42]</a></sup> but in July 2013, <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures" title="Sony Pictures">Sony Pictures</a> announced they would instead adapt <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inferno_(Dan_Brown_novel)" title="Inferno (Dan Brown novel)">Inferno</a></i> for an October 14, 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> release date with <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a> as director, <a href="/wiki/David_Koepp" title="David Koepp">David Koepp</a> adapting the screenplay and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> reprising his role as Robert Langdon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Imagine_Entertainment" title="Imagine Entertainment">Imagine Entertainment</a> is set to produce a television series based on <i>Digital Fortress</i>, to be written by Josh Goldin and Rachel Abramowitz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_infringement_cases">Copyright infringement cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Copyright infringement cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In August 2005 author <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Perdue" title="Lewis Perdue">Lewis Perdue</a> unsuccessfully sued Brown for <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarism</a>, on the basis of claimed similarity between <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> and his novels, <i>The Da Vinci Legacy</i> (1983) and <i>Daughter of God</i> (2000). Judge George Daniels said, in part: "A reasonable average lay observer would not conclude that <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> is substantially similar to <i>Daughter of God</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2006 Brown's publisher, <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>, won a <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> case brought by authors <a href="/wiki/Michael_Baigent" title="Michael Baigent">Michael Baigent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Leigh_(author)" title="Richard Leigh (author)">Richard Leigh</a>, who claimed that Brown stole ideas from their 1982 book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Blood_Holy_Grail" title="Holy Blood Holy Grail">Holy Blood Holy Grail</a></i> for his 2003 novel <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>. It was in the book <i>Holy Blood Holy Grail</i> that Baigent, Leigh, and co-author <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lincoln" title="Henry Lincoln">Henry Lincoln</a> had advanced the theory that Jesus and <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a> married and had a child and that the bloodline continues to this day. Brown apparently alluded to the two authors' names in his book. <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Da_Vinci_Code_characters#Sir_Leigh_Teabing.2FThe_Teacher" title="List of The Da Vinci Code characters">Leigh Teabing</a>, a lead character in both the novel and the film, uses Leigh's name as the first name, and anagrammatically derives his last name from Baigent's. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Smith_(judge)" title="Peter Smith (judge)">Mr Justice Peter Smith</a> found in Brown's favor in the case, and as a private amusement, embedded his own <a href="/wiki/Smithy_code" title="Smithy code">Smithy code</a> in the written judgment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 28, 2007, Brown's publisher, <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>, won an appeal <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> case. The <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal of England and Wales</a> rejected the efforts from Baigent and Leigh, who became liable for paying legal expenses of nearly US$6 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_work">Charity work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Charity work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2004, Brown and his siblings donated US$2.2 million to <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Exeter_Academy" title="Phillips Exeter Academy">Phillips Exeter Academy</a> in honor of their father, to set up the Richard G. Brown Technology Endowment to help "provide computers and high-tech equipment for students in need."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brown and his wife Blythe are supporters of the <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_Charitable_Foundation" title="New Hampshire Charitable Foundation">New Hampshire Charitable Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 14, 2011, Dan and Blythe Brown created an eponymous scholarship fund to celebrate his 25th reunion from Amherst College, a permanently endowed scholarship fund at the college whose income provides financial aid to students there, with preference for incoming students with an interest in writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amherst8.12_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amherst8.12-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 16, 2016, Dan Brown donated US$337,000 to the Ritman Library in Amsterdam to digitize a collection of ancient books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="Criticism of The Da Vinci Code">Criticism of The Da Vinci Code</a></div>
<p>Brown's prose style has been criticized as clumsy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> with <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> being described as 'committing style and word choice blunders in almost every paragraph'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Much of the criticism was centered on Brown's claim found in its preface that the novel is based on fact in relation to <a href="/wiki/Opus_Dei" title="Opus Dei">Opus Dei</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a>, and that "all descriptions of artwork, architecture, documents and secret rituals in [the] novel are accurate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Matt_Lauer" title="Matt Lauer">Matt Lauer</a> on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Today_(NBC_program)" title="Today (NBC program)">Today</a></i> in September 2009, Brown responded by saying, "I do something very intentional and specific in these books. And that is to blend fact and fiction in a very modern and efficient style, to tell a story. There are some people who understand what I do, and they sort of get on the train and go for a ride and have a great time, and there are other people who should probably just read somebody else."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
