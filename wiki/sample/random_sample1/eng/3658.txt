<p><b>Daniel Grayling "Dan" Fogelberg</b> (August 13, 1951 – December 16, 2007) was an American musician, songwriter, composer, and multi-instrumentalist whose music was inspired by sources as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a>, pop, rock, classical, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass</a>. He is best known for his early 1980s hits, including "<a href="/wiki/Longer" title="Longer">Longer</a>" (1980), "<a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Band" title="Leader of the Band">Leader of the Band</a>" (1981), and "<a href="/wiki/Same_Old_Lang_Syne" title="Same Old Lang Syne">Same Old Lang Syne</a>" (1981).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_family">Early life and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Fogelberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dan Fogelberg, the youngest of three sons, was born in <a href="/wiki/Peoria,_Illinois" title="Peoria, Illinois">Peoria, Illinois</a>, the son of Margaret (née Irvine), a classically trained pianist, and Lawrence Peter Fogelberg, a high school band director who spent most of his career at Peoria's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodruff_High_School_(Peoria,_Illinois)" title="Woodruff High School (Peoria, Illinois)">Woodruff High School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pekin,_Illinois" title="Pekin, Illinois">Pekin</a> High School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Dan's mother was a Scottish immigrant, and his father was of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-2">[2]</a></sup> His father would later be the inspiration for the song, "<a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Band" title="Leader of the Band">Leader of the Band</a>". Using a <a href="/wiki/Mel_Bay" title="Mel Bay">Mel Bay</a> course book, Dan taught himself to play a Hawaiian <a href="/wiki/Slide_guitar" title="Slide guitar">slide guitar</a> that his grandfather gave him. He also learned to play the piano. At 14 he joined a band, The Clan, which covered <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>. His second band was another <a href="/wiki/Cover_band" title="Cover band">cover band</a>, The Coachmen, who, in 1967, released two singles written by Fogelberg. They were cut at Golden Voice Recording studio in South Pekin IL and released on Ledger Record's label: "Maybe Time Will Let Me Forget" and "Don't Want To Lose Her."</p>
<p>After graduating from Woodruff High School in 1969, Fogelberg studied theater arts and painting at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a> while playing local venues with a folk-rock band, The Ship. He began performing as a solo acoustic player in area cafes. One of these included the Red Herring, which is where he made his first solo recordings as part of a folk festival in 1971. He was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Irving_Azoff" title="Irving Azoff">Irving Azoff</a>, who started his music management career promoting another Champaign-Urbana act, <a href="/wiki/REO_Speedwagon" title="REO Speedwagon">REO Speedwagon</a>. Azoff sent him to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a> to hone his skills. There he became a <a href="/wiki/Session_musician" title="Session musician">session musician</a> and recorded his first album with producer <a href="/wiki/Norbert_Putnam" title="Norbert Putnam">Norbert Putnam</a>. In 1972, Fogelberg released his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Free_(album)" title="Home Free (album)">Home Free</a></i> to lukewarm response, although it eventually reached platinum status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DanFogelbergCom_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-DanFogelbergCom-3">[3]</a></sup> He performed as an opening act for <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Fogelberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fogelberg's second effort was much more successful – the 1974 <a href="/wiki/Joe_Walsh" title="Joe Walsh">Joe Walsh</a>-produced album <i><a href="/wiki/Souvenirs_(Dan_Fogelberg_album)" title="Souvenirs (Dan Fogelberg album)">Souvenirs</a></i>. The song "Part of the Plan" became Fogelberg's first hit. After <i>Souvenirs</i>, he released a string of <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold</a> and platinum albums, including <i><a href="/wiki/Captured_Angel" title="Captured Angel">Captured Angel</a></i> (1975) and <i><a href="/wiki/Nether_Lands" title="Nether Lands">Nether Lands</a></i> (1977). His 1978 <i><a href="/wiki/Twin_Sons_of_Different_Mothers" title="Twin Sons of Different Mothers">Twin Sons of Different Mothers</a></i> was the first of two collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> flautist <a href="/wiki/Tim_Weisberg" title="Tim Weisberg">Tim Weisberg</a>, which found commercial success with songs such as "The Power of Gold". <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(Dan_Fogelberg_album)" title="Phoenix (Dan Fogelberg album)">Phoenix</a></i>, from 1979, reached the Top 10, with "<a href="/wiki/Longer" title="Longer">Longer</a>" becoming a #2 pop hit (and wedding standard) in 1980. The track peaked at #59 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> – his sole entry on that chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-4">[4]</a></sup> The album reached #42 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>, likewise his only entry there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-4">[4]</a></sup> It was followed by a Top 20 hit "Heart Hotels".</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Innocent_Age" title="The Innocent Age">The Innocent Age</a></i>, released in October 1981, was Fogelberg's critical and commercial peak. The double album included four of his biggest hits: "<a href="/wiki/Same_Old_Lang_Syne" title="Same Old Lang Syne">Same Old Lang Syne</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hard_to_Say" title="Hard to Say">Hard to Say</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Band" title="Leader of the Band">Leader of the Band</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Run_for_the_Roses_(song)" title="Run for the Roses (song)">Run for the Roses</a>". He drew inspiration for <i>The Innocent Age</i> from Thomas Wolfe's novel <i>Of Time and the River</i>. A 1982 <a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_(Dan_Fogelberg_album)" title="Greatest Hits (Dan Fogelberg album)">greatest hits album</a> contained two new songs, both of which were released as singles: "Missing You" and "<a href="/wiki/Make_Love_Stay" title="Make Love Stay">Make Love Stay</a>." In 1984, he released the album <i><a href="/wiki/Windows_and_Walls" title="Windows and Walls">Windows and Walls</a></i>, containing the singles "The Language of Love" and "<a href="/wiki/Believe_in_Me_(Dan_Fogelberg_song)" title="Believe in Me (Dan Fogelberg song)">Believe in Me</a>."</p>
<p>Fogelberg released <i><a href="/wiki/High_Country_Snows" title="High Country Snows">High Country Snows</a></i> in 1985. Recorded in Nashville, it showcased his and some of the industry's best talent in <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vince_Gill" title="Vince Gill">Vince Gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Skaggs" title="Ricky Skaggs">Ricky Skaggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doc_Watson" title="Doc Watson">Doc Watson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Douglas" title="Jerry Douglas">Jerry Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Grisman" title="David Grisman">David Grisman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hillman" title="Chris Hillman">Chris Hillman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Herb_Pedersen" title="Herb Pedersen">Herb Pedersen</a> contributed to the record. In a world he defined as "life in the fast lane," Fogelberg described the music as "life in the off-ramp." In late 1985, he switched gears and took to the road with a group of musician friends, including <a href="/wiki/Joe_Vitale_(musician)" title="Joe Vitale (musician)">Joe Vitale</a> and Rick Rosas, playing blues in small clubs throughout <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> as Frankie and the Aliens, covering songs by <a href="/wiki/Cream_(band)" title="Cream (band)">Cream</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">Muddy Waters</a>, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> 1987 heralded a return to rock with <i><a href="/wiki/Exiles_(album)" title="Exiles (album)">Exiles</a></i>, an album that contained "What You're Doing," a throwback to the old Stax Records sound made famous in Memphis during the 1960s. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Places_(Dan_Fogelberg_album)" title="The Wild Places (Dan Fogelberg album)">The Wild Places</a></i>, an album whose theme was the preservation of nature, was released in 1990 followed by a tour. His live "Greetings From The West" album, and full-length concert film (with interview segments) of the same name, was released in 1991.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/River_of_Souls" title="River of Souls">River of Souls</a></i>, released in 1993, was Fogelberg's last studio album for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Records" title="Sony Records">Sony Records</a>. In 1997, the box set <i>Portrait</i> encompassed his career with four discs, each highlighting a different facet of his music: "Ballads," "Rock and Roll," "Tales and Travels," and "Hits." In 1999, he released a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_album" title="Christmas album">Christmas album</a>, <i>The First Christmas Morning</i>, and in 2003, <i>Full Circle</i> showcased a return to the folk-influenced 1970s <a href="/wiki/Soft_rock" title="Soft rock">soft rock</a> style of music.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Fogelberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fogelberg was married three times: to Maggie Slaymaker, a dancer from Nashville, from 1982 to 1985; to Anastasia Savage, a nurse and artist from <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, from 1991 to 1996; and to musician Jean Marie Mayer, from April 7, 2002 until his death in late 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He had no children by any of his marriages.</p>
<p>In May 2004, Fogelberg was diagnosed with advanced <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">prostate cancer</a>. After undergoing therapy, he achieved a partial remission. On August 13, 2005, his 54th birthday, he announced the success of his cancer treatments. He said that he had no immediate plans to return to making music but was keeping his options open. However, his cancer returned, and on December 16, 2007, Fogelberg died at the age of 56 at his home in <a href="/wiki/Deer_Isle,_Maine" title="Deer Isle, Maine">Deer Isle, Maine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dfpeoria_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dfpeoria-8">[8]</a></sup> His ashes were scattered into the Atlantic Ocean.</p>
<p>Fogelberg's widow announced that a song written and recorded by Fogelberg for her Valentine's Day 2005, "Sometimes a Song," would be sold on the Internet and that all proceeds would go to the <a href="/wiki/Prostate_Cancer_Foundation" title="Prostate Cancer Foundation">Prostate Cancer Foundation</a>. The song was released Valentine's Day 2008 and was also included on a CD released in September 2009 titled <i>Love in Time</i>, a collection of 11 previously unpublished songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Fogelberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In tribute to Fogelberg and the Fogelberg family, the city of <a href="/wiki/Peoria,_Illinois" title="Peoria, Illinois">Peoria</a> renamed Abington Street in the city's East Bluff neighborhood "Fogelberg Parkway". The street runs along the northeast side of Woodruff High School, Fogelberg's alma mater, and where his father was a teacher and bandleader. "Fogelberg Parkway" continues northwest, then west, to the intersection of N. Prospect and E. Frye, the location of the convenience store where Fogelberg ran into his old high school sweetheart one Christmas Eve – a chance encounter described in the song "<a href="/wiki/Same_Old_Lang_Syne" title="Same Old Lang Syne">Same Old Lang Syne</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dfpeoria_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-dfpeoria-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group of Fogelberg fans raised funds to create a permanent memorial. The memorial garden, placed in Riverfront Park, was dedicated in a ceremony on August 28, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dfpeoria_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-dfpeoria-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
