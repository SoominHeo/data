<p><b>Thatgamecompany, LLC</b> (stylized as <b>thatgamecompany</b>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">independent video game development</a> company co-founded by <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> students <a href="/wiki/Jenova_Chen" title="Jenova Chen">Jenova Chen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kellee_Santiago" title="Kellee Santiago">Kellee Santiago</a> in 2006. The company was a developer for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony Computer Entertainment</a>, contracted to create three downloadable games for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>'s <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> service, and has since secured independent funding. The first of their games is a remake of Chen's award-winning <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a> title <i><a href="/wiki/Flow_(video_game)" title="Flow (video game)">Flow</a></i>, with enhanced visuals and sound, added <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_video_game" title="Multiplayer video game">multiplayer modes</a> and compatibility with the PlayStation 3's motion sensitive controller. The title was released on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Store" title="PlayStation Store">PlayStation Store</a> in 2007. The company's second PlayStation 3 game, <i><a href="/wiki/Flower_(video_game)" title="Flower (video game)">Flower</a></i>, was released on the PlayStation Store in 2009, and their third game, <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_(2012_video_game)" title="Journey (2012 video game)">Journey</a></i>, was released in March 2012 on the PlayStation Store. Their untitled fourth game is currently planned for 2017.</p>
<p>The company focuses on creating video games that provoke emotional responses from players. Its employees have stated that, while they are not opposed to making action-oriented games, they believe that enough such titles are released by the established video game industry. When designing a game, Thatgamecompany employees start by mapping out what they want the player to feel, rather than by establishing <a href="/wiki/Game_mechanics" title="Game mechanics">game mechanics</a>. Employees have stated that the company does not plan to produce large, blockbuster titles, due to their belief that the pressure for high sales would stifle innovation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatgamecompany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late 2005, <a href="/wiki/Jenova_Chen" title="Jenova Chen">Jenova Chen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kellee_Santiago" title="Kellee Santiago">Kellee Santiago</a> began thinking about creating their own video game company. The two were in their final year as master's students in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USC_Interactive_Media_Division" title="USC Interactive Media Division">Interactive Media Program</a> at the <a href="/wiki/USC_School_of_Cinematic_Arts" title="USC School of Cinematic Arts">University of Southern California's School of Cinematic Arts</a>, and had just released a video game—<i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_(video_game)" title="Cloud (video game)">Cloud</a></i>—that they had developed with several other students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIsantint_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GIsantint-1">[1]</a></sup> The group intended the project as an experiment, meant to reveal whether they could create a game that "expressed something different than video games had in the past", and to determine the public's level of interest in video games of that nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEDint_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TEDint-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to the game's strongly positive reception, Chen and Santiago began to consider founding their own company, so that they could continue making games like <i>Cloud</i>—in which the design is not based on <a href="/wiki/Game_mechanics" title="Game mechanics">gameplay mechanics</a>, but on inspiring emotions in players—after they left college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIsantint_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GIsantint-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time, <a href="/wiki/Digital_distribution" title="Digital distribution">digital distribution</a> was gaining popularity. The two saw it as an opportunity to create games without the high financial risk of retail distribution, which they believed would require them to first accumulate funds by working for other video game companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIsantint_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-GIsantint-1">[1]</a></sup> Thatgamecompany was founded on May 15, 2006, as Chen and Santiago finished their master's degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGCstart_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TGCstart-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPRint_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPRint-4">[4]</a></sup> The company soon signed a deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony Computer Entertainment</a>, which had been impressed by Chen's <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Flow_(video_game)" title="Flow (video game)">Flow</a></i>—a component of his master's thesis at USC. Thatgamecompany was contracted to produce three games for the upcoming <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> distribution system, and was given startup funding and a location at Sony's offices in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJedito2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJedito2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Initially, Thatgamecompany consisted of Chen, Santiago, Nick Clark, who had collaborated with Chen on <i>Flow</i>, and John Edwards. Santiago was the president of the company and the <a href="/wiki/Video_game_producer" title="Video game producer">producer</a> for its games, Clark was the <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design#Game_designer" title="Video game design">designer</a>, and Edwards was the <a href="/wiki/Game_programmer" title="Game programmer">lead engineer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGCflowstaff_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-TGCflowstaff-6">[6]</a></sup> Although Chen cofounded the company, he initially worked at <a href="/wiki/Maxis" title="Maxis">Maxis</a> on the game <i><a href="/wiki/Spore_(2008_video_game)" title="Spore (2008 video game)">Spore</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJedito2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJedito2-5">[5]</a></sup> The company considered adapting <i>Cloud</i> as their first product for Sony, but instead decided on <i>Flow</i>, as it was "more fleshed-out as a design". They felt that it would be easier than <i>Cloud</i> to develop while they built the company; no members of the team had experience with managing a business or with creating a commercial game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIsantint_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-GIsantint-1">[1]</a></sup> Several contract workers assisted Thatgamecompany with <i>Flow</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s development, including <a href="/wiki/Austin_Wintory" title="Austin Wintory">Austin Wintory</a>, the game's composer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJedito2_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSJedito2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGCflowstaff_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-TGCflowstaff-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company had believed that the PlayStation 3 version of <i>Flow</i> could be completed in four months, and that it would be ready for the November 2006 launch of the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a>. However, when it was released in February 2007, it did not include "half of the original design".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSchenint_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSchenint-7">[7]</a></sup> According to Santiago, the Sony producer assigned to the team had anticipated that they would underestimate the game's development length, and was not surprised by the delay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIsantint_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-GIsantint-1">[1]</a></sup> The game was well received; it became the most downloaded game on the PlayStation Network in 2007, and was nominated for the Best Downloadable Game of the Year award at the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Interactive_Arts_%26_Sciences" title="Academy of Interactive Arts &amp; Sciences">Academy of Interactive Arts &amp; Sciences</a> Interactive Achievement Awards, and for the Best Innovation award at the 2007 <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Academy of Film and Television Arts</a> (BAFTA) awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VARIETYpiece_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-VARIETYpiece-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIASflowaward_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AIASflowaward-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAFTAflowaward_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BAFTAflowaward-10">[10]</a></sup> After its release, an expansion pack and a <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PlayStation Portable</a> version of the game were created by <a href="/wiki/SuperVillain_Studios" title="SuperVillain Studios">SuperVillain Studios</a>. Thatgamecompany was not involved in the development of either project beyond ensuring that they retained the same <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design" title="Video game design">design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_art_design" title="Game art design">art direction</a> as the original, as they were busy creating their next title, <i><a href="/wiki/Flower_(video_game)" title="Flower (video game)">Flower</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSchenint_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-GSchenint-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Flower</i> was Thatgamecompany's "first game outside the safety net of academia", according to Santiago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAMAflowerdevint_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAflowerdevint-11">[11]</a></sup> Six to nine people were involved at different stages of development. Chen returned to work full-time at the company prior to the game's development, and served as the creative director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAMAflowerdevint2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAflowerdevint2-12">[12]</a></sup> The game's music was composed by Vincent Diamante, who had worked with Chen and Santiago on <i>Cloud</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAMAflowermusic_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAflowermusic-13">[13]</a></sup> The game was developed for two years, but the team spent three-fourths of that time in the prototyping stage. After they decided on the game's elements, <i>Flower</i> was produced in only six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSsantint_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSsantint-14">[14]</a></sup> Like <i>Flow</i>, the game was well received when it was released in February 2009, selling in the top ten PlayStation Network titles of the year and garnering several awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSsantint_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-GSsantint-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAFTAflower_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BAFTAflower-15">[15]</a></sup> After the release of <i>Flower</i>, Thatgamecompany moved into their own building in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSsantint_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-GSsantint-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company's latest project is <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_(2012_video_game)" title="Journey (2012 video game)">Journey</a></i>, which was released on March 13, 2012. It was the final game in Thatgamecompany's three-game contract with Sony, and was developed by a team of fourteen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGCjourneystaff_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-TGCjourneystaff-16">[16]</a></sup> This team did not include Santiago, who, in order to concentrate on her role as the company's president, was replaced as a producer by <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hunicke" title="Robin Hunicke">Robin Hunicke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSint1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSint1-17">[17]</a></sup> The game was in development for three years, despite having been expected to take one year, and the development team faced several problems in expanding the company from seven employees as they began the game to eighteen, and risked running out of money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNpm_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGNpm-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSpm_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSpm-19">[19]</a></sup> Upon release, the game achieved both critical and commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGCawards_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-TGCawards-20">[20]</a></sup> It became the fastest-selling game to date on <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Store" title="PlayStation Store">PlayStation Store</a> in North America and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SONYsales_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SONYsales-21">[21]</a></sup> After the game was released, as the company began work on another project, several employees left for other opportunities. Santiago left the company to pursue other ventures, designer Chris Bell left to form his own studio The Willderness, and Hunicke resigned to work at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiny_Speck" title="Tiny Speck">Tiny Speck</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSsandep_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSsandep-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TShunicke_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-TShunicke-23">[23]</a></sup> Chen attributes the exodus to the end of Thatgamecompany's three-game contract, and to the fact that the company had run out of money to pay employees, mandating an unpaid hiatus until the revenue from <i>Journey</i> came in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EDGE2013_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-EDGE2013-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the money from <i>Journey</i> began to arrive, Thatgamecompany brought back several of the employees affected by the cashflow problems, and some new developers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EDGE2013_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-EDGE2013-24">[24]</a></sup> The company, with its contract with Sony complete, raised $5.5 million in venture capitalist funding, which they hope to use to develop future games for multiple platforms without influences by publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSfunding_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSfunding-25">[25]</a></sup> The team has been working since the release of <i>Journey</i> on a new, unannounced game, and as of June 2013 was made up of around 12 people, only half of whom worked on <i>Journey</i>. Thatgamecompany hopes to release the game on "as many platforms as possible", and to include touch controls in an innovative way in the same way their previous games included tilting the controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EDGE2013_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-EDGE2013-24">[24]</a></sup> On May 27, 2014 it was reported that the next game from Thatgamecompany had received $7 million in funding from Capital Today and a team of other investors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Funding_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Funding-26">[26]</a></sup> While the unnamed game has been in development, Thatgamecompany has re-released <i>Flow</i> and <i>Flower</i> onto both the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a> and the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Vita" title="PlayStation Vita">PlayStation Vita</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vitaports_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vitaports-27">[27]</a></sup> and an updated port of <i>Journey</i> for the PlayStation 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Journeyps4_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Journeyps4-28">[28]</a></sup> The game was teased in November 2016 as being a multiplatform, mutiplayer game "about giving" for release in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giving_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-giving-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatgamecompany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Thatgamecompany designs a game, they begin by deciding on the emotions and feelings they wish to invoke in the player. This differs from the approach of most developers, who build from game mechanics or genre features. According to Santiago, the company creates emotional responses to demonstrate the wide range of possible experiences in video games, which she believes is larger than the few—excitement and fear, for example—that are typically presented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBCsantint_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBCsantint-30">[30]</a></sup> Chen has said that the company's games are meant to evoke emotions more than a message; he specifically changed the design of <i>Flower</i> when early testers felt that the game promoted green energy. Chen believes that he is "too young" to make a game with a strong message, and so designs the company's products to avoid overt meanings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GINFint_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-GINFint-31">[31]</a></sup> Santiago has said that Thatgamecompany's goal is "to create games that push the boundaries of videogames as a communicative medium, and to create games that appeal to a wide variety of people". She hopes to change the video game industry with this process, so that other companies approach video games as a "creative medium" instead of a mass product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISint_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISint-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thatgamecompany's employees are not opposed to making action titles, and, as a break from their regular projects, have internally created "exciting" games that were well received by Sony. However, Chen believes that there is no reason for the company to commercially produce such games, as they would not be creating new ideas that justified the cost of remaining an independent studio, as opposed to working for existing game developers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSchenint_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-GSchenint-7">[7]</a></sup> Similarly, Chen does not intend for Thatgamecompany to make "big budget blockbuster games", as he believes that the financial pressure would stifle innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIint_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-GIint-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thatgamecompany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Flow_(video_game)" title="Flow (video game)">Flow</a></i>, the player navigates a series of <a href="/wiki/2D_computer_graphics" title="2D computer graphics">two-dimensional</a> planes with an aquatic <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganism</a> that evolves by consuming other microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGreview_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-EGreview-34">[34]</a></sup> The game's design is based on Chen's research into <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_game_difficulty_balancing" title="Dynamic game difficulty balancing">dynamic difficulty adjustment</a> at the University of Southern California, and on psychologist <a href="/wiki/Mihaly_Csikszentmihalyi" title="Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi">Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi</a>'s theoretical concept of mental immersion or <a href="/wiki/Flow_(psychology)" title="Flow (psychology)">flow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSint_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSint-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJedito_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJedito-36">[36]</a></sup> It was released for the PlayStation 3 on February 22, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flowreleasedate_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flowreleasedate-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Flower_(video_game)" title="Flower (video game)">Flower</a></i> was intended as a <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_successor" title="Spiritual successor">spiritual successor</a> to <i>Flow</i>. Using the PlayStation 3's motion sensitive controller, the player controls wind that blows a flower <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petal</a> through the air. Flying close to flowers results in the player's petal being followed by other flower petals. Approaching flowers may also have side-effects on the game world, such as bringing vibrant color to previously dead fields or activating stationary windmills. The game features no text or dialogue, forming a narrative arc primarily through visual representation and emotional cues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPreview_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPreview-38">[38]</a></sup> It was released for the PlayStation 3 on February 12, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flowerreleasedate_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flowerreleasedate-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Journey_(2012_video_game)" title="Journey (2012 video game)">Journey</a></i> is Thatgamecompany's latest game. The player controls a robed figure who wakes up in a desert, with a large mountain in the distance as their destination. While traveling, the player can encounter other players over the Internet, one at a time. Players cannot communicate verbally, but may help each other or not as they wish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBCsantint_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBCsantint-30">[30]</a></sup> The game was released for the PlayStation 3 on March 13, 2012. Austin Wintory was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in 2013 for Best Score Soundtrack for <i>Journey</i>, the first such nomination for a full video game score, but lost to Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo_(2011_film)" title="The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2011 film)">The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Journeyreleasedate_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Journeyreleasedate-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
