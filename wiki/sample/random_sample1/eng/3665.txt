<p>The <b>northern lapwing</b> (<i>Vanellus vanellus</i>), also known as the <b>peewit</b>, <b>pewit</b>, <b>tuit</b> or <b>tew-it</b> (imitative of its cry), <b>green plover</b> (emphasising the colour of the plumage) or (in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>) just <b>lapwing</b> (which refers to its pretence of injury by dragging its wing to lure predators away from the nest), is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> family. It is common through temperate <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>.</p>
<p>It is highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> over most of its extensive range, wintering further south as far as north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It migrates mainly by day, often in large flocks. Lowland breeders in westernmost areas of Europe are resident. It occasionally is a vagrant to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, especially after storms, as in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> sightings after storms in December 1927 and in January 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Godfrey_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Godfrey-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> which breeds on cultivated land and other short vegetation habitats. 3–4 eggs are laid in a ground scrape. The nest and young are defended noisily and aggressively against all intruders, up to and including <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.</p>
<p>In winter, it forms huge flocks on open land, particularly <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mud-flats</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>lapwing</i> has been variously attributed to the "lapping" sound its wings make in flight, from the irregular progress in flight due to its large wings (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OED" title="OED">OED</a> derives this from an Old English word meaning "to totter"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">[3]</a></sup> or from its habit of drawing potential predators away from its nest by trailing a wing as if broken.</p>
<p>The scientific name <i>Vanellus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> for the northern lapwing and derives from <i>vannus</i> a <a href="/wiki/Winnowing#In_Greek_culture" title="Winnowing">winnowing fan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-job_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern lapwing is a 28–33 cm (11–13 in) long bird with a 67–87 cm (26–34 in) wingspan and a body mass of 128–330 g (4.5–11.6 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oiseaux-birds_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-oiseaux-birds-5">[5]</a></sup> It has rounded wings and a crest. It is also the shortest-legged of the lapwings. It is mainly black and white, but the back is tinted green. The male has a long crest and a black crown, throat and breast contrasting with an otherwise white face. Females and young birds have shorter crests, and have less strongly marked heads, but plumages are otherwise quite similar.</p>

<p>This is a vocal bird in the breeding season, with constant calling as the crazed tumbling display flight is performed by the male. The typical contact call is a loud, shrill "pee-wit" from which they get their other name of <b>peewit</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">[3]</a></sup> Displaying males usually make a wheezy "pee-wit, wit wit, eeze wit" during their display flight, these birds also make squeaking or mewing sounds.</p>
<p>It feeds primarily on insects and other small invertebrates. This species often feeds in mixed flocks with <a href="/wiki/European_golden_plover" title="European golden plover">golden plovers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_gull" title="Black-headed gull">black-headed gulls</a>, the latter often robbing the two plovers, but providing a degree of protection against predators.</p>
<p>Like the golden plovers, this species prefers to feed at night when there is moonlight.</p>
<p>The northern lapwing is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_decline">Population decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Population decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>National surveys of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> have shown a population decline between 1987 and 1998<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed for the whole sentence (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The numbers of this species have been adversely affected by intensive agricultural techniques. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowland" title="Lowland">lowlands</a> this includes the loss of rough grassland, conversion to <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> or improved grassland, loss of mixed farms, and switch from spring to autumn sown crops. In the uplands, the losses may have been due to increases in <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> density.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> gives grant aid to help restore lapwing habitat within its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" title="Environmental Stewardship">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme. The organisation suggests an option within this scheme called 'Fallow plots for ground-nesting birds'. Uncropped plots at least 2 ha (4.9 acres) in size provide nesting habitat and are located in suitable arable fields, which provide additional foraging habitat.</p>
<p>Locating the plots within 2 km (1.2 mi) of extensively grazed grassland will provide additional foraging habitat. The plots are cultivated in the spring to produce a rough fallow, which is retained without the input of fertiliser or pesticides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting_eggs">Harvesting eggs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Harvesting eggs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Plover's eggs" were an expensive <a href="/wiki/Delicacy" title="Delicacy">delicacy</a> in Victorian Europe, mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a>,</i> about aristocratic British society in 1920–40. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, there is a cultural-historical competition to find the first peewit egg of the year (<i>het eerste kievietsei</i>). It is especially popular in the province <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a>, but there are also regional competitions. Gathering peewit eggs is prohibited by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but Friesland was granted an exception for cultural-historical reasons. The Frisian exception was removed in 2005 by a court, which determined that the Frisian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gedeputeerde_Staten" title="Gedeputeerde Staten">executive councillors</a> had not properly followed procedure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walinga2005_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walinga2005-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-court_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-court-8">[8]</a></sup> As of 2006 it is again allowed to look for peewit eggs between 1 March and 9 April, though harvesting those eggs is now forbidden. The first egg of 2008 was found on 3 March, in <a href="/wiki/Eemnes" title="Eemnes">Eemnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utrecht_(province)" title="Utrecht (province)">Utrecht</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NederlandseOmroepStichting_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NederlandseOmroepStichting-9">[9]</a></sup> as was the first egg of 2009 found on 8 March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RadioNetherlands_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-RadioNetherlands-10">[10]</a></sup> Over the last century, the first peewit egg is found earlier and earlier. This is ascribed to both increased use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertiliser" title="Fertiliser">fertiliser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, causing earlier grass growth needed for egg laying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natuurcompendium_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natuurcompendium-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bird referred to in English translations of <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, book 6, as <i><a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garth_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garth-12">[12]</a></sup> is probably the northern lapwing. Tereus is turned into an <i>epops</i> (6.674); Ovid presumably had the <a href="/wiki/Hoopoe" title="Hoopoe">hoopoe</a> in mind, whose crest indicates his royal status and whose long, sharp beak is a symbol of his violent nature.</p>
