<p><i><b>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</b></i> is a 2013 American <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Don_Scardino" title="Don Scardino">Don Scardino</a> and written by <a href="/wiki/John_Francis_Daley" title="John Francis Daley">John Francis Daley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Goldstein_(screenwriter)" title="Jonathan Goldstein (screenwriter)">Jonathan Goldstein</a>, based on a story by <a href="/wiki/Chad_Kultgen" title="Chad Kultgen">Chad Kultgen</a> &amp; Tyler Mitchell, and Daley &amp; Goldstein. The film follows <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a> magician Burt Wonderstone (<a href="/wiki/Steve_Carell" title="Steve Carell">Steve Carell</a>) as he attempts to reunite with his former partner Anton Marvelton (<a href="/wiki/Steve_Buscemi" title="Steve Buscemi">Steve Buscemi</a>) to take on dangerous street magician Steve Gray (<a href="/wiki/Jim_Carrey" title="Jim Carrey">Jim Carrey</a>). It also features <a href="/wiki/Alan_Arkin" title="Alan Arkin">Alan Arkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Wilde" title="Olivia Wilde">Olivia Wilde</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Gandolfini" title="James Gandolfini">James Gandolfini</a>.</p>
<p>The film began development in 2006, when <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a> bought Kultgen's script, "Burt Dickenson: The Most Powerful Magician on Planet Earth". The development process gained momentum when Charles McDougall was hired as director in 2011, but he eventually left the project and was replaced with Scardino. Daley and Goldstein rewrote Kultgen's script which then saw further rewrites from <a href="/wiki/Jason_Reitman" title="Jason Reitman">Jason Reitman</a> in June 2011.</p>
<p>Filming was scheduled to begin in October 2011, in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> but was pushed back to January 2012. On a $30 million budget, filming began on January 10, 2012 in Nevada with filming later moving to Los Angeles. <i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> was released on March 15, 2013, and earned over $27 million. Reviews generally praised Carrey's and Arkin's performances, but criticized the plot's inconsistent tone and predictability. <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)"><i>Variety</i> magazine</a> listed <i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> as one of "Hollywood's biggest box office bombs of 2013" when it had made $27.4 million against a $30 million production cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1982, young Albert is harassed by bullies. His mother gives him a special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_trick" title="Magic trick">magic trick</a> set by veteran magician Rance Holloway as his birthday present. He studies the instructional video and begins to practice some tricks, attracting the attention of a classmate, Anthony. They practice together and eventually become professional magicians Burt Wonderstone and Anton Marvelton, earning them success and an ongoing headlining act at the <a href="/wiki/Bally%27s_Las_Vegas" title="Bally's Las Vegas">Bally's Hotel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>. However, after ten years of performing the same tricks over and over again, Anton is fed up with Burt's ego, which has already cost them previous female assistants, all called "Nicole" in the act. In a rush, Burt enlists production assistant Jane as the new Nicole.</p>
<p>Burt and Anton encounter up-and-coming street magician Steve Gray performing a unique yet horrifying card trick for his TV magic show, <i>Brain Rapist</i>. Audience numbers soon dwindle at Burt and Anton's show, upsetting Bally's owner Doug Munny. Taking a cue from Gray's endurance-based stunts, Anton suggests that he and Burt try a similar tack—locking themselves in a Plexiglas cage called the Hot Box hung above ground. Overconfident, Burt does not prepare for the stunt and almost instantly falls into a panic, causing the stunt to fail and injuring Anton. Anton angrily ends his partnership with Burt, and Jane also quits.</p>
<p>Burt refuses to change his act, staging his two-man show alone to disastrous results. Munny shuts down the production and Burt, having squandered his earnings over the years, is left broke. Despondent, Burt tries to find work and is eventually hired as an entertainer at an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assisted-Living_Facility" title="Assisted-Living Facility">assisted-living facility</a> catering to former Vegas entertainers. There he meets Holloway, who retired several years before because he found that he was no longer happy performing. Holloway counsels Burt about magic, inspiring him to remember the initial wonder that led him to become a magician. Burt is shocked to see Jane—herself an aspiring magician—working for Gray. Appalled by Gray's style, Holloway and Burt polish their own tricks. Jane visits her grandmother at the facility and patches things up with Burt.</p>
<p>Doug is opening a new casino-hotel and is offering a five-year <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> to the winner of a talent search on the casino's opening night. He invites Burt to do a magic show at his son's birthday party, but Gray also appears and tries to upstage Burt with his own tricks. Disgusted by Gray's actions, Jane leaves his show. Burt reconnects with Anton, who has been distributing magic sets in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mitragyna_speciosa" title="Mitragyna speciosa">A drug found in Cambodia called kratom</a> that puts users into a deep sleep gives them an idea for a sensational trick.</p>
<p>At the talent search show, Gray's performance involves him drilling into his brain, claiming it will not affect him. This however, backfires, leaving Gray with <a href="/wiki/Brain_damage" title="Brain damage">brain damage</a>. Holloway then introduces Burt, Anton, and Jane's performance, the "Disappearing Audience". The group secretly sedates the audience with kratom sleeping gas and then transports them to an outside location in the same seating arrangement. The audience awakes and responds with awe, and Doug awards the headlining act to Burt and Anton; they ask Jane to be their opening act. The trio then performs the Disappearing Audience trick again, returning everyone to the casino theater, as now mentally-impaired Gray watches on with the drill bit still in his skull.</p>
<p>As the film ends, the final scene shows how Burt, Anton, Holloway and others transported the audience to the open area, by unceremoniously dragging and transporting the unconscious audience members to the area, and hauling them back to the theater in a moving van.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film started development in 2006 when <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a> bought Chad Kultgen's script, titled "Burt Dickenson: The Most Powerful Magician on Planet Earth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarietyProduction_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-VarietyProduction-7">[7]</a></sup> By September 2010, Carell had joined the film, and the script had been completely rewritten by screenwriting team John Francis Daley and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Goldstein_(screenwriter)" title="Jonathan Goldstein (screenwriter)">Jonathan Goldstein</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarietyProduction_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-VarietyProduction-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Release2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Release2-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarietySep2410_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-VarietySep2410-32">[32]</a></sup> The pair extensively researched the lifestyle of Vegas magicians, taking note of the pressure of performing multiple times a day and the consequence of living within the "Vegas bubble where you're not exposed to the outside world [which] can actually make someone stir-crazy or egotistical."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Release2_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Release2-31">[31]</a></sup> In July 2011, the story was described as following Burt Wonderstone, a formerly successful magician who was overshadowed by a younger, edgier magician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeadlineProduction_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeadlineProduction-33">[33]</a></sup> However, by the time Carrey had joined the project in October 2011, the younger magician character had become simply a rival magician, and Burt was now part of a formerly successful magic duo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarietyOct3011_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-VarietyOct3011-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The script went through many changes during its years of development, with Daley and Goldstein alone writing approximately fifteen drafts over a three and a half year period. There were four different endings, four different final acts including one where Holloway is revealed as a betrayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntDaleyStein_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-IntDaleyStein-20">[20]</a></sup> Discussing the script changes which saw the project enter production, producer Chris Bender said: "...it was tone, finding the right tone, because it was originally written more broadly where certain magic things were happening that wouldn’t actually be real magic and also, over time, the references that we were making in terms of the new school of magic and the old school of magic were becoming dated... Once [Carell] came on board, that’s when things really took off... And then finding the right director, too. These kind of comedies scare directors a lot because you’re taking a chance and you’re going for something bold comedy-wise that either could be a big hit or it could really miss."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CastHerbig2_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-CastHerbig2-14">[14]</a></sup> Scenes of the young Wonderstone being inspired by a video of Rance Holloway, and buying a magic set were based on Daley's own childhood, in which he viewed a similar video of magician <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wilson_(magician)" title="Mark Wilson (magician)">Mark Wilson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntDaleyStein_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-IntDaleyStein-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2011, the film found a director in Charles McDougall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EmpireDirector_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-EmpireDirector-34">[34]</a></sup> but by April 2011, McDougall had left the project with no reason being cited for the departure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDougallExit_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDougallExit-35">[35]</a></sup> In June 2011, <i><a href="/wiki/30_Rock" title="30 Rock">30 Rock</a></i> director <a href="/wiki/Don_Scardino" title="Don Scardino">Don Scardino</a> was confirmed to take the director chair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeadlineProduction_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-DeadlineProduction-33">[33]</a></sup> On June 16, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Reitman" title="Jason Reitman">Jason Reitman</a> was brought aboard the project to perform additional rewrites to the Daley/Goldstein script. Reitman was reportedly paid for several weeks of work, but the extent of his involvement or the script changes was not detailed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReitmanInvolvement_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReitmanInvolvement-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Schur" title="Michael Schur">Michael Schur</a>, who produced and wrote for Carell on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Office" title="The Office">The Office</a></i>, also provided a rewrite of the script.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://variety.com/exec/michael-schur/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Filming had been scheduled to begin in October 2011, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeadlineProduction_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-DeadlineProduction-33">[33]</a></sup> but was pushed back to January 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CastMohr_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-CastMohr-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THRDec13_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-THRDec13-29">[29]</a></sup> with casting of the remaining lead roles occurring throughout October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarietyOct3011_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-VarietyOct3011-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> began on January 10, 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan11A_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan11A-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan11B_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan11B-37">[37]</a></sup> lasted approximately 47 days and had a $30 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeadlineOct3111_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-DeadlineOct3111-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntScardino_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-IntScardino-38">[38]</a></sup> The film was shot on <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">film stock</a> instead of digital because it was decided that the difference in cost between the two was negligible, and the day shooting and color palettes of the magicians' outfits such as black and red were thought to be captured better on the stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntScardino_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-IntScardino-38">[38]</a></sup> Filming started with four days of shooting in Las Vegas, Nevada, with over 400 extras hired to play background pedestrians, tourists and casino workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan11B_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan11B-37">[37]</a></sup> Among the locations used during the Las Vegas shooting are interiors and exteriors at the <a href="/wiki/Bally%27s_Las_Vegas" title="Bally's Las Vegas">Bally's Las Vegas</a> hotel and casino,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan11A_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan11A-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan11B_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan11B-37">[37]</a></sup> and exteriors of the <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas Strip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Las_Vegas" title="Downtown Las Vegas">Downtown Las Vegas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan912_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan912-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fremont_Street" title="Fremont Street">Fremont Street</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Binion%27s_Gambling_Hall_and_Hotel" title="Binion's Gambling Hall and Hotel">Binion's Gambling Hall and Hotel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scardino1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Scardino1-8">[8]</a></sup> Scardino, who had not filmed in Las Vegas since helming an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Tracey_Takes_On..." title="Tracey Takes On...">Tracey Takes On...</a></i> approximately fifteen years earlier, described shooting on location in the city as "an absolute must". Scardino added "We just felt that, to give the movie authenticity, it had to be on the Strip".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scardino1_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Scardino1-8">[8]</a></sup> The first day of shooting saw Carell and Buscemi suspended inside a perspex box, with Wilde and Gandolfini also on set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingJan11B_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingJan11B-37">[37]</a></sup> Carell was filmed on the Strip because Scardino believed his character is "a creature of the Strip," while Carrey was filmed "in and around Fremont Street," where his character felt more at home. Scardino explained the decision to film the characters in these environments, saying the "two different worlds" of Vegas "helps define our talent," with the "ever-changing Strip" providing contrast with "the frozen-in-time aspect of Fremont Street."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scardino1_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Scardino1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>By January 16, 2012, filming moved to Los Angeles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilminginLa2_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FilminginLa2-39">[39]</a></sup> and southern California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scardino1_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Scardino1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilminginLa1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FilminginLa1-40">[40]</a></sup> including the <a href="/wiki/Wadsworth_Theatre" title="Wadsworth Theatre">Wadsworth Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2012DecUpdate_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-2012DecUpdate-22">[22]</a></sup> Filming had concluded by March 13, 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmingEnd1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FilmingEnd1-41">[41]</a></sup> after forty-nine days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MiscLAT_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-MiscLAT-42">[42]</a></sup> When Scardino boarded the project, the script contained several magic tricks that could not realistically occur on stage such as lasers decapitating two individuals and the heads then switching bodies, which would have required the use of computer visual effects to accomplish. Scardino insisted that most of the magic tricks should be credible feats to make the magicians seem more believable, but some tricks still required the use of visual effects. Copperfield served as a technical advisor on the film and developed a live stage illusion for the film's climax, featuring a body switch between Carell's and Buscemi's characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scardino1_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Scardino1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntScardino_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-IntScardino-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MiscLAT_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-MiscLAT-42">[42]</a></sup> Scardino insisted that the illusion should not depend on camera tricks, stating "I wanted one big stage illusion where you go, 'Oh, wow, how'd they do that?'" Copperfield provided instructions on how the trick worked and was present on set during its filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MiscLAT_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-MiscLAT-42">[42]</a></sup> The production also used other magic advisors who ensured that hand movements during tricks were correct, and also served as hand-doubles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntScardino_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-IntScardino-38">[38]</a></sup> Producer Chris Bender said that the film is meant to be set in a fictional history of the world of magic, and so the filmmakers avoided casting many real magicians, so that it would not seem that the film was intended to be set in reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CastHerbig2_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-CastHerbig2-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> premiered as the opening film of the <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">SXSW</a> festival on March 8, 2013; it was introduced by Carell, Carrey and Wilde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Release1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Release1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Release2_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Release2-31">[31]</a></sup> The film received a wide release in North America and the United Kingdom on March 15, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReleaseDate_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReleaseDate-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Total_FilmFeb2013_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Total_FilmFeb2013-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film earned $22.5 million in North America and $4.9 million elsewhere for a worldwide total of $27.4 million, against a budget of $30 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM1-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the week prior to its release in North America, the film was predicted to earn approximately $18 million and finish as the number 2 film of the weekend behind <i><a href="/wiki/Oz_the_Great_and_Powerful" title="Oz the Great and Powerful">Oz the Great and Powerful</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOOpeningPre_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOOpeningPre-46">[46]</a></sup> <i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> earned $3.72 million through its opening day including takings from midnight showings at 1,800 theaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOOpening2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOOpening2-47">[47]</a></sup> During its opening weekend the film earned $10.2 million from 3,160 theaters—an average of $3,221 per theater—ranking third for the weekend behind new release <i><a href="/wiki/The_Call_(2013_film)" title="The Call (2013 film)">The Call</a></i> ($17.1 million) and holdover <i>Oz the Great and Powerful</i> ($41.3 million). The gross was one of the lowest openings for a Carell or Carrey film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOOpening3_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOOpening3-48">[48]</a></sup> In its second weekend, the film's takings dropped by 58% to $4.32 million, putting it in seventh place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BONAWeek2_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-BONAWeek2-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> received mixed reviews from critics. On review aggregation website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> the film has an approval rating of 36% based on 182 reviews, and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">average</a> rating of 5.1/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "<i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> serves up some goofy laughs, but given its outrageous conceit, it's surprisingly safe and predictable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReviewTomatoes_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReviewTomatoes-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> provides a score of 44 out of 100 from 35 critics, indicating "mixed or average reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReviewMC_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReviewMC-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CinemaScore" title="CinemaScore">CinemaScore</a> polls reported that the average grade moviegoers gave the film was a "C+" on a scale of A+ to F.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOOpening_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOOpening-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's plot was a focal point for criticism. Reviewers considered the tone uneven, segueing between dark comedy and family film, the extreme stunts of Carrey's Steve Gray, and sentimentality, and serious drama and farcical comedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEmpire_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevEmpire-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevLATimes_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevLATimes-54">[54]</a></sup> Several reviewers noted that the plot was predictable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevTotalFilm_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevTotalFilm-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEW_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevEW-56">[56]</a></sup> and dated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEbert_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevEbert-57">[57]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Matthew Leyland said that the attention to detail in terms of magician gestures, posture and dialog created a "withering showbiz satire", which is sidelined in favor of sentimentality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevTotalFilm_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevTotalFilm-55">[55]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Owen Gleiberman said that the film is too cautious and unimaginative, choosing "earnest and mushy" over increasingly wild surprise,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEW_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevEW-56">[56]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Helen O'Hara considered the script unfocused and the tone uneven, which undermined Carell's efforts to portray Wonderstone's return to glory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEmpire_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevEmpire-53">[53]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> Stephen Holden said that the film's message was unoriginal and delivered without any special conviction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevNYT_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevNYT-58">[58]</a></sup> In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Roeper" title="Richard Roeper">Richard Roeper</a> said that <i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> is "dark and wickedly funny",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEbert_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevEbert-57">[57]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Joe Leydon said that it neatly balanced sentimentality with edgy comedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVariety_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevVariety-59">[59]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Mary Pols said that while the film does not always work, it did so enough that she thought it could be "the kind of semi-bad, semi-inspired comedy that could not only stand repeated viewings but perhaps improve with them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevTime_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevTime-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carrey's performance was generally praised for his humor and return to the physical style of comedy employed early in his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEbert_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevEbert-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevNYT_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevNYT-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVariety_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevVariety-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVillage_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevVillage-60">[60]</a></sup> Roeper said that Carrey is "physical, he's intense, he's ridiculous—and he made me laugh more than any comedic character in recent memory",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEbert_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-RevEbert-57">[57]</a></sup> and Alan Scherstuhl said that Carrey's "cartoon expressiveness" was used to its most judicious ends in years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVillage_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevVillage-60">[60]</a></sup> Gleiberman said Carrey was one of the funniest components of the film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEW_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevEW-56">[56]</a></sup> and Holden said that Carrey steals every scene he is in with a "take-no-prisoners ferocity that he hasn’t demonstrated in years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevNYT_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevNYT-58">[58]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Ty Burr however said that Carrey is good at being loud, but not funny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevBoston_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevBoston-61">[61]</a></sup> Arkin was also praised, with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Tribune" title="The Chicago Tribune">The Chicago Tribune</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Michael Phillips saying that he "can't save the movie. But he can save his scenes,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevChicago_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-RevChicago-62">[62]</a></sup> Roeper labeled him a marvel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEbert_57-4"><a href="#cite_note-RevEbert-57">[57]</a></sup> and Leydon called his performance scene-stealing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVariety_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevVariety-59">[59]</a></sup> Leyland said that Carell's likability helped carry the sentimental segments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevTotalFilm_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevTotalFilm-55">[55]</a></sup> but Holden considered that his performance gave Wonderstone a soulfulness that undermined the film's farcical aspirations and left the character lacking "a shark's bite".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevNYT_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-RevNYT-58">[58]</a></sup> Leydon said that Buscemi offered an engaging performance as the amiable Anton Marvelton, and Wilde was generally considered to have delivered a fine performance in an underwritten role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevEbert_57-5"><a href="#cite_note-RevEbert-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevNYT_58-4"><a href="#cite_note-RevNYT-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVariety_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-RevVariety-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVillage_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevVillage-60">[60]</a></sup> Pols said that the prospect of Wonderstone attracting Wilde's wholesome and intelligent Jane were absurd, but that Wilde's portrayal was game enough to remove the creepiness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevTime_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-RevTime-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Phillips and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Los_Angeles_Times" title="The Los Angeles Times">The Los Angeles Times</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Kenneth Turan criticized Scardino's direction, saying that the film offers slow and crude scenes over the fleet-footed material directed by Scardino on <i>30 Rock</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevLATimes_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevLATimes-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevChicago_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-RevChicago-62">[62]</a></sup> Leydon conversely said that the director unobtrusively handled the gradual shifts in tone between black comedy and buddy comedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RevVariety_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-RevVariety-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Incredible_Burt_Wonderstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Incredible Burt Wonderstone</i> received three nominations for the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Teen_Choice_Awards" title="Teen Choice Awards">Teen Choice Awards</a>: <i>Choice Movie: Comedy</i>; <i>Choice Movie Actor: Comedy</i> for Steve Carell; and <i>Choice Movie Actress: Comedy</i> for Olivia Wilde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013TeenChoice_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013TeenChoice-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
