<p><i><b>The Tree</b></i> is a French-Australian 2010 film co-produced between Australia and France. It was filmed in the small town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boonah" title="Boonah">Boonah</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia and follows the lives of Dawn (<a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Gainsbourg" title="Charlotte Gainsbourg">Charlotte Gainsbourg</a>) and her four children after the unexpected death of her husband Peter (<a href="/wiki/Aden_Young" title="Aden Young">Aden Young</a>). The film is an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Debut_novel" title="Debut novel">debut novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Father_Who_Art_in_the_Tree" title="Our Father Who Art in the Tree">Our Father Who Art in the Tree</a></i> by Australian writer and performer <a href="/wiki/Judy_Pascoe" title="Judy Pascoe">Judy Pascoe</a>. The film closed the <a href="/wiki/2010_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2010 Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> on 23 May 2010 following the Awards Ceremony and received a seven-minute <a href="/wiki/Standing_ovation" title="Standing ovation">standing ovation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As well as this, The Tree premiered at the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Film_Festival" title="Sydney Film Festival">Sydney Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The film is distributed in the US by <a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist_Films" title="Zeitgeist Films">Zeitgeist Films</a>, opening on 15 July 2011 in New York, on 22 July in Los Angeles, Boston and Washington, D.C., and throughout the country over the summer.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tree_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dawn and Peter O'Neil live together with their children (three boys and a girl), on the outskirts of a small country town. Next to their rambling house stands the kids' favourite playground: a giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moreton_Bay_Fig" title="Moreton Bay Fig">Moreton Bay Fig</a> tree (now known in real-life as the <a href="/wiki/Teviotville_Tree" title="Teviotville Tree">Teviotville Tree</a>), whose branches reach high towards the sky and roots stretch far into the ground.</p>
<p>Everything seems perfect until Peter suffers a heart attack, crashing his car into the tree's trunk. Dawn is devastated, left alone with her grief and four children to raise. Until one day, 8-year-old Simone, reveals a secret to her mother. She's convinced her father whispers to her through the leaves of the tree and he's come back to protect them. Dawn takes comfort from Simone's imagination, and starts to believe in it herself; just like Simone, Dawn also likes to spend time in the tree. It starts to dominate their physical and emotional landscape. But the close bond between mother and Simone forged through a mutual sorrow and shared secret, is threatened by the arrival of George, the plumber, called in to remove the tree's troublesome roots. As the relationship between Dawn and George blossoms, the tree continues to grow, with its branches infiltrating the house, its roots destroy the foundations. Dawn decides the tree has to go. George and some other workmen arrive, but Simone climbs in the tree to defend it. Dawn and George try to convince her, but she refuses to come down. George argues to Dawn that the girl is only 8 years old, Dawn should not allow her to stop the necessary removal of the tree. This irritates Dawn, and she cancels the operation, and tells George she does not want to see him again.</p>
<p>In a big storm the house is demolished by the tree, and the family leaves the area, planning to start living somewhere else, perhaps in a tent.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tree_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Tree</i> was written and directed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Julie_Bertuccelli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Julie Bertuccelli (page does not exist)">Julie Bertuccelli</a>, it is based on the screenplay by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_J_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elizabeth J Mars (page does not exist)">Elizabeth J Mars</a>, produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sue_Taylor" title="Sue Taylor">Sue Taylor</a> of Taylor Media, Yael Fogiel and Laetitia Gonzalez of <a href="/wiki/Les_Films_du_Poisson" title="Les Films du Poisson">Les Films du Poisson</a>, and is a co-production between Australia and France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It came to be a co-production when Julie Bertuccelli was given the book <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Father_Who_Art_in_the_Tree" title="Our Father Who Art in the Tree">Our Father Who Art in the Tree</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> by a close friend. When she looked into getting the rights for the film she found that Australian producer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sue_Taylor" title="Sue Taylor">Sue Taylor</a> already had them, however she did not have a director. It just so happened that Julie is a director, and from there the co-production was born. The tree used in the film is <a href="/wiki/Teviotville_Tree" title="Teviotville Tree">Teviotville Tree</a>, located in the small town of Teviotville in the state of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>. It has a 34 m spread, 20 m height and 2.31 m diameter at 1 m above ground, which is the narrowest point. The tree has low branches which have not been pruned off, and when they are laden with fruit they reach the ground. It is very rare to find this in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moreton_Bay_Fig" title="Moreton Bay Fig">Moreton Bay Fig</a> tree. It is estimated that it was planted in 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
