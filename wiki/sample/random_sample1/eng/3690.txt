<p><b>The Palace of Auburn Hills</b>, commonly referred to as <b>The Palace</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Sports_in_Detroit" title="Sports in Detroit">sports</a> and entertainment venue in the <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> suburb of <a href="/wiki/Auburn_Hills,_Michigan" title="Auburn Hills, Michigan">Auburn Hills, Michigan</a>. The arena opened in 1988 and is the home of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Pistons" title="Detroit Pistons">Detroit Pistons</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> (NBA). It was the home of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Shock" title="Detroit Shock">Detroit Shock</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_National_Basketball_Association" title="Women's National Basketball Association">Women's National Basketball Association</a> (WNBA), the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Vipers" title="Detroit Vipers">Detroit Vipers</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Hockey_League_(1945-2001)" title="International Hockey League (1945-2001)">International Hockey League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Safari" title="Detroit Safari">Detroit Safari</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Indoor_Soccer_League" title="Continental Indoor Soccer League">Continental Indoor Soccer League</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Fury" title="Detroit Fury">Detroit Fury</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arena_Football_League_(1987-2008)" title="Arena Football League (1987-2008)">Arena Football League</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Palace_of_Auburn_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1957 to 1978, the Pistons competed in Detroit's <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Olympia" title="Detroit Olympia">Olympia Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calihan_Hall" title="Calihan Hall">Memorial Building</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cobo_Center#Cobo_Arena" title="Cobo Center">Cobo Arena</a>. In 1978, owner <a href="/wiki/Bill_Davidson_(businessman)" title="Bill Davidson (businessman)">Bill Davidson</a> elected not to share the new <a href="/wiki/Joe_Louis_Arena" title="Joe Louis Arena">Joe Louis Arena</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Red_Wings" title="Detroit Red Wings">Detroit Red Wings</a>, and instead chose to relocate the team to the <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Silverdome" title="Pontiac Silverdome">Pontiac Silverdome</a>, a venue constructed for <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a>, where they remained for the next decade. While the Silverdome could accommodate massive crowds, it offered substandard sight lines for basketball viewing. A group led by Davidson bought vacant land in Auburn Hills from Joseph Shewach and built The Palace there for the relatively low cost of $70 million, using entirely private funding. The Davidson family held a controlling interest in the arena until <a href="/wiki/Tom_Gores" title="Tom Gores">Tom Gores</a> bought it as part of his purchase of the Pistons in 2011.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basketball">Basketball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Palace_of_Auburn_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basketball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The arena opened in time for the Pistons' first NBA championship season, in 1988–1989. Since then, when one of The Palace's basketball occupants has won a championship, the number on its address has changed. Its current address is 6 Championship Drive, reflecting the Pistons' three NBA titles and the Detroit Shock's three WNBA titles (the Detroit Vipers' 1997 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Hockey_League_(1945-2001)" title="International Hockey League (1945-2001)">Turner Cup</a> championship has not been officially recognized in the arena's address; the address also remained unchanged despite the Shock's move to <a href="/wiki/Tulsa_Shock" title="Tulsa Shock">Tulsa, Oklahoma</a> in 2010). The original address was 3777 Lapeer Road.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_concerts">Notable concerts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Palace_of_Auburn_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable concerts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a> performed during his <a href="/wiki/...Nothing_Like_the_Sun" title="...Nothing Like the Sun">...Nothing Like the Sun</a> Tour on August 13, 1988, becoming the very first musical act to perform at The Palace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> performed three consecutive sold–out shows, during his <a href="/wiki/Bad_(tour)" title="Bad (tour)">Bad World Tour</a> on October 24–26, 1988 in front of 70,000 people, becoming the first music artist to sell out The Palace more than two nights in a row.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a> performed two consecutive shows, during their <a href="/wiki/Wish_(The_Cure_album)" title="Wish (The Cure album)">Wish</a> Tour on July 18–19, 1992, with The <a href="/wiki/Cranes_(band)" title="Cranes (band)">Cranes</a> as their opening act. The shows were recorded and released as a live album, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Show_(The_Cure_album)" title="Show (The Cure album)">Show</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grand_Funk_Railroad" title="Grand Funk Railroad">Grand Funk Railroad</a> performed a benefit show for the nation of <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> in March 1997 during their Reunion Tour. The show also featured <a href="/wiki/Peter_Frampton" title="Peter Frampton">Peter Frampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alto_Reed" title="Alto Reed">Alto Reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Shaffer" title="Paul Shaffer">Paul Shaffer</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Detroit Symphony Orchestra">Detroit Symphony Orchestra</a>. The performance was recorded, and released as the double-live <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_(album)" title="Bosnia (album)">Bosnia</a> album in October of that year.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Demi_Lovato" title="Demi Lovato">Demi Lovato</a> performed a show at the Palace on March 13, 2014, for her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neon_Lights_Tour" title="Neon Lights Tour">Neon Lights Tour</a>. The show was recorded for a <a href="/wiki/Vevo" title="Vevo">Vevo</a> Presents.</p>
<p>The Palace was the site of an assassination attempt on <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>, while he was on tour, with former band mate <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a>, during their <a href="/wiki/No_Quarter:_Jimmy_Page_and_Robert_Plant_Unledded" title="No Quarter: Jimmy Page and Robert Plant Unledded">No Quarter</a> Tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On March 31, 1995, Lance Alworth Cunningham, a 23-year-old, who thought that Led Zeppelin music contained "satanic messages", tried rushing the stage with a knife. He waited until the song "<a href="/wiki/Kashmir_(song)" title="Kashmir (song)">Kashmir</a>" started and then made his charge for the stage, waving the weapon. The man was tackled by patrons and security about 50 feet from the stage.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> performed two sold–out shows during her <a href="/wiki/Drowned_World_Tour" title="Drowned World Tour">Drowned World Tour</a> on August 25–26, 2001. The shows were recorded and broadcast live on <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> and were later released as a DVD, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Drowned_World_Tour_2001" title="Drowned World Tour 2001">Drowned World Tour 2001</a></i>.</p>
<p>The Palace played host to the politically motivated <a href="/wiki/Vote_for_Change" title="Vote for Change">Vote for Change</a> Tour on October 3, 2004, featuring performances by <a href="/wiki/My_Morning_Jacket" title="My Morning Jacket">My Morning Jacket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_5" title="Jurassic 5">Jurassic 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Harper" title="Ben Harper">Ben Harper</a> &amp; The Innocent Criminals and headliner The <a href="/wiki/Dave_Matthews_Band" title="Dave Matthews Band">Dave Matthews Band</a>, with unannounced guest <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Malice_at_the_Palace">The Malice at the Palace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Palace_of_Auburn_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Malice at the Palace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacers%E2%80%93Pistons_brawl" title="Pacers–Pistons brawl">Pacers–Pistons brawl</a></div>
<p>On November 19, 2004, a fight broke out between members of the NBA's Detroit Pistons and <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Pacers" title="Indiana Pacers">Indiana Pacers</a>. As the on-court fight died down, a fan threw a cup of <a href="/wiki/Diet_Coke" title="Diet Coke">Diet Coke</a> at Pacers forward <a href="/wiki/Metta_World_Peace" title="Metta World Peace">Ron Artest</a>, who then rushed into the crowd, sparking a melee between players and spectators. The fight resulted in the suspension of nine players, criminal charges against five players, and criminal charges against five spectators. The offending fans were banned from attending games at The Palace. In the aftermath of the fight, the NBA decided to increase the security presence between players and spectators. The fact that the fight took place at The Palace of Auburn Hills led to it becoming colloquially referred to as "The Malice at the Palace" and "Basketbrawl".</p>
<p>The Palace was also the site of a brawl between the WNBA's <a href="/wiki/Sparks%E2%80%93Shock_brawl" title="Sparks–Shock brawl">Shock and Sparks</a> on July 21, 2008.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Palace_of_Auburn_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2016, it was reported that the Pistons' ownership were negotiating a possible relocation to <a href="/wiki/Little_Caesars_Arena" title="Little Caesars Arena">Little Caesars Arena</a>, a new multi-purpose venue being constructed in midtown Detroit by <a href="/wiki/Olympia_Entertainment" title="Olympia Entertainment">Olympia Entertainment</a> to replace Joe Louis Arena as home of the NHL's Detroit Red Wings, as soon as the 2017–18 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gores_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gores-13">[13]</a></sup> On November 22, 2016, it was officially announced that the Pistons would play at Little Caesars Arena in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Pistons final regular season game at The Palace of Auburn Hills is scheduled for April 10, 2017 against the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Wizards" title="Washington Wizards">Washington Wizards</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facility_information">Facility information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Palace_of_Auburn_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Facility information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Then-Pistons owner William Davidson and two developers privately financed the $90 million construction of The Palace, and did not have to use public funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Palace was built with 180 <a href="/wiki/Luxury_box" title="Luxury box">luxury suites</a>, considered an exorbitant number when it opened, but it has consistently managed to lease virtually all of them. In December 2005, the Palace added five underground luxury suites, each containing 450 square feet (42 m<sup>2</sup>) of space and renting for $450,000 per year. Eight more luxury suites, also located below arena level, were opened in February 2006. They range in size from 800 to 1,200 square feet (74 to 111 m<sup>2</sup>) and rent for $350,000 annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The architectural design of the Palace, including its multiple tiers of luxury suites, has been used as the basis for many other professional sports arenas in North America since its construction, including the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Tire_Centre" title="Canadian Tire Centre">Canadian Tire Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, also designed by Rossetti Associates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPN_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Palace is widely considered to be the first of the modern-style NBA arenas, and its large number of luxury suites was a major reason for the building boom of new NBA arenas in the 1990s. Although the Palace is now one of the oldest arenas in the NBA, its foresighted design contains the amenities that most NBA teams have sought in new arenas built since that time. By contrast, of the other NBA venues that opened in 1988-89, <a href="/wiki/Amway_Arena" title="Amway Arena">Amway Arena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Coliseum" title="Charlotte Coliseum">Charlotte Coliseum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Miami_Arena" title="Miami Arena">Miami Arena</a> have been demolished, while the <a href="/wiki/Bradley_Center" title="Bradley Center">Bradley Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sleep_Train_Arena" title="Sleep Train Arena">Sleep Train Arena</a> are either undergoing major renovations or slated for replacement. All of these arenas were rendered obsolete by the lack of luxury suites and <a href="/wiki/Club_seating" title="Club seating">club seating</a>, lucrative revenue-generating features that made pro sports teams financially successful in order to remain competitive long-term, and also being located in suburban rather than downtown areas (The Palace, however, has remained one of the successful suburban arenas).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPN_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, Palace Sports &amp; Entertainment (PS&amp;E) has spent $117.5 million in upgrades and renovations to keep the arena updated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-17">[17]</a></sup> A new High-Definition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JumboTron" title="JumboTron">JumboTron</a> monitor, new LED video monitors, and more than 950 feet (290 m) of ribbon display technology from <a href="/wiki/Daktronics" title="Daktronics">Daktronics</a> was installed in the mid-2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scoreboard_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-scoreboard-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Pistons court was previously named the "William Davidson Court", in honor of the late owner, prior to the home opener on October 28, 2009; however, Davidson's signature, along with the retired numbers, were removed from the hardwood when <a href="/wiki/Tom_Gores" title="Tom Gores">Tom Gores</a> took over ownership of the Palace, and were re-retired instead atop the Palace rafters as replacement banners.</p>
<p>The Palace is one of only two <a href="/wiki/List_of_National_Basketball_Association_arenas" title="List of National Basketball Association arenas">NBA arenas</a> which has not sold its naming rights to a corporate sponsor. The other is <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a>, named after U.S. President <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a>. The Palace is one of just eight basketball arenas owned by their respective NBA franchises.</p>
