<p>The <b>Douglas DC-4</b> is a four-engine (piston) propeller-driven <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a> developed by the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Aircraft_Company" title="Douglas Aircraft Company">Douglas Aircraft Company</a>. Military versions of the plane, the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-54_Skymaster" title="Douglas C-54 Skymaster">C-54</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R5D" title="R5D">R5D</a>, served during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Airlift" title="Berlin Airlift">Berlin Airlift</a> and into the 1960s. From 1945, many civil airlines operated the DC-4 worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_DC-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4E" title="Douglas DC-4E">DC-4E</a> proved to be complicated to maintain and uneconomical to operate, Douglas responded to requests by <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Air_Lines" title="United Air Lines">United Air Lines</a> for a smaller and simpler replacement. Before the definitive DC-4 could enter service, the entry of the United States into World War II caused production to be channeled to its military, with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> aircraft designated <b><a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-54_Skymaster" title="Douglas C-54 Skymaster">C-54 Skymaster</a></b> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> aircraft designated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R5D" title="R5D">R5D</a>. The first, a C-54, flew from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clover_Field" title="Clover Field">Clover Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica, California</a> on 14 February 1942.</p>
<p>A total of 1,163 C-54/R5Ds were built for the United States military between 1942 and January 1946; another 79 DC-4s were built postwar. A variant was also built in Canada postwar as the <a href="/wiki/Canadair_North_Star" title="Canadair North Star">Canadair North Star</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_DC-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The DC-4/C-54 proved a popular and reliable type, 1245 being built between May 1942 and August 1947, including 79 postwar DC-4s. Several remain in service as of 2014. One current operator is <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Airways" title="Buffalo Airways">Buffalo Airways</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellowknife,_Northwest_Territories" title="Yellowknife, Northwest Territories">Yellowknife, Northwest Territories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Douglas continued to develop the type during the war in preparation for a return to airline use when peace returned. The type's sales prospects were affected when 500 wartime ex military C-54s and R5Ds came onto the civil market, many being converted to DC-4 standard by Douglas. DC-4s were a favorite of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charter_airline" title="Charter airline">charter airlines</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_Airlines" title="Great Lakes Airlines">Great Lakes Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_American_Airlines" title="North American Airlines">North American Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universal_Airlines_(United_States)" title="Universal Airlines (United States)">Universal Airlines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transocean_Airlines" title="Transocean Airlines">Transocean Airlines</a>. In the 1950s Transocean (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>) was the largest civil C-54/DC-4 operator.</p>

<p>Douglas produced 79 new-build DC-4s between January 1946 and August 9, 1947, the last example being delivered to South African Airways. <a href="/wiki/Cabin_pressurization" title="Cabin pressurization">Pressurization</a> was an option, but all civil DC-4s (and C-54s) were built un-pressurized.</p>
<p>Purchasers of new-build DC-4s included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_American_Airways" title="Pan American Airways">Pan American Airways</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Airlines_(NA)" title="National Airlines (NA)">National Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Airlines" title="Western Airlines">Western Airlines</a> in the USA, and <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> Royal Dutch Air Lines, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Airlines_System" title="Scandinavian Airlines System">Scandinavian Airlines System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a> Airlines of Spain, <a href="/wiki/Swissair" title="Swissair">Swissair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabena" title="Sabena">Sabena</a> Belgian World Airlines, <a href="/wiki/Cubana_de_Aviaci%C3%B3n" title="Cubana de Aviación">Cubana de Aviación</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avianca" title="Avianca">Avianca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerol%C3%ADneas_Argentinas" title="Aerolíneas Argentinas">Aerolíneas Argentinas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeropostal" title="Aeropostal">Aeropostal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> (1946) and <a href="/wiki/South_African_Airways" title="South African Airways">South African Airways</a> overseas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Several airlines used new-build DC-4s to start scheduled transatlantic flights between Latin America and Europe. Among the earliest were Aerolíneas Argentinas (1946), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeropostal" title="Aeropostal">Aeropostal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> (1946), <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a> Airlines of Spain (1946), and <a href="/wiki/Cubana_de_Aviaci%C3%B3n" title="Cubana de Aviación">Cubana de Aviación</a> (1948).</p>
<p>Basic prices for a new DC-4 in 1946-7 was around £140,000-£160,000. In 1960 used DC-4s were available for around £80,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Survivors">Survivors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_DC-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Survivors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="489" data-file-width="800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg/240px-Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg/360px-Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg/480px-Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Douglas_DC-4_Flying_Dutchman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A DC-4 painted in the KLM "Flying Dutchman" scheme of the Dutch Dakota Association, Lelystad, Holland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Very few DC-4s remain in service today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The last two passenger DC-4s operating worldwide are based in Johannesburg, South Africa. They fly with old <a href="/wiki/South_African_Airways" title="South African Airways">South African Airways</a> (SAA) colors. They are ZS-AUB "Outeniqua" and ZS-BMH "Lebombo" and are owned by the South African Airways Museum Society<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outeniqua_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Outeniqua-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebombo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lebombo-8">[8]</a></sup> and operated by Skyclass Aviation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> a company specializing in classic and VIP charters to exotic destinations in Africa. A 1944 built DC-4 is currently being restored in New South Wales, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Airways" title="Buffalo Airways">Buffalo Airways</a> in Canada's Northwest Territories owns roughly a dozen DC-4s (former C-54s of various versions) 4 for hauling cargo and 3 for aerial firefighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A 1945 built DC-4 (C-54E) c/n 27370 is currently operating as a flying museum to the Berlin Airlift. Called the "Spirit of Freedom", it has been touring the world for nearly 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Alaska Air Fuel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> also operates two DC4's out of Palmer, Alaska. One ex-Buffalo DC4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (N55CW c/n 10673, currently registered to AIRCRAFT GUARANTY CORP TRUSTEE) is fitted with spray bars on top of the wings and is currently based in Florida on standby for oil pollution control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_.28DC-4-1009.29">Specifications (DC-4-1009)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_DC-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Specifications (DC-4-1009)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DC4_Silh.jpg"><img alt="DC4 Silh.jpg" data-file-height="1185" data-file-width="951" height="374" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/DC4_Silh.jpg/300px-DC4_Silh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/DC4_Silh.jpg/450px-DC4_Silh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/DC4_Silh.jpg/600px-DC4_Silh.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> four</li>
<li><b>Capacity:</b> 40 to 80 passengers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boeing_history_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boeing_history-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 93 ft 10 in (28.6 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 117 ft 6 in (35.8 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 27 ft 6 in (8.38 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 1,460ft² (135.6 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 43,300 lb (19,640 kg)</li>
<li><b>Loaded weight:</b> 63,500 lb (28,800 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 73,000 lb (33,100 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 4 × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_R-2000" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2000">Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2000</a> <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engine</a>, 1,450 hp (1,081 kW) each</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 280 mph (450 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Vc" title="V speeds">Cruise speed</a>:</b> 227 mph/197kts (365 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 4,250 mi (6,839 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 22,300 ft (6,800 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 43.5 lb/ft² (212.4 kg/m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">Power/mass</a>:</b> 10.9 lb/hp (6.6 kg/kW)</li>
</ul>
