<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Antenna (biology)</h1>

<p><b>Antennae</b> (singular: <b>antenna</b>), sometimes referred to as "feelers," are paired <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendages</a> used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_system" title="Sensory system">sensing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>.</p>
<p>Antennae are connected to the first one or two <a href="/wiki/Segmentation_(biology)" title="Segmentation (biology)">segments</a> of the arthropod head. They vary widely in form, but are always made of one or more jointed segments. While they are typically sensory organs, the exact nature of what they sense and how they sense it is not the same in all groups. Functions may variously include sensing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactition" title="Tactition">touch</a>, air motion, heat, vibration (sound), and especially <a href="/wiki/Insect_olfaction" title="Insect olfaction">smell</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustation" title="Gustation">taste</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Insects_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Insects-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crustacea_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crustacea-2">[2]</a></sup> Antennae are sometimes modified for other purposes, such as mating, brooding, swimming, and even anchoring the arthropod to a substrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crustacea_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crustacea-2">[2]</a></sup> Larval arthropods have antennae that differ from those of the adult. Many crustaceans, for example, have free-swimming larval forms that use their antennae for swimming.</p>
<p>The common ancestor of all arthropods likely had one pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uniramous" title="Uniramous">uniramous</a> (unbranched) antenna-like structures, followed by one or more pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biramous" title="Biramous">biramous</a> (having two major branches) leg-like structures, as seen in some modern <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and fossil <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">trilobites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Except the <a href="/wiki/Chelicerata" title="Chelicerata">chelicerates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protura" title="Protura">proturans</a>, which have none, all non-crustacean arthropods have a single pair of antennae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crustaceans">Crustaceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antenna_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Crustaceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="548" data-file-width="649" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg/220px-Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg/330px-Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg/440px-Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barnacle_anatomy_-_antenna_highlighted.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cutaway diagram of a barnacle, with antennae highlighted by arrow</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">Crustaceans</a> bear two pairs of antennae. The pair attached to the first segment of the head are called <b>primary antennae</b> or <b>antennules</b>. This pair is generally uniramous, but is biramous in <a href="/wiki/Malacostraca" title="Malacostraca">crabs and lobsters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Remipedia" title="Remipedia">remipedes</a>. The pair attached to the second segment are called <b>secondary antennae</b> or simply <b>antennae</b>. The second antennae are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plesiomorphic" title="Plesiomorphic">plesiomorphically</a> biramous, but many species later evolved uniramous pairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crustacea_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Crustacea-2">[2]</a></sup> The second antennae may be significantly reduced (e.g. remipedes), apparently absent (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnacles" title="Barnacles">barnacles</a>), or modified to such an extent that they no longer resemble antennae (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Spiny_lobster" title="Spiny lobster">spiny lobsters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slipper_lobster" title="Slipper lobster">slipper lobsters</a>).</p>
<p>The subdivisions of crustacean antennae have many names, including flagellomeres (a shared term with insects), annuli, articles, and segments. The terminal ends of crustacean antennae have two major categorizations: segmented and flagellate. An antenna is considered segmented if each of the annuli is separate from those around it and has individual muscle attachments. Flagellate antennae, on the other hand, have muscle attachments only around the base, acting as a hinge for the flagellum—a flexible string of annuli with no muscle attachment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are several notable non-sensory uses of antennae in crustaceans. Many crustaceans have a mobile <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larval</a> stage called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauplius_(larva)" title="Nauplius (larva)">nauplius</a>, which is characterized by its use of antennae for swimming. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnacles" title="Barnacles">Barnacles</a>, a highly modified crustacean, use their antennae to attach to rocks and other surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crustacea_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Crustacea-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insects">Insects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antenna_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Insects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insect_anatomy#Antennae" title="Insect anatomy">Insect anatomy § Antennae</a>.</div>

<p>Insects evolved from prehistoric crustaceans, and they have secondary antennae like crustaceans, but not primary antennae. Antennae are the primary <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">olfactory</a> sensors of insects<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and are accordingly well-equipped with a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Sensillum" title="Sensillum">sensilla</a> (singular: <i>sensillum</i>). Paired, mobile, and segmented, they are located between the eyes on the forehead. Embryologically, they represent the appendages of the second head segment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gullan.26Cranston_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gullan.26Cranston-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>All insects have antennae, however they may be greatly reduced in the larval forms. Amongst the non-insect classes of the <a href="/wiki/Hexapoda" title="Hexapoda">Hexapoda</a>, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collembola" title="Collembola">Collembola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diplura" title="Diplura">Diplura</a> have antenna, but <a href="/wiki/Protura" title="Protura">Protura</a> do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chapman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-chapman-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antenna_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1280" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg/220px-Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg/330px-Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg/440px-Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vespula_vulgaris_SEM_Antenna_03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scanning_Electron_Microscope" title="Scanning Electron Microscope">Electron micrograph</a> of antenna surface detail of a wasp <i>(<a href="/wiki/Vespula_vulgaris" title="Vespula vulgaris">Vespula vulgaris</a>)</i></div>

<p>The three basic segments of the typical insect antenna are the <b>scape</b> or <i>scapus</i> (base), the <b>pedicel</b> or <i>pedicellus</i> (stem), and finally the <b>flagellum</b>, which often comprises many units known as <b>flagellomeres</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansson-9">[9]</a></sup> The pedicel (the second segment) contains the <a href="/wiki/Johnston%27s_organ" title="Johnston's organ">Johnston's organ</a> which is a collection of sensory cells.</p>
<p>The scape is mounted in a socket in a more or less ring-shaped <a href="/wiki/Sclerotin" title="Sclerotin">sclerotised</a> region called the <b>torulus</b>, often a raised portion of the insect's head capsule. The socket is closed off by the membrane into which the base of the scape is set. However, the antenna does not hang free on the membrane, but pivots on a rigidly sprung projection from the rim of the torulus. That projection on which the antenna pivots is called the <b>antennifer</b>. The whole structure enables the insect to move the antenna as a whole by applying internal muscles connected to the scape. The pedicel is flexibly connected to the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location" title="Anatomical terms of location">distal</a> end of the scape and its movements in turn can be controlled by muscular connections between the scape and pedicel. The number of flagellomeres can vary greatly between insect species, and often is of diagnostic importance.</p>
<p>True flagellomeres are connected by membranous <a href="/wiki/Linkage_(mechanical)#Biological_linkages" title="Linkage (mechanical)">linkage</a> that permits movement, though the flagellum of "true" insects does not have any <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Intrinsic_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Intrinsic (anatomy) (page does not exist)">intrinsic</a> muscles. Some other <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">Arthropoda</a> do however have intrinsic muscles throughout the flagellum. Such groups include the <a href="/wiki/Symphyla" title="Symphyla">Symphyla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">Collembola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diplura" title="Diplura">Diplura</a>. In many true insects, especially the more primitive groups such as <a href="/wiki/Thysanura" title="Thysanura">Thysanura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blattodea" title="Blattodea">Blattodea</a>, the flagellum partly or entirely consists of a flexibly connected string of small ring-shaped <b>annuli</b>. The annuli are not true flagellomeres, and in a given insect species the number of annuli generally is not as consistent as the number of flagellomeres in most species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansson_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hansson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In many <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalcidoidea" title="Chalcidoidea">chalcidoid wasps</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apical_(anatomy)" title="Apical (anatomy)">apical</a> flagellomeres form a <b>club</b> shape, and the collective term for the segments between the club and the antennal base is the <b>funicle</b>; traditionally in describing beetle anatomy, the term "funicle" refers to the segments between the club and the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/scape" title="wikt:scape">scape</a>. However, traditionally in working on wasps the funicle is taken to comprise the segments between the club and the pedicel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansson_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hansson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Quite commonly the funicle beyond the pedicel is quite complex in <a href="/wiki/Endopterygota" title="Endopterygota">Endopterygota</a> such as beetles, moths and <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>, and one common adaptation is the ability to fold the antenna in the middle, at the joint between the pedicel and the flagellum. This gives an effect like a "knee bend", and such an antenna is said to be <b>geniculate</b>. Geniculate antennae are common in the <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">Coleoptera</a> and Hymenoptera. They are important for insects like ants that follow scent trails, for bees and wasps that need to "sniff" the flowers that they visit, and for beetles such as <a href="/wiki/Scarabaeidae" title="Scarabaeidae">Scarabaeidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Curculionidae" title="Curculionidae">Curculionidae</a> that need to fold their antennae away when they self-protectively fold up all their limbs in defensive attitudes.</p>
<p>Because the funicle is without intrinsic muscles, it generally must move as a unit, in spite of being articulated. However, some funicles are complex and very mobile. For example, the Scarabaeidae have lamellate antennae that can be folded tightly for safety or spread openly for detecting odours or <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>. The insect manages such actions by changes in blood pressure, by which it exploits elasticity in walls and membranes in the funicles, which are in effect erectile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PassG_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PassG-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the groups with more uniform antennae (for example: <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipedes</a>), all segments are called <i>antennomeres</i>. Some groups have a simple or variously modified apical or subapical bristle called an <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arista_(biology)" title="Arista (biology)">arista</a></b> (this may be especially well-developed in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diptera" title="Diptera">Diptera</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antenna_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">Olfactory receptors</a> on the antennae bind to free-floating molecules, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">odours</a> including <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> that possess these receptors signal this binding by sending <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potentials</a> down their <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Antennal_lobe" title="Antennal lobe">antennal lobe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>. From there, neurons in the antennal lobes connect to <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_bodies" title="Mushroom bodies">mushroom bodies</a> that identify the odour. The sum of the electrical potentials of the antennae to a given odour can be measured using an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electroantennogram" title="Electroantennogram">electroantennogram</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Monarch_butterfly" title="Monarch butterfly">monarch butterfly</a>, antennae are necessary for proper time-compensated <a href="/wiki/Solar_compass" title="Solar compass">solar compass</a> orientation during migration. Antennal clocks exist in monarchs, and they are likely to provide the primary timing mechanism for sun compass orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the crepuscular hawk moth (<i><a href="/wiki/Manduca_sexta" title="Manduca sexta">Manduca sexta</a></i>), antennae aid in flight stabilization. Similar to <a href="/wiki/Halteres" title="Halteres">halteres</a> in Dipteran insects, the antennae transmit <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis forces</a> through the Johnston's organ that can then be used for corrective behaviour. A series of low-light, flight stability studies in which moths with flagellae amputated near the pedicel showed significantly decreased flight stability over those with intact antennae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> To determine whether there may be other antennal sensory inputs, a second group of moths had their antennae amputated and then re-attached, before being tested in the same stability study. These moths showed slightly decreased performance from intact moths, indicating there are possibly other sensory inputs used in flight stabilization. Re-amputation of the antennae caused a drastic decrease in flight stability to match that of the first amputated group.</p>
