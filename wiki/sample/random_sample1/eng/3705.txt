<p><b>Dublin Zoo</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga"><i><b>Zú Bhaile Átha Cliath</b></i></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), in <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Park" title="Phoenix Park">Phoenix Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, is the largest <a href="/wiki/Zoo" title="Zoo">zoo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and one of Dublin's most popular attractions. Opened in 1831,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ffhist_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ffhist-4">[4]</a></sup> the zoo describes its role as <a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">conservation</a>, study, and education. Its stated mission is to "work in partnership with zoos worldwide to make a significant contribution to the conservation of the <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a> on Earth".</p>
<p>Covering over 28 hectares (69 acres)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zoo_faq_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-zoo_faq-1">[1]</a></sup> of Phoenix Park, it is divided into areas named Asian Forests, Orangutan Forest, The Kaziranga Forest Trail, Fringes of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, Sea Lion Cove, African Plains, Roberts House, House of <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">Reptiles</a>, City Farm and South American House.{citation needed}</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_Ireland" title="Zoological Society of Ireland">Royal Zoological Society of Dublin</a> was established at a meeting held at the <a href="/wiki/Rotunda_Hospital" title="Rotunda Hospital">Rotunda Hospital</a> on 10 May 1830 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ffhist_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ffhist-4">[4]</a></sup> and the zoo, then called the Zoological Gardens Dublin, was opened on 1 September 1831it was open to the public .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ffhist_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ffhist-4">[4]</a></sup> The animals, 46 mammals and 72 birds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> were donated by <a href="/wiki/London_Zoo" title="London Zoo">London Zoo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zoo_about_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-zoo_about-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg"><img alt="Original lodge house of 1833, located at Dublin Zoo entrance." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="1930" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg/220px-Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg/330px-Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg/440px-Dublin_Zoo_house.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Original Zoo Lodge, built in 1833.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is no coincidence that the founders of Dublin Zoo were members of the medical profession. Their interest was in studying the animals while they were alive and more particularly getting hold of them when they were dead. In the 1830s the laws concerning cadavers for medical use changed. Up until then, anyone not associated with one of the big medical institutions had to resort to grave-robbing in order to obtain a cadaver for study, so getting hold of the corpse of a primate without having to rob a grave was considered quite a coup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial entry charge per person was <a href="/wiki/Sixpence_(Irish_coin)" title="Sixpence (Irish coin)">sixpence</a>, which was a sizable sum at the time and limited admission to relatively wealthy middle-class people. What made Dublin Zoo very different from some of its contemporaries was a decision to reduce the charge to one <a href="/wiki/Penny_(Irish_pre-decimal_coin)" title="Penny (Irish pre-decimal coin)">penny</a> on Sundays.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This made a day at the zoo something that nearly every Dubliner could afford once in a while and it became very popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1833, the original cottage-style entrance lodge to the zoo was built at a cost of £30. The thatch-roofed building is still visible to the right of the current entrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ffhist_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ffhist-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1838, to celebrate Queen Victoria's coronation, the zoo held an open day - 20,000 people visited, which is still the highest number of visitors in one day. President of the United States <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">Ulysses S. Grant</a> (after leaving office) was among the celebrities who came to see Dublin's world-famous lions in the 19th century. The first tearooms were built in 1898.</p>
<p>In 1844 the zoo received its first <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">giraffe</a>, and in 1855 it bought its first pair of <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a>. These bred for the first time in 1857. Reptiles got their own house in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zoo_history_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-zoo_history-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On 17 June 1903 an <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a> named Sita killed her keeper while he nursed her injured foot. She was put down by members of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Constabulary" title="Royal Irish Constabulary">Royal Irish Constabulary</a>. Times of trouble and war also caused problems for the zoo. Meat ran out during the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> of 1916. In order to keep the lions and <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tigers</a> alive, some of the other animals in the zoo were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zoo_history_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-zoo_history-9">[9]</a></sup> A lion named Slats was born in the zoo on 20 March 1919. According to <i>Dublin Zoo: An Illustrated History</i> by Catherine De Courcy it was one of many lions filmed by the <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a> film studio in 1928 to be used as their mascot Leo.</p>
<p>Between 1989 and 1990 the financial situation at the zoo became so serious that the council considered closing it. The Government then gave it a meaningful annual grant in line with what happens in other European countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Thirteen hectares (32 acres) of land surrounding the lake in the grounds of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ras_an_Uachtar%C3%A1in" title="Áras an Uachtaráin">Áras an Uachtaráin</a> were added in 1997. This made a profound improvement in the amount of space available for the animals.</p>

<p>In 2010, Dublin Zoo received 963,053 visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2015, Dublin Zoo was the third most popular visitor attraction in Ireland with 1,105,005 visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The RTÉ documentary TV series <i>The Zoo</i> produced by Moondance Productions is filmed almost entirely on location at Dublin Zoo, and is broadcast on <a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89_One" title="RTÉ One">RTÉ One</a> in Ireland since 2011, on <a href="/wiki/Vlaamse_Radio-_en_Televisieomroeporganisatie" title="Vlaamse Radio- en Televisieomroeporganisatie">VRT</a> in Belgium since 2011 and on <a href="/wiki/Animal_Planet_(UK_TV_channel)" title="Animal Planet (UK TV channel)">Discovery Animal Planet</a> in the UK since 2012.</p>
<p>In 2013, a two-year-old girl and her mother were injured by a <a href="/wiki/South_American_tapir" title="South American tapir">South American tapir</a> during a supervised visit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-examiner-tapir_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-examiner-tapir-13">[13]</a></sup> The zoo said it would review its procedures for such visits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-examiner-tapir_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-examiner-tapir-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The zoo is part of a worldwide programme to breed endangered species. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Endangered_Species_Programme" title="European Endangered Species Programme">European Endangered Species Programme</a> (EEP), which helps the conservation of endangered species in Europe. Each species supervised by the EEP has a single coordinator that is responsible for the building of breeding groups with the aim of obtaining a genetically balanced population.</p>
<p>Dublin Zoo manages the EEP for the <a href="/wiki/Golden_lion_tamarin" title="Golden lion tamarin">golden lion tamarin</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moluccan_cockatoo" title="Moluccan cockatoo">Moluccan cockatoo</a>. It also houses members of the species <a href="/wiki/Goeldi%27s_marmoset" title="Goeldi's marmoset">Goeldi's monkey</a> and the white-faced <a href="/wiki/Saki_monkey" title="Saki monkey">saki</a> which are part of EEPs coordinated by other zoos. The focus is on conservation, which includes breeding and protecting endangered species, as well as research, study and education.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rodrigues_fruit_bats">Rodrigues fruit bats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rodrigues fruit bats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_flying_fox" title="Rodrigues flying fox">Rodrigues fruit bats</a> are one of Dublin Zoo's endangered species. Fruit bats, as their name suggests, feed on fruit and because of that are very important to the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>. Bats cannot digest the seeds and pips of the fruit that they eat and so the seeds leave the bat's digestive system "wrapped" in fertilizer. Without bats, many rain forest trees would not be able to sow their own seeds.</p>
<p>Dublin Zoo has recently completed a larger <a href="/wiki/Asian_elephant" title="Asian elephant">Asian elephant</a> enclosure (complete with Asian rainforest) and the Nesbit House (bat house) has been demolished. The Rodrigues fruit bats are now in the Roberts House (or bird house), which is located beside the <a href="/wiki/Ring-tailed_lemur" title="Ring-tailed lemur">ring-tailed lemurs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golden_lion_tamarins">Golden lion tamarins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Golden lion tamarins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>This tiny monkey, named for its beautiful golden colour and the long hair around its head which resembles a lion's mane, is one of the rarest primates in the world. <a href="/wiki/Golden_lion_tamarin" title="Golden lion tamarin">Golden lion tamarins</a>, like many of the other tamarins found in South America, are threatened with extinction in their natural habitat. Dublin Zoo is involved in the international breeding programme and helps to fund researchers who study the tamarins in Brazil.</p>
<p>The forests that golden lion tamarins need in order to survive are cut down for timber and to make room for cattle ranches, farms and urbanization. Sometimes very small areas or 'pockets' of forest are left but these are too small for the tamarins to survive in. In the past, tamarins were collected for sale to the pet trade or for use in research laboratories. The golden lion tamarins are located in the South American house.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moluccan_cockatoos">Moluccan cockatoos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Moluccan cockatoos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dublin Zoo holds the European <a href="/wiki/Breed_registry" title="Breed registry">studbook</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moluccan_cockatoo" title="Moluccan cockatoo">Moluccan cockatoos</a>. A studbook is a record of all the individuals of a particular species that are held in zoos in a region. It contains information such as the sex of the animal, how old it is and who its parents were. This information is then used to decide which birds should be paired with which to get the best <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic mix</a>. This ensures that the captive population stays as genetically healthy as possible. Moluccan cockatoos are handsome birds, white-with-a-hint-of-pink feathers and a pink colour on the crest. This cockatoo is on the endangered species list.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals_and_exhibits">Animals and exhibits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Animals and exhibits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the result of protests against the standard of animal housing and welfare, led by former keeper Brendan Price, a "Plan for the Future of Dublin Zoo" was prepared by the Zoological Society of Ireland and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Public_Works" title="Office of Public Works">Office of Public Works</a>. In 1994 it was presented to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance" title="Minister of Finance">Minister of Finance</a> <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a>. The government granted the zoo <a href="/wiki/Irish_pound" title="Irish pound">IR£</a>15 million (€19 million) for improvements. Themed areas were decided on and the first, World of Primates, opened to the public in 1996. The latest, African Plains, opened in 2001.</p>

<p>African Plains is an Africa-themed area that covers 13 hectares and was opened in 2001. The main exhibit in African Plains is the "African Savanna", which houses <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostriches</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scimitar-horned_oryx" title="Scimitar-horned oryx">scimitar-horned oryx</a>, <a href="/wiki/White_rhinoceros" title="White rhinoceros">white rhinoceros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">giraffes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_zebra" title="Common zebra">common zebras</a>, all of which share a large, outdoor paddock. Gorilla Rainforest, an exhibit opened in 2012, houses a troop of <a href="/wiki/Western_lowland_gorilla" title="Western lowland gorilla">western lowland gorillas</a>: a silverback male named Harry, two adult females named Lena and Mayani, and two young gorillas named Kituba (the son of Lena) and Kambiri (the daughter of Mayani), both of which were born in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other animals displayed in the African Plains area include <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">common chimpanzees</a>, <a href="/wiki/African_wild_dog" title="African wild dog">African wild dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sooty_mangabey" title="Sooty mangabey">sooty mangabeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/African_spurred_tortoise" title="African spurred tortoise">African spurred tortoises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abyssinian_ground_hornbill" title="Abyssinian ground hornbill">Abyssinian ground hornbills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Okapi" title="Okapi">okapis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bongo_(antelope)" title="Bongo (antelope)">eastern bongo</a>. The area also features an Africa-themed restaurant (named the Nakuru restaurant) and gift shop. The last African lion living in the zoo died in 2012, so the zoo later received two female and one male Asian lions. A baby scimitar horned oryx was born in December 2016.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Reptile_House">The Reptile House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Reptile House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dublin Zoo's Reptile House was opened in 1876. It features reptiles such as <a href="/wiki/Nile_crocodile" title="Nile crocodile">Nile crocodiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gila_monster" title="Gila monster">Gila monsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veiled_chameleon" title="Veiled chameleon">veiled chameleons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancake_tortoise" title="Pancake tortoise">pancake tortoises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leopard_tortoise" title="Leopard tortoise">leopard tortoises</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plated_lizard" title="Plated lizard">plated lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_hinge-back_tortoise" title="Bell's hinge-back tortoise">Bell's hinge-back tortoises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burmese_python" title="Burmese python">Burmese pythons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_leaf_turtle" title="Asian leaf turtle">Asian leaf turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_water_monitor" title="Asian water monitor">Asian water monitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_African_black_mud_turtle" title="East African black mud turtle">East African black mud turtles</a>, as well as invertebrates such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macleay%27s_spectre" title="Macleay's spectre">Macleay's spectres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chilean_rose_tarantula" title="Chilean rose tarantula">Chilean rose tarantulas</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roberts_House">Roberts House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Roberts House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roberts House was originally built in 1898, and designed by the architect Laurence Aloysius McDonnell. The building is currently a free-flying aviary, which houses a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodrigues_fruit_bat" title="Rodrigues fruit bat">Rodrigues fruit bats</a>, as well as exotic birds such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Indian_hornbill" title="Great Indian hornbill">great Indian hornbills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_junglefowl" title="Red junglefowl">red junglefowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chattering_lory" title="Chattering lory">yellow-backed lories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_crowned_pigeon" title="Victoria crowned pigeon">Victoria crowned pigeons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_Forests">Asian Forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Asian Forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Asian Forests was originally opened in 1998, under the name "World of Cats". It consists of three enclosures, one housing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_lion" title="Asian lion">Asian lions</a> and designed to resemble the <a href="/wiki/Gir_Forest_National_Park" title="Gir Forest National Park">Gir forest</a> in India (the only place in the world where Asian lions still occur in the wild), and the other two housing <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tigers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulawesi_crested_macaque" title="Sulawesi crested macaque">Sulawesi crested macaques</a> respectively, both of which are designed to resemble the rainforests in <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Opened in June 2007. The Kaziranga Forest Trail is Dublin Zoo's <a href="/wiki/Asian_elephant" title="Asian elephant">Asian elephant</a> enclosure and is named after the <a href="/wiki/Kaziranga_National_Park" title="Kaziranga National Park">Kaziranga National Park</a> in India. The zoo owns a herd of eight elephants, two adult females named Bernhardine and Yasmin, two younger females named Asha (Bernhardine's daughter) and Anak (Yasmin's daughter) and a bull named Upali. The enclosure features a waterfall and two pools for the elephants, as well as sheltered viewing areas and a children's playground for visitors, and the elephants share their enclosure with a breeding pair of <a href="/wiki/Blackbuck" title="Blackbuck">blackbuck</a>.It is also home to two young male elephants named Kavi and Ashoka who were born on 17 July and 19 August 2014 and a young female called Samiya who was born 17 September 2014</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_American_House">South American House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: South American House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dublin Zoo's South American House is sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s" title="Kellogg's">Kellogg's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coco_Pops" title="Coco Pops">Coco Pops</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and houses various species from Central and South America, including <a href="/wiki/Golden_lion_tamarin" title="Golden lion tamarin">golden lion tamarins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goeldi%27s_marmoset" title="Goeldi's marmoset">Goeldi's marmosets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Two-toed_sloth" title="Two-toed sloth">two-toed sloths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_monkey" title="Squirrel monkey">squirrel monkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_macaw" title="Military macaw">military macaws</a>, <a href="/wiki/White-faced_saki" title="White-faced saki">white-faced saki</a> monkeys, <a href="/wiki/Painted_wood_turtle" title="Painted wood turtle">painted wood turtles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pygmy_marmoset" title="Pygmy marmoset">pygmy marmosets</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_of_Primates">World of Primates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: World of Primates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The World of Primates houses contain various species of apes and monkeys. The exhibit opened to the public in 1996. The area comprises a string of man-made islands in a natural lake. The islands range in size from 15 to 30 square metres and are linked by wooden bridges to sleeping quarters on the lake shore.</p>
<p>Some of the islands have climbing frames available for all the animals present in this location. Areas of each island have been sectioned off with hot-wire to facilitate the growth of vegetation and give each island a more natural appearance. On some islands, areas of foraging substrate, such as bark, have been provided to facilitate scatter feeding.</p>
<p>The provision of large viewing windows in the sleeping quarters gives the public access to what is generally an off-show area in many zoos. However, there are areas where the animals can hide from the public in order to receive privacy.</p>
<p>The islands mentioned above are inhabited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siamang_gibbon" title="Siamang gibbon">siamang gibbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_ruffed_lemur" title="Red ruffed lemur">red ruffed lemurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring-tailed_lemur" title="Ring-tailed lemur">ring-tailed lemurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider_monkey" title="Spider monkey">spider monkeys</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celebes_macaque" title="Celebes macaque">Celebes macaques</a>. Each species has unlimited access to their outdoor enclosures both day and night all year round.</p>

<p>The zoo has succeeded in breeding the primates on these islands. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celebes_macaque" title="Celebes macaque">Celebes macaque</a> group have done exceptionally well since their introduction to the island, and success has also been achieved with the <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siamang" title="Siamang">siamangs</a>.</p>
<p>In early 2008, an <a href="/wiki/Orangutan" title="Orangutan">orangutan</a> escaped her enclosure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> She had escaped for an hour and was on top of the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a> nighthouse before a group of school children alerted staff. That same year, an orangutan named Jorong was seen rescuing an injured <a href="/wiki/Moorhen" title="Moorhen">moorhen</a> chick from a pond, "patiently coaxing the bird ashore with a leaf before gently lifting it onto grass"; the rescue became known to the wider public in June 2011, when a four-minute video of the event was posted to <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_Farm">Family Farm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Family Farm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg/220px-Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg/330px-Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg/440px-Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goats_Dublin_Zoo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A mother goat and kid at Family Farm</div>

<p>Family Farm (formerly known as City Farm) originally opened in 1999, and re-developed in 2010. The area is designed to teach the public about modern Irish farming, and is a joint venture between Dublin Zoo and Agri Aware, a charitable trust that works to improve the image and understanding of Ireland's farming and food industry amongst the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Domestic livestock kept in Family Farm include <a href="/wiki/Greyface_Dartmoor" title="Greyface Dartmoor">Greyface Dartmoor</a> sheep, <a href="/wiki/Cheviot_sheep" title="Cheviot sheep">Cheviot sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pygmy_goat" title="Pygmy goat">pygmy goats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamworth_pig" title="Tamworth pig">Tamworth pigs</a> named Rose and Ginger, <a href="/wiki/Australorp" title="Australorp">Australorp chickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Call_duck" title="Call duck">call ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Runner_duck" title="Indian Runner duck">Indian runner ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simmental_cattle" title="Simmental cattle">Simmental cows</a>, a white rabbit named Roger and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holstein_Friesian" title="Holstein Friesian">Holstein Fresian cow</a> named Bella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_Lion_Cove">Sea Lion Cove</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dublin_Zoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sea Lion Cove">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>President of Ireland <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Higgins" title="Michael D. Higgins">Michael D. Higgins</a> opened the new Sea Lion Cove habitat in June 2015. Sea Lion Cove is home to the colony of <a href="/wiki/California_sea_lion" title="California sea lion">California sea lions</a>, three females Cassie, Florence and Seanna and one male Nico. The new saltwater habitat is the biggest and most exciting development undertaken by Dublin Zoo in recent years. It is inspired by the natural environment of the California sea lions and it includes a state of the art water filtration plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
