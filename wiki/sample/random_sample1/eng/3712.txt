<p><b>DuckDuckGo</b> (DDG) is an Internet <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">search engine</a> that emphasizes protecting searchers' <a href="/wiki/Internet_privacy" title="Internet privacy">privacy</a> and avoiding the <a href="/wiki/Filter_bubble" title="Filter bubble">filter bubble</a> of personalized search results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buys_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buys-2">[2]</a></sup> DuckDuckGo distinguishes itself from other search engines by not profiling its users and by deliberately showing all users the same search results for a given search term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> DuckDuckGo emphasizes getting information from the best sources rather than the most sources, generating its search results from key <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourced</a> sites such as <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> and from partnerships with other search engines like <a href="/wiki/Yandex_Search" title="Yandex Search">Yandex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_BOSS" title="Yahoo! Search BOSS">Yahoo!</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yummly" title="Yummly">Yummly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company is based in 20 Paoli Pike, <a href="/wiki/Paoli,_Pennsylvania" title="Paoli, Pennsylvania">Paoli, Pennsylvania</a>, United States, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Philadelphia" title="Greater Philadelphia">Greater Philadelphia</a>, and has 21 employees. The company name originates from the children's game <a href="/wiki/Duck,_duck,_goose" title="Duck, duck, goose">duck, duck, goose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPostNov2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPostNov2012-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of DuckDuckGo's source code is <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> hosted at <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Apache_License" title="Apache License">Apache 2.0 License</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Githubduckduckgo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Githubduckduckgo-9">[9]</a></sup> but the core is proprietary. On 21 May 2014, DuckDuckGo launched a redesigned version that focused on smarter answers and a more refined look. The new version added often requested features such as images, local search, auto-suggest and more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DuckDuckGo_Reimagined_.26_Redesigned_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-DuckDuckGo_Reimagined_.26_Redesigned-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 September 2014, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> included DuckDuckGo in its <a href="/wiki/Safari_(web_browser)" title="Safari (web browser)">Safari</a> browser as an optional search engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Big_Win_For_DuckDuckGo_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Win_For_DuckDuckGo-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apple_-_OS_X_Yosemite_-_Apps_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Apple_-_OS_X_Yosemite_-_Apps-12">[12]</a></sup> On 10 November 2014, <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> added DuckDuckGo as a search option to <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> 33.1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DuckDuckGo was founded in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> by Gabriel Weinberg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> an entrepreneur whose last venture, <a href="/wiki/Names_Database" title="Names Database">The Names Database</a>, was acquired by <a href="/wiki/United_Online" title="United Online">United Online</a> in 2006 for $10 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Initially self-funded by Weinberg, DuckDuckGo is now <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>-supported but the user has the option to disable ads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The search engine is written in <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perl_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perl-19">[19]</a></sup> and runs on <a href="/wiki/Nginx" title="Nginx">nginx</a>, <a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buys_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Buys-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinberg_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weinberg-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>DuckDuckGo is built primarily upon search <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">APIs</a> from various vendors. Because of this, <a href="/wiki/TechCrunch" title="TechCrunch">TechCrunch</a> characterized the service as a "hybrid" search engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFriday_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCFriday-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> At the same time, it produces its own content pages, and thus is similar to <a href="/wiki/Mahalo.com" title="Mahalo.com">Mahalo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosmix" title="Kosmix">Kosmix</a> and <a href="/wiki/SearchMe" title="SearchMe">SearchMe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name of the search engine has been called "silly" by Frederic Lardinois of <i>Read Write Web</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Weinberg explained the beginnings of the name with respect to the children's game <a href="/wiki/Duck,_duck,_goose" title="Duck, duck, goose">duck, duck, goose</a>. He said of the origin of the name, "Really it just popped in my head one day and I just liked it. It is certainly influenced/derived from duck duck goose, but other than that there is no relation, e.g., a metaphor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> DuckDuckGo has been featured on <a href="/wiki/TechCrunch" title="TechCrunch">TechCrunch</a>'s Elevator Pitch Friday<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFriday_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCFriday-22">[22]</a></sup> and it was a finalist in the <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_BOSS" title="Yahoo! Search BOSS">BOSS</a> <a href="/wiki/Mashable" title="Mashable">Mashable</a> Challenge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>We didn’t invest in it because we thought it would beat Google. We invested in it because there is a need for a private search engine. We did it for the Internet anarchists, people that hang out on <a href="/wiki/Reddit" title="Reddit">Reddit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hacker_News" title="Hacker News">Hacker News</a>.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Fred_Wilson_(financier)" title="Fred Wilson (financier)">Fred Wilson</a>, 2012 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TechCrunch_Disrupt" title="TechCrunch Disrupt">TechCrunch Disrupt</a> Conference in New York<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludwig1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludwig1-28">[28]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>In July 2010, Weinberg started a DuckDuckGo community website to allow the public to report problems, discuss means of spreading the use of the search engine, request features, and discuss open sourcing the code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Community_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Community-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2011 DuckDuckGo hired its first employee, Caine Tighe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inbound_Hiring_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inbound_Hiring-30">[30]</a></sup> The next month, <a href="/wiki/Union_Square_Ventures" title="Union Square Ventures">Union Square Ventures</a> invested in DuckDuckGo. Union Square partner Brad Burnham stated, "We invested in DuckDuckGo because we became convinced that it was not only possible to change the basis of competition in search, it was time to do it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burnham13Oct11_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burnham13Oct11-31">[31]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Trisquel" title="Trisquel">Trisquel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linux_Mint" title="Linux Mint">Linux Mint</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Midori_(web_browser)" title="Midori (web browser)">Midori</a> web browser switched to use DuckDuckGo as their default search engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>By May 2012, the search engine was attracting 1.5 million searches a day. Weinberg reported that it had earned <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$115,000</span> in revenue in 2011 and had three employees, plus a small number of contractors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farivar16May12_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farivar16May12-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Compete.com" title="Compete.com">Compete.com</a> estimated 277,512 monthly visitors to the site in August 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On April 12, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">Alexa</a> reported a 3-month growth rate of 51%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> DuckDuckGo's own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_statistics" title="Traffic statistics">traffic statistics</a> show that in August 2012 there were 1,393,644 visits per day, up from an average of 39,406 visits per day in April 2010 (the earliest data available).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a lengthy profile in November 2012, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> indicated that searches on DuckDuckGo numbered up to 45,000,000 per month in October 2012. The article concluded "Weinberg's non-ambitious goals make him a particularly odd and dangerous competitor online. He can do almost everything that <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> can’t because it could damage their business models, and if users figure out that they like the DuckDuckGo way better, Weinberg could damage the big boys without even really trying. It's asymmetrical digital warfare, and his backers at Union Square Ventures say Google is vulnerable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPostNov2012_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashPostNov2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/GNOME" title="GNOME">GNOME</a> replaced Google Search with DuckDuckGo as the default search engine in <a href="/wiki/Web_(web_browser)" title="Web (web browser)">Web</a>, the default GNOME web browser, starting with version 3.10, which was released on September 26, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gnomepeopleblog_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-gnomepeopleblog-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> At its keynote at WWDC 2014, Apple announced that DuckDuckGo would be included as an option for search on both <a href="/wiki/IOS_8" title="IOS 8">iOS 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/OS_X_Yosemite" title="OS X Yosemite">OS X Yosemite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 10, 2014 the <a href="/wiki/Pale_Moon_(web_browser)" title="Pale Moon (web browser)">Pale Moon</a> web browser, starting with version 24.4.0, included DuckDuckGo as its default search engine as well as listed it on the browser's homepage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palemoonrelease_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-palemoonrelease-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2014, DuckDuckGo released a redesigned version to beta testers through DuckDuckHack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> On 21 May 2014, DuckDuckGo officially released the redesigned version that focused on smarter answers and a more refined look. The new version added many new features such as images, local search, auto-suggest, weather, recipes and more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DuckDuckGo_Reimagined_.26_Redesigned_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-DuckDuckGo_Reimagined_.26_Redesigned-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>

<p>DuckDuckGo's results are a compilation of "about 50" sources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sources_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sources-42">[42]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_BOSS" title="Yahoo! Search BOSS">Yahoo! Search BOSS</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Alpha" title="Wolfram Alpha">Wolfram Alpha</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a>; its own <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">Web crawler</a>, the <i>DuckDuckBot</i>; and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buys_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Buys-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sources_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sources-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> It also uses data from <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourced</a> sites, including Wikipedia, to populate "Zero-click Info" boxes – grey boxes above the results that display topic summaries and related topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>DuckDuckGo positions itself as a search engine that puts privacy first and as such it does not store IP addresses, does not log user information and uses cookies only when needed. Weinberg states "By default, DuckDuckGo does not collect or share personal information. That is our privacy policy in a nutshell." However, they do maintain logs of all search terms used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Weinberg has refined the quality of his search engine results by deleting search results for companies he believes are <i><a href="/wiki/Content_farm" title="Content farm">content mills</a></i>, like <a href="/wiki/Demand_Media" title="Demand Media">Demand Media</a>'s <a href="/wiki/EHow" title="EHow">eHow</a>, which publishes 4000 articles per day produced by paid freelance writers, which Weinberg says is, "...low-quality content designed specifically to rank highly in Google's search index." DuckDuckGo also filters pages with substantial advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mims26Jul10_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mims26Jul10-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instant_Answers">Instant Answers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Instant Answers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the indexed search results, DuckDuckGo displays relevant results, called Instant Answers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> on top of the search page. These Instant Answers are collected from either 3rd party APIs or static data sources like text files. The Instant Answers are called <i>zeroclickinfo</i> because the intention behind these is to provide what the user is searching for on the search result page itself so that the user doesn't have to click any results to find what they are looking for. As of August 20, 2016, there are 989 Instant Answers active.</p>
<p>The Instant Answers are open source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> They are maintained on Github and anyone can build or work on them.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tor_hidden_service">Tor hidden service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tor hidden service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2010, DuckDuckGo introduced anonymous searching, including an exit enclave, for its search engine traffic using <a href="/wiki/Tor_(anonymity_network)" title="Tor (anonymity network)">Tor network</a> and enabling access through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tor_hidden_service" title="Tor hidden service">Tor hidden service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> This allows anonymity by routing traffic through a series of encrypted relays. Weinberg stated: "I believe this fits right in line with our privacy policy. Using Tor and DDG, you can now be end to end anonymous with your searching. And if you use our encrypted homepage, you can be end to end encrypted as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinberg13Aug10_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weinberg13Aug10-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_search">Voice search</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Voice search">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, DuckDuckGo introduced voice search for users of the <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Voice_search" title="Voice search">voice search</a> extension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangs">Bangs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bangs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuckDuckGo includes "!Bang" commands, which give users the ability to conveniently search on specific websites – using the site's own search engine if applicable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bang_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-bang-53">[53]</a></sup> For example, searching for <i><code class="mw-highlight" dir="ltr" id="" style="">!w climate</code></i> on DuckDuckgo will instantly return Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">Climate</a> entry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_model">Business model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Business model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuckDuckGo earns revenue in two ways:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Serving ads from the Yahoo–Bing search alliance network, and</li>
<li>Affiliate relationships with several companies</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a June 2011 article, Harry McCracken of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i> magazine</a></i> commended DuckDuckGo, comparing it to his favorite hamburger restaurant, <a href="/wiki/In-N-Out_Burger" title="In-N-Out Burger">In-N-Out Burger</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It feels a lot like early Google, with a stripped-down home page. Just as In-N-Out doesn't have lattes or Asian salads or sundaes or scrambled eggs, DDG doesn't try to do news or blogs or books or images. There's no auto-completion or instant results. It just offers core Web search—mostly the "ten blue links" approach that's still really useful, no matter what its critics say...As for the quality, I'm not saying that Weinberg has figured out a way to return more relevant results than Google's mighty search team. But DuckDuckGo...is really good at bringing back useful sites. It all feels meaty and straightforward and filler-free...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCracken16Jun11_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCracken16Jun11-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The barebones approach cited in his quote have since changed; DuckDuckGo now has auto-completion and instant results for example. McCracken included the site in <i>Time'</i>s list of "50 Best Websites of 2011".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thom Holwerda, who reviewed the search engine for <i><a href="/wiki/OSNews" title="OSNews">OSNews</a></i>, praised its privacy features and shortcuts to site-specific searches as well as criticizing Google for "track[ing] pretty much everything you do", particularly because of the risk of such information being subject to a <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">U.S. government</a> subpoena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holwerda21Jun11_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holwerda21Jun11-57">[57]</a></sup> In 2012, in response to accusations that it was a monopoly, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> identified DuckDuckGo as a competitor. Weinberg was reportedly "pleased and entertained" by that acknowledgment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPostNov2012_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-WashPostNov2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>It took 1445 days to get 1M searches,<br/>
483 days to get 2M searches,<br/>
and then just 8 days to pass 3M searches: <a class="external free" href="https://duckduckgo.com/traffic/" rel="nofollow">https://duckduckgo.com/traffic/</a></p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">DuckDuckGo <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account (@duckduckgo), 18 June 2013<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sel3m_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-sel3m-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nextweb3m_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-nextweb3m-60">[60]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>In June 2013, DuckDuckGo indicated that it had seen a significant traffic increase; according to the website's <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account, on Monday June 17, 2013, it had three million daily direct searches. On average during May 2013, it had 1.8 million daily direct searches. Some<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> relate this claim to the exposure of <a href="/wiki/PRISM_(surveillance_program)" title="PRISM (surveillance program)">PRISM</a> and to the fact that other programs operated by the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA) were leaked by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">Edward Snowden</a>. <a href="/wiki/Danny_Sullivan_(technologist)" title="Danny Sullivan (technologist)">Danny Sullivan</a> wrote on <i>Search Engine Land</i> that despite the search engine's growth "it's not grown anywhere near the amount to reflect any substantial or even mildly notable switching by the searching public" for reasons due to privacy, and he concluded "No One Cares About "Private" Search".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In response, Caleb Garling of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> argued: "I think this thesis suffers from a few key failures in logic" because a traffic increase had occurred and because there was a lack of widespread awareness of the existence of DuckDuckGo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Later in September 2013, the search engine hit 4 million searches per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> On March 23, 2015, DuckDuckGo retrieved more than 9 million searches for the first day in its history. That month also saw the search engine retrieve more than 250 million searches, another record for the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
