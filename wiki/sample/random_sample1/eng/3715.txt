<p><b>Princess Deokhye of Korea</b> (25 May 1912 – 21 April 1989) was the last princess of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a>.</p>
<p>She was born on 25 May 1912 at <a href="/wiki/Changdeokgung" title="Changdeokgung">Changdeok Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>. She was the youngest daughter of Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Gojong of the Korean Empire">Gojong</a> and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a>, then known as Yang Gui-in. Then, <a href="/wiki/Gojong_of_Korea" title="Gojong of Korea">Emperor Gojong</a> bestowed a loyal title, Boknyeong,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> to Yang when she gave birth to Princess Deokhye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-word_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-word-3">[3]</a></sup> Princess Deokhye was not formally recognized as a princess by Japan until 1917, because she was not a daughter of the queen. In 1917, her name was formally entered into the Imperial Family's registry. Her father, Emperor Gojong, loved her greatly and established the <a href="/wiki/Deoksugung" title="Deoksugung">Deoksu Palace(덕수궁)</a> Kindergarten for her in Junmyungdang(준명당<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>), Hamnyeong hall. Girls her age from noble families attended the kindergarten. Princess Deokhye is called Deokhye Ongju in Korea, not Gongju. Gongju refers to the daughters of the queen, and Ongju refers to the daughters of the concubine.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_and_early_life">Birth and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Deokhye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Birth and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was born on 25 May 1912, a daughter of Yang Gui-in (later Lady Boknyeong) and Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Gojong of the Korean Empire">Gojong</a> when he was 60 years old. Upon having no given name, she was ignored and was treated like she did not exist. She was then nicknamed "BoknyeongDang". In 1917, Emperor Gojong persuaded <a href="/wiki/Terauchi_Masatake" title="Terauchi Masatake">Terauchi Masatake</a>, the then-ruling Governor General of Korea to enter her name into the registry of the imperial family, offering her legitimacy and granting her the title of princess. In 1919, Emperor Gojong planned the secret engagement between Princess Deokhye and Kim Jang-han, a nephew of Kim Hwangjin, a court chamberlain. Emperor Gojong had sought to protect his daughter from Japan through this engagement, but the engagement failed due to Japan's intervention. After the failed engagement, Kim Hwangjin was not permitted to enter <a href="/wiki/Deoksugung" title="Deoksugung">Deoksu Palace</a> and Emperor Gojong died suddenly on 21 January 1919. In 1921, Princess Deokhye went to Hinodae elementary school in Seoul.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_in_Japan_and_arranged_marriage">Life in Japan and arranged marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Deokhye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life in Japan and arranged marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so,_1931.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1382" data-file-width="919" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so%2C_1931.JPG/220px-Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so%2C_1931.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so%2C_1931.JPG/330px-Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so%2C_1931.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so%2C_1931.JPG/440px-Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so%2C_1931.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Princess_dukhye_and_takeyuki_so,_1931.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Takeyuki Sō and Deokhye (1931)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1925, she was taken to <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Japan</a> under the pretense of continuing her studies. Like her brothers, she attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gakushuin" title="Gakushuin">Gakushuin</a>. She was described as silent, isolated and weak. Upon the news of her mother's death in 1929, she was finally given permission to visit Korea temporarily to attend her mother's funeral in 1930. However, she was not allowed to attend her mother's funeral with proper clothes. In the Spring of 1930, upon the onset of mental illness (manifested by <a href="/wiki/Sleepwalking" title="Sleepwalking">sleepwalking</a>), she moved to King Lee's Palace, her brother Crown Prince <a href="/wiki/Yi_Un" title="Yi Un">Eun</a>'s house in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. During this period, she often forgot to eat and drink. Her physician diagnosed her illness as <a href="/wiki/Dementia_praecox" title="Dementia praecox">precocious dementia</a>, but by the following year, her condition seemed to have improved. This may be attributed to her upbringing.</p>
<p>In May 1931, after "matchmaking" by <a href="/wiki/Empress_Teimei" title="Empress Teimei">Empress Teimei</a>, the consort of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taish%C5%8D" title="Emperor Taishō">Emperor Taishō</a> of Japan, she married Count <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8D_clan" title="Sō clan">Sō Takeyuki</a> (武志; 1923-1985), a Japanese aristocrat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreanT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-koreanT-5">[5]</a></sup> The marriage had in fact been decided in 1930; her brother had protested it, and it had been postponed because of her condition, but when she recovered, she was immediately given instructions that the marriage was to take place. She gave birth to a daughter, Masae (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">正惠</span>), or Jeonghye (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">정혜</span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in Korea, on 14 August 1932. In 1933, Deokhye was again afflicted with mental illness to the point of <a href="/wiki/Fecal_incontinence" title="Fecal incontinence">incontinence</a>, and after this, she spent many years in various mental health clinics.</p>
<p>With the defeat of Japan in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Korea once again became independent and her husband lost his nobility title, as the <a href="/wiki/Kazoku" title="Kazoku">peerage</a> was abolished. The arranged marriage no longer made sense, and they became increasingly detached from one another, until they finally divorced in 1953. Takeyuki So is known to have remarried in 1955 to Japanese Yoshie Katsumura. Having suffered an unhappy marriage, her grief was compounded by the loss of her only daughter who disappeared in 1956, reportedly committing suicide due to the stress of her parents' divorce. As a result, her condition deteriorated at a slow yet considerable pace.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Korea">Return to Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Deokhye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Return to Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She returned to Korea at the invitation of the South Korean government on 26 January 1962, after 37 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asiaone_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-asiaone-7">[7]</a></sup> At first, the Korean government refused to allow the return of the last royal bloodline, because president <a href="/wiki/Syngman_Rhee" title="Syngman Rhee">Lee Seung Man</a> wanted to avoid political chaos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, reporter Kim Eul Han found the princess and persuaded the Korean government to allow her return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She cried while approaching her motherland, and despite her mental state, accurately remembered the court manners. She lived in Nakseon Hall, <a href="/wiki/Changdeokgung" title="Changdeokgung">Changdeok Palace</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with Crown Prince and Princess Eun, their son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yi_Ku" title="Yi Ku">Prince Gu</a>, his wife <a href="/wiki/Julia_Mullock" title="Julia Mullock">Julia Mullock</a>, and Mrs Byeon Bokdong, her lady-in-waiting. She died on 21 April 1989 at Sugang Hall, Changdeok Palace, and was buried at Hongryureung in <a href="/wiki/Namyangju" title="Namyangju">Namyangju</a>, near Seoul.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Deokhye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Singer Ho Shim-nam created a 1963 song based upon the life of Princess Deokhye.</li>
<li>In 1995, a play based upon Princess Deokhye was held at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seoul_Art_Center" title="Seoul Art Center">Seoul Art Center</a>.</li>
<li>A biography for Princess Deokhye was published by Japanese author Yasuko Honma (本馬恭子) and was subsequently translated into Korean by Hoon Lee and published in 1996.</li>
<li>The best-selling novel <i>Princess Deokhye</i> by Kwon Bi-young was published in 2009.</li>
<li>Korean singer Heo Jinsul's 2010 song "The Rose of Tears" (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">눈물꽃</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Nun Mul Kkot</span></i>) is based upon the life of Princess Deokhye, and was recorded in both English and Korean.</li>
<li>The 2013 Korean musical <i>Deokhye, the Last Princess</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">덕혜옹주</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Deokhye Ongju</span></i>) is based upon the life of Princess Deokhye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Korean film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Princess_(film)" title="The Last Princess (film)">The Last Princess</a></i> is based on Kwon Bi-young's novel and stars <a href="/wiki/Son_Ye-jin" title="Son Ye-jin">Son Ye-jin</a> as the princess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
