<p><i><b>The Last Princess</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">덕혜옹주</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">德惠翁主</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Deokhye-ongju</span></i>; lit. <i><b>Princess Deokhye</b></i>) is a 2016 South Korean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_drama" title="Period drama">period drama</a> film directed by <a href="/wiki/Hur_Jin-ho" title="Hur Jin-ho">Hur Jin-ho</a> with a screenplay by Hur, Lee Han-eol, and Seo Yoo-min, based on the best-selling novel by Kwon Bi-young. It stars <a href="/wiki/Son_Ye-jin" title="Son Ye-jin">Son Ye-jin</a> as <a href="/wiki/Princess_Deokhye" title="Princess Deokhye">Princess Deokhye</a>, the last princess of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swept_away_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-swept_away-2">[2]</a></sup> The film depicts Princess Deokhye's life in Japan after she was forced to move there at age 13 by the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Imperial Japanese government</a>, and her attempts to return to Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swept_away_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-swept_away-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portray_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-portray-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Last_Princess_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1925, Korea is <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">ruled by Japan</a>, and 13-year-old <a href="/wiki/Princess_Deokhye" title="Princess Deokhye">Princess Deokhye</a> – the last princess of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> – is forced to move to Japan to attend school there. She misses her home, and after she finishes school she makes several attempts to return, but is prevented by pro-Japanese general Han Taek-soo. One day, she is reunited with her childhood friend, Kim Jang-han, an officer in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Japanese army</a> who is also part of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_independence_movement" title="Korean independence movement">Korean independence movement</a>. Kim plans a secret operation to move Deokhye and her brother <a href="/wiki/Yi_Un" title="Yi Un">Yi Un</a> to Shanghai, site of the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea">Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea</a>. This attempt fails after Han Taek-soo discovers the plan, and Deokhye and Kim are separated, losing contact with each other. She is forced to marry Count Takeyuki So in 1931, and develops <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> after giving birth to their daughter the following year. Kim becomes a newspaper reporter and eventually finds Deokhye in a Japanese mental hospital, decades after they were separated. He then persuades the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_South_Korea" title="Government of South Korea">South Korean government</a> to allow her in the country, and in 1962, Deokhye is finally able to return to her homeland.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Last_Princess_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Director <a href="/wiki/Hur_Jin-ho" title="Hur Jin-ho">Hur Jin-ho</a> decided to make a film about <a href="/wiki/Princess_Deokhye" title="Princess Deokhye">Princess Deokhye</a> after watching a documentary about her on television; he could not forget the scene showing the princess reuniting with her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_ladies" title="Court ladies">court ladies</a> at the Gimpo Airport, when she was finally allowed to return to Korea after 38 years in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tragic_life_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-tragic_life-5">[5]</a></sup> The film's screenplay was co-written by Hur, Lee Han-eol, and Seo Yoo-min, based on the best-selling novel <i>Princess Deokhye</i> (2009) by Kwon Bi-young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjd_review_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-kjd_review-6">[6]</a></sup> The story is a mix of fact and fiction, as Kim Jang-han is a fictional character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tragic_life_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-tragic_life-5">[5]</a></sup> Princess Deokhye's story had never been made into a film before this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-career-defining_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-career-defining-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Busan_International_Film_Festival" title="Busan International Film Festival">Busan International Film Festival</a> (BIFF), Hur's film project was selected to receive co-production development support from the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Film_Council" title="Korean Film Council">Korean Film Council</a>. Pre-production for <i>The Last Princess</i> began in 2014. In August 2015, <a href="/wiki/Son_Ye-jin" title="Son Ye-jin">Son Ye-jin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Park_Hae-il" title="Park Hae-il">Park Hae-il</a> were announced to be starring in the film. Son had previously worked with Hur on the 2005 film <i><a href="/wiki/April_Snow" title="April Snow">April Snow</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>The Last Princess</i> was launched in October 2015 at BIFF's Asian Film Market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Principal photography commenced on 30 November and was completed on 23 March 2016, with filming taking place in Japan and South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The film was initially funded with US$8.6 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and Son invested <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_won" title="South Korean won">₩</a>1 billion (US$886,500) into the film after production costs rose, so the staff would have a "more comfortable working environment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gave_her_all_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gave_her_all-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Son accepted the role of Princess Deokhye with no hesitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gave_her_all_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-gave_her_all-13">[13]</a></sup> In an interview, she said "I know how hard it is to find a film that places such importance on a female character and deals with her life's journey...I have no hope that I'll come across another film like this for the rest of my career."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-career-defining_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-career-defining-7">[7]</a></sup> However, she felt "tremendous pressure" portraying a historical figure, and it was her first time playing such a role. At a press conference, she said of the role: "The most difficult part about acting out Princess Deokhye was to think over and over what the princess would have done whenever I was confronted with the discrepancy between the existing archives and the film-adaptation version."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> After seeing the completed film, she said she had no regrets about her performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gave_her_all_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-gave_her_all-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Last_Princess_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Last_Princess_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Last Princess</i> opened in third place at the box office on August 3, and rose to the number one spot during the weekend of August 5–7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Over the weekend, 1.2 million tickets were sold across 961 screens, accounting for 24 percent of all ticket sales in South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The film earned US$12.4 million in a five-day period (August 3–7), with 1.7 million tickets sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The film grossed US$35.4 million in South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kobiz_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kobiz-18">[18]</a></sup> It grossed <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$40.35 million</span> worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-numbers_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-numbers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Last_Princess_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film received generally positive reviews by critics. Sung So-young of the <i><a href="/wiki/Korea_JoongAng_Daily" title="Korea JoongAng Daily">Korea JoongAng Daily</a></i> praised the film for being "interesting enough to hold the audience's interest from beginning to end", even though she felt Hur's imagination went too far in several scenes. She said major events from Princess Deokhye's life were portrayed well, and the film's biggest virtue was how it made audiences want to know more about her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjd_review_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-kjd_review-6">[6]</a></sup> Woo Jae-yeon, writing for <a href="/wiki/Yonhap" title="Yonhap">Yonhap</a>, said the film confirms Hur's reputation as a director who "has a proven track record in weaving a love story from a delicate web of emotions that each character experiences". Woo also praised Son Ye-jin's "outstanding" performance for her portrayal of "the unfathomable depths of the emotional ups and downs of Deokhye."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swept_away_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-swept_away-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rumy Doo of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Korea_Herald" title="The Korea Herald">The Korea Herald</a></i> said the film was a "refreshing change from typical dramas about the Japanese occupation of Korea, which are usually intent on delivering a message of patriotism, and tend to be heavy-handed in their emotional arcs". Doo appreciated Hur's "muted approach" in his handling of the character's emotions, and praised the performances of the two leads, saying Park Hae-il showed "deft skill and subtlety" and Son Ye-jin proved herself a "sensitive and technically refined performer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Yun Suh-young of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Korea_Times" title="The Korea Times">The Korea Times</a></i> praised the film for being "engaging, entertaining and moving", rare for a historical film, and complemented Hur's "masterful" direction and the cast's "superb" acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tragic_life_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-tragic_life-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
