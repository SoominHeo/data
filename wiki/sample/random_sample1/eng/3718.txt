<p><b>Dumbo</b> (or <b>DUMBO</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolkart_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dolkart-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2007-12-25_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2007-12-25-4">[4]</a></sup> short for <b>D</b>own <b>U</b>nder the <b>M</b>anhattan <b>B</b>ridge <b>O</b>verpass) is a neighborhood in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>. It encompasses two sections: one located between the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Bridge" title="Manhattan Bridge">Manhattan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridges</a>, which connect Brooklyn to <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> across the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a>, and another that continues east from the Manhattan Bridge to the <a href="/wiki/Vinegar_Hill,_Brooklyn" title="Vinegar Hill, Brooklyn">Vinegar Hill</a> area. The neighborhood is bounded by <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge_Park" title="Brooklyn Bridge Park">Brooklyn Bridge Park</a> to the north, the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridge</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Heights" title="Brooklyn Heights">Brooklyn Heights</a> to the south and Vinegar Hill to the east. Dumbo is part of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Community_Board_2" title="Brooklyn Community Board 2">Brooklyn Community Board 2</a>.</p>
<p>The area was originally a ferry landing, characterized by 19th and early 20th century industrial and warehouse buildings, <a href="/wiki/Sett_(paving)" title="Sett (paving)">Belgian block</a> streets, and its location on the East River by the imposing anchorage of the Manhattan Bridge. The entirety of Dumbo was bought by developer <a href="/wiki/David_Walentas" title="David Walentas">David Walentas</a> and his company Two Trees Management in the late 20th century and remade into an upscale residential and commercial community first becoming a haven for artist <a href="/wiki/Art_museum" title="Art museum">galleries</a> and presently a center for technology <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startups</a>. The large community of tech startups earned DUMBO the moniker as "the center of the Brooklyn Tech Triangle". In that time, Dumbo has become Brooklyn's most expensive neighborhood, as well as New York City's fourth richest community overall, due in part to the large concentration of technology <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startups</a>, its close proximity to <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> and the prevalence of converted industrial buildings into spacious <a href="/wiki/Luxury_real_estate" title="Luxury real estate">luxury</a> residential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lofts" title="Lofts">lofts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The neighborhood currently serves as the corporate headquarters for e-commerce retailer <a href="/wiki/Etsy" title="Etsy">Etsy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and home furnishing stores company <a href="/wiki/West_Elm" title="West Elm">West Elm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbo,_Brooklyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name is an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for <b>Down Under the Manhattan Bridge Overpass</b>. Several other New York City neighborhoods are known by <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteaus</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Tribeca" title="Tribeca">Tribeca</a>, <a href="/wiki/SoHo,_Manhattan" title="SoHo, Manhattan">SoHo</a>, <a href="/wiki/NoHo,_Manhattan" title="NoHo, Manhattan">NoHo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NoMad" title="NoMad">NoMad</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nolita" title="Nolita">Nolita</a> in Manhattan, and <a href="/wiki/BoCoCa" title="BoCoCa">BoCoCa</a> in Brooklyn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolkart_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dolkart-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2007-12-25_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2007-12-25-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbo,_Brooklyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4012" data-file-width="2664" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg/200px-Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg/300px-Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg/400px-Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Empire_State_framed_by_the_Manhattan_Bridge_July_2009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> framed by the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Bridge" title="Manhattan Bridge">Manhattan Bridge</a>, as seen from Washington Street</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 1890s, the western portion of the neighborhood was known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulton_Landing" title="Fulton Landing">Fulton Landing</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> stop that connected it to Manhattan before the Brooklyn Bridge opened. At that time, it was primarily a manufacturing district, with warehouses and factories that made machinery, paper boxes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brillo" title="Brillo">Brillo</a> soap pads. The area has been known, variously, as Rapailie, Olympia, and Walentasville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DumboNYC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-DumboNYC-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cardboard_box" title="Cardboard box">cardboard box</a> was invented in the Robert Gair building on Washington Street by Robert Gair, a Scottish emigrant; because of Gair's fame, the area was known as Gairsville for a long time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DumboNYC_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-DumboNYC-8">[8]</a></sup> The Gair building is now home to <a href="/wiki/Etsy" title="Etsy">Etsy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-usa-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">deindustrialization</a> it began becoming primarily residential, when artists and other young homesteaders seeking relatively large and inexpensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loft_apartment" title="Loft apartment">loft apartment</a> spaces for studios and homes began moving there in the late 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2007-12-25_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2007-12-25-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> Dumbo arose in 1978, when new residents coined it in the belief such an unattractive name would help deter developers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2007-12-25_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2007-12-25-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Near the end of the 20th century, as property became more and more expensive in Manhattan, Dumbo became increasingly <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrified</a>. Still, by 1997, the acronym Dumbo was largely unknown, and the area itself was very inclusive, serving mainly as an enclave for artists located along the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a> and under the Manhattan Bridge. There were still many air conditioner repair shops, auto shops, and "seedy back alleys and wharves"; however, as the neighborhood was still gentrifying from an industrial district, it lacked even a bookstore, coffee shop, or laundromat at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_19971010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt_19971010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The efforts of <a href="/wiki/Joy_Glidden" title="Joy Glidden">Joy Glidden</a>, the Founding Director of the Dumbo Arts Center (DAC) and co-founder of the Dumbo Art Under the Bridge Festival, achieved successful development in Dumbo, which is now a model for similar waterfront developments around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Glidden stated of Dumbo's gentrification, "It may be one of the last of what could be considered a true arts community in New York."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_19971010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt_19971010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The DUMBO Historic District, a historic industrial complex and national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_district_(United_States)" title="Historic district (United States)">historic district</a> in Dumbo, was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nris-2">[2]</a></sup> It consists of 95 contributing buildings, and the manufacturing concerns located in this district included Arbuckle Brothers (coffee and sugar), J.W. Masury &amp; Son (paint), Robert Gair (paper boxes), E.W. Bliss (machinery), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brillo" title="Brillo">Brillo</a> (soap pads). The district includes the earliest, large scale <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> factory buildings in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrhpinv_ny_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpinv_ny-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 18, 2007, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Landmarks_Preservation_Commission" title="New York City Landmarks Preservation Commission">New York City Landmarks Preservation Commission</a> voted unanimously to designate Dumbo as the city's 90th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_district_(United_States)" title="Historic district (United States)">historic district</a>. The Dumbo historic district consists of properties bound by John Street to the north, York Street to the south, Main Street to the west, and Bridge Street to the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art.2C_business.2C_and_leisure">Art, business, and leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbo,_Brooklyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Art, business, and leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The area has emerged as one of New York City's premier arts districts, with a cluster of for-profit art galleries such as the Klompching Gallery and such not-for-profit institutions as the <a href="/wiki/St._Ann%27s_Warehouse" title="St. Ann's Warehouse">St. Ann's Warehouse</a> and the <a href="/wiki/A.I.R._Gallery" title="A.I.R. Gallery">A.I.R. Gallery</a>.</p>
<p>Chef <a href="/wiki/Chocolate_with_Jacques_Torres" title="Chocolate with Jacques Torres">Jacques Torres</a> opened a chocolate factory in Dumbo in December 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other culinary businesses in the area include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimaldi%27s" title="Grimaldi's">Grimaldi's</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Ice_Cream_Factory" title="Brooklyn Ice Cream Factory">Brooklyn Ice Cream Factory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and The River Café,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> all clustered in Fulton Landing, also home to <a href="/wiki/Bargemusic" title="Bargemusic">Bargemusic</a>, a floating venue for classical music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Invitations for the <a href="/wiki/First_inauguration_of_Barack_Obama" title="First inauguration of Barack Obama">2009 presidential inauguration</a> of U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> were printed by Dumbo printer Precise Continental.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first public space in the neighborhood was <a href="/wiki/Fulton_Ferry,_Brooklyn" title="Fulton Ferry, Brooklyn">Fulton Ferry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire-Fulton_Ferry_State_Park" title="Empire-Fulton Ferry State Park">Empire-Fulton Ferry State Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The first six acres of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge_Park" title="Brooklyn Bridge Park">Brooklyn Bridge Park</a>, a joint state/city venture presently under development, were opened in March 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The building at 200 Water Street, which the Brillo Manufacturing Co. once occupied, is being renovated as a high-end condo building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tech_hub">Tech hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbo,_Brooklyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tech hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dumbo has New York City's highest concentration of technology firms by neighborhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-usa-9">[9]</a></sup> Dumbo is home to 25 percent of New York City-based tech firms. Within a 10-block radius are 500 tech and creative firms that employ over 10,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-usa-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The City of New York, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>, installed an incubator in Dumbo to support development of tech start-ups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-usa-9">[9]</a></sup> Dumbo's average office rent of $25 per square foot makes it more attractive to start-ups than Manhattan, where rents averaged $40 per square foot in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-usa-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area has been compared to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_Roundabout" title="Silicon Roundabout">Silicon Roundabout</a> area in <a href="/wiki/Shoreditch" title="Shoreditch">Shoreditch</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_London" title="East London">East London</a>, as well as to Manhattan's <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Alley" title="Silicon Alley">Silicon Alley</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbo,_Brooklyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a> stations are located at <a href="/wiki/York_Street_(IND_Sixth_Avenue_Line)" title="York Street (IND Sixth Avenue Line)">York Street</a> (<span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/F_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="F (New York City Subway service)">F</a></span> train) on the <a href="/wiki/IND_Sixth_Avenue_Line" title="IND Sixth Avenue Line">IND Sixth Avenue Line</a>, and <a href="/wiki/High_Street_(IND_Eighth_Avenue_Line)" title="High Street (IND Eighth Avenue Line)">High Street</a> (<span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/A_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="A (New York City Subway service)">A</a></span> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/C_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="C (New York City Subway service)">C</a></span> trains) on the <a href="/wiki/IND_Eighth_Avenue_Line" title="IND Eighth Avenue Line">IND Eighth Avenue Line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_Bus" title="New York City Bus">New York City Bus</a> service is provided by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B25_(New_York_City_bus)" title="B25 (New York City bus)">B25</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B67_(New_York_City_bus)" title="B67 (New York City bus)">B67</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B69_(New_York_City_bus)" title="B69 (New York City bus)">B69</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ramps and staircases connects the Brooklyn Bridge and Manhattan Bridge walkways to Dumbo.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_River_Ferry" title="East River Ferry">East River Ferry</a> began operations in July 2011, connecting the wharf at Fulton Landing to stations in Manhattan and <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Brooklyn" title="Williamsburg, Brooklyn">Williamsburg</a>. <a href="/wiki/NY_Waterway" title="NY Waterway">NY Waterway</a> formerly ran a water ferry from the Fulton Slip at Fulton Landing. The East River route will become part of the <a href="/wiki/Citywide_Ferry_Service" title="Citywide Ferry Service">Citywide Ferry Service</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> starting in 2017, and a new route to <a href="/wiki/Bay_Ridge,_Brooklyn" title="Bay Ridge, Brooklyn">Bay Ridge</a> would also stop at Dumbo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing">Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbo,_Brooklyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The neighborhood contains the <a href="/wiki/Farragut_Houses" title="Farragut Houses">Farragut Houses</a>, a group of ten towers managed by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Housing_Authority" title="New York City Housing Authority">New York City Housing Authority</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nation5613_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nation5613-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
