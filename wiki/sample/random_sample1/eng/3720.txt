<p>The <b>red river hog</b> (<i>Potamochoerus porcus</i>), also known as the <b>bush pig</b> (but not to be confused with <i>P. larvatus</i>, common name "<a href="/wiki/Bushpig" title="Bushpig">bushpig</a>"), is a wild member of the <a href="/wiki/Suidae" title="Suidae">pig family</a> living in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, with most of its distribution in the <a href="/wiki/Guinean_Forests_of_West_Africa" title="Guinean Forests of West Africa">Guinean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Congolian_forests" title="Congolian forests">Congolian forests</a>. It is rarely seen away from <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, and generally prefers areas near rivers or swamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdon_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kingdon-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_river_hog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg/220px-Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg/330px-Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg/440px-Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schwein_Pinselohrschwein_0509011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Male with large humps on the snout</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The red river hog has striking orange to reddish-brown fur, with black legs and a tufted white stripe along the spine. Adults have white markings around the eyes and on the cheeks and jaws; the rest of the muzzle and face are a contrasting black. The fur on the jaw and the flanks is longer than that on the body, with the males having especially prominent facial whiskers. Unlike other species of pig native to tropical Africa, the entire body is covered in hair, with no bare skin visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adults weigh 45 to 115 kg (99 to 254 lb) and stand 55 to 80 cm (22 to 31 in) tall, with a length of 100 to 145 cm (39 to 57 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdon_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kingdon-2">[2]</a></sup> The thin tail is 30 to 45 cm (12 to 18 in) long<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdon_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kingdon-2">[2]</a></sup> and ends in a tuft of black hair. The ears are also long and thin, ending in tufts of white or black hair that may each 12 cm (4.7 in) in length. Boars are somewhat larger than sows, and have distinct conical protuberances on either side of the snout and rather small, sharp tusks. The facial protuberances are bony and probably protect the boar's facial tendons during head-to-head combat with other males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Red river hogs have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_formula" title="Dental formula">dental formula</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;" title="upper dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.3-4.3</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;" title="lower dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.3-4.3</span></span></span>, similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>. Both sexes have <a href="/wiki/Scent_gland" title="Scent gland">scent glands</a> close to the eyes and on the feet; males have additional glands near the tusks on the upper jaw and on the penis. There is also a distinctive glandular structure about 2 cm (0.79 in) in diameter on the chin, which probably has a tactile function. Females have six teats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_river_hog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red river hog lives in <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, wet dense <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a>, and forested <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valleys</a>, and near rivers, lakes and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>. The species' distribution ranges from the <a href="/wiki/Congo_Basin" title="Congo Basin">Congo area</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia">Gambia</a> to the eastern Congo, southwards to the <a href="/wiki/Kasai_River" title="Kasai River">Kasai</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a>. The exact delineation of its range versus that of the <a href="/wiki/Bushpig" title="Bushpig">bushpig</a> is unclear; but in broad terms, the red river hog occupies western and central Africa, and the bushpig occupies eastern and southern Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup> Where the two meet, they are sometimes said to interbreed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghiglieri1982_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ghiglieri1982-4">[4]</a></sup> although other authorities dispute this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup> Although numerous subspecies have been identified in the past, none are currently recognised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_river_hog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3168" data-file-width="4752" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG/220px-Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG/330px-Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG/440px-Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinselohrschwein_im_Durrell_Wildlife_Park.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Red river hog at Durrell Wildlife Park (Jersey)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The species is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous">omnivorous</a>, eating mainly <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>, and supplements its diet with fruit, grasses, herbs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">dead animal and plant remains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>. It uses its large muzzle to snuffle about in the soil in search of food, as well as scraping the ground with their tusks and fore-feet. They can cause damage to agricultural crops, such as <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yam_(vegetable)" title="Yam (vegetable)">yams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Red river hogs are often active during the day, but are primarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gessner2014-5">[5]</a></sup> They typically live in small groups of approximately six to ten animals, composed of a single adult male, and a number of adult females and their young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup> However, much larger groups, some with over 30 individuals, have been noted in particularly favourable habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White1994_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-White1994-6">[6]</a></sup> The boar defends its harem aggressively against predators, with <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a> being a particularly common threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayward2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hayward2006-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>They communicate almost continuously with grunts and squeals with a repertoire that can signal alarm, distress, or passive contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdon_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kingdon-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

Piglets</div>

<p>Red river hogs breed seasonally, so that the young are born between the end of the dry season in February and the midpoint of the rainy season in July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdon_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kingdon-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oestrus" title="Oestrus">oestrus</a> cycle lasts 34 to 37 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berger2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berger2006-8">[8]</a></sup> The male licks the female's genital region before mating, which lasts about five to ten minutes. Gestation lasts 120 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdon_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kingdon-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mother constructs a nest from dead leaves and dry grass before giving birth to a litter of up to six piglets, with three to four being most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup> The piglets weigh 650 to 900 g (23 to 32 oz) at birth, and are initially dark brown with yellowish stripes and spots. They are weaned after about four months, and develop the plain reddish adult coat by about six months; the dark facial markings do not appear until they reach adulthood at about two years of age. They probably live for about fifteen years in the wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie2015_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
