<p><b>Frederick Duncan Michael Haldane</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-4">[4]</a></sup> (born 14 September 1951),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">[1]</a></sup> known as <b>F. Duncan Haldane</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> born physicist who is Eugene Higgins Professor of Physics at the <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> department of <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, and a Distinguished Visiting Research Chair<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Perimeter_Institute_for_Theoretical_Physics" title="Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics">Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics</a>. He won the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> with <a href="/wiki/David_J._Thouless" title="David J. Thouless">David J. Thouless</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Michael_Kosterlitz" title="John Michael Kosterlitz">John Michael Kosterlitz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelnature_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelnature-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Devlin_Sample_2016_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Devlin_Sample_2016-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haldane1983_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haldane1983-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duncan_Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haldane was educated at <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_School,_London" title="St Paul's School, London">St Paul's School, London</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ's College, Cambridge">Christ's College, Cambridge</a> where he was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree followed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> in 1978<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haldanephd_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-haldanephd-9">[9]</a></sup> for research supervised by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Warren_Anderson" title="Philip Warren Anderson">Philip Warren Anderson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathgene_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mathgene-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_research">Career and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duncan_Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haldane worked as a physicist at <a href="/wiki/Institut_Laue%E2%80%93Langevin" title="Institut LaueâLangevin">Institut LaueâLangevin</a> in France between 1977 and 1981, before joining the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Haldane is known for a wide variety of fundamental contributions to <a href="/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics" title="Condensed matter physics">condensed matter physics</a> including the theory of <a href="/wiki/Luttinger_liquid" title="Luttinger liquid">Luttinger liquids</a>, the theory of one-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Spin_model" title="Spin model">spin chains</a>, the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fractional_quantum_hall_effect" title="Fractional quantum hall effect">fractional quantum hall effect</a>, exclusion statistics, entanglement spectra and much more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googlescholar_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-googlescholar-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scopus_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-scopus-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duncan_Haldane&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> he is developing a new geometric description of the fractional quantum Hall effect that introduces the "shape" of the "composite boson", described by a "unimodular" (determinant 1) spatial metric-tensor field as the fundamental collective degree of freedom of <a href="/wiki/Fractional_quantum_Hall_effect" title="Fractional quantum Hall effect">Fractional quantum Hall effect (FQHE)</a> states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This new "Chern-Simons + quantum geometry" description is a replacement for the "Chern-Simons + Ginzburg-Landau" paradigm introduced c.1990. Unlike its predecessor, it provides a description of the FQHE collective mode that agrees with the Girvin-Macdonald-Platzman "single-mode approximation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duncan_Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haldane is a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> citizen. He resides permanently in the United States. Haldane and his wife, Odile Belmont, live in <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
