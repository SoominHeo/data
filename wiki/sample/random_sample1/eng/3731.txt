<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dead time</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Dead_Time_(disambiguation)" title="Dead Time (disambiguation)">Dead Time (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>For detection systems that record discrete events, such as <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear</a> <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">detectors</a>, the <b>dead time</b> is the time after each event during which the system is not able to record another event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leo-1">[1]</a></sup> An everyday life example of this is what happens when someone takes a photo using a flash - another picture cannot be taken immediately afterward because the flash needs a few seconds to recharge. In addition to lowering the detection efficiency, dead times can have other effects, such as creating possible exploits in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_cryptography" title="Quantum cryptography">quantum cryptography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead_time&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The total dead time of a detection system is usually due to the contributions of the intrinsic dead time of the detector (for example the drift time in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaseous_ionization_detector" title="Gaseous ionization detector">gaseous ionization detector</a>), of the analog front end (for example the shaping time of a spectroscopy amplifier) and of the <a href="/wiki/Data_acquisition" title="Data acquisition">data acquisition</a> (the conversion time of the <a href="/wiki/Analog-to-digital_converter" title="Analog-to-digital converter">analog-to-digital converters</a> and the readout and storage times).</p>
<p>The intrinsic dead time of a detector is often due to its physical characteristics; for example a <a href="/wiki/Spark_chamber" title="Spark chamber">spark chamber</a> is "dead" until the potential between the plates recovers above a high enough value. In other cases the detector, after a first event, is still "live" and does produce a signal for the successive event, but the signal is such that the detector readout is unable to discriminate and separate them, resulting in an event loss or in a so-called "pile-up" event where, for example, a (possibly partial) sum of the deposited energies from the two events is recorded instead. In some cases this can be minimised by an appropriate design, but often only at the expense of other properties like energy resolution.</p>
<p>The analog electronics can also introduce dead time; in particular a shaping spectroscopy amplifier needs to integrate a fast rise, slow fall signal over the longest possible time (usually from .5 up to 10 microseconds) to attain the best possible resolution, such that the user needs to choose a compromise between event rate and resolution.</p>
<p>Trigger logic is another possible source of dead time; beyond the proper time of the signal processing, spurious triggers caused by noise need to be taken into account.</p>
<p>Finally, digitisation, readout and storage of the event, especially in detection systems with large number of channels like those used in modern High Energy Physics experiments, also contribute to the total dead time. To alleviate the issue, medium and large experiments use sophisticated pipelining and multi-level trigger logic to reduce the readout rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the total time a detection system is running, the dead time must be subtracted to obtain the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Live_time&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Live time (page does not exist)">live time</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paralyzable_and_non-paralyzable_behaviour">Paralyzable and non-paralyzable behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead_time&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Paralyzable and non-paralyzable behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A detector, or detection system, can be characterized by a <i>paralyzable</i> or <i>non-paralyzable</i> behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leo-1">[1]</a></sup> In a non-paralyzable detector, an event happening during the dead time is simply lost, so that with an increasing event rate the detector will reach a saturation rate equal to the inverse of the dead time. In a paralyzable detector, an event happening during the dead time will not just be missed, but will restart the dead time, so that with increasing rate the detector will reach a saturation point where it will be incapable of recording any event at all. A semi-paralyzable detector exhibits an intermediate behaviour, in which the event arriving during dead time does extend it, but not by the full amount, resulting in a detection rate that decreases when the event rate approaches saturation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time-To-Count">Time-To-Count</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead_time&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Time-To-Count">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With a modern microprocessor based <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ratemeter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ratemeter (page does not exist)">ratemeter</a> one technique for measuring field strength with detectors (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93M%C3%BCller_tube#Quenching_and_dead_time" title="Geiger–Müller tube">Geiger–Müller tubes</a>) with a recovery time is Time-To-Count. In this technique, the detector is armed at the same time a counter is started. When a strike occurs, the counter is stopped. If this happens many times in a certain time period (e.g., two seconds), then the mean time between strikes can be determined, and thus the count rate. Live time, dead time, and total time are thus measured, not estimated. This technique is used quite widely in radiation monitoring systems used in nuclear power generating stations.</p>
