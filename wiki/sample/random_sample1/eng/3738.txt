<p>A <b>game demo</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freely distributed</a> piece of an upcoming or recently released <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a>. Demos are typically released by the game's <a href="/wiki/Video_game_publisher" title="Video game publisher">publisher</a> to help consumers get a feel of the game before deciding whether to buy the full version.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_demo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Shareware" title="Shareware">shareware</a> distribution was a popular method of publishing games for smaller developers, including then-fledgling companies such as <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">Apogee Software</a> (now 3D Realms), Epic MegaGames (now <a href="/wiki/Epic_Games" title="Epic Games">Epic Games</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a>. It gave consumers the chance to try a trial portion of the game, usually restricted to the game's complete first section or "episode", before purchasing the rest of the adventure. Racks of games on single 5<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span>" and later 3.5" <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disks</a> were common in many stores, often very cheaply. Since the shareware versions were essentially free, the cost needed only the covering of the disk and minimal packaging. Sometimes, the demo disks were packaged within the box of another game by the same company. As the increasing size of games in the mid-90s made them impractical to fit on floppies, and retail publishers and developers began to earnestly mimic the practice, shareware games were replaced by shorter demos that were either distributed free on CDs with gaming magazines or as free <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">downloads</a> over the Internet, in some cases becoming exclusive content for specific <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">websites</a>.</p>
<p>Shareware was also the distribution method of choice of early modern <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooters</a> (FPS) like <i><a href="/wiki/Wolfenstein_3D" title="Wolfenstein 3D">Wolfenstein 3D</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(1993_video_game)" title="Doom (1993 video game)">Doom</a></i>.</p>
<p>There is a technical difference between shareware and demos. Up to the early 1990s, shareware could easily be upgraded to the full version by adding the "other episodes" or full portion of the game; this would leave the existing shareware files intact. Demos are different in that they are "self-contained" programs which are <i>not</i> upgradable to the full version. A good example is the <a href="/wiki/Descent_(video_game)" title="Descent (video game)">Descent</a> shareware versus the Descent II demo; players were able to retain their saved games on the former but not the latter.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Magazine" title="Magazine">Magazines</a> that include the demos on a <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CD</a> or <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and likewise may be exclusive to a certain publication. Demos are also sometimes released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_tape/disk" title="Cover tape/disk">cover tape/disks</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainland_Europe" title="Mainland Europe">mainland Europe</a>, but given the increasing size of demos and widespread availability of broadband internet, this common practice throughout the 1980s and '90s gradually lost cover focus to <a href="/wiki/Covermount#Full_product_covermount_discs" title="Covermount">full games</a>. With the advent of console online services such as <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live" title="Xbox Live">Xbox Live</a> or <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a>, demos are also becoming available as a free or premium download<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Console manufacturers also often release their systems with a demo disc containing playable previews of games to be released for their console.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medium">Medium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_demo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Medium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The availability of demos varies between formats. Systems that use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartridge_(electronics)" title="Cartridge (electronics)">cartridges</a> typically did not have demos available to them, due to the cost of duplication, whereas systems supporting more cheaply produced media, such as <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape_data_storage" title="Magnetic tape data storage">tapes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disks</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DVD-ROM" title="DVD-ROM">DVD-ROM</a> have; the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> has more recently been a source for demos, although typically this is in addition to other distribution media available for the system in question. Also the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live" title="Xbox Live">Xbox Live</a> as well as other gaming services have demos available for download 24/7.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_demo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Game demos come in two variations: <b>playable</b> and <b>non-playable</b> (also called a "rolling demo"). Playable demos generally have exactly the same <a href="/wiki/Gameplay" title="Gameplay">gameplay</a> as the upcoming full game, although game advancement is usually limited to a certain point, and occasionally some advanced features might be disabled. A non-playable demo is essentially the gaming equivalent of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teaser_trailer" title="Teaser trailer">teaser trailer</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playable">Playable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_demo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Playable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally, playable demos are stripped down versions of the full game, restricting gameplay to some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Level_(computer_and_video_games)" title="Level (computer and video games)">levels</a>, only allowing access to some features, or limiting the amount of time playable in the game.</p>
<p>However, some demos provide content not available in the full game. An example of this was the <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Empires" title="Age of Empires">Age of Empires</a></i> demo which included a <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> campaign and two maps not available in the full version. Also, the <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i> demo <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Uplink" title="Half-Life: Uplink">Half-Life: Uplink</a></i> is a self-contained game, adapted from material cut from the development of the main game.</p>
<p>In other cases a demo may differ from the equivalent section in the full game, for instance when the demo is released as a preview before the full game is completed. An example of this is the demo for <i><a href="/wiki/Mafia_II" title="Mafia II">Mafia II</a></i> which took place in an altered version of the <i>Buzzsaw</i> mission set in the 1950s, as opposed to the equivalent mission in the full game, which was set in 1945.</p>
<p>Demos for <a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">platform</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action games</a> generally only include the first few levels of the game. Demos of <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure games</a> are often limited to a very small number of rooms, and have the "<a href="/wiki/Saved_game" title="Saved game">save game</a>" feature disabled. Demos of <a href="/wiki/Sports_game" title="Sports game">sports games</a> usually limit play to an accelerated half-time or complete match between a small number of teams (which at the same time led to the practice of "demo expanders" that allow the tweaking of some of those settings). Likewise, demos of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racing_game" title="Racing game">racing games</a> are ordinarily restricted to a single race with a pre-selected car.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-playable">Non-playable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_demo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Non-playable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A non-playable demo is a recording of gameplay, either recorded in a video, or played through using the game's own engine showing off the game's features. They are mainly displayed at <a href="/wiki/Gaming_convention" title="Gaming convention">gaming conventions</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E3_(Electronic_Entertainment_Expo)" title="E3 (Electronic Entertainment Expo)">E3</a>, when the game is still in early production as a technology or gameplay preview. Such demos might also be distributed through the Internet or with magazines as trailers for an upcoming game, or featured at retail stores (often among playable demos).</p>
<p>Some developers release a non-playable demo to the public. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Street_Fighter_IV" title="Street Fighter IV">Street Fighter IV</a> demo on the PC was a simple stress test so potential customers could be sure that their system would be able to run the game.</p>
