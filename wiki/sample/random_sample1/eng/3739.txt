<p>The <b>demi-culverin</b> was a medium <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a> similar to but slightly larger than a <a href="/wiki/Saker_(cannon)" title="Saker (cannon)">saker</a> and smaller than a regular <a href="/wiki/Culverin" title="Culverin">culverin</a> developed in the late 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATTA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATTA-1">[1]</a></sup> Barrels of demi-culverins were typically about 11 feet (3.4 m) long, had a <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">calibre</a> of 4 inches (10 cm) and could weigh up to 3,400 pounds (1,500 kg). It required 6 pounds (2.7 kg) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">black powder</a> to fire an 8-pound (3.6 kg) <a href="/wiki/List_of_cannon_projectiles" title="List of cannon projectiles">round shot</a> (though there were heavier variants firing 9-pound (4.1 kg) or 10-pound (4.5 kg) round shot). The demi-culverin had an effective range of 1,800 feet (550 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATTA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATTA-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demi-culverins were valued by generals for their range, accuracy and effectiveness. They were often used in sieges for wall and building demolition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATTA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ATTA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
