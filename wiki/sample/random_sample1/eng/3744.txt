<p><i><b>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> also known as <i><b>DemiKids</b></i>, is a series of <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> primarily developed by Multimedia Intelligence Transfer and published by <a href="/wiki/Atlus" title="Atlus">Atlus</a>. It is a spin-off from Atlus' <i><a href="/wiki/Megami_Tensei" title="Megami Tensei">Megami Tensei</a></i> franchise, and began in 2000 with the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a> games <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i>. Five more role-playing games and three games in other genres were released until 2004, followed by no new releases until the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Social_network_game" title="Social network game">social game</a> <i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children</i>. In addition to the games, the series has been adapted into <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trading_card_game" title="Trading card game">trading card game</a>, and two soundtrack albums have been released by First Smile Entertainment.</p>
<p>The series follows demon-human <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> called devil children, who journey from Japan to the demon world, and are joined by talking companion monsters who give them guidance. The player takes the role of one such devil child, and battles against demons; the devil children do not fight directly themselves, however, but have allied demons in their party fight for them. The player can choose to talk to demons instead of fighting them, to try to make them join the player's party, and can increase their allied demons' power by fusing multiple demons with each other.</p>
<p>While Multimedia Intelligence Transfer developed most of the games in the series, <a href="/wiki/Enterbrain" title="Enterbrain">Enterbrain</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_Corporation" title="Index Corporation">Index Corporation</a> also developed one game each. The music for the first two games was composed by Tomoyuki Hamada, who made use of both <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yuji_Himukai" title="Yuji Himukai">Yuji Himukai</a>, known for his work on the <i><a href="/wiki/Etrian_Odyssey_(series)" title="Etrian Odyssey (series)">Etrian Odyssey</a></i> series, worked as character designer and illustrator on the 2011 game, creating new art for returning characters. The series received a wide range of reviews, from negative to positive. Reviewers enjoyed the demon fusion, while the music and visuals were criticized.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role-playing_games">Role-playing games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Megami_Tensei:_Devil_Children&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role-playing games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b><i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children – Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i></b> were released for the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a> on November 17, 2000 in Japan, and re-released digitally through the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a> service on the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> on November 13, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_black_red_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_black_red-2">[1]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_(console)" title="PlayStation (console)">PlayStation</a> remake of both games was released on March 28, 2002, and re-released digitally on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Store" title="PlayStation Store">PlayStation Store</a> on October 13, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_black_red_remake_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_black_red_remake-3">[2]</a></sup> <i>Black Book</i> follows Setsuna, whose little brother has been kidnapped by an army of <a href="/wiki/Jack_Frost" title="Jack Frost">Jack Frosts</a> and taken to the demon world, and who is accompanied by the Cerberus Cool. <i>Red Book</i> follows Mirai, whose father has disappeared in the demon world, and who is accompanied by the pink bird Veil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><b>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children – White Book</b></i> was released for the Game Boy Color on July 27, 2001 in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_white_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_white-5">[4]</a></sup> A sequel to the first two games, it follows Masaki, who has a connection to a quiet boy named Takaharu, and who is accompanied by the green tiger Cray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><i>DemiKids: Light Version</i> and <i>Dark Version</i></b> were released for the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a> on November 15, 2002 in Japan under the titles <i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children – Light Book</i> and <i>Dark Book</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_light_dark_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_light_dark-6">[5]</a></sup> and on October 7, 2003 in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_light_dark_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign_light_dark-7">[6]</a></sup> <i>Light Version</i> follows Jin, who is sucked into <a href="/wiki/Valhalla" title="Valhalla">Valhalla</a> and fights against the Imperium forces together with the rebels; he is accompanied by the sun wolf Rand. <i>Dark Version</i> follows Akira, who is summoned by <a href="/wiki/Lucifer" title="Lucifer">Lucifer</a> to a land called Dem, and is tasked with erasing portals; he is accompanied by the dragon Gale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children – Book of Fire</i> and <i>Book of Ice</i></b> were released for the Game Boy Advance on September 12, 2003 in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_fire_ice_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_fire_ice-8">[7]</a></sup> They are sequels to <i>Light Version</i> and <i>Dark Version</i>, again following Jin and Akira.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fire_ice_official_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-fire_ice_official-9">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<li><i><b>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children</b></i> was released for <a href="/wiki/Mobile_game" title="Mobile game">mobile phones</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Social_networking_service" title="Social networking service">social networking service</a> <a href="/wiki/GREE" title="GREE">GREE</a> on July 13, 2011 in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_gree_event_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_gree_event-14">[13]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Free-to-play" title="Free-to-play">free-to-play</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_network_game" title="Social network game">social game</a>, and the first entry in the series since 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sil_social_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sil_social-16">[15]</a></sup> Mirai and Setsuna from <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i> appear as navigator characters, assisting the player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_himukai_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam_himukai-17">[16]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_elements">Common elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Megami_Tensei:_Devil_Children&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Common elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the <i>Devil Children</i> games are released in pairs, similarly to the <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(video_game_series)" title="Pokémon (video game series)">Pokémon</a></i> series, each release is its own separate game, with its own story and protagonist, although the stories in each pair's two releases sometimes intertwine. The series' protagonists are the titular "devil children", or "demikids" – children who are demon-human <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>, and wield superpowers. They all live in modern-day Japan, but end up traveling to the demon world as part of their adventures. The devil children are given weaponry, such as guns, and are joined by guiding companion monsters who can speak human language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Devil_Children_battle.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="240" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f8/Devil_Children_battle.png/220px-Devil_Children_battle.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f8/Devil_Children_battle.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Devil_Children_battle.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The player battles enemy demons using their own allied demons, shown as icons at the bottom of the screen.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>Devil Children</i> titles are role-playing games in which the player battles against demons. While the player controls a devil child, that character does not directly fight themselves; instead, they send in their allied demons to fight against the opponents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup> This was changed in <i>Book of Fire</i> and <i>Book of Ice</i>, where the devil children are able to fight by using cards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fire_ice_official_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-fire_ice_official-9">[8]</a></sup> If one of the player's demons falls in battle, another is automatically sent in to take the first one's place. To recruit new demons to their party, the player needs to choose to talk to the demon instead of fighting it, with the demon's response mostly being based on chance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup> The player can trade demons with other players, which is required to be able to collect all demon types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> While the player's character gains <a href="/wiki/Experience_point" title="Experience point">experience points</a>, their allied demons do not; to increase their power, the player needs to fuse their demons with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The non-role-playing games all have different gameplay: <i>Card Summoner</i> is based on the <i>Devil Children</i> card game, and has the player do card battles against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-player_characters" title="Non-player characters">non-player characters</a>. The gameplay is similar to <i><a href="/wiki/Magic:_The_Gathering" title="Magic: The Gathering">Magic: The Gathering</a></i>, with cards being used to summon demons, to protect the player's <a href="/wiki/Health_(gaming)" title="Health (gaming)">health</a> and attack the opponent. <i>Puzzle de Call!</i> is a puzzle game in which the player pushes around boxes to make their way to the pentagram at the end of each screen. Additionally, there is an egg placed in each screen, which disappears if the player does not reach it in a certain number of turns; if they reach it in time, it hatches, giving the player a new demon that can be used when solving the puzzles. Each level, which consists of a number of screens, ends with a boss battle in which the player needs to collect icons while avoiding the bosses' attacks. <i>Messiah Riser</i> is a real-time strategy game in which the player battles by giving their demons commands. It focuses entirely on combat, with no exploration segments between battles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg2_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg2-11">[10]</a></sup> The 2011 <i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children</i> is a social game in which the player collects demons, referred to as "devils", and complete quests to become the strongest Devil King; the player can test themselves in daily tournaments. Like in the earlier games, collected demons can be fused with each other; there is also an added "social fusion" feature, where the player can fuse one of their demons with another player's demon. Players can also help one another by giving them demons and money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sil_social_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-sil_social-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Megami_Tensei:_Devil_Children&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="192" data-file-width="300" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg/220px-TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg/330px-TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg/440px-TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TMS_Entertainment_logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/TMS_Entertainment" title="TMS Entertainment">TMS Entertainment</a> created the 2000–2001 <i>DeviChil</i> anime.</div>

<p><i>Devil Children</i> was created as a response to the popularity of the <i>Pokémon</i> series of games, and as an alternative take on the game <i><a href="/wiki/Shin_Megami_Tensei" title="Shin Megami Tensei">Shin Megami Tensei</a></i> for younger players, featuring child characters and cute monsters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup> Most of the games were developed by Multimedia Intelligence Transfer and published by <a href="/wiki/Atlus" title="Atlus">Atlus</a>, with the exception of <i>Card Summoner</i>, which was developed and published by <a href="/wiki/Enterbrain" title="Enterbrain">Enterbrain</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg2_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg2-11">[10]</a></sup> and the 2011 <i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children</i>, which was developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_Corporation" title="Index Corporation">Index Corporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_gree_event_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-fam_gree_event-14">[13]</a></sup> The only games in the series to have been released in English are <i>Dark Version</i> and <i>Light Version</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tt_importables_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-tt_importables-19">[18]</a></sup> The soundtracks for <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i> were composed by Tomoyuki Hamada from the group T's Music, using <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral music</a> blended with elements of <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vgmo_black_red_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-vgmo_black_red-20">[19]</a></sup> For the 2011 game, <a href="/wiki/Yuji_Himukai" title="Yuji Himukai">Yuji Himukai</a>, known for his work on Atlus' <i><a href="/wiki/Etrian_Odyssey_(series)" title="Etrian Odyssey (series)">Etrian Odyssey</a></i> series, was tasked with creating new designs and illustrations of Mirai and Setsuna; they were designed to look older than in their original appearances in <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_himukai_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-fam_himukai-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The popularity of the series in Japan led to the creation of two <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> series and two <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tt_importables_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-tt_importables-19">[18]</a></sup> as well as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trading_card_game" title="Trading card game">trading card game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg2_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg2-11">[10]</a></sup> The first anime, <i>Shin Megami Tensei: DeviChil</i>, was created at the animation studio <a href="/wiki/TMS_Entertainment" title="TMS Entertainment">TMS Entertainment</a>, and aired for 50 episodes from October 7, 2000 to September 29, 2001;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> the second, <i>Shin Megami Tensei: D-Children – Light &amp; Dark</i>, aired for 52 episodes from October 5, 2002 to September 27, 2003, and was created by two animation studios – Actas for the first half of the series, and <a href="/wiki/Studio_Comet" title="Studio Comet">Studio Comet</a> for the second half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Two <i>Devil Children</i> music albums were released by First Smile Entertainment on December 20, 2000 and February 21, 2001: <i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children Perfect Soundtracks</i>, which features the soundtracks of <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i>, as well as one vocal theme composed by <a href="/wiki/Hiro_Takahashi" title="Hiro Takahashi">Hiro Takahashi</a> and one by Nao Ito;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vgmo_black_red_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-vgmo_black_red-20">[19]</a></sup> and <i>Shin Megami Tensei: Devil Children Arrange Tracks</i>, which features new arrangements by Hamada and <a href="/wiki/Motoi_Sakuraba" title="Motoi Sakuraba">Motoi Sakuraba</a> of <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i> music. There were plans for individual soundtrack albums for <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i>, but they were cancelled due to low demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vgmo_black_red_arr_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-vgmo_black_red_arr-23">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Devil Children</i> has received a wide range of reviews, ranging from negative to positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_black_red_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam_black_red-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_black_red_remake_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam_black_red_remake-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_white_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam_white-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_light_dark_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam_light_dark-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_fire_ice_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam_fire_ice-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_card_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam_card-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_messiah_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_messiah-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mc_light_dark_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-mc_light_dark-24">[23]</a></sup> Writers at <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> commented that the series did not change much between installments, although one said they enjoyed the stability and reliability in that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_fire_ice_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_fire_ice-8">[7]</a></sup> Kurt Kalata and Christopher J. Snelgrove of Hardcore Gaming 101 described the games as "relatively decent for being fairly conventional RPGs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several writers at <i>Famitsu</i> praised the demon fusion mechanic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_black_red_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_black_red-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_fire_ice_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-fam_fire_ice-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_light_dark_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_light_dark-6">[5]</a></sup> with one of them calling it the best aspect of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_fire_ice_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-fam_fire_ice-8">[7]</a></sup> Kalata and Snelgrove noted that the series was unusally simplistic for the <i>Megami Tensei</i> series, but still found its gameplay to have more depth than <i>Pokémon</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span>, and that it was able to challenge the player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup> On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a>'s Frank Provo said that it was not "nearly as fun or diverse", in his review of <i>Light Version</i> and <i>Dark Version</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_light_dark_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot_light_dark-25">[24]</a></sup> Reviewing <i>White Book</i>, one writer at <i>Famitsu</i> said that the tempo of the game was good, allowing for a stress-free experience playing the game, while another thought that the enemies were too strong at the beginning, forcing the player to repeatedly fuse their demons to make any progress at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_white_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_white-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kalata and Snelgrove were critical of the Game Boy Color titles' visuals, and were disappointed in the updated music and graphics in the PlayStation remake, saying that it looked like "a subpar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Famicom" title="Super Famicom">Super Famicom</a> RPG".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup> <i>Famitsu</i> was also critical of the updates, calling them insignificant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_black_red_remake_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_black_red_remake-3">[2]</a></sup> Kalata and Snelgrove did however find the Game Boy Advance games' graphics to be a large improvement over the previous games'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg1_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg1-4">[3]</a></sup> Patrick Gann at RPGFan was underwhelmed by the music in <i>Black Book</i> and <i>Red Book</i>, calling it "far from noteworthy" and saying that it had failed to leave a lasting impression on him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rpgfan_black_red_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-rpgfan_black_red-26">[25]</a></sup> Chris Greening at VGMO said that the music fulfilled its purpose within the game, but that it was otherwise poor and "samey", with a few catchy compositions as exceptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vgmo_black_red_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-vgmo_black_red-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kalata and Snelgrove thought that <i>Puzzle de Call</i> featured interesting ideas, but that there was not anything special about it. They enjoyed how <i>Messiah Riser</i> was different from other Japanese role-playing games, but found the user interface hard to use and that the artificial intelligence felt erratic; they also commented on how demons only have one magic attack each made the game feel limiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcg101_pg2_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-hcg101_pg2-11">[10]</a></sup> <i>Famitsu</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> writers thought that <i>Card Summoner</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> rules were very complex, but noted that the tutorial made it easy to learn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_card_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-fam_card-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
