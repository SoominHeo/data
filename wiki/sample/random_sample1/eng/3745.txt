<p><b>Deborah Elizabeth Meyer</b> (born August 14, 1952), also known by her married name <b>Deborah Weber</b>, is an American former competition swimmer, a three-time Olympic champion, and a former world record-holder in four events. Meyer won the 200-, 400-, and 800-meter <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_swimming" title="Freestyle swimming">freestyle swimming</a> races in the <a href="/wiki/1968_Summer_Olympics" title="1968 Summer Olympics">1968 Summer Olympics</a> in Mexico City. While she was still a 16-year-old student at <a href="/wiki/Rio_Americano_High_School" title="Rio Americano High School">Rio Americano High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>, she became the first swimmer to win three individual gold medals in one Olympics, winning the 200-, 400-, and 800-meter <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_swimming" title="Freestyle swimming">freestyle swimming</a> races.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sroprofile_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sroprofile-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Katie_Ledecky" title="Katie Ledecky">Katie Ledecky</a> is the only other female swimmer to have done the same, in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Summer_Olympics" title="2016 Summer Olympics">2016 Summer Olympics</a> in Rio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meyer set world records in 200-meter, 400-meter, and 800-meter freestyle swimming events at the U.S. Olympics trials. Her winning times at the Olympic Games were 2:10.5 for the 200-meter, 4:31.8 for the 400-meter, and 9:24.0 for the 800-meter distances, all of them new or first-time Olympic records.</p>
<p>In 1968, the women's freestyle races at 200-meter and 800-meter distances were added to the Summer Olympics for the first time. Before this, the longest race for women was the <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Swimming at the Summer Olympics">400-meter freestyle</a>, despite the fact that the male competitors had had the <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Swimming at the Summer Olympics">1,500-meter freestyle race</a> (the metric mile) for decades, dating back to 1896.</p>
<p>While overcoming her problems with <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, Meyer broke 15 world records in swimming during her career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sroprofile_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sroprofile-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishofprofile_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ishofprofile-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meyer broke 24 American records and won 19 <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Athletic_Union" title="Amateur Athletic Union">Amateur Athletic Union</a> (AAU) national championships. In 1968, she won the <a href="/wiki/James_E._Sullivan_Award" title="James E. Sullivan Award">James E. Sullivan Award</a>. In 1969, she was named <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press_Athlete_of_the_Year" title="Associated Press Athlete of the Year">Associated Press Athlete of the Year</a>. She was named <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimming_World_Magazine" title="Swimming World Magazine">Swimming World</a>'</i>s World Swimmer of the Year in 1967, 1968 and 1969. In 1972, Meyer retired from competitive swimming. She was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Swimming_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Swimming Hall of Fame">International Swimming Hall of Fame</a> in 1977, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Hall_of_Fame" title="United States Olympic Hall of Fame">United States Olympic Hall of Fame</a> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishofprofile_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ishofprofile-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 5, 2004, Meyer was inducted into the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_High_School_Hall_of_Fame" title="National High School Hall of Fame">National High School Hall of Fame</a>. Meyer uses the custom California automobile license plate "3GOLD68".</p>
<p>Meyer is married to Bill Weber. She owns the Debbie Meyer Swim School in <a href="/wiki/Carmichael,_California" title="Carmichael, California">Carmichael, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sroprofile_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-sroprofile-2">[2]</a></sup> According to the business website, Meyer has taught swimming in the area around Sacramento, since the 1970s, and she opened her own school in 1993. Along with teaching both children and adults to be safe in the water Meyer is coaching the Truckee Tahoe Swim Team in <a href="/wiki/Truckee,_California" title="Truckee, California">Truckee, California</a>.</p>
<p>Meyer has a daughter, son, and step-daughter.</p>
