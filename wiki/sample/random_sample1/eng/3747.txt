<p><i><b>Desulforudis audaxviator</b></i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacterium</a>, which lives in depths from 1.5 km to 3 km (.9 to 1.8 miles) below the Earth's surface in the groundwater.</p>
<p>It is the only bacterium found in water samples obtained 2.8 km (1.7 miles) underground in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mponeng" title="Mponeng">Mponeng</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_mine" title="Gold mine">gold mine</a> in South Africa. Approximately four <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a> in length, it has survived for millions of years on chemical food sources that derive from the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of minerals in the surrounding rock, making it one of the few organisms known that does not depend on sunlight for nourishment and the only species known to be alone in its <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discover_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discover-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>D. audaxviator</i> has genes for extracting carbon from dissolved <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>. It may also have acquired genes from a species of <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> by <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewSci_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewSci-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>They have been isolated from Earth's surface for several million years because analyses of the water that they live in showed that it is very old and has not been diluted by surface water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-physorg-3">[3]</a></sup> As the environment at that depth is so much like the early Earth, it gives a handle on what kind of creatures might have existed before there was an oxygen atmosphere. Billions of years ago, some of the first bacteria on the planet may have thrived in similar conditions. The newly discovered microbes could shed light into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origins_of_life" title="Origins of life">origins of life</a> on Earth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i>D. audaxviator</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram_positive" title="Gram positive">Gram positive</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacterium" title="Sulfate-reducing bacterium">sulfate-reducing bacterium</a> (making it the first complete such <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>). The genome contains an unusual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transposon" title="Transposon">transposon</a> and possesses many sites of insertion. Its complete intolerance of oxygen suggests long-term isolation. The <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> in that environment do not come from living organisms. The source of the hydrogen needed for their respiration comes from the decomposition of water by <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>. The radiation allows for the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a> compounds that these bacteria can use as a high-energy source of food.</p>
<p><i>D. audaxviator</i> not only survives a complete absence of organic compounds, light, and oxygen, but also temperatures as high as 60 °C (140 °F) and a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 9.3. The physiology that enables it to live in these extreme conditions is a tribute to its unusually large genome, consisting of 2157 genes instead of the 1500 of its peers. If conditions become unfavorable for normal life, <i>D. audaxviator</i> is able to <a href="/wiki/Microbial_cyst" title="Microbial cyst">encyst</a>, safeguarding its DNA from heat, extreme pH, and the lack of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name comes from a quotation from <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_Center_of_the_Earth" title="Journey to the Center of the Earth">Journey to the Center of the Earth</a></i>. The hero, Professor Lidenbrock, finds a secret inscription in Latin that reads: <i>Descende, audax viator, et terrestre centrum attinges</i> (Descend, bold traveller, and you will attain the center of the Earth).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discover_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Discover-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
