<p><b>Death Cab for Cutie</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> band, formed in <a href="/wiki/Bellingham,_Washington" title="Bellingham, Washington">Bellingham, Washington</a> in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The band comprises <a href="/wiki/Ben_Gibbard" title="Ben Gibbard">Ben Gibbard</a> (vocals, guitar, piano), <a href="/wiki/Nick_Harmer" title="Nick Harmer">Nick Harmer</a> (bass), Dave Depper (guitar, keyboards, vocals), Zac Rae (keyboards, guitar), and <a href="/wiki/Jason_McGerr" title="Jason McGerr">Jason McGerr</a> (drums). In 2014, founding <a href="/wiki/Guitarist" title="Guitarist">guitarist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Producer_(music)" title="Producer (music)">producer</a> <a href="/wiki/Chris_Walla" title="Chris Walla">Chris Walla</a> announced that he would be departing from the band after recording their eighth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Kintsugi_(album)" title="Kintsugi (album)">Kintsugi</a></i>.</p>
<p>The band was originally a solo project by Ben Gibbard, when he released the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demo_album" title="Demo album">demo album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/You_Can_Play_These_Songs_with_Chords" title="You Can Play These Songs with Chords">You Can Play These Songs with Chords</a></i> to positive reception. This led to a record deal with <a href="/wiki/Barsuk_Records" title="Barsuk Records">Barsuk Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Gibbard decided to expand the project into a complete band, releasing their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Something_About_Airplanes" title="Something About Airplanes">Something About Airplanes</a></i> in 1998, and their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/We_Have_the_Facts_and_We%27re_Voting_Yes" title="We Have the Facts and We're Voting Yes">We Have the Facts and We're Voting Yes</a></i>, in 2000; both records were positively received in the indie community. Their third album, 2001's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Photo_Album" title="The Photo Album">The Photo Album</a></i>, gave the band their first charting single, and the release of the group's fourth album <i><a href="/wiki/Transatlanticism" title="Transatlanticism">Transatlanticism</a></i>, in 2003, gained the band mainstream critical and commercial success. After signing with <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a>, Death Cab For Cutie released their fifth album and major-label debut <i><a href="/wiki/Plans_(album)" title="Plans (album)">Plans</a></i> in 2005, which received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_certification" title="Platinum certification">platinum certification</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. The band released their sixth album <i><a href="/wiki/Narrow_Stairs" title="Narrow Stairs">Narrow Stairs</a></i> in 2008, which served as a stylistic departure for the group. Their seventh album, 2011's <i><a href="/wiki/Codes_and_Keys" title="Codes and Keys">Codes and Keys</a></i>, featured the band's first number one single, "<a href="/wiki/You_Are_a_Tourist" title="You Are a Tourist">You Are a Tourist</a>". Their eighth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Kintsugi_(album)" title="Kintsugi (album)">Kintsugi</a></i>, the last to feature Walla, was released on March 31, 2015.</p>
<p>Death Cab for Cutie's music has been labeled as <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">indie pop</a>, and alternative rock. It is noted for its use of unconventional instrumentation, as well as Gibbard's distinctive voice and unique lyrical style. Since their formation, the band has released eight full-length studio albums, four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extended_Play" title="Extended Play">EPs</a>, two live EPs, one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_album" title="Live album">live album</a>, and one demo album.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_.281997.E2.80.932002.29">Early years (1997–2002)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years (1997–2002)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Death Cab for Cutie began as a solo project of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Gibbard" title="Ben Gibbard">Ben Gibbard</a> in 1997, while he was the guitar player for the band Pinwheel, and was recorded under the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-Time_Quarterback" title="All-Time Quarterback">All-Time Quarterback</a>. As Death Cab for Cutie, Gibbard released a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_audio_cassette" title="Compact audio cassette">cassette</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/You_Can_Play_These_Songs_with_Chords" title="You Can Play These Songs with Chords">You Can Play These Songs with Chords</a></i> the same year. The release was surprisingly successful, and Gibbard decided to expand the project into a complete band, recruiting <a href="/wiki/Chris_Walla" title="Chris Walla">Chris Walla</a> (who had also worked on the cassette) as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_guitarist" title="Lead guitarist">lead guitarist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Harmer" title="Nick Harmer">Nick Harmer</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bass_player" title="Bass player">bass player</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Good" title="Nathan Good">Nathan Good</a> as <a href="/wiki/Drummer" title="Drummer">drummer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-8">[8]</a></sup> Death Cab for Cutie was officially formed at <a href="/wiki/Western_Washington_University" title="Western Washington University">Western Washington University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bellingham,_Washington" title="Bellingham, Washington">Bellingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, and lyrics from early songs include local references that were important to the band's development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washington_post_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-washington_post-9">[9]</a></sup> Many of the early songs were recorded in the basement of an Ellis Street home Gibbard lived in with several roommates in Bellingham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The four released their debut full-length <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Something_About_Airplanes" title="Something About Airplanes">Something About Airplanes</a></i>, on August 18, 1998. The album was favorably reviewed in the independent music scene. In 1998 the band also met their long-term manager Jordan Kurland. Kurland had heard good things about them, and after a failed attempt to see them play at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_By_Southwest" title="South By Southwest">South By Southwest</a> finally hooked up with them when touring with his then client, the band Crumb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band released their follow-up second album, <i><a href="/wiki/We_Have_the_Facts_and_We%27re_Voting_Yes" title="We Have the Facts and We're Voting Yes">We Have the Facts and We're Voting Yes</a></i>, in March 2000. Nathan Good left the band at some point during this album's production, and was briefly replaced by Jayson Tolzdorf-Larson. Gibbard played drums on the majority of the album, with Good's playing on “The Employment Pages” and “Company Calls Epilogue” being kept on the final release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-8">[8]</a></sup> Although Tolzdorf-Larson did not contribute to the album, he did appear on the song "Spring Break Broke", from the "Death Cab for Fiver" 7-inch record, and also joined the band on two tours, including their first full tour of the United States. Tolzdorf-Larson was later replaced by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schorr" title="Michael Schorr">Michael Schorr</a>, who would first appear on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Forbidden_Love_EP" title="The Forbidden Love EP">The Forbidden Love EP</a></i>, released on October 24, 2000.</p>
<p>In 2001, Death Cab for Cutie released their third album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Photo_Album" title="The Photo Album">The Photo Album</a></i>. Limited editions of this album contained three bonus tracks, which were later released separately as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stability_EP" title="The Stability EP">The Stability EP</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The album produced the band's first charting single "<a href="/wiki/A_Movie_Script_Ending" title="A Movie Script Ending">A Movie Script Ending</a>", which reached number 123 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>, and was the first of three songs by the band to be featured on the television show <i><a href="/wiki/The_O.C." title="The O.C.">The O.C.</a></i>. <i>The Photo Album'</i>s two other singles, "I Was a Kaleidoscope" and "We Laugh Indoors", also reached numbers 115 and 122 on the UK Singles Chart, respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transatlanticism_.282003.29"><i>Transatlanticism</i> (2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transatlanticism (2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003 there was yet another change of drummer with <a href="/wiki/Jason_McGerr" title="Jason McGerr">Jason McGerr</a>, who had previously played in the band <a href="/wiki/Eureka_Farm" title="Eureka Farm">Eureka Farm</a> with Gibbard and Harmer, joining the band. McGerr's debut would be playing drums on Death Cab for Cutie's next release, their fourth album <i><a href="/wiki/Transatlanticism" title="Transatlanticism">Transatlanticism</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-8">[8]</a></sup> which was released in October 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-8">[8]</a></sup> The album was received to critical acclaim, and launched the band into mainstream commercial success, with the two singles "<a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Settling" title="The Sound of Settling">The Sound of Settling</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Title_and_Registration" title="Title and Registration">Title and Registration</a>", appearing in the soundtracks of the television shows <i><a href="/wiki/The_O.C." title="The O.C.">The O.C.</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washington_post_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-washington_post-9">[9]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)" title="Six Feet Under (TV series)">Six Feet Under</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_Miami" title="CSI: Miami">CSI: Miami</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Californication_(TV_series)" title="Californication (TV series)">Californication</a></i>, and the films <i><a href="/wiki/Wedding_Crashers" title="Wedding Crashers">Wedding Crashers</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Easy_A" title="Easy A">Easy A</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Mean_Creek" title="Mean Creek">Mean Creek</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Signing_to_Atlantic_and_Plans_.282004.E2.80.932006.29">Signing to Atlantic and <i>Plans</i> (2004–2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Signing to Atlantic and Plans (2004–2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg/220px-Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg/330px-Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg/440px-Ben_Gibbard_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In early 2004 the band recorded a live <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_John_Byrd_EP" title="The John Byrd EP">The John Byrd EP</a></i>, named for their sound engineer was released on <a href="/wiki/Barsuk_Records" title="Barsuk Records">Barsuk Records</a> in March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Death Cab for Cutie had been contacted by major labels on-and-off for several years, but it was only after the proven success of <i>Transatlanticism</i> that they decided to start talking to labels about a potential deal. The fact that they had already achieved considerable success allowed the band to negotiate with a lot of creative freedom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-11">[11]</a></sup> According to their manager Jordan Kurland, the band had spoken to "pretty much all of them", and then decided they were most satisfied with their offer from <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-11">[11]</a></sup> In November 2004, the band signed a “long-term worldwide deal” with Atlantic, leaving their long-time label Barsuk Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Gibbard stated on the band's official website that nothing would change, except that "next to the picture of Barsuk holding a 7", there will be the letter "A" on both the spine and back of our upcoming albums."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> After signing to Atlantic, the band was still nervous about corporate economics, and encouraged fans to download its songs from the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMattersBendery_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopMattersBendery-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band released their fifth studio album and debut major-label release, <i><a href="/wiki/Plans_(album)" title="Plans (album)">Plans</a></i>, on August 30, 2005, to critical and commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plans_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-plans-17">[17]</a></sup> Two singles from the album, “<a href="/wiki/Soul_Meets_Body" title="Soul Meets Body">Soul Meets Body</a>” and “<a href="/wiki/Crooked_Teeth" title="Crooked Teeth">Crooked Teeth</a>”, reached the top ten of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Alternative_Songs_chart" title="Billboard Alternative Songs chart">US <i>Billboard</i> Alternative Songs</a> chart, while the single "<a href="/wiki/I_Will_Follow_You_into_the_Dark" title="I Will Follow You into the Dark">I Will Follow You into the Dark</a>" became the band's best-selling single to date. Death Cab for Cutie performed "Crooked Teeth" live on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> on January 14, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>Plans</i> received a nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for Best Alternative Album of 2005, as well as achieving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_certification" title="Gold certification">gold certification</a> in 2006 after being featured on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Album_Chart" title="Billboard Album Chart"><i>Billboard</i> Album chart</a> for 47 consecutive weeks, and later was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_certification" title="Platinum certification">certified platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> in early May 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band released a touring <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drive_Well,_Sleep_Carefully" title="Drive Well, Sleep Carefully">Drive Well, Sleep Carefully</a></i>, in 2005. Copies of the DVD were given away to promote <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a>, and the band are supporters of the activist group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETA" title="PETA">PETA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animal_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-animal-20">[20]</a></sup> In early 2006, the band announced the upcoming release of <i><a href="/wiki/Directions:_The_Plans_Video_Album" title="Directions: The Plans Video Album">Directions: The Plans Video Album</a></i>, which features eleven short films inspired by songs from the <i>Plans</i> album, each directed by a different person. The videos were posted one at a time at the band's website, and the DVD went on sale April 11, 2006. The <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a> began selling the videos (formatted for <a href="/wiki/IPod" title="IPod">iPod</a>) early on March 28, 2006. <a href="/wiki/Lance_Bangs" title="Lance Bangs">Lance Bangs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P.R._Brown" title="P.R. Brown">P.R. Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ace_Norton" title="Ace Norton">Ace Norton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Brown_(comics)" title="Jeffrey Brown (comics)">Jeffrey Brown</a>, Lightborne, <a href="/wiki/Autumn_de_Wilde" title="Autumn de Wilde">Autumn de Wilde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rob_Schrab" title="Rob Schrab">Rob Schrab</a>, Laurent Briet and Monkmus, as well as Aaron Stewart-Ahn, are among directors that have contributed to the project. An episode of <a href="/wiki/MTV2" title="MTV2">MTV2</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subterranean_(TV_show)" title="Subterranean (TV show)">Subterranean</a></i> played these videos for the whole hour, plus discussion with members of the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Death Cab for Cutie made their first appearance at <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a>'s annual <a href="/wiki/Bridge_School_Benefit" title="Bridge School Benefit">Bridge School Benefit</a>, and completed their lengthy 2006 tour of the United States on December 10, 2006, finishing with a show at the <a href="/wiki/KeyArena" title="KeyArena">KeyArena</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrow_Stairs_and_The_Open_Door_EP_.282007.E2.80.932009.29"><i>Narrow Stairs</i> and <i>The Open Door EP</i> (2007–2009)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Narrow Stairs and The Open Door EP (2007–2009)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Narrow_Stairs" title="Narrow Stairs">Narrow Stairs</a></div>
<p>Walla claimed on October 18, 2007, that a new album was "in full swing" and that they had six songs completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-six-23">[23]</a></sup> He went on to call the new music "weird," "spectacular," and "creepy," saying that it contained "lots of blood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-six-23">[23]</a></sup> He noted that the album had a "<a href="/wiki/Can_(band)" title="Can (band)">Can</a> jam" that lasted 10 minutes, which Walla said that he would have never imagined doing in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-six-23">[23]</a></sup> In a <i>Billboard</i> piece in January 2008, the band promised the album to be a "curve ball" and said that although it would have slower songs, there would be some surprises. Walla said, "I'm really excited about it. It's really got some teeth. The landscape of the thing is way, way more lunar than the urban meadow sort of thing that has been happening for the last couple of records."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curve_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-curve-24">[24]</a></sup> Walla added that the album was "louder and more dissonant and ... abrasive." They claimed that they were influenced by "<a href="/wiki/Electropunk" title="Electropunk">synth-punk</a> band <a href="/wiki/Brainiac_(band)" title="Brainiac (band)">Brainiac</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curve_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-curve-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their sixth full-length album, <i><a href="/wiki/Narrow_Stairs" title="Narrow Stairs">Narrow Stairs</a></i>, was released on May 12, 2008. The first single, "<a href="/wiki/I_Will_Possess_Your_Heart" title="I Will Possess Your Heart">I Will Possess Your Heart</a>", was released on March 18, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The album version of the song is over eight minutes in length, leading radio and promotional edits to remove the extended intro to shorten the song to four minutes. The second single, "<a href="/wiki/Cath..." title="Cath...">Cath...</a>", was released on July 21, 2008, and the third single "<a href="/wiki/Grapevine_Fires" title="Grapevine Fires">Grapevine Fires</a>", was released on March 3, 2009. The two singles "I Will Possess Your Heart" and "Cath..." both reached the top ten of the Alternative Songs chart, while "Grapevine Fires" reached number 21. In an album review, MTV writer James Montgomery said "Narrow Stairs is a great album, one that could make them very famous, but could very well also kill their careers," and although "Death Cab for Cutie had gone insane," he believed the LP could be "an early contender for the best album of 2008."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Indeed, <i>Narrow Stairs</i> was nominated for "Best Alternative Music Album" and "I Will Possess Your Heart" received a nomination for "Best Rock Song" at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/51st_Grammy_Awards" title="51st Grammy Awards">51st Grammy Awards</a>. The band lost in both categories, but prompted debate after appearing at the ceremony sporting blue ribbons to protest against what they view as the excessive use of <a href="/wiki/Auto-Tune" title="Auto-Tune">Auto-Tune</a> in the music industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <i>Narrow Stairs</i> was their first album to reach No.1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart on May 31, 2008. Though the album achieved strong success, Gibbard went on to call it the band's most "depressing record".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 31, 2009, the band released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Open_Door_EP" title="The Open Door EP">The Open Door EP</a></i>, containing tracks left off <i>Narrow Stairs</i> as well as a demo for "Talking Bird".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <i>The Open Door EP</i> was nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_Alternative_Music_Album" title="Best Alternative Music Album">Best Alternative Music Album</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/52nd_Grammy_Awards" title="52nd Grammy Awards">52nd Grammy Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2009 the band wrote the song, "<a href="/wiki/Meet_Me_on_the_Equinox" title="Meet Me on the Equinox">Meet Me on the Equinox</a>" for the <a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Saga:_New_Moon_(soundtrack)" title="The Twilight Saga: New Moon (soundtrack)"><i>The Twilight Saga: New Moon</i> soundtrack</a>. "Meet Me on the Equinox" was not the first song that Death Cab for Cutie contributed to a soundtrack, as they contributed "Soul Meets Body" to the soundtrack for <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_Release_(film)" title="Catch and Release (film)">Catch and Release</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Codes_and_Keys_.282010.E2.80.932012.29"><i>Codes and Keys</i> (2010–2012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Codes and Keys (2010–2012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Codes_and_Keys" title="Codes and Keys">Codes and Keys</a></div>
<p>The band's seventh album, <i><a href="/wiki/Codes_and_Keys" title="Codes and Keys">Codes and Keys</a></i>, was released on May 31, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Ben Gibbard and Nick Harmer have both been quoted as saying that the album was "a much less guitar-centric album than we’ve ever made before".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2011, Ben Gibbard performed a new Death Cab for Cutie song at a solo concert in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, which would later be revealed as the title track from <i>Codes and Keys</i>. The track list for the album was released on Death Cab for Cutie's website on March 15, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first single from the album, "<a href="/wiki/You_Are_a_Tourist" title="You Are a Tourist">You Are a Tourist</a>", was released on March 29, 2011. The song is notable for its music video being first ever live, scripted, one-take music video shoot ever: the group streamed a live performance of the music video as it was being recorded on April 5, 2011. The video was accomplished in a single take, using multiple cameras, and no edits or re-takes. The innovative and artistic production employed dancers, actors, and projected images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> "You Are a Tourist" is also notable as Death Cab for Cutie's first (and to date, only) single to reach number one, topping the <i>Billboard</i> US Alternative Songs, <a href="/wiki/Adult_Alternative_Songs" title="Adult Alternative Songs">US Adult Alternative Songs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bubbling_Under_Hot_100_Singles" title="Bubbling Under Hot 100 Singles">Bubbling Under Hot 100 Singles</a> charts, as well as reaching number three on the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_Songs_(chart)" title="Rock Songs (chart)">Rock Songs</a> chart. The band released the video for the song "Home is a Fire" on May 9, 2011, featuring street artist <a href="/wiki/Shepard_Fairey" title="Shepard Fairey">Shepard Fairey</a> plastering lyrics from the song around <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. "Stay Young, Go Dancing" was released as the second single on September 26, 2011, reaching number 31 on the Alternative Songs chart. "Underneath the Sycamore" was released as the third single on January 10, 2012, but did not chart. <i>Codes and Keys</i> was nominated for Best Alternative Music Album at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/54th_Grammy_Awards" title="54th Grammy Awards">54th Grammy Awards</a> in 2012.</p>
<p>The band was due to play at the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Bluesfest" title="Ottawa Bluesfest">Ottawa Bluesfest</a> on July 17, 2011, but the outdoor stage collapsed earlier in the evening after sudden severe weather hit the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> On their website, the band posted: "Our hearts go out to those that were injured and we are so thankful that no one was killed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 the band toured across the globe, starting in Australia, New Zealand, Southeast Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In April and May, the band toured in the United States with members of the Magik*Magik Orchestra, who collaborated on tracks on <i>Codes and Keys</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> After headlining the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Bunbury_Music_Festival" title="Bunbury Music Festival">Bunbury Music Festival</a> in Cincinnati, the band played summer festivals in Europe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Departure_of_Walla_and_Kintsugi_.282013.E2.80.93present.29">Departure of Walla and <i>Kintsugi</i> (2013–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Departure of Walla and Kintsugi (2013–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kintsugi_(album)" title="Kintsugi (album)">Kintsugi (album)</a></div>
<p>On October 11, 2013, the band reportedly began working on their eighth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Kintsugi_(album)" title="Kintsugi (album)">Kintsugi</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Rich_Costey" title="Rich Costey">Rich Costey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This was then confirmed by the group's official Instagram profile in which an image was uploaded with the caption "DCFC LP8 begins".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Stereogum" title="Stereogum">Stereogum</a>, Gibbard said of the new album, "I do think from start to finish it's a much better record than <i>Codes And Keys</i>. If that record turned anybody off, I feel pretty strongly that this one could win them back. There are threads in this one that connect back to our earliest stuff that people love."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereogum_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-stereogum-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 29, 2013, the band released a remastered tenth-anniversary version of their 2003 album <i>Transatlanticism</i>. The new album included a vinyl LP and MP3 download, with demos for all the songs from the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of the 2014 Record Store Day, the band released its first live album, a vinyl-only double LP recorded during various 2012 tour dates with Magik*Magik Orchestra. Included within the packaging was a code for a digital download of the recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 13, 2014, after 17 years as a member of Death Cab for Cutie, guitarist and songwriter Chris Walla decided to part ways with the band, with his last performance occurring on September 13, 2014 at the <a href="/wiki/Rifflandia_Music_Festival" title="Rifflandia Music Festival">Rifflandia Music Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_British_Columbia" title="Victoria, British Columbia">Victoria, British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Walla states that he plans to "...continue making music, producing records, and erring on the side of benevolence and beauty whenever possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rolling_stone_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-rolling_stone-46">[46]</a></sup> When asked in an interview about Walla's involvement in the eighth album, McGerr confirmed that Walla "played on everything and has been involved all the way through, even in the mixing. Even though he's played his last show with us, he's still been involved in everything involving this record."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereogum_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-stereogum-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>An October 2014 <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a> interview with Gibbard revealed new songs entitled "No Room In Frame", "Black Sun", "Beverly Drive", and "Good Help" would feature alongside seven further tracks on the new record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On January 12, 2015, the band officially announced the <i>Kintsugi</i> album, which was released on March 31, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 16, 2015, the band announced their first 2015 show at the Crocodile Cafe in Seattle set for later that week, with new touring members Dave Depper (guitar/keyboards) and Zac Rae (keyboards/guitar) replacing Walla in the live band. At the show the band debuted several new songs from <i>Kintsugi</i> including first single "Black Sun", which was later released on January 26. The band toured <i>Kintsugi</i> across the world, playing their final show on the album cycle in September 2016. <i>Kintsugi</i> was also nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rock_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rock Album">Grammy Award for Best Rock Album</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2016, the band announced the release of a new standalone single, entitled "<a href="/wiki/Million_Dollar_Loan" title="Million Dollar Loan">Million Dollar Loan</a>". The song, intended to be a <a href="/wiki/Protest_song" title="Protest song">protest song</a> against <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> presidential nominee <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>, was released as a part of a campaign entitled <a class="external text" href="http://www.30days30songs.com/" rel="nofollow">30 Days, 30 Songs</a>, in which a previously unreleased song by a different artist will be put out each day. Other artists participating in the project include <a href="/wiki/Aimee_Mann" title="Aimee Mann">Aimee Mann</a>, who sang on Gibbard's solo album, as well as <a href="/wiki/My_Morning_Jacket" title="My Morning Jacket">My Morning Jacket</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jim_James" title="Jim James">Jim James</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.E.M._(band)" title="R.E.M. (band)">R.E.M.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In a Facebook post about the song, Gibbard mentioned both Depper and Rae as part of the band and contributors to the writing of the song's arrangement, indicating that the two have joined the band on a full-time basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style">Musical style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Musical style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Death Cab for Cutie's music has been labeled <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark_Beaumont_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mark_Beaumont-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reviewed_music_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-reviewed_music-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">indie pop</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slantmagazine1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-slantmagazine1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vegas_Weekly_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vegas_Weekly-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">emo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinner_Emo_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinner_Emo-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_VH1_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork_VH1-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYMag_Catucci_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYMag_Catucci-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WBEZ_DeRogatis_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-WBEZ_DeRogatis-56">[56]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fintan_Walsh_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fintan_Walsh-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic1_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-2">[2]</a></sup> Death Cab for Cutie's early work on <i><a href="/wiki/You_Can_Play_These_Songs_with_Chords" title="You Can Play These Songs with Chords">You Can Play These Songs with Chords</a></i> was described by <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> as "emotion through its lack of emotion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> also remarked that the work on the cassette was "ultra-lo-fi".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> On <i><a href="/wiki/Something_About_Airplanes" title="Something About Airplanes">Something About Airplanes</a></i> the band's style remained similar, with some new instrumental work introduced; "flute, synth, or cello" were noted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a>'s Nitsuh Abebe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> On <i><a href="/wiki/We_Have_the_Facts_and_We%27re_Voting_Yes" title="We Have the Facts and We're Voting Yes">We Have the Facts and We're Voting Yes</a></i> the band again expanded their use of unorthodox instruments, including <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glockenspiel" title="Glockenspiel">glockenspiel</a>. Pitchfork Media called them a "gentle niche" in the current rock climate, compared with bands such as <a href="/wiki/Modest_Mouse" title="Modest Mouse">Modest Mouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Built_to_Spill" title="Built to Spill">Built to Spill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Rolling Stone</i> reviewed <i><a href="/wiki/Transatlanticism" title="Transatlanticism">Transatlanticism</a></i> and commented that it contained "melodic, melancholy songs about feeling both smart and confused, hopelessly romantic but wary of love."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trans_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-trans-61">[61]</a></sup> Gibbard's voice was described as "plaintive boy-next-door"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trans_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-trans-61">[61]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> commented on the music on <i><a href="/wiki/Plans_(album)" title="Plans (album)">Plans</a></i>, saying "The lush arrangements are long on hothouse organs and pianos, but short on the squirmy guitars and squirrelly beats that, on Gibbard's best work, offset his sweet voice and borderline-maudlin poetics with a sense of emotional danger."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plans_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-plans-17">[17]</a></sup> The band's music on <i>Plans</i> was described by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas_Morning_News" title="Dallas Morning News">Dallas Morning News</a></i> as "a literate, whispery style, the kind of stuff that normally sounds better in headphones than in large venues".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Shave_(magazine)" title="Shave (magazine)">Shave Magazine</a>, Ben Gibbard commented on his song writing saying that he "never sit[s] down to write an album number one. I just kind of sit down and write songs and the theme kind of makes itself apparent. But I would never say I was writing about searching for something as much as just trying to document with every song where I am in that moment when I’m writing that song. If a theme kind of makes itself apparent in a record, it has more to do with the fact that just what’s been on my mind recently. So I guess clearly I have been and was and am, but it was never a conscious decision."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shavemagazine_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-shavemagazine-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Band_name">Band name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_Cab_for_Cutie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Band name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibbard took the band name from the song "<a href="/wiki/Death_Cab_for_Cutie_(song)" title="Death Cab for Cutie (song)">Death Cab for Cutie</a>" written by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Innes" title="Neil Innes">Neil Innes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Stanshall" title="Vivian Stanshall">Vivian Stanshall</a> and performed by their group the <a href="/wiki/Bonzo_Dog_Doo-Dah_Band" title="Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band">Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band</a>. The song was performed by the Bonzos in the Beatles film <i><a href="/wiki/Magical_Mystery_Tour_(film)" title="Magical Mystery Tour (film)">Magical Mystery Tour</a></i>. The song's name was in turn taken from an invented <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp fiction crime magazine</a>, devised by the English academic <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hoggart" title="Richard Hoggart">Richard Hoggart</a> in his 1957 study of <a href="/wiki/Working_class_culture" title="Working class culture">working class culture</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Uses_of_Literacy" title="The Uses of Literacy">The Uses of Literacy</a></i>. In a 2011 interview, Gibbard stated, "The name was never supposed to be something that someone was going to reference 15 years on. So yeah, I would absolutely go back and give it a more obvious name."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
