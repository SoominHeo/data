<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Desktop computer</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:283px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Desktop_computer_clipart_-_Yellow_theme.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="203" data-file-width="281" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Desktop_computer_clipart_-_Yellow_theme.svg/281px-Desktop_computer_clipart_-_Yellow_theme.svg.png" width="281"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">A stylized <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustration</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a>, consisting of a case (containing the motherboard and processor), a monitor, a keyboard and a mouse</div>

<p>A <b>desktop computer</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a> designed for regular use at a single location on or near a <a href="/wiki/Desk" title="Desk">desk</a> or table due to its size and <a href="/wiki/Mains_electricity" title="Mains electricity">power requirements</a>. The most common configuration has a <a href="/wiki/Computer_case" title="Computer case">case</a> that houses the <a href="/wiki/Power_supply_unit_(computer)" title="Power supply unit (computer)">power supply</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motherboard" title="Motherboard">motherboard</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit board</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing unit</a> (CPU), <a href="/wiki/Computer_memory" title="Computer memory">memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus_(computing)" title="Bus (computing)">bus</a>, and other electronic components), <a href="/wiki/Disk_storage" title="Disk storage">disk storage</a> (usually one or more <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_disc_drive" title="Optical disc drive">optical disc drives</a>, and in early models a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floppy_disk_drive" title="Floppy disk drive">floppy disk drive</a>); a <a href="/wiki/Computer_keyboard" title="Computer keyboard">keyboard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_mouse" title="Computer mouse">mouse</a> for <a href="/wiki/Input_(computer_science)" title="Input (computer science)">input</a>; and a <a href="/wiki/Computer_monitor" title="Computer monitor">computer monitor</a>, and, often, a <a href="/wiki/Printer_(computing)" title="Printer (computing)">printer</a> for output. The case may be oriented <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_and_vertical" title="Horizontal and vertical">horizontally or vertically</a> and placed either underneath, beside, or on top of a desk.</p>
<p>An all-in-one desktop computer typically combines the case and monitor in one unit.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HP9830A-HP9866.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="787" data-file-width="820" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/HP9830A-HP9866.png/275px-HP9830A-HP9866.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/HP9830A-HP9866.png/413px-HP9830A-HP9866.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/HP9830A-HP9866.png/550px-HP9830A-HP9866.png 2x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HP9830A-HP9866.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sophisticated programmable calculator, the <a href="/wiki/HP_9800_series" title="HP 9800 series">HP 9830A</a>, was actually an early desktop computer with a printer.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to the widespread use of microprocessors, a computer that could fit on a desk was considered remarkably small; the type of computers most commonly used were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minicomputers" title="Minicomputers">minicomputers</a>, which were themselves desk-sized. Early computers took up the space of a whole room. Minicomputers generally fit into one or a few refrigerator-sized racks.</p>
<p>The very first "programmable calculator/computer" was marketed in the second half of the 1960s, starting with the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> machinery <a href="/wiki/Programma_101" title="Programma 101">Programma 101</a> (1965) computer at typewriter size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pingdom_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pingdom-1">[1]</a></sup> More desktop models were introduced in 1971, leading to a model programmable in <a href="/wiki/BASIC" title="BASIC">BASIC</a> in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timeline_of_Computer_History_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline_of_Computer_History-2">[2]</a></sup> This one used a smaller version of a <a href="/wiki/Minicomputer" title="Minicomputer">minicomputer</a> design based on <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">read-only memory</a> (ROM) and had small one-line LED alphanumeric displays. They could draw computer graphics with a plotter.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_development">Growth and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, desktop computers became the predominant type, the most popular being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> and its <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">clones</a>, followed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" title="Apple Macintosh">Apple Macintosh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thirty_years_of_Mac_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thirty_years_of_Mac-3">[3]</a></sup> with the third-placed <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a> <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a> having some success in the mid-1980s but declining by the early 1990s.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ibm_pc_5150.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1784" data-file-width="1920" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ibm_pc_5150.jpg/220px-Ibm_pc_5150.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ibm_pc_5150.jpg/330px-Ibm_pc_5150.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ibm_pc_5150.jpg/440px-Ibm_pc_5150.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ibm_pc_5150.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
IBM 5150 Personal Computer</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early personal computers, like the original <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer" title="IBM Personal Computer">IBM Personal Computer</a>, were enclosed in a "desktop case", horizontally oriented to have the display screen placed on top, thus saving space on the user's actual desk, although these cases had to be sturdy enough to support the weight of CRT displays that were widespread at the time. Over the course of the 1990s, desktop cases gradually became less common than the more-accessible <a href="/wiki/Computer_case" title="Computer case">tower cases</a> that may be located on the floor under or beside a desk rather than on a desk. Not only these tower cases had more room for expansion, but also they have freed up desk space for monitors which were becoming larger every year. Desktop cases, particularly the compact form factors, remain popular for corporate computing environments and kiosks. Some computer cases can be interchangeably positioned either horizontally (desktop) or upright (mini-tower).</p>
<p>Influential games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(series)" title="Doom (series)">Doom</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(series)" title="Quake (series)">Quake</a></i> during the 1990s had pushed <a href="/wiki/Gaming_computer" title="Gaming computer">gamers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enthusiast_computing" title="Enthusiast computing">enthusiasts</a> to frequently upgrade to the latest CPUs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphics_cards" title="Graphics cards">graphics cards</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3dfx" title="3dfx">3dfx</a>, <a href="/wiki/ATI_Technologies" title="ATI Technologies">ATI</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nvidia" title="Nvidia">Nvidia</a>) for their desktops (usually a tower case) in order to run these applications, though this has slowed since the late 2000s as the growing popularity of Intel integrated graphics forced game developers to scale back. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Technology" title="Creative Technology">Creative Technology</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sound_Blaster" title="Sound Blaster">Sound Blaster</a> series were a <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> standard for sound cards in desktop PCs during the 1990s until the early 2000s, when they were reduced to a niche product, as OEM desktop PCs came with sound boards integrated directly onto the motherboard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While desktops have long been the most common configuration for PCs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech.com-4">[4]</a></sup> by the mid-2000s the growth shifted from desktops to laptops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2006-5">[5]</a></sup> Notably, while desktops were mainly produced in the United States, laptops had long been produced by <a href="/wiki/Contract_manufacturer" title="Contract manufacturer">contract manufacturers</a> based in Asia, such as <a href="/wiki/Foxconn" title="Foxconn">Foxconn</a>. This shift led to the closure of the many desktop assembly plants in the United States by 2010. Another trend around this time was the increasing proportion of inexpensive base-configuration desktops being sold, hurting PC manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a> whose build-to-order customization of desktops relied on upselling added features to buyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Battery-powered portable computers had just 2% worldwide market share in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, laptops have become increasingly popular, both for business and personal use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computer_Economics.2C_Inc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Computer_Economics.2C_Inc-8">[8]</a></sup> Around 109 million notebook PCs shipped worldwide in 2007, a growth of 33% compared to 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssev2011-09-12_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssev2011-09-12-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2008, it was estimated that 145.9 million notebooks were sold, and that the number would grow in 2009 to 177.7 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ee2009-01-10_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ee2009-01-10-10">[10]</a></sup> The third quarter of 2008 was the first time when worldwide notebook PC shipments exceeded desktops, with 38.6 million units versus 38.5 million units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computer_Economics.2C_Inc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Computer_Economics.2C_Inc-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-is2009-01-13_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-is2009-01-13-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT09_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT09-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sales breakdown of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" title="Apple Macintosh">Apple Macintosh</a> have seen sales of desktop Macs staying mostly constant while being surpassed by that of Mac notebooks whose sales rate has grown considerably; seven out of ten Macs sold were laptops in 2009, a ratio projected to rise to three out of four by 2010. The change in sales of form factors is due to the desktop iMac moving from affordable (<a href="/wiki/IMac_G3" title="IMac G3">iMac G3</a>) to upscale (<a href="/wiki/IMac_G4" title="IMac G4">iMac G4</a>) and subsequent releases are considered premium all-in-ones. By contrast, the MSRP of the MacBook laptop lines have dropped through successive generations such that the MacBook Air and MacBook Pro constitute the lowest price of entry to a Mac, with the exception of the even more inexpensive Mac Mini (albeit without a monitor and keyboard), not surprisingly the MacBooks are the top-selling form factors of the Macintosh platform today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The decades of development means that most people already own desktop computers that meet their needs and have no need of buying a new one merely to keep pace with advancing technology. Notably the successive release of new versions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows" title="Windows">Windows</a> (Windows 95, 98, XP, Vista, 7, 8, 10 and so on) had been drivers for the replacement of PCs in the 1990s, but this slowed down in the 2000s due to the poor reception of Windows Vista over Windows XP. Recently, some analysts have suggested that <a href="/wiki/Windows_8" title="Windows 8">Windows 8</a> has actually hurt sales of PCs in 2012, as businesses have decided to stick with <a href="/wiki/Windows_7" title="Windows 7">Windows 7</a> rather than upgrade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-15">[15]</a></sup> Some suggested that Microsoft has acknowledged "implicitly ringing the desktop PC death knell" as Windows 8 offers little upgrade in desktop PC functionality over <a href="/wiki/Windows_7" title="Windows 7">Windows 7</a>; instead, Windows 8's innovations are mostly on the mobile side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Post-PC_era" title="Post-PC era">post-PC trend</a> has seen a decline in the sales of desktop and laptop PCs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The decline has been attributed to increased power and applications of alternative computing devices, namely smartphones and tablet computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Although most people exclusively use their smartphones and tablets for more basic tasks such as <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casual_gaming" title="Casual gaming">casual gaming</a>, these devices have in many instances replaced a second or third PC in the household that would have performed these tasks, though most families still retain a powerful PC for serious work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among PC form factors, desktops remain a staple in the enterprise market but have lost popularity among home buyers. PC makers and electronics retailers have responded by investing their engineering and marketing resources towards laptops (initially <a href="/wiki/Netbook" title="Netbook">netbooks</a> in the late 2000s, and then the higher-performance <a href="/wiki/Ultrabook" title="Ultrabook">Ultrabooks</a> from 2011 onwards), which manufacturers believe have more potential to revive the PC market<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech.com_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech.com-4">[4]</a></sup> than desktops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="All-in-one">All-in-one</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: All-in-one">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>An <i>all-in-one</i> desktop computer integrates the system's internal components into the same case as the display, thus occupying a smaller footprint than desktops that incorporate a tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag-howtoaio_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-howtoaio-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The all-in-one form factor was popular during the early 1980s for computers intended for professional use such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaypro_II" title="Kaypro II">Kaypro II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osborne_1" title="Osborne 1">Osborne 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/TRS-80_Model_II" title="TRS-80 Model II">TRS-80 Model II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compaq_Portable" title="Compaq Portable">Compaq Portable</a>. Many manufacturers of <a href="/wiki/Home_computer" title="Home computer">home computers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atari" title="Atari">Atari</a> included the computer's motherboard into the same enclosure as the keyboard; these systems were most often connected to a <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> set for display. Apple has manufactured several popular examples of all-in-one computers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Macintosh_128K" title="Macintosh 128K">original Macintosh</a> of the mid-1980s and the <a href="/wiki/IMac" title="IMac">iMac</a> of the late 1990s and 2000s. Some all-in-one desktops, such as the <a href="/wiki/IMac_G4" title="IMac G4">iMac G4</a>, have used laptop components in order to reduce the size of the system case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> By the mid 2000s, many all-in-one designs have used <a href="/wiki/Flat_panel_display" title="Flat panel display">flat panel displays</a>, and later models have incorporated <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a> displays, allowing them to be used similarly to a mobile <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag-howtoaio_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-howtoaio-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like laptops, some all-in-one desktop computers are characterized by an inability to customize or upgrade internal components, as the systems' cases do not provide convenient access to upgradable components, and faults in certain aspects of the hardware may require the entire computer to be replaced, regardless of the health of its remaining components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-aio_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-aio-22">[22]</a></sup> There have been exceptions to this; the monitor portion of <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a>'s Z1 workstation can be angled flat, and opened like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hood_(vehicle)" title="Hood (vehicle)">vehicle hood</a> for access to internal hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag-hpz1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-hpz1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_laptops">Comparison with laptops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desktop_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comparison with laptops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laptop#Disadvantages" title="Laptop">Laptop § Disadvantages</a></div>
<p>Desktops have the advantage over <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptops</a>, as the spare parts and extensions tend to be standardized, resulting in lower prices and greater availability. For example, the size and mounting of the <a href="/wiki/Motherboard" title="Motherboard">motherboard</a> is standardized into <a href="/wiki/ATX" title="ATX">ATX</a>, <a href="/wiki/MicroATX" title="MicroATX">microATX</a>, <a href="/wiki/BTX_(form_factor)" title="BTX (form factor)">BTX</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Computer_form_factor" title="Computer form factor">form factors</a>. Desktops have several standardized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expansion_slot" title="Expansion slot">expansion slots</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Conventional_PCI" title="Conventional PCI">conventional PCI</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCI_express" title="PCI express">PCI express</a>, while laptops only tend to have one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini-PCI" title="Mini-PCI">mini-PCI</a> slot and one <a href="/wiki/PC_Card" title="PC Card">PC Card</a> slot (or <a href="/wiki/ExpressCard" title="ExpressCard">ExpressCard</a> slot). Procedures for assembly and disassembly of desktops tend to be simple and standardized as well. This tends not to be the case for laptops, though adding or replacing some parts, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_drive" title="Optical drive">optical drive</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk" title="Hard disk">hard disk</a>, or adding an extra <a href="/wiki/Memory_module" title="Memory module">memory module</a> is often quite simple. This means that a desktop computer configuration, usually a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tower_case" title="Tower case">tower case</a>, can be customized and upgraded to a greater extent than laptops. This customization has kept tower cases popular among <a href="/wiki/Gaming_computer" title="Gaming computer">gamers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enthusiast_computing" title="Enthusiast computing">enthusiasts</a>.</p>
<p>Another advantage of the desktop is that (apart from <a href="/wiki/Green_computing" title="Green computing">environmental concerns</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_consumption" title="Power consumption">power consumption</a> is not as critical as in laptop computers because the desktop is exclusively powered from the wall socket. Desktop computers also provide more space for cooling fans and vents to dissipate heat, allowing enthusiasts to overclock with less risk. The two large microprocessor manufacturers, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMD" title="AMD">AMD</a>, have developed <a href="/wiki/Notebook_processor" title="Notebook processor">special CPUs for mobile computers (i.e. laptops)</a> that consume <a href="/wiki/CPU_power_dissipation" title="CPU power dissipation">less power</a> and lower heat, but with lower performance levels.</p>
<p>Laptop computers, conversely, offer portability that desktop systems (including small form factor and all-in-one desktops) can not due to their compact size and clamshell design. The laptop's all-in-one design provides a built-in keyboard and a pointing device (such as a trackpad) for its user, and can draw on power supplied by a rechargeable battery.</p>
<p>Laptops also commonly integrate wireless technologies like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WiFi" title="WiFi">WiFi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> and <a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">3G</a>, giving them a broader range of options for connecting to the internet, though this trend is changing as more desktop computers come integrated with one or more of these technologies.</p>
<p>A desktop computer needs a <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">UPS</a> to handle electrical disturbances like short interruptions, blackouts and spikes; achieving an on-battery time of more than 20–30 minutes for a desktop PC requires a large and expensive UPS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A laptop with sufficiently charged battery can continue to be used for hours in case of a power outage and is not affected by short power interruptions and blackouts.</p>
