<p>The <b>decibel</b> (<b>dB</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logarithmic_unit" title="Logarithmic unit">logarithmic unit</a> used to express the ratio of two values of a <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">physical quantity</a>. One of these values is often a standard reference value, in which case the decibel is used to express the <a href="/wiki/Level_(logarithmic_quantity)" title="Level (logarithmic quantity)">level</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> of the other value relative to this reference.</p>
<p>The number of decibels is ten times the <a href="/wiki/Common_logarithm" title="Common logarithm">logarithm to base 10</a> of the ratio of two power quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> A change in power by a factor of 10 corresponds to a 10 dB change in level. At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_power_point" title="Half power point">half power point</a> an audio circuit or an antenna exhibits an <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a> of approximately 3 dB. A change in amplitude by a factor of 10 results in a change in power by a factor of 100, which corresponds to a 20 dB change in level. A change in amplitude ratio by a factor of 2 (equivalently factor of 4 in power change) approximately corresponds to a 6 dB change in level.</p>
<p>The definition of the decibel is based on the measurement of power in <a href="/wiki/Telephony" title="Telephony">telephony</a> of the early 20th century in the <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> in the United States. One decibel is one tenth of one <b>bel</b>, named in honor of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>; however, the bel is seldom used. Today, the decibel is used for a wide variety of measurements in science and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, most prominently in <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a>. In electronics, the <a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gains</a> of amplifiers, <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a> of signals, and <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">signal-to-noise ratios</a> are often expressed in decibels.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities" title="International System of Quantities">International System of Quantities</a>, the decibel is defined as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">unit of measurement</a> for quantities of type level or level difference, which are defined as the <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithm</a> of the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Field,_power,_and_root-power_quantities" title="Field, power, and root-power quantities">power- or field-type quantities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-standardization2006_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-standardization2006-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The decibel symbol is often qualified with a suffix that indicates the reference quantity that has been used or some other property of the quantity being measured. For example, <a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a> indicates a reference power of one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliwatt" title="Milliwatt">milliwatt</a>, while <a href="#Voltage">dBV</a> is referenced to 1 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a> <a href="/wiki/Root_mean_square" title="Root mean square">RMS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clqgmk_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-clqgmk-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History"><span id="MSC"></span><span id="TU"></span><span id="bel"></span>History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The decibel originates from methods used to quantify signal loss in telegraph and telephone circuits. The unit for loss was originally <i>Miles of Standard Cable</i> (MSC). 1 MSC corresponded to the loss of power over a 1 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mile</a> (approximately 1.6 km) length of standard telephone cable at a frequency of 5000 <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">radians</a> per second (795.8 Hz), and matched closely the smallest attenuation detectable to the average listener. The standard telephone cable implied was "a cable having uniformly distributed resistance of 88 ohms per loop mile and uniformly distributed <a href="/wiki/Shunt_(electrical)" title="Shunt (electrical)">shunt</a> <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a> of 0.054 microfarad per mile" (approximately 19 <a href="/wiki/Wire_gauge" title="Wire gauge">gauge</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1924, <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a> received favorable response to a new unit definition among members of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Advisory_Committee_on_Long_Distance_Telephony&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Advisory Committee on Long Distance Telephony (page does not exist)">International Advisory Committee on Long Distance Telephony</a> in Europe and replaced the MSC with the <i>Transmission Unit</i> (TU). 1 TU was defined such that the number of TUs was ten times the base-10 logarithm of the ratio of measured power to a reference power level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> The definition was conveniently chosen such that 1 TU approximated 1 MSC; specifically, 1 MSC was 1.056 TU. In 1928, the Bell system renamed the TU into the decibel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> being one tenth of a newly defined unit for the base-10 logarithm of the power ratio. It was named the <i>bel</i>, in honor of the telecommunications pioneer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> The bel is seldom used, as the decibel was the proposed working unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The naming and early definition of the decibel is described in the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">NBS</a> Standard's Yearbook of 1931:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Since the earliest days of the telephone, the need for a unit in which to measure the transmission efficiency of telephone facilities has been recognized. The introduction of cable in 1896 afforded a stable basis for a convenient unit and the "mile of standard" cable came into general use shortly thereafter. This unit was employed up to 1923 when a new unit was adopted as being more suitable for modern telephone work. The new transmission unit is widely used among the foreign telephone organizations and recently it was termed the "decibel" at the suggestion of the International Advisory Committee on Long Distance Telephony.</p>
<p>The decibel may be defined by the statement that two amounts of power differ by 1 decibel when they are in the ratio of 10<sup>0.1</sup> and any two amounts of power differ by <i>N</i> decibels when they are in the ratio of 10<sup>'N<i>(0.1)</i></sup><i>. The number of transmission units expressing the ratio of any two powers is therefore ten times the common logarithm of that ratio. This method of designating the gain or loss of power in telephone circuits permits direct addition or subtraction of the units expressing the efficiency of different parts of the circuit...</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In April 2003, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> (CIPM) considered a recommendation for the inclusion of the decibel in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI), but decided against the proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> However, the decibel is recognized by other international bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> (IEC) and <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEC60027-3_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEC60027-3-12">[11]</a></sup> The IEC permits the use of the decibel with field quantities as well as power and this recommendation is followed by many national standards bodies, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIST" title="NIST">NIST</a>, which justifies the use of the decibel for voltage ratios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST2008-13">[12]</a></sup> The term <i>field quantity</i> is <a href="/wiki/Deprecation" title="Deprecation">deprecated</a> by ISO 80000-1, which favors <a href="#Field_quantities">root-power</a>. In spite of their widespread use, <a href="#Suffixes_and_reference_values">suffixes</a> (such as in <a href="/wiki/A-weighting" title="A-weighting">dBA</a> or dBV) are not recognized by the IEC or ISO.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ISO Standard <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_80000" title="ISO/IEC 80000">80000-3:2006</a> defines the following quantities. The decibel (dB) is one-tenth of a bel: <span class="nowrap">1 dB = 0.1 B</span>. The bel (B) is <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> ln(10) <a href="/wiki/Neper" title="Neper">nepers</a>: <span class="nowrap">1 B = <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> ln(10) Np</span>. The neper is the change in the <a href="/wiki/Level_(logarithmic_quantity)" title="Level (logarithmic quantity)">level</a> of a field quantity when the field quantity changes by a factor of <a href="/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant)" title="E (mathematical constant)">e</a>, that is <span class="nowrap">1 Np = ln(e) = 1</span>, thereby relating all of the units as nondimensional natural log of field-quantity ratios, <span class="nowrap">1 dB = 0.11513… Np = 0.11513…</span>. Finally, the level of a quantity is the logarithm of the ratio of the value of that quantity to a reference value of the same kind of quantity.</p>
<p>Therefore, the bel represents the logarithm of a ratio between two power quantities of 10:1, or the logarithm of a ratio between two field quantities of √10:1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two signals whose levels differ by one decibel have a power ratio of 10<sup>1/10</sup>, which is approximately 1.25892, and an amplitude (field quantity) ratio of 10<sup><span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>20</sub></span></sup> (1.12202).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bel is rarely used either without a prefix or with <a href="/wiki/Metric_prefix" title="Metric prefix">SI unit prefixes</a> other than <i><a href="/wiki/Deci-" title="Deci-">deci</a></i>; it is preferred, for example, to use <i>hundredths of a decibel</i> rather than <i>millibels</i>. Thus, five one-thousandths of a bel would normally be written '0.05 dB', and not '5 mB'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The method of expressing a ratio as a level in decibels depends on whether the measured property is a <i>power quantity</i> or a <i>field quantity</i>; see <a href="/wiki/Field,_power,_and_root-power_quantities" title="Field, power, and root-power quantities">Field, power, and root-power quantities</a> for details.</p>

<p>A change in power ratio by a factor of 10 corresponds to a change in level of <span class="nowrap">10 dB</span>. A change in power ratio by a factor of 2 is approximately a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3dB-point" title="3dB-point">change of 3 dB</a>. More precisely, the factor is 10<sup><span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>10</sub></span></sup>, or 1.9953, about 0.24% different from exactly 2. Similarly, an increase of <span class="nowrap">3 dB</span> implies an increase in voltage by a factor of approximately <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">2</span></span>, or about 1.41, an increase of <span class="nowrap">6 dB</span> corresponds to approximately four times the power and twice the voltage, and so on. In exact terms the power ratio is 10<sup><span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>6</sup>⁄<sub>10</sub></span></sup>, or about 3.9811, a relative error of about 0.5%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculations">Calculations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Calculations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In order to add or subtract levels the values that are expressed in decibel first must be divided by 10 (or 20). The definition of the decibel in ISO 80000-3 assigns a value to the decibel (approximately 0.11513) thus the division by 10 in formulas for the addition or subtraction of levels should be a division by 10 dB. Ignoring that can cause a mistake of a factor of 8.686 in the number of decibels. None of the formulas published in ISO standards regarding noise and acoustics take this into account and they shall be used as if the decibel has no value (or is unity).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The decibel has the following properties:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Logarithmic_scale" title="Logarithmic scale">logarithmic scale</a> nature of the decibel means that a very large range of ratios can be represented by a convenient number, in a similar manner to <a href="/wiki/Scientific_notation" title="Scientific notation">scientific notation</a>. This allows one to clearly visualize huge changes of some quantity. See <a href="/wiki/Bode_plot" title="Bode plot">Bode plot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semi-log_plot" title="Semi-log plot">semi-log plot</a>. For example, 120 dB SPL may be clearer than "a trillion times more intense than the threshold of hearing".</li>
<li>Level values in decibels can be added instead of multiplying the underlying power values, which means that the overall gain of a multi-component system, such as a series of <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplifier</a> stages, can be calculated by summing the gains in decibels of the individual components, rather than multiply the amplification factors; that is, log(<i>A</i> × <i>B</i> × <i>C</i>) = log(<i>A</i>) + log(<i>B</i>) + log(<i>C</i>). Practically, this means that, armed only with the knowledge that 1 dB is approximately 26% power gain, 3 dB is approximately 2× power gain, and 10 dB is 10× power gain, it is possible to determine the power ratio of a system from the gain in dB with only simple addition and multiplication. For example:
<dl>
<dd>A system consists of 3 amplifiers in series, with gains (ratio of power out to in) of 10 dB, 8 dB, and 7 dB respectively, for a total gain of 25 dB. Broken into combinations of 10, 3, and 1 dB, this is:
<dl>
<dd>25 dB = 10 dB + 10 dB + 3 dB + 1 dB + 1 dB</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>With an input of 1 watt, the output is approximately
<dl>
<dd>1 W x 10 x 10 x 2 x 1.26 x 1.26 ≈ 317.5 W</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Calculated exactly, the output is 1 W x 10<sup><span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>25</sup>⁄<sub>10</sub></span></sup> = 316.2 W. The approximate value has an error of only +0.4% with respect to the actual value which is negligible given the precision of the values supplied and the accuracy of most measurement instrumentation.</dd>
</dl>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporting_arguments">Supporting arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Supporting arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Mitschke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> "The advantage of using a logarithmic measure is that in a transmission chain, there are many elements concatenated, and each has its own gain or attenuation. To obtain the total, addition of decibel values is much more convenient than multiplication of the individual factors."</p>
<p>The human perception of the intensity of sound and light approximates the logarithm of intensity rather than a linear relationship (<a href="/wiki/Weber%E2%80%93Fechner_law" title="Weber–Fechner law">Weber–Fechner law</a>), making the dB scale a useful measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decibels are still the commonly used units to express ratios in a number of fields, even when the original meaning of the term is obscured. Decibels are the traditional way of expressing gain or margin in such diverse disciplines as control theory, antenna and radio frequency transmission theory, and even assessment of nuclear hardness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Decibel#Dubious" title="Talk:Decibel">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various published articles have criticized the unit decibel as having shortcomings that hinder its understanding and use:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling-28">[27]</a></sup> According to its critics, the decibel creates confusion, obscures reasoning, is more related to the era of <a href="/wiki/Slide_rule" title="Slide rule">slide rules</a> than to modern digital processing, are cumbersome and difficult to interpret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Representing the equivalent of zero watts is not possible, causing problems in conversions. Hickling concludes "Decibels are a useless affectation, which is impeding the development of noise control as an engineering discipline".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common source of confusion in using the decibel occurs when deciding about the use of 10 × log or 20 × log. In the original definition, it was a power measurement, and as employed in that context, the formulation 10 × log should be used, as <i>deci</i> means one tenth. The user must be clear whether the quantity expressed is power or amplitude. It is useful to consider how power or energy is expressed, e.g., current × current × resistance, <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> × velocity × velocity × mass. Where the power is a square function of a field variable (such as voltage, current, or pressure), then 10 × log is the correct expression for the square, or 20 × log for the field variable itself.</p>
<p>Quantities in decibels are not necessarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensional_homogeneity" title="Dimensional homogeneity">additive</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> thus being "of unacceptable form for use in <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the same reason that humans excel at additive operation over multiplication, decibels are awkward in inherently additive operations:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> "if two machines each individually produce a [sound pressure] level of, say, 90 dB at a certain point, then when both are operating together we should expect the combined sound pressure level to increase to 93 dB, but certainly not to 180 dB!" "suppose that the noise from a machine is measured (including the contribution of background noise) and found to be 87 dBA but when the machine is switched off the background noise alone is measured as 83 dBA. ... the machine noise [level (alone)] may be obtained by 'subtracting' the 83 dBA background noise from the combined level of 87 dBA; i.e., 84.8 dBA." "in order to find a representative value of the sound level in a room a number of measurements are taken at different positions within the room, and an average value is calculated. (...) Compare the logarithmic and arithmetic averages of ... 70 dB and 90 dB: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logarithmic_average" title="Logarithmic average">logarithmic average</a> = 87 dB; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_average" title="Arithmetic average">arithmetic average</a> = 80 dB."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics">Acoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Acoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>where <i>p</i><sub>ref</sub> is the standard reference sound pressure of 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micropascal" title="Micropascal">micropascals</a> in air<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElectronicEngineerrsHandbook_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ElectronicEngineerrsHandbook-33">[32]</a></sup> or 1 micropascal in water.</p>
<p>The human ear has a large <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a> in sound reception. The ratio of the sound intensity that causes permanent damage during short exposure to that of the quietest sound that the ear can hear is greater than or equal to 1 trillion (10<sup>12</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Such large measurement ranges are conveniently expressed in <a href="/wiki/Logarithmic_scale" title="Logarithmic scale">logarithmic scale</a>: the base-10 logarithm of 10<sup>12</sup> is 12, which is expressed as a sound pressure level of 120 dB re 20 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">μPa</a>.</p>
<p>Since the human ear is not equally sensitive to all sound frequencies, noise levels at maximum human sensitivity, somewhere between 2 and 4 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">kHz</a>, are factored more heavily into some measurements using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_weighting" title="Frequency weighting">frequency weighting</a>. (See also <a href="/wiki/Stevens%27_power_law" title="Stevens' power law">Stevens' power law</a>.)</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In electronics, the decibel is often used to express power or amplitude ratios (<a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gains</a>), in preference to <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> ratios or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percent" title="Percent">percentages</a>. One advantage is that the total decibel gain of a series of components (such as <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplifiers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attenuator_(electronics)" title="Attenuator (electronics)">attenuators</a>) can be calculated simply by summing the decibel gains of the individual components. Similarly, in telecommunications, decibels denote signal gain or loss from a transmitter to a receiver through some medium (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space_optical_communication" title="Free space optical communication">free space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optics" title="Fiber optics">fiber optics</a>, etc.) using a <a href="/wiki/Link_budget" title="Link budget">link budget</a>.</p>
<p>The decibel unit can also be combined with a suffix to create an absolute unit of electric power. For example, it can be combined with "m" for "milliwatt" to produce the "<a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a>". Zero dBm is the level corresponding to one milliwatt, and 1 dBm is one decibel greater (about 1.259 mW).</p>
<p>In professional audio specifications, a popular unit is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DBu" title="DBu">dBu</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DBu" title="DBu">dBu</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Root_mean_square" title="Root mean square">root mean square</a> (RMS) measurement of voltage that uses as its reference approximately 0.775 V<sub>RMS</sub>. Chosen for historical reasons, the reference value is the voltage level which delivers 1 mW of power in a 600-ohm resistor, which used to be the standard reference impedance in telephone circuits.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optics">Optics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Optics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an <a href="/wiki/Optical_link" title="Optical link">optical link</a>, if a known amount of <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optical</a> power, in <a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a> (referenced to 1 mW), is launched into a <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">fiber</a>, and the losses, in dB (decibels), of each component (e.g., connectors, splices, and lengths of fiber) are known, the overall link loss may be quickly calculated by addition and subtraction of decibel quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In spectrometry and optics, the <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">blocking unit</a> used to measure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_density" title="Optical density">optical density</a> is equivalent to −1 B.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_and_digital_imaging">Video and digital imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Video and digital imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In connection with video and digital <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">image sensors</a>, decibels generally represent ratios of video voltages or digitized light levels, using 20 log of the ratio, even when the represented optical power is directly proportional to the voltage or level, not to its square, as in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCD_imager" title="CCD imager">CCD imager</a> where response voltage is linear in intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Thus, a camera <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">signal-to-noise ratio</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a> of 40 dB represents a power ratio of 100:1 between signal power and noise power, not 10,000:1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Sometimes the 20 log ratio definition is applied to electron counts or photon counts directly, which are proportional to intensity without the need to consider whether the voltage response is linear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, as mentioned above, the 10 log intensity convention prevails more generally in physical optics, including fiber optics, so the terminology can become murky between the conventions of digital photographic technology and physics. Most commonly, quantities called "dynamic range" or "signal-to-noise" (of the camera) would be specified in 20 log dB, but in related contexts (e.g. attenuation, gain, intensifier SNR, or rejection ratio) the term should be interpreted cautiously, as confusion of the two units can result in very large misunderstandings of the value.</p>
<p>Photographers typically use an alternative base-2 log unit, the <a href="/wiki/F-number#Stops.2C_f-stop_conventions.2C_and_exposure" title="F-number">stop</a>, to describe light intensity ratios or dynamic range.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffixes_and_reference_values">Suffixes and reference values<span id="Suffixes"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Suffixes and reference values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suffixes are commonly attached to the basic dB unit in order to indicate the reference value by which the ratio is calculated. For example, dBm indicates power measurement relative to 1 milliwatt.</p>
<p>In cases where the unit value of the reference is stated, the decibel value is known as "absolute". If the unit value of the reference is not explicitly stated, as in the dB gain of an amplifier, then the decibel value is considered relative.</p>
<p>The SI does not permit attaching qualifiers to units, whether as suffix or prefix, other than standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">SI prefixes</a>. Therefore, even though the decibel is accepted for use alongside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">SI units</a>, the practice of attaching a suffix to the basic dB unit, forming compound units such as dBm, dBu, dBA, etc., is not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST2008_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIST2008-13">[12]</a></sup> The proper way, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEC_60027-3" title="IEC 60027-3">IEC 60027-3</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEC60027-3_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEC60027-3-12">[11]</a></sup> is either as <i>L</i><sub><i>x</i></sub> (re <i>x</i><sub>ref</sub>) or as <i>L</i><sub><i>x</i>/<i>x</i><sub>ref</sub></sub>, where <i>x</i> is the quantity symbol and <i>x</i><sub>ref</sub> is the value of the reference quantity, e.g., <i>L</i><sub><i>E</i></sub> (re 1 μV/m) = <i>L</i><sub><i>E</i>/(1 μV/m)</sub> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_field_strength" title="Electric field strength">electric field strength</a> <i>E</i> relative to 1 μV/m reference value.</p>
<p>Outside of documents adhering to SI units, the practice is very common as illustrated by the following examples. There is no general rule, with various discipline-specific practices. Sometimes the suffix is a unit symbol ("W","K","m"), sometimes it is a transliteration of a unit symbol ("uV" instead of μV for microvolt), sometimes it is an acronym for the unit's name ("sm" for square meter, "m" for milliwatt), other times it is a mnemonic for the type of quantity being calculated ("i" for antenna gain with respect to an isotropic antenna, "λ" for anything normalized by the EM wavelength), or otherwise a general attribute or identifier about the nature of the quantity ("A" for <a href="/wiki/A-weighting" title="A-weighting">A-weighted</a> sound pressure level). The suffix is often connected with a dash (dB-Hz), with a space (dB HL), with no intervening character (dBm), or enclosed in parentheses, dB(sm).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics_2">Acoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Acoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Probably the most common usage of "decibels" in reference to sound level is dB SPL, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_pressure_level" title="Sound pressure level">sound pressure level</a> referenced to the nominal threshold of human hearing:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> The measures of pressure (a field quantity) use the factor of 20, and the measures of power (e.g. dB SIL and dB SWL) use the factor of 10.</p>
<dl>
<dt>dB SPL</dt>
<dd>dB SPL (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_pressure_level" title="Sound pressure level">sound pressure level</a>) – for sound in air and other gases, relative to 20 micropascals (μPa) = 2×10<sup>−5</sup> Pa, approximately the quietest sound a human can hear. For <a href="/wiki/Underwater_acoustics" title="Underwater acoustics">sound in water</a> and other liquids, a reference pressure of 1 μPa is used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup>
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
An RMS sound pressure of one pascal corresponds to a level of 94 dB SPL.</dd>
<dt>dB SIL</dt>
<dd>dB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_intensity_level" title="Sound intensity level">sound intensity level</a> – relative to 10<sup>−12</sup> W/m<sup>2</sup>, which is roughly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threshold_of_human_hearing" title="Threshold of human hearing">threshold of human hearing</a> in air.</dd>
<dt>dB SWL</dt>
<dd>dB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_power_level" title="Sound power level">sound power level</a> – relative to 10<sup>−12</sup> W.</dd>
<dt>dB(A), dB(B), and dB(C)</dt>
<dd>These symbols are often used to denote the use of different <a href="/wiki/Weighting_filter" title="Weighting filter">weighting filters</a>, used to approximate the human ear's <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(psychology)" title="Stimulus (psychology)">response</a> to sound, although the measurement is still in dB (SPL). These measurements usually refer to noise and its effects on humans and other animals, and they are widely used in industry while discussing noise control issues, regulations and environmental standards. Other variations that may be seen are dB<sub>A</sub> or <a href="/wiki/A-weighting" title="A-weighting">dBA</a>. According to ANSI standards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> the preferred usage is to write L<sub>A</sub> = x dB. Nevertheless, the units dBA and dB(A) are still commonly used as a shorthand for A-weighted measurements. Compare <a href="/wiki/DBc" title="DBc">dBc</a>, used in telecommunications.</dd>
<dt>dB HL</dt>
<dd>dB hearing level is used in <a href="/wiki/Audiogram" title="Audiogram">audiograms</a> as a measure of hearing loss. The reference level varies with frequency according to a <a href="/wiki/Minimum_audibility_curve" title="Minimum audibility curve">minimum audibility curve</a> as defined in ANSI and other standards, such that the resulting audiogram shows deviation from what is regarded as 'normal' hearing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
<dt>dB Q</dt>
<dd>sometimes used to denote weighted noise level, commonly using the <a href="/wiki/ITU-R_468_noise_weighting" title="ITU-R 468 noise weighting">ITU-R 468 noise weighting</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audio_electronics">Audio electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Audio electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>See also dBV and dBu above.</p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a></dt>
<dd>dB(mW) – power relative to 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliwatt" title="Milliwatt">milliwatt</a>. In audio and telephony, dBm is typically referenced relative to a 600 ohm impedance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> which corresponds to a voltage level of 0.775 volts or 775 millivolts.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/DBFS" title="DBFS">dBFS</a></dt>
<dd>dB(<a href="/wiki/Full_scale" title="Full scale">full scale</a>) – the <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a> of a signal compared with the maximum which a device can handle before <a href="/wiki/Clipping_(signal_processing)" title="Clipping (signal processing)">clipping</a> occurs. Full-scale may be defined as the power level of a full-scale <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sinusoid</a> or alternatively a full-scale <a href="/wiki/Square_wave" title="Square wave">square wave</a>. A signal measured with reference to a full-scale sine-wave will appear 3 dB weaker when referenced to a full-scale square wave, thus: 0 dBFS(fullscale sine wave) = −3 dBFS(fullscale square wave).</dd>
<dt>dBTP</dt>
<dd>dB(true peak) - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peak_amplitude" title="Peak amplitude">peak amplitude</a> of a signal compared with the maximum which a device can handle before clipping occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> In digital systems, 0 dBTP would equal the highest level (number) the processor is capable of representing. Measured values are always negative or zero, since they are less than or equal to full-scale.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/DBm0" title="DBm0">dBm0</a></dt>
<dd>Power in dBm measured at a zero transmission level point.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radar">Radar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Radar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/DBZ_(meteorology)" title="DBZ (meteorology)">dBZ</a></dt>
<dd>dB(Z) – decibel relative to Z = 1 mm<sup>6</sup>·m<sup>−3</sup>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> energy of reflectivity (weather radar), related to the amount of transmitted power returned to the radar receiver. Values above 15–20 dBZ usually indicate falling precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>dBsm</dt>
<dd>dB(m<sup>2</sup>) – decibel relative to one square meter: measure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_cross_section" title="Radar cross section">radar cross section</a> (RCS) of a target. The power reflected by the target is proportional to its RCS. "Stealth" aircraft and insects have negative RCS measured in dBsm, large flat plates or non-stealthy aircraft have positive values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_power.2C_energy.2C_and_field_strength">Radio power, energy, and field strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Radio power, energy, and field strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/DBc" title="DBc">dBc</a></dt>
<dd>relative to carrier—in <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a>, this indicates the relative levels of noise or sideband power, compared with the carrier power. Compare dBC, used in acoustics.</dd>
<dt>dBJ</dt>
<dd>energy relative to 1 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>. 1 joule = 1 watt second = 1 watt per hertz, so <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_spectral_density" title="Power spectral density">power spectral density</a> can be expressed in dBJ.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a></dt>
<dd>dB(mW) – power relative to 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliwatt" title="Milliwatt">milliwatt</a>. In the radio field, dBm is usually referenced to a 50 ohm load, with the resultant voltage being 0.224 volts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>dBμV/m, dBuV/m, or dBμ</dt>
<dd><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dB.C2.B5_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-dB.C2.B5-52">[51]</a></sup> dB(μV/m) – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_field_strength" title="Electric field strength">electric field strength</a> relative to 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microvolt" title="Microvolt">microvolt</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">meter</a>. Often used to specify the signal strength from a <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast" title="Broadcast">broadcast</a> at a receiving site (the signal measured <i>at the antenna output</i> will be in dBμV).</dd>
<dt>dBf</dt>
<dd>dB(fW) – power relative to 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Femtowatt" title="Femtowatt">femtowatt</a>.</dd>
<dt>dBW</dt>
<dd>dB(W) – power relative to 1 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>.</dd>
<dt>dBk</dt>
<dd>dB(kW) – power relative to 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatt</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antenna_measurements">Antenna measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Antenna measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>dBi</dt>
<dd>dB(isotropic) – the forward <a href="/wiki/Antenna_gain" title="Antenna gain">gain of an antenna</a> compared with the hypothetical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic_antenna" title="Isotropic antenna">isotropic antenna</a>, which uniformly distributes energy in all directions. <a href="/wiki/Linear_polarization" title="Linear polarization">Linear polarization</a> of the EM field is assumed unless noted otherwise.</dd>
<dt>dBd</dt>
<dd>dB(dipole) – the forward gain of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antenna_(electronics)" title="Antenna (electronics)">antenna</a> compared with a half-wave <a href="/wiki/Dipole_antenna" title="Dipole antenna">dipole antenna</a>. 0 dBd = 2.15 dBi</dd>
<dt>dBiC</dt>
<dd>dB(isotropic circular) – the forward gain of an antenna compared to a <a href="/wiki/Circular_polarization" title="Circular polarization">circularly polarized</a> isotropic antenna. There is no fixed conversion rule between dBiC and dBi, as it depends on the receiving antenna and the field polarization.</dd>
<dt>dBq</dt>
<dd>dB(quarterwave) – the forward gain of an antenna compared to a quarter wavelength whip. Rarely used, except in some marketing material. 0 dBq = −0.85 dBi</dd>
<dt>dBsm</dt>
<dd>dB(m<sup>2</sup>) – decibel relative to one square meter: measure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antenna_effective_area" title="Antenna effective area">antenna effective area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>dBm<sup>−1</sup></dt>
<dd>dB(m<sup>−1</sup>) – decibel relative to reciprocal of meter: measure of the <a href="/wiki/Antenna_factor" title="Antenna factor">antenna factor</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_measurements">Other measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Other measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fractions">Fractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decibel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Fractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">Attenuation</a> constants, in fields such as <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a> communication and <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">radio propagation</a> <a href="/wiki/Path_loss" title="Path loss">path loss</a>, are often expressed as a <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)" title="Fraction (mathematics)">fraction</a> or ratio to distance of transmission. <i>dB/m</i> means decibels per meter, <i>dB/mi</i> is decibels per mile, for example. These quantities are to be manipulated obeying the rules of <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>, e.g., a 100-meter run with a 3.5 dB/km fiber yields a loss of 0.35 dB = 3.5 dB/km × 0.1 km.</p>
