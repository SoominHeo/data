<p><i><b>There Will Be Blood</b></i> is a 2007 American <a href="/wiki/Epic_film" title="Epic film">epic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_drama_film" title="Historical drama film">historical drama film</a> written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Thomas_Anderson" title="Paul Thomas Anderson">Paul Thomas Anderson</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Day-Lewis" title="Daniel Day-Lewis">Daniel Day-Lewis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dano" title="Paul Dano">Paul Dano</a>. The film was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">Upton Sinclair</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Oil!" title="Oil!">Oil!</a></i> It tells the story of a silver miner-turned-oilman (Day-Lewis) on a ruthless quest for wealth during <a href="/wiki/History_of_oil_in_California_through_1930" title="History of oil in California through 1930">Southern California's oil boom</a> of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. <a href="/wiki/Kevin_J._O%27Connor_(actor)" title="Kevin J. O'Connor (actor)">Kevin J. O'Connor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ciar%C3%A1n_Hinds" title="Ciarán Hinds">Ciarán Hinds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dillon_Freasier" title="Dillon Freasier">Dillon Freasier</a> are also featured in the film.</p>
<p>The film received significant critical praise, with the performance of Day-Lewis as Daniel Plainview receiving overwhelming acclaim and other aspects such as cinematography, direction and screenplay being praised as well, receiving numerous awards and nominations. It premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin International Film Festival</a>, where it won the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Bear_for_Best_Director" title="Silver Bear for Best Director">Silver Bear Award for Best Director</a> and a Special Artistic Contribution Award for <a href="/wiki/Jonny_Greenwood" title="Jonny Greenwood">Jonny Greenwood</a>'s score. It also appeared on many critics' "top ten" lists for the year, notably the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute_Awards_2007" title="American Film Institute Awards 2007">American Film Institute</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/National_Society_of_Film_Critics" title="National Society of Film Critics">National Society of Film Critics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Board_of_Review:_Top_Ten_Films#2000s" title="National Board of Review: Top Ten Films">National Board of Review</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Film_Critics_Association" title="Los Angeles Film Critics Association">Los Angeles Film Critics Association</a>. Day-Lewis won <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Oscar</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">BAFTA</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">Screen Actors Guild</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYFCC_Award" title="NYFCC Award">NYFCC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFTA_Award" title="IFTA Award">IFTA</a> Best Actor awards for his performance, cementing his position as one of the most acclaimed actors of his generation. The film was nominated for eight <a href="/wiki/80th_Academy_Awards" title="80th Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>, winning <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Best Actor</a> for Day-Lewis and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Cinematography" title="Academy Award for Best Cinematography">Best Cinematography</a> for <a href="/wiki/Robert_Elswit" title="Robert Elswit">Robert Elswit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>There Will Be Blood</i> is now widely regarded as one of the greatest films of the 2000s, and was among the highest-ranking 21st century films in the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s 2012 <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> polls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1898, Daniel Plainview, a prospector in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_Territory" title="New Mexico Territory">New Mexico</a>, mines a potentially precious ore <a href="/wiki/Vein_(geology)" title="Vein (geology)">vein</a> from a pit mine hole. In the process of <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamiting</a> the <a href="/wiki/Lode" title="Lode">lode</a>, he falls from a broken rung of the tunnel ladder and breaks his leg. He saves a silver sample, climbs out of the mine, and drags himself to the nearest assay office to evaluate his find and receives a silver and gold certificate <a href="/wiki/Land_claim" title="Land claim">claim</a>. In 1902, he <a href="/wiki/History_of_oil_in_California_through_1930" title="History of oil in California through 1930">discovers oil</a> near <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and establishes a small drilling company. Following the death of a worker in an accident, Daniel adopts the man's orphaned son. The boy, named H. W., becomes his nominal "business partner", allowing Daniel to paint himself to potential investors as "a family man".</p>
<p>In 1911, Daniel is approached by Paul Sunday, who tells him of an oil deposit under his family's property in Little Boston, California. Daniel attempts to buy the farm at a bargain price. However, Eli, Paul's twin brother and pastor of a local church, is aware of his plan. Eli demands $10,000 and states that it is for the church. An agreement is made and Daniel goes on to acquire all the available land in the area, except for one holdout: William Bandy. Oil production begins, but an on-site accident kills a worker and a <a href="/wiki/Blowout_(well_drilling)" title="Blowout (well drilling)">gas blowout</a> robs H. W. of his hearing. Eli blames the disasters on the well not being properly blessed. When Eli demands the $5,000 Daniel still owes his family, Daniel beats and humiliates him. At the dinner table, Eli berates his father for trusting Daniel.</p>
<p>A man arrives at Daniel's doorstep, claiming to be his half-brother, Henry. Daniel hires Henry to work for him, and the two grow closer. H. W. sets fire to their house, intending for it to kill Henry. Angered by his son's behavior, Daniel sends him away to a school for the deaf in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, and leaves him on the train by himself. A representative from <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil" title="Standard Oil">Standard Oil</a> offers to buy out Daniel's local interests, but Daniel elects to strike a deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Oil" title="Union Oil">Union Oil</a> and construct a pipeline to the California coast, though the Bandy ranch remains an impediment.</p>
<p>While reminiscing about his childhood, Daniel becomes suspicious of Henry and one night holds him at gunpoint. "Henry" confesses that he was a friend of the real Henry, who died from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. In a fit of rage, Daniel murders the impostor and buries his body. The next morning, Daniel is awakened by Mr. Bandy, who knows of the previous night's events and wants Daniel to repent. At the church, as part of Daniel's <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>, Eli humiliates him and coerces him into acknowledging that he is a bad father. Some time later, as the pipeline is well under way, H. W. returns and reunites with Daniel, while Eli leaves town to perform missionary work.</p>
<p>In 1927, H. W. marries Eli and Paul's sister Mary. Daniel, now extremely wealthy but a raging alcoholic, lives as a recluse in a large mansion. Through a <a href="/wiki/Language_interpretation#Sign_language" title="Language interpretation">sign language interpreter</a>, H. W. asks Daniel to dissolve their partnership so that he can establish his own oil company in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Daniel reacts brutally, mocks H. W.'s deafness and reveals his true origins as an orphan. H. W. leaves, but not before telling Daniel he is now grateful that they are not related.</p>
<p>Soon after, Eli visits Daniel, who is drunk and passed out in his private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowling_alley" title="Bowling alley">bowling alley</a>. Eli, now a radio preacher, offers to sell Daniel the land of William Bandy, who recently died. Daniel agrees on the condition that Eli loudly denounces his faith and his own credibility. Swallowing his pride, Eli reluctantly does so. Daniel reveals that the property is now worthless because he has already drained its oil through surrounding wells. Shaken and desperate, Eli confesses to being in dire financial straits. Daniel taunts Eli and claims that Paul now has his own oil company. Daniel then snaps and begins attacking Eli, and chases him around in the bowling room. Eventually, Daniel kills Eli by smashing his head in with a bowling pin. When Daniel's butler comes down to check on him, Daniel says casually, "I'm finished."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2208" data-file-width="2504" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg/220px-Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg/330px-Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg/440px-Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Thomas_Anderson_%26_Daniel_Day-Lewis-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Paul_Thomas_Anderson" title="Paul Thomas Anderson">Paul Thomas Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Day-Lewis" title="Daniel Day-Lewis">Daniel Day-Lewis</a> in New York, December 2007.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Eric_Schlosser" title="Eric Schlosser">Eric Schlosser</a> finished writing <i><a href="/wiki/Fast_Food_Nation" title="Fast Food Nation">Fast Food Nation</a></i>, reporters kept asking him about Upton Sinclair, and although he had read Sinclair's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">The Jungle</a></i>, he did not know about his other works or anything about Sinclair himself. He decided to read most of Sinclair's works, and eventually read the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Oil!" title="Oil!">Oil!</a></i>, which he loved. Schlosser, who found the book to be exciting and thought it would make a great film, sought out the Sinclair estate and purchased the film rights. He then thought that he would try to find a director that was as passionate about the book as he was, but Paul Thomas Anderson approached him first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ES-TNYT-2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ES-TNYT-2008-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson had been working on a screenplay about two fighting families. He struggled with the script and soon realized it was not working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup> Homesick, he purchased a copy of <i>Oil!</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London,_England" title="London, England">London</a>, drawn to its cover illustration of a California oilfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin-7">[7]</a></sup> As he read, Anderson became even more fascinated with the novel, and after contacting Schlosser, adapted the first 150 pages to a screenplay. He began to get a real sense of where his script was going after making many trips to museums dedicated to early oilmen in <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup> Anderson changed the title from <i>Oil!</i> to <i>There Will Be Blood</i> because he felt "there's not enough of the book to feel like it's a proper adaptation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup> He said of writing the screenplay:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I can remember the way that my desk looked, with so many different scraps of paper and books about the oil industry in the early 20th century, mixed in with pieces of other scripts that I'd written. Everything was coming from so many different sources. But the book was a great stepping-stone. It was so cohesive, the way Upton Sinclair wrote about that period, and his experiences around the oil fields and these independent oilmen. That said, the book is so long that it's only the first couple hundred pages that we ended up using, because there is a certain point where he strays really far from what the original story is. We were really unfaithful to the book. That's not to say I didn't really like the book; I loved it. But there were so many other things floating around. And at a certain point, I became aware of the stuff he was basing it on. What he was writing about was the life of [oil barons] <a href="/wiki/Edward_L._Doheny" title="Edward L. Doheny">Edward Doheny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ford_Sinclair" title="Harry Ford Sinclair">Harry Sinclair</a>. So it was like having a really good collaborator, the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modell_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Modell-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Anderson, who had previously stated that he would like to work with Daniel Day-Lewis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patterson_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patterson-10">[10]</a></sup> wrote the screenplay with Day-Lewis in mind and approached the actor when the script was nearly complete. Anderson had heard that Daniel Day-Lewis liked his earlier film <i><a href="/wiki/Punch-Drunk_Love" title="Punch-Drunk Love">Punch-Drunk Love</a></i>, which gave him the confidence to hand Day-Lewis a copy of the incomplete script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimesLewis_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesLewis-11">[11]</a></sup> According to Day-Lewis, being asked to do the film was enough to convince him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freydkin_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freydkin-12">[12]</a></sup> In an interview with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_York_Observer" title="The New York Observer">The New York Observer</a></i>, he elaborated that what drew him to the project was "the understanding that [Anderson] had already entered into that world, [he] wasn't observing it – [he'd] entered into it – and indeed [he'd] populated it with characters who [he] felt had lives of their own".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vilkomerson_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vilkomerson-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson has stated that the famous line in the final scene, "I drink your <a href="/wiki/Milkshake" title="Milkshake">milkshake</a>!", was paraphrased from a quote by former Secretary of the Interior and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senator</a> from <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Fall" title="Albert Fall">Albert Fall</a>, speaking before a Congressional investigation into the 1920s oil-related <a href="/wiki/Teapot_Dome_scandal" title="Teapot Dome scandal">Teapot Dome scandal</a>. Anderson said he was fascinated "to see that word [milkshake] among all this official testimony and terminology" to explain the complicated process of oil drainage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2013, an independent attempt to locate the statement in Fall's testimony proved unsuccessful—an article published in the Case Western Reserve Law Review suggested that the actual source of the paraphrased quote may instead have been remarks in 2003 by Sen. <a href="/wiki/Pete_Domenici" title="Pete Domenici">Pete Domenici</a> of New Mexico during a debate over drilling in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Arctic National Wildlife Refuge">Arctic National Wildlife Refuge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In those remarks, Domenici stated:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The oil is underground, and it is going to be drilled and come up. Here is a giant reservoir underground. Just like a curved straw, you put it underground and maneuver it, and the 'milk shake' is way over there, and your little child wants the milk shake, and they sit over here in their bedroom where they are feeling ill, and they just gobble it up from way down in the kitchen, where you don't even have to move the Mix Master that made the ice cream for them. You don't have to take it up to the bedroom. This describes the actual drilling that is taking place.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to Joanne Sellar, one of the film's producers, it was a hard film to finance because "the studios didn't think it had the scope of a major picture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin-7">[7]</a></sup> It took two years to acquire financing for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup> For the role of Plainview's "son", Anderson looked at people in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, but he realized that they needed someone from <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> who knew how to shoot <a href="/wiki/Shotgun" title="Shotgun">shotguns</a> and "live in that world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup> The filmmakers asked around at a school and the principal recommended <a href="/wiki/Dillon_Freasier" title="Dillon Freasier">Dillon Freasier</a>. They did not have him read any scenes and instead talked to him, realizing that he was the perfect person for the role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>To build his character, Day-Lewis started with the voice. Anderson sent him recordings from the late 19th century to 1927 and a copy of the 1948 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Treasure_of_the_Sierra_Madre_(film)" title="The Treasure of the Sierra Madre (film)">The Treasure of the Sierra Madre</a></i>, including documentaries on its director, <a href="/wiki/John_Huston" title="John Huston">John Huston</a>, an important influence on Anderson's film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin-7">[7]</a></sup> According to Anderson, he was inspired by the fact that <i>Sierra Madre</i> is "about greed and ambition and paranoia and looking at the worst parts of yourself".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup> While writing the script, he would put the film on before he went to bed at night. To research for the role, Day-Lewis read letters from laborers and studied photographs from the time period. He also read up on oil tycoon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Doheny" title="Edward Doheny">Edward Doheny</a>, upon whom Sinclair's book is loosely based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> began in June 2006 on a ranch in <a href="/wiki/Marfa,_Texas" title="Marfa, Texas">Marfa, Texas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup> and took three months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin-7">[7]</a></sup> Other location shooting took place in Los Angeles. Anderson tried to shoot the script in sequence with most of the sets on the ranch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup> Two weeks in, Anderson replaced the actor playing Eli Sunday with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dano" title="Paul Dano">Paul Dano</a>, who had originally only been cast in the much smaller role of Paul Sunday, the brother who tipped off Plainview about the oil on the Sunday ranch. A profile of Day-Lewis in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i> suggested that the original actor, <a href="/wiki/Kel_O%27Neill" title="Kel O'Neill">Kel O'Neill</a>, had been intimidated by Day-Lewis's intensity and habit of staying in character on and off the set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-17">[17]</a></sup> Both Anderson and Day-Lewis deny this claim,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-17">[17]</a></sup> and Day-Lewis stated, "I absolutely don't believe that it was because he was intimidated by me. I happen to believe that—and I hope I'm right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson first saw Dano in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Jack_and_Rose" title="The Ballad of Jack and Rose">The Ballad of Jack and Rose</a></i> and thought that he would be perfect to play Paul Sunday, a role he originally envisioned to be a 12- or 13-year-old boy. Dano only had four days to prepare for the much larger role of Eli Sunday,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Public_Radio_Audio_Interview.2C_January_8.2C_2008_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-National_Public_Radio_Audio_Interview.2C_January_8.2C_2008-19">[19]</a></sup> but he researched the time period that the film is set in as well as <a href="/wiki/Evangelism" title="Evangelism">evangelical</a> preachers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup> The previous two weeks of scenes with Sunday and Plainview had to be re-shot with Dano instead of O'Neill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg-8">[8]</a></sup> The interior mansion scenes were filmed at the <a href="/wiki/Greystone_Mansion" title="Greystone Mansion">Greystone Mansion</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_California" title="Beverly Hills, California">Beverly Hills</a>, the former real-life home of Edward Doheny Jr., a gift from his father, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Doheny" title="Edward Doheny">Edward Doheny</a>. Scenes filmed at Greystone involved the careful renovation of the basement's two-lane bowling alley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimesHobart_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesHobart-20">[20]</a></sup> Anderson said it was "a particular situation, because it was so narrow that there could only be a very limited number of people at any given time, maybe five or six behind the camera and then the two boys."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modell_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Modell-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson dedicated the film to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Altman" title="Robert Altman">Robert Altman</a>, who died while Anderson was editing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>There Will Be Blood</i> was shot using <a href="/wiki/Panavision" title="Panavision">Panavision</a> XL <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/35_mm" title="35 mm">35 mm</a> cameras outfitted primarily with Panavision C series and high-speed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anamorphic" title="Anamorphic">anamorphic</a> lenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DigContentProducer_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-DigContentProducer-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Day-Lewis broke a rib in a fall during filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/There_Will_Be_Blood_(album)" title="There Will Be Blood (album)">There Will Be Blood (album)</a></div>
<p>Anderson had been a fan of <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>'s music and was impressed with <a href="/wiki/Jonny_Greenwood" title="Jonny Greenwood">Jonny Greenwood</a>'s scoring of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Bodysong" title="Bodysong">Bodysong</a></i>. While writing the script for <i>There Will Be Blood</i>, Anderson heard Greenwood's orchestral piece "Popcorn Superhet Receiver", which prompted him to ask Greenwood to work with him. After initially agreeing to score the film, Greenwood had doubts and thought about backing out, but Anderson's reassurance and enthusiasm for the film convinced him to stick with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willman_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ponto_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ponto-24">[24]</a></sup> Anderson gave Greenwood a copy of the film and three weeks later he came back with two hours of music recorded at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-6">[6]</a></sup> Concerning his approach to composing the soundtrack, Greenwood said to <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I think it was about not necessarily just making period music, which very traditionally you would do. But because they were traditional orchestral sounds, I suppose that's what we hoped was a little unsettling, even though you know all the sounds you're hearing are coming from very old technology. You can just do things with the classical orchestra that do unsettle you, that are sort of slightly wrong, that have some kind of undercurrent that's slightly sinister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In December 2008, Greenwood's score was nominated for a Grammy in the category of "Best Score Soundtrack Album For Motion Picture, Television Or Other Visual Media" for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/51st_Grammy_Awards" title="51st Grammy Awards">51st Grammy Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It features classical music. Brahms' <i><a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Brahms)" title="Violin Concerto (Brahms)">Violin Concerto in D major, Op. 77, 3rd movement, "Vivace ma non troppo"</a></i> can be heard in the ending titles and during the film <a href="/wiki/Arvo_P%C3%A4rt" title="Arvo Pärt">Arvo Pärt</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fratres" title="Fratres">Fratres</a></i> for cello and piano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sarris" title="Andrew Sarris">Andrew Sarris</a> called the film "an impressive achievement in its confident expertness in rendering the simulated realities of a bygone time and place, largely with an inspired use of regional amateur actors and extras with all the right moves and sounds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarris_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarris-28">[28]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Premiere_(magazine)" title="Premiere (magazine)">Premiere</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Kenny" title="Glenn Kenny">Glenn Kenny</a> praised Day-Lewis's performance: "Once his Plainview takes wing, the relentless focus of the performance makes the character unique."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenny_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenny-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Manohla_Dargis" title="Manohla Dargis">Manohla Dargis</a> wrote, in her review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, "the film is above all a consummate work of art, one that transcends the historically fraught context of its making, and its pleasures are unapologetically aesthetic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dargis_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dargis-30">[30]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i> also praised Day-Lewis' performance: "what's most fun, albeit in a frightening way, is watching this greedmeister become more and more unhinged as he locks horns with Eli Sunday … both Anderson and Day-Lewis go for broke. But it's a pleasure to be reminded, if only once every four years, that subtlety can be overrated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dangelo_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dangelo-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Schickel" title="Richard Schickel">Richard Schickel</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> praised <i>There Will Be Blood</i> as "one of the most wholly original American movies ever made."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schickel_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schickel-32">[32]</a></sup> Critic Tom Charity, writing about <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>'s ten-best films list, calls the film the only "flat-out masterpiece" of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charity_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Charity-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schickel also named the film one of the Top 10 Movies of 2007, ranking it at #9, calling Daniel Day-Lewis' performance "astonishing", and calling the film "a mesmerizing meditation on the American spirit in all its maddening ambiguities: mean and noble, angry and secretive, hypocritical and more than a little insane in its aspirations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>James Christopher, chief film critic for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, published a list in April 2008 of his top 100 films, placing <i>There Will Be Blood</i> in second place, behind only <i><a href="/wiki/Casablanca_(film)" title="Casablanca (film)">Casablanca</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i> placed it at number three in their list of the 50 best movies of Total Film's lifetime,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> ranked it 17th best arthouse film of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, some critics were less laudatory. <a href="/wiki/Mick_LaSalle" title="Mick LaSalle">Mick LaSalle</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> challenged the film's high praise by saying "there should be no need to pretend <i>There Will Be Blood</i> is a masterpiece just because Anderson sincerely tried to make it one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFCreview_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFCreview-38">[38]</a></sup> Several months after his initial review of the film, LaSalle reiterated that while he felt it was "clear" that <i>There Will Be Blood</i> was not a masterpiece, he wondered if its "style, an approach, an attitude... might become important in the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maximum_Strength_Mick_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maximum_Strength_Mick-39">[39]</a></sup> Carla Meyer, of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Bee" title="Sacramento Bee">Sacramento Bee</a></i>, gave the film three and a half out of four stars; while calling it a "masterpiece", she said that the final confrontation between Daniel and Eli marked when <i>There Will Be Blood</i> "stops being a masterpiece and becomes a really good movie. What was grand becomes petty, then overwrought."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> gave the film three-and-a-half out of four stars, saying that, "<i>There Will Be Blood</i> is the kind of film that is easily called great. I am not sure of its greatness. It was filmed in the same area of Texas used by <i><a href="/wiki/No_Country_for_Old_Men_(film)" title="No Country for Old Men (film)">No Country for Old Men</a></i>, and that is a great film, and a perfect one. But <i>There Will Be Blood</i> is not perfect, and in its imperfections (its unbending characters, its lack of women or any reflection of ordinary society, its ending, its relentlessness) we may see its reach exceeding its grasp. Which is not a dishonorable thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2008, the film has been included in the book <i><a href="/wiki/1001_Movies_You_Must_See_Before_You_Die" title="1001 Movies You Must See Before You Die">1001 Movies You Must See Before You Die</a></i> and every revised edition released afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Review aggregation website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives a score of 91% based on reviews from 216 critics, with an average rating of 8.4/10. The consensus reads, "Widely touted as a masterpiece, this sparse and sprawling epic about the underhanded 'heroes' of capitalism boasts incredible performances by leads Daniel Day-Lewis and Paul Dano, and is director Paul Thomas Anderson's best work to date."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has an average score of 92/100, based on 39 reviews indicating "universal acclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes_and_analysis">Themes and analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Themes and analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many have seen the film as a commentary on the nature of capitalism and greed, and its inherent national presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Daniel Plainview's "I have a competition in me" speech has been looked at as key when analyzing the film from this angle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> David Denby of the <i>New Yorker</i> described the film as being about "...the driving force of capitalism as it both creates and destroys the future..." and goes on to say that "this movie is about the vanishing American frontier. The thrown-together buildings look scraggly and unkempt, the homesteaders are modest, stubborn, and reticent, but, in their undreamed-of future, Wal-Mart is on the way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others have noted themes of faith, religion, and family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Many feel that the contrast between Daniel and Eli highlights the disparity between faith and avarice. James Christopher of <i>The Times</i> viewed the film as "...a biblical parable about America's failure to square religion and greed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Others have noted how the absence of family and resulting isolation in Daniel's life plays a large role in his eventual descent into madness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> By the film's end, having felt betrayed by both Henry (his false brother) and H.W (his non-biological son), he reacts by murdering Eli. Critics and essayists also argue that these killings and rejections are Daniel's way of eliminating any possible source of competition in the oil industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Top_ten_lists">Top ten lists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Top ten lists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The film was on the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>'s 10 Movies of the Year; AFI's jury said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>There Will Be Blood</i> is bravura film-making by one of American film's modern masters. Paul Thomas Anderson's epic poem of savagery, optimism and obsession is a true meditation on America. The film drills down into the dark heart of capitalism, where domination, not gain, is the ultimate goal. In a career defined by transcendent performances, Daniel Day-Lewis creates a character so rich and so towering, that "Daniel Plainview" will haunt the history of film for generations to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Best_films_of_the_decade.22_lists">"Best films of the decade" lists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title='Edit section: "Best films of the decade" lists'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Review aggregator site <i><a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a></i>, when comparing over 40 'top ten of the decade' lists from various notable publications, found <i>There Will Be Blood</i> to be the most mentioned, appearing on 46% of critics' lists and being ranked the decade's best film on five of them.</p>
<p>In December 2009, Peter Travers of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> chose the film as the #1 film of the decade, saying:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Two years after first seeing <i>There Will Be Blood</i>, I am convinced that Paul Thomas Anderson's profound portrait of an American primitive—take that, <i>Citizen Kane</i>—deserves pride of place among the decade's finest. Daniel Day-Lewis gave the best and ballsiest performance of the past 10 years. As Daniel Plainview, a prospector who loots the land of its natural resources in silver and oil to fill his pockets and gargantuan ego, he showed us a man draining his humanity for power. And Anderson, having extended Plainview's rage from Earth to heaven in the form of a corrupt preacher (Paul Dano), managed to "drink the milkshake" of other risk-taking directors. If I had to stake the future of film in the next decade on one filmmaker, I'd go with PTA. Even more than <i>Boogie Nights</i> and <i>Magnolia</i>—his rebel cries from the 1990s—<i>Blood</i> let Anderson put technology at the service of character. The score by Radiohead's Jonny Greenwood was a sonic explosion that reinvented what film music could be. And the images captured by Robert Elswit, a genius of camera and lighting, made visual poetry out of an oil well consumed by flame. For the final word on <i>Blood</i>, I'll quote Plainview: "It was one goddamn hell of a show."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/At_the_Movies_(U.S._TV_series)" title="At the Movies (U.S. TV series)">At the Movies</a></i> critic <a href="/wiki/Michael_Phillips_(critic)" title="Michael Phillips (critic)">Michael Phillips</a> named <i>There Will Be Blood</i> the decade's best film. Phillips stated:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>This most eccentric and haunting of modern epics is driven by oilman Daniel Plainview, who, in the hands of actor Daniel Day-Lewis, becomes a Horatio Alger story gone horribly wrong. Writer/director Paul Thomas Anderson's camera is as crucial to the films hypnotic pull as the performance at its center. For its evocation of the early 1900s, its relentless focus on one man's fascinating obsessions, and for its inspiring example of how to freely adapt a novel—plus, what I think is the performance of the new century—<i>There Will Be Blood</i> stands alone. The more I see it, the sadder, and stranger, and more visually astounding it grows—and the more it seems to say about the best and worst in the American ethos of rugged individualism. Awfully good!<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> critic Lisa Schwarzbaum named <i>There Will Be Blood</i> the decade's best film as well. In her original review, Schwarzbaum stated:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Anyhow, a fierce story meshing big exterior-oriented themes of American character with an interior-oriented portrait of an impenetrable man (two men, really, including the false prophet Sunday) is only half Anderson's quest, and his exciting achievement. The other half lies in the innovation applied to the telling itself. For a huge picture, There Will Be Blood is exquisitely intimate, almost a collection of sketches. For a long, slow movie, it speeds. For a story set in the fabled bad-old-days past, it's got the terrors of modernity in its DNA. Leaps of romantic chordal grandeur from Brahms' Violin Concerto in D Major announce the launch of a fortune-changing oil well down the road from Eli Sunday's church—and then, much later, announce a kind of end of the world. For bleakness, the movie can't be beat—nor for brilliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In December 2009, the website <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gawker.com" title="Gawker.com">Gawker.com</a> determined that <i>There Will Be Blood</i> is film critics' consensus best film of the decade when aggregating all Best of the Decade lists, stating: "And when the votes were all in, by a nose, <i>There Will Be Blood</i> stood alone at the top of the decade, its straw in the whole damn cinema's milkshake."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office_performance">Box office performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Box office performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first public screening of <i>There Will Be Blood</i> was on September 29, 2007, at <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Fest" title="Fantastic Fest">Fantastic Fest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. The film was released on December 26, 2007, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a> where it grossed <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>190,739 on its opening weekend. The film then opened in 885 theaters in selected markets on January 25, 2008, grossing $4.8 million on its opening weekend. The film went on to make $40.2 million in North America and $35.9 million in the rest of the world, with a worldwide total of $76.1 million, well above its $25 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoxOfficeMojo_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoxOfficeMojo-1">[1]</a></sup> But the prints and advertising cost for the film's <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> release was about $40 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was released on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> on April 8, 2008. It was released with one and two-disc editions, both are packaged in a cardboard case. Anderson has refused to record an <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">audio commentary</a> for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">HD DVD</a> release was announced, but later canceled due to the discontinuation of the format. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> edition was released on June 3, 2008. The film has grossed $23,604,823 through DVD sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The-Numbers_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-The-Numbers-80">[80]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=There_Will_Be_Blood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The line "I drink your milkshake!", has been used in other media repeatedly. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>In season 24 of <i><a href="/wiki/Jeopardy!" title="Jeopardy!">Jeopardy!</a></i>, "I Drink Your Milkshake" was the title of a category about milkshakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jon_Stewart" title="Jon Stewart">Jon Stewart</a>, host of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Show" title="The Daily Show">The Daily Show</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/80th_Academy_Awards" title="80th Academy Awards">80th Academy Awards</a> (for which <i>There Will Be Blood</i> was nominated for eight Oscars), has referenced the phrase "I drink your milkshake" several times on his show in response to news involving oil drilling, including during interviews with <a href="/wiki/Ted_Koppel" title="Ted Koppel">Ted Koppel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Pelosi" title="Nancy Pelosi">Nancy Pelosi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></li>
<li>In February 2008, a <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> skit featured a <a href="/wiki/Food_Network" title="Food Network">Food Network</a> show called "I Drink Your Milkshake", in which Daniel Plainview (<a href="/wiki/Bill_Hader" title="Bill Hader">Bill Hader</a>) and H. W. (<a href="/wiki/Amy_Poehler" title="Amy Poehler">Amy Poehler</a>) travel from state to state looking for the perfect milkshake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Breast_Cancer_Show_Ever" title="Breast Cancer Show Ever">Breast Cancer Show Ever</a>" (October 15, 2008) parodies the final scene of the film: after <a href="/wiki/List_of_students_at_South_Park_Elementary#Wendy_Testaburger" title="List of students at South Park Elementary">Wendy</a> beats up <a href="/wiki/Eric_Cartman" title="Eric Cartman">Cartman</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_staff_at_South_Park_Elementary#Mr._Mackey" title="List of staff at South Park Elementary">Mr. Mackey</a> approaches and says, "Wendy!" to which she replies, "I'm finished," as Cartman lies facedown in blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
