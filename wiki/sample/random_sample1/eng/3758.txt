<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Deodorant</h1>

<p>A <b>deodorant</b> is a substance applied to the body to prevent <a href="/wiki/Body_odor" title="Body odor">body odor</a> caused by the bacterial breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Perspiration" title="Perspiration">perspiration</a> in armpits, feet, and other areas of the body. A subgroup of deodorants, <b>antiperspirants</b>, affect odor as well as prevent sweating by affecting <a href="/wiki/Sweat_gland" title="Sweat gland">sweat glands</a>.</p>
<p>Antiperspirants are typically applied to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underarm" title="Underarm">underarms</a>, while deodorants may also be used on feet and other areas in the form of <a href="/wiki/Body_spray" title="Body spray">body sprays</a>. In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> classifies and regulates most deodorants as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmetics_ingredients" title="Cosmetics ingredients">cosmetics</a>, but classifies antiperspirants as <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda-cosmetics_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fda-cosmetics-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first commercial deodorant, <a href="/wiki/Mum_(deodorant)" title="Mum (deodorant)">Mum</a>, was introduced and patented in the late nineteenth century by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, Edna Murphey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joey_Green_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joey_Green-2">[2]</a></sup> The product was briefly withdrawn from the market in the U.S.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joey_Green_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joey_Green-2">[2]</a></sup> but is currently available at U.S. retailers under the brand Ban.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nashville_Business_Journal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nashville_Business_Journal-3">[3]</a></sup> The modern formulation of the antiperspirant was <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patented</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Montenier" title="Jules Montenier">Jules Montenier</a> on January 28, 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jules_Montenier_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jules_Montenier-4">[4]</a></sup> This formulation was first found in "Stopette" deodorant spray, which <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine called "the best-selling deodorant of the early 1950s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-5">[5]</a></sup> Stopette was later eclipsed by many other brands as the 1941 patent expired.</p>
<p>There is a popular myth that deodorant use is linked to breast cancer, but so far no such causal link was substantiated in research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sbm_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sbm-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-namer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-namer-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1888, the first commercial deodorant, <a href="/wiki/Mum_(deodorant)" title="Mum (deodorant)">Mum</a>, was developed and patented by a U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, whose name has been lost to history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joey_Green_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Joey_Green-2">[2]</a></sup> The small company was bought by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bristol-Myers" title="Bristol-Myers">Bristol-Myers</a> in 1931 and in the late 1940s, Helen Barnett Diserens developed an underarm applicator based on the newly invented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ball-point_pen" title="Ball-point pen">ball-point pen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Digest2015_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Digest2015-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Socol2011_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Socol2011-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1952, the company began marketing the product under the name Ban Roll-On.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Digest2015_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Digest2015-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Socol2011_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Socol2011-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup> The product was briefly withdrawn from the market in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joey_Green_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Joey_Green-2">[2]</a></sup> It is once again available at retailers in the U.S. under the brand Ban.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nashville_Business_Journal_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nashville_Business_Journal-3">[3]</a></sup> In the UK it is sold under the names Mum Solid and Mum Pump Spray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joey_Green_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Joey_Green-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chattem" title="Chattem">Chattem</a> acquired Ban deodorant brand in 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and subsequently sold it to <a href="/wiki/Kao_Corporation" title="Kao Corporation">Kao Corporation</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1903, the first commercial antiperspirant was Everdry.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.smithsonianmag.com/history-archaeology/How-Advertisers-Convinced-Americans-They-Smelled-Bad-164779646.html#ixzz2Z49wDhld" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> The modern formulation of the antiperspirant was patented by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Montenier" title="Jules Montenier">Jules Montenier</a> on January 28, 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jules_Montenier_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jules_Montenier-4">[4]</a></sup> This patent addressed the problem of the excessive acidity of aluminum chloride and its excessive irritation of the skin, by combining it with a soluble <a href="/wiki/Nitrile" title="Nitrile">nitrile</a> or a similar compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jules_Montenier_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jules_Montenier-4">[4]</a></sup> This formulation was first found in "Stopette" deodorant spray, which <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time Magazine</a> called "the best-selling deodorant of the early 1950s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time-5">[5]</a></sup> "Stopette" gained its prominence as the first and long-time sponsor of the game show <i><a href="/wiki/What%27s_My_Line%3F" title="What's My Line?">What's My Line?</a></i>, and was later eclipsed by many other brands as the 1941 patent expired.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Between 1942 and 1957 the market for deodorants increased 600 times to become a $70 million market. Deodorants were originally marketed primarily to women, but by 1957 the market had expanded to male users, and estimates were that 50% of men were using deodorants by that date. The Ban Roll-On product led the market in sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1957_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1957-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1960s, the first <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray" title="Aerosol spray">aerosol</a> antiperspirant in the marketplace was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillette_(brand)" title="Gillette (brand)">Gillette</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Right_Guard" title="Right Guard">Right Guard</a>, whose brand was later sold to <a href="/wiki/Henkel" title="Henkel">Henkel</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Aerosols were popular because they let the user dispense a spray without coming in contact with the underarm area. By the late 1960s, half of all the antiperspirants sold in the U.S. were aerosols, and continued to grow in all sales to 82% by the early 1970s. However, in the late 1970s two problems arose which greatly changed the popularity of these products. First, in 1977 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) banned the active ingredient used in aerosols, aluminium zirconium chemicals, due to safety concerns over long term inhalation. Second, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) limited the use of <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbon</a> (CFC) propellants used in aerosols due to awareness that these gases can contribute to depleting the ozone layer. As the popularity of aerosols slowly decreased, stick antiperspirants became more popular.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stick_deodorant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="656" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Stick_deodorant.jpg/220px-Stick_deodorant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Stick_deodorant.jpg/330px-Stick_deodorant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Stick_deodorant.jpg/440px-Stick_deodorant.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stick_deodorant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stick antiperspirant/deodorant</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Human perspiration is largely odorless until it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermented</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that thrive in hot, humid environments. The human underarm is among the most consistently warm areas on the surface of the human body, and <a href="/wiki/Sweat_gland" title="Sweat gland">sweat glands</a> provide moisture, which when excreted, has a vital cooling effect. When adult armpits are washed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> pH soap, the skin loses its <a href="/wiki/Acid_mantle" title="Acid mantle">acid mantle</a> (pH 4.5 - 6), raising the skin pH and disrupting the skin barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As many bacteria thrive in this elevated pH environment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stenzaly-Achtert_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stenzaly-Achtert-16">[16]</a></sup> this makes the skin susceptible to bacterial colonization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stenzaly-Achtert_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stenzaly-Achtert-16">[16]</a></sup> The bacteria feed on the sweat from the <a href="/wiki/Apocrine_sweat_gland" title="Apocrine sweat gland">apocrine glands</a> and on dead skin and hair cells, releasing <a href="/wiki/Trans-3-Methyl-2-hexenoic_acid" title="Trans-3-Methyl-2-hexenoic acid">trans-3-Methyl-2-hexenoic acid</a> in their waste, which is the primary cause of body odor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Underarm_hair" title="Underarm hair">Underarm hair</a> wicks the moisture away from the skin and aids in keeping the skin dry enough to prevent or diminish bacterial colonization. The hair is less susceptible to bacterial growth and therefore is ideal for preventing the bacterial odor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deodorant">Deodorant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deodorant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deodorants are classified and regulated as <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda-cosmetics_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-fda-cosmetics-1">[1]</a></sup> and are designed to eliminate odor. Deodorants are often <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>-based. Alcohol initially stimulates sweating, but may also temporarily kill bacteria. Other active ingredients in deodorants include <a href="/wiki/Sodium_stearate" title="Sodium stearate">sodium stearate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stearyl_alcohol" title="Stearyl alcohol">stearyl alcohol</a>. Deodorants can be formulated with other, more persistent <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial" title="Antimicrobial">antimicrobials</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Triclosan" title="Triclosan">triclosan</a> that slow bacterial growth or with metal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelant" title="Chelant">chelant</a> compounds such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a>. Deodorants may contain <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfume</a> fragrances or natural <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oils</a> intended to mask the odor of perspiration. In the past, deodorants included chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>, acids, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, but some of these ingredients were messy, irritating to the skin or even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over-the-counter products labeled as "natural deodorant crystal" containing the chemical <a href="/wiki/Potassium_alum" title="Potassium alum">potassium alum</a>, which contains aluminum, have gained new-found popularity as an alternative health product, in spite of concerns about possible contact dermatitis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallego_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gallego-20">[20]</a></sup> A popular alternative to modern commercial deodorants is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_alum" title="Ammonium alum">ammonium alum</a>, which is a common type of <a href="/wiki/Alum" title="Alum">alum</a>, also containing aluminum, sold in crystal form and often referred to as a <i>deodorant crystal</i>. It has been used as a deodorant throughout history in Thailand, the Far East, Mexico and other countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deodorant_antiperspirant">Deodorant antiperspirant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deodorant antiperspirant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BlueSand_Picture.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="223" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/BlueSand_Picture.jpg/110px-BlueSand_Picture.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/BlueSand_Picture.jpg/165px-BlueSand_Picture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/BlueSand_Picture.jpg/220px-BlueSand_Picture.jpg 2x" width="110"/></a>

Methenamine based antiperspirant for treatment of excessive sweating</div>

<p>Deodorants combined with antiperspirant agents are classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drugs" title="Drugs">drugs</a> by the FDA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda-cosmetics_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-fda-cosmetics-1">[1]</a></sup> Antiperspirants attempt to stop or significantly reduce perspiration and thus reduce the moist climate in which bacteria thrive. <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_chloride" title="Aluminium chloride">Aluminium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_chlorohydrate" title="Aluminium chlorohydrate">aluminium chlorohydrate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> compounds, most notably <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_zirconium_tetrachlorohydrex_gly" title="Aluminium zirconium tetrachlorohydrex gly">aluminium zirconium tetrachlorohydrex gly</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_zirconium_trichlorohydrex_gly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aluminium zirconium trichlorohydrex gly (page does not exist)">aluminium zirconium trichlorohydrex gly</a>, are frequently used in antiperspirants. Aluminium chlorohydrate and aluminium-zirconium tetrachlorohydrate gly are the most frequent active ingredients in commercial antiperspirants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lukacs_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lukacs-21">[21]</a></sup> Aluminium-based complexes react with the <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> in the sweat to form a gel plug in the duct of the sweat gland. The plugs prevent the gland from excreting liquid and are removed over time by the natural <a href="/wiki/Desquamation" title="Desquamation">sloughing</a> of the skin. The metal salts work in another way to prevent sweat from reaching the surface of the skin: the aluminium salts interact with the keratin fibrils in the sweat ducts and form a physical plug that prevents sweat from reaching the skin’s surface. Aluminium salts also have a slight <a href="/wiki/Astringent" title="Astringent">astringent</a> effect on the pores; causing them to contract, further preventing sweat from reaching the surface of the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The blockage of a large number of sweat glands reduces the amount of sweat produced in the underarms, though this may vary from person to person. <a href="/wiki/Hexamethylenetetramine" title="Hexamethylenetetramine">Methenamine</a> in the form of cream and spray is successfully used for treatment of excessive sweating and related to it odour. Antiperspirants are usually better applied before bed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Form">Form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deodorants and antiperspirants come in many forms. What is commonly used varies in different countries. In Europe, <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray" title="Aerosol spray">aerosol sprays</a> are popular, as are cream and roll-on forms. In the United States, solid or gel forms are dominant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After using a deodorant containing zirconium, the skin may develop an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergic" title="Allergic">allergic</a>, axillary <a href="/wiki/Granuloma" title="Granuloma">granuloma</a> response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Antiperspirants with <a href="/wiki/Propylene_glycol" title="Propylene glycol">propylene glycol</a>, when applied to the axillae, can cause irritation and may promote sensitization to other ingredients in the antiperspirant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Deodorant crystals containing synthetically made <a href="/wiki/Potassium_alum" title="Potassium alum">potassium alum</a> were found to be a weak <a href="/wiki/Irritation" title="Irritation">irritant</a> to the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallego_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gallego-20">[20]</a></sup> Alcohol-free deodorant is available for those with sensitive skin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Frequent use of deodorants was associated with blood concentrations of the <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_musk" title="Synthetic musk">synthetic musk</a> <a href="/wiki/Galaxolide" title="Galaxolide">galaxolide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aluminum_toxicity">Aluminum toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aluminum toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">Aluminum</a> is present most often in antiperspirants in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_chlorohydrate" title="Aluminum chlorohydrate">aluminum chlorohydrate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lukacs_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lukacs-21">[21]</a></sup> Aluminum chlorohydrate is not the same as the compound <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_chloride" title="Aluminum chloride">aluminum chloride</a>, which has been established as a <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxin" title="Neurotoxin">neurotoxin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lankoff_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lankoff-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> At high doses, aluminum itself adversely affects the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a>, is capable of causing <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> damage, and has adverse <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epigenetic" title="Epigenetic">epigenetic</a> effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lankoff_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lankoff-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">Food and Drug Administration</a>, in a monograph dedicated to analysing the safety of deodorants, concluded that "despite many investigators looking at this issue, the agency does not find data from topical and inhalation chronic exposure animal and human studies submitted to date sufficient to change the monograph status of aluminum containing antiperspirants", therefore allowing their use and vowing to keep monitoring the scientific literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA_Register_Vol_68_No_110_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDA_Register_Vol_68_No_110-32">[32]</a></sup> Members of the Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (Europe) concluded that "due to the lack of adequate data on dermal penetration to estimate the internal dose of aluminium following cosmetic uses, risk assessment cannot be performed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breast_cancer">Breast cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breast cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The myth that breast cancer is believed to be linked with deodorant use has been widely circulated, and appears to originate from a <a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">spam</a> email sent in 1999;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sbm_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-sbm-6">[6]</a></sup> however, there is no evidence to support the existence of such a link.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-namer_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-namer-7">[7]</a></sup> One constituent of deodorant products which has given cause for concern are <a href="/wiki/Paraben" title="Paraben">parabens</a>, a chemical additive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paraben_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-paraben-34">[34]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Cancer_Society" title="American Cancer Society">American Cancer Society</a> "studies have not shown any direct link between parabens and many health problems, including breast cancer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paraben_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-paraben-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kidney_dysfunction">Kidney dysfunction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kidney dysfunction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The FDA has "acknowledge[d] that small amounts of aluminium can be absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and through the skin.", leading to a warning "that people with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">kidney disease</a> may not be aware that the daily use of antiperspirant drug products containing aluminium may put them at a higher risk because of exposure to aluminium in the product."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA_Register_Vol_68_No_110_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-FDA_Register_Vol_68_No_110-32">[32]</a></sup> The agency warns people with kidney dysfunction to consult a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">doctor</a> before using antiperspirants containing aluminum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA_Register_Vol_68_No_110_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-FDA_Register_Vol_68_No_110-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerosol_burns_and_frostbite">Aerosol burns and frostbite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aerosol burns and frostbite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If aerosol deodorant is held close to the skin for long enough, it can cause an <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_burn" title="Aerosol burn">aerosol burn</a>—a form of <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cold_burn_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-cold_burn-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In controlled tests, spray deodorants have been shown to cause temperature drops of over 60 °C in a short period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cold_burn_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-cold_burn-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clothing">Clothing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deodorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Clothing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aluminium_zirconium_tetrachlorohydrex_gly" title="Aluminium zirconium tetrachlorohydrex gly">Aluminium zirconium tetrachlorohydrex gly</a>, a common antiperspirant, can react with sweat to create yellow stains on clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Underarm_liners" title="Underarm liners">Underarm liners</a> are an alternative to antiperspirants that do not leave stains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
