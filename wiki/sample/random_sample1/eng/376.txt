<p>The <b>United States Senate elections, 2006</b> were held on November 7, 2006, with all 33 <a href="/wiki/Classes_of_United_States_Senators" title="Classes of United States Senators">Class 1 Senate seats</a> being contested. The term of office for those elected in 2006 ran from January 3, 2007, to January 3, 2013. Prior to the election, the Republican Party controlled 55 of the 100 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> seats.</p>
<p>The Senate elections were part of the Democratic sweep of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_general_elections,_2006" title="United States general elections, 2006">2006 elections</a>, in which Democrats made numerous gains and no Congressional or gubernatorial seat held by a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> was won by a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Six Republican incumbents were defeated by Democrats:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jim_Talent" title="Jim Talent">Jim Talent</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Missouri,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Missouri, 2006">Missouri</a>) lost to <a href="/wiki/Claire_McCaskill" title="Claire McCaskill">Claire McCaskill</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conrad_Burns" title="Conrad Burns">Conrad Burns</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Montana,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Montana, 2006">Montana</a>) lost to <a href="/wiki/Jon_Tester" title="Jon Tester">Jon Tester</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mike_DeWine" title="Mike DeWine">Mike DeWine</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Ohio,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Ohio, 2006">Ohio</a>) lost to <a href="/wiki/Sherrod_Brown" title="Sherrod Brown">Sherrod Brown</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rick_Santorum" title="Rick Santorum">Rick Santorum</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Pennsylvania,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 2006">Pennsylvania</a>) lost to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Casey_Jr." title="Bob Casey Jr.">Bob Casey Jr.</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Chafee" title="Lincoln Chafee">Lincoln Chafee</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Rhode_Island,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Rhode Island, 2006">Rhode Island</a>) lost to <a href="/wiki/Sheldon_Whitehouse" title="Sheldon Whitehouse">Sheldon Whitehouse</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Allen_(U.S._politician)" title="George Allen (U.S. politician)">George Allen</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Virginia,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Virginia, 2006">Virginia</a>) lost to <a href="/wiki/Jim_Webb" title="Jim Webb">Jim Webb</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Incumbent Democrat <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lieberman" title="Joe Lieberman">Joe Lieberman</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Connecticut,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Connecticut, 2006">Connecticut</a>) lost the <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">Democratic primary</a>, but won re-election as an independent. Democrats kept their two <a href="/wiki/Incumbent#In_politics" title="Incumbent">open seats</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Minnesota,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Minnesota, 2006">Minnesota</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Maryland,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Maryland, 2006">Maryland</a>, and Republicans held onto their lone open seat in <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Tennessee,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Tennessee, 2006">Tennessee</a>. In <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Vermont,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Vermont, 2006">Vermont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a>, an independent, was elected to the seat left open by independent Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jeffords" title="Jim Jeffords">Jim Jeffords</a>. In the 2006 election, two new female Senators (<a href="/wiki/Claire_McCaskill" title="Claire McCaskill">Claire McCaskill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amy_Klobuchar" title="Amy Klobuchar">Amy Klobuchar</a>) were elected to seats previously held by men. This brought the total number of <a href="/wiki/Women_in_the_United_States_Senate" title="Women in the United States Senate">female senators</a> to an all-time high of 16.</p>
<p>Following the elections, no party held a majority of seats for the first time since <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_elections,_1954" title="United States Senate elections, 1954">January 1955</a>. However, the Democrats were able to control the chamber because independents Bernie Sanders and Joe Lieberman chose to <a href="/wiki/Party_caucuses_and_conferences_in_the_United_States_Congress" title="Party caucuses and conferences in the United States Congress">caucus</a> with the Democrats. The Democrats needed at least 51 seats to control the Senate because <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a> would have broken any 50–50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tie-breaking_votes_cast_by_Vice_Presidents_of_the_United_States" title="List of tie-breaking votes cast by Vice Presidents of the United States">tie</a> in favor of the Republicans.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arizona">Arizona</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arizona">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Arizona,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Arizona, 2006">United States Senate election in Arizona, 2006</a></div>
<p>Wealthy real estate developer <a href="/wiki/Jim_Pederson" title="Jim Pederson">Jim Pederson</a> declared his intention to challenge Senator <a href="/wiki/Jon_Kyl" title="Jon Kyl">Jon Kyl</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> on September 14, 2005. Pederson served as Arizona Democratic Party Chairman from 2001 to 2005 while spending millions of his own money to help Democrats modernize and to elect <a href="/wiki/Janet_Napolitano" title="Janet Napolitano">Janet Napolitano</a> as governor. Kyl got an unexpected boost when <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">TIME</a></i> listed him as one of the Ten Best Senators. While polling in October showed Pederson catching up, Kyl was re-elected 53%-44%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Connecticut">Connecticut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Connecticut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Connecticut,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Connecticut, 2006">United States Senate election in Connecticut, 2006</a></div>
<p>Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lieberman" title="Joe Lieberman">Joe Lieberman</a> of <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> was originally thought to be a shoo-in, but his reelection prospects were complicated by political cross-currents. Lieberman drew fire from the more anti-war elements within the Democratic party for his continual support for the foreign policy of <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and for statements in which he has criticized other Democrats for "undermining the President" during a time of war. <a href="/wiki/Greenwich,_Connecticut" title="Greenwich, Connecticut">Greenwich</a> telecom-networking businessman <a href="/wiki/Ned_Lamont" title="Ned Lamont">Ned Lamont</a> declared his candidacy for the Democratic nomination in March, and while the Democratic state convention in May overwhelmingly endorsed Lieberman, Lamont's 33.4% support was more than twice the 15% needed to force a primary.</p>
<p>Lamont defeated Lieberman for the Democratic nomination in the August 8 primary 52%-48%. Lieberman decided to remain in the race as a "petitioning candidate,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[2]</a></sup> having announced on July 3, 2006, that he would begin collecting the necessary signatures to run as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" title="Independent (politician)">independent</a> in case he lost the primary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup> He also filed to create a new independent party, "<a href="/wiki/Connecticut_for_Lieberman" title="Connecticut for Lieberman">Connecticut for Lieberman</a>."</p>
<p>Challenging Lamont and Lieberman in the general election was Republican <a href="/wiki/Alan_Schlesinger" title="Alan Schlesinger">Alan Schlesinger</a>, former mayor of <a href="/wiki/Derby,_Connecticut" title="Derby, Connecticut">Derby</a> and a former state representative. Schlesinger had a history of winning crossover Democratic voters, but he had never run in a large constituency. Schlesinger was embarrassed when it was revealed that he was thrown out of a casino for counting cards under an assumed name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lieberman went on to win the election with 50% of the vote to Lamont's 40%. Schlesinger trailed far behind with only 10%, in part due to Lieberman receiving support from only 33% of Democrats but a commanding 70% of Republicans. While Lieberman won as the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_for_Lieberman" title="Connecticut for Lieberman">CFL</a> nominee, he decided to serve as an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Democrat" title="Independent Democrat">Independent Democrat</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/110th_Congress" title="110th Congress">current Congress</a> and continue to caucus with Senate Democrats.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maryland">Maryland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Maryland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Maryland,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Maryland, 2006">United States Senate election in Maryland, 2006</a></div>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sarbanes" title="Paul Sarbanes">Paul Sarbanes</a> announced on March 11, 2005, that he would retire rather than run for re-election in 2006. Sarbanes' seat had been considered safe, considering <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>'s Democratic voting tendencies and the overall pro-Democratic undercurrents of the 2006 elections. Representative <a href="/wiki/Ben_Cardin" title="Ben Cardin">Ben Cardin</a> bested former Representative and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a> President <a href="/wiki/Kweisi_Mfume" title="Kweisi Mfume">Kweisi Mfume</a> and others in the Democratic primary. <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Maryland" title="Lieutenant Governor of Maryland">Lieutenant Governor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_S._Steele" title="Michael S. Steele">Michael S. Steele</a>, a Republican, announced his candidacy on October 25, 2005, and won the Republican nomination over token opposition. Democrats had a natural advantage in Maryland, with its large number of African-American voters and government workers, but Steele's personal popularity and potential appeal with fellow blacks kept the race somewhat competitive. On November 7, Cardin was victorious over Steele by a vote of 54%-44%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minnesota">Minnesota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Minnesota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Minnesota,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Minnesota, 2006">United States Senate election in Minnesota, 2006</a></div>
<p>On February 9, 2005, Senator <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dayton" title="Mark Dayton">Mark Dayton</a> announced that he would not seek a second term in the Senate. <a href="/wiki/Hennepin_County,_Minnesota" title="Hennepin County, Minnesota">Hennepin County</a> Attorney <a href="/wiki/Amy_Klobuchar" title="Amy Klobuchar">Amy Klobuchar</a> was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minnesota_Democratic-Farmer-Labor_Party" title="Minnesota Democratic-Farmer-Labor Party">Democratic-Farmer-Labor Party</a> (DFL) nominee.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_of_Minnesota" title="Republican Party of Minnesota">Republican</a> Representative <a href="/wiki/Mark_Kennedy_(politician)" title="Mark Kennedy (politician)">Mark Kennedy</a> secured major GOP endorsements in early 2005 and defeated nominal opposition in the primary. Kennedy benefited from high-profile Republicans coming to do fundraisers for him, including <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a> in July 2005 and <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> in December 2005. An October 30 Mason-Dixon poll had Klobuchar leading Kennedy 50% to 40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup> On November 7, 2006, Amy Klobuchar won the race with 58% of the vote to Mark Kennedy's 38%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Missouri">Missouri</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Missouri">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Missouri,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Missouri, 2006">United States Senate election in Missouri, 2006</a></div>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Talent" title="Jim Talent">Jim Talent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, who was narrowly elected in a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_elections,_2002" title="United States Senate elections, 2002">2002 special election</a> for the remaining four years of one term, faced a strong Democratic challenge for his seat. Missouri did not hold an election for governor in 2006, making this the only major statewide race in a traditional battleground state. Talent faced state Auditor <a href="/wiki/Claire_McCaskill" title="Claire McCaskill">Claire McCaskill</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Jackson_County,_Missouri" title="Jackson County, Missouri">Jackson County</a> Prosecutor and the 2004 Democratic <a href="/wiki/United_States_gubernatorial_elections,_2004" title="United States gubernatorial elections, 2004">gubernatorial</a> nominee.</p>
<p>McCaskill carried some political baggage from her 2004 loss; however, Talent was elected to the Senate after a nearly successful gubernatorial bid, the same position McCaskill was in for the 2006 election. McCaskill went out of her way to appeal to rural voters, who had largely favored her opponent in the gubernatorial race. She also benefited from talking up her support of <a href="/wiki/Embryonic_stem_cell" title="Embryonic stem cell">embryonic stem cell</a> research, which a slight majority of Missourians supported but which Talent opposed. A related <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Constitutional_Amendment_2_(2006)" title="Missouri Constitutional Amendment 2 (2006)">constitutional amendment</a> was also on the ballot and narrowly passed.</p>
<p>The race was among the most competitive in the nation. McCaskill and Talent exchanged small leads in various polls throughout the campaign. In the end, McCaskill defeated Talent 50%-47%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Montana">Montana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Montana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Montana,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Montana, 2006">United States Senate election in Montana, 2006</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Burns" title="Conrad Burns">Conrad Burns</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> faced a strong challenge from <a href="/wiki/Brian_Schweitzer" title="Brian Schweitzer">Brian Schweitzer</a> (who later became governor) in 2000, but was re-elected by a mere 3% in a state that went for Bush twice by margins of over 20%. This, combined with the increasing strength of the state Democratic Party and accusations of ethical issues related to the <a href="/wiki/Jack_Abramoff_Indian_lobbying_scandal" title="Jack Abramoff Indian lobbying scandal">Jack Abramoff influence-peddling scandal</a>, made this a highly competitive race. Burns faced Democratic primary winner and state Senate President <a href="/wiki/Jon_Tester" title="Jon Tester">Jon Tester</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_farmer" title="Organic farmer">organic farmer</a> from Big Sandy.</p>
<p>On July 27, Burns was forced to apologize after he verbally attacked out-of-state firefighters who were preparing to leave Montana after helping contain a summer forest fire and directly questioned their competence and skill; Burns was strongly criticized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>For much of the campaign, Tester led by substantial margins. Burns narrowed the gap by attacking Tester as a liberal extremist. November 2, Mason-Dixon polled Tester and Burns tied at 47% with 5% undecided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> On November 4, Rasmussen Reports had Tester leading 50% to 48%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly before noon Mountain time November 8, Tester was declared the victor by a slim margin, 198,032 votes to 194,904.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> The race was the closest Senate election of 2006 in terms of absolute vote difference; the closest race by percentage difference was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_United_States_Senate_election,_2006" title="Virginia United States Senate election, 2006">Virginia senate election</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Jersey">New Jersey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New Jersey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_New_Jersey,_2006" title="United States Senate election in New Jersey, 2006">United States Senate election in New Jersey, 2006</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jon_Corzine" title="Jon Corzine">Jon Corzine</a>, elected to the Senate in 2000, was elected <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_Jersey" title="Governor of New Jersey">Governor of New Jersey</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_gubernatorial_election,_2005" title="New Jersey gubernatorial election, 2005">2005</a>. Corzine appointed Rep. <a href="/wiki/Bob_Menendez" title="Bob Menendez">Bob Menendez</a> to serve the last year of the Senate term, and Menendez was sworn in to fill Corzine's vacancy on January 18, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> Republican State Senator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Kean,_Jr." title="Thomas Kean, Jr.">Thomas Kean, Jr.</a> (the son of former Governor and <a href="/wiki/9/11_Commission" title="9/11 Commission">9/11 Commission</a> Chairman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kean" title="Thomas Kean">Thomas Kean</a>) announced on March 25, 2005, that he would run for the U.S. Senate. Both nominees had problems within their own parties; Menendez alienated many fellow Democrats with his aggressiveness and abrasiveness, while Kean was unpopular with party conservatives who considered him to be too liberal.</p>
<p>Menendez had an approval rating of 38%, which was thought to be a sign of vulnerability for the incumbent, especially since his disapproval was at 50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup> Although incumbents approval ratings below 50% are generally considered vulnerable, this standard perhaps did not apply to Menendez as he had just been appointed at the start of 2006 and was not well known statewide, a far different situation from most incumbents. President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> was highly unpopular in the state, but Governor Corzine's early performance in office met with widespread disapproval, contributing to the large number of undecided voters. The campaign became increasingly aggressive, with Menendez calling Kean a Bush lackey while Kean repeatedly attacked Menendez as corrupt.</p>
<p>An October 16 Zogby poll had Kean leading Menendez 47% to 45%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> An October 23 LA Times/Bloomberg poll had Menendez leading Kean 45% to 41%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> A Rasmussen Reports poll from October 25 had Kean leading Menendez 43% to 41%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> A November 2 poll by Zogby/Reuters showed a 49% to 37% Menendez lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> Another November 2 poll by Rutgers showed Menendez up 46% to 42%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> and a third by Public Mind showed a 48% to 38% Menendez edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> A November 3 poll by [Rasmussen] showed a 48% to 43% Menendez lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> A Monmouth University November 3 had Menendez leading Kean 45% to 42% with 10% undecided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On election night Menendez defeated Kean Jr. by a vote of 53% to 44%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ohio">Ohio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ohio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Ohio,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Ohio, 2006">United States Senate election in Ohio, 2006</a></div>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Mike_DeWine" title="Mike DeWine">Mike DeWine</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> had uninspiring approval ratings and the current <a href="/wiki/Coingate_scandal" title="Coingate scandal">Coingate scandal</a> involving the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Republican_Party" title="Ohio Republican Party">Ohio Republican Party</a> and the widespread unpopularity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Ohio" title="Governor of Ohio">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Taft" title="Bob Taft">Bob Taft</a> were thought to be hurting his re-election chances months before the election. He faced primary challenges from several more conservative Republicans, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_G._Pierce&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William G. Pierce (page does not exist)">William G. Pierce</a>, who were unhappy with his relatively centrist stances including his role as one of the <a href="/wiki/Gang_of_14" title="Gang of 14">Gang of 14</a> who intervened to stop a showdown over judicial nominations.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">Lawyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> <a href="/wiki/Veteran" title="Veteran">veteran</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hackett_(politician)" title="Paul Hackett (politician)">Paul Hackett</a>, who <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_second_congressional_district_election,_2005" title="Ohio second congressional district election, 2005">narrowly lost</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jean_Schmidt" title="Jean Schmidt">Jean Schmidt</a> in the 2nd congressional district on August 2, 2005, said in October 2005 he would seek the Democratic nomination to challenge DeWine. Rep. <a href="/wiki/Sherrod_Brown" title="Sherrod Brown">Sherrod Brown</a> announced his candidacy that same month. Hackett withdrew from the race on February 14, 2006. Both DeWine and Brown won their primaries easily. An October 12 Rasmussen Reports poll had Brown leading DeWine 46% to 41%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> An October 30 Reuters/Zogby poll had Brown leading DeWine 49% to 42%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> A Rasmussen poll released November 4 showed Brown pulling away from DeWine with a 53% to 41% lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brown comfortably won the election, garnering 56% of the vote to DeWine's 44%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pennsylvania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Pennsylvania,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 2006">United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 2006</a></div>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Rick_Santorum" title="Rick Santorum">Rick Santorum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, then the third-highest-ranking member of the Republican caucus, was the Democrats' top target in 2006. He was a very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_conservatism" title="American conservatism">conservative</a> member of the Senate in a state that last voted for a Republican presidential nominee in 1988.</p>
<p>In his last election in the year 2000, Santorum received 7,706 more votes than <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>, the Democratic candidate for president, who won Pennsylvania by 4.5 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup> That year, Santorum ran against U.S. Rep. <a href="/wiki/Ron_Klink" title="Ron Klink">Ron Klink</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-life" title="Pro-life">pro-life</a> Democrat who wasn't supported by party contributors and was heavily outspent. Democrats thus saw Santorum's seat as extremely vulnerable and made it a priority for a pick-up in 2006. Popular pro-life <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Treasurer" title="State Treasurer">State Treasurer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bob_Casey,_Jr." title="Bob Casey, Jr.">Bob Casey, Jr.</a> was the Democratic nominee and was fully supported by the party establishment.</p>
<p>Santorum did not benefit from his recent controversial book, <i><a href="/wiki/It_Takes_a_Family" title="It Takes a Family">It Takes a Family</a></i>, in which he criticized public schools and questioned whether or not both parents in a family should work, alleging that women who work are making a selfish decision and only do so because they find it "empowering". These stances were seized on by the Casey campaign as proof that Santorum was too conservative for Pennsylvania voters. Santorum also suffered from <a href="/wiki/Rick_Santorum#Residency_and_tuition_controversy" title="Rick Santorum">controversy</a> concerning both his residency and a <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Cyber_Charter_School" title="Pennsylvania Cyber Charter School">charter school</a> his children were enrolled in. He also voiced many social views that some thought extreme, including arguing that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rick_Santorum_controversy" title="Rick Santorum controversy">homosexual</a> relationships were no more deserving of legal endorsement than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bestiality" title="Bestiality">bestiality</a>.</p>
<p>Every public poll taken during the campaign showed Casey ahead. Most polling done after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_Day_(United_States)" title="Labor Day (United States)">Labor Day</a> showed Casey with a double-digit lead. On election night, Casey defeated Santorum 59%-41%. Polls conducted in the final closing days of the campaign showed Casey leading 52%-39% and 48%-40%, meaning that the Democrat had performed much better than what the polls showed. This was the largest margin of defeat for an incumbent Senator since <a href="/wiki/George_McGovern" title="George McGovern">George McGovern</a>'s loss to <a href="/wiki/James_Abdnor" title="James Abdnor">James Abdnor</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_elections,_1980" title="United States Senate elections, 1980">1980</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhode_Island">Rhode Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rhode Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Rhode_Island,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Rhode Island, 2006">United States Senate election in Rhode Island, 2006</a></div>

<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Chafee" title="Lincoln Chafee">Lincoln Chafee</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, a liberal Republican, faced a primary challenge from conservative <a href="/wiki/Cranston,_Rhode_Island" title="Cranston, Rhode Island">Cranston</a> Mayor <a href="/wiki/Steve_Laffey" title="Steve Laffey">Steve Laffey</a>. Former state Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Sheldon_Whitehouse" title="Sheldon Whitehouse">Sheldon Whitehouse</a>, who narrowly lost the gubernatorial nomination in 2002, faced token opposition in the Democratic primary.</p>
<p>Laffey received support from the conservative <a href="/wiki/Club_for_Growth" title="Club for Growth">Club for Growth</a>. Although he was the most liberal Republican in the Senate and was repeatedly accused of being a <a href="/wiki/Republican_In_Name_Only" title="Republican In Name Only">RINO</a> by members of his own party, the <a href="/wiki/National_Republican_Senatorial_Committee" title="National Republican Senatorial Committee">NRSC</a> spent a large amount of money backing Chafee, and, in an unprecedented move, announced that they would abandon the race if Laffey won. Chafee prevailed in the September 12 primary 54%-46%, and Laffey endorsed him for re-election. Chafee, however, may have been damaged by the contentious primary that potentially alienated Republican voters.</p>
<p>Chafee faced a complicated situation due to his political beliefs. He was unpopular with conservative Republicans whose votes he would need in order to win the primary, yet represented a heavily Democratic constituency that overwhelmingly disapproved of <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and the Republican-controlled Congress. As a liberal Democrat running in a liberal, Democratic stronghold, Whitehouse did not face these problems. The Whitehouse campaign sought to characterize the election as a referendum on Bush and the Republican Congress; critics argued that Whitehouse was simply casting himself as a proxy vote for a Democratic majority in the Senate.</p>
<p>Polls showed a close race, with Whitehouse holding a narrow lead going into the election. In the end, however, voters seemed to place more emphasis on party control than their personal affection for Chafee. Whitehouse prevailed over Chafee on election night winning by a vote of 53%-47%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tennessee">Tennessee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tennessee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Tennessee,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Tennessee, 2006">United States Senate election in Tennessee, 2006</a></div>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Bill_Frist" title="Bill Frist">Bill Frist</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majority_Leader_of_the_United_States_Senate" title="Majority Leader of the United States Senate">Majority Leader</a>, had previously promised to leave the Senate when his second term ended in January 2007, and was widely considered to have <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">presidential</a> aspirations for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_2008" title="U.S. presidential election, 2008">2008</a> (however, he announced in late November 2006, that he would not pursue a Presidential run).</p>
<p>Although Tennessee's electoral votes went to George W. Bush in 2000 and 2004, a majority of the state offices were held by Democrats. Tennessee also has more registered Democratic voters than Republican voters and was at the time one of two states in the south to send more Democrats to the U.S. House of Representatives than Republicans, the other being Arkansas.</p>
<p>The Democratic nominee was Representative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_Ford,_Jr." title="Harold Ford, Jr.">Harold Ford, Jr.</a> and the Republican nominee was <a href="/wiki/Bob_Corker" title="Bob Corker">Bob Corker</a>, both of whom won primaries on August 3. Corker, former mayor of <a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga</a> and 1994 Senate candidate, was well funded and advertised heavily in the western portion of the state during his primary campaign, where he was relatively unknown before this race. Ford was the representative from <a href="/wiki/Tennessee%27s_9th_congressional_district" title="Tennessee's 9th congressional district">Tennessee's 9th congressional district</a> in the United States House of Representatives. Like Corker, Ford showed exceptional fundraising prowess, and the race was an expensive one for both parties.</p>
<p>The candidates exchanged leads in the polls, and there were a number of negative charges. Ford attacked Corker's business dealings. Corker portrayed Ford as a hyper-political Washington insider with nothing in common with Tennessee residents. The campaign made headlines when the Republican National Committee ran an ad that, among other things, ridiculed Ford for attending a party thrown by the Playboy corporation. It featured a <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Goldsmith" title="Johanna Goldsmith">fictional blond Playboy Bunny</a> squealing, "I met Harold at the Playboy Party!" and then winking and saying, "Harold, call me." Democrats called the ad an attempt to play on racial prejudice, and Corker distanced himself from the ad.</p>
<p>Corker won the election 51%-48%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vermont">Vermont</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Vermont">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Vermont,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Vermont, 2006">United States Senate election in Vermont, 2006</a></div>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jeffords" title="Jim Jeffords">Jim Jeffords</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> left the Republican Party to become an independent soon after being reelected as a Republican in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate_election,_2000" title="U.S. Senate election, 2000">2000 election</a>. On April 20, 2005, he declared he would not seek another term. The national Democratic Party put independent Representative <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> on their party's ballot in order to keep other Democrats from having a possible "spoiler" effect on the general election results. Sanders won both the Democratic line and an independent line on the ballot.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Tarrant" title="Richard Tarrant">Richard Tarrant</a> was the Republican nominee, after winning the primary election on September 12. Sanders, a popular political figure in Vermont, won with 65% of the vote.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia">Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Senate_elections,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Virginia,_2006" title="United States Senate election in Virginia, 2006">United States Senate election in Virginia, 2006</a></div>
<p>Early in the 2006 campaign, freshman incumbent Republican <a href="/wiki/George_Allen_(U.S._politician)" title="George Allen (U.S. politician)">George Allen</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> held a double-digit lead in nearly all polls, and had positioned himself as a potential presidential candidate in 2008. That status was seriously jeopardized by a series of controversial events occurring during Allen's re-election campaign, culminating in his loss to his Democratic opponent, former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Navy" title="United States Secretary of the Navy">Secretary of the Navy</a> and decorated combat veteran <a href="/wiki/Jim_Webb" title="Jim Webb">Jim Webb</a>. Retired Air Force officer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_Green_Party_of_Virginia" title="Independent Green Party of Virginia">Gail Parker</a> ran as the Independent Green party candidate.</p>
<p>Among the most bitter Senate contests of the year, Allen's approval rating had dropped in statewide polls due in part to a series of embarrassing incidents during the campaign. In mid-August at a campaign stop in southwest Virginia, Allen called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S._R._Sidarth" title="S. R. Sidarth">S. R. Sidarth</a>, a Webb volunteer of Indian descent, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macaca_(slur)" title="Macaca (slur)">macaca</a>" and welcomed him to America, although he was born in Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> Controversy surrounding Allen continued into September following his reaction to questions about his Jewish heritage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> Additional reports surfaced in late September that Allen uttered the <a href="/wiki/Nigger#The_N-word_euphemism" title="Nigger">N-word</a> on a frequent basis while a student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a>, according to former college football teammates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> Allen fired back by pointing out remarks that Webb made during the 1980s that were demeaning to female veterans. He struck again when he released excerpts of graphic sexual scenes from some of the books Webb had penned, portraying the writing as misogynistic and pornography. Webb responded that these were based on events that he personally witnessed while in the military and while working as a journalist.</p>
<p>As controversy and allegations on both sides increased, the gap between the candidates tightened significantly. On October 30, Reuters/Zogby had Webb leading Allen 45% to 44%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> A November 2 Rasmussen Reports poll had Allen and Webb tied at 49%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> A November 3 Mason-Dixon poll had Webb leading 46% to 45% with 7% undecided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>As polls closed on November 7, 2006, the margin of votes between Webb and Allen was approximately 7,000 votes, or less than 0.5% of all votes cast, a margin eligible to trigger a recount per Virginia election law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> On the evening of November 8, 2006, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> declared Webb the winner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBCcallsVA_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBCcallsVA-37">[35]</a></sup> Following recanvassing, the Virginia Board of Elections declared Webb the winner by 9,162 votes, a margin of 0.38%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the afternoon of November 9, 2006, Allen gave a speech conceding the election to Webb, stating "The people of Virginia have spoken and I respect their decision. The Bible teaches us there is a time and place for everything, and today I called and congratulated Jim Webb."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllenConcedes_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-AllenConcedes-39">[37]</a></sup> Web's win was the pivotal seat for Democrats to regain the majority in the Senate (51-49). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
