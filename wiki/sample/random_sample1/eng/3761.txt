<p><i><b>Deinotherium</b></i> ("terrible beast" derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>δεινός, deinos</i> meaning "terrible" and <i>θηρίον, therion</i> meaning "beast") was a large prehistoric relative of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> that appeared in the Middle <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> and survived until the Early <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>. During that time it changed very little. In life, it probably resembled modern <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>, except that its trunk was shorter, and it had downward curving tusks attached to the lower jaw.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinotherium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Deinotherium</i> is the type genus of the family <a href="/wiki/Deinotheriidae" title="Deinotheriidae">Deinotheriidae</a>, which evolved from the smaller, early Miocene <i><a href="/wiki/Prodeinotherium" title="Prodeinotherium">Prodeinotherium</a></i>. These proboscideans represent a totally distinct line of evolutionary descent to that of other elephants, one that probably diverged very early in the history of the group as a whole. The large group to which <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> belong formerly contained several other related groups: besides the deinotheres, there were the <a href="/wiki/Gomphothere" title="Gomphothere">gomphotheres</a> (some of which had shovel-like lower front teeth), and the <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">mastodons</a>. Only elephants survive today.</p>
<p>Three species are recognized, all of great size. <i>Deinotherium giganteum</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, and is described above. It is primarily a late Miocene species, most common in Europe, and is the only species known from the circum-Mediterranean. Its last reported occurrence is from the middle <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> (2 to 4 million <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>). An entire skull, found in the Lower Pliocene beds of <a href="/wiki/Eppelsheim" title="Eppelsheim">Eppelsheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse-Darmstadt</a> in 1836, was 4 ft (1.2 m) long and 3 ft (0.9 meters) wide, indicating an animal exceeding modern elephants in size. <i>Deinotherium indicum</i> is the Asian species, known from India and Pakistan. It is distinguished by a more robust <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dentition</a> and p4-m3 intravalley tubercles. <i>D. indicum</i> appears in the middle Miocene, and is most common in the late Miocene. It disappeared from the fossil record about 7 million years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> (late Miocene). <i>Deinotherium bozasi</i> is the African species. It is characterized by a narrower rostral trough, a smaller but higher <a href="/wiki/Nostril" title="Nostril">nasal aperture</a>, a higher and narrower cranium, and a shorter mandibular symphysis than the other two species. <i>D. bozasi</i> appears at the beginning of the late Miocene, and continues there after the other two species have died out elsewhere. The youngest fossils are from the Kanjera Formation, Kenya, about a million years old (early <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>).</p>

<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png/220px-Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png/330px-Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png/440px-Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deinotherium_bozasi_JG.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i>Deinotherium bozasi</i></div>

<p><i>Deinotherium</i> was a large proboscidean. Two adults of <i>D. giganteum</i> are around 3.63–4 metres (11.9–13.1 ft) tall and weight 8.8–12 tonnes (8.7–11.8 long tons; 9.7–13.2 short tons). This is similar to adult males of <i>D. proavum</i>, one of which weighted 10.3 tonnes (10.1 long tons; 11.4 short tons) and was 3.59 metres (11.8 ft) tall. However, both these species are smaller than a 45-year-old male of <i>D. "thraceiensis"</i>, at 4.01 metres (13.2 ft) tall and 13.2 tonnes (13.0 long tons; 14.6 short tons) in weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proboscideans_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-proboscideans-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Permanent tooth formula of <i>D. giganteus</i> is 0-0-2-3/1-0-2-3 (<a href="/wiki/Deciduous_teeth" title="Deciduous teeth">deciduous</a> 0-0-3/1-0-3), with vertical cheek tooth replacement. Two sets of bilophodont and trilophodont teeth. Molars and rear premolars tapiroid, vertical shearing teeth, and show that deinotheres became an independent evolutionary branch very early on; other premolars used for crushing. The <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">cranium</a> is short, low, and flattened on the top (in contrast to more advanced proboscids, which have a higher and more domed forehead; the implication may be that deinotheres were less intelligent than other proboscids), with very large, elevated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occipital_condyle" title="Occipital condyle">occipital condyles</a>. The nasal opening is retracted and large, indicating a large trunk. The <a href="/wiki/Rostrum_(anatomy)" title="Rostrum (anatomy)">rostrum</a> is long and the rostral fossa broad. Mandibular symphyses (the lower jaw-bone) is very long and curved downward, which, with the backward curved tusks, is a distinguishing feature of the group; it possessed no upper tusks.</p>
<p><i>Deinotherium</i> is distinguished from its predecessor <i>Prodeinotherium</i> by its much greater size, greater <a href="/wiki/Tooth" title="Tooth">crown</a> dimensions, and reduced development of posterior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cingulum_(anatomy)" title="Cingulum (anatomy)">cingula</a> ornamentation in the second and third <a href="/wiki/Molar_(tooth)" title="Molar (tooth)">molar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoecology">Paleoecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinotherium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paleoecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dinotherium.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="409" data-file-width="656" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Dinotherium.jpg/220px-Dinotherium.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Dinotherium.jpg/330px-Dinotherium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Dinotherium.jpg/440px-Dinotherium.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dinotherium.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Harder" title="Heinrich Harder">Heinrich Harder</a> illustration of <i>Deinotherium</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The way <i>Deinotherium</i> used its curious tusks has been much debated. It may have rooted in soil for underground plant parts like roots and tubers, pulled down branches to snap them and reach leaves, or stripped soft bark from tree trunks. <i>Deinotherium</i> fossils have been uncovered at several of the African sites where remains of prehistoric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_ape" title="Great ape">hominid</a> relatives of modern <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> have also been found.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deinotherium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Deinotherium's</i> range covered parts of Asia, Africa, and Europe. <a href="/wiki/Adrienne_Mayor" title="Adrienne Mayor">Adrienne Mayor</a>, in <i>The First Fossil Hunters: Paleontology In Greek and Roman Times,</i> has suggested that deinothere fossils found in Greece helped generate myths of archaic giant beings. A tooth of a deinothere found on the island of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, in shallow marine sediments of the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> suggests that Crete was closer or connected to the mainland during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messinian_Salinity_Crisis" title="Messinian Salinity Crisis">Messinian Salinity Crisis</a>.</p>
