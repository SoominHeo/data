<p><b>Alfred Damon Runyon</b> (October 4, 1880<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> – December 10, 1946) was an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newspaperman" title="Newspaperman">newspaperman</a> and short story writer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was best known for his short stories celebrating the world of <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a> in New York City that grew out of the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> era. To New Yorkers of his generation, a "Damon Runyon character" evoked a distinctive social type from the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demi-monde" title="Demi-monde">demi-monde</a>. The adjective "Runyonesque" refers to this type of character as well as to the type of situations and dialog that Runyon depicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He spun humorous and sentimental tales of gamblers, hustlers, actors, and gangsters, few of whom go by <a href="/wiki/Square_(slang)" title="Square (slang)">"square"</a> names, preferring instead colorful monikers such as "Nathan Detroit", "Benny Southstreet", "Big Jule", "Harry the Horse", "Good Time Charley", "Dave the Dude", or "The Seldom Seen Kid". His distinctive vernacular style is known as "Runyonese": a mixture of formal speech and colorful slang, almost always in present tense, and always devoid of <a href="/wiki/Contraction_(grammar)" title="Contraction (grammar)">contractions</a>. He is credited with coining the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Hooray_Henry" title="Hooray Henry">Hooray Henry</a>", a term now used in British English to describe an upper-class, loud-mouthed, arrogant twit.</p>
<p>Runyon's fictional world is also known to the general public through the musical <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guys_and_Dolls_(musical)" title="Guys and Dolls (musical)">Guys and Dolls</a></i> based on two of his stories, "<a href="/wiki/The_Idyll_of_Miss_Sarah_Brown" title="The Idyll of Miss Sarah Brown">The Idyll of Miss Sarah Brown</a>" and "Blood Pressure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The musical additionally borrows characters and story elements from a few other Runyon stories, most notably "Pick The Winner". The film <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Miss_Marker" title="Little Miss Marker">Little Miss Marker</a></i> (and its two remakes, <i><a href="/wiki/Sorrowful_Jones" title="Sorrowful Jones">Sorrowful Jones</a></i> and the 1980 <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Miss_Marker_(1980_film)" title="Little Miss Marker (1980 film)">Little Miss Marker</a></i>) grew from his short story of the same name.</p>
<p>Runyon was also a well-known newspaper reporter, covering sports and general news for decades for various publications and syndicates owned by <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a>. Already famous for his fiction, he wrote a well-remembered "present tense" article on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a>'s Presidential inauguration in 1933 for the Universal Service, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst</a> syndicate, which was merged with the co-owned <a href="/wiki/International_News_Service" title="International News Service">International News Service</a> in 1937.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damon_Runyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg/250px-Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg/375px-Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg/500px-Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Damon_Runyon_House_2012-10-16_11-27-19.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boyhood home of Damon Runyon in Manhattan, Kansas.</div>

<p>Damon Runyon was born <b>Alfred Damon Runyan</b> to Alfred Lee and Elizabeth (Damon) Runyan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His relatives in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan,_Kansas" title="Manhattan, Kansas">Manhattan, Kansas</a> included several newspapermen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-house-7">[7]</a></sup> His grandfather was a newspaper printer from <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> who had relocated to Manhattan, Kansas in 1855, and his father was editor of his own newspaper in the town. In 1882 Runyon's father was forced to sell his newspaper, and the family moved westward. The family eventually settled in <a href="/wiki/Pueblo,_Colorado" title="Pueblo, Colorado">Pueblo, Colorado</a> in 1887, where Runyon spent the rest of his youth. By most accounts, he attended school only through the fourth grade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He began to work in the newspaper trade under his father in Pueblo. In present-day Pueblo, Runyon Field, the Damon Runyon Repertory Theater Company, and <a href="/wiki/Runyon_Lake" title="Runyon Lake">Runyon Lake</a> are named in his honor.</p>
<p>In 1898, when still in his teens, Runyon enlisted in the U.S. Army to fight in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>. While in the service, he was assigned to write for the <i>Manila Freedom</i> and <i>Soldier's Letter</i>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1570" data-file-width="2250" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg/250px-Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg/375px-Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg/500px-Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Runyon-Selective-Service-Registration-1918.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Runyon's World War I draft registration (September 1918)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After his military service, he worked for various Colorado newspapers, beginning in Pueblo. His first job as a reporter was in September 1900, when he was hired by the <i>Pueblo Star</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> he then worked in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountain</a> area during the first decade of the 1900s: at the <i>Denver Daily News</i>, he served as "sporting editor" (what would today be called "sports editor") and then worked as a staff writer. His expertise was in covering the semi-professional teams in Colorado; he even briefly managed a semi-pro team in Trinidad, Colorado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At one of the newspapers where he worked, the spelling of his last name was changed from "Runyan" to "Runyon", a change he let stand.</p>
<p>After a notable failure in trying to organize a <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball">minor baseball league</a>, which lasted less than a week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Runyon moved to New York City in 1910. In his first New York byline, the <i>American</i> editor dropped the "Alfred" and the name "Damon Runyon" appeared for the first time. For the next ten years he covered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> and professional boxing for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_American" title="New York American">New York American</a></i>.</p>
<p>He was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst newspapers'</a> baseball columnist for many years, beginning in 1911, and his knack for spotting the eccentric and the unusual, on the field or in the stands, is credited with revolutionizing the way baseball was covered. Perhaps as confirmation, Runyon was inducted into the writers' wing (the <a href="/wiki/J._G._Taylor_Spink_Award" title="J. G. Taylor Spink Award">J. G. Taylor Spink Award</a>) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in 1967. He is also a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Boxing_Hall_Of_Fame" title="International Boxing Hall Of Fame">International Boxing Hall Of Fame</a> and is known for dubbing heavyweight champion <a href="/wiki/James_J._Braddock" title="James J. Braddock">James J. Braddock</a>, the "<a href="/wiki/Cinderella_Man" title="Cinderella Man">Cinderella Man</a>". Runyon frequently contributed sports poems to the <i>American</i> on boxing and baseball themes, and also wrote numerous short stories and essays.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:320px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #FFFFF0;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">
<div class="poem">
<p>If I have all the tears that are shed on Broadway by guys in love, I will have enough salt water to start an opposition ocean to the Atlantic and Pacific, with enough left over to run the Great Salt Lake out of business. But I wish to say I never shed any of these tears personally, because I am never in love, and furthermore, barring a bad break, I never expect to be in love, for the way I look at it love is strictly the old phedinkus, and I tell the little guy as much.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">from "Tobias the Terrible",<br/>
collected in <i>More than Somewhat</i> (1937)</cite></div>
<p>One year, while covering spring training in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, he met <a href="/wiki/Pancho_Villa" title="Pancho Villa">Pancho Villa</a> in a bar and later accompanied the unsuccessful American expedition into <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> searching for Villa. It was while he was in Mexico that he met the young girl whom he eventually married.</p>
<p>Gambling, particularly on <a href="/wiki/Craps" title="Craps">craps</a> or horse races, was a common theme of Runyon's works, and he was a notorious gambler himself. One of his paraphrases from a well-known line in <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastes" title="Ecclesiastes">Ecclesiastes</a> ran: "The race is not always to the swift, nor the battle to the strong, but that's how the smart money bets."</p>
<p>A heavy drinker as a young man, he seems to have quit drinking soon after arriving in New York, after his drinking nearly cost him the courtship of the woman who became his first wife, Ellen Egan. He remained a heavy smoker.</p>
<p>His best friend was mobster accountant <a href="/wiki/Otto_Berman" title="Otto Berman">Otto Berman</a>, and he incorporated Berman into several of his stories under the alias "Regret, the horse player". When Berman was killed in a hit on Berman's boss, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Schultz" title="Dutch Schultz">Dutch Schultz</a>, Runyon quickly assumed the role of damage control for his deceased friend, correcting erroneous press releases (including one that stated Berman was one of Schultz's gunmen, to which Runyon replied, "Otto would have been as effective a bodyguard as a two-year-old.").</p>

<p>Runyon's marriage to Ellen Egan produced two children (Mary and Damon, Jr.), but broke up in 1928 over rumors that Runyon had become infatuated with Patrice Amati del Grande, a Mexican woman he had first met while covering the Pancho Villa raids in 1916 and discovered once again in New York, when she called the <i>American</i> seeking him out. Runyon had promised her in Mexico that if she would complete the education he paid for her, he would find her a dancing job in New York. She became his companion after he separated from his wife. After Ellen Runyon died of the effects of her own drinking problems, Runyon and del Grande married; that marriage ended in 1946 when she left him for a younger man.</p>
<p>Runyon died in New York City from <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_cancer" title="Esophageal cancer">throat cancer</a> in late 1946, at age 66. His body was cremated, and his ashes were illegally scattered from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DC-3" title="DC-3">DC-3</a> airplane over Broadway in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> by <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States_O-3)" title="Captain (United States O-3)">Captain</a> <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Rickenbacker" title="Eddie Rickenbacker">Eddie Rickenbacker</a> on December 18, 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The family plot of Damon Runyon is located at <a href="/wiki/Woodlawn_Cemetery_(Bronx,_New_York)" title="Woodlawn Cemetery (Bronx, New York)">Woodlawn Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">The Bronx</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damon_Runyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>After Runyon's death, his friend and fellow journalist, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Winchell" title="Walter Winchell">Walter Winchell</a>, went on his radio program and appealed for contributions to help fight cancer, eventually establishing the <a href="/wiki/Damon_Runyon_Cancer_Research_Foundation" title="Damon Runyon Cancer Research Foundation">Damon Runyon Cancer Memorial Fund</a> to support scientific research into causes of, and prevention of, cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>The first-ever <a href="/wiki/Telethon" title="Telethon">telethon</a> was hosted by <a href="/wiki/Milton_Berle" title="Milton Berle">Milton Berle</a> in 1949 to raise funds for the Damon Runyon Cancer Research Foundation.</li>
<li>Each year the Denver Press Club assigns the Damon Runyon Award to a prominent journalist. Past winners include <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Breslin" title="Jimmy Breslin">Jimmy Breslin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Royko" title="Mike Royko">Mike Royko</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Will" title="George Will">George Will</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Costas" title="Bob Costas">Bob Costas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Damon Runyon Elementary school in <a href="/wiki/Littleton,_Colorado" title="Littleton, Colorado">Littleton, Colorado</a>, is named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Damon_Runyon_Stakes" title="Damon Runyon Stakes">Damon Runyon Stakes</a> is a thoroughbred horse race run every December at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueduct_Race_Track" title="Aqueduct Race Track">Aqueduct Race Track</a>. Runyon loved horse racing and ran a small stable of his own.</li>
<li>In the mid-1930s, Runyon persuaded promoter <a href="/wiki/Leo_Seltzer" title="Leo Seltzer">Leo Seltzer</a> to formally change his <a href="/wiki/History_of_roller_derby" title="History of roller derby">Roller Derby</a> spectacle from a marathon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roller-skating" title="Roller-skating">roller-skating</a> race into a full-contact team sport,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> an innovation that was eventually revived in a DIY spirit seven decades later.</li>
<li>Alice Cooper makes a reference to Damon Runyon in the song, <i>Department Of Youth</i> (from his 1975 album, <i>Welcome To My Nightmare</i>), in the lyric, "And we've never heard of Billy Sunday Damon Runyon manners or Couth."</li>
<li>One block of West 45th Street (between 8th and 9th Avenues) in Manhattan's Hell's Kitchen is named Runyon's Way.</li>
<li>The house in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan,_Kansas" title="Manhattan, Kansas">Manhattan, Kansas</a>, where Runyon was born is listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-house-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2008, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Library_of_America" title="The Library of America">The Library of America</a> selected "The Eternal Blonde", Runyon's account of a 1927 murder trial, for inclusion in its two-century retrospective of American Crime Writing.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_style">Literary style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damon_Runyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literary style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="400" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png/220px-Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png/330px-Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/77/Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Breach_of_promise_by_Nicolas_Bentley.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An illustration from "Breach of Promise" showing Spanish John and Harry the Horse</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Muir" title="Frank Muir">Frank Muir</a> comments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> that Runyon's plots were, in the manner of <a href="/wiki/O._Henry" title="O. Henry">O. Henry</a>, neatly constructed with professionally wrought endings, but their distinction lay in the manner of their telling, as the author invented a peculiar argot for his characters to speak. Runyon almost totally avoids the past tense (English humourist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.C._Bentley" title="E.C. Bentley">E.C. Bentley</a> thought there was only one instance, and was willing to "lay plenty of 6 to 5 that it is nothing but a misprint"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> but "was" appears in the short stories "The Lily of St Pierre"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broadway.2C_Pan_Books_1975.2C_p._116_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broadway.2C_Pan_Books_1975.2C_p._116-20">[20]</a></sup> and "A Piece of Pie";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> "had" appears in "The Lily of St Pierre",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broadway.2C_Pan_Books_1975.2C_p._116_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Broadway.2C_Pan_Books_1975.2C_p._116-20">[20]</a></sup> "Undertaker Song"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and "Bloodhounds of Broadway"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>), and makes little use of the future tense, using the present for both. He also avoided the conditional, using instead the future indicative in situations that would normally require conditional. An example: "Now most any doll on Broadway will be very glad indeed to have Handsome Jack Madigan give her a tumble" (<i>Guys and Dolls</i>, "Social error"). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._C._Bentley" title="E. C. Bentley">E. C. Bentley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> comments that "there is a sort of ungrammatical purity about it [Runyon's resolute avoidance of the past tense], an almost religious exactitude." There is an homage to Runyon that makes use of this peculiarity ("<a href="/wiki/Chronic_Offender" title="Chronic Offender">Chronic Offender</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Spider_Robinson" title="Spider Robinson">Spider Robinson</a>) which involves a time machine and a man going by the name "Harry the Horse".</p>
<p>He uses many slang terms (which go unexplained in his stories), such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>pineapple = pineapple <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenade</a></li>
<li>roscoe/john roscoe/the old equalizer/that thing = gun</li>
<li>shiv = knife</li>
<li>noggin = head</li>
<li>snoot = nose</li>
</ul>
<p>There are many recurring composite phrases such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>ever-loving wife (occasionally "ever-loving doll")</li>
<li>more than somewhat (or "no little, and quite some"); this phrase was so typical that it was used as the title of one of his short story collections</li>
<li>loathe and despise</li>
<li>one and all</li>
</ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._C._Bentley" title="E. C. Bentley">E. C. Bentley</a> notes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> that Runyon's "telling use of the recurrent phrase and fixed epithet" demonstrates a debt to <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>.</p>
<p>Runyon's stories also employ occasional rhyming slang, similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockney_slang" title="Cockney slang">cockney</a> variety but native to New York (e.g.: "Miss Missouri Martin makes the following crack one night to her: 'Well, I do not see any Simple Simon on your lean and linger.' This is Miss Missouri Martin's way of saying she sees no diamond on Miss Billy Perry's finger." (from "Romance in the Roaring Forties")).</p>
<p>The comic effect of his style results partly from the juxtaposition of broad slang with mock-pomposity. Women, when not "dolls", "Judies", "pancakes", "tomatoes", or "broads", may be "characters of a female nature", for example. He typically avoided contractions such as "don't" in the example above, which also contributes significantly to the humorously pompous effect. In one sequence, a gangster tells another character to do as he is told, or else "find another world in which to live".</p>
<p>Runyon's short stories are told in the first person by a protagonist who is never named, and whose role is unclear; he knows many gangsters and does not appear to have a job, but he does not admit to any criminal involvement, and seems to be largely a bystander. He describes himself as "being known to one and all as a guy who is just around".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rob_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-rob-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damon_Runyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twenty of his stories became motion pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lady_for_a_Day" title="Lady for a Day">Lady for a Day</a></i> (1933)—Adapted by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Riskin" title="Robert Riskin">Robert Riskin</a>, who suggested the name change from Runyon's title "Madame La Gimp", the film garnered Academy Award nominations for Best Picture, Best Director (<a href="/wiki/Frank_Capra" title="Frank Capra">Frank Capra</a>), Best Actress (<a href="/wiki/May_Robson" title="May Robson">May Robson</a>), and Best Adaptation for the Screen (Riskin). It was remade as <i><a href="/wiki/Pocketful_of_Miracles" title="Pocketful of Miracles">Pocketful of Miracles</a></i> in 1961, with <a href="/wiki/Bette_Davis" title="Bette Davis">Bette Davis</a> in the Apple Annie role (fused with the "raggedy doll" from Runyon's short story "The Brain Goes Home"); <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a> recorded the upbeat title song (his rendition is not used in the film). The film received Oscar nominations for composers <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Cahn" title="Sammy Cahn">Sammy Cahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Van_Heusen" title="Jimmy Van Heusen">Jimmy Van Heusen</a> and for co-star <a href="/wiki/Peter_Falk" title="Peter Falk">Peter Falk</a> (Best Supporting Actor). In 1989, <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Chan" title="Jackie Chan">Jackie Chan</a> adapted the story yet again for the Hong Kong action film <i><a href="/wiki/Miracles_(1989_film)" title="Miracles (1989 film)">Miracles</a></i>, adding several of his trademark stunt sequences.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Little_Miss_Marker" title="Little Miss Marker">Little Miss Marker</a></i> (1934)—The film that made <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Temple" title="Shirley Temple">Shirley Temple</a> a star, launched her career as perhaps America's most beloved <a href="/wiki/Child_actor" title="Child actor">child film star</a>, and pushed her past <a href="/wiki/Greta_Garbo" title="Greta Garbo">Greta Garbo</a> as the nation's biggest film draw of the year. Also starred <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bickford" title="Charles Bickford">Charles Bickford</a>. Subsequent remakes include <i><a href="/wiki/Sorrowful_Jones" title="Sorrowful Jones">Sorrowful Jones</a></i> (1949) with <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">Bob Hope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Ball" title="Lucille Ball">Lucille Ball</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/40_Pounds_of_Trouble" title="40 Pounds of Trouble">40 Pounds of Trouble</a></i> (1962) with <a href="/wiki/Tony_Curtis" title="Tony Curtis">Tony Curtis</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Miss_Marker_(1980_film)" title="Little Miss Marker (1980 film)">Little Miss Marker</a></i> (1980) with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Matthau" title="Walter Matthau">Walter Matthau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">Julie Andrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Newhart" title="Bob Newhart">Bob Newhart</a> and Curtis.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damon_Runyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Damon Runyon Theater</i> radio series dramatized 52 of Runyon's short stories in weekly broadcasts running from October 1948 to September 1949 (with reruns until 1951).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digideli_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-digideli-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The series was produced by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ladd" title="Alan Ladd">Alan Ladd</a>'s Mayfair Transcription Company for syndication to local radio stations. <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_(actor)" title="John Brown (actor)">John Brown</a> played the character "Broadway", who doubled as host and narrator. The cast also comprised <a href="/wiki/Alan_Reed" title="Alan Reed">Alan Reed</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luis_Van_Rooten" title="Luis Van Rooten">Luis Van Rooten</a>, Joseph Du Val, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Mohr" title="Gerald Mohr">Gerald Mohr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lovejoy" title="Frank Lovejoy">Frank Lovejoy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herb_Vigran" title="Herb Vigran">Herb Vigran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheldon_Leonard" title="Sheldon Leonard">Sheldon Leonard</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Conrad" title="William Conrad">William Conrad</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeff_Chandler_(actor)" title="Jeff Chandler (actor)">Jeff Chandler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Stander" title="Lionel Stander">Lionel Stander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Miller_(actor)" title="Sidney Miller (actor)">Sidney Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive_Deering" title="Olive Deering">Olive Deering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_De_Santis" title="Joe De Santis">Joe De Santis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pat_O%27Brien_(actor)" title="Pat O'Brien (actor)">Pat O'Brien</a> was initially engaged for the role of "Broadway". The original stories were adapted for the radio by <a href="/wiki/Russell_S._Hughes" title="Russell S. Hughes">Russell Hughes</a>.</p>
<p>"Broadway's New York had a crisis each week, though the streets had a rose-tinged aura", wrote radio historian <a href="/wiki/John_Dunning_(writer)" title="John Dunning (writer)">John Dunning</a>. "The sad shows then were all the sadder; plays like <i>For a Pal</i> had a special poignance. The bulk of Runyon's work had been untapped by radio, and the well was deep."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:189</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Damon_Runyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Damon_Runyon_Theater" title="Damon Runyon Theater">Damon Runyon Theatre</a></i> aired on <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS-TV</a> from <a href="/wiki/1955%E2%80%9356_United_States_network_television_schedule" title="1955–56 United States network television schedule">1955 to 1956</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mike_McShane" title="Mike McShane">Mike McShane</a> told Runyon stories as monologues on British TV in 1994, and an accompanying book was released, both called <i>Broadway Stories</i>.</p>
