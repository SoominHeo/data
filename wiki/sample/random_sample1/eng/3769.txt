<p><b>David Elieser Deutsch</b>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society#Fellows" title="Royal Society">FRS</a> (born 18 May 1953), is an Israeli-born British <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>. He is a Visiting Professor in the Department of Atomic and Laser Physics at the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Quantum_Computation" title="Centre for Quantum Computation">Centre for Quantum Computation</a> (CQC) in the <a href="/wiki/Clarendon_Laboratory" title="Clarendon Laboratory">Clarendon Laboratory</a> of the University of Oxford. He pioneered the field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computation" title="Quantum computation">quantum computation</a> by formulating a description for a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_Turing_machine" title="Quantum Turing machine">quantum Turing machine</a>, as well as specifying an algorithm designed to run on a quantum computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deutsch1985_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deutsch1985-3">[3]</a></sup> He is a proponent of the <a href="/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation" title="Many-worlds interpretation">many-worlds interpretation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deutsch was born in <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a> in Israel on 18 May 1953, the son of Oskar and Tikva Deutsch. He attended <a href="/wiki/William_Ellis_School" title="William Ellis School">William Ellis School</a> in London (then a <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_aided_school" title="Voluntary aided school">voluntary aided</a> <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar school</a>) before reading <a href="/wiki/Natural_Sciences_(Cambridge)" title="Natural Sciences (Cambridge)">Natural Sciences</a> at <a href="/wiki/Clare_College,_Cambridge" title="Clare College, Cambridge">Clare College, Cambridge</a> and taking <a href="/wiki/Part_III_of_the_Mathematical_Tripos" title="Part III of the Mathematical Tripos">Part III of the Mathematical Tripos</a>. He went on to <a href="/wiki/Wolfson_College,_Oxford" title="Wolfson College, Oxford">Wolfson College, Oxford</a> for his doctorate in <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a> and wrote his thesis on <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curved_space-time" title="Curved space-time">curved space-time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peach_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-peach-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society of London</a>'s announcement that Deutsch had become a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 2008, the Society described Deutsch's contributions thus:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>David Deutsch laid the foundations of the quantum theory of computation, and has subsequently made or participated in many of the most important advances in the field, including the discovery of the first quantum algorithms, the theory of quantum logic gates and quantum computational networks, the first quantum error-correction scheme, and several fundamental quantum universality results. He has set the agenda for worldwide research efforts in this new, interdisciplinary field, made progress in understanding its philosophical implications (via a variant of the many-universes interpretation) and made it comprehensible to the general public, notably in his book The Fabric of Reality.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>His published work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_algorithms" title="Quantum algorithms">quantum algorithms</a> began with a ground-breaking 1985 paper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deu85_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deu85-5">[5]</a></sup> later expanded in 1992 along with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Jozsa" title="Richard Jozsa">Richard Jozsa</a> to produce the <a href="/wiki/Deutsch%E2%80%93Jozsa_algorithm" title="Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm">Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm</a>, one of the first examples of a quantum algorithm that is exponentially faster than any possible deterministic classical algorithm.</p>
<p>He is currently working on <a href="/wiki/Constructor_theory" title="Constructor theory">constructor theory</a>, an attempt at generalizing the quantum theory of computation to cover not just computation but all physical processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Together with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chiara_Marletto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chiara Marletto (page does not exist)">Chiara Marletto</a>, he published a paper in December 2014 entitled <i>Constructor theory of information</i>, that conjectures that information can be expressed solely in terms of which transformations of physical systems are possible and which are impossible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirac_Prize" title="Dirac Prize">Dirac Prize</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Physics" title="Institute of Physics">Institute of Physics</a> in 1998,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> and the Edge of Computation Science Prize in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <i>Fabric of Reality</i> was shortlisted for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_Prizes_for_Science_Books" title="Royal Society Prizes for Science Books">Rhone-Poulenc science book award</a> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_science_books">Popular science books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popular science books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Fabric_of_Reality"><i>The Fabric of Reality</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Fabric of Reality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Fabric_of_Reality" title="The Fabric of Reality">The Fabric of Reality</a></div>
<p>In his 1997 book <i>The Fabric of Reality</i>, Deutsch details his "Theory of Everything." It aims not at the reduction of everything to particle physics, but rather mutual support among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiversal" title="Multiversal">multiversal</a>, computational, epistemological, and evolutionary principles. His theory of everything is somewhat <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergentist</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Reductionism" title="Reductionism">reductive</a>.</p>
<p>There are "four strands" to his theory:</p>
<ol>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_Everett" title="Hugh Everett">Hugh Everett</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation" title="Many-worlds interpretation">many-worlds interpretation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum physics</a>, "the first and most important of the four strands."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, especially its anti-inductivism and requiring a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_realism" title="Scientific realism">realist</a> (non-instrumental) interpretation of scientific theories, as well as its emphasis on taking seriously those bold conjectures that resist falsification.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a>'s theory of computation, especially as developed in Deutsch's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turing_principle" title="Turing principle">Turing principle</a>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Turing_machine" title="Universal Turing machine">Universal Turing machine</a> is replaced by Deutsch's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_quantum_computer" title="Universal quantum computer">universal quantum computer</a>. ("<i>The</i> theory of computation is now the quantum theory of computation.")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a>'s refinement of <a href="/wiki/Darwinism" title="Darwinism">Darwinian</a> evolutionary theory and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_evolutionary_synthesis" title="Modern evolutionary synthesis">modern evolutionary synthesis</a>, especially the ideas of replicator and meme as they integrate with Popperian problem-solving (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epistemological" title="Epistemological">epistemological</a> strand).</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Beginning_of_Infinity"><i>The Beginning of Infinity</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Beginning of Infinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Beginning_of_Infinity" title="The Beginning of Infinity">The Beginning of Infinity</a></div>
<p>Deutsch's second book, <i>The Beginning of Infinity: Explanations that Transform the World</i>, was published on 31 March 2011. In this book Deutsch views the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> of the 18th century as near the beginning of a potentially unending sequence of purposeful knowledge creation. He examines the nature of <a href="/wiki/Meme" title="Meme">memes</a> and how and why <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity</a> evolved in humans.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deutsch is an atheist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He is also a founding member of the parenting and educational method known as <a href="/wiki/Taking_Children_Seriously" title="Taking Children Seriously">Taking Children Seriously</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
