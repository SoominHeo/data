<p><b>David Stephen Mitchell</b> (born 12 January 1969) is an English novelist. He has written seven novels, two of which, <i><a href="/wiki/Number9dream" title="Number9dream">number9dream</a></i> (2001) and <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(novel)" title="Cloud Atlas (novel)">Cloud Atlas</a></i> (2004), were shortlisted for the <a href="/wiki/Booker_Prize" title="Booker Prize">Booker Prize</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mitchell_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mitchell was born in <a href="/wiki/Southport" title="Southport">Southport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>), England, and raised in <a href="/wiki/Malvern,_Worcestershire" title="Malvern, Worcestershire">Malvern, Worcestershire</a>. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Hanley_Castle_High_School" title="Hanley Castle High School">Hanley Castle High School</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kent" title="University of Kent">University of Kent</a>, where he obtained a degree in English and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Literature" title="American Literature">American Literature</a> followed by an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">M.A.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Comparative_literature" title="Comparative literature">Comparative Literature</a>.</p>
<p>Mitchell lived in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> for a year, then moved to <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>, Japan, where he taught English to technical students for eight years, before returning to England, where he could live on his earnings as a writer and support his pregnant wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mitchell_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mitchell's first novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Ghostwritten" title="Ghostwritten">Ghostwritten</a></i> (1999), moves around the globe, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa,_Japan" title="Okinawa, Japan">Okinawa</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> to pre-Millennial <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, as nine narrators tell stories that interlock and intersect. The novel won the <a href="/wiki/John_Llewellyn_Rhys_Prize" title="John Llewellyn Rhys Prize">John Llewellyn Rhys Prize</a> (for best work of British literature written by an author under 35) and was shortlisted for the <a href="/wiki/Guardian_First_Book_Award" title="Guardian First Book Award">Guardian First Book Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His two subsequent novels, <i><a href="/wiki/Number9dream" title="Number9dream">number9dream</a></i> (2001) and <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(novel)" title="Cloud Atlas (novel)">Cloud Atlas</a></i> (2004), were both shortlisted for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booker_prize" title="Booker prize">Man Booker Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2003, he was selected as one of <a href="/wiki/Granta" title="Granta">Granta</a>'s Best of Young British Novelists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2007, Mitchell was listed among <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i></a> magazine's 100 Most Influential People in The World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(novel)" title="Cloud Atlas (novel)">Cloud Atlas</a></i> was made into a <a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(film)" title="Cloud Atlas (film)">film</a>. One segment of <i>number9dream</i> was made into a BAFTA nominated short film in 2011 starring Martin Freeman, titled <i>The Voorman Problem</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In recent years he has also written opera libretti. <i>Wake</i>, based on the 2000 <a href="/wiki/Enschede_fireworks_disaster" title="Enschede fireworks disaster">Enschede fireworks disaster</a> and with music by <a href="/wiki/Klaas_de_Vries_(composer)" title="Klaas de Vries (composer)">Klaas de Vries</a>, was performed by the Dutch Nationale Reisopera in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He has also finished another opera, <i>Sunken Garden</i>, with the Dutch composer <a href="/wiki/Michel_van_der_Aa" title="Michel van der Aa">Michel van der Aa</a>, which premiered in 2013 by the <a href="/wiki/English_National_Opera" title="English National Opera">English National Opera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several of Mitchell's book covers were created by design duo Kai and Sunny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Mitchell has also collaborated with the duo, by contributing two short stories to their art exhibits in 2011 and 2014.</p>
<p>Mitchell's sixth novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bone_Clocks" title="The Bone Clocks">The Bone Clocks</a></i>, was published on 2 September 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In an interview in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i>, Mitchell said that the novel has "dollops of the fantastic in it", and is about "stuff between life and death".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blogs.spectator.co.uk_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-blogs.spectator.co.uk-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>The Bone Clocks</i> was longlisted for the <a href="/wiki/2014_Man_Booker_Prize" title="2014 Man Booker Prize">2014 Man Booker Prize</a>.</p>
<p>Mitchell was the second author to contribute to the <a href="/wiki/Future_Library_project" title="Future Library project">Future Library project</a> and delivered his book 'From Me Flows What You Call Time' on May 28, 2016.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mitchell_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After another stint in Japan, Mitchell currently lives with his wife, Keiko Yoshida, and their two children in <a href="/wiki/Ardfield" title="Ardfield">Ardfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clonakilty" title="Clonakilty">Clonakilty</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>, Ireland. In an essay for <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>, Mitchell wrote:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> "I knew I wanted to be a writer since I was a kid, but until I came to Japan to live in 1994 I was too easily distracted to do much about it. I would probably have become a writer wherever I lived, but would I have become the same writer if I'd spent the last six years in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Moose_Jaw" title="Moose Jaw">Moose Jaw</a>, on an oil rig or in the circus? This is my answer to myself."</p>
<p>Mitchell has the <a href="/wiki/Speech_disorder" title="Speech disorder">speech disorder</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stammering" title="Stammering">stammering</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prospect180_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-prospect180-15">[15]</a></sup> and considers the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_Speech" title="The King's Speech">The King's Speech</a></i> (2010) to be one of the most accurate portrayals of what it's like to be a stammerer:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prospect180_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-prospect180-15">[15]</a></sup> "I'd probably still be avoiding the subject today had I not outed myself by writing a semi-autobiographical novel, <i>Black Swan Green</i>, narrated by a stammering 13 year old."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prospect180_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-prospect180-15">[15]</a></sup> Mitchell is also a patron of the <a href="/wiki/British_Stammering_Association" title="British Stammering Association">British Stammering Association</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mitchell's son has <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>, and in 2013 he and his wife Keiko Yoshida translated into English a book written by <a href="/wiki/Naoki_Higashida" title="Naoki Higashida">Naoki Higashida</a>, a 13-year-old Japanese boy with autism, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Reason_I_Jump:_One_Boy%27s_Voice_from_the_Silence_of_Autism" title="The Reason I Jump: One Boy's Voice from the Silence of Autism">The Reason I Jump: One Boy's Voice from the Silence of Autism</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TimesReview_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-TimesReview-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
