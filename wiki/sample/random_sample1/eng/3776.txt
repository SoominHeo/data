<p><b>David's Sling</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">קלע דוד</span>‎, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Hebrew" title="Romanization of Hebrew">translit.</a> </small><i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Hebrew transliteration">Kelah Da'vid</span></i>‎), also formerly known as <b>Magic Wand</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">שרביט קסמים</span>‎, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Hebrew" title="Romanization of Hebrew">translit.</a> </small><i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Hebrew transliteration">Sharvit Ksamim</span></i>‎), is an <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a> military system being jointly developed by the <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_contractor" title="Defense contractor">defense contractor</a> <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Advanced_Defense_Systems" title="Rafael Advanced Defense Systems">Rafael Advanced Defense Systems</a> and the American defense contractor <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a>, designed to intercept tactical ballistic missiles, medium- to long-range <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_missiles" title="Cruise missiles">cruise missiles</a>, fired at ranges from 40 km (24.85 miles) to 300 km (186.41 miles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> David's Sling is meant to replace the <a href="/wiki/MIM-23_Hawk" title="MIM-23 Hawk">MIM-23 Hawk</a> and <a href="/wiki/MIM-104_Patriot" title="MIM-104 Patriot">MIM-104 Patriot</a> in the Israeli arsenal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patriot_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-patriot-2">[2]</a></sup> It is designed to intercept the newest generation of tactical ballistic missiles, such as <a href="/wiki/9K720_Iskander" title="9K720 Iskander">Iskander</a>, using an on-board dual <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">CCD</a>/<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">IR</a> seekers to distinguish between decoys and the actual warhead of the missile, in addition to tracking by <a href="/wiki/Elta" title="Elta">Elta</a> <a href="/wiki/EL/M-2084" title="EL/M-2084">EL/M-2084</a> <a href="/wiki/Active_electronically_scanned_array" title="Active electronically scanned array">Active electronically scanned array</a> multi-mode radar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The multi-stage interceptor consists of a solid-fuel, rocket motor booster, followed by an asymmetrical <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">kill vehicle</a> with advanced steering for super-maneuverability during the kill-stage. A three-pulse motor provides additional acceleration and maneuverability during the terminal phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> David's Sling was planned to be deployed in 2015, but budget shortfalls for infrastructure for deployable missile batteries will delay its operational date to 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>David's Sling is meant to bolster the second tier of Israel's theater missile defense system. The two-stage radar and electro-optic guided system has a 70–300 kilometer range, three times that of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Dome" title="Iron Dome">Iron Dome</a>. The system can be deployed against aircraft and cruise missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name David's Sling comes from the biblical story of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_and_Goliath" title="David and Goliath">David and Goliath</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It will form one level of Israel's future multi-tiered missile defense system that Israel is developing, which will also include <a href="/wiki/Arrow_(Israeli_missile)" title="Arrow (Israeli missile)">Arrow 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arrow_3" title="Arrow 3">Arrow 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Dome" title="Iron Dome">Iron Dome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Beam" title="Iron Beam">Iron Beam</a> from as early as 2018.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David%27s_Sling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The interceptor is a <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">two-stage</a> <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a>, with two targeting and guidance systems installed in its nose-tip (a <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and an electro-optical sensor). In 2006 Rafael was awarded a contract to develop a defense system to counter the threat of medium- to long-range rockets with ranges between 70 and 250 km (43 and 155 mi). In order to enable Israel to make use of the financial aid provided by the United States to further develop the system and to produce it, a partnership was established with Raytheon which will develop the missile firing unit and overall logistic system and assist Rafael with developing the interceptor. In some of Raytheon's publications, the interceptor is referred to as "<b>Stunner</b>". In November 2012, David's Sling was expected to enter operational service in 2013 or 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> David's Sling owes much to the <a href="/wiki/SPYDER" title="SPYDER">SPYDER</a> programme's modified Python 5 and DERBY seeker technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patriot_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-patriot-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the David's Sling system, which is designed to intercept medium- and long-range rockets, the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Dome" title="Iron Dome">Iron Dome</a> system, a separate system with which it will be used in conjunction, designed to intercept short-range rockets (4–70 km), and the <a href="/wiki/Arrow_(Israeli_missile)" title="Arrow (Israeli missile)">Arrow missile</a>, a separate system designed to intercept <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missiles</a>, are already in use.</p>
<p>On 25 November 2012, Israel successfully tested the Stunner interceptor missile. The David's Sling battery, stationed at an undisclosed desert location in Southern Israel, fired and destroyed the incoming missile with a two-stage interceptor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late November 2014, the IDF revealed that the David's Sling system would soon be deployed in various areas around Israel for a trial period before becoming operational. Initial trials will focus on its ability to intercept short and medium-range rockets and missiles with a range of coverage three times greater than Iron Dome. After that, the system needs to undergo two further trials, testing its ability to intercept aircraft and longer-range missiles and then cruise missiles. David's Sling will be linked up to the Home Front Command's command and control systems, as well as also having its own independent interception management center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2015, Israel asked the US Congress for $250M in additional assistance in producing David's Sling. US Companies that would be awarded contracts include Raytheon Co.; Arlington, Virginia-based Orbital ATK Inc.; and Falls Church, Virginia-based Northrop Grumman Corp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other defense websites reported that Israel had requested US $150M in funding for the procurement phase, which will include two systems controlling multiple fire units, covering the entire area of Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Still other sources said development costs for David's Sling were capped to around $250M so far. The $150M request is for initial procurement of one or two batteries. Since two batteries can cover the whole territory of Israel it is clear that Israeli initial procurement will start with just two batteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 21 December 2015, the David's Sling Weapon System (DSWS) demonstrated its ability to destroy salvos of heavy long-range rockets and short-range ballistic missiles, completing the first block of developmental tests. With the testing regiment completed, the system is slated for delivery to the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Air_Force" title="Israeli Air Force">Israeli Air Force</a> in the first quarter of 2016. David's Sling will protect areas above the short-range Iron Dome and under the upper-atmospheric Arrow-2, particularly against threats like the Russian <a href="/wiki/9K720_Iskander" title="9K720 Iskander">9K720 Iskander</a> tactical ballistic missiles, the Syrian <a href="/wiki/Khaibar-1" title="Khaibar-1">Khaibar-1</a> 302 mm rockets and the Iranian <a href="/wiki/Fateh-110" title="Fateh-110">Fateh-110</a> used by Hezbollah, as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scud-B" title="Scud-B">Scud-B</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_interest">Foreign interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David%27s_Sling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foreign interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 17 November 2010, in an interview Rafael's Vice President Mr. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lova_Drori&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lova Drori (page does not exist)">Lova Drori</a> confirmed that the David's Sling system has been offered to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Armed_Forces" title="Indian Armed Forces">Indian Armed Forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2015, it became publicized that all six countries of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council" title="Gulf Cooperation Council">Gulf Cooperation Council</a> (GCC) were interested in procuring the Israeli David's Sling missile defense system as a response to the Iranian missile threat. This comes after U.S. urging for GCC members to more closely cooperate on missile defense through joint procurement and information sharing. Any deal however would occur between Raytheon and other American companies, partly because of their involvement in the development of the system, and partly because of continuing local attitudes towards Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PAAC-4">PAAC-4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David%27s_Sling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: PAAC-4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Obering" title="Henry Obering">Henry Obering</a>, former director of the <a href="/wiki/Missile_Defense_Agency" title="Missile Defense Agency">U.S. Missile Defense Agency</a>, "We wanted a truly co-managed program because the United States will be very interested in this for our own purposes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2013, Raytheon revealed it was working with international partners to develop a new air-defense missile system. The system is based on the <a href="/wiki/MIM-104_Patriot#The_AN.2FMPQ-53_and_AN.2FMPQ-65_Radar_Set" title="MIM-104 Patriot">AN/MPQ-53</a> radar from the <a href="/wiki/MIM-104_Patriot" title="MIM-104 Patriot">MIM-104 Patriot</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Kongsberg_Defence_%26_Aerospace" title="Kongsberg Defence &amp; Aerospace">Kongsberg</a>/Raytheon Fire Direction Center, and the <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Advanced_Defense_Systems" title="Rafael Advanced Defense Systems">Rafael</a> Stunner surface-to-air missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2013, Raytheon and Rafael began to seek funding for a fourth-generation Patriot intercepting system, called the <b>Patriot Advanced Affordable Capability-4 (PAAC-4)</b>. The system aims to integrate the Stunner interceptor from the jointly-funded David's Sling program with Patriot PAC-3 radars, launchers, and engagement control stations. The two-stage, multimode seeking Stunner would replace single-stage, radar-guided PAC-3 missiles produced by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a>. Government and industry sources claim the Stunner-based PAAC-4 interceptors will offer improved operational performance at 20 percent of the $2 million unit cost of the Lockheed-built PAC-3 missiles. The companies are seeking $20 million in U.S. government funding to demonstrate cost and performance claims through a prototype PAAC-4 system. Israeli program officials have said that a previous teaming agreement between Raytheon and Rafael would allow the U.S. company to assume prime contractor status, and produce at least 60 percent of the Stunner missile in the United States. The <a href="/wiki/Missile_Defense_Agency" title="Missile Defense Agency">Missile Defense Agency</a> has said that the U.S. Army is considering use of the Stunner as a potential solution to future U.S. military requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
