<p><b>David Ayer</b> (born January 18, 1968) is an American film director, producer, and screenwriter. He is best known for being the writer of <i><a href="/wiki/Training_Day" title="Training Day">Training Day</a></i> (2001), and the director and writer of <i><a href="/wiki/Harsh_Times" title="Harsh Times">Harsh Times</a></i> (2005), <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Kings" title="Street Kings">Street Kings</a></i> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/End_of_Watch" title="End of Watch">End of Watch</a></i> (2012), <i><a href="/wiki/Sabotage_(2014_film)" title="Sabotage (2014 film)">Sabotage</a></i> (2014), <i><a href="/wiki/Fury_(2014_film)" title="Fury (2014 film)">Fury</a></i> (2014), and <i><a href="/wiki/Suicide_Squad_(film)" title="Suicide Squad (film)">Suicide Squad</a></i> (2016).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Ayer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ayer was born in <a href="/wiki/Champaign,_Illinois" title="Champaign, Illinois">Champaign, Illinois</a>, on January 18, 1968, and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Minnesota" title="Bloomington, Minnesota">Bloomington, Minnesota</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda, Maryland</a>, where he was kicked out of his house by his parents as a teenager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Ayer then lived with his cousin in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, where his experiences in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Central_Los_Angeles" title="South Central Los Angeles">South Central Los Angeles</a> became the inspiration for many of his films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Ayer then enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submariner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Shia_LaBeouf" title="Shia LaBeouf">Shia LaBeouf</a>, who starred in Ayer's film <i><a href="/wiki/Fury_(2014_film)" title="Fury (2014 film)">Fury</a></i>, Ayer is "a full subscriber to Christianity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Ayer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2520" data-file-width="1728" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg/170px-David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg/255px-David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg/340px-David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:David_Ayer_by_Gage_Skidmore.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Ayer's screenplay, <i><a href="/wiki/U-571_(film)" title="U-571 (film)">U-571</a></i> was based on his experiences as a Submariner in the US Navy. Ayer collaborated on the screenplay for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fast_and_the_Furious_(2001_film)" title="The Fast and the Furious (2001 film)">The Fast and the Furious</a></i> in 2001. Ayer wrote the screenplay for crime drama <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_Blue_(movie)" title="Dark Blue (movie)">Dark Blue</a></i>, and it was his research into the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">Los Angeles Police Department</a> that led to his most prominent screenplay, <i><a href="/wiki/Training_Day" title="Training Day">Training Day</a></i>. Ayer signed a contract to write a screenplay for <i><a href="/wiki/S.W.A.T._(film)" title="S.W.A.T. (film)">S.W.A.T.</a></i>, which was based on his original story pitch. The film was directed by <a href="/wiki/Clark_Johnson" title="Clark Johnson">Clark Johnson</a> and released in 2003.</p>
<p>Ayer's directorial debut was with the film <i><a href="/wiki/Harsh_Times" title="Harsh Times">Harsh Times</a></i>, a drama set on the streets of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Central_Los_Angeles" title="South Central Los Angeles">South Central Los Angeles</a>, showing how drug use and past military experiences affects people's attempts to lead normal lives. He then went on to direct the crime thriller <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Kings" title="Street Kings">Street Kings</a></i>, which was released in 2008.</p>
<p>Ayer later wrote and directed <i><a href="/wiki/End_of_Watch" title="End of Watch">End of Watch</a></i>, a drama about the daily lives of two South Central Los Angeles policemen, played by <a href="/wiki/Jake_Gyllenhaal" title="Jake Gyllenhaal">Jake Gyllenhaal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Michael Peña">Michael Peña</a>. The film was released in the fall of 2012 to profitable box-office returns and favorable reception from critics, with <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> naming it as the fourth-best film of 2012, hailing it as "one of the best police movies in recent years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His next film was the crime thriller <i><a href="/wiki/Sabotage_(2014_film)" title="Sabotage (2014 film)">Sabotage</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a>; the film was released on March 28, 2014. He wrote and directed the World War II-set film, <i><a href="/wiki/Fury_(2014_film)" title="Fury (2014 film)">Fury</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shia_LaBeouf" title="Shia LaBeouf">Shia LaBeouf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logan_Lerman" title="Logan Lerman">Logan Lerman</a>; the film was released in October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-furyref1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-furyref1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ayer wrote and directed the <a href="/wiki/Suicide_Squad_(film)" title="Suicide Squad (film)">film adaptation</a> of the comic book <i><a href="/wiki/Suicide_Squad" title="Suicide Squad">Suicide Squad</a></i>, which was released on August 5, 2016. The film, along with Ayer's directing, received negative reviews. Critics were negative to mixed in their opinions, though it became his most commercially successful film to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ayer is next set to direct "a contemporary cop thriller, but with fantastical elements" titled <i>Bright</i>, which is set to star <a href="/wiki/Will_Smith" title="Will Smith">Will Smith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joel_Edgerton" title="Joel Edgerton">Joel Edgerton</a> with a script penned by <a href="/wiki/Max_Landis" title="Max Landis">Max Landis</a> that Ayer himself rewrote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Netflix officially picked up the film for a $90 million deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Landis stated in an interview that official production is expected to begin in September 2016, but that they've already shot a small part in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 13, 2016, Ayer was brought on board to direct the spin-off of Suicide Squad, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gotham_City_Sirens_(film)" title="Gotham City Sirens (film)">Gotham City Sirens</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Margot_Robbie" title="Margot Robbie">Margot Robbie</a> reprising her role of <a href="/wiki/Harley_Quinn" title="Harley Quinn">Harley Quinn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
