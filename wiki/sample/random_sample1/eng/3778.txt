<p><b>David Clive Wilson, Baron Wilson of Tillyorn</b>, <span style="font-size:100%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_Companion_of_the_Order_of_the_Thistle" title="Knight Companion of the Order of the Thistle">KT</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_Grand_Cross_of_the_Order_of_St_Michael_and_St_George" title="Knight Grand Cross of the Order of St Michael and St George">GCMG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh">FRSE</a></span> (born 14 February 1935) is a retired Scottish <a href="/wiki/Administrator_of_the_Government" title="Administrator of the Government">administrator</a>, diplomat and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinologist" title="Sinologist">Sinologist</a>. Lord Wilson of Tillyorn was the penultimate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander-in-Chief" title="Commander-in-Chief">Commander-in-Chief</a> and 27th <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Hong_Kong" title="Governor of Hong Kong">Governor of Hong Kong</a> (from 1987 to 1992). He served as <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Commissioner_to_the_General_Assembly_of_the_Church_of_Scotland" title="Lord High Commissioner to the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland">Lord High Commissioner to the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland</a>, the British Monarch's representative to the Assembly, in 2010 and 2011.</p>
<p>He sits in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Crossbencher" title="Crossbencher">crossbencher</a>.</p>

<p>Wilson was born in <a href="/wiki/Alloa" title="Alloa">Alloa</a> in Scotland and was educated at <a href="/wiki/Glenalmond_College" title="Glenalmond College">Trinity College, Glenalmond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keble_College,_Oxford" title="Keble College, Oxford">Keble College, Oxford</a> (1955–58, Master of Arts), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_Oriental_and_African_Studies" title="School of Oriental and African Studies">School of Oriental and African Studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> (PhD in contemporary Chinese history, obtained in 1973). He studied Chinese at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hong_Kong" title="University of Hong Kong">University of Hong Kong</a> from 1960 to 1962 and then served in the British Mission in Beijing. He is fluent in Mandarin and spent 10 of his 30 years as a diplomat in China.</p>
<p>In 1968 Wilson resigned from the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office" title="Foreign and Commonwealth Office">Foreign and Commonwealth Office</a> to edit <i><a href="/wiki/The_China_Quarterly" title="The China Quarterly">The China Quarterly</a></i> at the School of Oriental and African Studies. After rejoining the Diplomatic Service in 1974 he worked in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office" title="Cabinet Office">Cabinet Office</a> and then, from 1977 to 1981, as Political Adviser to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crawford_Murray_MacLehose,_Baron_MacLehose_of_Beoch" title="Crawford Murray MacLehose, Baron MacLehose of Beoch">Sir Murray MacLehose</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Hong_Kong" title="Governor of Hong Kong">Governor of Hong Kong</a>. Following that he became Head of Southern European Department in the FCO and then Assistant Under Secretary for Asia and the Pacific during which time he was Head of the British side of the Working Group engaged in drafting the 1984 <a href="/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Declaration" title="Sino-British Joint Declaration">Sino-British Joint Declaration</a> on Hong Kong and then, in 1984, the first Senior British Representative on the <a href="/wiki/Sino-British_Joint_Liaison_Group" title="Sino-British Joint Liaison Group">Sino-British Joint Liaison Group</a> (中英聯合聯絡小組) set up under the Joint Declaration. When Sir <a href="/wiki/Edward_Youde" title="Edward Youde">Edward Youde</a> died in Beijing on 5 December 1986, Wilson replaced him to become the Governor of Hong Kong in 1987.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong_governorship">Hong Kong governorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wilson,_Baron_Wilson_of_Tillyorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hong Kong governorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As governor, Wilson had to deal with the fallout in Hong Kong from the 1989 <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989" title="Tiananmen Square protests of 1989">Tiananmen Square protests</a> in Beijing. He also encountered the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people_in_Hong_Kong" title="Vietnamese people in Hong Kong">Vietnamese refugee</a> problem, which steadily grew worse and led to the 1988 policy of repatriating those found not to qualify for refugee status (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%AFt_%C4%91%E1%BA%A7u_t%E1%BB%AB_nay" title="Bắt đầu từ nay">bắt đầu từ nay</a>). In October 1989, Wilson proposed, in the Governor's Annual Policy Address, the building of an airport on <a href="/wiki/Lantau_Island" title="Lantau Island">Lantau Island</a>, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rose_Garden_Project" title="Rose Garden Project">Rose Garden Project</a> (玫瑰園計劃 see <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">Hong Kong International Airport</a>). The proposal was created out of concern that the then-current airport at <a href="/wiki/Kai_Tak_Airport" title="Kai Tak Airport">Kai Tak</a>, which had been in use since the beginning of Aviation in Hong Kong, was not equipped to handle modern aviation needs.</p>
<p>In December 1991, Britain announced the removal of Wilson as the governor, which had been widely criticised by Hong Kong's pro-democratic camp, three months after their strong performance in Hong Kong's first direct elections to the Legislative Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Wilson left Hong Kong in June 1992 following the completion of his five-year term as governor. Before his retirement Wilson embarked on political reforms that paved the way for eighteen legislators of the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Council_of_Hong_Kong" title="Legislative Council of Hong Kong">Legislative Council</a> to be directly elected by the people of Hong Kong.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_in_Chinese">Name in Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wilson,_Baron_Wilson_of_Tillyorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name in Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Wilson studied <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a> at The University of Hong Kong, he was given the Chinese name Wei Dewei (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">魏德巍</span>; <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>: <i><span lang="yue-jyutping" xml:lang="yue-jyutping">Ngai6 Dak1-ngai4</span></i>); "Wei" was short for "Wilson", while "Dewei" is a <a href="/wiki/Transcription_into_Chinese_characters" title="Transcription into Chinese characters">Chinese transliteration</a> of David. However, when he arrived in Hong Kong to take up the position of <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Hong_Kong" title="Governor of Hong Kong">colonial governor</a>, it was noted that the Cantonese pronunciation of his earlier name sounded almost nothing like his actual name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristof_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristof-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nicholas Kristof of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> commented that Wilson's previous Chinese name sounded too much like "hypocrisy to the extent of danger" (偽得危). Kristof also noted that the surname and the third character can each be divided into components meaning "1,800 female ghosts" (千八女鬼).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristof_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kristof-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Chan Chung-kwong of <a href="/wiki/RTHK" title="RTHK">RTHK</a>, some locals referred to the name as "two ghosts knocking at the door" (雙鬼拍門), which is unlucky and inappropriate for a governor's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RTHK-CCK_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-RTHK-CCK-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Wilson assumed his position as Governor of Hong Kong, he changed his Chinese name to Wai Yik-Shun (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">衛奕信</span>; <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>: <i><span lang="yue-jyutping" xml:lang="yue-jyutping">Wai6 Jik6-seon3</span></i>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantonese_Chinese" title="Cantonese Chinese">Cantonese Chinese</a> pronunciation of which is more similar to his English name. The new name also has a more favourable meaning and was composed of 33 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stroke_(CJK_character)" title="Stroke (CJK character)">strokes</a>, said to be a lucky number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RTHK-CCK_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-RTHK-CCK-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-governorship">Post-governorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wilson,_Baron_Wilson_of_Tillyorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-governorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After his governorship and the elevation to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_peerage" title="Life peerage">life peerage</a> with the title <b>Baron Wilson of Tillyorn</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Finzean" title="Finzean">Finzean</a> in the District of <a href="/wiki/Kincardine_and_Deeside" title="Kincardine and Deeside">Kincardine and Deeside</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Fanling" title="Fanling">Fanling</a> in Hong Kong in 1992,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Wilson became the Chairman of the energy company <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Hydro_Electric" title="Scottish Hydro Electric">Scottish Hydro Electric</a> plc. (later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_and_Southern_Energy" title="Scottish and Southern Energy">Scottish and Southern Energy</a>) based in <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth, Scotland</a> from 1993 to 2000. He was a member of the Board of the <a href="/wiki/British_Council" title="British Council">British Council</a> (and Chairman of its Scottish Committee) from 1993 to 2002; a Director of the Martin Currie Pacific Trust from 1993 to 2002 and Chairman of the Trustees of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Museums_of_Scotland" title="National Museums of Scotland">National Museums of Scotland</a> from 2002 to 2006. In 1996 he was appointed a Vice-President of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Scottish_Geographical_Society" title="Royal Scottish Geographical Society">Royal Scottish Geographical Society</a>; and he was the Chancellor of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">University of Aberdeen</a> from 1997 to 2013, succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/Duchess_of_Rothesay" title="Duchess of Rothesay">Duchess of Rothesay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was President of the Bhutan Society of the UK (1993–2008), the Hong Kong Society (1994– ) and the Hong Kong Association (1994– ). Wilson was made a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Thistle" title="Order of the Thistle">Knight of the Thistle</a> in 2000. He served as Master of <a href="/wiki/Peterhouse,_Cambridge" title="Peterhouse, Cambridge">Peterhouse, Cambridge</a> from 2002 to 2008. He was President of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal Society of Edinburgh">Royal Society of Edinburgh</a>; from October 2008 to October 2011, when he was succeeded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Peebles_Arbuthnott" title="John Peebles Arbuthnott">Sir John Arbuthnott</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In January 2010 he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Commissioner_to_the_General_Assembly_of_the_Church_of_Scotland" title="Lord High Commissioner to the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland">Lord High Commissioner to the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland</a> for 2010.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_named_after_him">Places named after him</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wilson,_Baron_Wilson_of_Tillyorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Places named after him">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 78-kilometre <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Trail" title="Wilson Trail">Wilson Trail</a>, containing ten hiking segments aligned north-south in Hong Kong, is named after him, as is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Wilson_Heritage_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lord Wilson Heritage Trust (page does not exist)">Lord Wilson Heritage Trust</a> (衛奕信勳爵文物信託) established in December 1992 to preserve and conserve Hong Kong's heritage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wilson,_Baron_Wilson_of_Tillyorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wilson was criticised by the pro-democracy camp for not moving more quickly towards a fully elected Legislature based on universal suffrage and for paying too much attention to the views of the Government in China in agreeing arrangements for a process of increasing the number of fully elected seats up to and beyond the transfer of sovereignty in 1997.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
