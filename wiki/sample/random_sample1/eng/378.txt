<p>The <b>2006 United Nations Security Council election</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_A61PV32_2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_A61PV32_2006-1">[1]</a></sup> began on 16 October 2006 during the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sixty-first_session_of_the_United_Nations_General_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sixty-first session of the United Nations General Assembly (page does not exist)">61st session</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">General Assembly</a>, held at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Headquarters" title="United Nations Headquarters">United Nations Headquarters</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The elections were for five non-permanent seats on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">Security Council</a> for two-year mandates commencing on 1 January 2007.</p>
<p>In accordance with the Security Council's rotation rules, whereby the ten non-permanent Security Council seats rotate among the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Regional_Groups" title="United Nations Regional Groups">various regional blocs</a> into which UN member states traditionally divide themselves for voting and representation purposes, the five available seats were allocated as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>One for <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_geoscheme_for_Africa" title="United Nations geoscheme for Africa">Africa</a> (held by <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>)</li>
<li>One for <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_geoscheme_for_the_Americas" title="United Nations geoscheme for the Americas">Latin America and the Caribbean</a> (held by <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>)</li>
<li>One for <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_geoscheme_for_Asia" title="United Nations geoscheme for Asia">Asia</a> (held by <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>)</li>
<li>Two for the <a href="/wiki/Western_European_and_Others_Group" title="Western European and Others Group">Western European and Others Group</a> (held by <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>While the first three votes were uncontroversial and even the one contested seat was settled in the first round, the race for the Latin American and Caribbean seat went on for an almost unprecedented 48 rounds of voting over the space of three weeks. In five days of ballots, the General Assembly was unable to decide between <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, and the matter was only resolved when they both agreed to withdraw their candidacies and nominate <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> instead.</p>
<p>The final result of the election was that <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> were elected to serve on the Security Council for the 2007–08 period.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voting_mechanics">Voting mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Voting mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To win a seat, a candidate member must receive the favourable vote of two-thirds of the member states present and voting. Votes are taken by secret ballot, with each of the UN's 192 then member states allocated one vote, and a formal ballot is conducted even in those cases in which — generally due to pre-meeting negotiations among the members of the corresponding bloc — there is only one candidacy.</p>
<p>If a bloc proposes more than one candidate but, after several rounds of voting, the General Assembly is unable to decide between them, the vote is opened to all the bloc's other member states. Balloting then continues in groups of three successive "restricted" and "unrestricted" rounds of voting until a winner emerges.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="African_seat">African seat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: African seat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the only nation to contend for the African seat, was elected with 186 votes. The 2007–08 period is the first time that South Africa sits on the Security Council, occupying the seat then held by <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grabs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-grabs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_seat">Asian seat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Asian seat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were two contenders for the Asian seat: <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> received 158 votes to <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>'s 28. Indonesia replaced <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> on 1 January 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grabs_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-grabs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_European_and_Others_seats">Western European and Others seats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Western European and Others seats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Western_European_and_Others_Group" title="Western European and Others Group">Western European and Others Group</a> (WEOG), a bloc made up of 23 European nations as well as Turkey, Israel, Canada, the United States, Australia, and New Zealand, had two Council vacancies to fill in this election. It fielded two candidates — <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> — both of which were voted onto the Council, with 186 and 180 supporting votes, respectively. They replaced <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> at the end of 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grabs_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-grabs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_American_and_Caribbean_seat">Latin American and Caribbean seat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Latin American and Caribbean seat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Latin American and Caribbean Group (GRULAC) nominated two candidates — <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> — for its one available seat, then held by <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. After 47 rounds of deadlocked voting, both candidates withdrew their bids and supported the nomination of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> as a compromise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guatemala">Guatemala</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Guatemala">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Guatemala announced its candidacy in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.A9xico_frena_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.A9xico_frena-4">[4]</a></sup> It had the staunch support of the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_resumes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_resumes-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as that of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, the rest of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, and the remaining members of <a href="/wiki/Western_European_and_Others_Group" title="Western European and Others Group">WEOG</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_names_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_names-6">[6]</a></sup> The Central American nation <a href="/wiki/List_of_members_of_the_United_Nations_Security_Council#Non-members" title="List of members of the United Nations Security Council">has never sat on the Security Council</a>, and is one of only six of the original signatories of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">UN Charter</a> to be in that position (along with the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>). In recent years, following the conclusion of its 36-year-long <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Civil_War" title="Guatemalan Civil War">Civil War</a> and the succession of several democratically elected governments, it has been playing a more proactive role in multilateral affairs. For instance, after having been on the receiving end of UN assistance between 1994 and 2004 with the <a href="/wiki/MINUGUA" title="MINUGUA">MINUGUA</a> verification mission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> it is now a provider of troops for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace-keeping" title="Peace-keeping">peace-keeping</a> missions (215 worldwide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> most in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Mission_in_the_Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" title="United Nations Mission in the Democratic Republic of Congo">MONUC</a>), with Guatemalan soldiers deployed in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">DR Congo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire" title="Côte d'Ivoire">Côte d'Ivoire</a>, and other global hot spots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venezuela">Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Venezuela, under <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Venezuela" title="List of Presidents of Venezuela">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>, announced its plans to seek a UNSC seat in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.A9xico_frena_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.A9xico_frena-4">[4]</a></sup> It presented itself as an alternative to what it considered <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> global <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> and, in the words of foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Maduro" title="Nicolás Maduro">Nicolás Maduro</a>, an "end to the unipolar world that has been so damaging".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Venezuela conducted an extensive global campaign in search of support for its candidacy in the months prior to the vote, particularly in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, where it offered generous packages of oil-funded aid; it claimed to have had the backing of <a href="/wiki/Mercosur" title="Mercosur">Mercosur</a> and of significant portions of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_League" title="Arab League">Arab League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Community" title="Caribbean Community">Caribbean Community</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_names_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-New_names-6">[6]</a></sup> Venezuela has occupied a seat on the UNSC on four previous occasions: 1962–63, 1977–78, 1986–87 and 1992–93.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_support">Caribbean support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Caribbean support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Caribbean bloc of countries, also known as <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Community" title="Caribbean Community">CARICOM</a>, accounts for 14 votes in the GRULAC. Most of these states declared their backing for Venezuela at a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/CARICOM_Heads_of_Government" title="CARICOM Heads of Government">Caribbean Community Heads of Government</a> meeting held earlier in 2006.</p>
<p>The still unresolved border dispute between Guatemala and CARICOM-member <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> as well as a legal challenge brought by Guatemala before the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> (WTO) over the Caribbean nations' trade deal with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> at the WTO, were reported as having soured the majority of Caribbean states against any backing of a Guatemalan candidacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Belize" title="List of Prime Ministers of Belize">Belizean Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Said_Musa" title="Said Musa">Said Musa</a> brought up the UNSC contest during the heads of government meeting and urged the other heads to support anyone but Guatemala. Prior to its recognition of Belize's sovereignty in 1991, Guatemala claimed its neighbour's territory in its entirety, and ownership of some tracts of land along the countries' jungle border is still disputed. Fear that Guatemala would use its UNSC seat to unfair advantage in this dispute made Venezuela a more attractive choice for the Caribbean states.</p>
<p>Venezuela also has a territorial dispute with CARICOM member-state <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> but, in order to secure CARICOM's backing, Venezuelan <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Venezuela" title="List of Presidents of Venezuela">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a> stated publicly that his country would not use their role on the Security Council to further their territorial claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A further element in counteracting U.S. lobbying of the CARICOM states on behalf of Guatemala was Chávez's extensive petrodollar-funded programme of development aid in the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>Although the results were generally more favourable to Guatemala (which received more votes in every round except the tied sixth ballot), the General Assembly was unable to produce a two-thirds majority for either candidate after repeated rounds of voting. When all the then 192 members vote, a two-thirds majority corresponds to 128 supporting votes.</p>

<p>Following the second day of inconclusive balloting, the vote was postponed until the following Thursday, 19 October 2006. Given the high number of abstentions in the later rounds of Tuesday's voting, the BBC inferred that delegations may have been trying to send a signal that it is time to seek an alternative candidate; it mentioned the names of <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_resumes_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_resumes-5">[5]</a></sup> A private informal meeting of the Latin American and Caribbean Group was held on 18 October, but it failed to break the deadlock. After the meeting, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> ambassador <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Berruga" title="Enrique Berruga">Enrique Berruga</a> publicly called on Venezuela to withdraw on grounds of "diplomatic courtesy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.A9xico_frena_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.A9xico_frena-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Voting recommenced on 19 October. At the start of the session, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> proposed deferring the vote until Monday, 23 October, because of the delays being suffered by other pending business, but this suggestion was rejected by the Assembly.</p>

<p>Faced with continued deadlock, the General Assembly agreed to postpone further voting until Wednesday, 25 October, to enable the meeting to make progress with its delayed agenda on Friday and Monday, to allow the Latin American and Caribbean states to conduct negotiations towards resolving the stalemate, and to observe the scheduled one-day recess on Tuesday to mark the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eid_ul-Fitr" title="Eid ul-Fitr">Eid ul-Fitr</a> feast day at the end of <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Day_four">Day four</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Day four">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The evening before voting was scheduled to recommence, <a href="/wiki/President_of_Bolivia" title="President of Bolivia">Bolivian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Evo_Morales" title="Evo Morales">Evo Morales</a> announced at a rally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Alto,_Bolivia" title="El Alto, Bolivia">El Alto</a> that <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a> had spoken to him earlier that day and that, since it had been unable to secure a two-thirds majority, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> would withdraw in favour of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.C2.BFBolivia.3F_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-.C2.BFBolivia.3F-21">[21]</a></sup> No confirmation was forthcoming from <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">Caracas</a>, however, and it remained unclear whether Morales meant that Bolivia would be replacing Venezuela immediately or after another round of failed voting. The same afternoon, a spokesman for the Venezuelan foreign ministry cited three conditions which would have to be met for it to withdraw:</p>
<ul>
<li>Guatemala would also have to withdraw.</li>
<li>The United States would have to cease its campaign of "pressure and crude blackmail" on the world's governments.</li>
<li>Transparent negotiations would have to be held among the Latin American and Caribbean Group members to identify the best option for proceeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.C2.BFBolivia.3F_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-.C2.BFBolivia.3F-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Bolivia under Evo Morales is a close ally of Chávez's Venezuela (Chávez has spoken of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_good" title="Axis of good">axis of good</a> comprising the two nations, along with <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>), and it is likely that Bolivia's candidacy would have been just as unpalatable as Venezuela's had been to those countries opposing it. As events unfolded, nothing further was heard about this putative Venezuelan withdrawal in favour of Bolivia.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Day_six">Day six</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Day six">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Voting was scheduled to recommence in the afternoon of 1 November, but a joint communiqué released by the foreign ministers of Guatemala and Venezuela shortly after noon requested that the day's balloting be suspended. They then announced that after negotiations chaired by Diego Cordovez, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>'s representative to the UN and chair of the Latin American and Caribbean Group, the two had agreed to withdraw their candidacies and propose <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> as a consensus candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Day_seven">Day seven</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Day seven">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The GRULAC states unanimously endorsed Panama at a meeting on 3 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Panama has served four previous terms as an elected member of the Security Council — in 1958–59, 1972–73, 1976–77 and 1981–82 — and the country's endorsement by the General Assembly on Tuesday, 7 November, was considered a formality.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_precedents">Historical precedents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Security_Council_election,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Historical precedents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1979, during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, a similar contest between <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> ran to 155 polls over a period of three months. The stalemate was finally resolved with the withdrawal of both and the election of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> as a compromise candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the 36th round of voting, the 2006 contest became the third longest non-permanent seat vote in UN history, passing the race for the 1956–1957 period between <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. The second longest is the 52-round election between <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> for 1960–1961. The Poland–Turkey stand-off was resolved when the contenders agreed to serve one year each; in the 1956–1957 case, it appears Yugoslavia was ultimately victorious in the vote but stood down after one year to enable the election of the Philippines to complete its term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
