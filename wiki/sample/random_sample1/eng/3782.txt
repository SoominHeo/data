<p><b>David Koepp</b> (born June 9, 1963) is an American screenwriter and director. Koepp is the fifth most successful screenwriter of all time in terms of U.S. box office receipts with a total gross of over $2.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Koepp has achieved both critical and commercial success in a wide variety of genres: <a href="/wiki/Thriller_film" title="Thriller film">thriller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_(genre)" title="Drama (genre)">drama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crime_film" title="Crime film">crime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy</a>.</p>
<p>Some of the best known films he has written include the sci-fi adventure films <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> (1993), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></i> (1997), and <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Kingdom_of_the_Crystal_Skull" title="Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull">Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull</a></i> (2008); the action spy films <i><a href="/wiki/Mission:_Impossible_(film)" title="Mission: Impossible (film)">Mission: Impossible</a></i> (1996) and <i><a href="/wiki/Jack_Ryan:_Shadow_Recruit" title="Jack Ryan: Shadow Recruit">Jack Ryan: Shadow Recruit</a></i> (2014); the superhero film <i><a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_(2002_film)" title="Spider-Man (2002 film)">Spider-Man</a></i> (2002); the sci-fi disaster film <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Worlds_(2005_film)" title="War of the Worlds (2005 film)">War of the Worlds</a></i> (2005) and the mystery thriller <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons_(film)" title="Angels &amp; Demons (film)">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i> (2009). Koepp has directed six films over the course of his career: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trigger_Effect" title="The Trigger Effect">The Trigger Effect</a></i> (1996), <i><a href="/wiki/Stir_of_Echoes" title="Stir of Echoes">Stir of Echoes</a></i> (1999), <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Window" title="Secret Window">Secret Window</a></i> (2004), <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Town_(2008_film)" title="Ghost Town (2008 film)">Ghost Town</a></i> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/Premium_Rush" title="Premium Rush">Premium Rush</a></i> (2012), and <i><a href="/wiki/Mortdecai_(film)" title="Mortdecai (film)">Mortdecai</a></i> (2015).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koepp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a writer, Koepp has worked on blockbuster <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mission:_Impossible_(film)" title="Mission: Impossible (film)">Mission: Impossible</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_(2002_film)" title="Spider-Man (2002 film)">Spider-Man</a></i>. He appeared briefly as "Unlucky Bastard", the man mauled by a T. rex in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></i>, on which he worked as a writer and second unit director. Although Koepp did not write <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_III" title="Jurassic Park III">Jurassic Park III</a></i>, he did devise the film's basic storyline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Koepp later declined an offer to write a script for the series' fourth film, <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_World" title="Jurassic World">Jurassic World</a></i>, as he felt he had nothing left to contribute to the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Koepp was reportedly paid $4,000,000 for his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panic_Room_(film)" title="Panic Room (film)">Panic Room</a></i> screenplay. He wrote the screenplay for <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Kingdom_of_the_Crystal_Skull" title="Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull">Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull</a></i> and co-wrote and directed 2008's <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Town_(2008_film)" title="Ghost Town (2008 film)">Ghost Town</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Gervais" title="Ricky Gervais">Ricky Gervais</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greg_Kinnear" title="Greg Kinnear">Greg Kinnear</a>.</p>
<p>Koepp's work as a director has not had quite the same box office success. His films include <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Window" title="Secret Window">Secret Window</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stir_of_Echoes" title="Stir of Echoes">Stir of Echoes</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trigger_Effect" title="The Trigger Effect">The Trigger Effect</a></i>.</p>
<p>Koepp has also worked in television, creating the 2002 series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hack_(American_TV_series)" title="Hack (American TV series)">Hack</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/David_Morse_(actor)" title="David Morse (actor)">David Morse</a>.</p>
<p>In 2012, Koepp directed <i><a href="/wiki/Premium_Rush" title="Premium Rush">Premium Rush</a></i>, which he co-wrote with John Kamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In an August 2011 lawsuit, Joe Quirk, the author of the 1998 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ultimate_Rush" title="The Ultimate Rush">The Ultimate Rush</a></i>, accused Koepp and the makers of <i>Premium Rush</i> of copyright infringement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On April 2, 2013, U.S. District Judge Richard Seeborg dismissed this case, finding that the two works were not substantially similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 17, 2013, Koepp received the WGA East's Ian McClellan Hunter Award for Career Achievement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 10, 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lionsgate_Entertainment" title="Lionsgate Entertainment">Lionsgate</a> was reported to have acquired the comedic crime novel <i>The Great Mortdecai Moustache Mystery</i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Kyril_Bonfiglioli" title="Kyril Bonfiglioli">Kyril Bonfiglioli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Koepp directed the film, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Mortdecai_(film)" title="Mortdecai (film)">Mortdecai</a></i>, from a script by Eric Aronson;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Depp" title="Johnny Depp">Johnny Depp</a> played the lead role of Charlie Mortdecai,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the film also featured <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Paltrow" title="Gwyneth Paltrow">Gwyneth Paltrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ewan_McGregor" title="Ewan McGregor">Ewan McGregor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bettany" title="Paul Bettany">Paul Bettany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Koepp adapted the <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Sakey" title="Marcus Sakey">Marcus Sakey</a> novel <i>Brilliance</i>, which will star <a href="/wiki/Will_Smith" title="Will Smith">Will Smith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noomi_Rapace" title="Noomi Rapace">Noomi Rapace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 15, 2016, <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">the Walt Disney Company</a> announced a fifth installment of the <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones" title="Indiana Jones">Indiana Jones</a></i> saga, with Koepp as its screenwriter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koepp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Koepp was born in <a href="/wiki/Pewaukee,_Wisconsin" title="Pewaukee, Wisconsin">Pewaukee, Wisconsin</a>, to a family therapist mother, and Donald Koepp, who owns a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_(advertising)" title="Billboard (advertising)">billboard</a> company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> While attending <a href="/wiki/Kettle_Moraine_High_School" title="Kettle Moraine High School">Kettle Moraine High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales,_Wisconsin" title="Wales, Wisconsin">Wales, Wisconsin</a>, he worked evenings and weekends at the McDonald's restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Delafield,_Wisconsin" title="Delafield, Wisconsin">Delafield</a>. He received his bachelor's in film from <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">UCLA</a>. He has a wife, Melissa, and four children.</p>
