<p><b>David Koresh</b> (born <b>Vernon Wayne Howell</b>; August 17, 1959 – April 19, 1993) was the American leader of the <a href="/wiki/Branch_Davidians" title="Branch Davidians">Branch Davidians</a> <a href="/wiki/Sect" title="Sect">religious sect</a>, believing himself to be its final <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a>.</p>
<p>Coming from a dysfunctional family background, Koresh claimed to be a Christian in the <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a>, before joining a splinter group, based at the Mount Carmel Center near <a href="/wiki/Waco,_Texas" title="Waco, Texas">Waco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, which took the name Branch Davidians. Here he competed for dominance with another leader named George Roden, until Roden was jailed for murdering another rival. Koresh was then accused of seducing a 12-year-old girl, apparently with her parents’ consent, a relationship that he sanctified as a ‘spiritual marriage’.</p>
<p>The serving of arrest and search warrants by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms</a> (ATF) as part of an investigation into illegal possession of firearms and explosives provoked the historic <a href="/wiki/Waco_siege#The_initial_assault" title="Waco siege">1993 raid</a> on the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frontline.2FPBS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frontline.2FPBS-2">[2]</a></sup> Four ATF agents and six Davidians were killed during the initial two-hour firefight, both sides claiming the other side fired first. The subsequent siege by the FBI ended with the burning of the center, where Koresh and 79 others were found dead after the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koresh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Koresh was born Vernon Wayne Howell on August 17, 1959 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>, to a 14-year-old single mother, Bonnie Sue Clark (September 8, 1944 – January 23, 2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-4">[4]</a></sup> His father was Bobby Wayne Howell (August 3, 1939 – July 16, 2008). Before Koresh was born, his father met another teenage girl and abandoned Bonnie Sue. Koresh never met his father, and his mother began <a href="/wiki/Cohabitation" title="Cohabitation">cohabitating</a> with a violent alcoholic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, Koresh's mother left her boyfriend and placed her 4-year-old son in the care of his maternal grandmother, Earline Clark. His mother returned when he was seven, after her marriage to a carpenter named Roy Haldeman. Haldeman and Clark had a son together named Roger, who was born in 1966. Koresh described his early childhood as lonely, and it has been alleged that he was once gang-raped by older boys when he was 8 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-4">[4]</a></sup> Due to his poor study skills and dyslexia, he was put in <a href="/wiki/Special_education" title="Special education">special education</a> classes and nicknamed "Vernie" by his fellow students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Koresh dropped out of <a href="/wiki/Garland_High_School" title="Garland High School">Garland High School</a> in his junior year.</p>
<p>When he was 22, Koresh had an affair with a 15-year-old girl who became pregnant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (May 2012)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> He claimed to have become a born-again Christian in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Baptist_Church" title="Southern Baptist Church">Southern Baptist Church</a> and soon joined his mother's church, the <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a>. There he fell in love with the pastor's daughter and while praying for guidance he opened his eyes and allegedly found the Bible open at <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">Isaiah 34:16</a>, stating that "...none should want for her mate..."; convinced this was a sign from God, he approached the pastor and told him that God wanted him to have his daughter for a wife. The pastor threw him out, and when he continued to persist with his pursuit of the daughter he was expelled from the congregation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1982, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Waco,_Texas" title="Waco, Texas">Waco, Texas</a>, where he joined the <a href="/wiki/Branch_Davidians" title="Branch Davidians">Branch Davidians</a>, not to be confused with the original <a href="/wiki/Davidian_Seventh-day_Adventist" title="Davidian Seventh-day Adventist">Davidian Seventh-day Adventist</a> group. A man named Ben Roden originated the Branch group. Roden had studied under Victor Houteff but upon the death of Houteff in 1955, he formed his own group with new teachings that were not connected with the original Davidians. Koresh played guitar and sang in church services at Mount Carmel Center. His band played a few times at clubs in Waco, and former members (such as David Thibodeau) have written that he recruited them through music<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (December 2015)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Koresh also tried pursuing his own record company but because of lack of funds and support was not successful.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascent_to_leadership_of_the_Branch_Davidians">Ascent to leadership of the Branch Davidians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koresh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ascent to leadership of the Branch Davidians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1983, Koresh began claiming the gift of <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">prophecy</a>. It is speculated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2012)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that he had a sexual relationship with <a href="/wiki/Lois_Roden" title="Lois Roden">Lois Roden</a>, the prophetess and leader of the sect, who was then 65 years old, eventually claiming that God had chosen him to father a child with her, who would be the Chosen One.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1983, Lois Roden allowed Koresh to begin teaching his own message, called "The Serpent's Root," which caused controversy in the group. Lois Roden's son <a href="/wiki/George_Roden" title="George Roden">George Roden</a> intended to be the group's next leader and considered Koresh an interloper. When Koresh announced that God had instructed him to marry Rachel Jones (who then added Koresh to her name), there was a short period of calm at Mount Carmel Center, but it proved only temporary. In the ensuing power struggle, George Roden, claiming to have the support of the majority of the group, forced Koresh and his group off the property at gunpoint.</p>
<p>Disturbed by the events and the move away from the philosophy of the community's founders, a further splinter group led by Charles Joseph Pace, a Branch Davidian since 1973, moved out of Mount Carmel Center and set up a home church called Living Waters Branch of Righteousness in <a href="/wiki/Gadsden,_Alabama" title="Gadsden, Alabama">Gadsden, Alabama</a>.</p>
<p>In 1985, Koresh and around 25 followers set up camp at <a href="/wiki/Palestine,_Texas" title="Palestine, Texas">Palestine, Texas</a>, 90 miles (140 km) from Waco, where they lived under rough conditions in buses and tents for the next two years, during which time Koresh undertook recruitment of new followers in California, the United Kingdom, Israel and Australia. That same year Koresh traveled to Israel where he claimed he had a vision that he was the modern day <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus</a>. The founder of the Davidian movement, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Houteff" title="Victor Houteff">Victor Houteff</a>, wanted to be God's implement and establish the Davidic kingdom in Palestine. Koresh also wanted to be God's tool and set up the Davidic kingdom in Jerusalem. At least until 1990, he believed the place of his martyrdom might be in Israel, but by 1991 he was convinced that his martyrdom would be in the United States. Instead of Israel, he said the prophecies of Daniel would be fulfilled in Waco and that the Mount Carmel Center was the Davidic kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valentine_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-valentine-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After being exiled to the Palestine camp, Koresh and his followers eked out a primitive existence. When Lois Roden died in 1986, the exiled Branch Davidians wondered if they would ever be able to return to Mount Carmel Center. But despite the displacement, "Koresh now enjoyed the loyalty of the majority of the [Branch Davidian] community".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> By late 1987, George Roden's support was in steep decline. To regain it, he challenged Koresh to a contest to raise the dead, going so far as to exhume a corpse to demonstrate his spiritual supremacy. Koresh went to authorities to file charges against Roden for illegally exhuming a corpse, but was told he would have to show proof (such as a photograph of the corpse). Koresh seized the opportunity to seek criminal prosecution of Roden by returning to Mount Carmel Center with seven armed followers attempting to get photographic proof of the crime. Koresh's group was discovered by Roden and a gunfight broke out. When the sheriff arrived, Roden had already suffered a minor gunshot wound and was pinned down behind a tree. As a result of the incident, Koresh and his followers were charged with attempted murder. At the trial, Koresh explained that he went to Mount Carmel Center to uncover evidence of criminal disturbance of a corpse by Roden. Koresh's followers were acquitted, and in Koresh's case a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" title="Trial (law)">mistrial</a> was declared.</p>
<p>In 1989, Roden murdered Wayman Dale Adair with an axe blow to the skull after Adair stated his belief that he (Adair) was the true <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">messiah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Roden was convicted of murder and imprisoned in a mental hospital at Big Spring, Texas. Since Roden owed thousands of dollars in unpaid taxes on Mount Carmel Center, Koresh and his followers were able to raise the money and reclaim the property. Roden continued to harass the Koresh faction by filing legal papers while imprisoned. When Koresh and his followers reclaimed Mount Carmel Center, they discovered that tenants who had rented from Roden had left behind a <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">methamphetamine</a> laboratory, which Koresh reported to the local police department and asked to have removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thibodeau_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-thibodeau-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lujan_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-lujan-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_change">Name change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koresh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vernon Howell filed a petition in California State Superior Court in Pomona on May 15, 1990, to legally change his name "for publicity and business purposes" to David Koresh. On August 28, 1990, Judge Robert Martinez granted the petition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Koresh is the Persian name of <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a> (کوروش, Kurosh), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persian king</a> who is named a <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> for freeing Jews during the Babylonian Captivity. His first name, David, symbolized a lineage directly to the biblical King David, from whom the new messiah would descend. By taking the name of David Koresh, he was "professing himself to be the spiritual descendant of King David, a messianic figure carrying out a divinely commissioned errand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accusations_of_child_abuse_and_statutory_rape">Accusations of child abuse and statutory rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koresh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accusations of child abuse and statutory rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The child abuse and sexual abuse claims have been widely circulated in the press coverage though it is often difficult to separate the purported claims from the evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Koresh's doctrine of the House of David<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> did lead to "marriages" with both married and single women in the group and with at least one underage girl. The underage girl was Michelle Jones, the younger sister of Koresh's legal wife Rachel and the daughter of lifelong Branch Davidians Perry and Mary Belle Jones. Koresh had sex with Michelle when she was thirteen, evidently with the consent of the Joneses. This means Koresh was in violation of state law and could have been prosecuted for statutory rape in Texas. A six-month investigation of child abuse allegations by the Texas Child Protection Services in 1992 failed to turn up any evidence, most likely because the Branch Davidians concealed the spiritual marriage of Koresh to Michelle Jones, assigning a surrogate husband (David Thibodeau) to the girl for the sake of appearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A second allegation involved an underage girl, Kiri Jewell, who testified in the Congressional hearings in Waco in 1995. She claimed that, beginning from when she was 10 years old, Koresh forced her to perform sexual acts.</p>
<p>Regarding the allegations of child abuse, the evidence is less sure. In one widely reported incident, ex-members claimed that Koresh became irritated with the cries of his son Cyrus and spanked the child severely for several minutes on three consecutive visits to the child's bedroom. In a second report, Koresh was said to have beaten the eight-month-old daughter of another member for approximately 40 minutes until the girl's bottom bled. In a third incident, a man involved in a custody battle visited Mount Carmel Center and claimed to have seen the beating of a young boy with a stick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Finally, the FBI's justification for forcing an end to the 51-day standoff was predicated on the charge that Koresh was abusing children inside Mount Carmel Center. In hours following the deadly conflagration, Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Janet_Reno" title="Janet Reno">Janet Reno</a> told reporters that "We had specific information that babies were being beaten."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> But FBI Director William Sessions publicly denied the charge and told reporters that they had no such information about child abuse inside Mount Carmel Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A careful examination of the other child abuse charges found the evidence to be weak and ambiguous, casting doubt on the allegations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The allegations of child abuse stem largely from detractors and ex-members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The 1993 U.S. Department of Justice report cites allegations of child <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">sexual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_abuse" title="Physical abuse">physical</a> abuse. Legal scholars point out that the ATF had no legal jurisdiction in the matter of child protection and it appears that these accounts were inserted by the ATF to inflame the case against Koresh. For example, the account of former Branch Davidian Jeannine Bunds is reproduced in the affidavit. She claimed that Koresh had fathered at least 15 children with various women and that she had personally delivered seven of these children. Bunds also claims that Koresh would annul all marriages of couples who joined the group, had exclusive sexual access to the women, and would also have regular sexual relations with young girls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpmt_justice_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-dpmt_justice-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> There is no question that Koresh had multiple children by different women in the group. His House of David doctrine based on a purported revelation involved the reproduction of 24 children by chosen women in the community. These 24 children were to serve as the ruling elders over the millennium after the return of Christ. In his book, James Tabor states that Koresh acknowledged on a videotape sent out of the compound during the standoff that he had fathered more than 12 children by several "wives".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tabor_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-tabor-23">[23]</a></sup> On March 3, 1993, during negotiations to secure the release of the remaining children, Koresh advised the Negotiation Team that: "My children are different than those others", referring to his direct lineage versus those children previously released.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Raid_and_siege_by_federal_authorities">Raid and siege by federal authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koresh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Raid and siege by federal authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Waco_siege" title="Waco siege">Waco siege</a></div>
<p>On February 28, 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms</a> (ATF) raided Mount Carmel Center. The raid resulted in the deaths of four agents and six Branch Davidians. Shortly after the initial raid, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> HRT (<a href="/wiki/Hostage_Rescue_Team" title="Hostage Rescue Team">Hostage Rescue Team</a>) took command of the federal operation, since the FBI has jurisdiction over incidents involving the deaths of federal agents. Contact was established with Koresh inside the compound. Communication over the next 51 days included telephone exchanges with various FBI negotiators.</p>
<p>As the standoff continued, Koresh, who was seriously injured by a gunshot wound, along with his closest male leaders, negotiated delays, possibly so he could write religious documents he said he needed to complete before he surrendered. His conversations with the negotiators were dense with biblical imagery. The federal negotiators treated the situation as a hostage crisis.</p>
<p>The 51-day siege of Mount Carmel Center ended on April 19 when U.S. Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Janet_Reno" title="Janet Reno">Janet Reno</a> approved recommendations of FBI officials to proceed with a final assault in which the Branch Davidians were to be removed from their building by force. In the course of the assault, the church building caught on fire in circumstances that are still disputed. Barricaded inside the building, 80 Branch Davidians, including David Koresh, did not survive the fire; 22 of these victims were children under the age of 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frontline.2FPBS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frontline.2FPBS-2">[2]</a></sup> According to the FBI, Steve Schneider, Koresh's right-hand man who "probably realized he was dealing with a fraud," shot and killed Koresh and then committed suicide with the same gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The FBI played Nancy Sinatra's "These Boots Are Made for Walkin'" in an effort to flush Koresh out of the stronghold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Koresh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Koresh is buried at Memorial Park Cemetery, <a href="/wiki/Tyler,_Texas" title="Tyler, Texas">Tyler, Texas</a> in the "Last Supper" plot/section. Several of David Koresh's albums were released, including <i>Voice Of Fire</i> in 1994. In 2004, Koresh's 1968 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Camaro" title="Chevrolet Camaro">Camaro</a>, which had been damaged during the raid, sold for $37,000 at auction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Mount Carmel Center raid was cited by <a href="/wiki/Timothy_McVeigh" title="Timothy McVeigh">Timothy McVeigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Nichols" title="Terry Nichols">Terry Nichols</a> as motivations for the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_bombing" title="Oklahoma City bombing">Oklahoma City bombing</a> of April 19, 1995—timed to coincide with the second anniversary of the Waco assault.</p>
<p>On January 23, 2009, Koresh's mother, Bonnie Clark Haldeman, was stabbed to death in <a href="/wiki/Chandler,_Texas" title="Chandler, Texas">Chandler, Texas</a>; her sister, Beverly Clark, was charged with the murder.</p>
<p>Several documentary films have been made about the siege, including different versions of <i><a href="/wiki/Waco:_The_Rules_of_Engagement" title="Waco: The Rules of Engagement">Waco: The Rules of Engagement</a></i> , <i><a href="/wiki/Waco:_A_New_Revelation" title="Waco: A New Revelation">Waco: A New Revelation</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waco:_The_Big_Lie" title="Waco: The Big Lie">Waco: The Big Lie</a></i>.</p>
