<p>The <b>robust cottontail</b> or <b>Davis Mountains cottontail</b> (<i>Sylvilagus robustus</i>) is a species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cottontail" title="Cottontail">cottontail</a> rabbit endemic to four mountain ranges in the southwestern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and adjacent <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-2">[2]</a></sup> It was long considered to be a subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_cottontail" title="Eastern cottontail">eastern cottontail</a> (<i>S. floridanus</i>), but has recently been promoted to species level due to morphological analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Genetic data have confirmed the uniqueness of <i>S. robustus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>S. robustus</i> and the subspecies of <i>S. floridanus</i> are distinguished primarily by size, dental, and cranial differences. These rabbits typically average a total length of 42 cm, and weigh between 1.3 and 1.8 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOT-5">[5]</a></sup> This species is restricted to dry, brushy, mountains at elevations above 1500 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOT_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-MOT-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite its rarity, currently no governmental agency provides protection or listing for this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robust_cottontail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, this species was known to be endemic to four mountain ranges. In <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> it was known in the <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_Mountains" title="Guadalupe Mountains">Guadalupe Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davis_Mountains" title="Davis Mountains">Davis Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chisos_Mountains" title="Chisos Mountains">Chisos Mountains</a>. In <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> it was known from the Guadalupe Mountains. And in <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a>, Mexico it is known from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_de_la_Madera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sierra de la Madera (page does not exist)">Sierra de la Madera</a>. The Chisos and Guadalupe Mountains populations are believed to be <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
