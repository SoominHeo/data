<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">2006 Queens blackout</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>2006 Queens blackout</b> was an unresolved series of <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">power outages</a> that affected the northwest section of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a> in July 2006. The blackout primarily affected the neighborhoods of <a href="/wiki/Astoria,_Queens" title="Astoria, Queens">Astoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_City" title="Long Island City">Long Island City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunnyside,_Queens" title="Sunnyside, Queens">Sunnyside</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Woodside,_Queens" title="Woodside, Queens">Woodside</a>. The outages affected 174,000 people, caused business losses of tens of millions of dollars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> caused airport and transit delays and cancellations, and caused unsavory living conditions due to a concurrent <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat wave</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Queens_blackout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northwest Queens power outages coincided with a heat wave in the New York City area the week of July 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Their effects included knocking out power at <a href="/wiki/LaGuardia_Airport" title="LaGuardia Airport">LaGuardia Airport</a> and parts of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">subway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The outage also caused <a href="/wiki/Rikers_Island" title="Rikers Island">Rikers Island</a> to switch to backup generators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The worst result of the outages was a prolonged loss of power to 100,000 northwest Queens residents beginning on July 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This outage was originally estimated by Con Edison to have affected only 1,600 customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insights_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-insights-6">[6]</a></sup> Con Edison defines a customer as a single edifice such that an entire residential building (which could conceivably be home to hundreds of individuals) is counted as one customer. Con Edison later revised its estimates tenfold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insights_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-insights-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Queens_blackout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cause of the outages—which was undetermined for five days—appeared to be the company's decision to continue supplying power to the 400,000 people serviced by twenty-two feeder cables after ten of them had failed, overloading the remaining twelve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-7">[7]</a></sup> After these were repaired, a <a href="/wiki/Manhole" title="Manhole">manhole</a>-to-manhole inspection and repair of smaller cables which had also burned took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Edison" title="Consolidated Edison">Consolidated Edison</a> was due to make an initial status report, regarding the outage, on August 2, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Data submitted by Con Edison in August, 2006 indicated that the failed feeder cables had been in service an average of 16 years, with the oldest failed cable 59 years old.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration_of_power">Restoration of power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Queens_blackout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Restoration of power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Con Edison first believed it could restore power by the end of the day July 23, but their CEO Kevin Burke later stated he could not estimate how long it would take to restore full service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> By July 24 (one week after the incident), about half of the customers affected had their service restored. The other half were still in the dark by July 21, 2006 and New York City government planned to provide food and shelter until they were all restored. There have been reports of offers of reimbursement for spoiled food of up to $300 per customer, however officials from Con Edison’s claims department told residents they could submit a claim of up to $150 without receipts, or $350 with receipts, and merchants might file claims of up to $7,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Also, in a separate press release, the power company stated that the requirement for grocery receipts would be waived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_blackout">After the blackout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Queens_blackout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the blackout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The extent of the outage and perceived poor response on their behalf prompted criticism to be levied on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Con_Edison" title="Con Edison">Con Edison</a> and mayor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a press conference on Wednesday July 19, 2006 Mayor Bloomberg snapped at reporters for asking about the power outage in Astoria. The Queens power problem had been a constant item on <a href="/wiki/NY1" title="NY1">NY1</a>'s 9PM show <i>The Call</i> hosted by John Schiumo for the entire week of Monday July 17, 2006 to Friday July 21. He received calls from frustrated Astoria residents who managed to relocate with friends or relatives to be able to see the show and call in. In addition, there were calls from MTA officials explaining how the power problem affected the subways that week (July 17–21). By Friday, when the full extent of the problem was revealed, Bloomberg changed his stance.</p>
<p>In response to perceived inadequacies of Con Edison's response, on Monday July 24, Councilmember <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gioia" title="Eric Gioia">Eric Gioia</a> has called for the ousting of the CEO, Kevin Burke, on <a href="/wiki/WNYC" title="WNYC">WNYC</a>'s radio show <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Brian_Lehrer_Show" title="The Brian Lehrer Show">The Brian Lehrer Show</a></i>.</p>
<p>Hearings were held in August 2006 by the New York Public Service Commission. James Gallagher, Director of the Office of Electricity and Environment of the New York Department of Public Service, testified that the blackout represented a "significant public health and safety risk" and a "significant economic loss."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
