<p><b>Osamu Tezuka</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">手塚 治虫, born 手塚 治</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tezuka Osamu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, <span style="display:none">(<span class="bday">1928-11-03</span>)</span>3 November 1928 – 9 February 1989)</span> was a Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manga_artist" title="Manga artist">manga artist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">cartoonist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">animator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">film producer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_doctor" title="Medical doctor">medical doctor</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activist" title="Activist">activist</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Prefecture" title="Osaka Prefecture">Osaka Prefecture</a>, he is best known as the creator of the manga series <i><a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy" title="Astro Boy">Astro Boy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kimba_the_White_Lion" title="Kimba the White Lion">Kimba the White Lion</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Jack_(manga)" title="Black Jack (manga)">Black Jack</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(manga)" title="Phoenix (manga)">Phoenix</a></i>. His prolific output, pioneering techniques, and innovative redefinitions of genres earned him such titles as "the father of <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>", "the godfather of manga" and "the god of manga". Additionally, he is often considered the Japanese equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a>, who served as a major inspiration during Tezuka's formative years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tezuka was the eldest of three children in <a href="/wiki/Toyonaka,_Osaka" title="Toyonaka, Osaka">Toyonaka City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Prefecture" title="Osaka Prefecture">Osaka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPatten2004145_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPatten2004145-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otaku_Encyclopedia_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Otaku_Encyclopedia-3">[3]</a></sup> His nickname was <i>gashagasha-atama</i> (<i>gashagasha</i> is slang for messy, <i>atama</i> means head). His mother often comforted him by telling him to look to the blue skies, giving him confidence. His mother's stories inspired his creativity as well. Tezuka grew up in <a href="/wiki/Takarazuka,_Hy%C5%8Dgo" title="Takarazuka, Hyōgo">Takarazuka City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hy%C5%8Dgo_Prefecture" title="Hyōgo Prefecture">Hyōgo</a> and his mother often took him to the Takarazuka Theatre. The <a href="/wiki/Takarazuka_Revue" title="Takarazuka Revue">Takarazuka Revue</a> is performed by women, including the male characters. The Takarazuka Revue is known for its romantic musicals usually aimed at a female audience, thus having a large impact on the later works of Tezuka, including his costuming designs. He has said that he has a profound "spirit of nostalgia" for Takarazuka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gravett_p.77_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett_p.77-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tezuka started to draw comics around his second year of elementary school. Around his fifth year he found a bug named "<a href="/wiki/Ground_beetle" title="Ground beetle">Osamushi</a>". It so resembled his name that he adopted osamushi as his pen name. He came to the realization that he could use manga as a means of helping to convince people to care for the world. After World War II, he created his first piece of work (at age 17), <i>Diary of Ma-chan</i> and then <i><a href="/wiki/Shin_Takarajima" title="Shin Takarajima">Shin Takarajima</a></i> <i>(New Treasure Island)</i>, which began the golden age of manga, a craze comparable to <a href="/wiki/American_comic_book" title="American comic book">American comic books</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tezuka is a descendent of <a href="/wiki/Hattori_Hanz%C5%8D" title="Hattori Hanzō">Hattori Hanzō</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a famous ninja and samurai who faithfully served Tokugawa Ieyasu during the Sengoku period in Japan. His son <a href="/wiki/Makoto_Tezuka" title="Makoto Tezuka">Makoto Tezuka</a> became a film and anime director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osamu_Tezuka_Biography_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osamu_Tezuka_Biography-7">[7]</a></sup> Tezuka guided many well-known manga artists such as <a href="/wiki/Shotaro_Ishinomori" title="Shotaro Ishinomori">Shotaro Ishinomori</a> and <a href="/wiki/Go_Nagai" title="Go Nagai">Go Nagai</a>.</p>
<p>Tezuka enjoyed bug-collecting, entomology, Walt Disney, baseball, and licensed the "grown up" version of his character Kimba the White Lion as the logo for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seibu_Lions" title="Seibu Lions">Seibu Lions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Professional_Baseball" title="Nippon Professional Baseball">Nippon Professional Baseball League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osamu_Tezuka_Biography_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osamu_Tezuka_Biography-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Tezuka met Walt Disney in person at the 1964 New York World's Fair. In a 1986 entry in his personal diary, Tezuka stated that Disney wanted to hire him for a potential science fiction project. Tezuka was a fan of <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a> and was made honorary chairman of the Superman Fan Club in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_Osamu_Tezuka_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-About_Osamu_Tezuka-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tezuka was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agnostic" title="Agnostic">agnostic</a>, but was buried in a Buddhist cemetery in Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tezuka died of <a href="/wiki/Stomach_cancer" title="Stomach cancer">stomach cancer</a> on 9 February 1989 in Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patten2004_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patten2004-11">[11]</a></sup> His last words were: "I'm begging you, let me work!", spoken to a nurse who had tried to take away his drawing equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hinotori_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-hinotori-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Takarazuka,_Hy%C5%8Dgo" title="Takarazuka, Hyōgo">Takarazuka, Hyōgo</a>, where Tezuka grew up, opened a museum in his memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otaku_Encyclopedia_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Otaku_Encyclopedia-3">[3]</a></sup> Stamps were issued in his honor in 1997. Also, beginning in 2003, the Japanese toy company <a href="/wiki/Kaiyodo" title="Kaiyodo">Kaiyodo</a> began manufacturing a series of figurines of Tezuka's creations, including Princess Knight, Unico, the Phoenix, Dororo, Marvelous Melmo, Ambassador Magma and many others. To date three series of the figurines have been released.</p>
<p>His legacy has continued to be honored among Manga artists and animators. Artists such as <a href="/wiki/Hayao_Miyazaki" title="Hayao Miyazaki">Hayao Miyazaki</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Spirited_Away" title="Spirited Away">Spirited Away</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Akira_Toriyama" title="Akira Toriyama">Akira Toriyama</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Slump" title="Dr. Slump">Dr. Slump</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Ball" title="Dragon Ball">Dragon Ball</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SJ_interview_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SJ_interview-13">[13]</a></sup> have cited Tezuka as inspiration for their works.</p>
<p>From 2003 to 2009, <a href="/wiki/Naoki_Urasawa" title="Naoki Urasawa">Naoki Urasawa</a> and Takashi Nagasaki adapted an arc of <i>Astro Boy</i> into the murder mystery series <i><a href="/wiki/Pluto_(manga)" title="Pluto (manga)">Pluto</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pluto_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pluto-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tezuka was a personal friend (and apparent artistic influence) of Brazilian comic book artist <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_de_Sousa" title="Mauricio de Sousa">Mauricio de Sousa</a>. In 2012, Maurício published a two-issue story arc in the <i><a href="/wiki/Monica_Teen" title="Monica Teen">Monica Teen</a></i> comic book featuring some of Tezuka's main characters, such as Astro Boy, Black Jack, Sapphire and Kimba, joining Monica and her friends in an adventure in the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> against a smuggling organization chopping down hundreds of trees. This was the first time that <a href="/wiki/Tezuka_Productions" title="Tezuka Productions">Tezuka Productions</a> has allowed overseas artists to use Tezuka's characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His complete oeuvre includes over 700 volumes with more than 150,000 pages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TezukaStory_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-TezukaStory-17">[17]</a></sup> A complete list of his works can be found on the Tezuka Osamu Manga Museum website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>When he was younger, Tezuka's arms swelled up and he became ill. He was treated and cured by a doctor, which made him want to be a doctor. However, he began his career as a manga artist while a university student, drawing his first professional work while at school. At a crossing point, he asked his mother whether he should look into doing manga full-time or whether he should become a doctor. At the time, being a manga author was not a particularly rewarding job. The answer his mother gave was: "You should work doing the thing you like most of all." Tezuka decided to devote himself to manga creation on a full-time basis. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University" title="Osaka University">Osaka University</a> and obtained his medical degree, but he would later use his medical and scientific knowledge to enrich his sci-fi manga, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Jack_(manga)" title="Black Jack (manga)">Black Jack</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TezukaStory_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-TezukaStory-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanabatake_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanabatake-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tezuka's creations include <i><a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy" title="Astro Boy">Astro Boy</a></i> (<i>Mighty Atom</i> in Japan), <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Jack_(manga)" title="Black Jack (manga)">Black Jack</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Knight" title="Princess Knight">Princess Knight</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(manga)" title="Phoenix (manga)">Phoenix</a></i> (<i>Hi no Tori</i> in Japan), <i><a href="/wiki/Kimba_the_White_Lion" title="Kimba the White Lion">Kimba the White Lion</a></i> (<i>Jungle Emperor</i> in Japan), <i><a href="/wiki/Unico" title="Unico">Unico</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Message_to_Adolf" title="Message to Adolf">Message to Adolf</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_3" title="The Amazing 3">The Amazing 3</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Buddha_(manga)" title="Buddha (manga)">Buddha</a></i>. His "life's work" was <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(manga)" title="Phoenix (manga)">Phoenix</a></i>—a story of life and death that he began in the 1950s and continued until his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPatten2004199_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPatten2004199-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1965, Tezuka received a letter from American film director <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>, who had watched <i><a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy" title="Astro Boy">Astro Boy</a></i> and wanted to invite Tezuka to be the art director of his next movie, <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i> (1968). Although flattered by Kubrick's invitation, Tezuka could not afford to leave his studio for a year to live in England, so he had to turn it down. Although he could not work on it, he loved the film, and would play its soundtrack at maximum volume in his studio to keep him awake during long nights of work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many young manga artists once lived in the apartment where Tezuka lived, <a href="/wiki/Tokiwa-s%C5%8D" title="Tokiwa-sō">Tokiwa-sō</a>. The residents included <a href="/wiki/Shotaro_Ishinomori" title="Shotaro Ishinomori">Shotaro Ishinomori</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fujio_Akatsuka" title="Fujio Akatsuka">Fujio Akatsuka</a>, and Abiko Motou and Hiroshi Fujimoto (who worked together under the pen name <a href="/wiki/Fujiko_Fujio" title="Fujiko Fujio">Fujiko Fujio</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_Manga_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_Manga-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokiwa-so_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tokiwa-so-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tezuka is known for his imaginative stories and stylized Japanese adaptations of western literature with reading novels and watching films that came from the West. His early works included manga versions of Disney movies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bambi" title="Bambi">Bambi</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPatten2004234_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPatten2004234-25">[25]</a></sup> Tezuka's "cinematic" page layouts, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Milt_Gross" title="Milt Gross">Milt Gross</a>' early graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/He_Done_Her_Wrong" title="He Done Her Wrong">He Done Her Wrong</a></i>, which he read as a child, became a common characteristic for many manga artists who followed in Tezuka's footsteps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> His work, like that of other manga creators, was sometimes gritty and violent.</p>
<p>Tezuka headed the animation production studio <a href="/wiki/Mushi_Production" title="Mushi Production">Mushi Production</a> ("Bug Production"), which pioneered TV animation in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-foster-27">[27]</a></sup> The distinctive "large eyes" style of <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">Japanese animation</a> was invented by Tezuka,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPatten2004144_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPatten2004144-28">[28]</a></sup> drawing inspiration from Western cartoons and animated films of the time such as <i><a href="/wiki/Betty_Boop" title="Betty Boop">Betty Boop</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">Mickey Mouse</a></i> and other <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Disney</a> movies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum">Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The Osamu Tezuka Manga Museum <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">宝塚市立手塚治虫記念館</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, lit. "Takarazuka City Tezuka Osamu Memorial Hall")</span>, located in the city of <a href="/wiki/Takarazuka,_Hy%C5%8Dgo" title="Takarazuka, Hyōgo">Takarazuka, Hyōgo</a> Prefecture, was inaugurated on April 25, 1994, and has three floors (15069.47 ft²). In the basement there is an "Animation Workshop" in which visitors can make their own animation, and a mockup of the city of Takarazuka and a replica of the table where Osamu Tezuka worked.</p>
<p>On the ground floor on the way before the building's entrance, are imitations of the hands and feet of several characters from Tezuka (as in a true walk of fame) and on the inside, the entry hall, a replica of <a href="/wiki/Princess_Knight" title="Princess Knight">Princess Knight</a>'s furniture. On the same floor, is a permanent exhibition of manga and a room for the display of anime. The exhibition is divided into two parts: Osamu Tezuka and the city of Takarazuka and Osamu Tezuka, the author.</p>
<p>On the first floor are held several exhibitions and are available a manga library, with five hundred works of Tezuka (some foreign editions are also present), a video library and a lounge with a decor inspired by <i>Kimba the White Lion</i>.</p>
<p>There is also a center of glass that represents the planet Earth and is based on a book written by him in his childhood called "Our Earth of Glass".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bibliography_.28manga.29">Bibliography (manga)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osamu_Tezuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bibliography (manga)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more complete lists, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Osamu_Tezuka_manga" title="List of Osamu Tezuka manga">List of Osamu Tezuka manga</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Osamu_Tezuka_anime" title="List of Osamu Tezuka anime">List of Osamu Tezuka anime</a>.</div>
<ul>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy" title="Astro Boy">Astro Boy</a></b></i>, 1952–68. A sequel to <i>Captain ATOM</i> (1951), with Atom renamed Astro Boy in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mighty_Tezuka.21_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mighty_Tezuka.21-32">[32]</a></sup> as its main character. Eventually, Astro Boy would become Tezuka's most famous creation. He created the nuclear-powered, yet peace-loving, boy robot first after being punched in the face by a drunken GI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mighty_Tezuka.21_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mighty_Tezuka.21-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1963, <i>Astro Boy</i> made its debut as the first domestically produced animated program on Japanese television. The 30-minute weekly program (of which 193 episodes were produced) led to the first craze for anime in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In America, the TV series (which consisted of 104 episodes licensed from the Japanese run) was also a hit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadd20096_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadd20096-35">[35]</a></sup> becoming the first Japanese animation to be shown on US television, although the U.S. producers downplayed and disguised the show's Japanese origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadd200921_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadd200921-37">[37]</a></sup> Several other <i>Astro Boy</i> series have been made since, as well as a 2009 <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI-animated</a> feature film <i><a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy_(film)" title="Astro Boy (film)">Astro Boy</a></i>.</li>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(manga)" title="Phoenix (manga)">Phoenix</a></b></i>, 1956–89. Tezuka's most profound and ambitious work, dealing with man's quest for immortality, ranging from the distant past to the far future. The central character is the Phoenix, the physical manifestation of the cosmos, who carries within itself the power of immortality; either granted by the Phoenix or taken from the Phoenix by drinking a small amount of its blood. Other characters appear and reappear throughout the series; usually due to their reincarnation. The work remained unfinished at the time of Tezuka's death in 1989. <i>Phoenix</i> has been filmed several times, most notably as <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_2772" title="Phoenix 2772">Phoenix 2772</a></i> (1980). <a href="/wiki/Baku_Yumemakura" title="Baku Yumemakura">Baku Yumemakura</a> was influenced by <i>Phoenix</i>; Yumemakura would go on to write the script for <i><a href="/wiki/Alakazam_the_Great" title="Alakazam the Great">Boku no Son Goku</a></i>.</li>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Black_Jack_(manga)" title="Black Jack (manga)">Black Jack</a></b></i>, 1973–83. The story of <a href="/wiki/Black_Jack_(character)" title="Black Jack (character)">Black Jack</a>, a talented surgeon who operates illegally, using radical and supernatural techniques to combat rare afflictions. <i>Black Jack</i> received the Japan Cartoonists' Association Special Award in 1975 and the Koudansha Manga Award in 1977. Three <i>Black Jack</i> TV movies were released between 2000-01. In fall 2004, an anime TV series was aired in Japan with 61 episodes, releasing another movie afterward. A new series, titled <i><b>Black Jack 21</b></i>, started broadcasting on April 10, 2006. In September 2008, the first volume of the manga had been published in English by Vertical Publishing and more volumes are being published to this day.</li>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Buddha_(manga)" title="Buddha (manga)">Buddha</a></b></i>, 1972–83, is Tezuka's unique interpretation of the life of <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. The critically acclaimed series is often referred to as a gritty portrayal of the Buddha's life. The series began in September 1972 and ended in December 1983, as one of Tezuka's last <a href="/wiki/Epic_(genre)" title="Epic (genre)">epic</a> manga works. Nearly three decades after the manga was completed, an <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> film adaptation was released in 2011.</li>
</ul>
