<p><b>Dekopon</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">デコポン</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a seedless and sweet variety of <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin orange</a>.</p>
<p>It is a hybrid between <a href="/wiki/Kiyomi" title="Kiyomi">Kiyomi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ponkan" title="Ponkan">ponkan</a> (Nakano no.3), developed in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIFTS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIFTS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BNIFTS_35_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BNIFTS_35-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Originally a brand name, 'Dekopon' has become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> and it is used to refer to all brands of the fruit; the generic name is <i>shiranuhi or shiranui</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">不知火</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIFTS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIFTS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BNIFTS_35_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BNIFTS_35-2">[2]</a></sup> Dekopon is distinctive due to its sweet taste, large size and the large protruding bump on the top of the fruit.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dekopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name is most likely a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> between the word <i>deko</i> (凸, デコ; meaning <i>convex</i>) as a reference to its bump, and the <i>pon</i> in ponkan (<b>ポン</b>カン; one of the fruits that it is derived from) to create 'dekopon' (デコポン).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were many market names for 'dekopon' during the time 'dekopon' was a trademark of the product from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kumamoto,_Kumamoto" title="Kumamoto, Kumamoto">Kumamoto</a>. For instance, <i>himepon</i> was the market name for the fruits originating from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ehime_prefecture" title="Ehime prefecture">Ehime prefecture</a>. The ones grown in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiroshima_prefecture" title="Hiroshima prefecture">Hiroshima prefecture</a> were marketed as <i>hiropon</i>. However, after an agreement whereby anyone can use the name 'dekopon' if they pay a fee and meet certain quality standards, the name "dekopon" is used for products from anywhere in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>'Dekopon' does not have an agricultural variety registration number (<i>Nōrin Bangō</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> because of its bump, which at the time of its development was considered to be unsightly, and failure to reduce acidity in the fruit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dekopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fruits are usually grown in large greenhouses to keep them at a constant temperature, and are harvested from December to February (winter in Japan), while in the case of garden farming, they are harvested from March to April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After harvesting, dekopon are usually left for a period of 20–40 days so that the levels of <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a> in the fruit lower while the sugar levels increase, to make a more appealing taste for the market. Only products with sugar level above 13°<a href="/wiki/Brix" title="Brix">Bx</a> and citric acid below 1.0% can be sold with the name dekopon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_Japan">Outside Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dekopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Outside Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Brazil, 'dekopon' is marketed under the brand name of <i>Kinsei</i> which derived from the Japanese word for Venus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPTDA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPTDA-10">[10]</a></sup> Brazilian farmers have succeeded in adapting the variety to tropical to temperate climate in the highlands of São Paulo state. The work is done by Unkichi Taniwaki, a farmer of Japanese origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPTDA_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPTDA-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Kinsei</i> is easily harvested from May to September. In the high season for <i>kinsei</i>, each fruit costs around 0.50 USD at the Brazilian street market and supermarkets.</p>
<p>In South Korea, 'dekopon' is called <i>hallabong</i> (한라봉) named after <a href="/wiki/Hallasan" title="Hallasan">Hallasan</a> the mountain located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeju-do" title="Jeju-do">Jeju-do</a>, where it is primarily grown.</p>
<p>The citrus budwood was imported into the United States in 1998 by a California citrus grower, Brad Stark Jr. The rights to the sterilized budwood were purchased in 2005 by the Griffith family, owners of the nursery TreeSource and packing facility Suntreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffth_Farms_About_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griffth_Farms_About-11">[11]</a></sup> The dekopon was released as a commercial product in the US under the name "Sumo" in early 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dekopon_2011-02-17_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dekopon_2011-02-17-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dekopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>'Dekopon' have become so popular in Japan that the chewing candy brand giant <a href="/wiki/Hi-Chew" title="Hi-Chew">Hi-Chew</a> (ハイチュウ) has released a limited-edition 'dekopon' flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In commemoration of the 15th anniversary of the first shipment of 'dekopon', Japan Fruit Growers Cooperative Association designated March 1 "Dekopon day" in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
