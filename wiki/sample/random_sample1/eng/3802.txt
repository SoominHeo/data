<p>The <b>Deaflympics</b> (previously called <i>World Games for the Deaf</i>, and <i>International Games for the Deaf</i>) are an <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC)-sanctioned event at which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf" title="Deaf">deaf</a> athletes compete at an elite level. Unlike the athletes in other IOC-sanctioned events (the Olympics, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paralympics" title="Paralympics">Paralympics</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Special_Olympics" title="Special Olympics">Special Olympics</a>), the Deaflympians cannot be guided by sounds (e.g., the starter's guns, bullhorn commands or referee whistles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deaflympics.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-deaflympics.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The games have been organized by the <i><a href="/wiki/Comit%C3%A9_International_des_Sports_des_Sourds" title="Comité International des Sports des Sourds">Comité International des Sports des Sourds</a></i> (CISS, "The International Committee of Sports for the Deaf") since <a href="/wiki/1924_Summer_Deaflympics" title="1924 Summer Deaflympics">the first event</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deaflympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Deaflympics are held every four years, and are the longest running multi-sport event excluding the Olympics themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/1924_Summer_Deaflympics" title="1924 Summer Deaflympics">The first games, held in Paris in 1924</a>, were also the first ever international sporting event for athletes with a <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Future_Directions_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Future_Directions-4">[4]</a></sup> The event has been held every four years since, apart from a break for World War II, and an additional event, the Deaflympic Winter Games, was added in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historical_overview_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_overview-5">[5]</a></sup> The games began as a small gathering of 148 athletes from nine European nations competing in the International Silent Games in Paris, France, in 1924; now, they have grown into a global movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deaflympics.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-deaflympics.com-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Officially, the games were originally called the "International Games for the Deaf" from 1924 to 1965, but were sometimes also referred to as the "International Silent Games". From 1966 to 1999 they were called the "World Games for the Deaf", and occasionally referred to as the "World Silent Games". From 2001, the games have been known by their current name Deaflympics (often mistakenly called the Deaf Olympics).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historical_overview_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_overview-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>To qualify for the games, athletes must have a <a href="/wiki/Hearing_loss" title="Hearing loss">hearing loss</a> of at least 55 db in their "better ear". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_aids" title="Hearing aids">Hearing aids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochlear_implants" title="Cochlear implants">cochlear implants</a> and the like are not allowed to be used in competition, to place all athletes on the same level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historical_overview_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_overview-5">[5]</a></sup> Other examples of ways the games vary from hearing competitions are the manner in which they are officiated. To address the issue of Deaflympians not being able to be guided by sounds, certain sports use alternative methods of commencing the game. For example, the football referees wave a flag instead of blowing a whistle; on the track, races are started by using a light, instead of a starter pistol. It is also customary for spectators not to cheer or clap, but rather to wave – usually with both hands.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Host_nations_and_cities">Host nations and cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deaflympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Host nations and cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To date, the Summer Deaflympic Games have been hosted by 36 cities in 21 countries, but by cities outside Europe on only five occasions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a> 1965, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> 1985 , <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a> 1989, <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> 2005 and <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a> 2009). The last summer games was held in <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, Bulgaria in 2013, and the next scheduled summer games will be in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sansum" title="Sansum">Sansum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in 2017. The last winter games were held in <a href="/wiki/Khanty-Mansiysk" title="Khanty-Mansiysk">Khanty-Mansiysk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a> in 2015.</p>
<p>The 2011 Winter Games scheduled to be held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vysok%C3%A9_Tatry_(town)" title="Vysoké Tatry (town)">Vysoké Tatry</a>, Slovakia were cancelled due to the lack of readiness by the organizing committee to host the games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The International Committee of Deaf Sports filed a criminal complaint against the Slovak Deaflympics Organizing Committee and its President, Mr. Jaromír Ruda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deafsportsmag1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-deafsportsmag1-8">[8]</a></sup> The criminal complaint demands reimbursement of the funds that were transferred to the Slovak Deaflympics Organizing Committee from national deaf sports federations, to cover hotel accommodations and other Deaflympics-related expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deafsportsmag1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-deafsportsmag1-8">[8]</a></sup> According to the Slovak newspaper, <a href="/wiki/SME_(newspaper)" title="SME (newspaper)">SME</a>, "Jaromír Ruda, head of the Slovak Organising Committee, [is] a champion of promises and someone who is accused of a 1.6 million Euro Deaflympics-related fraud".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In a letter to the United States Deaflympians, International Committee of Sports for the Deaf <a href="/wiki/Comit%C3%A9_International_des_Sports_des_Sourds" title="Comité International des Sports des Sourds">ICSD</a> President <a href="/wiki/Craig_Crowley" title="Craig Crowley">Craig Crowley</a> expressed "his deep apologies for the cancellation of the 17th Winter Deaflympics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Currently, the Slovak Deaflympic Committee and the Slovakia Association of Deaf Sportsmen Unions have been suspended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2013 the Special Criminal Court in Banská Bystrica sentenced Ruda to a prison term of 14 and a half years for defrauding €1.6 million that should have been used for Winter Deaflympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
