<p><b>Emperor Tenmu</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">天武天皇</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tenmu tennō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, c. 631 – October 1, 686)</span> was the 40th <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kunaicho_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kunaicho-1">[1]</a></sup> according to the traditional <a href="/wiki/List_of_Emperors_of_Japan" title="List of Emperors of Japan">order of succession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ponsonby53_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ponsonby53-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tenmu's reign lasted from 673 until his death in 686.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_narrative">Traditional narrative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Tenmu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Traditional narrative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tenmu was the youngest son of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Jomei" title="Emperor Jomei">Emperor Jomei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Empress_K%C5%8Dgyoku" title="Empress Kōgyoku">Empress Kōgyoku</a>, and the younger brother of the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Tenji" title="Emperor Tenji">Emperor Tenji</a>. His name at birth was Prince Ōama (大海人皇子:Ōama no ōji). He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Empress_Jit%C5%8D" title="Empress Jitō">Empress Jitō</a>, who was both his niece and his wife. During the reign of his elder brother, Emperor Tenji, Tenmu was forced to marry several of Tenji's daughters because Tenji thought those marriages would help to strengthen political ties between the two brothers. The nieces he married included Princess Unonosarara, today known as Empress Jitō, and <a href="/wiki/Princess_%C5%8Cta" title="Princess Ōta">Princess Ōta</a>. Tenmu also had other consorts whose fathers were influential courtiers.</p>
<p>Tenmu had many children, including his crown <a href="/wiki/Prince_Kusakabe" title="Prince Kusakabe">prince Kusakabe</a> by Princess Unonosarara; <a href="/wiki/Princess_T%C5%8Dchi" title="Princess Tōchi">Princess Tōchi</a>; <a href="/wiki/Prince_%C5%8Ctsu" title="Prince Ōtsu">Prince Ōtsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_%C5%8Cku" title="Princess Ōku">Princess Ōku</a> by Princess Ōta (whose father also was Tenji); and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Toneri" title="Prince Toneri">Prince Toneri</a>, the editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i> and father of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Junnin" title="Emperor Junnin">Emperor Junnin</a>. Through Prince Kusakabe, Tenmu had two emperors and two empresses among his descendents. <a href="/wiki/Empress_K%C5%8Dken" title="Empress Kōken">Empress Kōken</a> was the last of these imperial rulers from his lineage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_of_Tenmu.27s_life">Events of Tenmu's life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Tenmu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Events of Tenmu's life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Emperor Tenmu is the first monarch of Japan, to whom the title <i>Tennō</i> (<a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a>) was assigned contemporaneously—not only by later generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The only document on his life was <i>Nihon Shoki</i>. However, it was edited by his son, Prince Toneri, and the work was written during the reigns of his wife and children, causing one to suspect its accuracy and impartiality. He is also mentioned briefly in the preface to the <i><a href="/wiki/Kojiki" title="Kojiki">Kojiki</a></i>, being hailed as the emperor to have commissioned them.</p>
<p>Tenmu's father died while he was young, and he grew up mainly under the guidance of Empress Saimei. He was not expected to gain the throne, because his brother Tenji was the crown prince, being the older son of their mother, the reigning empress.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Tenji_period" title="Tenji period">Tenji period</a>, Tenmu was appointed his crown prince. This was because Tenji had no appropriate heir among his sons at that time, as none of their mothers was of a rank high enough to give the necessary political support. Tenji was suspicious that Tenmu might be so ambitious as to attempt to take the throne, and felt the necessity to strengthen his position through politically advantageous marriages.</p>
<p>Tenji was particularly active in improving the military institutions which had been established during the Taika reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his old age, Tenji had a son, Prince Ōtomo, by a low-ranking consort. Since Ōtomo had weak political support from his maternal relatives, the general wisdom of the time<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> held that it was not a good idea for him to ascend to the throne, yet Tenji was obsessed with the idea.</p>
<p>In 671 Tenmu felt himself to be in danger and volunteered to resign the office of crown prince to become a monk. He moved to the mountains in Yoshino, <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Province" title="Yamato Province">Yamato Province</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Yoshino,_Nara" title="Yoshino, Nara">Yoshino, Nara</a>), officially for reasons of seclusion. He took with him his sons and one of his wives, Princess Unonosarara, a daughter of Tenji. However, he left all his other consorts at the capital, Omikyō in <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cmi_Province" title="Ōmi Province">Ōmi Province</a> (today in <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctsu" title="Ōtsu">Ōtsu</a>).</p>
<p>A year later, (in 672) Tenji died and Prince Ōtomo ascended to the throne as <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dbun" title="Emperor Kōbun">Emperor Kōbun</a>. Tenmu assembled an army and marched from Yoshino to the east, to attack the capital of Omikyō in a counterclockwise movement. They marched through Yamato, <a href="/wiki/Iga_Province" title="Iga Province">Iga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mino_Province" title="Mino Province">Mino Provinces</a> to threaten Omikyō in the adjacent province. The army of Tenmu and the army of the young Emperor Kōbun fought in the northwestern part of Mino (nowadays <a href="/wiki/Sekigahara,_Gifu" title="Sekigahara, Gifu">Sekigahara, Gifu</a>). Tenmu's army won and Kōbun committed suicide, an incident known as the <a href="/wiki/Jinshin_War" title="Jinshin War">Jinshin War</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Post-Meiji chronology</b>
<ul>
<li><i><b>In the 10th year of Tenji</b></i>, in the 11th month (671): Emperor Tenji, in the 10th year of his reign (天智天皇十年), designated his son as his heir; and modern scholars construe this as meaning that the son would have received the succession (<i>senso</i>) after his father's death. Shortly thereafter, Emperor Kōbun is said to have acceded to the throne (<i>sokui</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> If this understanding were valid, then it would it would follow:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><i><b>In the 1st year of <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dbun_(period)" title="Kōbun (period)">Kōbun</a></b></i> (672): Emperor Kōbun, in the 1st year of his reign (弘文天皇一年), died; and his uncle Ōaomi<i>-shinnō</i> received the succession (<i>senso</i>) after the death of his nephew. Shortly thereafter, Emperor Tenmu could be said to have acceded to the throne (<i>sokui</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><b>Pre-Meiji chronology</b></dd>
<dd>Prior to the 19th century, Otomo was understood to have been a mere interloper, a pretender, an anomaly; and therefore, if that commonly accepted understanding were to have been valid, then it would have followed:
<ul>
<li><i><b>In the 10th year of Tenji</b></i>, in the 11th month (671): Emperor Tenji, in the 10th year of his reign (天智天皇十年), died; and despite any military confrontations which ensued, the brother of the dead sovereign would have received the succession (<i>senso</i>); and after a time, it would have been understood that Emperor Tenmu rightfully acceded to the throne (<i>sokui</i>).</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>As might be expected, Emperor Tenmu was no less active than former-Emperor Tenji in improving the Taika military institutions. Tenmu's reign brought many changes, such as: (1) a centralized war department was organized; (2) the defenses of the Inner Country near the Capital were strengthened; (3) forts and castles were built near Capital and in the western parts of Honshū—and in Kyushu; (4) troops were reviewed; and all provincial governors were ordered to complete the collection of arms and to study tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>At Asuka, Emperor Tenmu was enthroned. He elevated Unonosarara to be his empress. Events of his reign include:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>674</b> {<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temmu_(period)" title="Temmu (period)">Temmu</a> 2</i>): Ambassadors of <i><a href="/wiki/Tane_Province" title="Tane Province">Tane no kuni</a></i> were received in the Japanese court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>680</b> (<i>Temmu 8</i>): <a href="/wiki/Yakushi-ji" title="Yakushi-ji">Yakushi-ji</a> was founded in the <a href="/wiki/Hakuh%C5%8D_period" title="Hakuhō period">Hakuhō period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Tenmu reigned from this capital until his death in 686. The actual site of his <a href="/wiki/Grave" title="Grave">grave</a> is known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kunaicho_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kunaicho-1">[1]</a></sup> This emperor is traditionally venerated at a memorial <a href="/wiki/Shrine" title="Shrine">shrine</a> (<i>misasagi</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Nara_Prefecture" title="Nara Prefecture">Nara Prefecture</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Household_Agency" title="Imperial Household Agency">Imperial Household Agency</a> designates this location as Tenmu's <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum" title="Mausoleum">mausoleum</a>. It is formally named <i>Hinokuma no Ōuchi no misasagi.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism">Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Tenmu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 675 Emperor Tenmu banned the consumption of animal meat (horse, cattle, dogs, monkeys, birds), due to the influence of Buddhism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the <i>Nihon Shoki</i>, Tenmu is described as a great innovator, but the neutrality of this description is doubtful, since the work was written under the control of his descendants. It seems clear, however, that Tenmu strengthened the power of the emperor and appointed his sons to the highest offices of his government, reducing the traditional influence of powerful clans such as the <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_clan" title="Ōtomo clan">Ōtomo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soga_clan" title="Soga clan">Soga clans</a>. He renewed the system of <i><a href="/wiki/Kabane" title="Kabane">kabane</a></i>, the hereditary titles of duty and rank, but with alterations, including the abolition of some titles. <a href="/wiki/Omi" title="Omi">Omi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muraji" title="Muraji">Muraji</a>, the highest kabane in the earlier period, were reduced in value in the new hierarchy, which consisted of eight kinds of kabane. Each clan received a new kabane according to its closeness to the imperial bloodline and its loyalty to Tenmu.</p>
<p>Tenmu attempted to keep a balance of power among his sons. Once he traveled to Yoshino together with his sons, and there had them swear to cooperate and not to make war on each other. This turned out to be ineffective: one of his sons, Prince Ōtsu, was later executed for treason after the death of Tenmu.</p>
<p>Tenmu's foreign policy favored the Korean kingdom <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>, which took over the entire Korean peninsula in 676. After the unification of Korea by Silla, Tenmu decided to break diplomatic relations with the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> of China, evidently in order to keep on good terms with Silla.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Tenmu used religious structures to increase the authority of the imperial throne. During his reign there was increased emphasis on the tie between the imperial household and <a href="/wiki/Ise_Grand_Shrine" title="Ise Grand Shrine">Ise Grand Shrine</a> (dedicated to the ancestor goddess of the emperors, <a href="/wiki/Amaterasu" title="Amaterasu">Amaterasu</a>) by sending his daughter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Oku" title="Princess Oku">Princess Oku</a> as the newly established <a href="/wiki/Sai%C5%8D" title="Saiō">Saiō</a> of the shrine, and several festivals were financed from the national budget. He also showed favor to <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and built several large temples and monasteries. It is said that Tenmu asked that each household was encouraged to build an altar with a dais where a Buddha-image and a sutra could be placed so that family worshiping could be held, thus inventing the <a href="/wiki/Butsudan" title="Butsudan">butsudan</a>. On the other hand, all Buddhist priests, monks and nuns were controlled by the state, and no one was allowed to become a monk without the state's permission. This was aimed at preventing cults and stopping farmers from turning into priests.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kugy.C5.8D"><i>Kugyō</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Tenmu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Kugyō">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Kugy%C5%8D" title="Kugyō">Kugyō</a></i> (公卿) is a collective term for the very few most powerful men attached to the court of the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a> in pre-<a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji</a> eras.</p>
<p>In general, this elite group included only three to four men at a time. These were hereditary courtiers whose experience and background would have brought them to the pinnacle of a life's career. During Tenmu's reign, this apex of the <i><a href="/wiki/Daij%C5%8D-kan" title="Daijō-kan">Daijō-kan</a></i> included:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sadaijin" title="Sadaijin">Sadaijin</a></i>, Soga no Akae no Omi <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b269_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-b269-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Udaijin" title="Udaijin">Udaijin</a></i>, Nakatomi no Kane no Muraji <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b269_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-b269-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Naidaijin" title="Naidaijin">Naidaijin</a></i></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Era_of_Tenmu.27s_reign">Era of Tenmu's reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Tenmu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Era of Tenmu's reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The years of Tenmu's reign were marked by only one <a href="/wiki/Japanese_era_name" title="Japanese era name">era name</a> or <i>nengō</i>, which was proclaimed in the final months of the emperor's life; and <i>Shuchō</i> ended with Tenmu's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t55-58_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-t55-58-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Shuch%C5%8D" title="Shuchō">Shuchō</a></i> (686)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-neng.C5.8D_period">Non<i>-nengō</i> period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Tenmu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-nengō period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The early years of Tenmu's reign are not linked by scholars to any era or <i>nengō</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t55-58_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-t55-58-16">[16]</a></sup> The Taika era innovation of naming time periods – <i>nengō</i> – was discontinued during these years, but it was reestablished briefly in 686. The use of <i>nengō</i> languished yet again after Tenmu's death until Emperor Mommu reasserted an imperial right by proclaiming the commencement of Taihō in 701.</p>
<ul>
<li>See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temmu_(period)" title="Temmu (period)">Temmu (period)</a> (673–686).</li>
</ul>
<p>In this context, Brown and Ishida's translation of <i>Gukanshō</i> offers an explanation about the years of Empress Jitō's reign which muddies a sense of easy clarity in the pre-Taihō time-frame:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>"The eras that fell in this reign were: (1) the remaining seven years of Shuchō [(686+7=692?)]; and (2) Taika, which was four years long [695–698]. (The first year of this era was <i>kinoto-hitsuji</i> [695].) ...In the third year of the Taika era [697], Empress Jitō yielded the throne to the Crown Prince."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
