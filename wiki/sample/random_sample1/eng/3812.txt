<p><b>Denholm Mitchell Elliott</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (31 May 1922 – 6 October 1992) was an English film, television and theatre actor with more than 120 film and television credits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Screen_Online_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Screen_Online-1">[1]</a></sup> Elliott was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor</a> for his performance in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Room_with_a_View" title="A Room with a View">A Room with a View</a></i> in 1985. He trained at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Dramatic_Art" title="Royal Academy of Dramatic Art">Royal Academy of Dramatic Art</a> in London. In the 1980s, he won the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Actor_in_a_Supporting_Role" title="BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role">BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role</a> in three consecutive years, the only actor ever to have achieved this. He is perhaps best known for portraying <a href="/wiki/List_of_Indiana_Jones_characters#Marcus_Brody" title="List of Indiana Jones characters">Dr Marcus Brody</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Raiders_of_the_Lost_Ark" title="Raiders of the Lost Ark">Raiders of the Lost Ark</a></i> (1981) and <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Last_Crusade" title="Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade">Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade</a></i> (1989) and as Coleman in <i><a href="/wiki/Trading_Places" title="Trading Places">Trading Places</a></i> (1983).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denholm_Elliott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elliott was born in London, the son of Nina (née Mitchell) and Myles Laymen Farr Elliott. His elder brother Neil was <a href="/wiki/Land_agent" title="Land agent">land agent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lady_Anne_Cavendish-Bentinck" title="Lady Anne Cavendish-Bentinck">Lady Anne Cavendish-Bentinck</a>. Myles was a barrister who had read Law and Arabic at Cambridge before fighting with the <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Regiment" title="Gloucestershire Regiment">Glosters</a> at Gallipoli and in Mesopotamia. In 1930 Myles Elliott was appointed Solicitor-General to the <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Government in Palestine</a>. Three years later, following a series of controversial government prosecutions, he was assassinated outside the <a href="/wiki/King_David_Hotel" title="King David Hotel">King David Hotel</a>, and buried in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Zion_Cemetery,_Jerusalem" title="Mount Zion Cemetery, Jerusalem">Protestant Cemetery on Mount Zion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Denholm Elliott attended <a href="/wiki/Malvern_College" title="Malvern College">Malvern College</a> and trained at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Dramatic_Art" title="Royal Academy of Dramatic Art">Royal Academy of Dramatic Art</a> in London. He was asked to leave RADA after one term. As Elliott later recalled: "They wrote to my mother and said, 'Much as we like the little fellow, he's wasting your money and our time. Take him away!'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>, training as a sergeant wireless operator/ air gunner and serving with <a href="/wiki/No._76_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 76 Squadron RAF">No. 76 Squadron RAF</a> under the command of <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Cheshire" title="Leonard Cheshire">Leonard Cheshire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the night of 23/24 September 1942, his <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Handley Page Halifax</a> DT508<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lost360_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-lost360-5">[5]</a></sup> bomber took part in an air raid on the <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> pens at <a href="/wiki/Flensburg" title="Flensburg">Flensburg</a>, Germany. The aircraft was hit by <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">flak</a> and subsequently ditched in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> near <a href="/wiki/Sylt" title="Sylt">Sylt</a>, Germany. Elliott and two other crew members survived and he spent the rest of the war in a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> camp in <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>. While imprisoned he became involved in amateur dramatics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denholm_Elliott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After making his film debut in <i><a href="/wiki/Dear_Mr._Prohack" title="Dear Mr. Prohack">Dear Mr. Prohack</a></i> (1949), he went on to play a wide range of parts, often ineffectual and occasionally seedy characters, such as the drunken journalist Bayliss in <i><a href="/wiki/Defence_of_the_Realm" title="Defence of the Realm">Defence of the Realm</a></i>, the criminal abortionist in <i><a href="/wiki/Alfie_(1966_film)" title="Alfie (1966 film)">Alfie</a></i>, and the washed-up film director in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Apprenticeship_of_Duddy_Kravitz_(film)" title="The Apprenticeship of Duddy Kravitz (film)">The Apprenticeship of Duddy Kravitz</a></i>. Elliott and <a href="/wiki/Natasha_Parry" title="Natasha Parry">Natasha Parry</a> played the main roles in the 1955 <a href="/wiki/Television_play" title="Television play">television play</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Apollo_of_Bellac" title="The Apollo of Bellac">The Apollo of Bellac</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-times-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elliott made many television appearances, notably in plays by <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Potter" title="Dennis Potter">Dennis Potter</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Follow_the_Yellow_Brick_Road" title="Follow the Yellow Brick Road">Follow the Yellow Brick Road</a></i> (1972), <i><a href="/wiki/Brimstone_and_Treacle" title="Brimstone and Treacle">Brimstone and Treacle</a></i> (1976) and <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_on_the_Feather" title="Blade on the Feather">Blade on the Feather</a></i> (1980). He took over for an ill <a href="/wiki/Michael_Aldridge" title="Michael Aldridge">Michael Aldridge</a> for one season of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Man_In_Room_17/The_Fellows_(Late_of_Room_17)" title="The Man In Room 17/The Fellows (Late of Room 17)">The Man in Room 17</a></i> (1966) and appeared in the series <i><a href="/wiki/Thriller_(UK_TV_series)" title="Thriller (UK TV series)">Thriller</a></i> (1975).</p>
<p>In the 1980s, he won three consecutive <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Academy of Film and Television Arts</a> (BAFTA) awards – Best Supporting Actor for <i><a href="/wiki/Trading_Places" title="Trading Places">Trading Places</a></i> as <a href="/wiki/Dan_Aykroyd" title="Dan Aykroyd">Dan Aykroyd</a>'s kindly butler, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Private_Function" title="A Private Function">A Private Function</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Defence_of_the_Realm" title="Defence of the Realm">Defence of the Realm</a></i> – as well as an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Award</a> nomination for <i><a href="/wiki/A_Room_with_a_View_(1985_film)" title="A Room with a View (1985 film)">A Room with a View</a></i>. He also became familiar to a wider audience as the well-meaning but addlepated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_Brody" title="Marcus Brody">Dr. Marcus Brody</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Raiders_of_the_Lost_Ark" title="Raiders of the Lost Ark">Raiders of the Lost Ark</a></i> (1981) and <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Last_Crusade" title="Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade">Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade</a></i>. A photograph of his character appears in <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Kingdom_of_the_Crystal_Skull" title="Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull">Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull</a></i> and a reference is made to Brody's death. There is also a statue dedicated to Marcus outside of Marshall College, the school where Indy teaches. In 1988, Elliott was the Russian <a href="/wiki/Mole_(espionage)" title="Mole (espionage)">mole</a> Povin, around whom the entire plot revolves, in the television miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/Codename:_Kyril" title="Codename: Kyril">Codename: Kyril</a></i>.</p>
<p>Having filmed <a href="/wiki/Michael_Winner" title="Michael Winner">Michael Winner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wicked_Lady_(1983_film)" title="The Wicked Lady (1983 film)">The Wicked Lady</a></i> (1983), Elliott was quoted in a <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a> interview as saying that he and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Sinden" title="Marc Sinden">Marc Sinden</a> "are the only two British actors I am aware of who have ever worked with Winner more than once and it certainly wasn't for love. But curiously, I never, ever saw any of the same crew twice." (Elliott in <i>You Must Be Joking!</i> (1965) and <i>The Wicked Lady</i> and Sinden in <i>The Wicked Lady</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Decadence_(film)" title="Decadence (film)">Decadence</a></i>). Elliott had worked with Sinden's father, Sir <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sinden" title="Donald Sinden">Donald Sinden</a>, in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cruel_Sea_(1953_film)" title="The Cruel Sea (1953 film)">The Cruel Sea</a></i> (1953).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Hepburn" title="Katharine Hepburn">Katharine Hepburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harold_Gould" title="Harold Gould">Harold Gould</a> in the television film, <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs._Delafield_Wants_to_Marry" title="Mrs. Delafield Wants to Marry">Mrs. Delafield Wants to Marry</a></i> (1986) and with <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Kidman" title="Nicole Kidman">Nicole Kidman</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Bangkok_Hilton" title="Bangkok Hilton">Bangkok Hilton</a></i> (1989).</p>
<p>In 1988, Elliott was appointed a Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) for his services to acting. His career included many stage performances, including with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Shakespeare_Company" title="Royal Shakespeare Company">Royal Shakespeare Company</a>, and a well acclaimed turn as the twin brothers in <a href="/wiki/Jean_Anouilh" title="Jean Anouilh">Jean Anouilh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ring_Round_the_Moon" title="Ring Round the Moon">Ring Round the Moon</a></i>. His scene-stealing abilities led <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Byrne" title="Gabriel Byrne">Gabriel Byrne</a>, his co-star in <i>Defence of the Realm</i>, to say: "Never act with children, dogs, or Denholm Elliott."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obit-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite being described by <a href="/wiki/Screenonline" title="Screenonline">Screenonline</a> as an actor of "versatile understanding and immaculate technique",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Screen_Online_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Screen_Online-1">[1]</a></sup> Elliott described himself as an instinctive actor and was a critic of <a href="/wiki/Stanislavski%27s_system" title="Stanislavski's system">Stanislavski's system</a> of acting, saying: "I mistrust and am rather bored with actors who are of the Stanislavski school who think about detail."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Times_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Times-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denholm_Elliott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Privately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bisexual" title="Bisexual">bisexual</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Susan_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Susan-11">[11]</a></sup> Elliott was married twice; first to actress <a href="/wiki/Virginia_McKenna" title="Virginia McKenna">Virginia McKenna</a> for a few months in 1954, and later, in an <a href="/wiki/Open_marriage" title="Open marriage">open marriage</a>, to <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> actress Susan Robinson (7 March 1942 – 12 April 2007), with whom he had two children, Mark and Jennifer. Jennifer committed suicide by hanging in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Susan_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Susan-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denholm_Elliott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Elliott was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> in 1987<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Susan_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Susan-11">[11]</a></sup> and died of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>-related <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> at his home in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Eul%C3%A0ria_des_Riu" title="Santa Eulària des Riu">Santa Eulària des Riu</a> on <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">Ibiza</a>, Spain, on 6 October 1992 at the age of 70. Tributes were paid by actors Sir <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sinden" title="Donald Sinden">Donald Sinden</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ustinov" title="Peter Ustinov">Peter Ustinov</a>, playwright <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Potter" title="Dennis Potter">Dennis Potter</a> and former wife Virginia McKenna. Sinden said, "He was one of the finest screen actors and a very special actor at that. He was one of the last stars who was a real gentleman. It is a very sad loss." Ustinov said, "He was a wonderful actor and a very good friend on the occasions that life brought us together." Potter commented, "He was a complicated, sensitive and slightly disturbing actor. Not only was he a very accomplished actor, he was a dry, witty and slightly menacing individual. As a man, I always found him very open, very straightforward and very much to the point." McKenna added: "It is absolutely dreadful, but the person I am thinking of at the moment more than anybody is his wife. It must be terrible for her."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ismail_Merchant" title="Ismail Merchant">Ismail Merchant</a> described Elliott as "an all-giving person, full of life ... He had an affection and feeling for other actors, which is very unusual in our business."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>His widow set up a charity, the <b>Denholm Elliott Project</b> and collaborated on his biography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> She worked closely with the <a href="/wiki/UK_Coalition_of_People_Living_with_HIV_and_AIDS" title="UK Coalition of People Living with HIV and AIDS">UK Coalition of People Living with HIV and AIDS</a>. Susan Robinson Elliott died on 12 April 2007, aged 65, following a fire in her flat in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Susan_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Susan-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
