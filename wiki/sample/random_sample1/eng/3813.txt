<p><b>Del</b>, or <b>nabla</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Operator_(mathematics)" title="Operator (mathematics)">operator</a> used in mathematics, in particular, in <a href="/wiki/Vector_calculus" title="Vector calculus">vector calculus</a>, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometry)" title="Vector (geometry)">vector</a> <a href="/wiki/Differential_operator" title="Differential operator">differential operator</a>, usually represented by the <a href="/wiki/Nabla_symbol" title="Nabla symbol">nabla symbol</a> <b>∇</b>. When applied to a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a> defined on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimension_(mathematics)" title="Dimension (mathematics)">one-dimensional</a> domain, it denotes its standard <a href="/wiki/Derivative" title="Derivative">derivative</a> as defined in <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>. When applied to a field (a function defined on a multi-dimensional domain), del may denote the <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> (locally steepest slope) of a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar field</a> (or sometimes of a <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">vector field</a>, as in the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations#Interpretation_as_v.C2.B7.28.E2.88.87v.29" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">divergence</a> of a vector field, or the <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a> (rotation) of a vector field, depending on the way it is applied.</p>
<p>Strictly speaking, del is not a specific operator, but rather a convenient <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_notation" title="Mathematical notation">mathematical notation</a> for those three operators, that makes many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equations" title="Equations">equations</a> easier to write and remember. The del symbol can be interpreted as a vector of <a href="/wiki/Partial_derivative" title="Partial derivative">partial derivative</a> operators, and its three possible meanings—gradient, divergence, and curl—can be formally viewed as the <a href="/wiki/Product_(mathematics)" title="Product (mathematics)">product</a> with a scalar, <a href="/wiki/Dot_product" title="Dot product">dot product</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">cross product</a>, respectively, of the del "operator" with the field. These formal products do not necessarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutative_operation" title="Commutative operation">commute</a> with other operators or products.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notational_uses">Notational uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Del&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notational uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Del is used as a shorthand form to simplify many long mathematical expressions. It is most commonly used to simplify expressions for the <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">divergence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Directional_derivative" title="Directional derivative">directional derivative</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laplacian" title="Laplacian">Laplacian</a>.</p>

<p>When del operates on a scalar or vector, either a scalar or vector is returned. Because of the diversity of vector products (scalar, dot, cross) one application of del already gives rise to three major derivatives: the gradient (scalar product), divergence (dot product), and curl (cross product). Applying these three sorts of derivatives again to each other gives five possible second derivatives, for a scalar field <i>f</i> or a vector field <i><b>v</b></i>; the use of the scalar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laplacian" title="Laplacian">Laplacian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vector_Laplacian" title="Vector Laplacian">vector Laplacian</a> gives two more:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Del&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the above vector properties (except for those that rely explicitly on del's differential properties—for example, the product rule) rely only on symbol rearrangement, and must necessarily hold if the del symbol is replaced by any other vector. This is part of the value to be gained in notationally representing this operator as a vector.</p>
<p>Though one can often replace del with a vector and obtain a vector identity, making those identities mnemonic, the reverse is <i>not</i> necessarily reliable, because del does not commute in general.</p>

</dl>
<p>Central to these distinctions is the fact that del is not simply a vector; it is a <a href="/wiki/Vector_operator" title="Vector operator">vector operator</a>. Whereas a vector is an object with both a magnitude and direction, del has neither a magnitude nor a direction until it operates on a function.</p>
<p>For that reason, identities involving del must be derived with care, using both vector identities and <i>differentiation</i> identities such as the product rule.</p>
