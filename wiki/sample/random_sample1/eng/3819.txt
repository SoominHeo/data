<p><b>Delta Connection</b> is a regional airline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brand_name" title="Brand name">brand name</a> for <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a>, under which a number of individually owned <a href="/wiki/Regional_airline" title="Regional airline">regional airlines</a> operate short- and medium-haul routes. Delta's lone wholly owned regional airline, <a href="/wiki/Endeavor_Air" title="Endeavor Air">Endeavor Air</a>, also resides under the <b>Delta Connection</b> banner. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainline_(flight)" title="Mainline (flight)">Mainline</a> carriers often use regional airlines to operate services in order to increase frequency, serve routes that would not sustain larger aircraft, or for other competitive reasons.</p>

<p>Delta Connection was founded in 1984 as a means of expanding the Delta network to smaller markets via partnerships with regional airlines. <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Southeast_Airlines" title="Atlantic Southeast Airlines">Atlantic Southeast Airlines</a> (ASA) began Delta Connection service on March 1, 1984, from their hub in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, and soon had a substantial presence at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas-Fort_Worth_International_Airport" title="Dallas-Fort Worth International Airport">Dallas-Fort Worth International Airport</a>. ASA was a wholly owned subsidiary of Delta Air Lines under the Delta Connection, Inc., holding company from May 11, 1999, to September 7, 2005, when it was purchased by SkyWest, Inc, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/SkyWest_Airlines" title="SkyWest Airlines">SkyWest Airlines</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ransome_Airlines" title="Ransome Airlines">Ransome Airlines</a> operated Delta Connection flights from March 1, 1984 to June 1, 1986, when it was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan Am</a>. <a href="/wiki/Comair" title="Comair">Comair</a> began Delta Connection service on September 1, 1984. In January, 2000, Comair became a wholly owned subsidiary of Delta Air Lines. <a href="/wiki/Rio_Airways" title="Rio Airways">Rio Airways</a> operated Delta Connection flights from their hub in Dallas/Fort Worth from June 1, 1984 to December 14, 1986, when the airline declared bankruptcy. <a href="/wiki/Business_Express_Airlines" title="Business Express Airlines">Business Express Airlines</a> operated Delta Connection flights in the northeastern US and Canada from June 1, 1986 to March 15, 2000. The company was purchased by <a href="/wiki/AMR_Corporation" title="AMR Corporation">AMR Corporation</a> in 1999 and integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Envoy_Air" title="Envoy Air">American Eagle Airlines</a> system in 2000. Following the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Western_Airlines" title="Western Airlines">Western Airlines</a> by Delta Air Lines, <a href="/wiki/SkyWest_Airlines" title="SkyWest Airlines">SkyWest Airlines</a>, which had been operating code share service flying as Western Express for Western, became a Delta Connection carrier in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trans_States_Airlines" title="Trans States Airlines">Trans States Airlines</a> operated Delta Connection flights from March 1998 to March 31, 2000, mainly from their focus cities in Boston and New York.</p>
<p>On November 2, 2004 Atlantic Coast Airlines ended service as a Delta Connection Carrier. Atlantic Coast Airlines reinvented itself as a low fare carrier called <a href="/wiki/Independence_Air" title="Independence Air">Independence Air</a>, based at <a href="/wiki/Washington_Dulles_International_Airport" title="Washington Dulles International Airport">Washington Dulles International Airport</a>.</p>
<p>On December 22, 2004, Delta Air Lines announced that Republic Airways would order and operate 16 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_170" title="Embraer 170">Embraer 170</a> aircraft under the Delta Connection banner. Since then, it has been announced that Republic Airways subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_America" title="Shuttle America">Shuttle America</a> would operate the flights. The initial flight took place on September 1, 2005. On May 4, 2005, Delta Air Lines announced that <a href="/wiki/Mesa_Air_Group" title="Mesa Air Group">Mesa Air Group</a> subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Airlines" title="Freedom Airlines">Freedom Airlines</a> would operate up to 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_CRJ-200" title="Bombardier CRJ-200">Bombardier CRJ-200</a> aircraft under the Delta Connection banner beginning in October 2005. Shortly after the announcement, the decision was made for Freedom Airlines to operate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_ERJ_145" title="Embraer ERJ 145">Embraer ERJ 145</a> for Delta Connection instead of the CRJ. After a legal battle with Mesa Air Group, Delta and Freedom Airlines terminated their contract on September 1, 2010. On December 21, 2006, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Big_Sky_Airlines" title="Big Sky Airlines">Big Sky Airlines</a> would become a Delta Connection carrier, using eight <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_1900" title="Beechcraft 1900">Beechcraft 1900</a> turboprops out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Logan_International_Airport" title="Boston Logan International Airport">Boston Logan International Airport</a>.</p>
<p>On March 1, 2007, it was announced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ExpressJet_Airlines" title="ExpressJet Airlines">ExpressJet</a> would operate 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_ERJ_145" title="Embraer ERJ 145">Embraer ERJ 145</a>XR aircraft under the Delta Connection banner beginning in June 2007 on flights from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">Los Angeles International Airport</a>. It was later announced that ExpressJet would operate an additional eight aircraft as Delta Connection. On July 3, 2008, Delta and ExpressJet announced that they had terminated their agreement and that ExpressJet operations as Delta Connection would end by September 1, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> On April 30, 2007, it was announced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Airlines" title="Pinnacle Airlines">Pinnacle Airlines</a> would operate 16 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_CRJ-900" title="Bombardier CRJ-900">Bombardier CRJ-900</a> under the Delta Connection banner starting in December 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Merging_Delta_Connection_and_Northwest_Airlink">Merging Delta Connection and Northwest Airlink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Delta_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Merging Delta Connection and Northwest Airlink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The merger of <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a> meant that Northwest's regional brand, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlink" title="Northwest Airlink">Northwest Airlink</a>, would be merged into Delta Connection. The new Delta Connection would include the <a href="/wiki/Regional_airline" title="Regional airline">regional airlines</a> from both the original Delta and Northwest. On November 8, 2008, Delta and <a href="/wiki/Mesaba_Airlines" title="Mesaba Airlines">Mesaba Airlines</a>, a former fully owned regional subsidiary of Northwest Airlines that operated as Northwest Airlink, announced that the seven CRJ-900 aircraft previously operated by Freedom as well as eight new-order aircraft would be operated for Delta Connection beginning February 12, 2009.</p>
<p>Citing cost reductions, Delta Air Lines sold former Northwest regional subsidiary Mesaba Airlines on July 1, 2010 to <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Airlines_Corp." title="Pinnacle Airlines Corp.">Pinnacle Airlines Corp.</a> for $62 million. Its headquarters were moved to Pinnacle's in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memphis" title="Memphis">Memphis</a> on December 26, 2011. Mesaba merged its operations into Pinnacle on January 4, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The same day, <a href="/wiki/Trans_States_Holdings" title="Trans States Holdings">Trans States Holdings</a> purchased Compass Airlines for $20.5 million USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has maintained both regional operations with the airlines as of January 1, 2012.</p>
<p>Delta announced that it will add <a href="/wiki/In-flight_entertainment#Wi-Fi" title="In-flight entertainment">in-flight WiFi</a> to 223 Delta Connection aircraft beginning in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regional carrier <a href="/wiki/GoJet_Airlines" title="GoJet Airlines">GoJet Airlines</a>, also owned by Trans States Holdings, began operations from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit_Wayne_County_Metropolitan_Airport" title="Detroit Wayne County Metropolitan Airport">Detroit Wayne County Metropolitan Airport</a> to cities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a> using 15 CRJ-700 aircraft on January 11, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following a merger between <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Southeast_Airlines" title="Atlantic Southeast Airlines">Atlantic Southeast Airlines</a> (ASA) and <a href="/wiki/ExpressJet" title="ExpressJet">ExpressJet</a>, Delta Connection flights operated under the latter's name and ceased operations as ASA. All routes remained the same, but the flights began operating as ExpressJet beginning in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 25, 2012, Delta announced that its wholly owned subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Comair" title="Comair">Comair</a> would cease all operations at midnight on September 28, 2012.</p>
<p>On May 1, 2013, as a condition of exiting bankruptcy, Pinnacle Airlines became a subsidiary of Delta and was subsequently renamed Endeavor Air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 31, 2014 Chautauqua operated its last flight for Delta Connection. All aircraft and crew &amp; maintenance bases will be absorbed by the Shuttle America certificate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_Connection_Academy" title="Delta Connection Academy">Delta Connection Academy</a> is an airline flight school, formerly wholly owned by Delta Air Lines, Inc. until its sale in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readtiger.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-readtiger.com-14">[14]</a></sup> The academy is located in <a href="/wiki/Sanford,_Florida" title="Sanford, Florida">Sanford, Florida</a> on the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sanford_International_Airport" title="Orlando Sanford International Airport">Orlando Sanford International Airport</a>. The school serves all the Delta Connection carriers above, and has been known to train pilots for over 30 other airlines in the world. The school currently issues more FAA certificates than any other <a href="/wiki/Pilot_certification_in_the_United_States" title="Pilot certification in the United States">Part 141</a> school in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readtiger.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-readtiger.com-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_and_accidents">Incidents and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Delta_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Incidents and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>January 15, 1987: <a href="/wiki/Skywest_Airlines_Flight_1834" title="Skywest Airlines Flight 1834">SkyWest Airlines Flight 1834</a> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairchild_Metro" title="Fairchild Metro">Fairchild Metro</a> collided with a <a href="/wiki/Mooney_M20" title="Mooney M20">Mooney M20</a> transporting an instructor and a student, while on a flight between <a href="/wiki/Pocatello,_Idaho" title="Pocatello, Idaho">Pocatello</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_Utah" title="Salt Lake City, Utah">Salt Lake City</a> in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Kearns,_Utah" title="Kearns, Utah">Kearns, Utah</a>. All 10 people on Flight 1834 and the two occupants of the Mooney were killed. The accident was found to be a navigation error of the student pilot aboard the Mooney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>On February 1, 1991, SkyWest Flight 5569, an <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Swearingen_Metroliner" title="Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner">Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner</a>, was waiting for takeoff clearance on a runway at <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">Los Angeles International Airport</a> when <a href="/wiki/USAir_Flight_1493" title="USAir Flight 1493">USAir Flight 1493</a> collided with it. The ten passengers and two crew members onboard Flight 5569 were killed as well as twenty-two passengers and crew on USAir Flight 1493. The crash was blamed on the Air Traffic Controller who allowed the USAir plane to land on the same runway that the SkyWest flight was using.</li>
<li>On April 5, 1991, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Southeast_Airlines_Flight_2311" title="Atlantic Southeast Airlines Flight 2311">Atlantic Southeast Airlines Flight 2311</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Embraer_EMB_120_Brasilia" title="Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia">Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia</a>, crashed on approach to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glynco_Jetport" title="Glynco Jetport">Glynco Jetport</a> serving Brunswick, GA. Twenty passengers and three crew members onboard all died in the crash. The cause of the crash was an engine malfunction coupled with crew fatigue.</li>
<li>On August 21, 1995, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Southeast_Airlines_Flight_529" title="Atlantic Southeast Airlines Flight 529">Atlantic Southeast Airlines Flight 529</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Embraer_EMB_120_Brasilia" title="Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia">Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia</a>, crashed near Carrollton, Georgia. Officials determined that a propeller blade loss and inability to feather the remaining blades caused the accident, which killed 8 of the 28 passengers and crew on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoundedBird_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-WoundedBird-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BigStoryLog_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-BigStoryLog-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>On January 9, 1997, <a href="/wiki/Comair_Flight_3272" title="Comair Flight 3272">Comair Flight 3272</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Embraer_EMB_120_Brasilia" title="Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia">Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia</a>, crashed near Monroe, Michigan. The flight, which originated from Cincinnati, Ohio, was on approach to Detroit. All 29 passengers and crew were killed when the plane crashed 18 miles from the airport. The cause is listed to be the "FAA's failure to establish adequate aircraft certification standards for flight in icing conditions, the FAA's failure to ensure that an FAA/CTA-approved procedure for the accident airplane's deice system operation was implemented by U.S.-based air carriers, and the FAA's failure to require the establishment of adequate minimum airspeeds for icing conditions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight3272_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-flight3272-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>On August 27, 2006, <a href="/wiki/Comair_Flight_5191" title="Comair Flight 5191">Comair Flight 5191</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_CRJ-100" title="Bombardier CRJ-100">Bombardier CRJ-100</a>, crashed on takeoff at <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington, Kentucky</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Blue_Grass_Airport" title="Blue Grass Airport">Blue Grass Airport</a>, with 47 passengers and three crew members on board. Only the First Officer survived. The pilots took off from the wrong runway, which was not long enough for the aircraft.</li>
<li>On April 12, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlink" title="Northwest Airlink">Northwest Airlink</a> Flight 4712, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_CRJ200LR" title="Bombardier CRJ200LR">Bombardier CRJ200LR</a> from <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis%E2%80%93Saint_Paul_International_Airport" title="Minneapolis–Saint Paul International Airport">Minneapolis–Saint Paul International Airport</a> overran the runway when landing at <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Capital_Airport" title="Cherry Capital Airport">Cherry Capital Airport</a> (TVC), Traverse City, Michigan. The plane was damaged, but no one was injured. The NTSB determined that the cause of the accident was the "pilots’ decision to land at TVC without performing a landing distance assessment", which in turn was caused by fatigued pilots and unclear directions from the TVC controller tower. The report recommended more landing distance training, post-accident drug testing, and further criteria for runway closures in snow and ice conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flight_4712_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flight_4712-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
