<p><i><b>Dogma</b></i> is a 1999 American <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a>, written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Smith" title="Kevin Smith">Kevin Smith</a>, who also stars along with <a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Damon" title="Matt Damon">Matt Damon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linda_Fiorentino" title="Linda Fiorentino">Linda Fiorentino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rickman" title="Alan Rickman">Alan Rickman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bud_Cort" title="Bud Cort">Bud Cort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salma_Hayek" title="Salma Hayek">Salma Hayek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rock" title="Chris Rock">Chris Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Lee_(actor)" title="Jason Lee (actor)">Jason Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Carlin" title="George Carlin">George Carlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janeane_Garofalo" title="Janeane Garofalo">Janeane Garofalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alanis_Morissette" title="Alanis Morissette">Alanis Morissette</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jason_Mewes" title="Jason Mewes">Jason Mewes</a>. It is the fourth film in Smith's <a href="/wiki/View_Askewniverse" title="View Askewniverse">View Askewniverse</a> series. <a href="/wiki/Brian_O%27Halloran" title="Brian O'Halloran">Brian O'Halloran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Anderson" title="Jeff Anderson">Jeff Anderson</a>, stars of the first Askewniverse film <i><a href="/wiki/Clerks" title="Clerks">Clerks</a></i>, have <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo roles</a>, as do Smith regulars <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mosier" title="Scott Mosier">Scott Mosier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Ewell" title="Dwight Ewell">Dwight Ewell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Flanagan" title="Walt Flanagan">Walt Flanagan</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryan_Johnson_(filmmaker)" title="Bryan Johnson (filmmaker)">Bryan Johnson</a>.</p>
<p>The film's irreverent treatment of <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> triggered considerable controversy, even before its opening. The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(U.S.)" title="Catholic League (U.S.)">Catholic League</a> denounced it as "blasphemy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Organized protests delayed its release in many countries and led to at least two death threats against Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The plot revolves around two <a href="/wiki/Fallen_angel" title="Fallen angel">fallen angels</a> who plan to employ an alleged loophole in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogma_(Roman_Catholic)" title="Dogma (Roman Catholic)">Catholic dogma</a> to return to Heaven after being cast out by <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>; but as existence is founded on the principle that God is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infallible" title="Infallible">infallible</a>, their success would prove God wrong and thus undo all creation. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desposyni" title="Desposyni">last scion</a> and two prophets are sent by the <a href="/wiki/Seraph" title="Seraph">seraph</a> <a href="/wiki/Metatron" title="Metatron">Metatron</a> to stop them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_minor_View_Askewniverse_characters#Bartleby_and_Loki" title="List of minor View Askewniverse characters">Bartleby</a> (Affleck) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_minor_View_Askewniverse_characters#Bartleby_and_Loki" title="List of minor View Askewniverse characters">Loki</a> (Damon) are <a href="/wiki/Fallen_angel" title="Fallen angel">fallen angels</a>, eternally banished from <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a> to <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> for insubordination, after an inebriated Loki (with Bartleby's encouragement) resigned as the Angel of Death. In a newspaper article that arrives anonymously, the angels find a way home: The trendy Cardinal Ignatius Glick (Carlin) is rededicating his cathedral in <a href="/wiki/Red_Bank,_New_Jersey" title="Red Bank, New Jersey">Red Bank, New Jersey</a> in the image of the "<a href="/wiki/Buddy_Christ" title="Buddy Christ">Buddy Christ</a>". Anyone entering the cathedral during the rededication festivities will receive a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plenary_indulgence" title="Plenary indulgence">plenary indulgence</a>, remitting all sins, and permitting direct entry into Heaven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They receive encouragement from an unexpected source: the <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demon</a> <a href="/wiki/Azrael" title="Azrael">Azrael</a> (Lee), and the <a href="/wiki/Styx" title="Styx">Stygian</a> Triplets, three teenage hoodlums who serve Azrael in <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">Hell</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bethany_Sloane" title="Bethany Sloane">Bethany Sloane</a> (Fiorentino)—a depressed, <a href="/wiki/Infertility" title="Infertility">infertile</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divorced" title="Divorced">divorced</a> <a href="/wiki/Abortion_clinic" title="Abortion clinic">abortion clinic</a> counselor—attends a service at her church in Illinois. Donations are solicited for a campaign to stop a New Jersey hospital from disconnecting life support on John Doe Jersey (Cort), a homeless man who was beaten senseless by the Triplets. <a href="/wiki/Metatron" title="Metatron">Metatron</a> (Rickman)—a <a href="/wiki/Seraph" title="Seraph">seraph</a>, and the voice of God—appears to Bethany in a pillar of fire and explains that if Bartleby and Loki succeed in re-entering Heaven, they will overrule the word of God, disprove the fundamental concept of God's <a href="/wiki/Omnipotence" title="Omnipotence">omnipotence</a>, and nullify all of existence. Bethany, aided by two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophets" title="Prophets">prophets</a>, must stop the angels and save the universe.</p>
<p>Now a target, Bethany is attacked by the Triplets, who are driven off by the two foretold prophets—drug-dealing stoners <a href="/wiki/Jay_and_Silent_Bob" title="Jay and Silent Bob">Jay and Silent Bob</a> (Mewes and Smith). Bethany and the prophets are joined by Rufus (Rock), the thirteenth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_apostles" title="Twelve apostles">apostle</a> (never mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, he says, because he is <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black</a>), and Serendipity (Hayek), the Muse of creative inspiration, now working in a strip club in search of inspiration of her own. Azrael summons the Golgothan—a vile creature made of human excrement—but Silent Bob immobilizes it with aerosol air freshener.</p>
<p>On a train to New Jersey, a drunken Bethany reveals her mission to Bartleby, who tries to kill her; Bob throws the angels off the train. Bartleby and Loki now realize the consequences of their scheme; Loki wants no part of destroying all existence, but Bartleby remains angry at God for his expulsion—and for granting <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a> to humans while demanding servitude of angels—and resolves to proceed.</p>
<p>In New Jersey, Bethany demands answers: Who told the angels about the plenary indulgence loophole? Why must she stop them? Why hasn't God intervened himself? Metatron explains that God's whereabouts are unknown; he disappeared while visiting New Jersey in human form to play <a href="/wiki/Skee_ball" title="Skee ball">skee ball</a>. So the task falls to Bethany, because she is the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_of_Jesus" title="Brothers of Jesus">last scion</a>, the great-great-great-great-great-great-great-great-grandniece of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>.</p>
<p>The group attempts in vain to persuade Glick to cancel the celebration; a frustrated Jay steals one of Glick's golf clubs. Their last recourse is to keep the angels out of the cathedral; but Azrael and the Triplets trap them in a bar. Azrael reveals that he sent the news clipping to the angels; he would rather not exist at all than spend eternity in Hell. Silent Bob kills Azrael with Glick's blessed golf club. Bethany blesses the bar sink, turning its contents to <a href="/wiki/Holy_water" title="Holy water">holy water</a>, and the others drown the Triplets in it. At the cathedral, Bartleby is venting eons of frustration by killing Glick and his parishioners; when Loki attempts to stop him he tears off Loki's wings, making him mortal. The protectors block Bartleby's path to the church; Bartleby kills Loki and fights off the others; Jay opens fire with an <a href="/wiki/Uzi" title="Uzi">Uzi</a>, shooting off Bartleby's wings.</p>
<p>In a desperate attempt to seduce Bethany before existence ends, Jay mentions John Doe Jersey, and Bethany puts it all together. As the others continue battling Bartleby, Bethany and Bob race across the street to the hospital, where Bethany disconnects John's life support, liberating God, but killing herself. At the cathedral entrance, God manifests before Bartleby as a woman (Morissette), and annihilates him with the power of her voice. Bob arrives with Bethany's lifeless body; God resurrects her and conceives a child—the new last scion—within her womb. God, Metatron, Rufus, and Serendipity return to Heaven, leaving Bethany and the prophets to reflect on the past, and future.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smith and his production partner <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mosier" title="Scott Mosier">Scott Mosier</a> assembled a group of visual artists to realize their concept of a surreal, abstract environment "somewhere between reality and unreality": production designer Robert Holtzman, special effects supervisor Charles Belardinelli, creature effects supervisor Vincent Guastini, costume designer Abigail Murray, and director of photography <a href="/wiki/Robert_Yeoman" title="Robert Yeoman">Robert Yeoman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-movie.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">Locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Triplets' attack on John Doe Jersey was filmed on the <a href="/wiki/Boardwalk" title="Boardwalk">boardwalk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Asbury_Park,_New_Jersey" title="Asbury Park, New Jersey">Asbury Park, New Jersey</a>. The remainder of the film was shot in and around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. The Mexican restaurant in which Metatron explains Bethany's mission was the Franklin Inn in <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Park,_Pennsylvania" title="Franklin Park, Pennsylvania">Franklin Park</a>, north of Pittsburgh. Serendipity's pole dance and the Golgothan confrontation took place at the Park View Cafe (since renamed Crazy Mocha) on East North Avenue in Pittsburgh. The heroes plan their strategy in the Grand Concourse Restaurant in the restored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_and_Lake_Erie_Railroad_Station" title="Pittsburgh and Lake Erie Railroad Station">Pittsburgh and Lake Erie Railroad Station</a>. St Michael’s Church, site of the apocalyptic climax, is the Saints Peter and Paul Church in <a href="/wiki/East_Liberty_(Pittsburgh)" title="East Liberty (Pittsburgh)">East Liberty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critics expressed surprise at the film's eclectic casting, which Smith said was done deliberately to emphasize the contrasts between characters—Rickman as the powerful Metatron, for example, opposite Mewes as the hopelessly verbose stoner Jay, "...a Shakespearean trained actor of the highest order next to a dude from New Jersey." Smith warned Mewes that he would have to take his acting to a higher level. "I really impressed upon him that he had to be prepared for this movie. 'There are real actors in this one,' we kept telling him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-movie.com-6">[6]</a></sup> In response, Mewes memorized not only his own dialogue but the entire screenplay, because he "didn't want to piss off that Rickman dude".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other unorthodox casting decisions included Mexican actress Salma Hayek as Serendipity—"the [Muse] who throughout history inspired all the geniuses of art and music, like Mozart and Michelangelo, and never got any of the credit"—and singer-songwriter Alanis Morissette as God. "There's a Zen Buddhist serenity to Alanis that calls to mind something otherworldly," Smith explained. "She's definitely ethereal in nature, even when not speaking, and she carries an air about her that played into the role."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie.com_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-movie.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deleted_scene">Deleted scene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Deleted scene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the film's official website, Smith described a scene that did not make the final cut: a climactic face-off in the hospital between Silent Bob, a badly burned and half-decomposed Triplet, and the Golgothan. The battle was to end with the Triplet killing Bethany (temporarily), and God, newly liberated, transforming the Golgothan into flowers. Test audiences felt there was "too much Golgothan", and the film's run time already exceeded two hours, so the scene was eliminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">Soundtrack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The soundtrack album accompanying the film was released in the United States on November 2, 1999, by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a>. It features the film's orchestral score by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Shore" title="Howard Shore">Howard Shore</a>, who receives sole credit on the album cover, and the song "Still" by <a href="/wiki/Alanis_Morissette" title="Alanis Morissette">Alanis Morissette</a>, who wrote and produced it for the film. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> gave the album a positive review, describing the score as "alternately melodramatic and humorous" and "rich, effective".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/London_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="London Philharmonic Orchestra">London Philharmonic Orchestra</a> performed tracks two through ten.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was nominated for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_Spirit_Award" title="Independent Spirit Award">Independent Spirit Award</a> for Best Screenplay as well as a <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction_and_Fantasy_Writers_of_America" title="Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of America">Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of America</a> honor for Best Screenplay.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i>Dogma</i> was the third-highest-grossing film in its opening weekend, behind <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bone_Collector" title="The Bone Collector">The Bone Collector</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon:_The_First_Movie" title="Pokémon: The First Movie">Pokémon: The First Movie</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The film grossed a domestic total of $30,652,890 from a modest $10 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critic analysis of the film was generally positive; it has a 67% "fresh" rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> with the consensus: "Provocative and audacious, <i>Dogma</i> is an uneven but thoughtful religious satire that's both respectful and irreverent." It fared better with fans, ranking 84% by the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film received a rating of 62 percent based on 36 reviews, and 7.7/10 by fans, based on 35 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some religious groups—in particular the activist <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(U.S.)" title="Catholic League (U.S.)">Catholic League</a>—angrily denounced the film as "blasphemous".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other groups staged protests outside theaters screening the film;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> but film critic <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>—himself a Catholic—noted that there was no official objection from the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> itself. "We are actually free in this country to disagree about religion," Ebert wrote, "and blasphemy is not a crime." Ebert awarded the film 3.5 out of 4 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogerebert_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogerebert-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was screened, but was not entered in competition, at the <a href="/wiki/1999_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1999 Cannes Film Festival">1999 Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-festival-cannes.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-festival-cannes.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel">Sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogma_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late November 2005, Smith was asked about a possible <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> on the ViewAskew.com message boards:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">So weird you should ask this, because ever since <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11</a>, I have been thinking about a sequel of sorts. I mean, the worst <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorist</a> attack on American soil was religiously bent. In the wake of said attack, the leader of the "Free World" outed himself as pretty damned <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2004" title="United States presidential election, 2004">last election</a>, rather than <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">a quagmire war abroad</a>, the big issue was whether or not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_marriage" title="Gay marriage">gay marriage</a> was moral. Back when I made <i>Dogma</i>, I always maintained that another movie about religion wouldn't be forthcoming, as <i>Dogma</i> was the product of 28 years of religious and spiritual meditation, and I'd kinda shot my wad on the subject. Now? I think I might have more to say. And, yes, the Last Scion would be at the epicenter of it. And she'd have to be played by Alanis. And we'd need a bigger budget, because the entire third act would be the <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse" title="Apocalypse">Apocalypse</a>. Scary thing is this: the film would have to touch on <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. And unlike the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(U.S.)" title="Catholic League (U.S.)">Catholic League</a>, when those cats don't like what you do, they issue a <a href="/wiki/Fatwa" title="Fatwa">death warrant</a> on your ass. And now that I've got a family, I'm not as free to stir the shit-pot as I was when I was single, back when I made <i>Dogma</i>. I mean, now I've gotta think about more than my own safety and well-being. But regardless – yeah, a <i>Dogma</i> followup's been swimming around in my head for some time now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Over a decade later, there has apparently been no further discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
