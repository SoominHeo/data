<p><b>Donna Douglas</b> (born <b>Doris Ione Smith</b>; September 26, 1932 – January 1, 2015) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actress" title="Actress">actress</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singer" title="Singer">singer</a>, known for her role as <a href="/wiki/The_Beverly_Hillbillies#Elly_May_Clampett" title="The Beverly Hillbillies">Elly May Clampett</a> in <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beverly_Hillbillies" title="The Beverly Hillbillies">The Beverly Hillbillies</a></i> (1962–1971). Following her acting career, Douglas became a real estate agent, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">Gospel singer</a>, inspirational speaker, and author of books for children and adults.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Douglas was born <b>Doris Ione Smith</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in the community of <a href="/wiki/Pride,_Louisiana" title="Pride, Louisiana">Pride</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Baton_Rouge_Parish,_Louisiana" title="East Baton Rouge Parish, Louisiana">East Baton Rouge Parish, Louisiana</a>, on September 26, 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findagrave_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-findagrave-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVoice_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SVoice-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britt-5">[5]</a></sup> The younger of two children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVoice_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-SVoice-3">[3]</a></sup> she was the only daughter of Emmett Ratcliff Smith, Sr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[Footnotes 1]</a></sup> and his wife, Elma (née Robinson).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[Footnotes 2]</a></sup> Her birth name is listed both as Dorothy Smith and Doris Smith in various articles.</p>
<p>Douglas attended <a href="/wiki/Redemptorist_High_School" title="Redemptorist High School">St. Gerard Catholic High School</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britt_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britt-5">[5]</a></sup> where she played softball and basketball<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britt_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britt-5">[5]</a></sup> and was a member of the school's first graduating class. Douglas was named "Miss Baton Rouge"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britt_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Britt-5">[5]</a></sup> and "Miss New Orleans" in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Douglas moved to New York City to pursue a career in show business and started as an illustration model for toothpaste advertisements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> She was featured as the "Letters Girl" on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Perry_Como" title="Perry Como">The Perry Como Show</a></i> in 1957 and as the "Billboard Girl" on NBC's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Steve_Allen_Show" title="The Steve Allen Show">The Steve Allen Show</a></i> in 1959. These and other television appearances led New York photographers and newspaper reporters to award her the "Miss By-line" crown, which she wore on CBS's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hal_B._Wallis" title="Hal B. Wallis">Hal B. Wallis</a> saw the Sullivan episode and cast her in the role of Marjorie Burke in the movie drama <i><a href="/wiki/Career_(1959_film)" title="Career (1959 film)">Career</a></i> (1959), starring <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Franciosa" title="Anthony Franciosa">Anthony Franciosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_MacLaine" title="Shirley MacLaine">Shirley MacLaine</a>. This was followed by a bit part in the <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical comedy</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Li%27l_Abner_(1959_film)" title="Li'l Abner (1959 film)">Li'l Abner</a></i> (1959) and the role of a secretary in the comedy/romance <i><a href="/wiki/Lover_Come_Back_(1961_film)" title="Lover Come Back (1961 film)">Lover Come Back</a></i> (1961), starring <a href="/wiki/Rock_Hudson" title="Rock Hudson">Rock Hudson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doris_Day" title="Doris Day">Doris Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_News_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC_News-11">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>She made numerous television appearances in the late 1950s and early 1960s, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)">The Twilight Zone</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Eye_of_the_Beholder" title="The Eye of the Beholder">The Eye of the Beholder</a>" (1960).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_News_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABC_News-11">[9]</a></sup> She played Barbara Simmons in four 1961 episodes of the CBS detective series <i><a href="/wiki/Checkmate_(TV_series)" title="Checkmate (TV series)">Checkmate</a></i>. Her other credits included in <i><a href="/wiki/Sheriff_of_Cochise" title="Sheriff of Cochise">U.S. Marshal</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tightrope_(TV_series)" title="Tightrope (TV series)">Tightrope</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Ozzie_and_Harriet" title="The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet">The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bachelor_Father_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Bachelor Father (U.S. TV series)">Bachelor Father</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Adam-12" title="Adam-12">Adam-12</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Route_66_(TV_series)" title="Route 66 (TV series)">Route 66</a></i>. Douglas also appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Thriller_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Thriller (U.S. TV series)">Thriller</a></i>, season 1, episode 16, "The Hungry Glass".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Beverly_Hillbillies"><i>The Beverly Hillbillies</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Beverly Hillbillies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although Douglas was an active actress in the 1960s, she was still relatively unknown when selected from among 500 young actresses to work alongside veteran actor and dancer <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Ebsen" title="Buddy Ebsen">Buddy Ebsen</a> on <i>The Beverly Hillbillies</i>. This series ran for nine consecutive seasons, beginning in 1962 and ending in 1971. Continually <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">typecast</a> as a result of her <i>Hillbillies</i> role, Douglas decided to focus on her career as a gospel singer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the 1966 summer hiatus of <i>The Beverly Hillbillies</i>, Douglas made her only starring feature film appearance, cast as Frankie in <a href="/wiki/Fred_de_Cordova" title="Fred de Cordova">Fred de Cordova</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Frankie_and_Johnny_(1966_film)" title="Frankie and Johnny (1966 film)">Frankie and Johnny</a></i> (1966) alongside <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Return_of_the_Beverly_Hillbillies_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Return_of_the_Beverly_Hillbillies-13">[11]</a></sup> The film proved popular and is among Presley's most frequently televised movies,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but it did little to advance Douglas' big-screen career.</p>
<p>With the 1973 death of <i>Hillbillies</i> co-star <a href="/wiki/Irene_Ryan" title="Irene Ryan">Irene Ryan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Max_Baer_Jr." title="Max Baer Jr.">Max Baer Jr.</a>'s refusal to participate, Douglas joined <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Kulp" title="Nancy Kulp">Nancy Kulp</a> and Buddy Ebsen in 1981 as the only original cast members to appear in the reunion movie, <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Beverly_Hillbillies" title="Return of the Beverly Hillbillies">Return of the Beverly Hillbillies</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Return_of_the_Beverly_Hillbillies_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Return_of_the_Beverly_Hillbillies-13">[11]</a></sup> Douglas was a guest star on a number of other television programs and the subject of paper dolls, dolls, coloring books, and various toys during the height of the show's popularity. In a 2003 interview with "Confessions of a Pop Culture Addict", she summed up her views on the role: "Elly May was like a slice out of my life. She is a wonderful little door opener for me because people love her, and they love the Hillbillies. Even to this day it's shown every day somewhere. But, as with any abilities, she may open a door for you, but you have to have substance or integrity to advance you through that door."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popcultureaddict.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-popcultureaddict.com-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, Douglas and Baer attended Buddy Ebsen's 84th birthday celebration in <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_California" title="Beverly Hills, California">Beverly Hills, California</a>. In 1993, Douglas, Ebsen, and Baer reunited on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jerry_Springer_Show" title="The Jerry Springer Show">The Jerry Springer Show</a></i>, and for a final time in a CBS-TV television special, <i>The Legend of The Beverly Hillbillies</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In December 2010, Mattel released a new collection of three <a href="/wiki/Barbie" title="Barbie">Barbies</a> called the Classic TV Collection. These dolls were <a href="/wiki/List_of_Bewitched_characters#Samantha_Stephens" title="List of Bewitched characters">Samantha Stephens</a> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Bewitched" title="Bewitched">Bewitched</a></i>, played by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Montgomery" title="Elizabeth Montgomery">Elizabeth Montgomery</a>), Jeannie (from <i><a href="/wiki/I_Dream_of_Jeannie" title="I Dream of Jeannie">I Dream of Jeannie</a></i>, played by <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Eden" title="Barbara Eden">Barbara Eden</a>) and Elly May Clampett.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_acting_career">Post acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Douglas received her real estate license after <i>The Beverly Hillbillies</i> finished production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVoice_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-SVoice-3">[3]</a></sup> She did not work in that field long, however, as she remained in show business and found other projects.</p>
<p>Douglas frequently performed as a <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> singer and was a speaker at church groups, youth groups, schools and colleges across the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVoice_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-SVoice-3">[3]</a></sup> One focus of her charitable work was speaking in support of various Christian children's homes, mostly in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">American South</a>. She appeared at <a href="/wiki/Fan_convention" title="Fan convention">conventions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade_fair" title="Trade fair">trade fairs</a>. She recorded several gospel albums, the first released in 1982,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and recorded a few minor country music records during the 1970s and 1980s.</p>
<p>Douglas wrote and published a religious-based <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children's literature">children's book</a> titled <i>Donna's Critters &amp; Kids: Children's Stories with a Bible Touch.</i> The book included Bible stories featuring animals combined with a <a href="/wiki/Coloring_book" title="Coloring book">coloring book</a>. In November 2011, she released a new children's book titled <i>Miss Donna's Mulberry Acres Farm</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bweek_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-bweek-16">[14]</a></sup> In 2013, Douglas published a cookbook, <i>Southern Favorites with a Taste of Hollywood</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> which collects recipes of <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_the_Southern_United_States" title="Cuisine of the Southern United States">Southern cooking</a> from show business friends and colleagues such as Buddy Ebsen, <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Diller" title="Phyllis Diller">Phyllis Diller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Harper" title="Valerie Harper">Valerie Harper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Reynolds" title="Debbie Reynolds">Debbie Reynolds</a>. The book also has a section on good manners called "Hollywood Social Graces".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVoice_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-SVoice-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Douglas married her first husband, Roland Bourgeois, Jr., in 1949, with whom she had her only child, Danny Bourgeois, in 1954. The couple divorced that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> She married <a href="/wiki/Robert_M._Leeds" title="Robert M. Leeds">Robert M. Leeds</a>, director of <i>The Beverly Hillbillies</i>, in 1971; they divorced in 1980.</p>
<p>Douglas remained a close friend of Buddy Ebsen for 32 years. In a 2011 interview with <i>The Lincoln Times-News</i>, she described Ebsen as "a wonderful man, very much like my own father, a quiet, reserved, and caring person".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lincolntimesnews.com_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lincolntimesnews.com-19">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Douglas' mother, Elma Smith, and Buddy Ebsen died. Douglas and Baer visited Ebsen in the hospital, and following his death both delivered a eulogy at his funeral. A decade later, Douglas revealed the depth of her feelings for Ebsen in an interview with "Confessions of a Pop Culture Addict": "I loved Buddy Ebsen. He reminded me so much of my own dad. Most of my scenes were with Buddy, and most of Max's with Irene. Buddy was just a wonderful man. I related to him so easily. The night before he died, Max and I went up to the hospital to see him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popcultureaddict.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-popcultureaddict.com-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sister_Act"><i>Sister Act</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sister Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On June 10, 1993, Douglas and her partner Curt Wilson in Associated Artists Entertainment, Inc., filed a $200 million lawsuit against <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Pictures" title="Walt Disney Pictures">Disney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whoopi_Goldberg" title="Whoopi Goldberg">Whoopi Goldberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bette_Midler" title="Bette Midler">Bette Midler</a>, their production companies, and <a href="/wiki/Creative_Artists_Agency" title="Creative Artists Agency">Creative Artists Agency</a> claiming that <i><a href="/wiki/Sister_Act" title="Sister Act">Sister Act</a></i> was plagiarized from a book, <i>A Nun in the Closet</i>, owned by the partners. Douglas and Wilson claimed that in 1985 they had developed a screenplay from the book.</p>
<p>The lawsuit claimed that there were more than 100 similarities and plagiarisms between the movie and the book/screenplay owned by Douglas and Wilson. The lawsuit claimed that the developed screenplay had been submitted to Disney, Goldberg, and Midler three times during 1987 and 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> In 1994, Douglas and Wilson declined a $1 million offer to settle the case. The judge found in favor of Walt Disney Pictures and the other defendants. Wilson stated at the time, "They would have had to copy our stuff verbatim for us to prevail."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mattel">Mattel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mattel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On May 4, 2011, Douglas filed a federal lawsuit claiming that <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> and CBS Consumer Products used her name and likeness for a Barbie doll in the Classic TV Collection without her authorization. The suit alleged that packaging for the "Elly May" Barbie doll featured a photo of her portraying the character. Douglas maintained she never endorsed the doll nor gave Mattel permission to use her name to promote its sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> On December 27, 2011, Douglas settled her suit against CBS Consumer Products and Mattel, in which she had been seeking at least $75,000. In the lawsuit, Douglas claimed that CBS and Mattel needed her approval to design the doll, while CBS and Mattel maintained that they didn't need her consent or approval because the network holds exclusive rights to the character. Details of the settlement were confidential; both sides claimed to be content with the outcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bweek_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-bweek-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years_and_death">Final years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Final years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to her frequent traveling for celebrity appearances and speeches, Douglas enjoyed gardening, spending time with friends and family, and answering her <a href="/wiki/Fan_mail" title="Fan mail">fan mail</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVoice_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-SVoice-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Douglas died at Baton Rouge General Hospital, aged 82, on January 1, 2015, from <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrobit_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrobit-23">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stegall-WAFB_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stegall-WAFB-24">[22]</a></sup> Her interment was in <a href="/wiki/East_Feliciana_Parish,_Louisiana" title="East Feliciana Parish, Louisiana">East Feliciana Parish, Louisiana</a>'s Bluff Creek Cemetery.</p>
<p>Charlene Smith, Douglas' niece by marriage, said that Douglas returned to live in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Baton_Rouge_Parish" title="East Baton Rouge Parish">East Baton Rouge Parish</a> in about 2005: "She was always happy, always beautiful. You always saw her with all her makeup on. She never looked her age."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wirtandgallo_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-wirtandgallo-25">[23]</a></sup> Smith said that on Christmas 2014 Douglas gave her a Bible that she had signed. Douglas often added scriptural verses to her gifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wirtandgallo_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-wirtandgallo-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
