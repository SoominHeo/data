<p><b>Donald MacKay Fraser</b> (born February 20, 1924) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_politician" title="American politician">American politician</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_M._Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fraser was born in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, to Everett and Lois Fraser, immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. His father studied law at Harvard, began teaching at <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">George Washington University</a> and became dean of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Law_School" title="University of Minnesota Law School">University of Minnesota Law School</a> in 1920. Fraser graduated from University High School in 1941 and that year, he entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a>. During college, he was a member of the varsity swimming team.</p>
<p>Having joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROTC" title="ROTC">ROTC</a>, he was placed on active duty in July 1942 and continued his naval studies on campus until February 1944, when he was commissioned an officer and sent to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean_theater_of_World_War_II" title="Pacific Ocean theater of World War II">Pacific Theater</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Fraser worked as a <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> officer into the peacetime that followed, ending in 1946. In June 1946 Fraser returned to Minneapolis to study law at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Law_School" title="University of Minnesota Law School">University of Minnesota Law School</a>.</p>
<p>Fraser served as a member of the <i>Minnesota Law Review</i> and wrote a law review article on the illegality of racial covenants for land, a position supported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Supreme_Court" title="US Supreme Court">US Supreme Court</a> in 1948. Fraser earned his law degree and was admitted to the bar, the same year. He joined the politically active firm of Larson, Loevinger, Lindquist, Freeman, and Fraser. Fraser engaged in general law practice and served as municipal attorney for the suburban community of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Center,_Minnesota" title="Brooklyn Center, Minnesota">Brooklyn Center, Minnesota</a>. He married <a href="/wiki/Arvonne_Fraser" title="Arvonne Fraser">Arvonne Skelton</a> in 1950 and the following year, they welcomed the first of their six children: Thomas, Mary, John, Lois, Anne, and Jean (born in that order).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_M._Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="693" data-file-width="467" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg/100px-1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg/150px-1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg/200px-1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1973_Congressional_Pictorial_Donald_Fraser.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1973, <i>Congressional Pictorial Directory</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1954, Fraser was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Senate" title="Minnesota Senate">Minnesota Senate</a> and served for eight years. In 1962, he was elected to the House of Representatives from Minnesota's Fifth District. He served there in the <a href="/wiki/88th_United_States_Congress" title="88th United States Congress">88th</a>, <a href="/wiki/89th_United_States_Congress" title="89th United States Congress">89th</a>, <a href="/wiki/90th_United_States_Congress" title="90th United States Congress">90th</a>, <a href="/wiki/91st_United_States_Congress" title="91st United States Congress">91st</a>, <a href="/wiki/92nd_United_States_Congress" title="92nd United States Congress">92nd</a>, <a href="/wiki/93rd_United_States_Congress" title="93rd United States Congress">93rd</a>, <a href="/wiki/94th_United_States_Congress" title="94th United States Congress">94th</a>, and <a href="/wiki/95th_United_States_Congress" title="95th United States Congress">95th congresses</a>, from January 3, 1963 until January 3, 1979. He gave up his seat to run for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Senate" title="US Senate">US Senate</a>. He narrowly lost the 1978 Senate primary election to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Short" title="Bob Short">Bob Short</a>, who then lost in the general election to <a href="/wiki/David_Durenberger" title="David Durenberger">David Durenberger</a>.</p>
<p>Fraser served as president of <a href="/wiki/Americans_for_Democratic_Action" title="Americans for Democratic Action">Americans for Democratic Action</a> from 1974 to 1976. He was elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> of Minneapolis in 1979, taking office on January 1, 1980. His first mayoral term was two years, and he was subsequently reelected to three four-year terms. He was the longest-serving mayor in Minneapolis history.</p>
<p>Fraser left office on December 31, 1993, succeeded by the city's first female and first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> mayor, <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Sayles_Belton" title="Sharon Sayles Belton">Sharon Sayles Belton</a>.</p>
<p>He served as a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minnesota_Democratic-Farmer-Labor_Party" title="Minnesota Democratic-Farmer-Labor Party">Minnesota Democratic-Farmer-Labor Party</a>. Arvonne Fraser ran for lieutenant governor in 1986.</p>
