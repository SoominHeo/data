<p><b>Donald David Dixon Ronald O’Connor</b> (August 28, 1925 – September 27, 2003) was an American dancer, singer, and actor who came to fame in a series of movies in which he co-starred alternately with <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Jean" title="Gloria Jean">Gloria Jean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Ryan" title="Peggy Ryan">Peggy Ryan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Francis_the_Talking_Mule" title="Francis the Talking Mule">Francis the Talking Mule</a>.</p>
<p>He is best known today for his role as Don Lockwood's friend and colleague Cosmo Brown in <i><a href="/wiki/Singin%27_in_the_Rain" title="Singin' in the Rain">Singin' in the Rain</a></i> (1952).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_O%27Connor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though he considered <a href="/wiki/Danville,_Illinois" title="Danville, Illinois">Danville, Illinois</a> to be his hometown, O’Connor was born in St. Elizabeth Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. His parents, Effie Irene (née Crane) and John Edward "Chuck" O'Connor, were <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">vaudeville</a> entertainers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scribner_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-scribner-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> His father's family was from <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>, Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-current_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-current-3">[3]</a></sup> When O'Connor was only a few years old, he and his sister Arlene were in a car crash outside a theater in <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford, Connecticut</a>; O'Connor survived, but his sister was killed. Several weeks later, his father died of a heart attack while dancing on stage in <a href="/wiki/Brockton,_Massachusetts" title="Brockton, Massachusetts">Brockton, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-severo-4">[4]</a></sup> O'Connor at the time was being held in the arms of the theater manager, Mr. Morris Sims.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_O%27Connor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Connor began performing in movies in 1937. He appeared opposite <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Sing_You_Sinners_(film)" title="Sing You Sinners (film)">Sing You Sinners</a></i> at age 12. <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> used him in both <a href="/wiki/B_movies_(Hollywood_Golden_Age)" title="B movies (Hollywood Golden Age)">A and B films</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Sawyer,_Detective" title="Tom Sawyer, Detective">Tom Sawyer, Detective</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Beau_Geste_(1939_film)" title="Beau Geste (1939 film)">Beau Geste</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-severo-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1940, when he had outgrown child roles, he returned to vaudeville.</p>
<p>In 1941, O’Connor signed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>, where he began by appearing in seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-picture" title="B-picture">B-picture</a> musicals in a row, starting with <i><a href="/wiki/What%27s_Cookin%27%3F" title="What's Cookin'?">What's Cookin'?</a></i> (1942) and ending with <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_Big_(1943_film)" title="Mister Big (1943 film)">Mister Big</a></i> (1943).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Ryan" title="Peggy Ryan">Peggy Ryan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Jean" title="Gloria Jean">Gloria Jean</a> were in four each. Before <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_Big_(1943_film)" title="Mister Big (1943 film)">Mister Big</a></i> (1943) was released, O’Connor’s popularity soared. Universal added $50,000 in musical numbers to the films and promoted the "B" movie to "A" status.</p>

<p>On his 18th birthday in August 1943, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> O'Connor was drafted into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>. Before he reported for induction on Feb. 6, 1944, Universal already had four O’Connor films completed. They rushed production to complete three more by that date. With a backlog of seven features, deferred openings kept O’Connor‘s screen presence uninterrupted during the two years he was overseas. Upon his return, a merger in 1946 had reorganized the studio as Universal-International.</p>
<p>In 1949, he played the lead role in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_(1950_film)" title="Francis (1950 film)">Francis</a></i>, the story of a soldier befriended by a talking mule. The film was a huge success. As a consequence, his musical career was constantly interrupted by production of one <i>Francis</i> film per year until 1955. O'Connor later said the films "were fun to make. Actually, they were quite challenging. I had to play straight in order to convince the audience that the mule could talk."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1952 O'Connor signed a three-picture deal with Paramount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was because of the <i>Francis</i> series that O'Connor missed playing Bing Crosby's partner in <i><a href="/wiki/White_Christmas_(film)" title="White Christmas (film)">White Christmas</a></i>. O'Connor was unavailable because he contracted an illness transmitted by the mule, and was replaced in the film by <a href="/wiki/Danny_Kaye" title="Danny Kaye">Danny Kaye</a>.</p>
<p>O'Connor's role as Cosmo the piano player in <i><a href="/wiki/Singin%27_in_the_Rain" title="Singin' in the Rain">Singin' in the Rain</a></i> earned him a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a> for Best Performance by an Actor in a Comedy or Musical. The film featured his memorable rendition of "<a href="/wiki/Make_%27Em_Laugh" title="Make 'Em Laugh">Make 'Em Laugh</a>". O'Connor said he smoked 4 packs of cigarettes a day during filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another notable role was as Tim Donahue in the <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> musical <i><a href="/wiki/There%27s_No_Business_Like_Show_Business" title="There's No Business Like Show Business">There's No Business Like Show Business</a></i> (1954), which featured <a href="/wiki/Irving_Berlin" title="Irving Berlin">Irving Berlin</a>'s music and also starred with <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Merman" title="Ethel Merman">Ethel Merman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitzi_Gaynor" title="Mitzi Gaynor">Mitzi Gaynor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johnnie_Ray" title="Johnnie Ray">Johnnie Ray</a>.</p>
<p>O'Connor was a regular host of <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colgate_Comedy_Hour" title="Colgate Comedy Hour">Colgate Comedy Hour</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severo_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-severo-4">[4]</a></sup> He hosted a color television special on NBC in 1957, one of the earliest color programs to be preserved on a color <a href="/wiki/Kinescope" title="Kinescope">kinescope</a>; an excerpt of the telecast was included in NBC's 50th anniversary special in 1976. In 1954, he starred in his own television series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Donald_O%27Connor_Show" title="The Donald O'Connor Show">The Donald O'Connor Show</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>. In 1968, O'Connor hosted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_syndication" title="Television syndication">syndicated</a> <a href="/wiki/Talk_show" title="Talk show">talk show</a> also called <i>The Donald O'Connor Show</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He suffered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>O'Connor overcame <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a> after being hospitalized for three months after collapsing in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severo_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-severo-4">[4]</a></sup> His career had a boost when he hosted the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, which earned him two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy" title="Primetime Emmy">Primetime Emmy</a> nominations. He appeared as a gaslight-era entertainer in the 1981 film <i><a href="/wiki/Ragtime_(film)" title="Ragtime (film)">Ragtime</a></i>, notable for similar encore performances by <a href="/wiki/James_Cagney" title="James Cagney">James Cagney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pat_O%27Brien_(actor)" title="Pat O'Brien (actor)">Pat O'Brien</a>. It was his first feature film role in 16 years.</p>
<p>O'Connor appeared in the short-lived <i><a href="/wiki/Bring_Back_Birdie" title="Bring Back Birdie">Bring Back Birdie</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> in 1981 and continued to make film and television appearances into the 1990s, including the <a href="/wiki/Robin_Williams" title="Robin Williams">Robin Williams</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Toys_(film)" title="Toys (film)">Toys</a></i> as the president of a toy-making company. He had guest roles in 1996 in a pair of popular TV comedy series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nanny" title="The Nanny">The Nanny</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Frasier" title="Frasier">Frasier</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1998, he received a Golden Palm Star on the <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs,_California" title="Palm Springs, California">Palm Springs, California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs_Walk_of_Stars" title="Palm Springs Walk of Stars">Walk of Stars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stars_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-stars-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>O'Connor's last feature film was the <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lemmon" title="Jack Lemmon">Jack Lemmon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Walter_Matthau" title="Walter Matthau">Walter Matthau</a> comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Out_to_Sea" title="Out to Sea">Out to Sea</a></i>, in which he played a dance host on a cruise ship. O’Connor was still making public appearances well into 2003.</p>
<p>The most distinctive characteristic of O'Connor's dancing style was its athleticism, for which he had few rivals. Yet it was his boyish charm that audiences found most engaging, and which remained an appealing aspect of his personality throughout his career. In his early Universal films, O'Connor closely mimicked the smart alec, fast-talking personality of <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Rooney" title="Mickey Rooney">Mickey Rooney</a> of rival MGM Studio. For <i><a href="/wiki/Singin%27_in_the_Rain" title="Singin' in the Rain">Singin' in the Rain</a></i>, however, MGM cultivated a much more sympathetic sidekick persona, and that remained O'Connor's signature image.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_O%27Connor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Connor was married twice and had four children. His first marriage was in 1944 to Gwendolyn Carter, with whom he had a daughter, Donna. The couple divorced in 1954. He got married for a second time, to Gloria Noble, in 1956. Together they had three children; Alicia, Donald Frederick, and Kevin. O'Connor and Noble remained married until his death in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severo_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-severo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_O%27Connor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Connor had undergone quadruple heart bypass surgery in 1990,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and he nearly died from double <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> in January 1998. He died from complications of <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a> on September 27, 2003, at age 78 at the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_%26_Television_Country_House_and_Hospital" title="Motion Picture &amp; Television Country House and Hospital">Motion Picture &amp; Television Country House and Hospital</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Woodland_Hills,_Los_Angeles" title="Woodland Hills, Los Angeles">Woodland Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> His remains were cremated and buried at the <a href="/wiki/Forest_Lawn_Memorial_Park_(Hollywood_Hills)" title="Forest Lawn Memorial Park (Hollywood Hills)">Forest Lawn–Hollywood Hills Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.</p>
<p>O'Connor was survived by his wife, Gloria, and four children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severo_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-severo-4">[4]</a></sup> Gloria O'Connor died from natural causes on June 4, 2013, aged 84.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
