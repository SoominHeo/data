<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pole of inaccessibility</h1>

<p>A <b>pole of inaccessibility</b> marks a location that is the most challenging to reach owing to its remoteness from <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> features that could provide access. Often it refers to the most distant point from the coastline. The term describes a geographic construct, not an actual physical phenomenon. Subject to varying definitions, it is of interest mostly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adventurer" title="Adventurer">adventurers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility">Northern pole of inaccessibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Northern pole of inaccessibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1759" data-file-width="1993" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg/220px-C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg/330px-C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg/440px-C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C%E1%BB%B1c_b%E1%BA%A5t_kh%E1%BA%A3_ti%E1%BA%BFp_c%E1%BA%ADn_B%E1%BA%AFc_-_Northern_pole_of_inaccessibility.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Northern pole of inaccessibility</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The northern pole of inaccessibility, sometimes known as the Arctic pole of inaccessibility, or just Arctic pole, is located on the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pack_ice" title="Pack ice">pack ice</a> at a distance farthest from any land mass. It lies at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=85_48_N_176_9_W_&amp;title=True+Northern+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">85°48′N</span> <span class="longitude">176°9′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">85.800°N 176.150°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">85.800; -176.150</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">True Northern Pole of Inaccessibility</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, 1,008 km (626 mi) from the three closest landmasses, <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Komsomolets_Island" title="Komsomolets Island">Komsomolets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Island" title="Henrietta Island">Genriyetta</a> Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This follows from simple geometry, where three points suffice to define a circle; the pole of inaccessibility is then defined as the center of the largest circle that can be drawn within the Arctic ocean without including any land. Due to constant motion of the pack ice, no permanent structure can exist at this pole.</p>
<p>Until a 2013 review of satellite cartography, the northern pole of inaccessibility was thought to lie at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=84_03_N_174_51_W_&amp;title=Old+Northern+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">84°03′N</span> <span class="longitude">174°51′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">84.050°N 174.850°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">84.050; -174.850</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Old Northern Pole of Inaccessibility</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, 1,094 km (680 mi) away from Ellesmere, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz-Josef_Land" title="Franz-Josef Land">Franz-Josef Land</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Siberian_Islands" title="New Siberian Islands">New Siberian Islands</a>. This point is 661 km (411 mi) from the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a>, 1,453 km (903 mi) north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrow,_Alaska" title="Barrow, Alaska">Barrow, Alaska</a>, and 214 km (133 mi) away from the actual northern pole of inaccessibility.</p>
<p>This false northern pole of inaccessibility was first crossed by Sir <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Wilkins" title="Hubert Wilkins">Hubert Wilkins</a>, who flew by <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> in 1927; in 1958, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Icebreaker" title="Icebreaker">icebreaker</a> reached this point.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wally_Herbert" title="Wally Herbert">Sir Wally Herbert</a> claimed to be the first to reach what was then considered to be the northern pole of inaccessibility on foot, arriving by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogsled" title="Dogsled">dogsled</a> in 1968. Explorer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_McNeill,_Polar_Explorer" title="Jim McNeill, Polar Explorer">Jim McNeill</a> claimed in 2006 that Herbert did not quite make the pole, and launched his own unsuccessful attempt to reach it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2010, McNeill said that no one had reached either the false or true northern pole of inaccessibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The southern pole of inaccessibility is the point on the <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctic</a> continent most distant from the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>. A variety of coordinate locations have been given for this pole. The discrepancies are due to the question of whether the "coast" is measured to the grounding line or to the edges of ice shelves, the difficulty of determining the location of the "solid" coastline, the movement of ice sheets and improvements in the accuracy of survey data over the years, as well as possible topographical errors. The pole of inaccessibility commonly refers to the site of the Soviet Union research station mentioned below, which was constructed at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=82_06_S_54_58_E_&amp;title=Pole+of+Inaccessibility+%28WMO%29"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">82°06′S</span> <span class="longitude">54°58′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">82.100°S 54.967°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-82.100; 54.967</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Pole of Inaccessibility (WMO)</span>)</span></span></span></a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (though some sources give <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=83_06_S_54_58_E_&amp;title=South+Pole+of+Inaccessibility+%28IPHC%29"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">83°06′S</span> <span class="longitude">54°58′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">83.100°S 54.967°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-83.100; 54.967</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">South Pole of Inaccessibility (IPHC)</span>)</span></span></span></a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>). This lies 878 km (546 mi) from the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>, at an <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> of 3,718 m (12,198 ft). Using different criteria, the <a href="/wiki/Scott_Polar_Research_Institute" title="Scott Polar Research Institute">Scott Polar Research Institute</a> locates this pole at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=85_50_S_65_47_E_&amp;title=South+Pole+of+Inaccessibility+%28SPRI%29"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">85°50′S</span> <span class="longitude">65°47′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">85.833°S 65.783°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-85.833; 65.783</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">South Pole of Inaccessibility (SPRI)</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i>ThePoles.com</i>, the point farthest from the sea accounting only for the Antarctic land surface proper is at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=82_53_14_S_55_4_30_E_&amp;title=point+farthest+from+the+sea"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">82°53′14″S</span> <span class="longitude">55°4′30″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">82.88722°S 55.07500°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-82.88722; 55.07500</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">point farthest from the sea</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, and the farthest point when ice sheets are taken into account is <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=83_50_37_S_65_43_30_E_&amp;title=point+farthest+from+the+sea+counting+ice"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">83°50′37″S</span> <span class="longitude">65°43′30″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">83.84361°S 65.72500°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-83.84361; 65.72500</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">point farthest from the sea counting ice</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>. These points, calculated by the <a href="/wiki/British_Antarctic_Survey" title="British Antarctic Survey">British Antarctic Survey</a>, are quoted as being "the most accurate measure available" (as of 2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thepoles.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-thepoles.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The southern pole of inaccessibility is far more remote and difficult to reach than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_South_Pole" title="Geographic South Pole">geographic South Pole</a>. On 14 December 1958, the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Soviet_Antarctic_Expedition" title="3rd Soviet Antarctic Expedition">3rd Soviet Antarctic Expedition</a> for <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a> research work, led by <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Tolstikov" title="Yevgeny Tolstikov">Yevgeny Tolstikov</a>, established the temporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pole_of_inaccessibility_(Antarctic_research_station)" title="Pole of inaccessibility (Antarctic research station)">Pole of Inaccessibility Station</a> (<i>Polyus Nedostupnosti</i>) at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=82_06_S_54_58_E_&amp;title=Pole+of+Inaccessibility+Station"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">82°06′S</span> <span class="longitude">54°58′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">82.100°S 54.967°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-82.100; 54.967</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Pole of Inaccessibility Station</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>. A second Russian team returned there in 1967. Today, a building still remains at this location, marked by a bust of <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> that faces towards <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, and protected as a historical site. Inside the building, there is a golden visitors' book for those who make it to the site to sign.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On 4 December 2006, Team N2i, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cookson" title="Henry Cookson">Henry Cookson</a>, Rupert Longsdon, Rory Sweet and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Landry" title="Paul Landry">Paul Landry</a>, embarked on an expedition to be the first to reach the historic pole of inaccessibility location without direct mechanical assistance, using a combination of traditional <a href="/wiki/Manhauling" title="Manhauling">man hauling</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_skiing" title="Kite skiing">kite skiing</a>. The team reached the old abandoned station on 20 January 2007, rediscovering the forgotten statue of Lenin left there by the Soviets some 48 years previously. The explorers were picked up from the spot by a plane from <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">Vostok</a> base, flown to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_Base" title="Progress Base">Progress Base</a> and taken back to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> on the <i><a href="/wiki/Akademik_Fyodorov" title="Akademik Fyodorov">Akademik Fyodorov</a></i>, a Russian polar research vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-9">[9]</a></sup> The team found that only the bust on top of the building remained visible; the rest was buried under the snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 11 December 2005, at 7:57 UTC, <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Hernando_de_Larramendi" title="Ramón Hernando de Larramendi">Ramón Hernando de Larramendi</a>, Juan Manuel Viu, and Ignacio Oficialdegui, members of the Spanish Transantarctic Expedition, reached for the first time in history the southern pole of inaccessibility at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=82_53_14_S_55_04_30_E_&amp;title=British+Antarctic+Survey-accredited+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">82°53′14″S</span> <span class="longitude">55°04′30″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">82.88722°S 55.07500°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-82.88722; 55.07500</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">British Antarctic Survey-accredited Pole of Inaccessibility</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, updated that year by the British Antarctic Survey. The team continued their journey towards the second southern pole of inaccessibility, the one that accounts for the ice shelves as well as the continental land, and they were the first expedition to reach it, on 14 December 2005, at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=83_50_37_S_65_43_30_E_&amp;title=British+Antarctic+Survey-accredited+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">83°50′37″S</span> <span class="longitude">65°43′30″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">83.84361°S 65.72500°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-83.84361; 65.72500</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">British Antarctic Survey-accredited Pole of Inaccessibility</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>. Both achievements took place within an ambitious pioneer crossing of the eastern Antarctic Plateau that started at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novolazerevskaya_Base" title="Novolazerevskaya Base">Novolazerevskaya Base</a> and ended at Progress Base after more than 4,500 km (2,800 mi). This was the fastest polar journey ever achieved without mechanical aid, with an average rate of around 90 km (56 mi) per day and a maximum of 311 km (193 mi) per day, using kites as their power source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thepoles.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-thepoles.com-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 27 December 2011, <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Copeland" title="Sebastian Copeland">Sebastian Copeland</a> and partner Eric McNair-Landry also reached the <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=82_06_S_54_58_E_&amp;title=Pole+of+Inaccessibility+Station"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">82°06′S</span> <span class="longitude">54°58′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">82.100°S 54.967°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-82.100; 54.967</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Pole of Inaccessibility Station</span>)</span></span></span></a></span> southern pole of inaccessibility departing from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novolazerevskaya_Base" title="Novolazerevskaya Base">Novolazerevskaya Base</a> on their way to the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> to complete the first partial crossing of Antarctica through both poles, over 4,000 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>As mentioned above, due to improvements in technology and the position of the continental edge of Antarctica being debated, the exact position of our best estimate of the pole of inaccessibility may vary. However, for the convenience of sport expeditions, a fixed point is preferred, and the Soviet station has been used for this role. This has been recognised by <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> in regard to Team N2i's expedition in 2006–2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teamn2i_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-teamn2i-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_pole_of_inaccessibility">Oceanic pole of inaccessibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oceanic pole of inaccessibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The oceanic pole of inaccessibility (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=48_52.6_S_123_23.6_W_&amp;title=Oceanic+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">48°52.6′S</span> <span class="longitude">123°23.6′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">48.8767°S 123.3933°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-48.8767; -123.3933</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Oceanic Pole of Inaccessibility</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>) is the place in the ocean that is farthest from land. It lies in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Ocean" title="South Pacific Ocean">South Pacific Ocean</a>, 2,688 km (1,670 mi) from the nearest lands: <a href="/wiki/Ducie_Island" title="Ducie Island">Ducie Island</a> (part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Island" title="Pitcairn Island">Pitcairn Islands</a>) in the north, <a href="/wiki/Motu_Nui" title="Motu Nui">Motu Nui</a> (part of the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Islands</a>) in the northeast, and <a href="/wiki/Maher_Island" title="Maher Island">Maher Island</a> (near the larger <a href="/wiki/Siple_Island" title="Siple Island">Siple Island</a>, off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Marie_Byrd_Land" title="Marie Byrd Land">Marie Byrd Land</a>, Antarctica) in the south. Known as "<b>Point Nemo</b>", Latin for "no one" and also a reference to <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Captain_Nemo" title="Captain Nemo">Captain Nemo</a>, it lies more than 1,400 nautical miles from the nearest land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This Oceanic Point of Inaccessibility was used by <a href="/wiki/H._P._Lovecraft" title="H. P. Lovecraft">H. P. Lovecraft</a> as <a href="/wiki/R%27lyeh" title="R'lyeh">R'lyeh</a>, the earthly home of <a href="/wiki/Cthulhu" title="Cthulhu">Cthulhu</a>. The virtual band <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a> claim that Point Nemo is the location of their headquarters, a floating island made of junk known as <a href="/wiki/Plastic_Beach" title="Plastic Beach">Plastic Beach</a>. The area is also known as a "<a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_cemetery" title="Spacecraft cemetery">spacecraft cemetery</a>" because hundreds of decommissioned satellites, space stations, and other spacecraft have been deposited there upon re-entering the atmosphere to lessen the risk of hitting any inhabited locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Point Nemo is relatively lifeless; its location within the <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Gyre" title="South Pacific Gyre">South Pacific Gyre</a> blocks nutrients from reaching the area, and being so far from land it gets little nutrient run-off from coastal waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, the Continental Pole of Inaccessibility (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=46_17_N_86_40_E_&amp;title=Continental+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°17′N</span> <span class="longitude">86°40′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.283°N 86.667°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.283; 86.667</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Continental Pole of Inaccessibility</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>) is the place on land that is farthest from the ocean, and it lies in north-western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> border. Earlier calculations suggested that it is 2,645 km (1,644 mi) from the nearest coastline, located approximately 320 km (200 mi) north of the city of <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi" title="Ürümqi">Ürümqi</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang Autonomous Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dzoosotoyn_Elisen_Desert" title="Dzoosotoyn Elisen Desert">Dzoosotoyn Elisen Desert</a>. The nearest settlements to this location are <a href="/wiki/Hoxtolgay" title="Hoxtolgay">Hoxtolgay</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_(PRC)" title="Town (PRC)">Town</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=46_34_N_85_58_E_&amp;title=Hoxtolgay"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°34′N</span> <span class="longitude">85°58′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.567°N 85.967°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.567; 85.967</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Hoxtolgay</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, about 50 km (31 mi) to the northwest, Xazgat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Township_(PRC)" title="Township (PRC)">Township</a> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%A4%8F%E5%AD%9C%E7%9B%96%E4%B9%A1" title="zh:夏孜盖乡">夏孜盖乡</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Xiàzīgài xiāng</span></i>) at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=46_20_N_86_22_E_&amp;title=Xazgat"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°20′N</span> <span class="longitude">86°22′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.333°N 86.367°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.333; 86.367</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Xazgat</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, about 20 km (12 mi) to the west, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suluk" title="Suluk">Suluk</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=46_15_N_86_50_E_&amp;title=Suluk"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°15′N</span> <span class="longitude">86°50′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.250°N 86.833°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.250; 86.833</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Suluk</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, about 10 km (6.2 mi) to the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msn_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-msn-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the previous pole location disregards the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Ob" title="Gulf of Ob">Gulf of Ob</a> as part of the oceans, and a recent study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-PIA-1">[1]</a></sup> proposes two other locations as the ones farther from any ocean (within the uncertainty of coastline definition): EPIA1 <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=44.29_N_82.19_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">44°17′N</span> <span class="longitude">82°11′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">44.29°N 82.19°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">44.29; 82.19</span></span></span></a></span> and EPIA2 <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=45.28_N_88.14_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">45°17′N</span> <span class="longitude">88°08′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">45.28°N 88.14°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">45.28; 88.14</span></span></span></a></span>, located respectively at 2,510±10 km (1560±6 mi) and 2,514±7 km (1,562±4 mi) from the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-PIA-1">[1]</a></sup> These points lie in a close triangle about the <a href="/wiki/Dzungarian_Gate" title="Dzungarian Gate">Dzungarian Gate</a>, a significant historical gateway to migration between the East and West.</p>
<p>Elsewhere in Xinjiang, the location <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=43_40_52_N_87_19_52_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">43°40′52″N</span> <span class="longitude">87°19′52″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">43.68111°N 87.33111°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">43.68111; 87.33111</span></span></span></a></span> in the southwestern suburbs of <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi" title="Ürümqi">Ürümqi</a> (<a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi_County" title="Ürümqi County">Ürümqi County</a>) was designated by local geography experts as the "center point of Asia" in 1992, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographical_Center_of_Asian_Continent" title="Geographical Center of Asian Continent">monument to this effect</a> was erected there in the 1990s. The site is a local tourist attraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-confluence_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-confluence-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coincidentally, the continental and oceanic poles of inaccessibility have a similar radius; the Eurasian poles EPIA1 and EPIA2 are about 178 km (111 mi) closer to the ocean than the oceanic pole is to land.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the continental pole of inaccessibility is in southwest <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a> about 11 km (7 mi) north of the town of <a href="/wiki/Allen,_South_Dakota" title="Allen, South Dakota">Allen</a>, located 1,650 km (1,030 mi) from the nearest coastline at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=43.36_N_101.97_W_&amp;title=Pole+of+Inaccessibility+North+America"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">43°22′N</span> <span class="longitude">101°58′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">43.36°N 101.97°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">43.36; -101.97</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Pole of Inaccessibility North America</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-PIA-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> pole of inaccessibility is allegedly in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jackfish_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jackfish River (page does not exist)">Jackfish River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=59.03_N_112.82_W_&amp;title=Pole+of+Inaccessibility+Canada"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">59°02′N</span> <span class="longitude">112°49′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">59.03°N 112.82°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">59.03; -112.82</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Pole of Inaccessibility Canada</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, a few kilometres up the <a href="/wiki/Peace_River" title="Peace River">Peace River</a> from where the Jackfish River (one of six Canadian rivers of that name) flows into the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, the continental pole of inaccessibility is in Brazil at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=14.05_S_56.85_W_&amp;title=Continental+Pole+of+Inaccessibility+in+South+America"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">14°03′S</span> <span class="longitude">56°51′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">14.05°S 56.85°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-14.05; -56.85</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Continental Pole of Inaccessibility in South America</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, near <a href="/wiki/Aren%C3%A1polis" title="Arenápolis">Arenápolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-PIA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australia</a>, the continental pole of inaccessibility is located either at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=23.17_S_132.27_E_&amp;title=Continental+Pole+of+Inaccessibility+of+Australia"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">23°10′S</span> <span class="longitude">132°16′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">23.17°S 132.27°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-23.17; 132.27</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Continental Pole of Inaccessibility of Australia</span>)</span></span></span></a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-PIA-1">[1]</a></sup> or at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=23_2_S_132_10_E_&amp;title=Australian+Pole+of+Inaccessibility"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">23°2′S</span> <span class="longitude">132°10′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">23.033°S 132.167°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-23.033; The nearest town is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papunya,_Northern_Territory" title="Papunya, Northern Territory">Papunya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>, about 30 km (19 mi) to the southwest of both locations. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the pole of inaccessibility is at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Pole_of_inaccessibility&amp;params=5.65_N_26.17_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">5°39′N</span> <span class="longitude">26°10′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">5.65°N 26.17°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">5.65; 26.17</span></span></span></a></span>, 1,814 km (1,127 mi) from the coast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-PIA-1">[1]</a></sup> near the town of <a href="/wiki/Obo" title="Obo">Obo</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> and close to the country's <a href="/wiki/Tripoint" title="Tripoint">tripoint</a> with <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>.</p>
