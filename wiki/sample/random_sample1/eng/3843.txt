<p><b>Dorothea Jordan</b> (22 November 1761 – 5 July 1816) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" title="Anglo-Irish">Anglo-Irish</a> actress, <a href="/wiki/Courtesan" title="Courtesan">courtesan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mistress_(lover)" title="Mistress (lover)">mistress</a> and companion of the future King <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="William IV of the United Kingdom">William IV of the United Kingdom</a>, for 20 years while he was Duke of Clarence. Together they had ten illegitimate children, all of whom took the surname <i><a href="/wiki/FitzClarence" title="FitzClarence">FitzClarence</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dorothea_Jordan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dorothea (sometimes called <b>Dorothy</b> or <b>Dora</b>) Bland was born near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterford_City" title="Waterford City">Waterford</a>, Ireland, on 22 November 1761, and was baptised at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Martin_in_the_Fields" title="St Martin in the Fields">St Martin in the Fields</a>, Middlesex, on 5 December of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-2">[2]</a></sup> She was the third of six children born from Francis Bland (b. 1736 – d. Dover, 2 January 1778) and his mistress, Grace Phillips (b. ca. 1740 – d. Edinburgh, 1789). Her older siblings are George Bland (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1758</span> – Boston, Massachusetts, 1807; actor and singer) and Hester Bland (baptised at St Anne Soho, Middlesex, 2 March 1760 – buried at St David's, as of Trelethin, 8 March 1848), and her younger siblings are Lucy Bland (1763/64 – Trelethin, St David's, 1778, aged 14), Francis Bland (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 1813</span>; a captain, unmarried and without issue) and Nathaniel Phillips Bland (born 1766/67 – buried at St David's, Pembrokeshire, 3 June 1830, aged 63).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her paternal grandparents were Nathaniel Bland (Killarney, co. Kerry, 1695/96 – 1760), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicar_General" title="Vicar General">Vicar General</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ardfert" title="Ardfert">Ardfert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aghada" title="Aghada">Aghada</a>, and Judge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prerogative_Court" title="Prerogative Court">Prerogative Court</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and his second wife Lucy (née Heaton).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-2">[2]</a></sup> The reports about Dorothea's maternal ancestry are very sketchy, although is generally stated that Grace Phillips was probably the daughter of a Welsh clergyman (who lived in the early 1750s in Bristol) but he has not been identified with certainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before April 1774, when she was 13, Dorothea's father, who worked as a stagehand, abandoned the family<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-2">[2]</a></sup> to marry an Irish actress. However, he continued to support the family by sending them meagre sums of money. This situation forced Dorothea to work to help her siblings. Her mother, an actress by profession, saw potential in Dorothea and put her on the stage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_life_and_early_relationships">Stage life and early relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dorothea_Jordan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stage life and early relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She became a famous actress of the day and was said to have the most beautiful legs ever seen on the stage. She made her first appearance in 1777, in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, as Phoebe in <i><a href="/wiki/As_You_Like_It" title="As You Like It">As You Like It</a></i>. After acting elsewhere in Ireland she appeared in 1782 at <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, and other Yorkshire towns, in a variety of parts, including Lady Teazle in <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_for_Scandal" title="The School for Scandal">The School for Scandal</a></i>. It was at this time that she began calling herself Mrs Jordan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1785, she made her first <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> appearance at <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Royal,_Drury_Lane" title="Theatre Royal, Drury Lane">Drury Lane</a> as Peggy in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Country_Girl" title="A Country Girl">A Country Girl</a></i>. Her engagement at Drury Lane lasted until 1809, and she played a large variety of parts. But gradually it came to be recognized that her special talent lay in comedy, her Lady Teazle, Rosalind (<i>As You Like It</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Imogen_(Cymbeline)" title="Imogen (Cymbeline)">Imogen (<i>Cymbeline</i>)</a> being specially liked. Audiences also enjoyed her performances in <a href="/wiki/Breeches_role" title="Breeches role">breeches roles</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> such as William in <i><a href="/wiki/Rosina_(opera)" title="Rosina (opera)">Rosina</a></i>. During the rebuilding of Drury Lane she played at the Haymarket; she transferred her services in 1811 to Covent Garden. Here, in 1814, she made her last appearance on the London stage, and the following year, at Margate, retired altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>She had an affair with her first manager, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Daly_(actor)" title="Richard Daly (actor)">Richard Daly</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">manager</a> of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theatre_Royal,_Cork&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theatre Royal, Cork (page does not exist)">Theatre Royal, Cork</a>, who was married, and had an illegitimate child with him:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frances_Alsop" title="Frances Alsop">Frances Daly</a> (also called <i>Fanny</i>; born in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in September 1782; she later changed her name to <i>Frances Bettesworth</i> in 1806 and married Thomas Alsop in 1809).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In England, she had a short lived affair with an army lieutenant, Charles Doyne, who proposed marriage. But she turned him down and went to work for the theatre company operated by <a href="/wiki/Tate_Wilkinson" title="Tate Wilkinson">Tate Wilkinson</a>. It was at this point she adopted the name "Mrs. Jordan" – a reference to her escape across the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>, likened to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Jordan" title="River Jordan">River Jordan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She appeared at Wilkinson's York Circuit theatres, including <a href="/wiki/The_Theatre,_Leeds" title="The Theatre, Leeds">The Theatre, Leeds</a>, where she complained of uncomfortable working conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leodis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leodis-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly after her affair with Wilkinson was over, she began an affair with George Inchbald, the male lead in the Wilkinson company. According to Claire Tomalin, Dorothea's biographer, Dorothea would have married Inchbald, so greatly was she in love with him, but he never asked. Broken-hearted, she left him in 1786 to begin an affair with Sir Richard Ford, a police <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrate</a> and a lawyer. She moved in with Ford when he promised to marry her. They had three illegitimate children:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Dorothea Maria Ford (born August 1787, married Frederick Edward March in 1809).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>A son (who died at birth in October or November 1788).</li>
<li>Lucy Hester Ford (born 1789, married Samuel Hawker—later General and Sir—in 1810).</li>
</ul>
<p>She left him to begin her affair with the Duke of Clarence, once she realised that Ford was never going to marry her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Jordan's children were placed under the care of her sister Hester, who moved with them to a house in Brompton. By a settlement dated 4 November 1791, Jordan transferred all her savings to Ford and Hester for the maintenance and education of the children; in addition, she allowed them an annual payment and granted Hester an allowance for her services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_William_IV">Relationship with William IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dorothea_Jordan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship with William IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pretty, witty and intelligent, Jordan soon came to the attention of wealthy men. She became the mistress of William, Duke of Clarence, later <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="William IV of the United Kingdom">King William IV</a>, in 1791, living with him at <a href="/wiki/Bushy_House" title="Bushy House">Bushy House</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and seemed to have not bothered herself with politics or the political intrigues that often went on behind the scenes in royal courts. Together they had at least ten illegitimate children, all of whom took the surname <a href="/wiki/FitzClarence" title="FitzClarence">FitzClarence</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_FitzClarence,_1st_Earl_of_Munster" title="George FitzClarence, 1st Earl of Munster">George FitzClarence</a> (29 January 1794 – 20 March 1842), created <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Munster" title="Earl of Munster">Earl of Munster</a> in 1831.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henry_FitzClarence" title="Henry FitzClarence">Henry Edward FitzClarence</a> (27 March 1795 – September 1817). No issue.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sophia_Sidney,_Baroness_De_L%27Isle_and_Dudley" title="Sophia Sidney, Baroness De L'Isle and Dudley">Sophia FitzClarence</a> (August 1796 – 10 April 1837), married <a href="/wiki/Philip_Sidney,_1st_Baron_De_L%27Isle_and_Dudley" title="Philip Sidney, 1st Baron De L'Isle and Dudley">Philip Sidney, 1st Baron De L'Isle and Dudley</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lady_Mary_Fox" title="Lady Mary Fox">Mary FitzClarence</a> (19 December 1798 – 13 July 1864), married General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Richard_Fox" title="Charles Richard Fox">Charles Richard Fox</a>. No issue.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Lord_Frederick_FitzClarence" title="Lord Frederick FitzClarence">Lord Frederick FitzClarence</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Guelphic_Order" title="Royal Guelphic Order">GCH</a> (9 December 1799 – 30 October 1854), officer in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dorothea_Jordan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1811, when she and the Duke separated, she was given a yearly stipend by him and custody of their daughters while he retained custody of their sons. Part of her stipend included money for the care of the children with a stipulation stating that in order to continue receiving that money, and retain custody, Dorothea must not return to the stage. In 1814, when a son-in-law became heavily in debt, Dorothea returned to the stage to help pay off that debt. Once the Duke received word of this, he removed their remaining daughters from her care, and took back her yearly stipend. To avoid creditors, she fled to France in 1815 and died at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Cloud" title="Saint-Cloud">Saint-Cloud</a>, near Paris, in poverty just a year later.</p>
