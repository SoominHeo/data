<p><b>Paint</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, liquefiable, or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mastic" title="wikt:mastic">mastic</a> composition that, after application to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">substrate</a> in a thin layer, converts to a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> film. It is most commonly used to protect, color, or provide texture to objects. Paint can be made or purchased in many colors—and in many different types, such as watercolor, synthetic, etc. Paint is typically stored, sold, and applied as a liquid, but most types dry into a solid.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lascaux,_Megaloceros.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1744" data-file-width="1775" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Lascaux%2C_Megaloceros.jpg/220px-Lascaux%2C_Megaloceros.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Lascaux%2C_Megaloceros.jpg/330px-Lascaux%2C_Megaloceros.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Lascaux%2C_Megaloceros.jpg/440px-Lascaux%2C_Megaloceros.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lascaux,_Megaloceros.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A charcoal and ochre cave painting of <a href="/wiki/Megaloceros" title="Megaloceros">Megaloceros</a> from <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux</a>, France</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2001 and 2004, South African archeologists reported finds in Blombos Cave of a 100,000-year-old human-made <a href="/wiki/Ochre" title="Ochre">ochre</a>-based mixture that could have been used like paint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> Further excavation in the same cave resulted in the 2011 report of a complete toolkit for grinding pigments and making a primitive paint-like substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_paintings" title="Cave paintings">Cave paintings</a> drawn with red or yellow ochre, <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese_oxide" title="Manganese oxide">manganese oxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> may have been made by early <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> as long as 40,000 years ago.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4256" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG/220px-Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG/330px-Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG/440px-Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_paint_holder_HARGM10425.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A piece of <a href="/wiki/Giant_clam" title="Giant clam">Giant clam</a> shell used to hold ochre paint in <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Egypt" title="Prehistoric Egypt">pre-dynastic ancient Egypt</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ancient colored walls at <a href="/wiki/Dendera" title="Dendera">Dendera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, which were exposed for years to the elements, still possess their brilliant color, as vivid as when they were painted about 2,000 years ago. The Egyptians mixed their colors with a gummy substance, and applied them separately from each other without any blending or mixture. They appear to have used six colors: white, black, blue, red, yellow, and green. They first covered the area entirely with white, then traced the design in black, leaving out the lights of the ground color. They used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_tetroxide" title="Lead tetroxide">minium</a> for red, and generally of a dark tinge.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> mentions some painted ceilings in his day in the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ardea_(RM)" title="Ardea (RM)">Ardea</a>, which had been done prior to the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. He expresses great surprise and admiration at their freshness, after the lapse of so many centuries.</p>
<p>Paint was made with the yolk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a> and therefore, the substance would harden and adhere to the surface it was applied to. Pigment was made from plants, sand, and different soils. Most paints used either oil or water as a <a href="/wiki/Binder_(material)" title="Binder (material)">base</a> (the diluent, solvent or vehicle for the pigment).</p>
<p>A still extant example of 17th-century house oil painting is <a href="/wiki/Ham_House" title="Ham House">Ham House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, where a primer was used along with several undercoats and an elaborate decorative overcoat; the pigment and oil mixture would have been ground into a paste with a mortar and pestle. The process was done by hand by the painters and exposed them to lead poisoning due to the white-lead powder.</p>
<p>In 1718, Marshall Smith invented a "Machine or Engine for the Grinding of Colours" in England. It is not known precisely how it operated, but it was a device that increased the efficiency of pigment grinding dramatically. Soon, a company called Emerton and Manby was advertising exceptionally low-priced paints that had been ground with labour-saving technology:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>One Pound of Colour ground in a Horse-Mill will paint twelve Yards of Work, whereas Colour ground any other Way, will not do half that Quantity.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>By the proper onset of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, paint was being ground in steam-powered mills and an alternative to lead-based pigments was found in a white derivative of zinc oxide. Interior house painting increasingly became the norm as the 19th century progressed, both for decorative reasons and because the paint was effective in preventing the walls rotting from damp. Linseed oil was also increasingly used as an inexpensive binder.</p>
<p>In 1866, <a href="/wiki/Sherwin-Williams" title="Sherwin-Williams">Sherwin-Williams</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> opened as a large paint-maker and invented a paint that could be used from the tin without preparation.</p>
<p>It was not until the stimulus of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> created a shortage of linseed oil in the supply market that artificial resins, or alkyds, were invented. Cheap and easy to make, they also held the color well and lasted for a long time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle">Vehicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vehicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The vehicle is composed of the binder; or, if it is necessary to thin the binder with a diluent like solvent or water, it is the combination of binder + diluent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wicks_5_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wicks_5-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In this case, once the paint has dried or cured very nearly all of the diluent has evaporated and only the binder is left on the coated surface. Thus, an important quantity in coatings formulation is the "vehicle solids", sometimes called the "resin solids" of the formula. This is the proportion of the wet coating weight that is binder, i.e. the polymer backbone of the film that will remain after drying or curing is complete.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Binder_or_film_former">Binder or film former</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Binder or film former">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The binder is the film-forming component of paint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is the only component that is always present among all the various types of formulations. Many binders are too thick to be applied and must be thinned. The type of thinner, if present, varies with the binder.</p>
<p>The binder imparts properties such as gloss, durability, flexibility, and toughness.</p>
<p>Binders include synthetic or natural resins such as alkyds, <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_resin" title="Acrylic resin">acrylics</a>, vinyl-acrylics, vinyl acetate/ethylene (VAE), <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyesters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melamine_resin" title="Melamine resin">melamine resins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a>, silanes or siloxanes or <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oils</a>.</p>
<p>Binders can be categorized according to the mechanisms for film formation. Thermoplastic mechanisms include drying and coalescence. Drying refers to simple evaporation of the solvent or thinner to leave a coherent film behind. Coalescence refers to a mechanism that involves drying followed by actual interpenetration and fusion of formerly discrete particles. Thermoplastic film-forming mechanisms are sometimes described as "thermoplastic cure" but that is a misnomer because no chemical curing reactions are required to knit the film. Thermosetting mechanisms, on the other hand, are true curing mechanism that involve chemical reaction(s) among the polymers that make up the binder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Thermoplastic mechanisms</b>: Some films are formed by simple cooling of the binder. For example, <a href="/wiki/Encaustic_painting" title="Encaustic painting">encaustic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a> paints are liquid when warm, and harden upon cooling. In many cases, they resoften or liquify if reheated.</p>
<p>Paints that dry by solvent evaporation and contain the solid binder dissolved in a solvent are known as lacquers. A solid film forms when the solvent evaporates. Because no chemical crosslinking is involved, the film can re-dissolve in solvent; as such, lacquers are unsuitable for applications where chemical resistance is important. Classic <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a> lacquers fall into this category, as do non-grain raising stains composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyes" title="Dyes">dyes</a> dissolved in solvent. Performance varies by formulation, but lacquers generally tend to have better <a href="/wiki/UV_coating" title="UV coating">UV resistance</a> and lower corrosion resistance than comparable systems that cure by polymerization or coalescence.</p>
<p>The paint type known as <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">Emulsion</a> in the UK and <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">Latex</a> in the United States is a water-borne dispersion of sub-micrometer polymer particles. These terms in their respective countries cover all paints that use synthetic polymers such as acrylic, vinyl acrylic (<a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate" title="Polyvinyl acetate">PVA</a>), styrene acrylic, etc. as binders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The term "latex" in the context of paint in the United States simply means an aqueous dispersion; <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> rubber from the rubber tree is not an ingredient. These dispersions are prepared by <a href="/wiki/Emulsion_polymerization" title="Emulsion polymerization">emulsion polymerization</a>. Such paints cure by a process called coalescence where first the water, and then the trace, or coalescing, solvent, evaporate and draw together and soften the binder particles and fuse them together into irreversibly bound networked structures, so that the paint cannot redissolve in the solvent/water that originally carried it. The residual <a href="/wiki/Surfactants_in_paint" title="Surfactants in paint">surfactants in paint</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolytic</a> effects with some polymers cause the paint to remain susceptible to softening and, over time, degradation by water. The general term of latex paint is usually used in the United States, while the term emulsion paint is used for the same products in the UK and the term latex paint is not used at all.</p>
<p><b>Thermosetting mechanisms</b>: Paints that cure by polymerization are generally one- or two-package coatings that polymerize by way of a chemical reaction, and cure into a crosslinked film. Depending on composition they may need to dry first, by evaporation of solvent. Classic two-package <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethanes</a> would fall into this category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ber114_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ber114-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "drying oils", counter-intuitively, actually cure by a crosslinking reaction even if they are not put through an oven cycle and seem to simply dry in air. The film formation mechanism of the simplest examples involve first evaporation of solvents followed by reaction with oxygen from the environment over a period of days, weeks and even months to create a crosslinked network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wicks_5_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wicks_5-5">[5]</a></sup> Classic <a href="/wiki/Alkyd" title="Alkyd">alkyd</a> enamels would fall into this category. Oxidative cure coatings are catalyzed by metal complex driers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_naphthenate" title="Cobalt naphthenate">cobalt naphthenate</a>.</p>
<p>Recent environmental requirements restrict the use of <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOCs), and alternative means of curing have been developed, generally for industrial purposes. <a href="/wiki/UV_curing" title="UV curing">UV curing</a> paints, for example, enable formulation with very low amounts of solvent, or even none at all. This can be achieved because of the monomers and oligomers used in the coating have relatively very low molecular weight, and are therefore low enough in viscosity to enable good fluid flow without the need for additional thinner. If solvent is present in significant amounts, generally it is mostly evaporated first and then crosslinking is initiated by ultraviolet light. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Powder_coating" title="Powder coating">powder coatings</a> contain little or no solvent. Flow and cure are produced by heating of the substrate after electrostatic application of the dry powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Combination mechanisms</b>: So-called "catalyzed" lacquers" or "crosslinking latex" coatings are designed to form films by a combination of methods: classic drying plus a curing reaction that benefits from the catalyst. There are paints called plastisols/organosols, which are made by blending PVC granules with a plasticiser. These are stoved and the mix coalesces.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diluent_or_solvent_or_thinner">Diluent or solvent or thinner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diluent or solvent or thinner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The main purposes of the diluent are to dissolve the polymer and adjust the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the paint. It is volatile and does not become part of the paint film. It also controls flow and application properties, and in some cases can affect the stability of the paint while in liquid state. Its main function is as the carrier for the non volatile components. To spread heavier oils (for example, linseed) as in oil-based interior house paint, a thinner oil is required. These volatile substances impart their properties temporarily—once the solvent has evaporated, the remaining paint is fixed to the surface.</p>
<p>This component is optional: some paints have no <a href="/wiki/Diluent" title="Diluent">diluent</a>.</p>
<p>Water is the main diluent for water-borne paints, even the co-solvent types.</p>
<p>Solvent-borne, also called oil-based, paints can have various combinations of organic solvents as the diluent, including <a href="/wiki/Aliphatic_compound" title="Aliphatic compound">aliphatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_spirit" title="White spirit">white spirit</a>. Specific examples are organic solvents such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turpentine_substitute" title="Turpentine substitute">petroleum distillate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycol" title="Glycol">glycol</a> ethers, and the like. Sometimes volatile low-molecular weight synthetic resins also serve as diluents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigment_and_filler">Pigment and filler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pigment and filler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">Pigment</a></div>
<p>Pigments are granular solids incorporated in the paint to contribute color. Fillers are granular solids incorporate to impart toughness, texture, give the paint special properties, or to reduce the cost of the paint. Alternatively, some paints contain <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a> instead of or in combination with pigments.</p>
<p>Pigments can be classified as either natural or synthetic. Natural pigments include various <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silicas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talcs</a>. Synthetics would include engineered molecules, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcining" title="Calcining">calcined</a> clays, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blanc_fixe" title="Blanc fixe">blanc fixe</a>, precipitated calcium carbonate, and synthetic pyrogenic silicas.</p>
<p>Hiding pigments, in making paint opaque, also protect the substrate from the harmful effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>. Hiding pigments include <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phthalocyanine_Blue_BN" title="Phthalocyanine Blue BN">phthalo blue</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_iron_oxide" title="Red iron oxide">red iron oxide</a>, and many others.</p>
<p>Fillers are a special type of pigment that serve to thicken the film, support its structure and increase the volume of the paint. Fillers are usually cheap and inert materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" title="Lime (mineral)">lime</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barytes" title="Barytes">barytes</a>, clay, etc. Floor paints that must resist abrasion may contain fine quartz sand as a filler. Not all paints include fillers. On the other hand, some paints contain large proportions of pigment/filler and binder.</p>
<p>Some pigments are toxic, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> pigments that are used in <a href="/wiki/Lead_paint" title="Lead paint">lead paint</a>. Paint manufacturers began replacing white lead pigments with titanium white (titanium dioxide), before lead was banned in paint for residential use in 1978 by the US Consumer Product Safety Commission. The titanium dioxide used in most paints today is often coated with silica/alumina/zirconium for various reasons, such as better exterior durability, or better hiding performance (opacity) promoted by more optimal spacing within the paint film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Micaceous <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">Iron Oxide</a> (MIO) is another alternative to lead for protection of steel, giving more protection against water and light damage than most paints. When MIO pigments are ground into fine particles, most cleave into shiny layers, which reflect light, thus minimising <a href="/wiki/UV_degradation" title="UV degradation">UV degradation</a> and protecting the <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a> binder. Most pigments used in paint tend to be spherical, but lamellar pigments, such as glass flake and MIO have overlapping plates, which impede the path of water molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> For optimum performance MIO should have a high content of thin flake-like particles resembling <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>. <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> 10601 sets two levels of MIO content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> MIO is often derived from a form of <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additives">Additives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Additives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Besides the three main categories of ingredients, paint can have a wide variety of miscellaneous additives, which are usually added in small amounts, yet provide a significant effect on the product. Some examples include additives to modify <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>, improve flow properties, improve the finished appearance, increase wet edge, improve pigment stability, impart <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">antifreeze</a> properties, control foaming, control skinning, etc. Other types of additives include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>, thickeners, stabilizers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsifier" title="Emulsifier">emulsifiers</a>, texturizers, adhesion promoters, UV stabilizers, flatteners (de-glossing agents), biocides to fight <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> growth, and the like.</p>
<p>Additives normally do not significantly alter the percentages of individual components in a formulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Color-changing_paint">Color-changing paint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Color-changing paint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Various technologies exist for making paints that change color. Thermochromic paints and coatings contain materials that change conformation when heat is applied or removed, and so they change color. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal" title="Liquid crystal">Liquid crystals</a> have been used in such paints, such as in the thermometer strips and tapes used in aquaria and novelty/promotional thermal cups and straws. These materials are used to make eyeglasses.</p>
<p>Color-changing paints can also be made by adding halochrome compounds or other organic pigments. One patent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> cites use of these indicators for wall coating applications for light colored paints. When the paint is wet it is pink in color but upon drying it regains its original white color. As cited in patent, this property of the paint enabled two or more coats to be applied on a wall properly and evenly. The previous coats having dried would be white whereas the new wet coat would be distinctly pink. Ashland Inc. introduced foundry refractory coatings with similar principle in 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> for use in foundries.</p>
<p>Electrochromic paints change color in response to an applied electric current. Car manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a> has been reportedly working on an electrochromic paint, based on particles of <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>. When subjected to an electromagnetic field the paramagnetic particles change spacing, modifying their color and reflective properties. The electromagnetic field would be formed using the conductive metal of the car body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Electrochromic paints can be applied to plastic substrates as well, using a different coating chemistry. The technology involves using special dyes that change conformation when an electric current is applied across the film itself. This new technology has been used to achieve glare protection at the touch of a button in passenger airplane windows.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">Painting</a></div>

<p>Since the time of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siccative" title="Siccative">siccative</a> <a href="/wiki/Drying_oil" title="Drying oil">(drying) oil</a> paints, primarily <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a>, have been the most commonly used kind of paints in fine art applications; <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oil paint</a> is still common today. However, in the 20th century, water-based paints, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercolor" title="Watercolor">watercolors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_paint" title="Acrylic paint">acrylic paints</a>, became very popular with the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acryl_group" title="Acryl group">acrylic</a> and other latex paints. <a href="/wiki/Milk_paint" title="Milk paint">Milk paints</a> (also called <a href="/wiki/Casein" title="Casein">casein</a>), where the medium is derived from the natural emulsion that is <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, were popular in the 19th century and are still available today. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_tempera" title="Egg tempera">Egg tempera</a> (where the medium is an <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a> of raw <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_yolk" title="Egg yolk">egg yolk</a> mixed with oil) is still in use as well, as are encaustic <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a>-based paints. <a href="/wiki/Gouache" title="Gouache">Gouache</a> is a variety of opaque watercolor that was also used in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and Renaissance for manuscript illuminations. The pigment was often made from ground semiprecious stones such as <a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a> and the binder made from either <a href="/wiki/Gum_arabic" title="Gum arabic">gum arabic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Egg_white" title="Egg white">egg white</a>. Gouache, also known as 'designer color' or 'body color' is commercially available today.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Poster_paint" title="Poster paint">Poster paint</a> has been used primarily in the creation of student works, or by children.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paint can be applied as a solid, a gaseous suspension (<a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a>) or a liquid. Techniques vary depending on the practical or artistic results desired.</p>
<p>As a <b>solid</b> (usually used in industrial and automotive applications), the paint is applied as a very fine powder, then baked at high temperature. This melts the powder and causes it to adhere to the surface. The reasons for doing this involve the chemistries of the paint, the surface itself, and perhaps even the chemistry of the substrate (the object being painted). This is called "<a href="/wiki/Powder_coating" title="Powder coating">powder coating</a>" an object.</p>
<p>As a <b>gas</b> or as a gaseous suspension, the paint is suspended in solid or liquid form in a gas that is <a href="/wiki/Spray_painting" title="Spray painting">sprayed on</a> an object. The paint sticks to the object. This is called "spray painting" an object. The reasons for doing this include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The application mechanism is air and thus no solid object touches the object being painted;</li>
<li>The distribution of the paint is uniform, so there are no sharp lines;</li>
<li>It is possible to deliver very small amounts of paint;</li>
<li>A chemical (typically a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>) can be sprayed along with the paint to dissolve together both the delivered paint and the chemicals on the surface of the object being painted;</li>
<li>Some chemical reactions in paint involve the orientation of the paint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In the <b>liquid</b> application, paint can be applied by direct application using <a href="/wiki/Brush" title="Brush">brushes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paint_rollers" title="Paint rollers">paint rollers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palette_knife" title="Palette knife">blades</a>, scrapers, other instruments, or body parts such as fingers and thumbs.</p>
<p>Rollers generally have a handle that allows for different lengths of poles to be attached, allowing painting at different heights. Generally, roller application requires two coats for even color. A roller with a thicker <a href="/wiki/Nap_(textile)" title="Nap (textile)">nap</a> is used to apply paint on uneven surfaces. Edges are often finished with an angled brush.</p>
<ul>
<li>Using the finish flat one would most likely use a 1/2" nap roller</li>
<li>Using the finish eggshell one would most likely use a 3/8" nap roller</li>
<li>Using the finish satin or pearl one would most likely use a 3/8" nap roller</li>
<li>Using the finish semi-gloss or gloss one would most likely use a 3/16" nap roller</li>
</ul>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Open_time"></span>After liquid paint is applied, there is an interval during which it can be blended with additional painted regions (at the "wet edge") called "open time". The open time of an oil or alkyd-based emulsion paint can be extended by adding <a href="/wiki/White_spirit" title="White spirit">white spirit</a>, similar glycols such as Dowanol (propylene glycol ether) or open time prolongers. This can also facilitate the mixing of different wet paint layers for aesthetic effect. Latex and acrylic emulsions require the use of drying retardants suitable for water-based coatings.</p>
<p>Paint application by spray is the most popular method in industry. In this, paint is atomized by the force of compressed air or by the action of high pressure compression of the paint itself, and the paint is turned into small droplets that travel to the article to be painted. Alternate methods are airless spray, hot spray, hot airless spray, and any of these with an electrostatic spray included. There are numerous electrostatic methods available.</p>
<p>Dipping used to be the norm for objects such as filing cabinets, but this has been replaced by high speed air turbine driven bells with electrostatic spray. Car bodies are primed using cathodic elephoretic primer, which is applied by charging the body depositing a layer of primer. The unchanged residue is rinsed off and the primer stoved.</p>
<p>Many paints tend to separate when stored, the heavier components settling to the bottom, and require mixing before use. Some paint outlets have machines for mixing the paint by shaking the can vigorously for a few minutes.</p>
<p>The opacity and the film thickness of paint may be measured using a <a href="/wiki/Drawdown_card" title="Drawdown card">drawdown card</a>.</p>
<p>Water-based paints tend to be the easiest to clean up after use; the brushes and rollers can be cleaned with soap and water.</p>
<p>Proper disposal of left over paint is a challenge. Sometimes it can be recycled: Old paint may be usable for a primer coat or an intermediate coat, and paints of similar chemistry can be mixed to make a larger amount of a uniform color.</p>
<p>To dispose of paint it can be dried and disposed of in the domestic waste stream, provided that it contains no prohibited substances (see container). Disposal of liquid paint usually requires special handling and should be treated as hazardous waste, and disposed of according to local regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Primer_(paint)" title="Primer (paint)">Primer</a></b> is a preparatory coating put on materials before applying the paint itself. The primed surface ensures better <a href="/wiki/Adhesion" title="Adhesion">adhesion</a> of the paint, thereby increasing the durability of the paint and providing improved protection for the painted surface. Suitable primers also may block and seal stains, or hide a color that is to be painted over.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">Emulsion</a> paints</b> are water-based paints in which the paint material is dispersed in a liquid that consists mainly of water. For suitable purposes this has advantages in fast drying, low toxicity, low cost, easier application, and easier cleaning of equipment, among other factors.</li>
<li><b>Flat Finish</b> paint is generally used on ceilings or walls that are in bad shape. This finish is useful for hiding imperfections in walls and it is economical in effectively covering relatively great areas. However this finish is not easily washable and is subject to staining.</li>
<li><b>Matte Finish</b> is generally similar to flat finish, but such paints commonly offer superior washability and coverage. (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloss_and_matte_paint" title="Gloss and matte paint">Gloss and matte paint</a>.)</li>
<li><b>Eggshell Finish</b> has some sheen, supposedly like that of the shell on an egg. This finish provides great washability, but is not very effective at hiding imperfections on walls and similar surfaces. Eggshell finish is valued for bathrooms because it is washable and water repellent, so that it tends not to peel in a wet environment.</li>
<li><b>Pearl (Satin) Finish</b> is very durable in terms of washability and resistance to moisture, even in comparison to eggshell finish. It protects walls from dirt, moisture and stains. Accordingly, it is exceptionally valuable for bathrooms, furniture, and kitchens, but it is shinier than eggshell, so it is even more prone to show imperfections.</li>
<li><b>Semi-Gloss Finish</b> typically is used on trim to emphasise detail and elegance, and to show off woodwork, such as on doors and furniture. It provides a shiny surface and provides good protection from moisture and stains on walls. Its gloss does however emphasise imperfections on the walls and similar surfaces. It is popular in schools and factories where washability and durability are the main considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Varnish" title="Varnish">Varnish</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Shellac" title="Shellac">shellac</a></b> are in effect paints without pigment; they provide a protective coating without substantially changing the color of the surface, though they can emphasise the colour of the material.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wood_stain" title="Wood stain">Wood stain</a></b> is a type of paint that is formulated to be very "thin", meaning low in viscosity, so that the pigment soaks into a material such as wood rather than remaining in a film on the surface. Stain is mainly dissolved <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dye</a> plus binder material in solvent. It is designed to add color without providing a surface coating.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lacquer" title="Lacquer">Lacquer</a></b> is a solvent-based paint or varnish that produces an especially hard, durable finish. Usually it is a rapidly drying formulation.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Enamel_paint" title="Enamel paint">Enamel paint</a></b> is formulated to give an especially hard, usually glossy, finish. Some enamel paints contain fine glass powder or metal flake instead of the color pigments in standard oil-based paints. Enamel paint sometimes is mixed with varnish or urethane to improve its shine and hardness.</li>
<li><b>A <a href="/wiki/Glaze_(painting_technique)" title="Glaze (painting technique)">glaze</a></b> is an additive used with paint to slow drying time and increase translucency, as in <a href="/wiki/Faux_painting" title="Faux painting">faux painting</a> and for some artistic effects.</li>
<li><b>A <a href="/wiki/Roof_coating" title="Roof coating">roof coating</a></b> is a fluid that sets as an elastic membrane that can stretch without harm. It provides UV protection to polyurethane foam and is widely used in roof restoration.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Fingerpaint" title="Fingerpaint">Fingerpaints</a></b> are formulations suitable for application with the fingers; they are popular for use by children in primary school activities.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">Inks</a></b> are similar to paints, except that they are typically made using finely ground pigments or dyes, and are not designed to leave a thick film of binder. They are used largely for writing or <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphy</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Anti-graffiti_coating" title="Anti-graffiti coating">Anti-graffiti coatings</a></b> are used to defeat the marking of surfaces by <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> artists or vandals. There are two categories of anti-graffiti coatings: sacrificial and non-bonding:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Sacrificial coatings</b> are clear coatings that allow the removal of graffiti, usually by washing the surface with high-pressure water that removes the graffiti together with the coating (hence the term "sacrificial"). After removal of the graffiti, the sacrificial coating must be re-applied for continued protection. Such sacrificial protective coatings are most commonly used on natural-looking masonry surfaces, such as statuary and marble walls, and on rougher surfaces that are difficult to clean.</li>
<li><b>Non-bonding coatings</b> are clear, high-performance coatings, usually catalyzed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyurethanes" title="Polyurethanes">polyurethanes</a>, that do not bond strongly to paints used for graffiti. Graffiti on such a surface can be removed with a solvent wash, without damaging either the underlying surface or the protective non-bonding coating. These coatings work best on smooth surfaces, and are especially useful on decorative surfaces such as mosaics or painted murals, which might be expected to suffer harm from high pressure sprays.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Anti-climb_paint" title="Anti-climb paint">Anti-climb paint</a></b> is a non-drying paint that appears normal but is extremely slippery. It is useful on drainpipes and ledges to deter burglars and vandals from climbing them, and is found in many public places. When a person attempts to climb objects coated with the paint, it rubs off onto the climber, as well as making it hard for them to climb.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Anti-fouling_paint" title="Anti-fouling paint">Anti-fouling paint</a></b>, or bottom paint, prevents barnacles and other marine organisms from adhering to the <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hulls</a> of ships.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Insulative_paint" title="Insulative paint">Insulative paint</a></b> or <b>insulating paint</b>, reduces the rate of thermal transfer through a surface it's applied to. One type of formulation is based on the addition of hollow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microspheres" title="Microspheres">microspheres</a> to any suitable type of paint.</li>
<li><b>Anti-slip paint</b> contains chemicals or grit to increase the friction of a surface so as to decrease the risk of slipping, particularly in wet conditions.</li>
<li><b>Road marking paint</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-road_marking_paint_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-road_marking_paint-24">[24]</a></sup> is specially used to marking and painting road traffic signs and lines, to form a durable coating film on the road surface. It must be fast drying, provide a thick coating, and resist wear and slipping, especially in wet conditions.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Luminous_paint" title="Luminous paint">Luminous paint</a></b> or luminescent paint is paint that exhibits luminescence. In other words, it gives off visible light through fluorescence, phosphorescence, or radioluminescence.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure_of_a_paint">Failure of a paint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Failure of a paint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main reasons of paint failure after application on surface are the applicator and improper treatment of surface.</p>
<p>Defects or degradation can be attributed to:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Dilution</dt>
<dd>This usually occurs when the dilution of the paint is not done as per manufacturers recommendation. There can be a case of over dilution and under dilution, as well as dilution with the incorrect diluent.</dd>
<dt>Contamination</dt>
<dd>Foreign contaminants added without the manufacturers consent can cause various film defects.</dd>
<dt>Peeling/Blistering</dt>
<dd>Most commonly due to improper surface treatment before application and inherent moisture/dampness being present in the substrate. The degree of blistering can be assessed according to ISO 4628 Part 2 or ASTM Method D714 (Standard Test Method for Evaluating Degree of Blistering of Paints).</dd>
<dt>Chalking</dt>
<dd>Chalking is the progressive powdering of the paint film on the painted surface. The primary reason for the problem is <a href="/wiki/Polymer_degradation" title="Polymer degradation">polymer degradation</a> of the paint matrix due to exposure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_radiation" title="UV radiation">UV radiation</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunshine" title="Sunshine">sunshine</a> and condensation from dew. The degree of chalking varies as epoxies react quickly while acrylics and polyurethanes can remain unchanged for long periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The degree of chalking can be assessed according to <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Standard</a> ISO 4628 Part 6 or 7 or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Testing_and_Materials" title="American Society of Testing and Materials">American Society of Testing and Materials</a>(ASTM) Method D4214 (Standard Test Methods for Evaluating the Degree of Chalking of Exterior Paint Films).</dd>
<dt>Cracking</dt>
<dd>Cracking of paint film is due to the unequal expansion or contraction of paint coats. It usually happens when the coats of the paint are not allowed to cure/dry completely before the next coat is applied. The degree of cracking can be assessed according to International Standard ISO 4628 Part 4 or ASTM Method D661 (Standard Test Method for Evaluating Degree of Cracking of Exterior Paints).</dd>
<dt>Erosion</dt>
<dd>Erosion is very quick chalking. It occurs due to external agents like air,water etc. It can be evaluated using ASTM Method ASTM D662 (Standard Test Method for Evaluating Degree of Erosion of Exterior Paints). The generation of acid by fungal species can be a significant component of erosion of painted surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The fungus <a href="/wiki/Aureobasidium_pullulans" title="Aureobasidium pullulans">Aureobasidium pullulans</a> is known for damaging wall paints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">Dangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dangers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">Volatile organic compounds</a> (VOCs) in paint are considered harmful to the environment and especially for people who work with them on a regular basis. Exposure to VOCs has been related to organic solvent syndrome, although this relation has been somewhat controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The controversial solvent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-butoxyethanol" title="2-butoxyethanol">2-butoxyethanol</a> is also used in paint production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ethylene_Glycol_Mono-N-Butyl_Ether_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ethylene_Glycol_Mono-N-Butyl_Ether-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the US, environmental regulations, consumer demand, and advances in technology led to the development of low-VOC and zero-VOC paints and finishes. These new paints are widely available and meet or exceed the old high-VOC products in performance and cost-effectiveness while having significantly less impact on human and environmental health.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A polychlorinated biphenyl (PCB) was reported ( published in 2009 ) in air samples collected in Chicago, Philadelphia, the Arctic, and several sites around the Great Lakes. PCB is a global pollutant and was measured in the wastewater effluent from paint production. The widespread distribution of PCB suggests volatilization of this compound from surfaces, roofs etc. PCB is present in consumer goods including newspapers, magazines, and cardboard boxes, which usually contain color pigments. Therefore exist hypothesis that PCB congeners are present as byproduct in some current commercial pigments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
