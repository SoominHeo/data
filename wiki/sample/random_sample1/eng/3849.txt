<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Doric order</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Doric order</b> was one of the <a href="/wiki/Classical_order" title="Classical order">three orders</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Ancient_Greece" title="Architecture of Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">classical architecture</a>; the other two <a href="/wiki/Canon_(basic_principle)" title="Canon (basic principle)">canonical</a> orders were the <a href="/wiki/Ionic_order" title="Ionic order">Ionic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a>. The Doric is most easily recognised by the simple circular <a href="/wiki/Capital_(architecture)" title="Capital (architecture)">capitals</a> at the top of <a href="/wiki/Column" title="Column">columns</a>. It was the earliest and in its essence the simplest of the orders, though still with complex details in the <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a> above.</p>
<p>The Greek Doric column was fluted or smooth-surfaced,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and had no base, dropping straight into the <a href="/wiki/Stylobate" title="Stylobate">stylobate</a> or platform on which the temple or other building stood. The capital was a simple circular form, with some mouldings, under a square cushion that is very wide in early versions, but later more restrained. Above a plain <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a>, the complexity comes in the <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a>, where the two features originally unique to the Doric, the <a href="/wiki/Triglyph" title="Triglyph">triglyph</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guttae" title="Guttae">guttae</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Skeuomorph" title="Skeuomorph">skeuomorphic</a> memories of the beams and retaining pegs of the wooden constructions that preceded stone Doric temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In stone they are purely <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(art)" title="Ornament (art)">ornamental</a>.</p>
<p>The relatively uncommon Roman and Renaissance Doric retained these, and often introduced thin layers of moulding or further ornament, as well as often using plain columns. More often they used versions of the <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_order" title="Tuscan order">Tuscan order</a>, elaborated for nationalistic reasons by <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> writers, which is in effect a simplified Doric, with un-fluted columns and a simpler entablature with no triglyphs or guttae. The Doric order was much used in <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival_architecture" title="Greek Revival architecture">Greek Revival architecture</a> from the 18th century onwards; often earlier Greek versions were used, with wider columns and no bases to them.</p>
<p>Since at least <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> it has been customary for writers to associate the Doric with masculine virtues (the Ionic representing the feminine).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also normally the cheapest of the orders to use. When the three orders are used one above the other, it is usual for the Doric to be at the bottom, with the Ionic and then the Corinthian above, and the Doric, as "strongest", is often used on the ground floor below another order in the storey above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doric_order&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek">Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doric_order&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In their original Greek version, Doric <a href="/wiki/Column" title="Column">columns</a> stood directly on the flat pavement (the <i>stylobate</i>) of a <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temple</a> without a base. With a height only four to eight times their diameter, the columns were the most squat of all the classical orders; their vertical shafts were fluted with 20 parallel concave <a href="/wiki/Fluting_(architecture)" title="Fluting (architecture)">grooves</a>; and they were topped by a smooth <a href="/wiki/Capital_(architecture)" title="Capital (architecture)">capital</a> that flared from the column to meet a square <a href="/wiki/Abacus_(architecture)" title="Abacus (architecture)">abacus</a> at the intersection with the horizontal <a href="/wiki/Beam_(structure)" title="Beam (structure)">beam</a> (<a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a>) that they carried. The <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a> has the Doric design columns. It was most popular in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaic_period_in_Greece" title="Archaic period in Greece">Archaic Period</a> (750-480 BC) in mainland Greece, and also found in <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a> (southern Italy), as in the three temples at <a href="/wiki/Paestum" title="Paestum">Paestum</a>. These are in the Archaic Doric, where the capitals spread wide from the column compared to later Classical forms, as exemplified in the Parthenon.</p>
<p>Pronounced features of both Greek and Roman versions of the Doric order are the alternating <a href="/wiki/Triglyph" title="Triglyph">triglyphs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metope_(architecture)" title="Metope (architecture)">metopes</a>. The triglyphs are decoratively grooved with two vertical grooves ("tri-glyph") and represent the original wooden end-beams, which rest on the plain <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a> that occupies the lower half of the entablature. Under each triglyph are peglike "stagons" or "guttae" (literally: drops) that appear as if they were hammered in from below to stabilize the post-and-beam (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trabeated_(architecture)" title="Trabeated (architecture)">trabeated</a>) construction. They also served to "organize" rainwater runoff from above. The spaces between the triglyphs are the "metopes". They may be left plain, or they may be carved in low relief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacing_the_triglyphs">Spacing the triglyphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doric_order&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spacing the triglyphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The spacing of the triglyphs caused problems which took some time to resolve. A triglyph is centered above every column, with another (or sometimes two) between columns, though the Greeks felt that the corner triglyph should form the corner of the entablature, creating an inharmonious mismatch with the supporting column.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doric-corner-conflict.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="749" data-file-width="257" height="437" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Doric-corner-conflict.png/150px-Doric-corner-conflict.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Doric-corner-conflict.png/225px-Doric-corner-conflict.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ad/Doric-corner-conflict.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doric-corner-conflict.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Doric corner conflict</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The architecture followed rules of harmony. Since the original design probably came from wooden temples and the triglyphs were real heads of wooden beams, every column had to bear a beam which lay across the centre of the column. Triglyphs were arranged regularly; the last triglyph was centred upon the last column (<i>illustration, right: <b>I.</b></i>). This was regarded as the ideal solution which had to be reached.</p>
<p>Changing to stone cubes instead of wooden beams required full support of the <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a> load at the last column. At the first temples the final triglyph was moved (<i>illustration, right: <b>II.</b></i>), still terminating the sequence, but leaving a gap disturbing the regular order. Even worse, the last triglyph was not centered with the corresponding column. That “archaic” manner was not regarded as a harmonious design. The resulting problem is called <b>the doric corner conflict</b>. Another approach was to apply a broader corner triglyph (<i><b>III.</b></i>) but was not really satisfying.</p>
<p>Because the metopes are somewhat flexible in their proportions, the modular space between columns (“intercolumniation”) can be adjusted by the architect. Often the last two columns were set slightly closer together (<i>corner contraction</i>), to give a subtle visual strengthening to the corners. That is called the “classic” solution of the corner conflict (<i><b>IV.</b></i>). Triglyphs could be arranged in a harmonic manner again, and the corner was terminated with a triglyph. However, final triglyph and column were often not centered.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Temples">Temples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doric_order&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Early examples of the Doric order include the 6th-century BC temples at <a href="/wiki/Paestum" title="Paestum">Paestum</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, a region called <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a>, which was settled by Greek colonists. Compared to later versions, the columns are much more massive, with a strong <a href="/wiki/Entasis" title="Entasis">entasis</a> or swelling, and wider capitals.</p>

<p>The <b>Temple of the Delians</b> is a “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripteral" title="Peripteral">peripteral</a>” Doric order temple, the largest of three dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delos</a>. It was begun in 478 BC and never completely finished. During their period of independence from Athens, the Delians reassigned the temple to the island of <a href="/wiki/Poros" title="Poros">Poros</a>. It is “hexastyle”, with six columns across the <a href="/wiki/Pediment" title="Pediment">pedimented</a> end and thirteen along each long face. All the columns are centered under a triglyph in the <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a>, except for the corner columns. The plain, unfluted shafts on the columns stand directly on the platform (the <i>stylobate</i>), without bases. The recessed “necking” in the nature of fluting at the top of the shafts and the wide cushionlike echinus may be interpreted as slightly self-conscious archaising features, for Delos is Apollo's ancient birthplace. However, the similar fluting at the base of the shafts might indicate an intention for the plain shafts to be capable of wrapping in drapery.</p>

<p>A classic statement of the Greek Doric order is the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Hephaestus" title="Temple of Hephaestus">Temple of Hephaestus</a> in Athens, built about 447 BC. The contemporary <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a>, the largest temple in classical <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, is also in the Doric order, although the sculptural enrichment is more familiar in the Ionic order: the Greeks were never as doctrinaire in the use of the Classical vocabulary as <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> theorists or <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> architects. The detail, part of the basic vocabulary of trained architects from the later 18th century onwards, shows how the width of the metopes was flexible: here they bear the famous <a href="/wiki/Metopes_of_the_Parthenon" title="Metopes of the Parthenon">sculptures including the battle of Lapiths and Centaurs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman">Roman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doric_order&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Roman Doric version (<i>illustration, left</i>), the height of the entablature has been reduced. The endmost triglyph is centered over the column rather than occupying the corner of the architrave. The columns are slightly less robust in their proportions. Below their caps, an astragal molding encircles the column like a ring. <a href="/wiki/Crown_molding" title="Crown molding">Crown moldings</a> soften transitions between frieze and <a href="/wiki/Cornice" title="Cornice">cornice</a> and emphasize the upper edge of the <a href="/wiki/Abacus_(architecture)" title="Abacus (architecture)">abacus</a>, which is the upper part of the capital. Roman Doric columns also have moldings at their bases and stand on low square pads or are even raised on <a href="/wiki/Plinth" title="Plinth">plinths</a>. In the Roman Doric mode, columns are not invariably fluted. Since the Romans did not insist on a triglyph covered corner, now both columns and triglyphs could be arranged equidistantly again and centered together. The architrave corner needed to be left “blank” (<i>illustration, right, <b>V.</b></i>).</p>
<p>The Roman architect <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>, following contemporary practice, outlined in his treatise the procedure for laying out constructions based on a module, which he took to be one half a column's diameter, taken at the base. An illustration of <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>'s Doric order, as it was laid out, with modules identified, by Isaac Ware, in <i>The Four Books of Palladio's Architecture</i> (London, 1738) is illustrated at <a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_module" title="Vitruvian module">Vitruvian module</a>.</p>
<p>According to Vitruvius the height of Doric columns is six or seven times the diameter at the base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This gives the Doric columns a shorter, thicker look than Ionic columns, which have 8:1 proportions. It is suggested that these proportions give the Doric columns a masculine appearance, whereas the more slender Ionic columns appear to represent a more feminine look. This sense of masculinity and femininity was often used to determine which type of column would be used for a particular structure.</p>
<p>The most influential, and perhaps the earliest, use of the Doric in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance architecture</a> was in the circular <i>Tempietto</i> by <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Donato Bramante</a> (1502 or later), in the courtyard of <a href="/wiki/San_Pietro_in_Montorio" title="San Pietro in Montorio">San Pietro in Montorio</a>, Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doric_order&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg/220px-Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg/330px-Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg/440px-Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northington_Grange_-_Hampshire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/The_Grange,_Northington" title="The Grange, Northington">The Grange, Northington</a>, 1804, Europe's first house designed with all external detail of a Greek temple.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival_architecture" title="Greek Revival architecture">Greek Revival architecture</a> grew, initially in England, in the 18th century, the Greek or elaborated Roman Doric order had not been very widely used, though "Tuscan" types of round capitals were always popular, especially in less formal buildings. It was sometimes used in military contexts, for example the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Hospital_Chelsea" title="Royal Hospital Chelsea">Royal Hospital Chelsea</a> (1682 onwards, by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a>). The first engraved illustrations of the Greek Doric order dated to the mid-18th century. Its appearance in the new phase of <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">Classicism</a> brought with it new connotations of high-minded primitive simplicity, seriousness of purpose, noble sobriety.</p>
<p>In Germany it suggested a contrast with the French, and in the United States <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republican</a> virtues. In a customs house, Greek Doric suggested incorruptibility; in a Protestant church a Greek Doric porch promised a return to an untainted early church; it was equally appropriate for a library, a bank or a trustworthy public utility. The revived Doric did not return to <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> until 1789, <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_Baroque#Final_period" title="Sicilian Baroque">when a French architect</a> researching the ancient Greek temples designed an entrance to the Botanical Gardens in <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a>.</p>
