<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tomoe</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"Mitsudomoe" redirects here. For the media franchise, see <a href="/wiki/Mitsudomoe_(manga)" title="Mitsudomoe (manga)">Mitsudomoe (manga)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Tomoe_(disambiguation)" title="Tomoe (disambiguation)">Tomoe (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <i><b>tomoe</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">巴 or 鞆絵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, ともえ)</span> and <i>tomowe</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ともゑ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in its archaic form, is a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract</a> <a href="/wiki/Shape" title="Shape">shape</a> described as a swirl that resembles a <a href="/wiki/Comma" title="Comma">comma</a> or the usual form of a <a href="/wiki/Magatama" title="Magatama">magatama</a>. The origin of tomoe is uncertain. Some think that it originally meant <i>tomoe</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">鞆絵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, or drawings on <i>tomo</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">鞆</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, a round arm protector used by an archer, whereas others see tomoe as stylized magatama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nihon_no_Kamon_Taizen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nihon_no_Kamon_Taizen-1">[1]</a></sup> It is a common design element in Japanese family emblems <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">家紋</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mon_(crest)" title="Mon (crest)">kamon</a></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> and <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporate</a> <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logos</a>, particularly in triplicate whorls known as <b>mitsudomoe</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">三つ巴</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. Some view the mitsudomoe as representative of the threefold division (Man, Earth, and Sky) at the heart of the <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> religion. Originally, it was associated with the Shinto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_god" title="War god">war deity</a> <a href="/wiki/Hachiman" title="Hachiman">Hachiman</a>, and through that was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> as their traditional symbol. One mitsudomoe variant, the Hidari Gomon, is the traditional symbol of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ry%C5%ABky%C5%AB_Kingdom" title="Ryūkyū Kingdom">Ryūkyū Kingdom</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koyasan" title="Koyasan">Koyasan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shingon" title="Shingon">Shingon</a> sect of Buddhism uses the Hidari Gomon as a visual representation of the cycle of life.</p>
<p>The two-fold tomoe is almost identical in its design elements to the Chinese symbol known as a <a href="/wiki/Taijitu" title="Taijitu">taijitu</a>, while the three-fold tomoe is very similar to the Korean <a href="/wiki/Taegeuk#Tricolored_Taegeuk" title="Taegeuk">tricolored taegeuk</a>. Also note that the negative space in between the swirls of a four-fold tomoe, forms the shape of a stylized <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a>, which is fairly prominent in many Indian religions such as <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>. On the opposite side of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque</a> <a href="/wiki/Lauburu" title="Lauburu">lauburu</a> and some forms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celt" title="Celt">Celtic</a> spiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triskele" title="Triskele">triskele</a> resemble small groups of tomoe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
