<p><b>.eu</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Country_code_top-level_domain" title="Country code top-level domain">country code top-level domain</a> (ccTLD) for the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Launched on 7 December 2005, the domain is available for organisations in and residents of <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">EU member states</a>. The TLD is administered by <a href="/wiki/EURid" title="EURid">EURid</a>, a consortium originally consisting of the national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLD</a> registry operators of Belgium, Sweden, and Italy, joined later by the national registry operator of the Czech Republic. Trademark owners were able to submit registrations through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunrise_process" title="Sunrise process">sunrise period</a>, in an effort to prevent <a href="/wiki/Cybersquatting" title="Cybersquatting">cybersquatting</a>. Full registration started on 7 April 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The .eu TLD was approved by <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> on 22 March 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-approval_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-approval-3">[3]</a></sup> and put in the Internet <a href="/wiki/DNS_root_zone" title="DNS root zone">root zone</a> on 2 May 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Even though the EU is not a country (it is a <a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a> <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_organization" title="Intergovernmental organization">intergovernmental</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supranational" title="Supranational">supranational</a> organisation), there are precedents of issuing top-level domains to other entities—e.g. <a href="/wiki/.nato" title=".nato">.nato</a>. See also <a href="/wiki/GeoTLD" title="GeoTLD">GeoTLD</a>.</p>
<p><i>.eu.int</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Subdomain" title="Subdomain">subdomain</a> most used by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/.int" title=".int">.int</a> <a href="/wiki/Generic_top-level_domain" title="Generic top-level domain">generic top-level domain</a> (gTLD) for international bodies, until 9 May 2006. The .eu domain (ccTLD) was launched in December 2005, and because of this most .eu.int domain names changed to .<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europa.eu" title="Europa.eu">europa.eu</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_day" title="Europe day">Europe day</a>, 9 May 2006.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunrise_period">Sunrise period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sunrise period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunrise_Period" title="Sunrise Period">Sunrise Period</a> was broken into two phases. The first phase, which began on 7 December 2005 was to facilitate applications by registrants with prior rights based on trademarks and geographic names. The second phase began on 7 February 2006 and covered company, trade and personal names. In the case of all Sunrise applications, the application needed to be accompanied by documents proving the claim to ownership of a certain right. The decision was then made by <a href="/wiki/PricewaterhouseCoopers" title="PricewaterhouseCoopers">PricewaterhouseCoopers</a> Belgium, which had been chosen as the validation agent by <a href="/wiki/EURid" title="EURid">EURid</a>.</p>
<p>On 7 February 2006, the registry was opened for company, trade and personal names. In the first 15 minutes, there were 27,949 total applications, and after one hour, 71,235.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landrush">Landrush</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landrush">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 7 April 2006 at 11 am CET registration became possible for non-trademark holders. Most people requesting domains had asked their registrars to put their requested domains in a queue, ensuring the best chance to register a domain. This way more than 700,000 domains were registered during the first 4 hours of operation. Some large registrars like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_Daddy" title="Go Daddy">Go Daddy</a> and small registrars like <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dotster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dotster (page does not exist)">Dotster</a> suffered from long queues and unresponsiveness, allowing people to 'beat the queue' by registering through a registrar that had already processed its queue. By August 2006, 2 Million .eu domains had been registered. It is now the fourth-largest ccTLD in Europe, after <a href="/wiki/.de" title=".de">.de</a>, <a href="/wiki/.uk" title=".uk">.uk</a> and <a href="/wiki/.nl" title=".nl">.nl</a>, and is one of the largest internationally.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bob_Parsons" title="Bob Parsons">Bob Parsons</a>, CEO and co-founder of Go Daddy, criticized the landrush process designed by EURid. Particularly, he condemned the use of shell companies by some registrars. In his blog, he stated "These companies, instead of only registering their real active registrars, created hundreds of new "phantom" registrars."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landrush_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-landrush-5">[5]</a></sup> Parsons cited a group of about 400 companies, all with similar address and contact information based in New York, each registered as an <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">LLC</a>; in his opinion, these were phantom registrars "created to hijack the .EU landrush."</p>
<p>These "phantom" registrars effectively had hundreds of opportunities of registering a domain whereas a genuine registrar effectively only had one opportunity to register the same domain. Thus some registrants were crowded out of the .eu landrush process and many generic .eu domain names are now owned by the companies using these "phantom" registrars.</p>
<p>Patrik Lindén, spokesman for EURid at the time, denied the allegations by Parsons, stating that "[EURid] verified that each registrar was an individual legal entity. Each had to sign an agreement with us, and prepay €10,000."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refutation_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-refutation-6">[6]</a></sup> Parsons didn't dispute that each registrar was a separate legal entity, but noted that creating such entities was trivial: "Mr. Linden seemed proud that the EURid registry verified that each applicant was a legal entity before it was accredited. Take a moment and think about what that means. You can form a “legal entity” for $50 – an LLC – and you are good to go. Is that what we want a registry to do? Don’t we want them instead to make sure that the organization it allows to provide end-users with its domain names – especially Europe’s very own domain name – are actually in the domain name registration business?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entity_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-entity-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The EURid organisation investigated some allegations of abuse, and in July 2006 announced the suspension of over 74,000 domain names and that they were suing 400 registrars for breach of contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The status of the domains was changed from ACTIVE to ON-HOLD. This meant that the domains could not be moved or have their ownership changed. The registrars also lost their access to the EURid registration database meaning that they could no longer register .eu domain names. The legal action relates to the practice of <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_warehousing" title="Domain name warehousing">Domain name warehousing</a>, whereby large numbers of domain names are registered, often by registrars, with the intention of subsequently selling them on to third parties. EURid rules state that applications for domains can only be made after a legitimate application has been made to a registrar. The 74,000 applications were made in the name of only three Cyprus registered companies— Ovidio Ltd, Fausto Ltd and Gabino Ltd.</p>
<p>The affected registrars, joined in the action by the affected registrants obtained a provisional order from the Court of First Instance in Brussels, Belgium on 27 September 2006. The court ordered EURid to release the blocked domain names or else pay a fine of €25,000 per hour for each affected domain name. EURid complied with the court order and changed the status of the domains from ON HOLD to ACTIVE and restored EURid registration database access to the affected registrars.</p>
<p>The main legal action, that of EURid seeking the registrar agreements between EURid and the registrars in question to be dissolved has still to be heard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_by_the_European_Union_institutions">Use by the European Union institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use by the European Union institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europa_(web_portal)" title="Europa (web portal)">Europa (web portal)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Second-level_domain" title="Second-level domain">second-level domain</a> .europa.eu has been reserved for EU institution sites, with institutions and agencies making the switch from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/.eu.int" title=".eu.int">.eu.int</a> to .<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europa.eu" title="Europa.eu">europa.eu</a> domains on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_day#Europe_Day" title="Europe day">Europe day</a> of 9 May 2006.</p>

<p>The main users of .eu domains are websites with pan-European or cross-border intentions and audiences. It is often used to emphasise the 'European identity' of a website, as opposed to the website having a strictly national ccTLD or global "dotcom" nature. Alternative (<a href="/wiki/Domain_hack" title="Domain hack">opportunistic</a>) uses include <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque</a> webpages (as the initial letters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euskadi" title="Euskadi">Euskadi</a> or the language <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euskara" title="Euskara">Euskara</a>) and Romanian, Portuguese, or <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galician</a> personal sites, as <i>eu</i> is the equivalent of the English pronoun 'I' in those languages.</p>
<p>In most countries of the EU, the national ccTLDs have the major share of the market with the remainder spread over <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a>/<a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a>/<a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a>/<a href="/wiki/.info" title=".info">.info</a>/<a href="/wiki/.biz" title=".biz">.biz</a>. As a result of this, .eu has had an uphill battle to gain a significant share of these national markets. The dominant players tend to be the national ccTLD and .com. The other TLDs such as <a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a>, <a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a> and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/.info" title=".info">.info</a> and <a href="/wiki/.biz" title=".biz">.biz</a> have progressively smaller shares of these national markets.</p>
<p>Over one year after the launch of .eu (5 July 2007), the number of <a href="/wiki/.de" title=".de">.de</a> domains registered was 11,079,557 according to the <a class="external text" href="http://www.denic.de/en/domains/statistiken/index.html" rel="nofollow">German .de registry's statistics page</a>, while number of German owned .eu domains according to <a class="external text" href="http://status.eurid.eu/registered.html" rel="nofollow">EURid's statistics page</a> was 796,561. The number of <a href="/wiki/.uk" title=".uk">.uk</a> domains registered was 6,038,732 according to <a class="external text" href="http://www.nominet.org.uk/intelligence/statistics/registration/" rel="nofollow">.uk registry Nominet's statistics page</a>. The number of apparently UK owned .eu domains was 344,584.</p>

<ul>
<li>Some domain registrants use their .eu website as a web portal containing a list of their national websites with national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLDs</a>.</li>
<li>Other registrants have registered a .eu domain name to protect the brand name of their main website or domain, and redirect visitors to their pre-existing national ccTLD or .com website. <i>(example: <a class="external text" href="http://www.champagne.eu/" rel="nofollow">www.champagne.eu</a>)</i></li>
<li>12.8% of .eu websites are parking pages with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pay_per_click" title="Pay per click">Pay Per Click</a> advertisements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurid.eu_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurid.eu-9">[9]</a></sup> ISPs and web hosters will often point unused domains to a parking webpage with PPC advertising. This percentage does not include .eu domains that are pointed to holding pages or not set up in DNS.</li>
<li>26% of .eu domain names are redirects for existing national ccTLD or .com websites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurid.eu_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-eurid.eu-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Domain_name_speculation" title="Domain name speculation">Domain name speculation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_warehousing" title="Domain name warehousing">Domain name warehousing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cybersquatting" title="Cybersquatting">cybersquatting</a> are always features of the launch of any new TLD; however, this was more widespread in the case of the .eu launch, as seen below.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to page 20 of <a href="/wiki/EURid" title="EURid">EURid</a>'s <a class="external text" href="http://www.eurid.eu/files/2006_annual_report.pdf" rel="nofollow">Annual Report for 2006</a>, the breakdown of .eu domain ownership figures on 31 December 2006 was:</p>
<ul>
<li>Registrants with more than 10,000 domains: 6</li>
<li>Registrants with 5,000–9,999 domains: 18</li>
<li>Registrants with 1,000–4,999 domains: 64</li>
<li>Registrants with 100-999 domains: 1,257</li>
<li>Registrants with 10–99 domains: 20,886</li>
<li>Registrants with 6–9 domains: 22,933</li>
<li>Registrants with 5 domains: 13,200 – (66,000 domains)</li>
<li>Registrants with 4 domains: 23,007 – (92,028 domains)</li>
<li>Registrants with 3 domains: 42,887 – (128,661 domains)</li>
<li>Registrants with 2 domains: 115,543 – (231,086 domains)</li>
<li>Registrants with 1 domain: 610,679</li>
</ul>
<p>The number of registrants with five domains or fewer registered in .eu ccTLD was, according to these statistics, 805,316. These registrants accounted for 1,128,454 domains out of 2,444,947 .eu domains registered as of 31 December 2006. These registrations, typically those of individuals and companies protecting their brand, only represent 46% of the number of registered .eu domains.</p>
<p>It had been actively targeted during the Sunrise period by speculators using fast track Benelux trademarks to create <i>prior rights</i> on various high value generic terms and during the landrush by speculators using EU front companies in the UK and Cyprus to register large numbers of domains. While speculative activity occurred with the launch of other domains, it was the scale of the activity that called into question the competence of EURid in protecting the integrity of eu ccTLD.</p>
<p>The number of .eu domain registrations during the year after the landrush 7 April 2006 to 6 April 2007 seems to have peaked at approximately 2.6 million .eu domains. The market adjustment that follows a landrush in any domain name extension ensures that the number of registered domains will fall as many speculative domain registrations that failed to be resold will not be renewed. This is sometimes referred to as the <i>Junk Dump</i>. On the morning of 7 April 2007, the number of active .eu domains stood at 2,590,160 with approximately 15,000 domains having been deleted since 5 April 2007.</p>
<p>Approximately 1.5 million .eu domains were up for renewal in April 2007. The EURid registry software is based on the DNS. be software and domains are physically renewed at the end of the month of their anniversary of registration. This process differs from more sophisticated registries like that of <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TLD" title="TLD">TLD</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLDs</a> that operate on a daily basis. As with any post-landrush phase, an extension shrinks as the <i>Junk Dump</i> takes effect.</p>
<p>The extent of the shrinkage of .eu ccTLD is difficult to estimate because EURid does not publish detailed statistics on the number of new domains registered each day. Instead it provides only a single figure for the number of active domains. The number of new registrations are combined with numbers of domains registered. Approximately 250,000 .eu domains were either deleted or moved into quarantine by 30 April 2007. In the intervening years the renewal rate has stabilised to approximately 80%, which is above the industry average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyrillic_domain">Cyrillic domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cyrillic domain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>.ею</b> , a top level domain using Cyrillic letters was put in operation in 1 June 2016. Cyrillic letters are used in Bulgaria. ею can be transcribed eyu. Domain names shall use Cyrillic letters (digits or hyphens allowed); in fact new Cyrillic domains shall from the .ею launch date use .ею, not .eu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Older Cyrillic domains under .eu were cloned into .ею at its launch.</p>
