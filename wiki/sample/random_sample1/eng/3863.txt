<p><b>Dominique Pire</b> (full name: <b>Georges Charles Clement Ghislain Pire</b>; February 10, 1910 – January 30, 1969) was a <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> Dominican <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friar</a> whose work helping <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refugees" title="Refugees">refugees</a> in post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Europe saw him receive the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> in 1958. On Dec. 11, 1958 Pire delivered his <i>Nobel Lecture</i> entitled "Brotherly Love: Foundation of Peace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_biography">Early biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominique_Pire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pire was born in <a href="/wiki/Dinant" title="Dinant">Dinant</a>, Belgium. He was the eldest child of four born to Georges Pire, Sr., a civic official, and Berthe (Ravet) Pire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the outbreak of the First World War in 1914 Pire's family fled from Belgium to France in a boat to escape advancing German troops. After the armistice of 1918 the family was able to return to Dinant, which had been reduced to ruins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominique_Pire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pire studied Classics and Philosophy at the <i>Collège de Bellevue</i> and at the age of eighteen entered the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> priory of La Sarte in <a href="/wiki/Huy" title="Huy">Huy</a>. He took his final vows on 23 September 1932, adopting the name Dominique, after the Order's founder. He then studied theology and social science at the Pontifical International Institute <i>Angelicum</i>, the future <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_University_of_Saint_Thomas_Aquinas" title="Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas">Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas, <i>Angelicum</i></a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, where he obtained his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Sacred_Theology" title="Doctor of Sacred Theology">doctorate in theology</a> in 1936 with a thesis entitled <i>L’Apatheia ou insensibilité irréalisable et destructrice</i> (<i>Apatheia or unrealisable and destructive insensitivity</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He then returned to the Studium of La Sarte where he taught sociology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominique_Pire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After completing his studies Pire returned to the priory at La Sarte, in <a href="/wiki/Huy" title="Huy">Huy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> where he dedicated himself to helping poor families live according to their dignity. During the second world war, Pire served as chaplain to the Belgian resistance, actively participating in its activities, such as helping smuggle Allied pilots out of the country. He received several medals for this service after the war.</p>
<p>In 1949, he began studying issues relating to postwar refugees (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Displaced_Person" title="Displaced Person">Displaced Persons</a> [DP]) and wrote a book about them, entitled <i>Du Rhin au Danube avec 60,000 D. P.</i>. He founded an organisation to help them. The organisation established sponsorships for refugee families, and during the 1950s built a number of villages in Austria and Germany to help house many refugees. Although a Dominican friar, Dominique Pire always refused to mix his personal faith with his commitments on behalf of social justice, a decision that was not always understood by his religious superiors.</p>
<p>After winning the Peace Prize, Pire also helped found a "Peace University" to raise global understanding. Later convinced that peace would not be achievable without the eradication of poverty, he founded "Islands of Peace", an NGO dedicated to the long term development of rural populations in developing countries. Projects were started in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</p>
<p>He died at <i>Louvain Roman Catholic Hospital</i> on January 30, 1969, from complications following surgery.</p>
<p>More than 30 years after his death, the four organizations he founded are still active. In 2008 a program was established in honour of his work at the Las Casas Institute at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackfriars_Hall" title="Blackfriars Hall">Blackfriars Hall</a>, University of Oxford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
