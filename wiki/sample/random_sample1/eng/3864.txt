<p><b>Dominique Jean Larrey</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[larɛ]</a></span>; 8 July 1766 – 25 July 1842) was a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgeon</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Arm%C3%A9e" title="Grand Armée">Grand Armée</a> and an important innovator in <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_medicine" title="Battlefield medicine">battlefield medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triage" title="Triage">triage</a>. He is often considered the first modern military surgeon.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominique_Jean_Larrey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larrey was born in the little village of <a href="/wiki/Beaud%C3%A9an" title="Beaudéan">Beaudéan</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> as the son of a shoemaker, who later moved to <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a>. Larrey was orphaned at the age of 13. He was then raised by his uncle Alexis, who was chief surgeon in <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>. After serving a 6-year apprenticeship, he went to Paris to study under <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph_Desault" title="Pierre-Joseph Desault">Pierre-Joseph Desault</a>, who was chief surgeon at the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel-Dieu_de_Paris" title="Hôtel-Dieu de Paris">Hôtel-Dieu de Paris</a>. His studies were cut short by war. Larrey went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brest_(France)" title="Brest (France)">Brest</a> where he was appointed in the navy and gave lectures. In 1788 he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>. In 1789 he was back in Paris and finished his thesis on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eskimos" title="Eskimos">Eskimos</a>. He cooperated with <a href="/wiki/Jean-Nicolas_Corvisart" title="Jean-Nicolas Corvisart">Jean-Nicolas Corvisart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marie_Fran%C3%A7ois_Xavier_Bichat" title="Marie François Xavier Bichat">Marie François Xavier Bichat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rapha%C3%ABl_Bienvenu_Sabatier" title="Raphaël Bienvenu Sabatier">Raphaël Bienvenu Sabatier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Les_Invalides" title="Les Invalides">Les Invalides</a>. In 1792, during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition" title="War of the First Coalition">War of the First Coalition</a> he joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Rhine_(France)" title="Army of the Rhine (France)">Army of the Rhine</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> he met with <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Thomas_von_S%C3%B6mmerring" title="Samuel Thomas von Sömmerring">Samuel Thomas von Sömmerring</a>.</p>
<p>During this time, Larrey initiated the modern method of army surgery, field hospitals and the system of army ambulance corps. After seeing the speed with which the carriages of the French <i><a href="/wiki/Horse_artillery" title="Horse artillery">flying artillery</a></i> maneuvered across the battlefields, Larrey adapted them as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flying_Ambulances&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flying Ambulances (page does not exist)">Flying Ambulances</a></i> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.acep.org/About-Us/The-Revolutionary-Flying-Ambulance-of-Napoleon-s-Surgeon/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> for rapid transport of the wounded and manned them with trained crews of drivers, corpsmen and litterbearers. Larrey also increased the mobility and improved the organization of field hospitals, effectively creating a forerunner of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_Army_Surgical_Hospital" title="Mobile Army Surgical Hospital">MASH</a> units. He established a rule for the triage of war casualties, treating the wounded according to the seriousness of their injuries and urgency of need for medical care, regardless of their rank or nationality. Soldiers of enemy armies, as well as those of the French and their allies, were treated.</p>

<p>In 1794 he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a>, where he met for the first time with Napoleon. He married the paintress <a href="/wiki/Marie-%C3%89lisabeth_Laville-Leroux" title="Marie-Élisabeth Laville-Leroux">Marie-Élisabeth Laville-Leroux</a>. In Spain he fell ill and was sent back to Paris. He became professor at <a href="/wiki/Val-de-Gr%C3%A2ce" title="Val-de-Grâce">Val-de-Grâce</a> but was appointed as surgeon-in-chief of the Napoleonic armies in Italy in 1797, and went to Egypt the year after. In the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Acre_(1799)" title="Siege of Acre (1799)">Battle of Acre</a> he was wounded. In 1801 he was back in France.</p>
<p>Larrey was made a Commandeur of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" title="Légion d'honneur">Légion d'honneur</a> on 12 May 1807. He joined in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aspern-Essling" title="Battle of Aspern-Essling">Battle of Aspern-Essling</a>, where he operated on Marshall <a href="/wiki/Jean_Lannes" title="Jean Lannes">Jean Lannes</a> and amputated one of his legs in two minutes. He became the favorite of the Emperor, who commented, "If the army ever erects a monument to express its gratitude, it should do so in honor of Larrey", he was ennobled as a <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a> on the field of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wagram" title="Battle of Wagram">Wagram</a> in 1809. In 1811, Baron Larrey co-led the surgical team that performed a pre-anesthetic <a href="/wiki/Mastectomy" title="Mastectomy">mastectomy</a> on <a href="/wiki/Frances_Burney" title="Frances Burney">Frances Burney</a> in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Her detailed account of this operation gives insight into early 19th century doctor-patient relationships, and early surgical methods in the home of the patient. Larrey was involved in the <a href="/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia" title="French invasion of Russia">French invasion of Russia</a>.</p>

<p>When Napoleon was sent to Elba, Larrey proposed to join him, but the former Emperor refused. At <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Waterloo</a> in 1815 his courage under fire was noticed by the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington</a> who ordered his soldiers not to fire in his direction so as to "give the brave man time to gather up the wounded" and saluted "the courage and devotion of an age that is no longer ours". Trying to escape to the French border, Larrey was taken prisoner by the Prussians who wanted to execute him on the spot. Larrey was recognized by one of the German surgeons, who pleaded for his life. Perhaps partly because he had saved the life of <a href="/wiki/Gebhard_Leberecht_von_Bl%C3%BCcher" title="Gebhard Leberecht von Blücher">Blücher</a>'s son when he was wounded near Dresden and taken prisoner by the French, he was pardoned, invited to Blücher's dinner table as a guest and sent back to France with money and proper clothes. He devoted the remainder of his life to writing, but after the death of Napoleon he started a new medical career in the army as chief-surgeon. In 1826 he visited England, received well by British surgeons. In 1829 he was appointed in the <a href="/wiki/Institut_de_France" title="Institut de France">Institut de France</a>. In 1842 he went to <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, together with his son, and fell ill. Larrey died on his way back in Lyon. His corpse was taken to Paris and buried at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re-Lachaise" title="Père-Lachaise">Père-Lachaise</a>, but his remains were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Les_Invalides" title="Les Invalides">Les Invalides</a> and re-interred near Napoleon's tomb in December 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Larrey's writings are still regarded as valuable sources of surgical and medical knowledge and have been translated into all modern languages. Between 1800 and 1840 at least 28 books or articles were published. His son Hippolyte (born 1808) was surgeon-in-ordinary to the emperor <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NATO_Award">NATO Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominique_Jean_Larrey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: NATO Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dominique-Jean Larrey Award is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Alliance" title="North Atlantic Alliance">North Atlantic Alliance</a>'s highest medical honour. It is bestowed annually by <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>'s senior medical body, the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Chiefs_of_Military_Medical_Services_in_NATO" title="Committee of Chiefs of Military Medical Services in NATO">Committee of Chiefs of Military Medical Services in NATO</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COMEDS" title="COMEDS">COMEDS</a>), which is composed of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgeon_General" title="Surgeon General">Surgeons General</a> of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> and partner nations. It is awarded in recognition of a significant and lasting contribution to <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> multi-nationality and/or interoperability, or to improvements in the provision of health care in NATO missions in the areas of medical support or healthcare development.</p>
