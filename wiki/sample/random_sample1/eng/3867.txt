<p><b>Ulpian</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Gnaeus Domitius Annius Ulpianus</i></span>; c. 170 – 223) was a prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurist</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyre_(Lebanon)" title="Tyre (Lebanon)">Tyrian</a> ancestry. He was considered one of the great legal authorities of his time and was one of the four jurists upon whom decisions were to be based according to the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Citations" title="Law of Citations">Law of Citations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_III" title="Valentinian III">Valentinian III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulpian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exact time and place of his birth are unknown, but the period of his literary activity was between AD 211 and 222. He made his first appearance in public life as assessor in the auditorium of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papinian" title="Papinian">Papinian</a> and member of the council of <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a>; under <a href="/wiki/Caracalla" title="Caracalla">Caracalla</a> he was master of the requests (<i>magister libellorum</i>). <a href="/wiki/Elagabalus" title="Elagabalus">Elagabalus</a> (also known as Heliogabalus) banished him from <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, but on the accession of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Severus" title="Alexander Severus">Severus Alexander</a> (222) he was reinstated, and finally became the emperor's chief adviser and <i>praefectus praetorio</i>. His curtailment of the privileges granted to the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian Guard</a> by <a href="/wiki/Elagabalus" title="Elagabalus">Elagabalus</a> provoked their enmity, and he narrowly escaped their vengeance; ultimately he was murdered in the palace, in the course of a riot between the soldiers and the mob.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulpian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His works include <i>Ad Sabinum</i>, a commentary on the <i>jus civile</i>, in over 50 books; <i>Ad edictum</i>, a commentary on the <i>Edict</i>, in 83 books; collections of opinions, responses and disputations; books of rules and institutions; treatises on the functions of the different magistrates — one of them, the <i>De officio proconsulis libri x.</i>, being a comprehensive exposition of the criminal law; monographs on various statutes, on testamentary trusts, and a variety of other works. His writings altogether have supplied to <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian</a>'s <i>Digest</i> about a third of its contents, and his commentary on the <i>Edict</i> alone about a fifth. As an author, he is characterized by doctrinal exposition of a high order, judiciousness of criticism, and lucidity of arrangement, style and language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> He is also credited with <a href="/wiki/Ulpian%27s_life_table" title="Ulpian's life table">the first</a> <a href="/wiki/Life_table" title="Life table">life table</a> ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Domitii Ulpiani fragmenta</i>, consisting of 29 titles, were first edited by Tilius (Paris, 1549). Other editions are by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustav_von_Hugo" title="Gustav von Hugo">Hugo</a> (Berlin, 1834), Booking (Bonn, 1836), containing fragments of the first book of the <i>Institutiones</i> discovered by Endlicher at Vienna in 1835, and in Girard's <i>Textes de droit romain</i> (Paris, 1890).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulpian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It had been assumed for a long time that Ulpian of Tyre was a model for <a href="/wiki/Athenaeus" title="Athenaeus">Athenaeus</a>' Ulpian in <i><a href="/wiki/Deipnosophistae" title="Deipnosophistae">The Deipnosophists</a></i> — or <i>The Banquet of the Learned</i>. Athenaeus makes 'Ulpian' out to be a grammarian and philologist, characterised by his customary interjections: "Where does this word occur in writing?". He is represented as a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/symposiarch" title="wiktionary:symposiarch">symposiarch</a> and he occupies a couch alone; his death is passed over in silence in Book XV 686 c. Scholars today agree that <a href="/wiki/Athenaeus" title="Athenaeus">Athenaeus</a>'s Ulpian is not the historical Ulpian, but possibly his father.</p>
<p>The date of the real Ulpian's death in 223 AD. has been wrongly used to estimate the date of completion of <i>The Deipnosophists</i>.</p>
<p>In the study of Law, he is mostly remembered for the phrase "Juris praecepta sunt haec: honeste vivere, alterum non laedere, suum cuique tribuere", which may be translated as: "Such are the [basic] principles of the Law: live honestly, do not offend others, give to each person what is due".</p>
