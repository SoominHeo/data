<p><b>Masahiro Doi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">土井 正博</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Doi Masahiro</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (born December 8, 1943) is a Japanese former professional baseball <a href="/wiki/Outfielder" title="Outfielder">outfielder</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Professional_Baseball" title="Nippon Professional Baseball">Nippon Professional Baseball</a>. He played for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kintetsu_Buffaloes" title="Kintetsu Buffaloes">Kintetsu Buffaloes</a> from 1962 to 1974 and the <a href="/wiki/Saitama_Seibu_Lions" title="Saitama Seibu Lions">Taiheiyo Club / Crown Lighter / Seibu Lions</a> from 1975 to 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Despite a stellar 20-year career as a slugger (including 15 <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Professional_Baseball_All-Star_Series" title="Nippon Professional Baseball All-Star Series">All-Star</a> team selections),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BR-1">[1]</a></sup> Doi never made it to the postseason or got a chance to play in the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Series" title="Japan Series">Japan Series</a>.</p>
<p>He is the current batting coach for the <a href="/wiki/Chunichi_Dragons" title="Chunichi Dragons">Chunichi Dragons</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masahiro_Doi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Kashiwara,_Osaka" title="Kashiwara, Osaka">Kashiwara, Osaka</a>, Doi attended Daitetsu High School, dropping out before graduation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Doi led the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_League" title="Pacific League">Pacific League</a> in hits in 1964 and 1967 with 168 and 147 respectively. He was a 1968 <a href="/wiki/Best_Nine_Award" title="Best Nine Award">Best Nine Award</a>-winner as an outfielder.</p>
<p>Doi was a bit player in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Mist_Scandal_(Japanese_baseball)" title="Black Mist Scandal (Japanese baseball)">Black Mist Scandal</a>, which embroiled NPB from 1979–1971. In July 1970 Doi was prosecuted for illegal gambling. He was later suspended by the league for a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-BR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following season was the best of Doi's career, as he hit .309 with 40 home runs, 113 RBI, and a .998 <a href="/wiki/On-base_plus_slugging" title="On-base plus slugging">OPS</a>.</p>
<p>In 1975, his first season with the Lions, Doi led the Pacific League in home runs, with 34.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-BR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Doi finished his career among the all-time NPB leaders with 465 home runs (<a href="/wiki/List_of_top_Nippon_Professional_Baseball_home_run_hitters" title="List of top Nippon Professional Baseball home run hitters">11th all-time</a>) and 1,400 RBI (12th all-time).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-BR-1">[1]</a></sup> WIth more than 2,000 career hits, he became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Meikyukai" title="Meikyukai">Meikyukai</a>, or the Golden Players Club.</p>
<p>After retiring from playing, he became a coach with the <a href="/wiki/Saitama_Seibu_Lions" title="Saitama Seibu Lions">Lions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-BR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
