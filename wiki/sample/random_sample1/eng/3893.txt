<p><b>Doyle Bramhall II</b> (born 24 December 1968)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is an American musician, producer, guitarist, and songwriter known for his work with <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Waters" title="Roger Waters">Roger Waters</a> and many others. He is the son of the songwriter and drummer <a href="/wiki/Doyle_Bramhall" title="Doyle Bramhall">Doyle Bramhall</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doyle_Bramhall_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bramhall was born in Texas and lived half of his life in Northern California. His father, <a href="/wiki/Doyle_Bramhall" title="Doyle Bramhall">Doyle Bramhall</a>, Sr., played drums for the legendary bluesmen <a href="/wiki/Lightnin%27_Hopkins" title="Lightnin' Hopkins">Lightnin' Hopkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_King" title="Freddie King">Freddie King</a> and was a lifelong collaborator with his childhood friends <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Ray_Vaughan" title="Stevie Ray Vaughan">Stevie Ray Vaughan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Vaughan" title="Jimmie Vaughan">Jimmie Vaughan</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doyle_Bramhall_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Bramhall was 18, he toured with Jimmie Vaughan's band, <a href="/wiki/The_Fabulous_Thunderbirds" title="The Fabulous Thunderbirds">The Fabulous Thunderbirds</a>. Two years later he co-founded the blues rock band <a href="/wiki/Arc_Angels" title="Arc Angels">Arc Angels</a> with fellow Austinite <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Sexton" title="Charlie Sexton">Charlie Sexton</a> and members from Stevie Ray Vaughan's rhythm section, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Layton" title="Chris Layton">Chris Layton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Shannon" title="Tommy Shannon">Tommy Shannon</a>. In 1996,he released his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Doyle_Bramhall_II_(album)" title="Doyle Bramhall II (album)">Doyle Bramhall II</a>.</i> Following the 1999 release of <i><a href="/wiki/Jellycream" title="Jellycream">Jellycream</a></i>, Bramhall's second album and first for RCA, he received phone calls from both <a href="/wiki/Roger_Waters" title="Roger Waters">Roger Waters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Bramhall joined Waters on his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_the_Flesh_(1999-2002_concert_tour_by_Roger_Waters)" title="In the Flesh (1999-2002 concert tour by Roger Waters)">In the Flesh tour</a>, playing lead guitar and providing background and lead vocals, replacing Pink Floyd's lead singer and guitarist <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a> in the Pink Floyd songs. His performances were recorded on the subsequent live album and DVD, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Flesh_%E2%80%93_Live" title="In the Flesh – Live">In the Flesh – Live</a></i>. Bramhall also played guitar on Clapton's <i><a href="/wiki/Riding_with_the_King_(B.B._King_and_Eric_Clapton_album)" title="Riding with the King (B.B. King and Eric Clapton album)">Riding With The King</a></i>, a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/B.B._King" title="B.B. King">B.B. King</a>. The album included versions of Clapton's and King's songs, and covers of blues songs—including two Bramhall-penned tracks originally from <i>Jellycream</i>.</p>
<p>In 2001, Bramhall released <i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_(Doyle_Bramhall_II_album)" title="Welcome (Doyle Bramhall II album)">Welcome</a></i> with his new band, Smokestack. The album's lead single, "Green Light Girl," was somewhat of a hit in the blues world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2006, Bramhall and Smokestack released a live DVD entitled <i>Live at the Great Wall of China</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Two audio tracks from the DVD were released on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> under the title of <i>Doyle Bramhall II (Live At the Great Wall of China)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In th fhe following years, Bramhall collaborated with artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-Bone_Burnett" title="T-Bone Burnett">T-Bone Burnett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Trucks" title="Derek Trucks">Derek Trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Tedeschi" title="Susan Tedeschi">Susan Tedeschi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gary_Clark,_Jr." title="Gary Clark, Jr.">Gary Clark, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Allman" title="Gregg Allman">Gregg Allman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._John" title="Dr. John">Dr. John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Toussaint" title="Allen Toussaint">Allen Toussaint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Preston" title="Billy Preston">Billy Preston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erykah_Badu" title="Erykah Badu">Erykah Badu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Questlove" title="Questlove">Questlove</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meshell_Ndegeocello" title="Meshell Ndegeocello">Meshell Ndegeocello</a>. Bramhall has produced several records, including <a href="/wiki/Sheryl_Crow" title="Sheryl Crow">Sheryl Crow</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/100_Miles_from_Memphis" title="100 Miles from Memphis">100 Miles from Memphis</a></i> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also joined her on a tour for this album<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Bramhall completed co-production on Eric Clapton's 2013 release <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Sock" title="Old Sock">Old Sock</a></i>, and played guitars on his 2014 release <i><a href="/wiki/The_Breeze:_An_Appreciation_of_JJ_Cale" title="The Breeze: An Appreciation of JJ Cale">The Breeze: An Appreciation of JJ Cale</a></i>. He joined Clapton on his 50th anniversary tour as an opening act and backing musician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He appeared at Clapton's <a href="/wiki/Crossroads_Guitar_Festival" title="Crossroads Guitar Festival">Crossroads Guitar Festival</a> and was recorded for the subsequent live album and DVD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Bramhall played lead guitars on <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>'s 2013 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Diving_Board" title="The Diving Board">The Diving Board</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> he joined the Tedeschi Trucks Band and Sharon Jones &amp; the Dap Kings for the "Wheels of Soul" tour across the United States. He performed both as an opening act and as a rhythm guitarist in the Tedeschi Trucks Band, performing songs ranging from his days with the <a href="/wiki/Arc_Angels" title="Arc Angels">Arc Angels</a> to unreleased songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Also in 2015, he joined the Tedeschi Trucks Band and alumni from the 1970 <a href="/wiki/Joe_Cocker" title="Joe Cocker">Joe Cocker</a> Mad Dogs and Englishmen Tour, including <a href="/wiki/Leon_Russell" title="Leon Russell">Leon Russell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rita_Coolidge" title="Rita Coolidge">Rita Coolidge</a>, Bobby Torres, <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Lennear" title="Claudia Lennear">Claudia Lennear</a>, Chuck Blackwell, <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Polland" title="Pamela Polland">Pamela Polland</a>, Daniel Moore, Matthew Moore, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Stainton" title="Chris Stainton">Chris Stainton</a>, and photographer <a href="/wiki/Linda_Wolf" title="Linda Wolf">Linda Wolf</a> and in a tribute concert to honor Joe Cocker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 30 2016, Bramhall released his 4th studio album titled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rich_Man_(Doyle_Bramhall_II_album)" title="Rich Man (Doyle Bramhall II album)"><i>Rich Man</i></a>. The album consists of 12 originals and a cover of <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Hear_My_Train_A_Comin%27" title="Hear My Train A Comin'">Hear My Train A Comin'</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doyle_Bramhall_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bramhall is notable as one of the few guitarists who plays with his instrument strung upside-down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He plays left-handed, but the instrument is strung upside-down with the high E on the top. This unusual arrangement of the strings gives his playing a unique sound, because he bends the strings by pulling them downwards rather than upwards, the customary approach. Others who have bent strings in this direction include <a href="/wiki/Albert_King" title="Albert King">Albert King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gales" title="Eric Gales">Eric Gales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otis_Rush" title="Otis Rush">Otis Rush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Lynn" title="Barbara Lynn">Barbara Lynn</a>, Coco Montoya and the late blues guitarist <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Healey" title="Jeff Healey">Jeff Healey</a>, who played with his instrument flat on his lap for stability and increased string flexibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doyle_Bramhall_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bramhall was married to former-Prince collaborator <a href="/wiki/Susannah_Melvoin" title="Susannah Melvoin">Susannah Melvoin</a> from 1997 to 2010, and the couple have two daughters. In December 2013, a California court ordered Bramhall to pay his ex-wife $4,332 per month in child support, and $6,378 per month in spousal support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, by December 2016, the sums had allegedly accrued to a point where Melvoin's lawyers took Bramhall to court, and as part of their request of Bramhall asked for asset and earnings statements from Bramhall's girlfriend of four years, actress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renee_Zellweger" title="Renee Zellweger">Renee Zellweger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMail4016710_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMail4016710-20">[20]</a></sup> Having met Bramhall whilst both where students at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Texas" title="University of Texas">University of Texas</a> in the 1980's, Zellweger submitted papers to the court and was subject to cross examination, during which she stated that her personal and professional life are separate from Doyle's case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMail4016710_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-DMail4016710-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discography">Discography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doyle_Bramhall_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
