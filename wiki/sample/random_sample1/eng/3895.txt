<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Telephone tapping</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">For the method of making telephone calls by depressing the hook switch, see <a href="/wiki/Pulse_dialing#Tapping" title="Pulse dialing">Pulse dialing § Tapping</a>.</div>
<p><b>Telephone tapping</b> (also <b>wire tapping</b> or <b>wiretapping</b> in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) is the monitoring of <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> conversations by a third party, often by covert means. The wire tap received its name because, historically, the monitoring connection was an actual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tap_(transformer)" title="Tap (transformer)">electrical tap</a> on the telephone line. Legal wiretapping by a <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government agency</a> is also called <a href="/wiki/Lawful_interception" title="Lawful interception">lawful interception</a>. <b>Passive wiretapping</b> monitors or records the traffic, while <b>active wiretapping</b> alters or otherwise affects it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Telephone_recording_laws" title="Telephone recording laws">Telephone recording laws</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2576" data-file-width="1932" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg/220px-IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg/330px-IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg/440px-IDET2007_Jitka_phone_tapping_device.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Telephone line control device "Jitka", used in late 1960s by Czechoslovakian <a href="/wiki/StB" title="StB">StB</a> to signal line occupancy, and connect a recorder</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Lawful_interception" title="Lawful interception">Lawful interception</a> is officially strictly controlled in many countries to safeguard <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a>; this is the case in all developed <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracies</a>. In theory, telephone tapping often needs to be authorized by a <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">court</a>, and is, again in theory, normally only approved when <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">evidence</a> shows it is not possible to detect <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">criminal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subversion" title="Subversion">subversive</a> activity in less intrusive ways; often the law and regulations require that the crime investigated must be at least of a certain severity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsw3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsw3-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Illegal or unauthorized telephone tapping is often a criminal offense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsw3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-hsw3-3">[3]</a></sup> However, in certain jurisdictions such as <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, courts will accept illegally recorded phone calls without the other party's consent as evidence, but the unauthorized telephone tapping will still be prosecuted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, under the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Intelligence_Surveillance_Act" title="Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act">Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act</a>, federal intelligence agencies can get approval for wiretaps from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Foreign_Intelligence_Surveillance_Court" title="United States Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court">United States Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court</a>, a court with secret proceedings, or in certain circumstances from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">Attorney General</a> without a court order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">law of the United States</a> and most state laws, there is nothing illegal about one of the parties to a telephone call recording the conversation, or giving permission for calls to be recorded or permitting their telephone line to be tapped. However the <a href="/wiki/Telephone_recording_laws" title="Telephone recording laws">telephone recording laws</a> in most U.S. states require only one party to be aware of the recording, while <b>12 states</b> require both parties to be aware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In Nevada, the state legislature enacted a law making it legal for a party to record a conversation if one party to the conversation consented, but the Nevada Supreme Court issued two judicial opinions changing the law and requiring all parties to consent to the recording of a private conversation for it to be legal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is considered better practice to announce at the beginning of a call that the conversation is being recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canadian law, police are allowed to wiretap without the authorization from a court when there is the risk for imminent harm, such as kidnapping or a bomb threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They must believe that the interception is immediately necessary to prevent an unlawful act that could cause serious harm to any person or to property. This was introduced by Rob Nicholson on February 11, 2013, and is also known as Bill C-55. The Supreme Court gave Parliament twelve months to rewrite a new law. Bill C-51 was then released, which transformed the Canadian Security Intelligence Agency<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> from an intelligence gathering agency, to an agency actively engaged in countering national security threats.</p>
<p>Legal protection extends to 'private communications' where the participants would not expect unintended persons to learn the content of the communication. A single participant can legally, and covertly record a conversation. Otherwise police normally need a judicial warrant based upon probable grounds to record a conversation they are not a part of. In order to be valid wiretap authorization must state: 1) the offences being investigated by the wiretap, 2) the type of communication, 3) the identity of the people or places targeted, 4) the period of validity (60 days from issue).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In India, the lawful interception of communication by authorized law enforcement agencies (LEAs) is carried out in accordance with Section 5(2) of the Indian Telegraph Act, 1885 read with Rule 419A of Indian Telegraph (Amendment) Rules, 2007. Directions for interception of any message or class of messages under sub-section (2) of Section 5 of the Indian Telegraph Act, 1885 shall not be issued except by an order made by the Secretary to the Government of India in the Ministry of Home Affairs in the case of Government of India and by the Secretary to the State Government in-charge of the Home Department in the case of a state government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The government has set up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centralized_Monitoring_System" title="Centralized Monitoring System">Centralized Monitoring System</a> (CMS) to automate the process of lawful interception and monitoring of telecommunications technology. The government of India on 2015 December 2 in a reply to parliament question no. 595 on scope, objectives and framework of the CMS has struck a balance between national security, online privacy and free speech informed that to take care of the privacy of citizens, lawful interception and monitoring is governed by the Section 5(2) of Indian Telegraph Act, 1885 read with Rule 419A of Indian Telegraph (Amendment) Rules, 2007 wherein oversight mechanism exists in form of review committee under chairmanship of the Cabinet Secretary at Central Government level and Chief Secretary of the State at the state government level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_use">Official use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Official use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lawful_interception" title="Lawful interception">Lawful interception</a></div>
<p>The contracts or licenses by which the state controls <a href="/wiki/Telephone_company" title="Telephone company">telephone companies</a> often require that the companies must provide access for tapping lines to law enforcement. In the U.S., telecommunications carriers are required by law to cooperate in the interception of communications for law enforcement purposes under the terms of <a href="/wiki/Communications_Assistance_for_Law_Enforcement_Act" title="Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act">Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act</a> (CALEA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchanges</a> were mechanical, a tap had to be installed by technicians, linking circuits together to route the audio signal from the call. Now that many exchanges have been converted to digital technology, tapping is far simpler and can be ordered remotely by computer. This central office switch wiretapping technology using the Advanced Intelligent Network (AIN) was invented by Wayne Howe and Dale Malik at BellSouth's Advanced Technology R&amp;D group in 1995 and was issued as US Patent #5,590,171.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Public_switched_telephone_network" title="Public switched telephone network">Telephone services</a> provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cable_TV" title="Cable TV">cable TV</a> companies also use digital switching technology. If the tap is implemented at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_switch" title="Digital switch">digital switch</a>, the switching computer simply copies the digitized bits that represent the phone conversation to a second line and it is impossible to tell whether a line is being tapped. A well-designed tap installed on a phone wire can be difficult to detect. In some instances some law enforcement may be able to even access a mobile phone's internal microphone even while it isn't actively being used on a phone call (unless the battery is removed or drained).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The noises that some people believe to be telephone taps are simply <a href="/wiki/Crosstalk" title="Crosstalk">crosstalk</a> created by the <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(electronics)" title="Coupling (electronics)">coupling</a> of signals from other phone lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Data on the calling and called number, time of call and duration, will generally be collected automatically on all calls and stored for later use by the <a href="/wiki/Invoice" title="Invoice">billing</a> department of the phone company. These data can be accessed by security services, often with fewer legal restrictions than for a tap. This information used to be collected using special equipment known as <i><a href="/wiki/Pen_register" title="Pen register">pen registers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Trap_and_trace_device" title="Trap and trace device">trap and trace devices</a></i> and U.S. law still refers to it under those names. Today, a list of all calls to a specific number can be obtained by sorting billing records. A telephone tap during which only the call information is recorded but not the contents of the phone calls themselves, is called a <i>pen register</i> tap.</p>
<p>For telephone services via digital exchanges, the information collected may additionally include a log of the type of communications media being used (some services treat data and voice communications differently, in order to conserve bandwidth).</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivore_(FBI)" title="Carnivore (FBI)">Carnivore (FBI)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-official_use">Non-official use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-official use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TelRecAdapter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1464" data-file-width="1188" height="370" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/TelRecAdapter.jpg/300px-TelRecAdapter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/TelRecAdapter.jpg/450px-TelRecAdapter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/TelRecAdapter.jpg/600px-TelRecAdapter.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TelRecAdapter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A telephone recording adapter (in-line tap). The <a href="/wiki/Telephone_plug" title="Telephone plug">phone jack</a> connects to the wall socket while the phone being monitored is connected to the adapter's socket. The <a href="/wiki/Phone_connector_(audio)" title="Phone connector (audio)">audio plug</a> connects to the recording device (computer, tape recorder, etc.).</div>

<p>Conversations can be recorded or monitored unofficially, either by tapping by a third party without the knowledge of the parties to the conversation, or recorded by one of the parties. This may or may not be illegal, according to the circumstances and the jurisdiction.</p>
<p>There are a number of ways to monitor telephone conversations. One of the parties may record the conversation, either on a tape or solid-state recording device, or on a computer running <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Call_recording_software" title="Call recording software">call recording software</a>. The recording, whether overt or covert, may be started manually, automatically by detecting sound on the line (<a href="/wiki/Voice-operated_switch" title="Voice-operated switch">VOX</a>), or automatically whenever the phone is off the hook.</p>
<ul>
<li>using an inductive <a href="/wiki/Coil_tap" title="Coil tap">coil tap</a> (telephone pickup coil) attached to the handset or near the base of the telephone;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>fitting an in-line tap, as discussed below, with a recording output;</li>
<li>using an in-ear microphone while holding the telephone to the ear normally; this picks up both ends of the conversation without too much disparity between the volumes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>more crudely and with lower quality, simply using a speakerphone and recording with a normal microphone</li>
</ul>
<p>The conversation may be monitored (listened to or recorded) covertly by a third party by using an <a href="/wiki/Induction_coil" title="Induction coil">induction coil</a> or a direct electrical connection to the line using a <a href="/wiki/Beige_box_(phreaking)" title="Beige box (phreaking)">beige box</a>. An induction coil is usually placed underneath the base of a telephone or on the back of a telephone handset to pick up the signal inductively. An electrical connection can be made anywhere in the telephone system, and need not be in the same premises as the telephone. Some apparatus may require occasional access to replace batteries or tapes. Poorly designed tapping or transmitting equipment can cause interference audible to users of the telephone.</p>
<p>The tapped signal may either be recorded at the site of the tap or transmitted by radio or over the telephone wires. As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> state-of-the-art equipment operates in the 30–300 GHz range to keep up with telephone technology compared to the 772 kHz systems used in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The transmitter may be powered from the line to be maintenance-free, and only transmits when a call is in progress. These devices are low-powered as not much power can be drawn from the line, but a state-of-the-art receiver could be located as far away as ten kilometers under ideal conditions, though usually located much closer. Research has shown that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">satellite</a> can be used to receive terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">transmissions</a> with a power of a few milliwatts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Any sort of radio transmitter whose presence is suspected is detectable with suitable equipment.</p>
<p>Conversation on many early <a href="/wiki/Cordless_telephone" title="Cordless telephone">cordless telephones</a> could be picked up with a simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_scanner" title="Radio scanner">radio scanner</a> or sometimes even a domestic radio. Widespread digital <a href="/wiki/Spread_spectrum" title="Spread spectrum">spread spectrum</a> technology and <a href="/wiki/Cordless_telephone#Security" title="Cordless telephone">encryption</a> has made eavesdropping increasingly difficult.</p>
<p>A problem with recording a telephone conversation is that the recorded volume of the two speakers may be very different. A simple tap will have this problem. An in-ear microphone, while involving an additional distorting step by converting the electrical signal to sound and back again, in practice gives better-matched volume. Dedicated, and relatively expensive, telephone recording equipment equalizes the sound at both ends from a direct tap much better.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_data">Location data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Location data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">Mobile phones</a> are, in <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a> terms, a major liability. This liability will only increase as the new third-generation (<a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">3G</a>), <a href="/wiki/LTE_(telecommunication)" title="LTE (telecommunication)">LTE</a>, <a href="/wiki/WiMAX" title="WiMAX">WiMAX</a>, and fourth-generation (<a href="/wiki/4G" title="4G">4G</a>) phones are introduced, as the <a href="/wiki/Base_station" title="Base station">base stations</a> will be located closer together. For mobile phones the major threat is the collection of communications data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This data does not only include information about the time, duration, originator and recipient of the call, but also the identification of the base station where the call was made from, which equals its approximate geographical location. This data is stored with the details of the call and has utmost importance for <a href="/wiki/Traffic_analysis" title="Traffic analysis">traffic analysis</a>.</p>
<p>It is also possible to get greater resolution of a phone's location by combining information from a number of cells surrounding the location, which cells routinely communicate (to agree on the next handoff—for a moving phone) and measuring the <a href="/wiki/Timing_advance" title="Timing advance">timing advance</a>, a correction for the speed of light in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_System_for_Mobile_Communications" title="Global System for Mobile Communications">GSM</a> standard. This additional precision must be specifically enabled by the telephone company—it is not part of the network's ordinary operation.</p>

<p>In 1995, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Garza" title="Peter Garza">Peter Garza</a>, a Special Agent with the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Criminal_Investigative_Service" title="Naval Criminal Investigative Service">Naval Criminal Investigative Service</a>, conducted the <a class="external text" href="http://www.usdoj.gov/opa/pr/1996/March96/146.txt" rel="nofollow">first court-ordered Internet wiretap</a> in the United States while investigating Julio Cesar Ardita ("<a class="external text" href="https://fas.org/irp/news/1998/05/arditasnt.htm" rel="nofollow">El Griton</a>").</p>
<p>As technologies emerge, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VoIP" title="VoIP">VoIP</a>, new questions are raised about law enforcement access to communications (see <a href="/wiki/VoIP_recording" title="VoIP recording">VoIP recording</a>). In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> was asked to clarify how the <a href="/wiki/Communications_Assistance_for_Law_Enforcement_Act" title="Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act">Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act</a> (CALEA) related to Internet service providers. The FCC stated that “providers of broadband Internet access and voice over Internet protocol (“VoIP”) services are regulable as “telecommunications carriers” under the Act.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Those affected by the Act will have to provide access to law enforcement officers who need to monitor or intercept communications transmitted through their networks. As of 2009, warrantless surveillance of internet activity has consistently been upheld in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FISA_court" title="FISA court">FISA court</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a> has decided not to consider requirements for wiretapping as part of the process for creating and maintaining IETF standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typically, illegal Internet wiretapping will be conducted via <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> connection to someone's internet by cracking the <a href="/wiki/Wired_Equivalent_Privacy" title="Wired Equivalent Privacy">WEP</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_Protected_Access" title="Wi-Fi Protected Access">WPA</a> key, using a tool such as <a href="/wiki/Aircrack-ng" title="Aircrack-ng">Aircrack-ng</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kismet_(software)" title="Kismet (software)">Kismet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Once in, the intruder will rely on a number of potential tactics, for example an <a href="/wiki/ARP_spoofing" title="ARP spoofing">ARP spoofing</a> attack which will allow the intruder to view <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packet_(information_technology)" title="Packet (information technology)">packets</a> in a tool such as <a href="/wiki/Wireshark" title="Wireshark">Wireshark</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ettercap_(computing)" title="Ettercap (computing)">Ettercap</a>.</p>
<p>One issue that Internet wiretapping is yet to overcome is that of <a href="/wiki/Steganography" title="Steganography">steganography</a>, whereby a user encodes, or “hides”, one file inside another (usually a larger, dense file like a <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> or <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEG</a> image). With modern advancements in encoding technologies, the resulting combined file is essentially indistinguishable to anyone attempting to view it, unless they have the necessary protocol to extract the hidden file.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> US News reported that this technique was commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Osama_bin_Laden" title="Osama bin Laden">Osama bin Laden</a> as a way to communicate with his terrorist cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are a number of steganographic programs available online, such as <a class="external text" href="http://www.jjtc.com/Security/stegtools.htm" rel="nofollow">Wnstorm</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.quickcrypto.com/page22.htm" rel="nofollow">QuickCrypto</a>, and <a class="external text" href="http://www.compris.com/TextHide/en/" rel="nofollow">TextHide</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_phone">Mobile phone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mobile phone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mobile phones have numerous privacy issues. Governments, law enforcement and intelligence services use mobiles to perform surveillance in the UK and the US. They possess technology to activate the microphones in cell phones remotely in order to listen to conversations that take place near to the person who holds the phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roving-bugs_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-roving-bugs-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uk-mobile-bug_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-uk-mobile-bug-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mobile phones are also commonly used to collect location data. While the phone is turned on, the geographical location of a mobile phone can be determined easily (whether it is being used or not), using a technique known as <a href="/wiki/Multilateration" title="Multilateration">multilateration</a> to calculate the differences in time for a signal to travel from the cell phone to each of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_towers" title="Cell towers">cell towers</a> near the owner of the phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc-phone-locate_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-phone-locate-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foxnews-phone-locate_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews-phone-locate-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second generation mobile phones (circa 1978 through 1990) could be easily monitored by anyone with a <a href="/wiki/Scanner_(radio)" title="Scanner (radio)">'scanning all-band receiver'</a> because the system used an analog transmission system-like an ordinary radio transmitter. The third generation digital phones are harder to monitor because they use digitally encoded and compressed transmission. However the government can tap mobile phones with the cooperation of the phone company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It is also possible for organizations with the correct technical equipment to monitor mobile phone communications and decrypt the audio.</p>
<p>To the mobile phones in its vicinity, a device called an "<a href="/wiki/IMSI-catcher" title="IMSI-catcher">IMSI-catcher</a>" pretends to be a legitimate base station of the mobile phone network, thus subjecting the communication between the phone and the network to a <a href="/wiki/Man-in-the-middle_attack" title="Man-in-the-middle attack">man-in-the-middle attack</a>. This is possible because, while the mobile phone has to authenticate itself to the mobile telephone network, the network does not authenticate itself to the phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> There is no defense against IMSI-catcher based eavesdropping, except using end-to-end call encryption; products offering this feature, <a href="/wiki/Secure_telephone" title="Secure telephone">secure telephones</a>, are already beginning to appear on the market, though they tend to be expensive and incompatible with each other, which limits their proliferation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Webtapping">Webtapping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Webtapping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Logging the <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a> of users that access certain websites is commonly called "webtapping".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Webtapping is used to monitor websites that presumably contain dangerous or sensitive materials, and the people that access them. Though it is allowed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA_PATRIOT_Act" title="USA PATRIOT Act">USA PATRIOT Act</a>, it is considered a questionable practice by many citizens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> if not an all-out violation of <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telephones_recording">Telephones recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Telephones recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, anyone is legally allowed to record a conversation, as long as they are involved in the conversation. Canadians are not allowed to record a conversation legally that they are not a part of. The police must apply for a warrant beforehand to legally eavesdrop on the conversation. It must be expected that it will reveal evidence to a crime. State agents are lawfully allowed to record conversations but, to reveal the evidence in court, they must obtain a warrant.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telephone_tapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many state legislatures in the United States enacted statutes that prohibited anybody from listening in on telegraph communication. Telephone wiretapping began in the 1890s, following the invention of the telephone recorder,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and its <a href="/wiki/Constitutionality" title="Constitutionality">constitutionality</a> was established in the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">Prohibition</a>-Era conviction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rum_running" title="Rum running">bootlegger</a> <a href="/wiki/Roy_Olmstead" title="Roy Olmstead">Roy Olmstead</a>. Wiretapping has also been carried out under most Presidents, sometimes with a lawful warrant since the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> ruled it constitutional in 1928. On October 19, 1963, U.S. Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>, who served under <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, authorized the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> to begin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiretapping" title="Wiretapping">wiretapping</a> the communications of Rev. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr" title="Martin Luther King, Jr">Martin Luther King, Jr</a>. The wiretaps remained in place until April 1965 at his home and June 1966 at his office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The history of voice communication technology begins in 1876 with the invention of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>’s telephone. In the 1890s, “law enforcement agencies begin tapping wires on early telephone networks”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSUU_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISSUU-49">[49]</a></sup> Remote voice communications “were carried almost exclusively by circuit-switched systems,” where telephone switches would connect wires to form a continuous circuit and disconnect the wires when the call ended). All other telephone services, such as call forwarding and message taking, were handled by human operators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIENTIFIC_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCIENTIFIC-50">[50]</a></sup> However, the first computerized telephone switch was developed by Bell Labs in 1965. This got rid of standard wiretapping techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSUU_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-ISSUU-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1970s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_fibers" title="Optical fibers">optical fibers</a> become a medium for telecommunications. These fiber lines, which are “long, thin strands of glass that carry signals via laser light”, are more secure than radio, and have become very cheap. From the 1990s to the present, the majority of communications between fixed locations is achieved by fiber. Because these fiber communications are wired, they're given greater protection under U.S. law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSUU_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-ISSUU-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIENTIFIC_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-SCIENTIFIC-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest wiretaps were extra wires —physically inserted to the line between the switchboard and the subscriber— that carried the signal to a pair of earphones and a recorder. Later on wiretaps were installed at the central office on the frames that held the incoming wires.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIENTIFIC_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-SCIENTIFIC-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a> and the subsequent entry of the United States into <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the U.S. House of Representatives held hearings on the legality of wiretapping for national defense. Significant legislation and judicial decisions on the legality and constitutionality of wiretapping had taken place years before World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> However, it took on new urgency at that time of national crisis. The actions of the government regarding wiretapping for the purpose of national defense in the current war on terror have drawn considerable attention and criticism. In the World War II era, the public was also aware of the controversy over the question of the constitutionality and legality of wiretapping. Furthermore, the public was concerned with the decisions that the legislative and judicial branches of the government were making regarding wiretapping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1967 the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that wiretapping (or “intercepting communications”) requires a warrant in <i><a href="/wiki/Katz_v._United_States" title="Katz v. United States">Katz v. United States</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In 1968 Congress passed a law that provided warrants for wiretapping in criminal investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In 1978 the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) created a "secret federal court" for issuing wiretap warrants in national security cases. This was in response to findings from the Watergate break-in, which allegedly uncovered a history of presidential operations that had used surveillance on domestic and foreign political organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, Congress approved the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA), which “requires telephone companies to be able to install more effective wiretaps. In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> (FBI), <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> (DOJ), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms,_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms, and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms, and Explosives</a> (ATF), and <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> (DEA) wanted to expand CALEA requirements to VoIP service.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSUU_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-ISSUU-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIENTIFIC_50-3"><a href="#cite_note-SCIENTIFIC-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) ruled in August 2005 that “broadband-service providers and interconnected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VoIP" title="VoIP">VoIP</a> providers fall within CALEA’s scope. Currently, instant messaging, web boards and site visits are not included in CALEA’s jurisdiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In 2007 Congress amended FISA to “allow the government to monitor more communications without a warrant”. In 2008 President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> expanded the surveillance of internet traffic to and from the U.S. government by signing a national security directive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSUU_49-4"><a href="#cite_note-ISSUU-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_telephone_tapping_case_2004%E2%80%932005" title="Greek telephone tapping case 2004–2005">Greek telephone tapping case 2004–2005</a> more than 100 mobile phone numbers belonging mostly to members of the Greek government, including the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Greece" title="Prime Minister of Greece">Prime Minister of Greece</a>, and top-ranking civil servants were found to have been illegally tapped for a period of at least one year. The Greek government concluded this had been done by a foreign intelligence agency, for security reasons related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Olympic_Games" title="2004 Olympic Games">2004 Olympic Games</a>, by unlawfully activating the lawful interception subsystem of the <a href="/wiki/Vodafone_Greece" title="Vodafone Greece">Vodafone Greece</a> mobile network. An Italian tapping case which surfaced in November 2007 revealed significant manipulation of the news at the national television company <a href="/wiki/RAI" title="RAI">RAI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i> and other media reported a lamplighter disclosed a "Quantico Circuit", a 45-megabit/second DS-3 line linking a carrier's most sensitive network in an affidavit that was the basis for a lawsuit against Verizon Wireless. The circuit provides direct access to all content and all information concerning the origin and termination of telephone calls placed on the Verizon Wireless network as well as the actual content of calls, according to the filing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most recent case of U.S. wiretapping was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSA_warrantless_surveillance_controversy" title="NSA warrantless surveillance controversy">NSA warrantless surveillance controversy</a> discovered in December 2005. It aroused much controversy after then President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> admitted to violating a specific federal statute (FISA) and the warrant requirement of the Fourth Amendment to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. The President claimed his authorization was consistent with other federal statutes (<a href="/wiki/Authorization_for_Use_of_Military_Force_Against_Terrorists" title="Authorization for Use of Military Force Against Terrorists">AUMF</a>) and other provisions of the Constitution, it was necessary to keep America safe from <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a> and could lead to the capture of notorious terrorists responsible for the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in 2001.</p>
<p>One difference between foreign wiretapping and domestic wiretapping is that, when operating in other countries, “American intelligence services could not place wiretaps on phone lines as easily as they could in the U.S.” Also, domestically, wiretapping is regarded as an extreme investigative technique, whereas outside of the country, the interception of communications is huge. The National Security Agency (NSA) “spends billions of dollars every year intercepting foreign communications from ground bases, ships, airplanes and satellites”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIENTIFIC_50-4"><a href="#cite_note-SCIENTIFIC-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>FISA distinguishes between U.S. persons and foreigners, between communications inside and outside the U.S., and between wired and wireless communications. Wired communications within the United States are protected, since intercepting them requires a warrant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIENTIFIC_50-5"><a href="#cite_note-SCIENTIFIC-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
