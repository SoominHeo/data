<p><b>Tokyo Institute of Technology</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">東京工業大学</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tōkyō Kōgyō Daigaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, informally <b>Tokyo Tech</b>, <b>Tokodai</b> or <b>TITech</b>)</span> is a national <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university">research university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Tokyo_Area" title="Greater Tokyo Area">Greater Tokyo Area</a>, Japan. Tokyo Tech is the largest institution for higher education in Japan dedicated to science and technology, and is generally considered to be one of the most prestigious universities in Japan.</p>
<p>Tokyo Tech's main campus is located at Ōokayama on the boundary of <a href="/wiki/Meguro" title="Meguro">Meguro</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cta,_Tokyo" title="Ōta, Tokyo">Ota</a>, with its main entrance facing the <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cokayama_Station" title="Ōokayama Station">Ōokayama Station</a>. Other campuses are located in <a href="/wiki/Suzukakedai_Station" title="Suzukakedai Station">Suzukakedai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tamachi" title="Tamachi">Tamachi</a>. Tokyo Tech is organised into 6 schools, within which there are over 40 departments and research centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Tokyo Tech enrolled 4,734 undergraduates and 1,464 graduate students for 2015-2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It employs around 1,100 faculty members.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_and_early_years_.281881.E2.80.931922.29">Foundation and early years (1881–1922)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation and early years (1881–1922)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tokyo Institute of Technology was founded by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Japan" title="Government of Japan">government of Japan</a> as the Tokyo Vocational School on May 26, 1881,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 14 years after the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>. To accomplish the quick catch-up to the West, the government expected this school to cultivate new modernized craftsmen and engineers. In 1890, it was renamed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Technical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tokyo Technical School (page does not exist)">Tokyo Technical School</a>. In 1901, it changed name to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Higher_Technical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tokyo Higher Technical School (page does not exist)">Tokyo Higher Technical School</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Kant.C5.8D_earthquake_and_World_War_II_.281923.E2.80.931945.29">Great Kantō earthquake and World War II (1923–1945)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Great Kantō earthquake and World War II (1923–1945)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early days, the school was located in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kuramae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kuramae (page does not exist)">Kuramae</a>, the eastern area of the Greater Tokyo Area, where many craftsmens' workshops had been since the old Shogun's era. The buildings in Kuramae campus were destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kantō earthquake">Great Kantō earthquake</a> in 1923. In the following year, the Tokyo Higher Technical School moved from Kuramae to the present site in Ookayama, a south suburb of the Greater Tokyo Area. In 1929 the school became Tokyo Institute of Technology, gaining a status of <a href="/wiki/National_university" title="National university">national university</a>, which allowed the university to award degrees. The university had the Research Laboratory of Building Materials in 1934, and its five years later the Research Laboratory of Resources Utilisation and the Research Laboratory of Precision Machinery were constructed. The Research Laboratory of Ceramic Industry was made in 1943, and one year before the World War Two finished the Research Laboratory of Fuel Science and the Research Laboratory of Electronics were made.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-War_Era_.281946.E2.80.93present.29">Post-War Era (1946–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-War Era (1946–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the new education system was promulgated in 1949 with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_National_School_Establishment_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The National School Establishment Law (page does not exist)">the National School Establishment Law</a>, and Tokyo Institute of Technology was reorganized. Many three-year courses were turned into four-year courses with the start of the School of Engineering this year. The university started graduate programmes in engineering in 1953. In the following year, the five research laboratories were integrated and reorganised into four new labs: the Research Laboratory of Building Materials, the Research Laboratory of Resources Utilization, the Precision and Intelligence Laboratory and the Research Laboratory of Ceramic Industry, and the School of Engineering was renamed the School of Science and Engineering.</p>
<p>Throughout the post-war reconstruction of the 1950s, the high economic growth era of the 1960s, and the aggressive economic era marching to the Bubble Economy of the 1980s, TIT kept providing Japan its leading engineers, researchers, and business persons. Since April 2004, it has been semi-privatized into the National University Incorporation of Tokyo Institute of Technology under a new law<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which applied to all national universities.</p>
<p>Operating the world-class <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputer</a> <b><a href="/wiki/Tsubame_(supercomputer)" title="Tsubame (supercomputer)">Tsubame 2.0</a></b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and making a breakthrough in <a href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity" title="High-temperature superconductivity">high-temperature superconductivity</a>, Tokyo Tech is a major centre for supercomputing technology and condensed matter research in the world.</p>
<p>In 2011, it celebrated the 130th anniversary of its founding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2014, it joined the edX consortium and formed the Online Education Development Office (OEDO) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> to create MOOCS, which are hosted on the edX website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its 130 years, Tokyo Tech has provided scientific researchers and engineers and many social leaders, including <a href="/wiki/Naoto_Kan" title="Naoto Kan">Naoto Kan</a> who is a former prime minister.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries">Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main library of Tokyo Tech is the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology_Library" title="Tokyo Institute of Technology Library">Tokyo Institute of Technology Library</a> in Ookayama. It is the home of Japan's largest science and technology library. The library was founded in 1882,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and it lost nearly 28,000 books during the <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kantō earthquake">Great Kantō earthquake</a> in 1923. Moved to Ookayama in 1936, it has been the national science and technology library of Japan.</p>
<p>1,200 students and staff visit the library each day.<br/>
It has 674,000 books and 2,500 journals, including 1,600 foreign academic journals; the number of international research collections is the largest in Japan. It provides around 7,000 registered electronic journals each year. The library is therefore recognised for the outstanding national and international importance and awarded 'Centre of foreign journals' by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Japan" title="Government of Japan">government of Japan</a>. Renewal construction of the library was completed on July 2011.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_graduate_programmes">International graduate programmes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: International graduate programmes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tokyo Tech runs intensive programmes for obtaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_degree" title="Master degree">master degree</a> or PhD. Called the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Tech%27s_International_Graduate_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tokyo Tech's International Graduate Program (page does not exist)">Tokyo Tech's International Graduate Program</a>, the programmes are targeted at international students of high academic potential who are not Japanese speakers. Lectures and seminars are given in English mainly by Tokyo Tech's faculty members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Programme starting dates are October or April. Public fundings for these courses are also available; those students who have academic excellence may apply for scholarships from the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education,_Culture,_Sports,_Science_and_Technology" title="Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology">Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology</a> of Japan.</p>

<p>Tokyo Tech is one of the most prestigious universities in Japan. It can be seen in the several rankings such as shown below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_rankings">General rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: General rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The university has been ranked 2nd(National) in 2011 in the field of Engineering "Entrance score ranking of Japanese universities-Department of Engineering" by Score-navi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In another ranking, Japanese prep school <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%B2%B3%E5%90%88%E5%A1%BE" title="ja:河合塾">Kawaijuku</a> ranked Tokyo Tech as the 4th best(overall), 2-3rd best in former semester and 1st in latter semester (Department of Engineering) university in Japan (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a>, Tokyo Tech was ranked 3rd in Japan and internationally ranked 20th in the field of Engineering and Technology, and 51st in Natural science in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The university was ranked 31st worldwide according to Global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_ranking" title="University ranking">University ranking</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gur_w_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-gur_w-30">[30]</a></sup> and 57th in 2011 according to <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It was also ranked 31st worldwide according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_University_Ranking" title="Global University Ranking">Global University Ranking</a> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gur_w_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-gur_w-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_performance">Research performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Research performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tokyo Tech is one of the top research institutions in natural sciences and technology in Japan. According to <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Reuters" title="Thomson Reuters">Thomson Reuters</a>, its research excellence(Pure science only for this information) is especially distinctive in Materials Science (5th in Japan, 24th in the world),Physics (5th in Japan, 31st in the world), and Chemistry (5th in Japan, 22nd in the world).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-executive_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-executive-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%80%B1%E5%88%8A%E3%83%80%E3%82%A4%E3%83%A4%E3%83%A2%E3%83%B3%E3%83%89" title="ja:週刊ダイヤモンド">Weekly Diamond</a> also reported that Tokyo Tech has the highest research standard in Japan in terms of research fundings per researchers in <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%B0%E3%83%AD%E3%83%BC%E3%83%90%E3%83%ABCOE%E3%83%97%E3%83%AD%E3%82%B0%E3%83%A9%E3%83%A0" title="ja:グローバルCOEプログラム">COE Program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In the same article, it's also ranked 8th in terms of the quality of education by <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%89%B9%E8%89%B2%E3%81%82%E3%82%8B%E5%A4%A7%E5%AD%A6%E6%95%99%E8%82%B2%E6%94%AF%E6%8F%B4%E3%83%97%E3%83%AD%E3%82%B0%E3%83%A9%E3%83%A0" title="ja:特色ある大学教育支援プログラム">GP</a> funds per student.</p>
<p>In addition, according to the <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a>　on 2012/9 surveyed about the general standards in Engineering&amp;Technology field, Tokyo Tech was placed 19th (world), 2nd(national).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsubame_2.0" title="Tsubame 2.0">Tsubame 2.0</a>, which is a large-scale supercomputer in Tokyo Tech, was ranked 5th of the world best-performed computer.(1st in the world as university's owned one) This supercomputer is used for simulation related to the complex systems such as the dynamics of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a> or financial systems.</p>
<p>As Tokyo Tech has been emphasizing on 'practical' research, Tokyo Tech got the 2nd place at the number of patents accepted (284) during 2009 among Japanese Universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alumni_rankings">Alumni rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Alumni rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alumni of Tokyo Tech enjoy their good success in Japanese industries. According to the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%A8%E3%82%B3%E3%83%8E%E3%83%9F%E3%82%B9%E3%83%88_(%E9%9B%91%E8%AA%8C)" title="ja:エコノミスト (雑誌)">Weekly Economist</a>'s 2010 rankings and the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%83%97%E3%83%AC%E3%82%B8%E3%83%87%E3%83%B3%E3%83%88%E7%A4%BE" title="ja:プレジデント社">PRESIDENT</a>'s article on 2006/10/16, graduates from Tokyo Tech have the 2nd best employment rate in 400 major companies, and the average graduate salary is the 9th best in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Mines_de_Paris" title="École des Mines de Paris">École des Mines de Paris</a> ranks Tokyo Tech as 92nd in the world in 2011 in terms of the number of alumni listed among CEOs in the 500 largest worldwide companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Also, according to the article of <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a>- Universities with the most employable students ranking 2012, Tokyo Tech ranked 14th place in the world(2nd in Asia, 1st in Japan).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity_and_selectivity">Popularity and selectivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Popularity and selectivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tokyo Tech is one of the most selective universities in Japan. Its entrance difficulty is usually considered as one of the most difficult in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
