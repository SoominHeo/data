<p>The <b>Tokyo International Anime Fair</b> also known as <b>Tokyo International Animation Fair</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">東京国際アニメフェア</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, <b>TAF</b>)</span> was one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> <a href="/wiki/Trade_fair" title="Trade fair">trade fairs</a> in the world, held annually in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The first event was held in 2002 as "Tokyo International Anime Fair 21". The event was held at <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Big_Sight" title="Tokyo Big Sight">Tokyo Big Sight</a>, a convention and exhibition center in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Bay" title="Tokyo Bay">Tokyo Bay</a>, in late March. Usually, the first one or two days of the fair were weekdays and the entrance was open only to industry members and the press; the last two days were scheduled on the weekend and the fair was open to the public.</p>
<p>Besides being an international trade fair, the TAF included related events such as business symposia and other events. Notably, the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Anime_Award" title="Tokyo Anime Award">Tokyo Anime Awards</a> were given for domestic and foreign creations and creators in the event with the name of the event. The event was supported by the Bureau of Industrial and Labor Affairs of Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Although the event did not have a long history, it and its prizes were recognized in the industry. In 2014, it was merged with the <a href="/wiki/Anime_Contents_Expo" title="Anime Contents Expo">Anime Contents Expo</a> to form <a href="/wiki/AnimeJapan" title="AnimeJapan">AnimeJapan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_event">2011 event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_International_Anime_Fair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2011 event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2010 a group of ten large manga publishers known as the Comic 10 Society <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">コミック10社会</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Comikku 10 Shakai</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> announced plans to boycott the 2011 event. The boycott was in protest at revisions to the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Metropolitan_Ordinance_Regarding_the_Healthy_Development_of_Youths" title="Tokyo Metropolitan Ordinance Regarding the Healthy Development of Youths">Tokyo Youth Development Ordinance</a> that increased regulation of manga and anime sales to under-18 year olds. This was seen as a deliberate snub of <a href="/wiki/Shintaro_Ishihara" title="Shintaro Ishihara">Shintaro Ishihara</a>, who was closely associated with both TAF and the changes to the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Japanese Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Naoto_Kan" title="Naoto Kan">Naoto Kan</a> had expressed concern about the impact of the boycott and urged the parties involved to work towards resolving the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">devastating earthquake and tsunami</a> that hit the northeast coast of Japan on March 11, 2011, it was announced five days later, that the 2011 event would be canceled. In addition, Tokyo Big Sight, where the event was held annually, suffered unspecified damage during the quake in the Tokyo area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
