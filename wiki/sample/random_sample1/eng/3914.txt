<p><b>Dotorimuk</b> (also spelled <b>tot'orimuk</b>) or <b>acorn jelly</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> food which is a <a href="/wiki/Gelatin_dessert" title="Gelatin dessert">jelly</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorn</a> <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>. Although "<i>muk</i>" means "jelly", when used without qualifiers, it usually refers to <i>dotorimuk</i>. The practice of making <i>dotorimuk</i> originated in mountainous areas of ancient Korea, when abundant <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees produced enough acorns each autumn to become a viable source of food. Like other <i><a href="/wiki/Muk_(food)" title="Muk (food)">muk</a></i>, <i>dotorimuk</i> is most commonly eaten in the form of <i>dotorimuk muchim</i> (도토리묵무침), a <a href="/wiki/Banchan" title="Banchan">side dish</a> in which small chunks of <i>dotorimuk</i> are seasoned and mixed with other ingredients such as slivered carrots and scallions, garlic, soy sauce, sesame oil, red chili pepper powder, and sesame seeds.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotorimuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg/220px-Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg/330px-Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg/440px-Korean_acorn_jelly-Dotorimuk-05A.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Unseasoned <i>dotorimuk</i></div>

<p>Despite being a rich source of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, acorns contain large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannins</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Polyphenol" title="Polyphenol">polyphenols</a>, which prevent the human body from digesting them properly. Harvested acorns must be properly leached of the tannins prior to consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Acorns are either collected directly from the ground or knocked off the tree branches. The acorns are opened and their innards ground into a fine orange-brown paste. The paste is then stirred into vats of water and the acorns' <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> is separated from the starch through sieving and settling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The starch-water mixture is collected and allowed to sit so that the tannins in the starch <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffuse</a> into the water, which is changed several times. The overall soaking time depends on the amount of tannins in the paste.</p>
<p>The now tannin-free starch-water suspension should have an off-white colour. This starch is allowed to completely settle at the bottom of the vat, the water drained away and the paste collected in trays to dry. The dried starch cake is then pulverized and packaged for sale. <i>Dotorimuk</i> is also commercially available in powdered form, which must be mixed with water, boiled until pudding-like in consistency, then set in a flat dish.</p>
