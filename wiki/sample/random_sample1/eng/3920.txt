<p><b>Independence Hall</b> is the building where both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">United States Declaration of Independence</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> were debated and adopted. It is now the centerpiece of the <a href="/wiki/Independence_National_Historical_Park" title="Independence National Historical Park">Independence National Historical Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.</p>
<p>The building was completed in 1753 as the colonial legislature (later <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_Capitol#History" title="Pennsylvania State Capitol">Pennsylvania State House</a>) for the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Province of Pennsylvania</a>. It became the principal meeting place of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a> from 1775 to 1783 and was the site of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Convention" title="Philadelphia Convention">Constitutional Convention</a> in the summer of 1787.</p>
<p>A convention held in Independence Hall in 1915, presided over by former US president <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>, marked the formal announcement of the formation of the <a href="/wiki/League_to_Enforce_Peace" title="League to Enforce Peace">League to Enforce Peace</a>, which led to the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> and eventually the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>. The building is part of <a href="/wiki/Independence_National_Historical_Park" title="Independence National Historical Park">Independence National Historical Park</a> and is listed as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_the_Americas#United_States" title="List of World Heritage Sites in the Americas">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WldHeritageSite_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-WldHeritageSite-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_for_construction">Preparation for construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation for construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1088" data-file-width="1866" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg/300px-Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg/450px-Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg/600px-Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Independence_Hall_Detail_1752.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail of <i>A Map of Philadelphia and Parts Adjacent</i>, depicting the State House as it appeared in 1752. The image shows the original bell tower, which lacked a clock.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By the spring of 1729 the citizens of Philadelphia were petitioning to be allowed to build a state house<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Independence_Hall#Dubious" title="Talk:Independence Hall">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>. Two thousand pounds were committed to the endeavor. A committee composed of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lawrence_(mayor)" title="Thomas Lawrence (mayor)">Thomas Lawrence</a>, Dr. John Kearsley, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Hamilton_(lawyer)" title="Andrew Hamilton (lawyer)">Andrew Hamilton</a> was charged with the responsibility of selecting a site for construction, acquiring plans for the building, and contracting a company for the purpose of construction. Hamilton and <a href="/wiki/William_Allen_(loyalist)" title="William Allen (loyalist)">William Allen</a> were named trustees of the purchasing and building fund and authorized to buy the land that would be the site of the state house. By October 1730 they had begun purchasing lots on <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Chestnut Street (Philadelphia)">Chestnut Street</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1732, even though Hamilton had acquired the deed for Lot no. 2 from surveyor David Powell, who had been paid for his work with the lot, tensions were rising among the committee members. Dr. John Kearsley and Hamilton disagreed on a number of issues concerning the state house. Kearsley, who is credited with the designs of both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ_Church_(Philadelphia)" title="Christ Church (Philadelphia)">Christ Church</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Church,_Philadelphia" title="St. Peter's Church, Philadelphia">St. Peter's Church</a>, had plans for the structure of the building, but so did Hamilton. The two men also disagreed on the building's site; Kearsley suggested High Street, now <a href="/wiki/Market_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Market Street (Philadelphia)">Market Street</a>, and Hamilton favored Chestnut Street. Lawrence said nothing on the matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matters reached a point where arbitration was needed. On August 8, 1733, Hamilton brought the matter before the House of Representatives. He explained that Kearsley did not approve of Hamilton’s plans for the location and architecture of the state house and went on to insist the House had not agreed to these decisions. In response to this, Hamilton, on August 11, showed his plans for the state house to the House, who accepted them. On August 14, the House sided with Hamilton, granting him full control over the project, and the site on the south side of Chestnut Street between Fifth and Sixth Streets was chosen for the location of the state house. Ground was broken for construction soon after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="9729" data-file-width="11689" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg/220px-Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg/330px-Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg/440px-Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Birch%27s_Views_Plate_23.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Independence Hall in 1799, with the wooden steeple removed and Thomas Stretch's clock (far left).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Independence Hall is a red brick building designed in the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian style</a>. It consists of a central building with belltower and steeple, attached to two smaller wings via arcaded <a href="/wiki/Hyphen_(architecture)" title="Hyphen (architecture)">hyphens</a>. The highest point to the tip of the steeple spire is 168 ft, 7<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> inches above the ground.</p>
<p>The State House was built between 1732 and 1751, designed by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Woolley" title="Edmund Woolley">Edmund Woolley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Hamilton_(lawyer)" title="Andrew Hamilton (lawyer)">Andrew Hamilton</a>, and built by Woolley. Its construction was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania colonial legislature</a> which paid for construction as funds were available, so it was finished piecemeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was initially inhabited by the colonial government of Pennsylvania as its State House, from 1732 to 1799.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WldHeritageSite_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-WldHeritageSite-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1752, when <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Norris_(statesman)" title="Isaac Norris (statesman)">Isaac Norris</a> was selecting a man to build the first clock for the State House, today known as Independence Hall, he chose <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stretch" title="Thomas Stretch">Thomas Stretch</a>, the son of Peter Stretch his old friend and fellow council member, to do the job.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1753 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stretch" title="Thomas Stretch">Thomas Stretch</a> erected a giant clock at the building's west end that resembled a tall clock (grandfather clock). The 40-foot-tall limestone base was capped with a 14-foot wooden case surrounding the clock's face, which was carved by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Harding_(cabinetmaker)" title="Samuel Harding (cabinetmaker)">Samuel Harding</a>. The giant clock was removed about 1830.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frazier_1974_287_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frazier_1974_287-9">[9]</a></sup> The clock’s dials were mounted at the east and west ends of the main building connected by rods to the clock movement in the middle of the building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The acquisition of the original clock and bell by the Pennsylvania Colonial Assembly is closely related to the acquisition of the <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bell" title="Liberty Bell">Liberty Bell</a>. By mid-1753, the clock had been installed in the State House attic, but six years were to elapse before Thomas Stretch received any pay for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demolition_and_reconstruction">Demolition and reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demolition and reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the shell of the central portion of the building is original, the side wings, steeple and much of the interior are reconstructions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original steeple was demolished due to structural problems in 1781. A more elaborate steeple, designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Strickland_(architect)" title="William Strickland (architect)">William Strickland</a>, was added in 1828.</p>
<p>The original wings and <a href="/wiki/Hyphen_(architecture)" title="Hyphen (architecture)">hyphens</a> were demolished in 1812. In 1898, these were in turn demolished and replaced with reconstructions of the original wings.</p>
<p>The building was renovated numerous times in the 19th and 20th century. The current interior is a mid-20th century reconstruction by the National Park Service with the public rooms restored to their 18th century appearance.</p>
<p>During the summer of 1973 a replica of the Thomas Stretch clock was restored to Independence Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frazier_1974_287_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frazier_1974_287-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second floor Governor's Council Chamber, furnished with important examples of the era by the National Park Service, includes a musical tall case clock made by Peter Stretch, c 1740, one of the most prominent clockmakers in early America and father of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stretch" title="Thomas Stretch">Thomas Stretch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two smaller buildings adjoin the wings of Independence Hall: <a href="/wiki/Old_City_Hall_(Philadelphia)" title="Old City Hall (Philadelphia)">Old City Hall</a> to the east, and <a href="/wiki/Congress_Hall" title="Congress Hall">Congress Hall</a> to the west. These three buildings are together on a <a href="/wiki/City_block" title="City block">city block</a> known as Independence Square, along with Philosophical Hall, the original home of the <a href="/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society" title="American Philosophical Society">American Philosophical Society</a>. Since its construction in the mid-20th century, to the north has been Independence Mall, which includes the current home of the Liberty Bell.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liberty_Bell">Liberty Bell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Liberty Bell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liberty_Bell,_Independence_Hall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Liberty_Bell%2C_Independence_Hall.jpg/200px-Liberty_Bell%2C_Independence_Hall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Liberty_Bell%2C_Independence_Hall.jpg/300px-Liberty_Bell%2C_Independence_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Liberty_Bell%2C_Independence_Hall.jpg/400px-Liberty_Bell%2C_Independence_Hall.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liberty_Bell,_Independence_Hall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Liberty Bell (foreground) was housed in the highest chamber of the brick tower. The Centennial Bell (top) hangs in the cupola of <a href="/wiki/William_Strickland_(architect)" title="William Strickland (architect)">William Strickland</a>'s 1828 wooden steeple.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bell" title="Liberty Bell">Liberty Bell</a></div>
<p>The lowest chamber of the original wooden steeple was the first home of the <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bell" title="Liberty Bell">Liberty Bell</a>. When that steeple was removed in the 1780s the bell was lowered into the highest chamber of the brick tower, where it remained until the 1850s. The much larger Centennial Bell, created for the United States <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Exposition" title="Centennial Exposition">Centennial Exposition</a> in 1876, hangs in the cupola of the 1828 steeple. The Liberty Bell, with its distinctive crack, was displayed on the ground floor of the hall from the 1850s until 1976, and is now on display across the street in the Liberty Bell Center.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Declaration_of_Independence_and_Second_Continental_Congress">Declaration of Independence and Second Continental Congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Declaration of Independence and Second Continental Congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2532" data-file-width="3950" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg/220px-IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg/330px-IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg/440px-IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IndependenceHallAssemblyRoom.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A National Park Service Ranger describes Independence Hall's Assembly Room, in which both the Declaration of Independence and Constitution were drafted and signed.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War#Saratoga_and_Philadelphia" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War § Saratoga and Philadelphia</a></div>
<p>From May 10, 1775,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capital_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-capital-14">[14]</a></sup> to 1783, the Pennsylvania State House served as the principal meeting place of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a>, a body of representatives from each of the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">thirteen British North American colonies</a>.</p>
<p>On June 14, 1775, delegates of the Continental Congress nominated <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> as commander-in-chief of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> in the Assembly Room of the Pennsylvania State House. The Congress appointed <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> to be the first <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postmaster_General" title="United States Postmaster General">Postmaster General</a> of what would later become the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Post_Office_Department" title="United States Post Office Department">United States Post Office Department</a> on July 26.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">United States Declaration of Independence</a> was approved there on July 4, 1776, and the Declaration was read aloud to the public in the area now known as Independence Square. This document unified the colonies in North America who declared themselves independent of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> and explained their justifications for doing so. These historic events are celebrated annually with a national holiday for <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">U.S. Independence Day</a>.</p>
<p>The Congress continued to meet there until December 12, 1776,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capital_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-capital-14">[14]</a></sup> after which the Congress evacuated Philadelphia. During the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_campaign#Capture_of_Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia campaign">British occupation</a> of Philadelphia, the Continental Congress met in Baltimore, Maryland (December 20, 1776 to February 27, 1777). The Congress returned to Philadelphia from March 4, 1777 to September 18, 1777.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capital_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-capital-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 1777, the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> again arrived to occupy Philadelphia, once again forcing the Continental Congress to abandon the State House. It then met in Lancaster, Pennsylvania, for one day (September 27, 1777) and in York, Pennsylvania, for nine months (September 30, 1777 to June 27, 1778), where the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a> were approved in November 1777. The Second Continental Congress again returned to Independence Hall, for its final meetings, from July 2, 1778 to March 1, 1781.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capital_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-capital-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_Confederation" title="Congress of the Confederation">Congress of the Confederation</a> initially met in Independence Hall, from March 1, 1781 to June 21, 1783.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Mutiny_of_1783" title="Pennsylvania Mutiny of 1783">Pennsylvania Mutiny of 1783</a>, Congress again moved from Philadelphia in June 1783 to Princeton, New Jersey, and eventually to other cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capital_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-capital-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Constitutional_Convention">U.S. Constitutional Convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: U.S. Constitutional Convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_(United_States)" title="Constitutional Convention (United States)">Constitutional Convention (United States)</a></div>
<p>In September 1786, commissioners from five states met in the <a href="/wiki/Annapolis_Convention_(1786)" title="Annapolis Convention (1786)">Annapolis Convention</a> to discuss adjustments to the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a> that would improve commerce. They invited state representatives to convene in Philadelphia to discuss improvements to the federal government. After debate, the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_Confederation" title="Congress of the Confederation">Congress of the Confederation</a> endorsed the plan to revise the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a> on February 21, 1787. Twelve states, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> being the exception, accepted this invitation and sent delegates to convene in June 1787 at Independence Hall.</p>
<p>The resolution calling the Convention specified its purpose as proposing amendments to the Articles, but the Convention decided to propose a rewritten <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Convention" title="Philadelphia Convention">Philadelphia Convention</a> voted to keep deliberations secret, and to keep the Hall's windows shut throughout the hot summer. The result was the drafting of a new fundamental government design. On September 17, 1787, the Constitution was completed, and took effect on March 4, 1789, when the new Congress met for the first time in New York's <a href="/wiki/Federal_Hall" title="Federal Hall">Federal Hall</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article One, Section Eight, of the United States Constitution</a> granted Congress the authority to create a federal district to serve as the national capital. Following the ratification of the Constitution, the Congress, while meeting in New York, passed the <a href="/wiki/Residence_Act" title="Residence Act">Residence Act</a> of 1790, which established the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a> as the new federal capital. However, a representative from Pennsylvania, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier)" title="Robert Morris (financier)">Robert Morris</a>, did manage to convince Congress to return to Philadelphia while the new permanent capital was being built. As a result, the Residence Act also declared Philadelphia to be the temporary capital for a period of ten years. The Congress moved back into Philadelphia on December 6, 1790 and met at <a href="/wiki/Congress_Hall" title="Congress Hall">Congress Hall</a>, adjacent to Independence Hall until moving to Washington, D.C. in 1800.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funerary_procession_of_Abraham_Lincoln">Funerary procession of Abraham Lincoln</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Funerary procession of Abraham Lincoln">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Abraham_Lincoln" title="Assassination of Abraham Lincoln">Assassination of Abraham Lincoln</a></div>
<p>Abraham Lincoln's funeral train was to take the body of the president (and the disinterred coffin of his son Willie, who had predeceased him in 1862) from Washington D.C. back to Springfield, Illinois for burial. It would essentially retrace the 1,654 mile route Mr. Lincoln had traveled as president-elect in 1861 (with the deletion of Pittsburgh and Cincinnati and the addition of Chicago). The train left Washington for Baltimore at 8:00 AM on April 21, 1865.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogerjnorton.com_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogerjnorton.com-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lincoln's funeral train (the "Lincoln Special") left Harrisburg on Saturday - April 22, 1865 at 11:15 AM and arrived at Philadelphia at <a href="/wiki/Broad_Street_Station_(Philadelphia)" title="Broad Street Station (Philadelphia)">Broad Street Station</a> that afternoon at 4:30 PM. It was carried by hearse past a crowd of 85,000 people and was held in state in the Assembly Room in the east wing of Independence Hall. While there, it was escorted and guarded by a detail of 27 Naval and Military officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> That evening, a private viewing was arranged for honored guests of the mourners. The next day (Sunday - April 23, 1865) lines began forming at 5:00 AM. Over 300,000 mourners viewed the body - some waiting 5 hours just to see him. The Lincoln Special left Philadelphia's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kensington,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Kensington, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Kensington Station</a> for New York City the next morning (Monday - April 24, 1865) at 4:00 A.M.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogerjnorton.com_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-rogerjnorton.com-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="League_to_Enforce_Peace">League to Enforce Peace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: League to Enforce Peace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The symbolic use of the Hall was illustrated on June 17, 1915, where the <a href="/wiki/League_to_Enforce_Peace" title="League to Enforce Peace">League to Enforce Peace</a> (LEP) was formed here with former President <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a> presiding. They proposed an international governing body under which participating nations would commit to "jointly...use...their economic and military forces against any one of their number making war against another" and "to formulate and codify rules of international law".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation">Preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="575" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg/220px-C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg/330px-C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg/440px-C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C_W_Peale_-_The_Artist_in_His_Museum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/The_Artist_in_His_Museum" title="The Artist in His Museum">The Artist in His Museum</a></i>, an 1822 self-portrait by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Willson_Peale" title="Charles Willson Peale">Charles Willson Peale</a> depicts his museum at the Old State House (The Long Room is shown in the background).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From 1802 to 1826-27, artist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Willson_Peale" title="Charles Willson Peale">Charles Willson Peale</a> housed his museum of <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a> specimens (including the skeleton of a <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">mastodon</a>) and portraits of famous Americans, on the second floor of the Old State House and in the Assembly Room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPS_Peale_museum_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPS_Peale_museum-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etting_-_Peale_Museum_to_Restoration_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Etting_-_Peale_Museum_to_Restoration-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1816, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(U.S._state)" title="Commonwealth (U.S. state)">Commonwealth</a> of Pennsylvania sold the State House to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Philadelphia" title="City of Philadelphia">City of Philadelphia</a>, with a contract signed by the governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WldHeritageSite_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-WldHeritageSite-3">[3]</a></sup> The deed, however, was not transferred until more than two years later. Philadelphia has owned the State House and its associated buildings and grounds since that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WldHeritageSite_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-WldHeritageSite-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1948, the building's interior was restored to its original appearance. <a href="/wiki/Independence_National_Historical_Park" title="Independence National Historical Park">Independence National Historical Park</a> was established by the <a href="/wiki/80th_United_States_Congress" title="80th United States Congress">80th U.S. Congress</a> later that year to preserve historical sites associated with the American Revolution. Independence National Historical Park comprises a landscaped area of four city blocks, as well as outlying sites that include: <a href="/wiki/Independence_Hall#Independence_Square" title="Independence Hall">Independence Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carpenters%27_Hall" title="Carpenters' Hall">Carpenters' Hall</a> (meeting place of the <a href="/wiki/First_Continental_Congress" title="First Continental Congress">First Continental Congress</a>), the site of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>'s home, the reconstructed Graff House (where <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> wrote the Declaration of Independence), <a href="/wiki/City_Tavern" title="City Tavern">City Tavern</a> (center of Revolutionary War activities), restored period residences, and several early banks. The park also holds the Liberty Bell, Franklin's desk, the <a href="/wiki/Syng_inkstand" title="Syng inkstand">Syng inkstand</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Portrait_Gallery" title="Portrait Gallery">portrait gallery</a>, gardens, and libraries. A product of extensive documentary research and archaeology by the federal government, the restoration of Independence Hall and other buildings in the park set standards for other <a href="/wiki/Historic_preservation" title="Historic preservation">historic preservation</a> and stimulated rejuvenation of old Philadelphia. The site, administered by the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>, is listed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> (joining only three other U.S. man-made monuments still in use, the others being the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taos_Pueblo" title="Taos Pueblo">Pueblo de Taos</a>, and the combined site of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monticello" title="Monticello">Monticello</a>).</p>
<p>Independence Hall and the Liberty Bell are now protected in a secure zone with entry at security screening buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>, as part of a national effort to safeguard historical monuments by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">United States Department of Homeland Security</a>, pedestrian traffic around Independence Square and part of <a href="/wiki/Independence_Mall_(Philadelphia)" title="Independence Mall (Philadelphia)">Independence Mall</a> was restricted by temporary bicycle barriers and park rangers. In 2006, the National Park Service proposed installing a seven-foot security fence around Independence Hall and bisecting Independence Square, a plan that met with opposition from Philadelphia city officials, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Pennsylvania" title="List of Governors of Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Ed_Rendell" title="Ed Rendell">Ed Rendell</a>, and Senator <a href="/wiki/Arlen_Specter" title="Arlen Specter">Arlen Specter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> As of January 2007, the National Park Service plan was revised to eliminate the fence in favor of movable bollards and chains, and also to remove at least some of the temporary barriers to pedestrians and visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Independence Hall has been used in more recent times as the staging ground for protests because of its symbolic history<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> in support of democratic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> movements. On October 26, 1918, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Masaryk" title="Tomáš Masaryk">Tomáš Masaryk</a> proclaimed the independence of <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> on the steps of Independence Hall. <a href="/wiki/National_Freedom_Day" title="National Freedom Day">National Freedom Day</a>, which commemorates the struggles of African Americans for equality and justice, has been celebrated at Independence Hall since 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On Independence Day, July 4, 1962, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> gave an address there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Annual demonstrations advocating for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_rights" title="Gay rights">Gay rights</a> were held in front of Independence Hall on July 4 from 1965 to 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Independence Hall is pictured on the back of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_one_hundred-dollar_bill" title="United States one hundred-dollar bill">$100 bill</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bicentennial" title="United States Bicentennial">bicentennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_half_dollar" title="Kennedy half dollar">Kennedy half dollar</a>. The Assembly Room is pictured on the reverse of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_two-dollar_bill" title="United States two-dollar bill">U.S. two-dollar bill</a>, from the original painting by <a href="/wiki/John_Trumbull" title="John Trumbull">John Trumbull</a> entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trumbull%27s_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Trumbull's Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a></i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Replicas">Replicas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Replicas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_Hall_replicas" title="Independence Hall replicas">Independence Hall replicas</a></div>
<p>Independence Hall served as the model for the Pennsylvania Building at the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a>, the Pennsylvania Building at the 1907 <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Exposition" title="Jamestown Exposition">Jamestown Exposition</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and the Pennsylvania Pavilion at the <a href="/wiki/1939_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1939 New York World's Fair">1939 New York World's Fair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Dozens of structures replicating or loosely inspired by Independence Hall's iconic design have been built elsewhere in the United States.</p>
