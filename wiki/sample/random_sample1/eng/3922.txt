<p>The <b>Independent Democratic Union</b> (<i>Unión Demócrata Independiente</i>, <b>UDI</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> right-wing, <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a>, founded in 1983. Its founder was the lawyer, politician and law professor <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Guzm%C3%A1n" title="Jaime Guzmán">Jaime Guzmán</a>, a civilian who collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a>. Guzmán was a senator from 1990 until his assassination on April 1, 1991.</p>
<p>Its ideological origins date back to Guzmán's <a href="/wiki/Gremialismo" title="Gremialismo">Guildist Movement</a>, born out of the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Catholic_University_of_Chile" title="Pontifical Catholic University of Chile">Pontifical Catholic University of Chile</a> in 1966, espousing the independence and depoliticization of intermediate bodies of civil society.</p>
<p>UDI, together with <a href="/wiki/National_Renewal_(Chile)" title="National Renewal (Chile)">National Renewal</a> (RN) and other minor movements, formed a coalition of right-wing parties called <a href="/wiki/Coalition_for_Change" title="Coalition for Change">Coalition for Change</a>, which is the successor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_for_Chile" title="Alliance for Chile">Alliance for Chile</a> and rose to power in March 2010, after victory in the presidential elections of January 2010. It was the largest political party in <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_of_Chile" title="National Congress of Chile">Congress</a> between 2010 and 2014.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_parliamentary_election,_2009" title="Chilean parliamentary election, 2009">Chilean parliamentary election, 2009</a>, UDI held the largest majority in the election of deputies, electing 40 deputies (one third of the House) with 23.04% (1,507,001 votes), and got 21.21% (369,594 votes) in the election of senators. Its bench is the largest obtained by a single party in Chile since 1990. UDI currently has 39 deputies and 8 senators.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_municipal_election,_2008" title="Chilean municipal election, 2008">Chilean municipal election, 2008</a>, UDI got 347 councilmen (16.16% of councilmen) by a vote of 15.11%, and obtained 58 mayors (16.81% of mayors) by a vote of 20.05%. That year, it was the largest party by elected councilmen and the most voted for party in the election of councilmen. Also, UDI is the second largest party by number of mayors in Chile (only one mayor less than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partido_Dem%C3%B3crata_Cristiano_de_Chile" title="Partido Demócrata Cristiano de Chile">Partido Demócrata Cristiano de Chile</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_.281967-1988.29">Origins (1967-1988)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins (1967-1988)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was during the university strikes of the 1960s when <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Guzm%C3%A1n" title="Jaime Guzmán">Jaime Guzmán</a>, President of the Law Students Union at the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Catholic_University_of_Chile" title="Pontifical Catholic University of Chile">Pontifical Catholic University of Chile</a> -who opposed the protests and strikes, led by the Christian-democrats and left-wing students- gathered a group of students and founded the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movimiento_Gremial" title="Movimiento Gremial">Movimiento Gremial</a></i> (Guildist Movement) and ran for the University's Student Union (Federación de Estudiantes) election. The movement quickly became one of the most important in the Catholic University, and later won the presidency of the University's Student Union.</p>
<p>Jaime Guzmán criticized liberal democracy and sought inspiration in authoritarian corporatism, proposing the <a href="/wiki/Subsidiarity_(Catholicism)" title="Subsidiarity (Catholicism)">principle of subsidiarity</a> and to invigorate intermediate social movements, by the way that these were independent to develop their own specific purposes. Well into the government of <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Allende" title="Salvador Allende">Salvador Allende</a>, some young members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Party_(Chile,_1966%E2%80%9394)" title="National Party (Chile, 1966–94)">National Party</a> and the Christian Democrats became part of the Gremialismo Movement of Jaime Guzmán.</p>
<p>Guzmán supported a military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coup" title="Coup">coup</a> against Allende's government, which happened shortly thereafter on September 11, 1973 (<i>see</i>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chilean_coup_of_1973" title="Chilean coup of 1973">Chilean coup of 1973</a>). He was a close advisor of General <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Guzmán was later appointed a member of the Commission for the Study of the New Constitution, who worded the new constitution promulgated in 1980.</p>
<p>After the 1982 economic crisis, which caused the temporary removal of the "<a href="/wiki/Chicago_Boys" title="Chicago Boys">Chicago Boys</a>" from cabinet, Guzmán moved away from the government and decided to found the movement he desired, establishing it on September 24, 1983 under the name <b>Independent Democratic Union Movement</b> (Movimiento Unión Demócrata Independiente).</p>
<p>The emerging movement, a supporter of the military government, had (as opposed to the traditional right-wing political groups) a strong empathy with the lower classes, in order to seize from the Marxist left its traditional domain. Amid the growing economic crisis of the time, UDI engaged in empowering leaders in the countryside and peripheral neighbourhoods that would help extend its influence in the middle and lower classes. One of them was Simon Yévenes, UDI member assassinated by left-wing resistance fighters on April 2, 1986.</p>
<p>On April 29, 1987, the Independent Democratic Union merged with other related movements such as National Union Movement, led by <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Allamand" title="Andrés Allamand">Andrés Allamand</a>, and National Labour Front, led by Sergio Onofre Jarpa, plus some former members and supporters of the National Party and the Christian Democrats, to form the National Renewal party (RN), who managed briefly to unite all the right movements in the country. However, UDI members maintained their own identity in the new party, which caused a crisis in 1988, culminating in the resignation of all former UDI members to National Renewal. Allamand stayed in charge of National Renewal, while Jaime Guzman managed to register a new political party: <b>Independent Democratic Union</b> in 1989.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_dictatorship_.281988-1989.29">End of dictatorship (1988-1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: End of dictatorship (1988-1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>UDI strongly supported Pinochet's remaining in power in the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_national_plebiscite,_1988" title="Chilean national plebiscite, 1988">Chilean national plebiscite, 1988</a>. After the "Yes" option was defeated and presidential elections were announced, the UDI joined <a href="/wiki/National_Renewal_(Chile)" title="National Renewal (Chile)">National Renewal</a> and formed the "Democracy and Progress" alliance (<i>Democracia y Progreso</i>). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hernan_B%C3%BCchi" title="Hernan Büchi">Hernan Büchi</a>, the former Minister of Finances under Pinochet, ran for president for this alliance. The alliance also ran a common Parliament list. The UDI's option lost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chilean_presidential_election,_1989" title="Chilean presidential election, 1989">the 1989 presidential election</a>, this time against the center-left <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coalition_of_Parties_for_Democracy" title="Coalition of Parties for Democracy">Concertación</a>'s leader, the Christian Democrat <a href="/wiki/Patricio_Aylwin" title="Patricio Aylwin">Patricio Aylwin</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1989 parliamentary elections, the Independent Democratic Union obtained a 9.82% of votes in deputies (14 deputies out of 120) and 5.11% in the Senate (2 senators elected on 38). Jaime Guzmán won a seat as <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Chile" title="Senate of Chile">Senator</a> for Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago</a> constituency. Although Guzman took third place with only 17% of the vote, behind Christian Democrat <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Zald%C3%ADvar" title="Andrés Zaldívar">Andrés Zaldívar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Party_for_Democracy_(Chile)" title="Party for Democracy (Chile)">Party for Democracy</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Lagos" title="Ricardo Lagos">Ricardo Lagos</a>, the two main leaders of the Coalition of Parties for Democracy, the binomial system allowed Zaldívar's and his election and deferred Ricardo Lagos who got 30%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_opposition_.281989-2003.29">Growth and opposition (1989-2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Growth and opposition (1989-2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>By 1990, Guzman was positioned as the leader of the opposition and was one of the harshest critics of the new democratic government, accusing it of softness in the fight against left-wing armed organizations which kept operating in Chile after the restoration of restricted democracy. On April 1, 1991, Guzmán was shot dead by members of the armed left-wing group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manuel_Rodriguez_Patriotic_Front" title="Manuel Rodriguez Patriotic Front">Manuel Rodriguez Patriotic Front</a> (<i>Frente Patriótico Manuel Rodríguez</i>), after leaving his lecture of Constitutional Law at the Pontifical Catholic University of Chile. He was replaced as senator by the National Renewal candidate for the same constituency, Miguel Otero.</p>
<p>The Independent Democratic Union remained as a minor party in the early years of transition, compared with its ally National Renewal, but over the years managed to win preferences, match and surpass them. In subsequent elections, UDI began to grow noticeably: got 12.11% in a congressional election in 1993, a 14.45% in elections in 1997 and 25.19% in the 2001 elections, when it became the largest party in Chile, removing that title to the Christian Democrats.</p>
<p>In 1998, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinochet%27s_arrest_and_trial" title="Pinochet's arrest and trial">Pinochet was arrested</a> in London, the UDI and National Renewal pressed the Frei government to return him to Chile.</p>
<p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Lav%C3%ADn" title="Joaquín Lavín">Joaquín Lavín</a>, the mayor of <a href="/wiki/Las_Condes" title="Las Condes">Las Condes</a> and member of UDI, was proclaimed as the Alliance for Chile candidate for the presidential election. Even as a relatively new face, a moderate support for Augusto Pinochet and a proposal eminently pragmatic rather than dogmatic, took him to get the 47.51% of the votes against the Concertación candidate Ricardo Lagos on the first ballot, with a difference of about 30,000 votes (i.e., almost one vote per polling place). Finally, in January 2000, Lavín got 48.69% of the votes against 51.31% of Lagos in the second round. That was the highest percentage of the vote received by any right-wing presidential candidate in the 20th century in Chile.</p>
<p>During the first half of the presidential term of Ricardo Lagos (2000–2006), UDI established itself as a relevant political actor of the opposition. Proof of this are the results of UDI in the 2000 municipal elections, the parliamentary elections of 2001, and the Lagos-Longueira agreement of January 17, 2003 to modernize the State administration and give a consensual political solution to <a href="/wiki/Inverlink_case" title="Inverlink case">Inverlink case</a> and <a href="/wiki/MOP-Gate_case" title="MOP-Gate case">MOP-Gate case</a>, which affected the institutional stability of the Lagos administration. The result of this is the election finance law, high public management law and others. During this period, especially outstanding figure is the party president, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Longueira" title="Pablo Longueira">Pablo Longueira</a>.</p>
<p>A milestone in the party's image came in 2003 when Longueira reported in a TV interview that he met with relatives of Disappeared Detainees, who saw the party as a serious and reliable institution, through which they could get some of the solutions that Socialist governments had not granted them. Of these numerous meetings, arose the document "Peace Now" ("La Paz Ahora"), which sought to give a sign of national reconciliation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes_with_National_Renewal_.282003-2006.29">Disputes with National Renewal (2003-2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disputes with National Renewal (2003-2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Also in 2003, stressed the frictions and conflicts between RN and UDI, mainly due to a dispute between the parties for the leadership within the Alliance for Chile, as well as personal disagreements between the presidents of both parties, Pablo Longueira and <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era" title="Sebastián Piñera">Sebastián Piñera</a>. That is Joaquin Lavin, who was then leader of the Alliance for Chile and only presidential candidate, had suddenly and publicly call on both the resignation from their posts.</p>
<p>In 2005, UDI selected Joaquín Lavín for presidential elections again, but National Renewal launched its own candidate, the millionaire businessman and former senator <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era" title="Sebastián Piñera">Sebastián Piñera</a>. Attempts to choose a single candidate for the right-wing failed. Piñera got second in the election, and there was a runoff between him and the <i>Concertación</i> candidate, <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Bachelet" title="Michelle Bachelet">Michelle Bachelet</a>. Lavín urged his supporters to vote for Piñera, whom he endorsed wholeheartedly. However, in the 2006 runoff, Piñera was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chilean_presidential_election,_2006" title="Chilean presidential election, 2006">defeated by Bachelet</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_parliamentary_election,_2005" title="Chilean parliamentary election, 2005">2005 parliamentary elections</a>, UDI maintained its status as largest party in Congress, electing 33 out of 120 deputies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_Bachelet_.282006-2010.29">Opposition to Bachelet (2006-2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Opposition to Bachelet (2006-2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the government of Michelle Bachelet (2006–2010), UDI was the majority party in both houses of Congress and successfully fought the municipal election of 2008. At the internal level, in July 2008 was first presented two lists to lead the party: one headed by Juan Antonio Coloma and Victor Perez Varela (who had the backing of the historical leaders of the party) and one by Jose Antonio Kast and Rodrigo Alvarez (supported mainly by the younger members). Coloma got 63% of member votes.</p>
<p>Coloma's board immediately got down to the details of the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Chilean_municipal_election,_2008" title="Chilean municipal election, 2008">Chilean municipal election, 2008</a>, and just finished it, the preparations for next year's parliamentary and presidential election. In December 2008, the highest party leaders decided to forgo the option to offer the country a UDI presidential candidate and provided support for Piñera's candidacy in order to avoid a fifth consecutive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concertacion" title="Concertacion">Concertacion</a> government. This decision was ratified later, unanimously by party members, August 22, 2009.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_government_.282010-2014.29">In government (2010-2014)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In government (2010-2014)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era" title="Sebastián Piñera">Sebastián Piñera</a>, the candidate of the <a href="/wiki/Coalition_for_Change" title="Coalition for Change">Coalition for Change</a>, was elected <a href="/wiki/President_of_Chile" title="President of Chile">President of the Republic of Chile</a> on January 17, 2010, in runoff against Senator <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Frei_Ruiz-Tagle" title="Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle">Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle</a>. UDI and its territorial deployment was key to the Piñera's success. Meanwhile, in <a href="/wiki/Chilean_parliamentary_election,_2009" title="Chilean parliamentary election, 2009">Chilean parliamentary election, 2009</a>, UDI managed to remain the largest party in the country and elected 40 deputies out of 120, representing the largest bank obtained by a single political party in Chile since 1990. In August 2010 they met for the second time the lists of Juan Antonio Coloma and Jose Antonio Kast to lead the party, again with a triumph for Coloma with more than 67% of the votes. Many members of UDI are part of Piñera's administration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_opposition_again_.282014-.29">In opposition again (2014-)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Democratic_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In opposition again (2014-)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The UDI forms a new centre-right coalition called <a href="/wiki/Chile_Vamos" title="Chile Vamos">Chile Vamos</a>.</p>
