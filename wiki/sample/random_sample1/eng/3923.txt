<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dependent and independent variables</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For dependent and independent random variables, see <a href="/wiki/Independence_(probability_theory)" title="Independence (probability theory)">Independence (probability theory)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_modelling" title="Mathematical modelling">mathematical modelling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">statistical modelling</a>, there are <b>dependent and independent variables</b>. The models investigate how the former depend on the latter. The dependent variables represent the output or outcome whose variation is being studied. The independent variables represent inputs or causes, i.e. potential reasons for variation. Models test or explain the effects that the independent variables have on the dependent variables. Sometimes, independent variables may be included for other reasons, such as for their potential <a href="/wiki/Confounding" title="Confounding">confounding</a> effect, without a wish to test their effect directly.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">Mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_and_independent_variables&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In mathematics, a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a> is a rule for taking an input (usually number or set of numbers)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carlson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-carlson-2">[2]</a></sup> and providing an output (which is also usually a number).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carlson_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-carlson-2">[2]</a></sup> A symbol that stands for an arbitrary input is called an <b>independent variable</b>, while a symbol that stands for an arbitrary output is called a <b>dependent variable</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stewart_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-stewart-3">[3]</a></sup> The most common symbol for the input is <i>x</i>, and the most common symbol for the output is <i>y</i>; the function itself is commonly written <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

For instance, in <a href="/wiki/Multivariable_calculus" title="Multivariable calculus">multivariable calculus</a>, one often encounters functions of the form <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_Theory" title="Set Theory">Set Theory</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a> between a <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">set</a> <b>X</b> and a set <b>Y</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subset</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_product" title="Cartesian product">Cartesian product</a> <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

In this situation, a symbol representing an element of <b>X</b> may be called an independent variable and a symbol representing an element of <b>Y</b> may be called a dependent variable, such as when <b>X</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manifold_(mathematics)" title="Manifold (mathematics)">manifold</a> and the symbol <i>x</i> represents an arbitrary point in the manifold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, many advanced textbooks do not distinguish between dependent and independent variables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_and_independent_variables&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an <a href="/wiki/Design_of_experiments" title="Design of experiments">experiment</a>, the dependent variable is the event expected to change when the independent variable is manipulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomHouse_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-randomHouse-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">data mining</a> tools (for <a href="/wiki/Multivariate_statistics" title="Multivariate statistics">multivariate statistics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>), the depending variable is assigned a <i>role</i> as <b>target variable</b> (or in some tools as <i>label attribute</i>), while a dependent variable may be assigned a role as <i>regular variable</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Known values for the target variable are provided for the training data set and <a href="/wiki/Test_data" title="Test data">test data</a> set, but should be predicted for other data. The target variable is used in <a href="/wiki/Supervised_learning" title="Supervised learning">supervised learning</a> algorithms but not in non-supervised learning.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simulation">Simulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_and_independent_variables&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Simulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a>, the dependent variable is changed in response to changes in the independent variables.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics_synonyms">Statistics synonyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_and_independent_variables&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statistics synonyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Depending on the context, an independent variable is sometimes called a "predictor variable", "regressor", "controlled variable", "manipulated variable", "explanatory variable", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exposure_variable" title="Exposure variable">exposure variable</a>" (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reliability_theory" title="Reliability theory">reliability theory</a>), "<a href="/wiki/Risk_factor" title="Risk factor">risk factor</a>" (see <a href="/wiki/Medical_statistics" title="Medical statistics">medical statistics</a>), "<a href="/wiki/Feature_(machine_learning)" title="Feature (machine learning)">feature</a>" (in <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pattern_recognition" title="Pattern recognition">pattern recognition</a>) or "input variable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodgeindepvar_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodgeindepvar-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodgeregression_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodgeregression-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the context, a dependent variable is sometimes called a "response variable", "regressand", "predicted variable", "measured variable", "explained variable", "experimental variable", "responding variable", "outcome variable", "output variable" or "label".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodgeregression_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dodgeregression-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Explanatory variable"<span id="explanatory_variable"></span> is preferred by some authors over "independent variable" when the quantities treated as independent variables may not be statistically independent or independently manipulable by the researcher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Everitt1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Everitt1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodge1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodge1-13">[13]</a></sup> If the independent variable is referred to as an "explanatory variable" then the term "response variable"<span id="response_variable"></span> is preferred by some authors for the dependent variable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodgeregression_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dodgeregression-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Everitt1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Everitt1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodge1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dodge1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Explained variable"<span id="explained_variable"></span> is preferred by some authors over "dependent variable" when the quantities treated as "dependent variables" may not be statistically dependent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAUME_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAUME-14">[14]</a></sup> If the dependent variable is referred to as an "explained variable" then the term "predictor variable"<span id="predictor_variable"></span> is preferred by some authors for the independent variable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAUME_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-DAUME-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Variables may also be referred to by their form: continuous, binary/dichotomous, nominal categorical, and ordinal categorical, among others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_variables">Other variables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_and_independent_variables&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other variables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A variable may be thought to alter the dependent or independent variables, but may not actually be the focus of the experiment. So that variable will be kept constant or monitored to try to minimise its effect on the experiment. Such variables may be designated as either a "controlled variable", "<a href="/wiki/Control_variable" title="Control variable">control variable</a>", or "extraneous variable".</p>
<p>Extraneous variables, if included in a <a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">regression</a> as independent variables, may aid a researcher with accurate response parameter estimation, <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">prediction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Goodness_of_fit" title="Goodness of fit">goodness of fit</a>, but are not of substantive interest to the <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> under examination. For example, in a study examining the effect of post-secondary education on lifetime earnings, some extraneous variables might be gender, ethnicity, social class, genetics, intelligence, age, and so forth. A variable is extraneous only when it can be assumed (or shown) to influence the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dependent_variable" title="Dependent variable">dependent variable</a>. If included in a regression, it can improve the fit of the model. If it is excluded from the regression and if it has a non-zero <a href="/wiki/Covariance" title="Covariance">covariance</a> with one or more of the independent variables of interest, its omission will <a href="/wiki/Bias_(statistics)" title="Bias (statistics)">bias</a> the regression's result for the effect of that independent variable of interest. This effect is called <a href="/wiki/Confounding" title="Confounding">confounding</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omitted_variable_bias" title="Omitted variable bias">omitted variable bias</a>; in these situations, design changes and/or <a href="/wiki/Controlling_for_a_variable" title="Controlling for a variable">statistical control</a> is necessary.</p>
<p>Extraneous variables are often classified into three types:</p>
<ol>
<li>Subject variables, which are the characteristics of the individuals being studied that might affect their actions. These variables include age, gender, health status, mood, background, etc.</li>
<li>Blocking variables or experimental variables are characteristics of the persons conducting the experiment which might influence how a person behaves. Gender, the presence of racial discrimination, language, or other factors may qualify as such variables.</li>
<li>Situational variables are features of the environment in which the study or research was conducted, which have a bearing on the outcome of the experiment in a negative way. Included are the air temperature, level of activity, lighting, and the time of day.</li>
</ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_and_independent_variables&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Effect of fertilizer on plant growth</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>In a study measuring the influence of different quantities of fertilizer on plant growth, the independent variable would be the amount of fertilizer used. The dependent variable would be the growth in height or mass of the plant. The controlled variables would be the type of plant, the type of fertilizer, the amount of sunlight the plant gets, the size of the pots, etc.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Effect of drug dosage on symptom severity</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>In a study of how different doses of a drug affect the severity of symptoms, a researcher could compare the frequency and intensity of symptoms when different doses are administered. Here the independent variable is the dose and the dependent variable is the frequency/intensity of symptoms.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Effect of temperature on pigmentation</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>In measuring the amount of color removed from beetroot samples at different temperatures, temperature is the independent variable and amount of pigment removed is the dependent variable.</dd>
</dl>
