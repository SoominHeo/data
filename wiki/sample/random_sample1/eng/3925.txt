<p>The <b>Type IX U-boat</b> was designed by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Kriegsmarine" title="Kriegsmarine">Kriegsmarine</a></i> in 1935 and 1936 as a large ocean-going <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> for sustained operations far from the home support facilities. Type IX boats were briefly used for patrols off the eastern United States in an attempt to disrupt the stream of troops and supplies bound for Europe. The extended range came at the cost of longer dive times and decreased maneuverability. It was derived from the <a href="/wiki/German_Type_I_submarine" title="German Type I submarine">Type IA</a>, and appeared in various sub-types.</p>
<p>Type IXs had six torpedo tubes; four at the bow and two at the stern. They carried six reloads internally and had five external torpedo containers (three at the stern and two at the bow) which stored ten additional torpedoes. The total of 22 torpedoes allowed U-boat commanders to follow a convoy and strike night after night. Some of the IXC boats were fitted for mine operations; as mine-layers they could carry 44 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TMA_mine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TMA mine (page does not exist)">TMA</a> or 66 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TMB_mine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TMB mine (page does not exist)">TMB mines</a>.</p>
<p>Secondary armament was provided by one <a href="/wiki/10.5_cm_SK_C/32_naval_gun" title="10.5 cm SK C/32 naval gun">10.5 cm (4.1 in) deck gun</a> with 180 rounds. Anti-aircraft armament differed throughout the war. They had two periscopes in the tower. Types IXA and IXB had an additional periscope in the control room, which was removed in Type IXC and afterward.</p>

<p>Type IXB was an improved model with an increased range. It was the most successful version overall with each boat averaging a total of over 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a> sunk.</p>
<p>Notable IXB boats included <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-123_(1940)" title="German submarine U-123 (1940)"><i>U-123</i></a> commanded by <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Hardegen" title="Reinhard Hardegen">Reinhard Hardegen</a>, which opened up the attack in the US waters in early 1942 known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Drumbeat" title="Operation Drumbeat">Operation Drumbeat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-107_(1940)" title="German submarine U-107 (1940)"><i>U-107</i></a> operating off <a href="/wiki/Freetown" title="Freetown">Freetown, Sierra Leone</a> under the command of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnther_Hessler" title="Günther Hessler">Günther Hessler</a>, which had the most successful single mission of the war ever with close to 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a> sunk.</p>

<p>Type IXC was a further refinement of the class with storage for an additional 43 tonnes of fuel, increasing the boat's range. This series omitted the control room periscope leaving the boats with two tower scopes.</p>
<p>As mine-layers they could carry 44 TMA or 66 TMB mines, though <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-162_(1941)" title="German submarine U-162 (1941)"><i>U-162</i></a> through <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-170" title="German submarine U-170"><i>U-170</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-505" title="German submarine U-505"><i>U-505</i></a> through <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-550" title="German submarine U-550"><i>U-550</i></a> (35 boats), were not fitted for mine operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGr.C3.B6ner199168_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGr.C3.B6ner199168-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>One IXC carries the dubious distinction of being the only U-boat sunk in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-166_(1941)" title="German submarine U-166 (1941)"><i>U-166</i></a>.</p>
<p><i>U-505</i> survives at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Science_and_Industry_(Chicago)" title="Museum of Science and Industry (Chicago)">Museum of Science and Industry</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and as of 2005 has been completely renovated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Type IXD was significantly longer and heavier than the IXC/40. It was faster than the IXC but at the cost of slightly reduced range. They had three pairs of Daimler Benz diesels: two pairs for cruise and one for high speed or battery recharge. There were three variants: the IXD1, IXD2 and IXD/42. The IXD1 had unreliable engines and they were later converted for use in surface transport vessels. The IXD2 comprised most of the class and had a range of 12,750 nautical miles (23,610 km; 14,670 mi). The IXD/42, was almost identical but with more engine power (5,400 ehp instead of 4,400).</p>
<p>In 1943 and 1944 the torpedo tubes were removed from a number of IXD boats converted for transport use. In their new role they could transport 252 tonnes of cargo. The range was extended to 31,500 nautical miles (58,300 km; 36,200 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGr.C3.B6ner199175_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGr.C3.B6ner199175-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
