<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of German divisions in World War II</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>This article lists <b><a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">divisions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> (German Armed Forces)</b>, including the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">Heer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kriegsmarine" title="Kriegsmarine">Kriegsmarine</a>, active during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>Upgrades and reorganizations are shown only to identify the variant names for what is notionally a single unit; other upgrades and reorganizations are deferred to the individual articles. Due to the scope of this list pre-war changes are not shown, nor are upgrades from units smaller than a division. Most of these divisions trained in Berlin which is also where new military technology was kept and tested.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_elements_not_usually_translated">Name elements not usually translated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name elements not usually translated">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grenadier_(soldier)" title="Grenadier (soldier)">Grenadier</a></i></dt>
<dd>A traditional term for heavy infantry.</dd>
<dt><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%A4ger_(military)" title="Jäger (military)">Jäger</a></i></dt>
<dd>A traditional term for light infantry (Translated "Hunter").</dd>
<dt><i><a href="/wiki/Gebirgsj%C3%A4ger" title="Gebirgsjäger">Gebirgsjäger</a></i></dt>
<dd>Traditional term for mountain and ski troops.</dd>
<dt><i>Lehr</i></dt>
<dd>A demonstration/training unit (Translated "Teach").</dd>
<dt><i>Nummer</i></dt>
<dd>"Number" (See description in Infantry Series Divisions, below).</dd>
<dt><i><a href="/wiki/Panzer" title="Panzer">Panzer</a></i></dt>
<dd>Armor (Translated "Armored").</dd>
<dt><i>Sturm</i></dt>
<dd>"Storm" or "Assault" (Translated "Storm").</dd>
<dt><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volk" title="Volk">Volks</a></i></dt>
<dd>"of the People" (Translated "People's").</dd>
<dt><i><a href="/wiki/ZbV" title="ZbV">zbV</a></i></dt>
<dd>Abbreviation for "zur besonderen Verwendung" Meaning "Special Purpose" (Translated "For Special Deployment").</dd>
</dl>
<p><i>Volks</i>, <i>Sturm</i>, and <i>Grenadier</i> were sometimes used simply as morale-building adjectives, often without any significance to a unit's organization or capabilities.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_divisions">Light divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Light divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The designation "Light" (<i>leichte</i>) had various meanings in the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">German Army</a> of World War II. There was a series of 5 Light divisions; the first four were pre-war mechanized formations organized for use as mechanized cavalry, and the fifth was an <i>ad hoc</i> collection of mechanized elements rushed to Africa to help the Italians and organized into a division once there. All five were eventually converted to ordinary Panzer divisions.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/6th_Panzer_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="6th Panzer Division (Wehrmacht)">1st Light Division</a> (later 6th Panzer Division)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2nd_Light_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="2nd Light Division (Wehrmacht)">2nd Light Division</a> (later 7th Panzer Division)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/3rd_Light_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="3rd Light Division (Wehrmacht)">3rd Light Division</a> (later 8th Panzer Division)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/9th_Panzer_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="9th Panzer Division (Wehrmacht)">4th Light Division</a> (later 9th Panzer Division)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/21st_Panzer_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="21st Panzer Division (Wehrmacht)">5th Light Afrika Division</a> (later 21st Panzer Division)</li>
</ul>
<p>Various other divisions were dubbed "Light" for other reasons, and are listed among the Infantry Series Divisions.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_division_in_the_series">Types of division in the series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types of division in the series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The backbone of the Heer (German Army) was the infantry division. Of the 154 divisions deployed against Soviet Union in 1941, including reserves, there were 100 infantry, 19 panzer, 11 motorised, 9 security, 5 Waffen-SS, 4 "light", 4 mountain, 1 SS police, and 1 cavalry. A typical infantry division in June 1941 had 17,734 men organized into the following sub-units:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>three infantry regiments with staff and communications units
<ul>
<li>three battalions with:
<ul>
<li>three infantry companies</li>
<li>one heavy weapons company</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>one PAK company (mot.)</li>
<li>one artillery company</li>
<li>one reconnaissance unit</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>one Panzerjäger battalion with:
<ul>
<li>three companies (each with twelve 3.7 cm guns)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>one artillery regiment
<ul>
<li>three battalions
<ul>
<li>three batteries</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>one pioneer battalion</li>
<li>one communications unit</li>
<li>one field replacement battalion</li>
<li>Supply, medical, veterinary, mail, and police</li>
</ul>
<p>German infantry divisions had a variety of designations and specializations, though numbered in a single series. The major variations are as follows:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Fortress (<i>Festung</i>)</dt>
<dd>Divisions of non-standard organization used to garrison critical sites. The smaller ones might consist of only two or three battalions.</dd>
<dt><i><a href="/wiki/Grenadier" title="Grenadier">Grenadier</a></i></dt>
<dd>A morale-building honorific usually indicative of reduced strength when used alone.</dd>
<dt><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%A4ger_(military)" title="Jäger (military)">Jäger</a></i></dt>
<dd>Reduced in size with only two regiments compared to an ordinary infantry divisions three and provided with less transport and lighter artillery. Those divisions were trained to fight in difficult terrain.</dd>
<dt>Light (<i>Leichte</i>)</dt>
<dd>There were several meanings of the term light division in the Wehrmacht:</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The initial light infantry divisions. Lighter than a normal infantry division; they had only two regiments and no heavy artillery. Most of them were converted to Jäger divisions in 1942.</li>
<li>The light Africa divisions. Created partially ad-hoc and sporting a unique composition, e.g. including penal units, they were usually lighter than a standard infantry divisions.</li>
<li>The light division. Any division with a lack of units, like only having two regiments, or less support units could be styled light. Usually only applied temporarily to a division; the respective unit would be drawn off from the front to reorganize and renamed again after it regained the lost units and manpower.</li>
<li>The light (mechanized) divisions that were the predecessors of the Panzer divisions. Those are already covered in the Panzer division section.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Motorized_infantry" title="Motorized infantry">Motorized</a></dt>
<dd>Provided with full motor transport for all infantry and weapons systems. Usually reduced in size compared to an ordinary infantry division. Motorized infantry divisions were renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Panzergrenadier" title="Panzergrenadier">Panzergrenadier</a></i> (armored infantry) divisions in 1943.</dd>
<dt><i>Division Nummer</i></dt>
<dd>A sort of placeholder division, with a number (<i>Nummer</i>) and staff but few if any combat assets. These divisions started out without any type in their name (e.g., <i>Division Nr. 179</i>), though some acquired a type later on (e.g., <i>Panzer Division Nr. 179</i>).</dd>
<dt><i>Panzergrenadier</i></dt>
<dd>As motorized, but with more self-propelled weapons and an added battalion of tanks or fully armored assault guns. What motorized divisions were referred to from 1943 forward.</dd>
<dt>Static (<i>bodenständige</i>)</dt>
<dd>Deficient in transport, even enough to move its own artillery. Many of these were divisions that had been mauled on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> and were sent west to serve as coastal defense garrisons until sufficient resources were available to rehabilitate them.</dd>
<dt><i><a href="/wiki/Volksgrenadier" title="Volksgrenadier">Volksgrenadier</a></i></dt>
<dd>A late-war reorganization with reduced size and increased short-range firepower. Many previously destroyed or badly mauled infantry divisions were reconstituted as <i>Volksgrenadier</i> divisions, and new ones were raised as well. Their fighting worth varied widely depending on unit experience and equipment. Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Volkssturm" title="Volkssturm">Volkssturm</a> a <i>national militia</i> in which units were supposed to be organized by local Nazi party leaders; trained by the SS; and come under Wehrmacht command in combat.</dd>
<dt><i>zbV</i></dt>
<dd>("zbV" is an abbreviation meaning "for special employment") An <i>ad hoc</i> division created to meet a special requirement. (E.g., <a href="/wiki/90th_Light_Infantry_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="90th Light Infantry Division (Wehrmacht)">Division zbV <i>Afrika</i></a>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>Most of the size reductions listed above were by about a third, either by the removal of an infantry regiment or the removal of one infantry battalion from each of the three regiments.</p>
<p>Infantry divisions were raised in <i>waves</i>, sets of divisions with a standardized table of organization and equipment. In general the later waves (i.e., the higher-numbered divisions) were of lower quality than the earlier ones.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_Force_.28Luftwaffe.29">Air Force (Luftwaffe)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Air Force (Luftwaffe)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hermann_G.C3.B6ring_divisions">Hermann Göring divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Hermann Göring divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a> formations grew from a single police detachment to an entire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_warfare" title="Armored warfare">armored</a> <a href="/wiki/Corps" title="Corps">corps</a> over the course of the war. The later epithet <i>Fallschirm</i> ("parachute") was purely honorific.</p>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring_Division" title="Hermann Göring Division">Hermann Göring Division</a> (later Panzer Division <i>Hermann Göring</i>, Parachute Panzer Division <i>1 Hermann Göring</i>)</li>

</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airborne_divisions">Airborne divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Airborne divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To keep its existence secret, the first German <a href="/wiki/Airborne_forces" title="Airborne forces">airborne</a> division was named as if a <i>Flieger</i> ("flier") division in the series of <i>Luftwaffe</i> divisions that controlled air assets rather than ground troops-named <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=7th_Flieger_Division_(Germany)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="7th Flieger Division (Germany) (page does not exist)">7th <i>Flieger</i> Division</a> (often translated <i>7th Air Division</i> - which see: <a href="/wiki/1st_Parachute_Division_(Germany)" title="1st Parachute Division (Germany)">1st Parachute Division (Germany)</a>) The division was later reorganized to start a series of nominally airborne divisions. Though named <i><a href="/wiki/Fallschirmj%C3%A4ger" title="Fallschirmjäger">Fallschirmjäger</a></i> ("paratrooper") divisions, only some of them participated in <a href="/wiki/Airdrop" title="Airdrop">airdrops</a> in the early part of the war, and in practice most operated as ordinary infantry throughout their existence. The lower-numbered ones earned and maintained an élite status, but quality generally declined among the higher-numbered divisions.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/7th_Parachute_Division_(Germany)" title="7th Parachute Division (Germany)">7th Parachute Division</a> (previously <i>Group <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Erdmann" title="Wolfgang Erdmann">Erdmann</a></i>, an <i>ad hoc</i> collection of <i>Luftwaffe</i> assets on the Western Front)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/20th_Parachute_Division_(Germany)" title="20th Parachute Division (Germany)">20th Parachute Division</a> (Formation ordered 20 March 1945 in the Netherlands, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parachute_Training_and_Replacement_Division" title="Parachute Training and Replacement Division">Parachute Training and Replacement Division</a>. However formation was not completed beyond cadre.)</li>

</ul>

<p><a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe_Field_Division" title="Luftwaffe Field Division">Luftwaffe Field Divisions</a> were ordinary <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> divisions organized from <i>Luftwaffe</i> personnel made available after mid-war due to the manpower crunch. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Waffen-SS_.28Schutzstaffel.29">Waffen-SS (Schutzstaffel)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_divisions_in_World_War_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Waffen-SS (Schutzstaffel)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Waffen-SS_divisions" title="List of Waffen-SS divisions">List of Waffen-SS divisions</a></div>
<p>All divisions in the <i><a href="/wiki/Waffen-SS" title="Waffen-SS">Waffen-SS</a></i> were ordered in a single series up to 38th, regardless of type. Those tagged with nationalities were at least nominally recruited from those nationalities. Many of the higher-numbered units were small battlegroups (<a href="/wiki/Kampfgruppe" title="Kampfgruppe">Kampfgruppen</a>), i.e. divisions in name only.</p>
<p>Also <a href="/wiki/Panzer_Division_Kempf" title="Panzer Division Kempf">Panzer Division <i>Kempf</i></a>, a temporary unit of mixed <i>Heer</i> and <i>Waffen-SS</i> components.</p>
