<p>The <b>German Democratic Party</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Deutsche Demokratische Partei</i></span>, DDP) was founded in November 1918 by leaders of the former <a href="/wiki/Progressive_People%27s_Party_(Germany)" title="Progressive People's Party (Germany)">Progressive People's Party</a> (<i>Fortschrittliche Volkspartei</i>), left members of the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(Germany)" title="National Liberal Party (Germany)">National Liberal Party</a> (<i>Nationalliberale Partei</i>), and a new group calling themselves the Democrats.</p>
<p>In 1930 the party changed to the <b><a href="/wiki/German_State_Party" title="German State Party">Deutsche Staatspartei</a></b> (DStP).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Democratic_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Democrats were a more left-wing or <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberal</a> party, whereas the <a href="/wiki/German_People%27s_Party" title="German People's Party">German People's Party</a> was right-wing liberal. Many of the leading figures in the party had been supporters of <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Imperial Germany</a>'s aim of <i><a href="/wiki/Weltpolitik" title="Weltpolitik">Weltpolitik</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mitteleuropa#The_Prussian_Mitteleuropa_Plan" title="Mitteleuropa">Mitteleuropa</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Along with the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democrats</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Germany)" title="Centre Party (Germany)">Centre Party</a>, the Democratic party was most committed to maintaining a democratic, republican form of government. Its social basis were middle class entrepreneurs, civil servants, teachers, scientists and craftsmen. It considered itself also a devotedly national party and opposed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> but emphasized on the other hand the need for international collaboration and the protection of ethnic minorities. The party was the one voted for by most Jews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The party was attacked by some for being a party of Jews and professors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Persons_and_governments">Persons and governments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Democratic_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Persons and governments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The party's first leader was Protestant parish priest <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Naumann" title="Friedrich Naumann">Friedrich Naumann</a>, who was popular and influential but failed with his <i><a href="/wiki/National-Social_Association" title="National-Social Association">Nationalsozialer Verein</a></i> ten years earlier to link progressive intellectuals with the worker's class. He died early in 1919. Other well-known politicians of the DDP were <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Preu%C3%9F" title="Hugo Preuß">Hugo Preuß</a>, the main author of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weimar_constitution" title="Weimar constitution">Weimar constitution</a>, and the eminent sociologist <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Schacht" title="Hjalmar Schacht">Hjalmar Schacht</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/Reichsbank" title="Reichsbank">Reichsbank</a> and one of the founders of the party, left the party in 1926 and became a supporter of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>.</p>
<p>Nearly all German governments from 1918 to 1931 included ministers from the DDP, such as <a href="/wiki/Walther_Rathenau" title="Walther Rathenau">Walther Rathenau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Schiffer" title="Eugen Schiffer">Eugen Schiffer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugo_Preuss" title="Hugo Preuss">Hugo Preuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Riezler" title="Kurt Riezler">Kurt Riezler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Gessler" title="Otto Gessler">Otto Gessler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erich_Koch-Weser" title="Erich Koch-Weser">Erich Koch-Weser</a>. From their 18% share of the first elections under <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> in <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_1919" title="German federal election, 1919">1919</a>, they dropped to for example 4.9% in <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_1928" title="German federal election, 1928">1928</a> and 1.0% in <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_November_1932" title="German federal election, November 1932">November 1932</a>.</p>
<p>The party merged with the more right-leaning <a href="/wiki/Young_German_Order" title="Young German Order">Young German Order</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/German_State_Party" title="German State Party">German State Party</a> in 1930. With <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Quidde" title="Ludwig Quidde">Ludwig Quidde</a> (Nobel Peace Prize winner of 1927) and others the party had a pacifist wing which left the Party in 1930 and founded the <i>Radical Democratic Party</i>, who represented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical_Democracy" title="Radical Democracy">radical democratic</a> and more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">Left-wing</a> policies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1945">After 1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Democratic_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After 1945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After 1945 former politicians of the DDP joined mainly the new <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democratic Party</a> (1945/1948), as did the liberals from the <a href="/wiki/German_People%27s_Party" title="German People's Party">German People's Party</a>. First Federal President <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Heuss" title="Theodor Heuss">Theodor Heuss</a>, a journalist and professor in history, had been a German State Party deputy in 1933. In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_occupation_zone" title="Soviet occupation zone">Soviet occupation zone</a> the liberal leader was former DDP minister <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_K%C3%BClz" title="Wilhelm Külz">Wilhelm Külz</a>.</p>
<p>Other DDP members went to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democrats</a>, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Lemmer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ernst Lemmer (page does not exist)">Ernst Lemmer</a>, the former leader of the Young Democrats and Federal Minister in 1956-1965.</p>
