<p>"<b>Heil dir im Siegerkranz</b>" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for "Hail to Thee in Victor's Crown") was the official <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> from 1871 to 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Before the foundation of the Empire, it had been the royal anthem of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> since 1795 and remained it after 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The melody of the hymn derived from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> anthem "<a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen" title="God Save the Queen">God Save the Queen</a>". For these reasons, the song failed to become popular within all of Germany. Not only did it fail to win the support of most German nationalists, it was never recognized by the southern German states, such as <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> or <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="WÃ¼rttemberg">WÃ¼rttemberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the German Empire came to an end and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Das_Lied_der_Deutschen" title="Das Lied der Deutschen">Das Lied der Deutschen</a>" became the national anthem of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Die_Wacht_am_Rhein" title="Die Wacht am Rhein">Die Wacht am Rhein</a>" ("The Watch on the Rhine") was also a patriotic hymn so popular that it was often regarded as an unofficial <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heil_dir_im_Siegerkranz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Harries" title="Heinrich Harries">Heinrich Harries</a> wrote the lyrics in 1790 in honour of King <a href="/wiki/Christian_VII_of_Denmark" title="Christian VII of Denmark">Christian VII of Denmark</a>, and the line "Heil, Kaiser, dir" originally read "Heil, Christian, dir". In 1793, Harries' text was adapted by Balthasar Gerhard Schumacher for use in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>. Schumacher shortened Harries' text and replaced the word <a href="/wiki/Christian_VII_of_Denmark" title="Christian VII of Denmark">Christian</a> with the word <i>KÃ¶nig</i> (king). After the proclamation of the German Empire, the word <i>KÃ¶nig</i> was replaced by <i>Kaiser</i> (emperor).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>These lyrics were used for the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_II" title="Kaiser Wilhelm II">Kaiser Wilhelm II</a>, German Emperor and King of Prussia. One of the jokes at the time was that the song's title be changed to "Heil Dir im Sonderzug" ("Hail to Thee in Thy Royal Train"), owing to the Kaiser's frequent travels.</p>
