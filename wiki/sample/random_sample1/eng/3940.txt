<p>The <b>Alternative for Germany</b> (German: <span lang="de"><i>Alternative für Deutschland</i></span>, <b>AfD</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_populism" title="Right-wing populism">right-wing populist</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decker2015_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Decker2015-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bieling2015_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bieling2015-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jahn2015_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jahn2015-29">[29]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Euroscepticism" title="Euroscepticism">Eurosceptic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lansford2014_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lansford2014-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DervisMistral2014_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-DervisMistral2014-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladrech2014_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ladrech2014-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniel2015_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daniel2015-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Germany" title="List of political parties in Germany">political party in Germany</a>.</p>
<p>Founded in April 2013, the party won 4.7% of the votes in the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">2013 federal election</a>, narrowly missing the 5% electoral threshold to sit in the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a>. In 2014 the party won 7.1% of the votes and 7 out of 96 German seats in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Germany)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Germany)">European election</a>, and subsequently joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Conservatives_and_Reformists" title="European Conservatives and Reformists">European Conservatives and Reformists</a> (ECR) group until its exclusion in April 2016. As of September 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the AfD had gained representation in ten of the 16 <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German state</a> parliaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The party is currently led by <a href="/wiki/Frauke_Petry" title="Frauke Petry">Frauke Petry</a> and <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rg_Meuthen" title="Jörg Meuthen">Jörg Meuthen</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_2012.E2.80.9313">Founding 2012–13</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding 2012–13">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2012, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gauland" title="Alexander Gauland">Alexander Gauland</a>, a former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State" title="Secretary of State">State Secretary</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Lucke" title="Bernd Lucke">Bernd Lucke</a>, an economist and Konrad Adam, a former editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung">Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung</a></i> from 1979 to 2000 and chief correspondent of <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Welt" title="Die Welt">Die Welt</a></i> until 2008, founded the political group <i>Electoral Alternative 2013</i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Wahlalternative 2013</i></span>) in <a href="/wiki/Bad_Nauheim" title="Bad Nauheim">Bad Nauheim</a>, to oppose German federal policies concerning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_sovereign-debt_crisis" title="European sovereign-debt crisis">eurozone crisis</a>. Their <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a> was endorsed by 68 economists, journalists, and business leaders, half of whom were professors and three-quarters of whom had academic degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The group stated that the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">eurozone</a> had proven to be "<a href="/wiki/Optimum_currency_area" title="Optimum currency area">unsuitable</a>" as a currency area and that southern European states were "sinking into poverty under the competitive pressure of the euro".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Welt0303_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Welt0303-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some members of the later AfD sought election in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a> as the <i>Electoral Alternative 2013</i> in alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Free_Voters" title="Free Voters">Free Voters</a>, an association participating in local elections without specific federal or foreign policies, and received 1% of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Welt0303_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Welt0303-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In February 2013 the group decided to found a new party to compete in the 2013 federal elections. The Free Voters leadership declined to join forces, according to a leaked email from Bernd Lucke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Advocating the abolition of the Euro, Alternative for Germany (AfD) took a more radical stance than the Free Voters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pop_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pop-39">[39]</a></sup> Likewise, the Pirate Party of Germany opposed any coalition with the AfD at their 2013 spring convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>The AfD's initial supporters were the same prominent economists, business leaders and journalists who had supported the <i>Electoral Alternative 2013</i>, including former members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a>, who had previously challenged the constitutionality of the German government's eurozone policies at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Constitutional_Court" title="German Constitutional Court">German Constitutional Court</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BusinessInsider_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessInsider-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 14 April 2013, the AfD announced its presence to the wider public when it held its first convention in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, elected the party leadership and adopted a party platform. Bernd Lucke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DailyPolitics_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-DailyPolitics-43">[43]</a></sup> entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Frauke_Petry" title="Frauke Petry">Frauke Petry</a> and Konrad Adam were elected as speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The AfD federal board also chose three deputy speakers, Alexander Gauland, Roland Klaus and Patricia Casale. The party elected treasurer Norbert Stenzel and the three assessors Irina Smirnova, Beatrix Diefenbach and Wolf-Joachim Schünemann. The economist <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joachim_Starbatty" title="de:Joachim Starbatty">Joachim Starbatty</a>, along with <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%B6rn_Kruse" title="de:Jörn Kruse">Jörn Kruse</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helga_Luckenbach" title="de:Helga Luckenbach">Helga Luckenbach</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirk_Meyer_(%C3%96konom)" title="de:Dirk Meyer (Ökonom)">Dirk Meyer</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roland_Vaubel" title="de:Roland Vaubel">Roland Vaubel</a> were elected to the party's scientific advisory board. Between 31 March and 12 May 2013 the AfD founded affiliates in all 16 <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German states</a> in order to participate in the federal elections. On 15 June 2013 the <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Alternative_for_Germany" title="Young Alternative for Germany">Young Alternative for Germany</a></i> was founded in <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt" title="Darmstadt">Darmstadt</a> as the AfD's youth organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernd_Lucke_und_die_wilde_Jugend_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernd_Lucke_und_die_wilde_Jugend-45">[45]</a></sup> In April 2013, during <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>'s visit to Germany, the British <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> was reported to have contacted both AfD and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Voters" title="Free Voters">Free Voters</a> to discuss possible cooperation, supported by the <a href="/wiki/European_Conservatives_and_Reformists" title="European Conservatives and Reformists">European Conservatives and Reformists</a> (ECR) group of the European Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In June 2013, Bernd Lucke gave a question and answer session organised by the Conservative Party-allied <a href="/wiki/Bruges_Group" title="Bruges Group">Bruges Group</a> <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portcullis_House" title="Portcullis House">Portcullis House</a>, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OELuckeLon_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-OELuckeLon-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In a detailed report in the conservative <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung">Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung</a></i> in April 2013, the paper's Berlin-based political correspondent Majid Sattar revealed that the SPD and CDU had conducted <a href="/wiki/Opposition_research" title="Opposition research">opposition research</a> to blunt the growth and attraction of the AfD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinthal_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weinthal-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The party was created Bernd Lucke, Alexander Gauland and Konrad Adam to confront German-supported bailouts for poorer southern European countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_federal_election">2013 federal election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2013 federal election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">German federal election, 2013</a></div>
<p>On 22 September 2013, the AfD won 4.7% of the votes in the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">2013 federal election</a>, missing the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_system_of_Germany#Election_threshold" title="Electoral system of Germany">5% barrier</a> to enter the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a>. The party won about 2 million party list votes and 810,000 constituency votes, which was 1.9% of the total of these votes cast across Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_state_elections">2013 state elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2013 state elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The AfD did not participate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bavaria_state_election,_2013" title="Bavaria state election, 2013">2013 Bavaria state election</a> held on 15 September 2013. The AfD gained its first representation in the state parliament of <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> with the defection of Jochen Paulus from the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democratic Party</a> (FDP) to the AfD in early May 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paulick_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paulick-53">[53]</a></sup> who was not re-elected and left office in January 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesse_state_election,_2013" title="Hesse state election, 2013">2013 Hesse state election</a> held on 22 September 2013, the same day as the 2013 federal election, the AfD failed to gain representation in the parliament with 4.0% of the vote.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_European_Parliament_election">2014 European Parliament election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2014 European Parliament election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Germany)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Germany)">European Parliament election, 2014 (Germany)</a></div>
<p>In early 2014, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany" title="Federal Constitutional Court of Germany">Federal Constitutional Court of Germany</a> ruled the proposed 3% vote hurdle for representation in the European elections unconstitutional, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Germany)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Germany)">2014 European Parliament election</a> became the first run in Germany without a barrier for representation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG/220px-Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG/330px-Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG/440px-Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wahlplakat_2013_AfD_01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Former "Mut zur Wahrheit! The Euro splits Europe" tagline on election placard 2013</div>

<p>The AfD held a party conference on 25 January 2014 at <a href="/wiki/F.a.n._Frankenstolz_Arena" title="F.a.n. Frankenstolz Arena">Frankenstolz Arena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a>, northwest <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. The conference chose the slogan <i>Mut zu Deutschland</i> ("Courage [to stand up] for Germany") to replace the former slogan <i>Mut zur Wahrheit</i> (lit. "Courage [to speak] the truth" or, more succinctly, "Telling it as it is"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benzow_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benzow-56">[56]</a></sup> which prompted disagreement among the federal board that the party could be seen as too anti-European. Eventually a compromise was reached by using the slogan "MUT ZU D*EU*TSCHLAND, with the "EU" in "DEUTSCHLAND" encircled by the 12 stars of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Europe" title="Flag of Europe">European flag</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lachmann_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lachmann-57">[57]</a></sup> The conference elected the top six candidates for the European elections on 26 January 2014 and met again the following weekend to choose the remaining euro candidates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benzow_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benzow-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lachmann_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lachmann-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Candidates from 7th–28th place on the party list were selected in Berlin on 1 February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Party chairman <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Lucke" title="Bernd Lucke">Bernd Lucke</a> was elected as lead candidate.</p>
<p>In February 2014, AfD officials said they had discussed alliances with Britain's anti-EU <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UK Independence Party</a> (UKIP), which Bernd Lucke and the federal board of AfD opposed, and also with the ECR group, to which the British Conservative Party belongs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-60">[60]</a></sup> In April 2014 <a href="/wiki/Hans-Olaf_Henkel" title="Hans-Olaf Henkel">Hans-Olaf Henkel</a>, AfD's second candidate on the European election list, ruled out forming a group with UKIP after the 2014 European election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaterfieldII_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaterfieldII-61">[61]</a></sup> stating that he saw the British Conservatives as the preferred partner in the European Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaterfieldII_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-WaterfieldII-61">[61]</a></sup> On 10 May 2014 Bernd Lucke had been in talks with the Czech and Polish member parties of ECR group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker11May_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker11May-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 25 May <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Germany)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Germany)">2014 European election</a>, the AfD came in fifth place in Germany, with 7.1% of the national vote (2,065,162 votes), and seven <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">members of the EU parliament</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bu_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bu-63">[63]</a></sup> On 12 June 2014 it was announced that the AfD had been accepted into the <a href="/wiki/European_Conservatives_and_Reformists" title="European Conservatives and Reformists">European Conservatives and Reformists</a> (ECR) group in the European Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolaou_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicolaou-64">[64]</a></sup> The official vote result was not released to the public, but figures of 29 votes for and 26 against were reported by the membership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolaou_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicolaou-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_state_elections">2014 state elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2014 state elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 31 August 2014, the AfD scored 9.7% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/Saxony_state_election,_2014" title="Saxony state election, 2014">Saxony state election</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> winning 14 seats in the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_the_Free_State_of_Saxony" title="Landtag of the Free State of Saxony">Landtag of Saxony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and on 14 September 2014 they obtained 10.6% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_state_election,_2014" title="Thuringian state election, 2014">Thuringian</a> and 12.2% in the <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_state_election,_2014" title="Brandenburg state election, 2014">Brandenburg state election</a>, winning 11 seats in both state parliaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_state_elections">2015 state elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2015 state elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 15 February 2015 AfD won 6.1% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_state_election,_2015" title="Hamburg state election, 2015">2015 Hamburg state election</a>, gaining the mandate for eight seats in the <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Parliament" title="Hamburg Parliament">Hamburg Parliament</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> winning their first seats in a western German state.</p>
<p>On 10 May the AfD secured in the 5.5% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/Bremen_state_election,_2015" title="Bremen state election, 2015">Bremen state election, 2015</a> gaining representation in their 5th state parliament on a 50% turnout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petry_assumes_leadership.2C_Lucke_quits">Petry assumes leadership, Lucke quits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Petry assumes leadership, Lucke quits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After months of factional infighting and a cancelled party gathering in June 2015, on 4 July 2015 <a href="/wiki/Frauke_Petry" title="Frauke Petry">Frauke Petry</a> was elected as the <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> principal speaker of the party with 60% of the member votes ahead of Bernd Lucke at a party congress in <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a>. Petry is a member of the national-conservative faction of the AfD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Her leadership was widely seen as heralding a shift of the party to the right, to focus more on issues such as migration, Islam and strengthening ties to Russia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> a shift which was claimed by Lucke as turning the party into a "<a href="/wiki/Pegida" title="Pegida">Pegida</a> party".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> In the following week, five MEPs exited the party on 7 July, the only remaining MEPs being <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_von_Storch" title="Beatrix von Storch">Beatrix von Storch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Pretzell" title="Marcus Pretzell">Marcus Pretzell</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> and on 8 July 2015, Lucke announced that he was resigning from the AfD, citing the rise of xenophobic and pro-Russian sentiments in the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> At a meeting of members of the Wake-up call (Weckruf 2015) group on 19 July 2015, the founder of the AfD Bernd Lucke and former AfD members announced they would form a new party, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Conservative_Reformers" title="Liberal Conservative Reformers">Alliance for Progress and Renewal</a> (ALFA), under the founding principles of the AfD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-operation_with_FP.C3.96_and_exclusion_from_ECR_group">Co-operation with FPÖ and exclusion from ECR group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Co-operation with FPÖ and exclusion from ECR group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2016, the AfD announced a cooperation pact with the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Party_of_Austria" title="Freedom Party of Austria">Freedom Party of Austria</a> (FPÖ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afdfpoe_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-afdfpoe-76">[76]</a></sup> On 8 March 2016, the bureau of the ECR Group began motions to exclude the AfD from their group due to its links with the far-right FPÖ <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euractiv1_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Euractiv1-77">[77]</a></sup> inviting the two remaining AfD MEPs to leave the group by 31 March, with a motion of exclusion to be tabled on 12 April if they refuse to leave voluntarily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUobserver_March_2016_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUobserver_March_2016-78">[78]</a></sup> While MEP <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_von_Storch" title="Beatrix von Storch">Beatrix von Storch</a> left the ECR group on 8 April to join the <a href="/wiki/Europe_of_Freedom_and_Direct_Democracy" title="Europe of Freedom and Direct Democracy">Europe of Freedom and Direct Democracy</a> group,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storch_quits_ECR_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storch_quits_ECR-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storch_joins_EFDD_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storch_joins_EFDD-80">[80]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Pretzell" title="Marcus Pretzell">Marcus Pretzell</a> let himself be expelled on 12 April 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pretzell_expelled_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pretzell_expelled-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_state_elections">2016 state elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2016 state elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/European_migrant_crisis" title="European migrant crisis">migrant debate</a> remaining the dominant national issue, on 13 March 2016 elections held in the three states of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a> saw the AfD receiving double-digit percentages of the vote in all three states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt_state_election,_2016" title="Saxony-Anhalt state election, 2016">2016 Saxony-Anhalt state election</a>, the AfD reached second place in the Landtag, receiving 24.2% of the vote. In the <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg_state_election,_2016" title="Baden-Württemberg state election, 2016">2016 Baden-Württemberg state election</a>, the AfD achieved third place with 15.1% of the vote. In the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate_state_election,_2016" title="Rhineland-Palatinate state election, 2016">2016 Rhineland-Palatinate state election</a>, the AfD again reached third place with 12.6% of the vote. In <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>'s home state of <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a> her <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">CDU</a> was beaten into third place following a strong showing of the AfD who contested at state level for the first time, to claim the second-highest polling with 20.8% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern_state_election,_2016" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern state election, 2016">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern state election, 2016</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> However AfD voter support in Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania appears to have come from both left and right wing parties with support for the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">SPD</a> down 4.9%, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">CDU</a> down 4.1%, <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">The Left</a> down 5.2%, <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Alliance '90/The Greens</a> down 3.9% and support for the <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party of Germany</a> halved, dropping 3.0%. Rising support for the AfD meant that <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">The Greens</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party of Germany</a> failed to reach the 5% threshold to qualify for seats in the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Landtag of Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Landtag of Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a> so consequently lost their seats. In the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_state_election,_2016" title="Berlin state election, 2016">Berlin state election, 2016</a> which the AfD also contested for the first time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> they achieved a vote of 14.2% making them the fifth largest party represented in the state assembly. Their vote seems to have come equally from the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">SPD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">CDU</a> whose votes declined 6.7% and 5.7% respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_party_congress">2016 party congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2016 party congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the party congress held on 30 April to 1 May 2016, the AfD adopted a policy platform based upon opposition to Islam, calling for the ban of Islamic symbols including <a href="/wiki/Burqa" title="Burqa">burkhas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minaret" title="Minaret">minarets</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Adhan" title="Adhan">call to prayer</a>, using the slogan "Islam is not a part of Germany".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-MM1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-MM2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-MM3-10">[10]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_and_policies">Ideology and policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ideology and policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AfD was founded as a <a href="/wiki/Centre-right_politics" title="Centre-right politics">centre-right</a> <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a> party of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a> with a tendency toward 'soft' Euroscepticism, being generally supportive of Germany's membership in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> but critical of further <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a>, the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> currency, and the bailouts by the eurozone for countries such as Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kessel2015_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kessel2015-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson2014_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson2014-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGowanPhinnemore2015_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGowanPhinnemore2015-91">[91]</a></sup> The party also advocated support for Swiss-style <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a>, dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">Eurozone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_immigration" title="Opposition to immigration">opposition to immigration</a>, and opposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_marriage" title="Gay marriage">gay marriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson2015_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson2015-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>By May 2015, the party became polarised into two factions, one centred around Lucke and his core economic policies and another group led by Petry, which favoured an <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_immigration" title="Opposition to immigration">anti-immigration</a> approach. The result was that Lucke's fraction left to found a new party: the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Conservative_Reformers" title="Liberal Conservative Reformers">Alliance for Progress and Renewal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-split_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-split-93">[93]</a></sup> later renamed the Liberal Conservative Reformers in November 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_equal_opportunities">Family and equal opportunities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Family and equal opportunities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to its interim electoral manifesto, the party is against same-sex marriage or adoption. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAZ_Wahlprogramm_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAZ_Wahlprogramm-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wahlprogramm2016_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wahlprogramm2016-95">[95]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Tageszeitung" title="Die Tageszeitung">Die Tageszeitung</a></i> described the group as advocating 'old gender roles'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party denies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic" title="Anthropogenic">Human involvement</a> in the role of global warming. Therefore, the party criticizes the energy transformation policies (<i>Energiewende</i>) in Germany; for instance, the party wants to stop "uncontrolled expansion of wind energy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wahlprogramm2016_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wahlprogramm2016-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conscription">Conscription</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Conscription">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>AfD wants a reinstatement of <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_Germany" title="Conscription in Germany">conscription</a>, starting for men at the age of 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAZ_Wahlprogramm_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAZ_Wahlprogramm-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wahlprogramm2016_95-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wahlprogramm2016-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Party_finances">Party finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Party finances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Party_finance_in_Germany" title="Party finance in Germany">Party finance in Germany</a></div>
<p>Because the 2013 federal election was the first fought by the party, the AfD had not received any federal funds in the run-up to it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winand_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winand-97">[97]</a></sup> but after receiving 2 million votes it crossed the threshold for party funding and was expected to receive an estimated 1.3 to 1.5 million Euro per year of state subsidies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_affiliations">European affiliations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: European affiliations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014" title="European Parliament election, 2014">2014 European Parliament elections</a>, on 12 June 2014 the AfD was accepted into the <a href="/wiki/European_Conservatives_and_Reformists" title="European Conservatives and Reformists">European Conservatives and Reformists</a> (ECR) group in the European Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolaou_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nicolaou-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2016, the AfD announced a closer cooperation with the right-wing populist party <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Party_of_Austria" title="Freedom Party of Austria">Freedom Party of Austria</a> (FPÖ), which is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Europe_of_Nations_and_Freedom" title="Europe of Nations and Freedom">Europe of Nations and Freedom</a> (ENF) group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afdfpoe_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-afdfpoe-76">[76]</a></sup> On 8 March 2016, the bureau of the ECR Group began motions to exclude AfD MEPs from their group due to the party's links with the far-right FPÖ and controversial remarks about shooting immigrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euractiv1_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-Euractiv1-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUobserver_March_2016_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-EUobserver_March_2016-78">[78]</a></sup> While MEP <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_von_Storch" title="Beatrix von Storch">Beatrix von Storch</a> pre-empted her imminent expulsion, leaving the ECR group to join the <a href="/wiki/Europe_of_Freedom_and_Direct_Democracy" title="Europe of Freedom and Direct Democracy">Europe of Freedom and Direct Democracy</a> group on 8 April,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storch_quits_ECR_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-Storch_quits_ECR-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storch_joins_EFDD_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-Storch_joins_EFDD-80">[80]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Pretzell" title="Marcus Pretzell">Marcus Pretzell</a> let himself be expelled from the ECR group on 12 April 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pretzell_expelled_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pretzell_expelled-81">[81]</a></sup> During the AfD party convention on 30 April 2016, Pretzell announced his intention to join the <a href="/wiki/Europe_of_Nations_and_Freedom" title="Europe of Nations and Freedom">Europe of Nations and Freedom</a> group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_image">Public image</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Public image">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the outset AfD presented itself as conservative and middle-class,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittrock_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wittrock-101">[101]</a></sup> catering to a well-educated demographic as more than two-thirds of its initial supporters held doctorates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-102">[102]</a></sup> giving it the nickname the "professors' party".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connelly_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Connelly-103">[103]</a></sup> The party was described as professors and academics who dislike the compromises inflicted on their purist theories by German party politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scally_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scally-104">[104]</a></sup> 86% of the party's initial supporters were male.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paulick_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paulick-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_extremes">Political extremes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Political extremes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternative for Germany party organisers have been sending out the message that they are not trying to attract right-wing radicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittrock_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wittrock-101">[101]</a></sup> The AfD check applicants for membership to exclude <a href="/wiki/Far-right_politics" title="Far-right politics">far-right</a> and former <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party of Germany</a> (NPD) members who support the anti-Euro policy (as other mainstream German political parties do).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittrock_101-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wittrock-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_102-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexander_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-105">[105]</a></sup> The party toned down rhetoric on their <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> page following media allegations that it too closely evoked the language of the far-right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittrock_101-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wittrock-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Former party chairman Bernd Lucke initially defended the choice of words, citing freedom of opinion, and a right to use "strong words", meanwhile he has also said that "The applause is coming from the wrong side" in regards to praise his party gained from the National Democratic Party of Germany (NPD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittrock_101-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wittrock-101">[101]</a></sup> Outside the Berlin hotel where the party held its inaugural meeting, it has been alleged that copies of <i><a href="/wiki/Junge_Freiheit" title="Junge Freiheit">Junge Freiheit</a></i>, a weekly that is also popular with the far-right were being handed out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barkin_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barkin-107">[107]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Rheinische_Post" title="Rheinische Post">Rheinische Post</a></i> pointed out that some AfD members and supporters write for the conservative paper <i>Junge Freiheit</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinthal_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weinthal-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> There was also a protest outside the venue of the party’s inaugural meeting by Andreas Storr, an NPD representative in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Saxony" title="Landtag of Saxony">Landtag of Saxony</a>, as the NPD sees the AfD as a rival for eurosceptic votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>An investigation conducted by the internet social analytic company Linkfluence showed little to no similarities in Facebook likes of AfD followers and those of the NPD supporter base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine30.2F8_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heine30.2F8-110">[110]</a></sup> AfD members interests tended towards euroscepticism and direct democracy, while NPD supporters showed interests in anti-Islamification, right-wing rock bands and the German military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine30.2F8_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heine30.2F8-110">[110]</a></sup> An evaluation between the hyperlinks included on AFD local party websites also showed few similarities, with the company's German chief-executive stating "The AfD supporter base and the right-wing extremist scene are digitally very far removed from one another"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine30.2F8_110-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heine30.2F8-110">[110]</a></sup> The analysis did point to AfD members favouring links with <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_populism" title="Right-wing populism">right-wing populist</a> reactionary conservative content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine30.2F8_110-3"><a href="#cite_note-Heine30.2F8-110">[110]</a></sup> The AfD's desire to break consensus-based politics and oppose political correctness as undermining freedom of speech, does lend it kudos as a legitimate mouthpiece for right-wing populism among some of the party membership and on regional AfD websites, which contrasts with the intellectual character of the party hierarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine30.2F8_110-4"><a href="#cite_note-Heine30.2F8-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>Left-wing criticism of the party took a more hardened tone over the late summer 2013,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with an array of political activists from far-left anti-fascist anarchists to the mainstream Green Party accusing it of pandering to xenophobic and nationalistic sentiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heine-111">[111]</a></sup> This ultimately led to the AfD complaining over incidents of verbal abuse and violence to its campaigners in Berlin, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and the university city of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heine-111">[111]</a></sup> Incidents in Göttingen flared after a party conference on 1 August, with police intervening later in the month in an attempted garage arson attack (in which there was said to be a car filled with AfD campaign literature) and to break up a dispute between the AfD and members of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Youth_(Germany)" title="Green Youth (Germany)">Green Youth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine_111-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heine-111">[111]</a></sup> Party leader Bernd Lucke described the events as a "slap in the face for every person who supports democracy" with the party in Lower Saxony left questioning whether to abandon their campaign in the state as local pub and restaurant owners denied the party access to their venues fearing for their businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heine_111-3"><a href="#cite_note-Heine-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 August 2013, Lucke and 16 other party members were reported to have been attacked in <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a> by opponents who used pepper spray and pushed Lucke from the stage. Initial reports by party officials and the police suggested that they were left-wing extremists and that about eight out of 20–25 attackers had succeeded in getting on to the stage. It was reported that a campaign worker had been cut with a knife. Later the police indicated that the number of people was probably around 10, of whom only two were known to have gained access to the stage, that only one of the opponents was known to be a left wing activist, and that the minor cut sustained by a campaign worker was probably not caused by a knife and was incurred later when attempting to apprehend a fleeing attacker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the German Federal Election 2013 the anti-Islam party <i><a href="/wiki/German_Freedom_Party" title="German Freedom Party">Die Freiheit</a></i> unilaterally pledged to support Alternative for Germany in the 2014 elections and concentrate its efforts on local elections only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stadke_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stadke-114">[114]</a></sup> Bernd Lucke responded by saying the recommendation was unwelcome and sent a letter to party associations recommending a hiring freeze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leber_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leber-115">[115]</a></sup> Earlier in September, Lucke described the Freedom Party members as coming from two camps, one of extreme Islam critics and populists, the other, ordinary democrats who were joining the AfD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stadke_114-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stadke-114">[114]</a></sup> Co-operation with the Freedom Party remains controversial within the ranks of the AfD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leber_115-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leber-115">[115]</a></sup> with some German state associations conducting vetting interviews with former Freedom Party members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stadke_114-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stadke-114">[114]</a></sup> Referring to an initiative for an <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> specific sex education in elementary school, Petry had asked on her social media presence if <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobia</a> was such a common prejudice among third and fourth grade children, that it would be necessary to confront them with it. An article in the German LGBT magazine <i>Queer</i> interpreted her statement as a demand to protect ″normal" (allegedly referring to heterosexual) families in elementary school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup></p>
<p>AfD MEP <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_von_Storch" title="Beatrix von Storch">Beatrix von Storch</a> is a known opponent of same-sex marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> She has accused school gay youth networks of using "forced sexualization" on their students.</p>
<p>In November 2015, a leading Berlin theatre, the <a href="/wiki/Schaub%C3%BChne" title="Schaubühne">Schaubühne</a>, was brought into legal conflict with members of the AfD over a piece, Falk Richter's FEAR, that parodied them as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zombies" title="Zombies">zombies</a> and mass murderers. AfD vice-president <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_von_Storch" title="Beatrix von Storch">Beatrix von Storch</a> is depicted facing retribution for her grandfather's role as a minister in <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler</a>'s government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> AfD Spokesperson, Christian Lüth, responded by interrupting a performance and filming it. <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_von_Storch" title="Beatrix von Storch">Beatrix von Storch</a>, and Conservative spokesperson Hedwig von Beverfoerde, then requested and obtained a preliminary injunction against the theatre, prohibiting it from using images of them in the production. They charged that the images' use violated their human dignity protected under the Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> On 15 December 2015, the court ruled against the complainants in favour of the theatre's freedom of expression and lifted the injunctions against using the images. The judges commented that 'any audience member can recognize that this is just a play'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2016, Frauke Petry argued for the possibility of shooting refugees at the borders to Germany. She did this twice but denied it and claimed that the press lied about her statement. <i>Rhein-Zeitung</i> then offered the audio-recording of the interview in which she advocates firing on refugees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PEGIDA">PEGIDA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: PEGIDA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEGIDA" title="PEGIDA">PEGIDA</a> movement and demonstrations, members of AfD have expressed different views, with Lucke describing the movement as "a sign that these people do not feel their concerns are understood by politicians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huggler14_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huggler14-122">[122]</a></sup> In response to the CDU Interior Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Maiziere" title="Thomas de Maiziere">Thomas de Maiziere</a> alleging an "overlap" between PEGIDA rallies and the AfD, Alexander Gauland stated that the AfD are "natural allies of this movement".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> However, Hans-Olaf Henkel asked members of the party not to join the demonstrations, telling <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Tagesspiegel" title="Der Tagesspiegel">Der Tagesspiegel</a></i> that he believed it could not be ruled out that they had "xenophobic or even racist connotations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huggler14_122-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huggler14-122">[122]</a></sup> A straw poll by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> found that nine out of ten PEGIDA protesters would back the AfD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Junge_Alternative_youth_organisation"><i>Junge Alternative</i> youth organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_for_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Junge Alternative youth organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Young_Alternative_for_Germany" title="Young Alternative for Germany">Young Alternative for Germany</a></div>
<p>The Young Alternative for Germany (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Junge Alternative für Deutschland</i></span> or <i>JA</i>), was founded in 2013 as the youth organisation of the AfD, while remaining legally independent from its mother party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernd_Lucke_und_die_wilde_Jugend_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernd_Lucke_und_die_wilde_Jugend-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
