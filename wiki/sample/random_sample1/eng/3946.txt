<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">German war crimes</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The governments of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> ordered, organized and condoned a substantial number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> respectively. The most notable of these is <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a> in which millions of people were systematically murdered or died from abuse and mistreatment. Millions also died as a result of other German actions in those two conflicts. The true number of victims may never be known, since much of the evidence was <a href="/wiki/Sonderaktion_1005" title="Sonderaktion 1005">deliberately destroyed by the perpetrators</a> in an attempt to conceal the crimes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-World_War_I">Pre-World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_war_crimes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herero_and_Namaqua_Genocide" title="Herero and Namaqua Genocide">Herero and Namaqua Genocide</a></div>
<p>Considered to have been the first <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> of the 20th century, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herero_and_Namaqua_Genocide" title="Herero and Namaqua Genocide">Herero and Namaqua Genocide</a> was perpetrated by the German Empire between 1904 and 1907 in <a href="/wiki/German_South-West_Africa" title="German South-West Africa">German South-West Africa</a> (modern day <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>), during the <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">scramble for Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OFAA20060831_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-OFAA20060831-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On January 12, 1904, the <a href="/wiki/Herero_people" title="Herero people">Herero people</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Maharero" title="Samuel Maharero">Samuel Maharero</a>, rebelled against <a href="/wiki/German_colonial_empire" title="German colonial empire">German colonialism</a>. In August, General <a href="/wiki/Lothar_von_Trotha" title="Lothar von Trotha">Lothar von Trotha</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_German_Army" title="Imperial German Army">Imperial German Army</a> defeated the Herero in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterberg" title="Battle of Waterberg">Battle of Waterberg</a> and drove them into the desert of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omaheke" title="Omaheke">Omaheke</a>, where most of them died of thirst. In October, the <a href="/wiki/Nama_people" title="Nama people">Nama people</a> also rebelled against the Germans only to suffer a similar fate.</p>
<p>In total, from 24,000 up to 100,000 Herero and 10,000 Nama died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walter_Nuhn_1904_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walter_Nuhn_1904-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The genocide was characterized by widespread death by starvation and thirst because the Herero who fled the violence were prevented from returning from the <a href="/wiki/Namib_Desert" title="Namib Desert">Namib Desert</a>. Some sources also claim that the German colonial army systematically <a href="/wiki/Well_poisoning" title="Well poisoning">poisoned desert wells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroll_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-kroll-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_war_crimes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Documentation regarding German war crimes in World War I were subsequently seized and destroyed by Nazi Germany during World War II, after occupying France, along with monuments commemorating their victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_weapons_in_warfare">Chemical weapons in warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_war_crimes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical weapons in warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapons_in_World_War_I" title="Chemical weapons in World War I">Chemical weapons in World War I</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_gas" title="Poison gas">Poison gas</a> was introduced by Imperial Germany, and was subsequently used by all major belligerents in the war against enemy soldiers, in violation of the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_of_1899_and_1907#Hague_Convention_of_1899" title="Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907">1899 Hague Declaration Concerning Asphyxiating Gases</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague_Convention_of_1907" title="Hague Convention of 1907">1907 Hague Convention on Land Warfare</a>, which explicitly forbade the use of "poison or poisoned weapons" in warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>In August 1914, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Schlieffen_Plan" title="Schlieffen Plan">Schlieffen Plan</a>, the German Army invaded and occupied the neutral nation of Belgium without explicit warning, which violated a <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_London_(1839)" title="Treaty of London (1839)">treaty of 1839</a> that the German chancellor dismissed as a "scrap of paper" and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague_Convention_of_1907" title="Hague Convention of 1907">1907 Hague Convention on Opening of Hostilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Within the first two months of the war, the German occupiers terrorized the Belgians, killing thousands of civilians and looting and burning scores of towns, including <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a>, which housed the country's preeminent university, mainly in fear of Belgian resistance fighters, or <i><a href="/wiki/Francs-tireurs" title="Francs-tireurs">francs-tireurs</a></i>. This action was in violation of the 1907 Hague Convention on Land Warfare provisions that prohibited <a href="/wiki/Collective_punishment" title="Collective punishment">collective punishment</a> on civilians and looting and destruction of civilian property in <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">occupied territories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bombardment_of_English_coastal_towns">Bombardment of English coastal towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_war_crimes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bombardment of English coastal towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Scarborough,_Hartlepool_and_Whitby" title="Raid on Scarborough, Hartlepool and Whitby">Raid on Scarborough, Hartlepool and Whitby</a></div>
<p>The raid on Scarborough, Hartlepool and Whitby, which took place on December 16, 1914, was an attack by the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_German_Navy" title="Imperial German Navy">Imperial German Navy</a> on the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">British</a> seaport towns of <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_North_Yorkshire" title="Scarborough, North Yorkshire">Scarborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hartlepool" title="Hartlepool">Hartlepool</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Hartlepool" title="West Hartlepool">West Hartlepool</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Whitby" title="Whitby">Whitby</a>. The attack resulted in 137 fatalities and 592 casualties. The raid was in violation of the ninth section of the 1907 Hague Convention which prohibited naval bombardments of undefended towns without warning,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> because only Hartlepool was protected by <a href="/wiki/Coastal_artillery" title="Coastal artillery">shore batteries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Germany was a signatory of the 1907 Hague Convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Another attack followed on 26 April 1916 on the coastal towns of <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Yarmouth_and_Lowestoft" title="Bombardment of Yarmouth and Lowestoft">Yarmouth and Lowestoft</a> but both were important naval bases and defended by shore batteries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unrestricted_submarine_warfare">Unrestricted submarine warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_war_crimes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unrestricted submarine warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/U-boat_Campaign_(World_War_I)" title="U-boat Campaign (World War I)">U-boat Campaign (World War I)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unrestricted_submarine_warfare" title="Unrestricted submarine warfare">Unrestricted submarine warfare</a> was instituted in 1915 in response to the <a href="/wiki/Blockade_of_Germany" title="Blockade of Germany">British blockade of Germany</a> in the North Sea. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prize_rules" title="Prize rules">Prize rules</a>, which were codified under the 1907 Hague Convention—such as those that required <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commerce_raiders" title="Commerce raiders">commerce raiders</a> to warn their targets and allow time for the crew to board lifeboats—were disregarded and <a href="/wiki/Merchant_vessel" title="Merchant vessel">commercial vessels</a> were sunk regardless of nationality, cargo, or destination. Following the sinking of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Lusitania" title="RMS Lusitania">RMS <i>Lusitania</i></a> on 7 May 1915 and subsequent public outcry in various neutral countries, including the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the practice was withdrawn. However, Germany resumed the practice on 1 February 1917 and declared that all merchant ships regardless of nationalities would be sunk without warning. This outraged the U.S. public, prompting the U.S. to break diplomatic relations with Germany two days later, and, along with the <a href="/wiki/Zimmermann_Telegram" title="Zimmermann Telegram">Zimmermann Telegram</a>, led the <a href="/wiki/American_entry_into_World_War_I" title="American entry into World War I">U.S. entry into the war</a> two months later on the side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_1" title="Allies of World War 1">Allied Powers</a>.</p>
