<p>A <b>dictator</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Roman_magistrate" title="Roman magistrate">magistrate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>, entrusted with the full authority of the state to deal with a military emergency or to undertake a specific duty. All other magistrates were subordinate to his <i><a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">imperium</a></i>, and the right of the <a href="/wiki/Tribune_of_the_Plebs" title="Tribune of the Plebs">plebeian tribunes</a> to veto his actions or of the people to appeal from them was extremely limited. However, in order to prevent the dictatorship from threatening the state itself, severe limitations were placed upon its powers: a dictator could only act within his intended sphere of authority; and he was obliged to resign his office once his appointed task had been accomplished, or at the expiration of six months. Dictators were regularly appointed from the earliest period of the Republic down to the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>, but the magistracy then went into abeyance for over a century, until it was revived in a significantly modified form, first by <a href="/wiki/Sulla" title="Sulla">Sulla</a>, and then by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Caesar</a>. The office was formally abolished after the death of Caesar, and not revived under the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the abolition of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Kingdom" title="Roman Kingdom">Roman monarchy</a> in 509 BC, the <i>imperium</i>, or executive power, of the king was divided between two annually-elected magistrates, known as <i>praetors</i>. In time they would come to be known as <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">consuls</a></i>, although probably not until the creation of a third, junior <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">praetor</a> in 367 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Neither consul was superior to the other, and the decisions of one could be appealed to the other (<i>provocatio</i>). Their insignia were the <i><a href="/wiki/Toga#varieties" title="Toga">toga praetexta</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Curule_seat" title="Curule seat">sella curulis</a></i>, and each was attended by an escort of twelve <a href="/wiki/Lictor" title="Lictor">lictors</a>, each of whom bore the <i><a href="/wiki/Fasces" title="Fasces">fasces</a></i>, a bundle of rods topped by an axe; but by custom the lictors had to remove the axes from their fasces within the <i><a href="/wiki/Pomerium" title="Pomerium">pomerium</a></i>, the sacred boundary of Rome, to signify that the <i>people</i>, and not the consuls, were sovereign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After several years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[i]</a></sup> the fear of impending war with both the <a href="/wiki/Sabines" title="Sabines">Sabines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Latin_League" title="Latin League">Latin League</a>, combined with widespread suspicion that one or both of the consuls favoured the restoration of the monarchy, led to the call for a <i>praetor maximus</i>, or <i>dictator</i> ("one who gives orders"), akin to the supreme magistrate of other Latin towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_ii_18_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_ii_18-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup> According to most authorities, the first dictator was <a href="/wiki/Titus_Lartius" title="Titus Lartius">Titus Lartius</a>, who appointed <a href="/wiki/Spurius_Cassius_Viscellinus" title="Spurius Cassius Viscellinus">Spurius Cassius</a> his <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_equitum" title="Magister equitum">magister equitum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_ii_18_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_ii_18-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[ii]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although there are indications that the term <i>praetor maximus</i> may have been used in the earliest period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[iii]</a></sup> the official title of the dictator throughout the history of the Republic was <i>magister populi</i>, or "master of the infantry". His lieutenant, the <i>magister equitum</i>, was the "master of the horse" (that is, of the cavalry<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[iv]</a></sup>). However, the use of <i>dictator</i> to refer to the <i>magister populi</i> seems to have been widespread from a very early period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomination">Nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The appointment of a dictator involved three steps: first, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a> would issue a decree known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Senatus_consultum" title="Senatus consultum">senatus consultum</a></i>, authorizing one of the consuls to nominate a dictator. Technically, a <i>senatus consultum</i> was advisory, and did not have the force of law, but in practice it was nearly always followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[v]</a></sup> Either consul could nominate a dictator. If both consuls were available, the dictator was chosen by agreement; if they could not agree, the consuls would draw lots for the responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[13]</a></sup> Finally, the <i><a href="/wiki/Curiate_Assembly" title="Curiate Assembly">Comitia Curiata</a></i> would be called upon to confer <i>imperium</i> on the dictator through the passage of a law known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Lex_curiata_de_imperio" title="Lex curiata de imperio">lex curiata de imperio</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dictator could be nominated for different reasons, or <i>causa</i>. The three most common were <i>rei gerundae causa</i>, "for the matter to be done", used in the case of dictators appointed to hold a military command against a specific enemy; <i>comitiorum habendorum causa</i>, for holding the <i>comitia</i>, or elections, when the consuls were unable to do so; and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clavum_fingere" title="Clavum fingere">clavi figendi causa</a></i>, an important religious rite involving the driving of a nail into the wall of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Jupiter_Optimus_Maximus" title="Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus">Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus</a>, as a protection against pestilence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[vi]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup> Other reasons included <i>seditionis sedandae causa</i> ("to quell sedition"); <i>ferarium constituendarum causa</i> (to establish a religious holiday in response to a dreadful portent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[vii]</a></sup>); <i>ludorum faciendorum causa</i> (to hold the <i><a href="/wiki/Ludi_Romani" title="Ludi Romani">Ludi Romani</a></i>, or "Roman Games", an ancient religious festival); <i>quaestionibus exercendis</i>, (to investigate certain actions);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[16]</a></sup> and in one extraordinary case, <i>senatus legendi causa</i>, to fill up the ranks of the Senate after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cannae" title="Battle of Cannae">Battle of Cannae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_xxiii._23._24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_xxiii._23.-24">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[18]</a></sup> These reasons could be combined (<i>seditionis sedandae et rei gerundae causa</i>), but are not always recorded or clearly stated in ancient authorities, and must instead be inferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the earlier period it was customary to nominate someone whom the consul considered the best available military commander; often this was a former consul, but this was never required. However, from 360 BC onward, the dictators were usually <i>consulares</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[viii]</a></sup> Normally there was only one dictator at a time, although a new dictator could be appointed following the resignation of another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[ix]</a></sup> A dictator could be compelled to resign his office without accomplishing his task or serving out his term if there were found to be a fault in the <a href="/wiki/Augury" title="Augury">auspices</a> under which he had been nominated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insignia">Insignia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Insignia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other curule magistrates, the dictator was entitled to the <i>toga praetexta</i> and the <i>sella curulis</i>. He received a ceremonial bodyguard that was unique in Roman tradition: "[t]wenty-four lictors indicated his quasi-regal power, which, however, was rather a concentration of the consular authority than a limited revival of the kingship."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[x]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a notable exception to the Roman reluctance to reconstitute the symbols of the kings, the lictors of the dictator never removed the axes from their fasces, even within the pomerium. Symbolizing their power over life and death, the axes of a dictator's lictors set him apart from all other magistrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup> In an extraordinary sign of deference, the lictors of other magistrates could not bear fasces at all when appearing before the dictator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_111_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_111-33">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the kings had been accustomed to appear on horseback, this right was forbidden to the dictator, unless he first received permission from the <i>comitia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_and_limitations">Powers and limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Powers and limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N26FabiusCunctator.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1625" data-file-width="808" height="342" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/N26FabiusCunctator.jpg/170px-N26FabiusCunctator.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/N26FabiusCunctator.jpg/255px-N26FabiusCunctator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/N26FabiusCunctator.jpg/340px-N26FabiusCunctator.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N26FabiusCunctator.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Roman Dictator <a href="/wiki/Fabius_Maximus" title="Fabius Maximus">Quintus Fabius Maximus Verrucosus</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addition to holding a military command and carrying out the actions decreed by the Senate, a dictator could summon the Senate or convene one of the legislative assemblies of the Roman people. The full extent of the dictatorial power was considerable, but not unlimited. It was circumscribed by the conditions of a dictator's appointment, as well as by the evolving traditions of <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>, and to a considerable degree depended on the dictator's ability to work together with other magistrates. The precise limitations of this power were not sharply defined, but subject to debate, contention, and speculation throughout Roman history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_112_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_112-37">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the pursuit of his <i>causa</i>, the dictator's authority was nearly absolute. However, as a rule he could not exceed the mandate for which he was appointed; a dictator nominated to hold the <i>comitia</i> could not then take up a military command against the wishes of the Senate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[xi]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[xii]</a></sup> Some dictators appointed to a military command also performed other duties, such as holding the <i>comitia</i>, or driving a nail into the wall of the Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus; but presumably they did so with the Senate's consent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>imperium</i> of the other magistrates was not vacated by the nomination of a dictator. They continued to perform the duties of their office, although subject to the dictator's authority, and continued in office until the expiration of their year, by which time the dictator had typically resigned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_111_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_111-33">[24]</a></sup> It is uncertain whether a dictator's <i>imperium</i> could extend beyond that of the consul by whom he was nominated; <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Mommsen" title="Theodor Mommsen">Mommsen</a> believed that his <i>imperium</i> would cease together with that of the nominating magistrate, but others have suggested that it could continue beyond the end of the civil year; and in fact there are several examples in which a dictator appears to have entered a new year without any consuls at all, although some scholars doubt the authenticity of these <i>dictator years</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broughton_DY_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broughton_DY-45">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially a dictator's power was not subject to either <i>provocatio</i>, the right to appeal from the decision of a magistrate, or <i>intercessio</i>, the veto of the tribunes of the plebs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_111_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_111-33">[24]</a></sup> However, the <i><a href="/wiki/Valerio-Horatian_Laws" title="Valerio-Horatian Laws">lex Valeria</a></i>, establishing the right of appeal, was not abrogated by the appointment of a dictator, and by 300 BC even the dictator was subject to <i>provocatio</i>, at least within the city of Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_111_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_111-33">[24]</a></sup> There is also evidence that the power of the plebeian tribunes was not vitiated by the dictator's commands, and 210 BC, the tribunes threatened to prevent elections held by the dictator, <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Fulvius_Flaccus_(consul_237_BC)" title="Quintus Fulvius Flaccus (consul 237 BC)">Quintus Fulvius Flaccus</a>, unless he agreed to withdraw his name from the list of candidates for the consulship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_xxvii_6_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_xxvii_6-49">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_111_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_111-33">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[xiii]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dictator was expected to resign his office upon the successful completion of the task for which he was appointed, or at the expiration of six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup> These sharp limitations were intended to prevent the dictatorship from too closely resembling the absolute power of the Roman kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup> But the six month limitation may have been dispensed with when the Senate deemed it expedient; no consuls are known for the years 333, 324, 309, and 301, and it is reported that the dictator and magister equitum continued in office without any consuls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broughton_DY_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Broughton_DY-45">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most authorities hold that a dictator could not be held to account for his actions after resigning his office, the prosecution of <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Furius_Camillus" title="Marcus Furius Camillus">Marcus Furius Camillus</a> for misappropriating the spoils of <a href="/wiki/Veii" title="Veii">Veii</a> being exceptional, as perhaps was that of Lucius Manlius Capitolinus in 362,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[xiv]</a></sup> which was dropped only because his son, <a href="/wiki/Titus_Manlius_Torquatus_(consul_347_BC)" title="Titus Manlius Torquatus (consul 347 BC)">Titus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[xv]</a></sup> threatened the life of the tribune who had undertaken the prosecution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup> However, some scholars suggest that the dictator was only immune from prosecution during his term of office, and could theoretically be called to answer charges of corruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_111_33-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_111-33">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magister_equitum">Magister equitum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Magister equitum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magister_equitum" title="Magister equitum">magister equitum</a></div>
<p>The dictator's lieutenant was the magister equitum, or "master of the horse". He would be nominated by the dictator immediately upon his own appointment, and unless the <i>senatus consultum</i> specified the name of the person to be appointed, the dictator was free to choose whomever he wished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.27s_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.27s-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup> It was customary for the dictator to nominate a magister equitum even if he were appointed for a non-military reason. Before the time of Caesar, the only dictator who refused to nominate a magister equitum was Marcus Fabius Buteo in 216 BC, and he strenuously objected to his own nomination, because there was already a dictator in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livy_xxiii._23._24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Livy_xxiii._23.-24">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like the dictator, the magister equitum was a curule magistrate, entitled to the <i>toga praetexta</i> and the <i>sella curulis</i>. His <i>imperium</i> was equivalent to that of a praetor (in the later use of the term), in that he was accompanied by six lictors, half the number accorded to the consuls. But like the dictator, he could summon the Senate, and probably also the popular assemblies. His authority was not subject to recall, although if the dictator were compelled to resign due to a fault in the auspices, the magister equitum was also expected to resign, and when the dictator laid down his <i>imperium</i>, so would the magister equitum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_112_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_112-37">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In theory, the magister equitum was commander of the cavalry, but he was not limited to that role. The dictator and magister equitum did not always take the field together; in some instances the magister equitum was assigned the defense of the city while the dictator took an army into the field, while on other occasions the dictator remained at Rome to see to some important duty, and entrusted the magister equitum with an army in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup> The magister equitum was necessarily subordinate to the dictator, although this did not always prevent the two from disagreeing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_112_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_112-37">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[xvi]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_and_disappearance">Decline and disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Decline and disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the first two centuries of the Republic, the dictatorship served as an expedient means by which a powerful magistracy could be created quickly in order to deal with extraordinary situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup> Created for military emergencies, the office could also be used to suppress sedition and prevent the growing number of plebeians from obtaining greater political power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_110_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_110-15">[11]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_the_Orders" title="Conflict of the Orders">Conflict of the Orders</a>, the dictator could generally be counted upon to support the patrician aristocracy, since he was always a patrician, and was nominated by consuls who were exclusively patrician. After the <i>lex Licinia Sextia</i> gave plebeians the right to hold one of the annual consulships, a series of dictators were appointed in order to hold elections, with the apparent goal of electing two patrician consuls, in violation of the Licinian law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broughton_125_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broughton_125-57">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[xvii]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Samnite_Wars#Second_.28or_Great.29_Samnite_War_.28326_to_304_BC.29" title="Samnite Wars">Second Samnite War</a>, the dictatorship was relegated almost exclusively to domestic activities. No dictator was nominated during the <a href="/wiki/Samnite_Wars#Third_Samnite_War_.28298_to_290_BC.29" title="Samnite Wars">Third Samnite War</a>, and the six-month limitation on its powers made the dictatorship impractical for campaigns beyond the Italian peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD2_Dictator_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-OCD2_Dictator-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lintott_112_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lintott_112-37">[27]</a></sup> In 249 BC, <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Atilius_Calatinus" title="Aulus Atilius Calatinus">Aulus Atilius Calatinus</a> became the only dictator to lead an army outside Italy, when he invaded <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, and he was the only dictator to hold a military command during the <a href="/wiki/First_Punic_War" title="First Punic War">First Punic War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[42]</a></sup> The last dictators to lead an army in the field were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quintus_Fabius_Maximus_Verrucosus" title="Quintus Fabius Maximus Verrucosus">Quintus Fabius Maximus Verrucosus</a> in 217, and Marcus Junius Pera the following year, during the early stages of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[43]</a></sup> All of the other dictators appointed during that conflict remained at Rome in order to hold the <i>comitia</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[xviii]</a></sup> the last dictator named in the traditional manner was Gaius Servilius Geminus, in 202 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[xix]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_dictatorship_revived">The dictatorship revived</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The dictatorship revived">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2508" data-file-width="1645" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg/170px-Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg/255px-Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg/340px-Sulla_Glyptothek_Munich_309.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>For the next century, Rome's ordinary magistrates and promagistrates successfully carried on all Roman campaigns, without the need for a dictator, and the office fell into abeyance. Then, in 82 BC, the dictatorship was suddenly revived by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucius_Cornelius_Sulla" title="Lucius Cornelius Sulla">Sulla</a>. Sulla, already a successful general, had previously marched on Rome and taken the city from his political opponents six years earlier; but after he permitted the election of magistrates for 87, and departed to campaign in the east, his enemies returned. In 83 he turned his attention to regaining Rome, and after defeating his opponents decisively the next year, the Senate and the people named him <i>dictator legibus faciendis et rei publicae constituendae</i>, giving Sulla the power to rewrite the Roman constitution, without any time limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD_Sulla_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-OCD_Sulla-68">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[xx]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sulla doubled the size of the Senate from 300 to 600, filling its ranks with his supporters. He then placed severe limits on the <a href="/wiki/Tribune_of_the_Plebs" title="Tribune of the Plebs">tribunician power</a>, limiting the veto and forbidding ex-tribunes from holding higher magistracies. Although he resigned the dictatorship in 81, and held the consulship in 80, before returning to private life, Sulla's actions had weakened the Roman state and set a precedent for the concentration of power without effective limitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCD_Sulla_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-OCD_Sulla-68">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dictatorial power was then granted to <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Caesar</a> in 49 BC, when he returned to Rome from his campaigns in Gaul, and put the forces of <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompeius</a> to flight. He resigned the dictatorship after only eleven days, having held the <i>comitia</i> at which he himself was elected consul for the following year. Late in 48, Caesar was named dictator <i>rei gerundae causa</i> with a term of one year, and granted the tribunician power for an indefinite period. He saw to the impeachment of two tribunes who had tried to obstruct him, and having been granted censorial powers, he filled the depleted numbers of the Senate with his supporters, raising the number of senators to 900. In 47, he was named dictator for a term of ten years. Shortly before his assassination in BC 44, Caesar was named <i>dictator perpetuo rei publicae constituendae</i>, and given the power to appoint magistrates at will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abolition">Abolition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_dictator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Abolition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Caesar's murder came at the hands of conspirators who presented themselves as saviours of the Republic. In order to maintain popular support, Caesar's followers took great care to show their own commitment to preserving the Roman state. The month after the assassination, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Antony" title="Mark Antony">Mark Antony</a>, who had been Caesar's magister equitum in BC 47, proposed a series of laws, confirming Caesar's actions, but allowing appeals and formally abolishing the dictatorship. These were passed, as the <i><a href="/wiki/Lex_Antonia" title="Lex Antonia">leges Antoniae</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 23 BC, when Caesar's nephew and heir <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> had attained full control of the state, the Senate offered to appoint him dictator, but he declined, while at the same time accepting proconsular <i>imperium</i> and the tribunician power for life. Thus, Augustus preserved the appearance of respecting Republican forms, even as he arrogated most of the powers of the Roman state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[54]</a></sup> Following his example, none of the emperors who succeeded him ever adopted the title of <i>dictator</i>. When <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine</a> chose to revive the ancient concept of the infantry commander, he pointedly gave the office the name of <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_militum#Establishment_and_development_of_the_office" title="Magister militum">magister peditum</a></i>, "master of the foot", rather than <i>magister populi</i>, the official style of a dictator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[55]</a></sup></p>
