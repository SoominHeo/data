<p><b>Donald</b> "<b>Don</b>" <b>McLean III</b> (born October 2, 1945) is an American singer-songwriter best known for the 1971 album <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(album)" title="American Pie (album)">American Pie</a></i>, containing the songs "<a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(song)" title="American Pie (song)">American Pie</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Vincent_(song)" title="Vincent (song)">Vincent</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_roots">Musical roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Musical roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McLean's grandfather and father were also named Donald McLean. The Buccis, the family of McLean's mother, Elizabeth, came from <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a> in central Italy. They left Italy and settled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Chester" title="Port Chester">Port Chester</a>, New York, at the end of the 19th century. He has other extended family in Los Angeles and Boston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though some of his early musical influences included <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> as a teenager, McLean became interested in <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, particularly the <a href="/wiki/The_Weavers" title="The Weavers">Weavers</a>' 1955 recording <i><a href="/wiki/The_Weavers_at_Carnegie_Hall" title="The Weavers at Carnegie Hall">At Carnegie Hall</a></i>. Childhood asthma meant that McLean missed long periods of school, particularly music lessons, and although he slipped back in his studies, his love of music was allowed to flourish. By age 16 he had bought his first guitar and began making contacts in the music business, becoming friends with folk singers <a href="/wiki/Erik_Darling" title="Erik Darling">Erik Darling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hellerman" title="Fred Hellerman">Fred Hellerman</a>, both members of the Weavers. Hellerman said, "He called me one day and said, 'I'd like to come and visit you', and that's what he did! We became good friends - he has the most remarkable music memory of anyone I've ever known."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>When McLean was 15, his father died. Fulfilling his father's request, McLean graduated from <a href="/wiki/Iona_Preparatory_School" title="Iona Preparatory School">Iona Preparatory School</a> in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> and briefly attended <a href="/wiki/Villanova_University" title="Villanova University">Villanova University</a>, dropping out after four months. After leaving Villanova, McLean became associated with famed folk music agent <a href="/wiki/Harold_Leventhal" title="Harold Leventhal">Harold Leventhal</a> for several months before teaming up with personal manager Herb Gart for 18 years. For the next six years he performed at venues and events including <a href="/wiki/The_Bitter_End" title="The Bitter End">The Bitter End</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Gaslight_Cafe" title="The Gaslight Cafe">Gaslight Cafe</a> in New York, the <a href="/wiki/Newport_Folk_Festival" title="Newport Folk Festival">Newport Folk Festival</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Cellar_Door" title="The Cellar Door">the Cellar Door</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Troubadour_(Los_Angeles)" title="The Troubadour (Los Angeles)">Troubadour</a> in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> He attended night school at <a href="/wiki/Iona_College_(New_York)" title="Iona College (New York)">Iona College</a> and received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a> in 1968.</p>
<p>He turned down a scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> Graduate School in favor of pursuing a career as a singer/songwriter, performing at such venues as <a href="/wiki/Caff%C3%A8_Lena" title="Caffè Lena">Caffè Lena</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saratoga_Springs,_New_York" title="Saratoga Springs, New York">Saratoga Springs, New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Main_Point" title="The Main Point">The Main Point</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Mawr,_Pennsylvania" title="Bryn Mawr, Pennsylvania">Bryn Mawr, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Later that year, with the help of a grant from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Council_on_the_Arts" title="New York State Council on the Arts">New York State Council on the Arts</a>, McLean began reaching a wider audience, with visits to towns up and down the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> He learned the art of performing from his friend and mentor <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>. McLean accompanied Seeger on his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sloop_Clearwater" title="Sloop Clearwater">Clearwater</a> boat trip up the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> in 1969 to raise awareness about environmental pollution in the river. During this time McLean wrote songs that would appear on his first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Tapestry_(Don_McLean_album)" title="Tapestry (Don McLean album)">Tapestry</a></i>. McLean co-edited the book <i>Songs and Sketches of the First Clearwater Crew</i> with sketches by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_B._Allen" title="Thomas B. Allen">Thomas B. Allen</a> for which Pete Seeger wrote the foreword. Seeger and McLean sang "<a href="/wiki/Oh_Shenandoah" title="Oh Shenandoah">Shenandoah</a>" on the 1974 Clearwater album.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_breakthrough">Early breakthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early breakthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McLean recorded <i>Tapestry</i> in 1969 in <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>, during the student riots. After being rejected 72 times by labels, the album was released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediarts" title="Mediarts">Mediarts</a>, a label that had not existed when Don first started to look for a label. It attracted good reviews but little notice outside the folk community, though on the Easy Listening chart "<a href="/wiki/Castles_in_the_Air_(song)" title="Castles in the Air (song)">Castles in the Air</a>" was a success, and in 1973 "And I Love You So" became a number 1 Adult Contemporary hit for <a href="/wiki/Perry_Como" title="Perry Como">Perry Como</a>.</p>
<p>McLean's major break came when Mediarts was taken over by <a href="/wiki/United_Artists_Records" title="United Artists Records">United Artists Records</a>, thus securing the promotion of a major label for his second album, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(album)" title="American Pie (album)">American Pie</a>.</i> The album spawned two No. 1 hits in the title song and "<a href="/wiki/Vincent_(song)" title="Vincent (song)">Vincent</a>". <i>American Pie</i>'s success made McLean an international star and piqued interest in his first album, which charted more than two years after its initial release.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22American_Pie.22">"American Pie"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: "American Pie"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(song)" title="American Pie (song)">American Pie (song)</a></div>
<p>McLean's <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a>, "<a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(song)" title="American Pie (song)">American Pie</a>", is a sprawling, impressionistic ballad inspired partly by the deaths of <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Valens" title="Ritchie Valens">Ritchie Valens</a> and J.P. Richardson (<a href="/wiki/The_Big_Bopper" title="The Big Bopper">The Big Bopper</a>) in a plane crash on February 3, 1959. The song popularized the expression "<a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Music_Died" title="The Day the Music Died">The Day the Music Died</a>" in reference to this event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song was recorded on May 26, 1971, and a month later received its first radio airplay on New York's WNEW-FM and WPLJ-FM to mark the closing of <a href="/wiki/Fillmore_East" title="Fillmore East">Fillmore East</a>, the famous New York concert hall. "American Pie" reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> from January 15-February 5, 1972, and remains McLean's most successful single release. The single also topped the <i>Billboard</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Adult_Contemporary_Tracks" title="Hot Adult Contemporary Tracks">Easy Listening</a> survey. With a total running time of 8:36 encompassing both sides of the single, it is also the longest song to reach No. 1. Some stations played only part one of the original split-sided single release.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/WCFL_(AM)" title="WCFL (AM)">WCFL</a> DJ Bob Dearborn unraveled the lyrics and first published his interpretation on January 7, 1972, eight days before the song reached No. 1 nationally (see "Further reading" under <a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(song)" title="American Pie (song)">American Pie</a>). Numerous other interpretations, which together largely converged on Dearborn's interpretation, quickly followed. McLean declined to say anything definitive about the lyrics until 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Since then McLean has stated that the lyrics are also somewhat autobiographical and present an abstract story of his life from the mid-1950s until the time he wrote the song in the late 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001 "American Pie" was voted No. 5 in a poll of the 365 <a href="/wiki/Songs_of_the_Century" title="Songs of the Century">Songs of the Century</a> compiled by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Endowment_for_the_Arts" title="National Endowment for the Arts">National Endowment for the Arts</a>. The top five: "<a href="/wiki/Over_the_Rainbow" title="Over the Rainbow">Over the Rainbow</a>", written by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Arlen" title="Harold Arlen">Harold Arlen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.Y._%22Yip%22_Harburg" title='E.Y. "Yip" Harburg'>E.Y. "Yip" Harburg</a> (performed by <a href="/wiki/Judy_Garland" title="Judy Garland">Judy Garland</a> in the 1939 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i>); "<a href="/wiki/White_Christmas_(song)" title="White Christmas (song)">White Christmas</a>", written by <a href="/wiki/Irving_Berlin" title="Irving Berlin">Irving Berlin</a> (best-known performance by <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a>); "<a href="/wiki/This_Land_Is_Your_Land" title="This Land Is Your Land">This Land Is Your Land</a>", written and performed by <a href="/wiki/Woody_Guthrie" title="Woody Guthrie">Woody Guthrie</a>; "<a href="/wiki/Respect_(song)" title="Respect (song)">Respect</a>", written by <a href="/wiki/Otis_Redding" title="Otis Redding">Otis Redding</a> (best-known performance by <a href="/wiki/Aretha_Franklin" title="Aretha Franklin">Aretha Franklin</a>); and "American Pie".</p>
<p>On April 7, 2015, McLean’s original working manuscript for “American Pie” sold for $1,205,000 (£809,524/€1,109,182) at Christie’s auction rooms, New York, making it the third highest auction price achieved for an American literary manuscript.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_recordings">Subsequent recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subsequent recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Don_McLean_1976.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="866" data-file-width="663" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Don_McLean_1976.JPG/220px-Don_McLean_1976.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Don_McLean_1976.JPG/330px-Don_McLean_1976.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Don_McLean_1976.JPG/440px-Don_McLean_1976.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Don_McLean_1976.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Don McLean publicity photo, 1976</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Personnel from the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(album)" title="American Pie (album)">American Pie</a></i> album sessions were retained for his third album <i><a href="/wiki/Don_McLean_(album)" title="Don McLean (album)">Don McLean</a></i>, including producer, Ed Freeman, <a href="/wiki/Rob_Stoner" title="Rob Stoner">Rob Rothstein</a> on bass and <a href="/wiki/Warren_Bernhardt" title="Warren Bernhardt">Warren Bernhardt</a> on piano. The song "The Pride Parade" provides an insight into McLean's immediate reaction to stardom. McLean told <i><a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a></i> magazine in 1973 that <i>Tapestry</i> was an album by someone previously concerned with external situations. <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(album)" title="American Pie (album)">American Pie</a></i> combines externals with internals and the resultant success of that album makes the third one (<i><a href="/wiki/Don_McLean_(album)" title="Don McLean (album)">Don McLean</a></i>) entirely introspective."</p>
<p>Other songs written by McLean for the album included "Dreidel" (number 21 on the Billboard chart) and "If We Try" (number 58), which was subsequently recorded by <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Newton-John" title="Olivia Newton-John">Olivia Newton-John</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> "On the Amazon" from the 1920s musical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mr_Cinders" title="Mr Cinders">Mr Cinders</a> was an unusual choice but became an audience favorite in concerts and featured in <i>Till Tomorrow</i>, a documentary film about McLean produced by Bob Elfstrom (Elfstrom held the role of Jesus Christ in Johnny and June Cash's <i>Gospel Road)</i>. The film shows McLean in concert at Columbia University as he was interrupted by a bomb scare. He left the stage while the audience stood up and checked under their seats for anything that resembled a bomb. After the all-clear, McLean re-appeared and sang "On the Amazon" from exactly where he had left off. Don Heckman reported the bomb scare in his review for <i>The New York Times</i> entitled "Don McLean Survives Two Obstacles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Playin%27_Favorites" title="Playin' Favorites">Playin' Favorites</a></i> was a top-40 hit in the UK in 1973 and included the Irish folk classic, "Mountains of Mourne" and <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Everyday_(Buddy_Holly_song)" title="Everyday (Buddy Holly song)">Everyday</a>", a live rendition of which returned McLean to the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>. McLean said, "The last album (<i><a href="/wiki/Don_McLean_(album)" title="Don McLean (album)">Don McLean</a></i>) was a study in depression whereas the new one (<i><a href="/wiki/Playin%27_Favorites" title="Playin' Favorites">Playin' Favorites</a></i>) is almost the quintessence of optimism, with a feeling of 'Wow, I just woke up from a bad dream.'"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The 1974 album <i><a href="/wiki/Homeless_Brother" title="Homeless Brother">Homeless Brother</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Joel_Dorn" title="Joel Dorn">Joel Dorn</a>, was McLean's final studio collaboration with United Artists. The album featured fine New York session musicians, including Ralph McDonald on percussion, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_McCracken" title="Hugh McCracken">Hugh McCracken</a> on guitar and a guest appearance by <a href="/wiki/Yusef_Lateef" title="Yusef Lateef">Yusef Lateef</a> on flute. <a href="/wiki/The_Persuasions" title="The Persuasions">The Persuasions</a> sang the background vocals on "Crying in the Chapel" and <a href="/wiki/Cissy_Houston" title="Cissy Houston">Cissy Houston</a> provided a backing vocal on "La La Love You". The album's title song was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>'s book, <i><a href="/wiki/Lonesome_Traveler" title="Lonesome Traveler">Lonesome Traveler</a></i> in which Kerouac tells the story of America's "homeless brothers", or hobos. The song features background vocals by <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The song "The Legend of Andrew McCrew" was based on an article published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> concerning a black Dallas hobo named Anderson McCrew who was killed when he leapt from a moving train. No one claimed him, so a carnival took his body, mummified it, and toured all over the South with him, calling him the "The Famous Mummy Man." McLean's song inspired radio station WGN in Chicago to tell the story and give the song airplay in order to raise money for a headstone for Anderson McCrew's grave. Their campaign was successful and McCrew's body was exhumed and buried in the Lincoln Cemetery in Dallas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The tombstone had an inscription with words from the fourth verse of McLean's song:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>What a way to live a life, and what a way to die<br/></p>
<p>Left to live a living death with no one left to cry<br/>
A petrified amazement, a wonder beyond worth<br/></p>
A man who found more life in death than life gave him at birth</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joel_Dorn" title="Joel Dorn">Joel Dorn</a> later collaborated on the McLean career retrospective <i><a href="/wiki/Rearview_Mirror:_An_American_Musical_Journey" title="Rearview Mirror: An American Musical Journey">Rearview Mirror</a></i>, released in 2005 on Dorn's own label, Hyena Records. In 2006, Dorn reflected on working with McLean:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Of the more than 200 studio albums I've produced in the past forty plus years, there is a handful; maybe fifteen or so that I can actually listen to from top to bottom. <i><a href="/wiki/Homeless_Brother" title="Homeless Brother">Homeless Brother</a></i> is one of them. It accomplished everything I set out to do. And it did so because it was a true collaboration. Don brought so much to the project that all I really had to do was capture what he did, and complement it properly when necessary.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>1977 saw a brief liaison with <a href="/wiki/Arista_Records" title="Arista Records">Arista Records</a> that yielded the <i><a href="/wiki/Prime_Time_(Don_McLean_album)" title="Prime Time (Don McLean album)">Prime Time</a></i> album and, in October 1978, the single "It Doesn't Matter Anymore". This was a track from the <i><a href="/wiki/Chain_Lightning_(album)" title="Chain Lightning (album)">Chain Lightning</a></i> album that should have been the second of four with Arista.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> McLean had started recording in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>'s backing singers, <a href="/wiki/The_Jordanaires" title="The Jordanaires">The Jordanaires</a>, and many of Elvis's musicians. However the Arista deal broke down following artistic disagreements between McLean and the Arista chief, <a href="/wiki/Clive_Davis" title="Clive Davis">Clive Davis</a>. Consequently, McLean was left without a record contract in the USA, but through continuing deals the <i><a href="/wiki/Chain_Lightning_(album)" title="Chain Lightning (album)">Chain Lightning</a></i> album was released by EMI in Europe and by Festival Records in Australia.</p>
<p>In April 1980, the track "<a href="/wiki/Crying_(Roy_Orbison_song)" title="Crying (Roy Orbison song)">Crying</a>" from the album began picking up airplay on Dutch radio stations and McLean was called to Europe to appear on several important musical variety shows to plug the song and support its release as a single by EMI. The song achieved number 1 status in the Netherlands first, followed by the UK and then Australia.</p>
<p>McLean's number 1 successes in Europe and Australia led to a new deal in the USA with Millennium Records. They issued the <i><a href="/wiki/Chain_Lightning_(album)" title="Chain Lightning (album)">Chain Lightning</a></i> album two and a half years after it had been recorded in Nashville, and two years after its release in Europe. It charted on February 14, 1981 and reached number 28 while "<a href="/wiki/Crying_(Roy_Orbison_song)" title="Crying (Roy Orbison song)">Crying</a>" climbed to number 5 on the pop singles chart. Roy Orbison himself thought that McLean’s version was the best cover he’d ever heard of one of his songs. Orbison thought McLean did a better job than he did and even went so far as to say that the voice of Don McLean is one of the great instruments of 20th Century America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skydoc_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-skydoc-10">[10]</a></sup> According to Brian Wilson of the Beach Boys, “McLean's voice could cut through steel - he is a very pure singer and he's up there with the best of them. He's a very talented singer and songwriter and he deserves his success.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skydoc_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-skydoc-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The early 1980s saw further chart successes in the U.S. with "<a href="/wiki/Since_I_Don%27t_Have_You" title="Since I Don't Have You">Since I Don't Have You</a>", a new recording of "Castles in the Air" and "It's Just the Sun". In 1987, the release of the country-based <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Tracks_(Don_McLean_album)" title="Love Tracks (Don McLean album)">Love Tracks</a></i> album gave rise to the hit singles "Love in My Heart" (a top-10 in Australia), "<a href="/wiki/You_Can%27t_Blame_the_Train" title="You Can't Blame the Train">You Can't Blame the Train</a>" (U.S. country No. 49), and "Eventually". The latter two songs were written by <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> native <a href="/wiki/Terri_Sharp" title="Terri Sharp">Terri Sharp</a>. In 1991, <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> reissued the "<a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(song)" title="American Pie (song)">American Pie</a>" single in the United Kingdom and McLean performed on <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i>. In 1992, previously unreleased songs became available on <i><a href="/wiki/Favorites_and_Rarities" title="Favorites and Rarities">Favorites and Rarities</a></i> while <i>Don McLean Classics</i> featured new studio recordings of "<a href="/wiki/Vincent_(song)" title="Vincent (song)">Vincent</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(song)" title="American Pie (song)">American Pie</a>".</p>
<p>McLean has continued to record new material including <i>River of Love</i> in 1995 on <a href="/wiki/Curb_Records" title="Curb Records">Curb Records</a> and, more recently, the albums <i>You've Got to Share</i>, <i>Don McLean Sings Marty Robbins</i> and <i>The Western Album</i> on his own Don McLean Music label. <i>Addicted to Black</i> was released in May 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_songs">Other songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McLean's other well-known songs include:</p>
<ul>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/And_I_Love_You_So_(song)" title="And I Love You So (song)">And I Love You So</a>" was covered by <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helen_Reddy" title="Helen Reddy">Helen Reddy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Bassey" title="Shirley Bassey">Shirley Bassey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glen_Campbell" title="Glen Campbell">Glen Campbell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engelbert_Humperdinck_(singer)" title="Engelbert Humperdinck (singer)">Engelbert Humperdinck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Keel" title="Howard Keel">Howard Keel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Fran%C3%A7ois" title="Claude François">Claude François</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/1973_in_music" title="1973 in music">1973 hit</a> for <a href="/wiki/Perry_Como" title="Perry Como">Perry Como</a>.</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Vincent_(song)" title="Vincent (song)">Vincent</a>", a tribute to the 19th-century Dutch painter, <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>. Although it only reached No. 12 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100, it proved to be a huge hit worldwide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> "You can't change a note in that song" (<a href="/wiki/Mike_Mills" title="Mike Mills">Mike Mills</a>, REM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skydoc_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-skydoc-10">[10]</a></sup> It was a No. 1 hit single in the UK Singles Chart. This song was covered by <a href="/wiki/NOFX" title="NOFX">NOFX</a> on their album titled <i>45 or 46 Songs That Weren't Good Enough to Go on Our Other Records</i>, and also appears on the <a href="/wiki/Fat_Wreck_Chords" title="Fat Wreck Chords">Fat Wreck Chords</a> compilation <i>Survival of the Fattest</i>. "Vincent" was also covered by <a href="/wiki/Josh_Groban" title="Josh Groban">Josh Groban</a> on his <a href="/wiki/Josh_Groban_(album)" title="Josh Groban (album)">2001 debut album</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Castles_in_the_Air_(song)" title="Castles in the Air (song)">Castles in the Air</a>", which McLean recorded twice. His 1981 re-recording was a top-40 hit, reaching #36 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 in late 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitburn_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitburn-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Wonderful_Baby" title="Wonderful Baby">Wonderful Baby</a>", a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Fred_Astaire" title="Fred Astaire">Fred Astaire</a> that Astaire himself recorded. Primarily rejected by pop stations, it reached #1 on the <i>Billboard</i> Easy Listening survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Superman's Ghost", a tribute to <a href="/wiki/George_Reeves" title="George Reeves">George Reeves</a>, who portrayed <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a> on television in the 1950s.</li>
<li>"The Grave", a song that McLean had written about the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, was covered by <a href="/wiki/George_Michael" title="George Michael">George Michael</a> in 2003 in protest against the Iraq War.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <i>American Pie</i> album features a version of <a href="/wiki/Psalm_137" title="Psalm 137">Psalm 137</a>, entitled "Babylon". The song is based on a canon by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Hayes_(composer)" title="Philip Hayes (composer)">Philip Hayes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and was arranged by McLean and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Hays" title="Lee Hays">Lee Hays</a> (of The Weavers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> "Babylon" was performed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Men" title="Mad Men">Mad Men</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylon_(Mad_Men_episode)" title="Babylon (Mad Men episode)">episode of the same name</a> despite the fact that the song would not be released until 10 years after the time in which the episode is set.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1981, McLean had an international number one hit with a cover of the <a href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison" title="Roy Orbison">Roy Orbison</a> classic, "<a href="/wiki/Crying_(Roy_Orbison_song)" title="Crying (Roy Orbison song)">Crying</a>". It was only after the record became a success overseas that it was released in the U.S.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The single hit No. 5 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitburn_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitburn-13">[13]</a></sup> Orbison himself once described McLean as "the voice of the century",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and a subsequent re-recording of the song saw Orbison incorporate elements of McLean's version.</p>
<p>For the 1982 animated cult-movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Flight_of_Dragons" title="The Flight of Dragons">The Flight of Dragons</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Bass" title="Jules Bass">Jules Bass</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Rankin,_Jr." title="Arthur Rankin, Jr.">Arthur Rankin, Jr.</a>, McLean sang the opening theme. However, no soundtrack has ever been released. Another hit song associated with McLean (though never recorded by him) is "<a href="/wiki/Killing_Me_Softly_with_His_Song" title="Killing Me Softly with His Song">Killing Me Softly with His Song</a>", which was claimed by <a href="/wiki/Lori_Lieberman" title="Lori Lieberman">Lori Lieberman</a> to have been written <i>about</i> McLean after she, also a singer/songwriter, saw him singing his composition "Empty Chairs" in concert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daily_News_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_News-18">[18]</a></sup> Afterwards (according to Lieberman) she wrote a poem about the experience and shared it with Norman Gimbel, who had long been searching for a way to use a phrase he had copied from a novel badly translated from Spanish to English, "killing me softly with his blues".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Allegedly, Gimbel and Charles Fox reworked the poem and the phrase into the song "Killing Me Softly with His Song",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_Magazine_1974._Page_53_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard_Magazine_1974._Page_53-20">[20]</a></sup> originally recorded by Lieberman and covered by <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Flack" title="Roberta Flack">Roberta Flack</a> (and later covered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fugees" title="The Fugees">The Fugees</a>). This claim was disputed, notably by Fox. Subsequently, however, the matter reached an unequivocal conclusion when contemporaneous articles from the early 1970s were exhumed, all of them vindicating Lieberman.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In an April 5, 1973 article in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i>, Norman Gimbel was quoted as follows: "She [Lori Lieberman] told us about this strong experience she had listening to McLean ("I felt all flushed with fever / Embarrassed by the crowd / I felt he had found my letters / And read each one out loud / I prayed that he would finish / But he just kept right on…"). I had a notion this might make a good song so the three of us discussed it. We talked it over several times, just as we did for the rest of the numbers we wrote for this album and we all felt it had possibilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daily_News_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_News-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>McLean's albums did not match the commercial success of <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(album)" title="American Pie (album)">American Pie</a></i> but he became a major concert attraction in the U.S. and overseas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His repertoire included old concert hall numbers and the catalogues of singers such as <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a>, and another McLean influence, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>. The years spent playing gigs in small clubs and coffee houses in the 1960s transformed into well-paced performances. McLean's first concerts at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hall" title="Carnegie Hall">Carnegie Hall</a> in New York and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Hall" title="Albert Hall">Albert Hall</a> in London in 1972 were critically acclaimed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In recent years McLean has continued to tour North America, Europe (2011, 2012) and Australia (2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In June 2011 McLean appeared at the <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Festival" title="Glastonbury Festival">Glastonbury Festival</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Pilton,_Somerset" title="Pilton, Somerset">Pilton</a>, UK, and in 2014 at California's <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach_Festival" title="Stagecoach Festival">Stagecoach Country Music Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2015, McLean undertook his 20th nationwide tour of the UK and Ireland.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_work_and_honors">Later work and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Later work and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1991, Don McLean returned to the UK top 20 with a re-issue of "American Pie". <a href="/wiki/Iona_College_(New_York)" title="Iona College (New York)">Iona College</a> conferred an honorary doctorate on McLean in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2002, "American Pie" was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Hall_of_Fame" title="Grammy Hall of Fame">Grammy Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2004, McLean was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Songwriters_Hall_of_Fame" title="Songwriters Hall of Fame">Songwriters Hall of Fame</a>. Garth Brooks presented the award and said "Don McLean: his work, like the man himself, is very deep and very compassionate. His pop anthem 'American Pie' is a cultural phenomenon".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Two years later, Brooks repaid the favor by appearing as a special guest (with <a href="/wiki/Nanci_Griffith" title="Nanci Griffith">Nanci Griffith</a>) on McLean's first American TV special, broadcast as the <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> special <i>Starry Starry Night</i>. A month later, McLean wound up the 20th century by performing "American Pie" at the Lincoln Memorial Gala in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2007, the biography <i>The Don McLean Story: Killing Us Softly With His Songs</i> was published. Biographer Alan Howard conducted extensive interviews for this, the only book-length biography of the often reclusive McLean to date.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In February 2012 McLean won the BBC Radio 2 Folk Awards Life Time Achievement award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2012, the PBS network broadcast a feature-length documentary about the life and music of Don McLean called "Don McLean: American Troubadour" produced by 4-time Emmy Award winning filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Jim_Brown_(director)" title="Jim Brown (director)">Jim Brown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLean is one of UK singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Jake_Bugg" title="Jake Bugg">Jake Bugg</a>'s primary influences. Bugg said McLean's song "<a href="/wiki/Vincent_(song)" title="Vincent (song)">Vincent</a>" was "the first song I liked" after hearing it on an episode of The Simpsons. He devoured McLean's back catalogue and then delved into the artists that inspired McLean - including Buddy Holly and The Weavers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> also cited McLean's "<a href="/wiki/Vincent_(song)" title="Vincent (song)">Vincent</a>" as a personal inspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLean is credited as writer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drake_(rapper)" title="Drake (rapper)">Drake</a>'s song "Doing it Wrong" featuring Stevie Wonder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The song includes lyrics from two McLean compositions - "The Wrong Thing To Do" and "When a Good Thing Goes Bad" both of which featured on his 1977 album "<a href="/wiki/Prime_Time_(Don_McLean_album)" title="Prime Time (Don McLean album)">Prime Time</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_McLean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until early 2016, McLean lived in <a href="/wiki/Camden,_Maine" title="Camden, Maine">Camden, Maine</a>, with his wife, Patrisha McLean, and their two children, Jackie and Wyatt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was raised in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> faith of his mother; his father was a Protestant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On January 18, 2016, McLean was arrested in Camden for a <a href="/wiki/Misdemeanor" title="Misdemeanor">misdemeanor</a> <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a> charge. McLean posted bail and appeared in court in February 2016, pleading not guilty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2016, after 30 years of marriage, Patrisha filed for divorce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 21, 2016, McLean plead guilty to domestic violence assault, domestic violence criminal threatening, criminal mischief and criminal restraint against Patrisha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
