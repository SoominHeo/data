<p><i><b>Don't Be Afraid of the Dark</b></i> is a 2010 American-Australian-Mexican <a href="/wiki/Dark_fantasy" title="Dark fantasy">dark fantasy</a> <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> written by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Robbins_(screenwriter)" title="Matthew Robbins (screenwriter)">Matthew Robbins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_del_Toro" title="Guillermo del Toro">Guillermo del Toro</a>, directed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_book_artist" title="Comic book artist">comic book artist</a> Troy Nixey and filmed at the Drusilla Mansion in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Macedon,_Victoria" title="Mount Macedon, Victoria">Mount Macedon, Victoria</a> and Melbourne, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The film stars <a href="/wiki/Katie_Holmes" title="Katie Holmes">Katie Holmes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Pearce" title="Guy Pearce">Guy Pearce</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bailee_Madison" title="Bailee Madison">Bailee Madison</a>, as a family moving into a 19th-century Rhode Island mansion, where the withdrawn daughter (Madison) begins to witness malevolent creatures that emerge from a sealed ash pit in the basement of the house. It is a remake of the 1973 <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> <a href="/wiki/Television_film" title="Television film">made-for-television</a> <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(1973_film)" title="Don't Be Afraid of the Dark (1973 film)">horror film of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-V1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At Blackwood Manor in <a href="/wiki/Providence_County,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence County, Rhode Island">Providence County, Rhode Island</a>, renowned wildlife painter Lord Blackwood summons his housekeeper into the basement where he reluctantly kills her with a hammer and chisel. He removes her teeth, as well as his own, and offers them to mysterious creatures down an ash pit within an old fireplace; all of a sudden, the creatures demand the teeth of children. Blackwood begs for them to give back his kidnapped son, only to be dragged down the ash pit by the creatures.</p>
<p>In the present day, 8-year old Sally Hurst arrives in Rhode Island to live with her father Alex and his girlfriend Kim, both restoring Blackwood Manor to put it on the market for their client Mr. Jacoby. Sally is depressed due to her mother forcefully putting her in Alex's care and giving her copious amounts of <a href="/wiki/Adderall" title="Adderall">Adderall</a>. On the first night of her stay, the melodious tune from a carousel-styled nightlight awakens the creatures in the ash pit. The next day, Sally wanders the grounds and finds the hidden basement's skylight. One of the workmen restoring the house, Mr. Harris, warns her, Alex and Kim not to venture into the basement, although they do regardless. Sally takes interest in the sealed fireplace where she hears the creatures calling her name and follows the mysterious voices. "BE AFRAID" is written in <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes">runes</a> above it.</p>
<p>Sally opens the fireplace to meet the creatures and finds one of the old housekeeper's teeth. The creatures quickly prove to be hostile, stealing Alex's razor and shredding Kim's clothes. Alex immediately blames Sally and finds a 19th-century silver coin in her possession, which she found under her pillow after the tooth disappeared. Alex and Kim head into town on a business trip and Sally sneaks to the basement to talk with the creatures, but Harris sends her away and tries to seal the fireplace. The creatures emerge and brutally wound him with his own tools and he is hospitalized. Sally's increasingly frightening encounters with the creatures prompt Alex to call a therapist to talk to Sally, who draws a sketch of one of the creatures that attacked her under her bedsheets.</p>
<p>Kim visits Harris in the hospital, who tells her to find the unpublished artwork of Lord Blackwood in the local library. The librarian reveals the artwork, one of which is of a creature whom he describes as being like <a href="/wiki/Tooth_fairy" title="Tooth fairy">tooth fairies</a>, which every now and again turns a human into one of their own. Kim races home as Sally is attacked again by the creatures while having a bath, the lead creature being a transformed Lord Blackwood who proclaims the creatures will make Sally one of their own. Kim finds an undiscovered mural painted by Lord Blackwood in the basement, depicting his son being taken below ground by the creatures. Kim confronts Alex who is more interested in hosting a dinner for Mr. Jacoby and friends. However, he finally realizes what is happening when Sally is trapped in the library by the creatures, but she fends them off by using her camera flash to distract them.</p>
<p>Alex and Kim decide to flee the house with Sally, but both are ambushed by the creatures and knocked out, Sally tries to wake Kim up but also gets ambushed by the creatures and is knocked unconscious. When Sally wakes up, her feet have been tied up with rope, and the creatures are starting to drag her to the basement for her transformation. Kim awakens and goes to the basement to confront the creatures, cutting the rope around Sally's feet but only to get herself in the ropes and her leg broken by it as she struggles to get free. The creatures drag Kim into the fireplace, as a distraught Sally crushes the creature who used to be Lord Blackwood to death with a large flashlight. Alex arrives just as Kim disappears, and the father and daughter mourn their loss.</p>
<p>Some time later, both return to the abandoned mansion to leave a drawing of Kim there. After they leave a strong draft pulls the drawing into the creatures' lair; the entrance now bolted with metal. A transformed Kim is heard convincing the creatures to stay in hiding – for [people] will forget in time, and others will come – and they "have all the time in the world".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Del Toro chose Troy Nixey to direct the film after seeing Nixey's short film <i>Latchkey's Lament</i>. For the design of the creatures in the film, Nixey drew inspiration from pictures of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mole_rats" title="Mole rats">mole rats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Del Toro has attributed the idea of giving the creatures in the film a fairy origin to the work of the writer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Machen" title="Arthur Machen">Arthur Machen</a>, saying in an interview: "I love the Welsh author Arthur Machen and his idea that fairy lore comes from a dark place, that it’s derived from little, pre-human creatures who are really, really nasty vermin but are magical in a way, living as they do for hundreds of years. His books are what compelled me to do this." Machen's stories are specifically mentioned in the film by the librarian character. Del Toro is a long-standing admirer of Machen and said his work was also an influence on <i><a href="/wiki/Pan%27s_Labyrinth" title="Pan's Labyrinth">El Laberinto Del Fauno</a></i> (<i>Pan's Labyrinth</i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Hellboy_II:_The_Golden_Army" title="Hellboy II: The Golden Army">Hellboy II: The Golden Army</a></i> both of which also feature fairy creatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The name of "Emerson Blackwood", the character who built the mansion in the film, is a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Blackwood" title="Algernon Blackwood">Algernon Blackwood</a>, another writer of supernatural horror stories.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This picture, which was developed with <a href="/wiki/Miramax" title="Miramax">Miramax</a> in the wake of the division's closure and sale, was released by <a href="/wiki/FilmDistrict" title="FilmDistrict">FilmDistrict</a>, and was rated R despite filmmaker ambitions to the contrary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> "We originally thought we could shoot it as PG-13 without compromising the scares," Del Toro said. "And then the MPAA came back and gave us a badge of honor. They gave us an R for 'Violence and Terror.' We asked them if there was anything we could do, and they said, 'Why ruin a perfectly scary movie?'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Troy Nixey narrated the film at a screening at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">2010 San Diego Comic-Con International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDCC-Images_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-SDCC-Images-9">[9]</a></sup> The initial release date was scheduled for January 21, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to the sale of Miramax by Disney on December 3, 2010 (but Disney kept the pre-2010 Miramax Films library), the release was put on hold until the sale was finalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The film was released on August 26, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was released on DVD and Blu-ray on January 3, 2012 and February 20, 2012 in the UK.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don%27t_Be_Afraid_of_the_Dark_(2010_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Don't Be Afraid of the Dark</i> has received mixed to positive reviews from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reported that 58% of critics gave the film a positive review based on 163 reviews, with an average rating of 5.8/10. The site's critical consensus states that "While it's pleasantly atmospheric and initially quite scary, the film ultimately fails to deliver the skin-crawling chills of the original". Another review aggregator, <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">weighted average</a> score out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, calculated an average score of 56/100 based on 35 reviews, indicating "mixed or average reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> gave the film 3½ stars out of 4, calling it "a very good haunted house film" and adding that it "milks our frustration deliciously."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bailee_Madison" title="Bailee Madison">Bailee Madison</a>'s acting was generally well received by critics, who have also praised the idea of turning the protagonist into a little girl, as opposed to an adult in the original film.</p>
