<p><b>Don Cossacks</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Донские казаки</span>) are <a href="/wiki/Cossacks" title="Cossacks">Cossacks</a> who settled along the middle and lower <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Don</a>. Historically, they have been located within what was the <b>Don Cossack Host</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Всевеликое Войско Донское, <i>Vsevelikoye Voysko Donskoye</i></span>), which was either an independent or an autonomous democratic republic in the present day Southern Russia and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donbas" title="Donbas">Donbas</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, from the end of the 16th century until 1918. As of 1992, by the presidential decree of the Russian Federation, Cossacks can be enrolled on a special register. A number of Cossack communities have been reconstituted to further the Cossack cultural traditions, including those of the Don Cossack Host.</p>
<p>Don Cossacks have had a rich military tradition, playing an important part in the historical development of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> and participating in most of its major wars.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_origins">Etymology and origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <b>Cossack</b> (казак, козак) was widely used to describe "free people" as opposed to others with different standing in a feudal society (i.e., peasants, nobles, clergy, etc.). The word 'cossack' was also applied to migrants, free-booters and <a href="/wiki/Outlaw" title="Outlaw">bandits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The exact origins of Cossacks are unknown. In modern view, Don Cossacks are descendants of Slavic people, who came from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dnepr" title="Dnepr">Dnieper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novgorod_Republic" title="Novgorod Republic">Novgorod Republic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Ryazan" title="Principality of Ryazan">Principality of Ryazan</a>, and of <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Goths</a>-<a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> people originating from the Western part of <a href="/wiki/North_Caucasus" title="North Caucasus">North Caucasus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More than two thousand years ago the <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> lived on the banks of the river Don. Many Scythian tombs have been found in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Subsequently, the area was inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Khazars" title="Khazars">Khazars</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polovtsians" title="Polovtsians">Polovtsians</a>. The steppes of the Don River were called "The Wild Field" (Дикое Поле). The area was under the general control of the Golden Horde, and numerous Tatar armed groups roamed there, attacking Russian and foreign merchants.</p>
<p>The first Christians to settle on the territories around the Don were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jassic_people" title="Jassic people">Jassi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Kosogi</a> tribes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Horde" title="Golden Horde">Golden Horde</a> in 1480, more Russian<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> colonists started to expand onto this land from the <a href="/wiki/Novgorod_Republic" title="Novgorod Republic">Novgorod Republic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shelon" title="Battle of Shelon">Battle of Shelon</a> and from neighboring <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Ryazan" title="Principality of Ryazan">Principality of Ryazan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Until the end of the 16th century, the Don Cossacks inhabited independent free territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="15th.E2.80.9317th_centuries">15th–17th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 15th–17th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cossacks of <a href="/wiki/Ryazan" title="Ryazan">Ryazan</a> are mentioned in 1444 as a defenders of <a href="/wiki/Pereslavl-Zalessky" title="Pereslavl-Zalessky">Pereslavl-Zalessky</a> against the units of <a href="/wiki/Golden_Horde" title="Golden Horde">Golden Horde</a> and in a letter of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_III_of_Russia" title="Ivan III of Russia">Ivan III of Russia</a> since 1502. After the Golden Horde fell in 1480, the area around the <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Don River</a> was divided between the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Khanate" title="Crimean Khanate">Crimean west side</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nogai_Horde" title="Nogai Horde">Nogai east side</a>. On their border since the 14th century the vast steppe of the Don region was populated by those people who were not satisfied with the existing social order, by those who did not recognize the power of the land-owners, by runaway serfs, by those who longed for freedom. In the course of time they turned into a united community and were called "the Cossacks". At first the main occupation of these small armed detachments was hunting and fishing—as well as the constant struggle against the Turks and the Tatars who attacked them. Only later they began to settle and work on the land.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century">16th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 16th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first records relating to the Cossack villages: the "stanitsa", date back to 1549. In the year 1552 Don Cossacks under the command of Ataman <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Susar_Fedov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Susar Fedov (page does not exist)">Susar Fedov</a> joined the Army of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible" title="Ivan the Terrible">Ivan the Terrible</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kazan" title="Siege of Kazan">Siege of Kazan</a> in 1552. On 2 June 1556 the Cossack regiment of Ataman <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lyapun_Filimonov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lyapun Filimonov (page does not exist)">Lyapun Filimonov</a>, together with the Army of Moscovits comprising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strelets" title="Strelets">strelets</a>, conquered and annexed the <a href="/wiki/Astrakhan_Khanate" title="Astrakhan Khanate">Astrakhan Khanate</a>.</p>
<p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible" title="Ivan the Terrible">Ivan the Terrible</a> (Ivan IV), the ataman <a href="/wiki/Yermak_Timofeyevich" title="Yermak Timofeyevich">Yermak Timofeyevich</a> went on an expedition to conquer <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. After defeating Khan Kuchum in the fall of 1582 and occupying <a href="/wiki/Qashliq" title="Qashliq">Isker</a>: the capital of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_Khanate" title="Siberian Khanate">Siberian Khanate</a>, Yermak sent a force of cossacks down the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irtysh" title="Irtysh">Irtysh</a> in the winter of 1583. The detachment led by Bogdan Bryazga (according to other sources, the Cossack chieftain Nikita Pan); passed through the lands of the Konda-Pelym <a href="/wiki/Mansi_people" title="Mansi people">Voguls</a> and reached the walls of the town of Samarovo. Taken by surprise by the cossack attack, the <a href="/wiki/Khanty_people" title="Khanty people">Ostyaks</a> surrendered. In fall 1585, shortly after Yermak's death, Cossacks led by voevoda (army commander) Ivan Mansurov founded the first Russian fortified town in Siberia, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Obskoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Obskoy (page does not exist)">Obskoy</a>, at the mouth of the Irtysh river on the right bank of the <a href="/wiki/Ob_River" title="Ob River">Ob</a> river. The Mansi and Khanty lands thus became part of the Russian state, finally secured by the founding of the cities of <a href="/wiki/Pelym" title="Pelym">Pelym</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Berezov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Berezov (page does not exist)">Berezov</a> in 1592 and <a href="/wiki/Surgut" title="Surgut">Surgut</a> in 1594. As a result of Yermak's expedition, Russia was able to annex Siberia.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Muscovite_War_(1605%E2%80%9318)" title="Polish–Muscovite War (1605–18)">Polish–Muscovite War (1605–18)</a>, the Polish–Lithuanian noble <a href="/wiki/Aleksander_J%C3%B3zef_Lisowski" title="Aleksander Józef Lisowski">Aleksander Józef Lisowski</a> founded a cavalry mercenary group (named <a href="/wiki/Lisowczycy" title="Lisowczycy">Lisowczycy</a> after his death) from various outlaws, partly Don Cossacks. This group served under the Polish Crown; after the war with Muscovites Lisowczycy took part in the <a href="/wiki/Moldavian_Magnate_Wars" title="Moldavian Magnate Wars">Moldavian Magnate Wars</a> (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Humenn%C3%A9" title="Battle of Humenné">Battle of Humenné</a> in November 23, 1619) at <a href="/wiki/Upper_Hungary" title="Upper Hungary">Upper Hungary</a>, (now eastern <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>), later they plundered <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> as allies of <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">Habsburg armies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Revolt" title="Bohemian Revolt">Bohemian Revolt</a> – first phase of <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. This phase culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Mountain" title="Battle of White Mountain">Battle of White Mountain</a> in November 8, 1620 (near <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, the capital of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Bohemia" title="Crown of Bohemia">Crown of Bohemia</a>, now the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>), where the Lisowczycy were sent by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tserclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" title="Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly">Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly</a> against <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_cavalry" title="Hungarian cavalry">Hungarian cavalry</a>. They were victorious, capturing twenty standards. After the battle, they terrorised village people around Prague and other cities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> so they were expeditiously paid and released from service in May 7, 1621. Some returned to Poland, others served under Habsburg <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Elector_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian I, Elector of Bavaria">Maximilian I, Elector of Bavaria</a>.</p>

<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter the Great</a> and subsequent rulers, the Don Cossacks participated in numerous military campaigns, which resulted in the expansion of the Russian Empire from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>. For years, the Cossacks waged war against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Khanate" title="Crimean Khanate">Crimean Khanate</a>. The Siege of <a href="/wiki/Azov" title="Azov">Azov</a> in 1641 was one of the key actions in Don Cossack history. After total taking of the Free Territories of Don Cossacks under the Moscovits control, Don Cossack history is totally intertwined with the history of the rest of Russia. In exchange for protection of the Southern borders of medieval Russia, the Don Cossacks were given the privilege of not paying taxes and the tsar's authority in Cossack lands was not as absolute as in other parts of Russia.</p>
<p>During this period, three of Russia's most notorious rebels, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stenka_Razin" title="Stenka Razin">Stenka Razin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kondraty_Bulavin" title="Kondraty Bulavin">Kondraty Bulavin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emelian_Pugachev" title="Emelian Pugachev">Emelian Pugachev</a>, were Don Cossacks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th.E2.80.9319th_centuries">18th–19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 18th–19th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After 1786, the territory of the Don Cossacks was officially called Don Voisko Lands, and was renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Voisko_Province" title="Don Voisko Province">Don Voisko Province</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru"><i>Oblast’ Voyska Donskogo</i></span>) in 1870 (presently part of the <a href="/wiki/Rostov" title="Rostov">Rostov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">Volgograd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Voronezh" title="Voronezh">Voronezh</a> regions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a> as well as part of the <a href="/wiki/Luhansk_Oblast" title="Luhansk Oblast">Luhansk</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>).</p>
<p>In 1805 the Don Cossack capital was shifted from Cherkassk to Novocherkassk (New Cherkassk).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Don Cossacks are credited with playing a significant part in repelling <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia" title="French invasion of Russia">Invasion of Russia</a>. Under the command of Count <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matvey_Ivanovich_Platov" title="Matvey Ivanovich Platov">Matvey Ivanovich Platov</a>, the Don Cossacks fought in a number of battles against the <a href="/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="Grande Armée">Grande Armée</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Borodino" title="Battle of Borodino">Battle of Borodino</a>, Don Cossacks made raids to the rear of the French Army. Platov commanded all the Cossack troops and successfully covered the retreat of the Russian Army to Moscow. The Don Cossacks distinguished themselves in subsequent campaigns, and took part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Paris_(1814)" title="Battle of Paris (1814)">capture of Paris</a>. Napoleon is credited with declaring, "Cossacks are the finest light troops among all that exist. If I had them in my army, I would go through all the world with them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the general census of 1884, the male population of the Don Cossacks was reported to number 425,000. The Don Cossacks were the largest of the ten cossack hosts then in existence, providing over a third of total cossack manpower available for military service.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On the eve of World War I, the Don Cossack Host comprised 17 regular regiments plus 6 detached sotnias (squadrons). In addition two regiments of the Imperial Guard were recruited from the Don territory. By 1916 the Don Host had expanded to 58 line regiments and 100 detached sotnias. The central location of the Don territories meant that these units were employed extensively on both the German and Austro-Hungarian fronts, though less so against the Ottoman Turks to the south. The continued value of the Don and other Cossacks as mounted troops was illustrated by the decision taken in 1916 to dismount about a third of the regular Russian cavalry, but to retain the cossack regiments in their traditional role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="February_1917_Revolution">February 1917 Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: February 1917 Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the outbreak of the February 1917 Revolution, three regiments of Don Cossacks (the 1st, 4th and 14th) formed part of the garrison of St. Petersburg. Consisting partly of new recruits from the poorer regions of the Host territory, these units were influenced by the general disillusionment with the Tsar's government. Accordingly, they did not act effectively when ordered to disperse the growing demonstrations in the city. Reports that the historically loyal Don Cossacks could no longer be relied on were a significant factor in the sudden collapse of the Tsarist regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bolshevik_persecution">Bolshevik persecution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bolshevik persecution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Red_Terror" title="Red Terror">Red Terror</a></div>
<p>The Don Cossack Host was disbanded on Russian soil in 1918, after the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>, but the Don Cossacks in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Army" title="White Army">White Army</a> and those who emigrated abroad, continued to preserve the traditions, musical and otherwise, of their host. Many found employment as <a href="/wiki/Trick_riding" title="Trick riding">trick riders</a> in various <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circuses</a> throughout Europe and the United States. Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Kolchak" title="Aleksandr Kolchak">Aleksandr Vasiliyevich Kolchak</a>, one of the leaders of the <a href="/wiki/White_movement" title="White movement">White movement</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>, was of Don Cossack descent. Following the defeat of the White Army in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>, a policy of <a href="/wiki/Decossackization" title="Decossackization">decossackization</a> ("Raskazachivaniye") took place on the surviving Cossacks and their homelands, since they were viewed as a threat to the new Soviet regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Don_Cossacks_in_World_War_II">Don Cossacks in World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Don Cossacks in World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 20 April 1936 the previous ban on Cossacks serving in the Red Army was lifted. Later the same year two existing Red Army cavalry divisions were re-designated as Don Cossacks. By 1939 a number of these regiments had been issued with traditional Cossack uniforms in ceremonial and field service versions. The dress of the Don Cossack units included the broad red stripes on dark-blue breeches, which had been their distinguishing feature prior to the Revolution. The Don Cossack Cavalry Corps saw extensive active service until 1943, after which its role diminished (as did that of the other remaining horse-mounted units in the Red Army).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However Don Cossack cavalry was still in existence in 1945 and participated in the Victory Parade in Moscow.</p>
<p>During World War II, the Don Cossacks mustered the largest single concentration of Cossacks within the German Army, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XVth_SS_Cossack_Cavalry_Corps" title="XVth SS Cossack Cavalry Corps">XVth SS Cossack Cavalry Corps</a>. A great part of the Cossacks were former Russian citizens who elected to fight not so much for Germany as against the Soviet Union. The XVth SS Cossack Cavalry Corps included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_Cossack_Division" title="1st Cossack Division">1st Cossack Division</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=2nd_Cossack_Division&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="2nd Cossack Division (page does not exist)">2nd Cossack Division</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Don_Cossacks">Modern Don Cossacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern Don Cossacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Today the Russian state is very different. Between 1917 and 1941, more than half of the Don Cossacks population were killed or deported. The heirs of those deported during the Soviet Terror cannot return to their homeland. This is because the law of <a href="/wiki/Restitution" title="Restitution">Restitution</a> does not recognize them as victims of communism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The pro-governmental Host divided in two different organisations. The Don Cossacks were revived in the early 1990s and were officially recognised by the Russian Federation Government in 1997.</p>
<p>In 1992 they joined the separatists forces during the <a href="/wiki/Transnistria_War" title="Transnistria War">Transnistria War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>There still exist several "Organisations of Anti-communist Cossacks Outside Russia." The most known is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Almighty_Don_Host_Outside_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Almighty Don Host Outside Russia (page does not exist)">Almighty Don Host Outside Russia</a>, formerly under chief <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">Major general</a> and last chevalier of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_St._George" title="Order of St. George">Order of St. George</a>, Ataman <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolai_Vasilievich_Fedorov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nikolai Vasilievich Fedorov (page does not exist)">Nikolai Vasilievich Fedorov</a> (1901—2003),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and since February 2006 under J. L. Miheev.</p>
<p>Since 2014, an unknown number of Don Cossacks have participated in the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Donbass" title="War in Donbass">war in Eastern Ukraine</a> as independent volunteers. Reportedly several military formations were formed though most of these groups were subsequently disbanded and integrated into the armed forces of the <a href="/wiki/Donetsk_People%27s_Republic" title="Donetsk People's Republic">DPR</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lugansk_People%27s_Republic" title="Lugansk People's Republic">LPR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniform">Uniform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Uniform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until 1914 the distinguishing colour of the Don Cossack Host was red: worn on the cap bands and wide trouser stripes of a dark blue uniform of the loose-fitting cut common to the Steppe Cossacks. Tall lambskin hats were worn on occasion, with red cloth tops. Shoulder-straps were the same dark blue as the caftan (coat).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A whip was used instead of spurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Prior to 1908, individual cossacks from all Hosts were required to provide their own uniforms (together with horses and harness). However the size and relative affluence of the Don Cossack Host permitted the setting up of communally owned clothing factories.</p>
<p>A khaki field tunic was adopted in 1908, replacing the dark blue coats or white (summer) blouses previously worn for ordinary duties. However the blue riding breeches with broad red stripes long characteristic of the Don Host, continued to be worn even on active service during both World Wars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthem_of_Don_Cossacks">Anthem of Don Cossacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Anthem of Don Cossacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Всколыхнулся, взволновался православный Тихий Дон</i> written by Fedor Anisimov in 1853.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <span class="languageicon" style="font-size:0.95em; font-weight:bold; color:#555;">(Russian)</span></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most Don Cossacks are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox" title="Russian Orthodox">Russian Orthodox</a>, who consider themselves guardians of the faith. However, a large percentage of Don Cossacks were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starovers" title="Starovers">Starovers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Even in 1903, a minimum of 150,000 from a total of the 2,500,000 parish members of the Don <a href="/wiki/Eparchy" title="Eparchy">Eparchy</a> were Starovers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Ataman count <a href="/wiki/Matvei_Platov" title="Matvei Platov">Matvei Platov</a> was of a <a href="/wiki/Popovtsy" title="Popovtsy">Popovtsy</a> <a href="/wiki/Old_Believers" title="Old Believers">Old Believers</a> Family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Don Cossacks were tolerant of other religions -with the exception of Jews- and accepted Buddhists, Muslims, <a href="/wiki/Old_Believers" title="Old Believers">Old Believers</a>, and pagans into their communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-One_Europe_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-One_Europe-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions_and_culture">Traditions and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Traditions and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Cossacks had a democratic society where the most important decisions were made during a <b>Common Assembly</b> (Казачий Круг). The assembly elected temporary authorities — <a href="/wiki/Ataman" title="Ataman">atamans</a>.</p>
<p>Don Cossacks were skilled horsemen and experienced warriors, due to their long conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Khanate" title="Crimean Khanate">Crimean Khanate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. They sold their military services to different powers in Eastern Europe. Together with the Polish King, they raided Moscow during the <a href="/wiki/Time_of_Troubles" title="Time of Troubles">Time of Troubles</a> (Смутное Время) and, under Russian authority, carried out raids and expeditions against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkey" title="Ottoman Turkey">Ottoman Turkey</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qajar_Persia" title="Qajar Persia">Qajar Persia</a>.</p>
<p>Isolated between Russian and Muslim territory, the Don Cossacks developed a distinct culture and language which fused Ukrainian, Russian, Kalmyk, and Tatar elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Don Cossacks have a tradition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choral_singing" title="Choral singing">choral singing</a> and many of their songs, such as <i>Chyorny Voron</i> (Black Raven) and <i>Lyubo, Bratsi, Lyubo</i> (It's good, brothers, good) became popular throughout the rest of Russia. Many of the songs are about death in war.</p>
<p>Up to the 18th century marriages and divorces were held in the Common Assembly (Казачий Круг). If a Cossack wanted to marry a woman he was expected to bring her to the Common Assembly for presentation. If the Common Assembly gave approval, the marriage followed. The same procedure took place if there was a divorce. <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter I</a> banned these practices in the Common Assembly, requiring Cossacks to marry only in church.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A Cossack marriage is a complex ritual, accompanied by songs, dances and performances. A bridegroom arrives on horseback and takes his bride to the church, followed by a marriage train. After the wedding all present would adjourn to the bridegroom's house. There, The parents would bless the couple, break a loaf of bread above their heads, and sprinkle them with wheat, nuts, sweets and hops. The bride's hair would then be unbraided according to traditional rites.</p>
<p>When a son was born to a Cossack family, his relatives presented him with an arrow, a bow, a cartridge, a bullet and a gun. All of these items were hung on the wall, over the boy's bed. At the age of three, the boy began to ride a horse. At the age of 7 to 8 he was allowed to ride in the street, to go fishing and hunt with grown-ups.</p>
<p>Horse racing was a popular pastime with Don Cossacks. Riders competed to hit a target, firing weapons from horseback. The most dexterous were able to do this standing on the horses back. It was traditional practice for Cossack families to provide a young Cossack with two horses, a uniform and weapons.</p>
<p>Cossack leave-taking was always festive. All departing Cossacks would gather in the church, then hang a small bag around their necks containing a pinch of their native soil before setting off singing. Having left their <a href="/wiki/Stanitsa" title="Stanitsa">stanitsa</a>, they drank a cup of vodka and said good-bye to their native land.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Don_Cossack_Choir_Serge_Jaroff">Don Cossack Choir Serge Jaroff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Cossacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Don Cossack Choir Serge Jaroff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Cossack_Choir_Serge_Jaroff" title="Don Cossack Choir Serge Jaroff">Don Cossack Choir Serge Jaroff</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Cossack_Choir_Serge_Jaroff" title="Don Cossack Choir Serge Jaroff">Don Cossack Choir Serge Jaroff</a> was a group of former officers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Imperial_Army" title="Russian Imperial Army">Russian Imperial Army</a> who were discovered singing in Çilingir (near Constantinopel), where they had fled after the defeat of their army in the <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>. They made their formal concert debut in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1923, led by their founder, conductor and composer, <a href="/wiki/Serge_Jaroff" title="Serge Jaroff">Serge Jaroff</a>.</p>
<p>The choir became popular in America, Japan and Europe, touring the world in the 1930s, 40s and 50s, till today. The men, dressed as Cossacks, sang <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> in a repertory of Russian sacred and secular music, army, folk and art songs. Cossack dancing was eventually added to their programme.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Sholokhov" title="Mikhail Sholokhov">Mikhail Sholokhov</a>'s monumental work, <i>"<a href="/wiki/And_Quiet_Flows_the_Don" title="And Quiet Flows the Don">And Quiet Flows the Don</a>"</i>, deals sympathetically with the Don Cossacks and depicts the destruction of their way of life as a result of World War I and the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>.</p>
