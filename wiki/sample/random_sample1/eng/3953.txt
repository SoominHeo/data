<p><i><b>Pittosporum tobira</b></i> is a species of flowering plant in the <a href="/wiki/Pittosporaceae" title="Pittosporaceae">Pittosporum family</a> known by several common names, including <b>Australian laurel</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>Japanese pittosporum</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>mock orange</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-1">[1]</a></sup> and <b>Japanese cheesewood</b>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">native</a> to Japan, China, and Korea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RHSAZ_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RHSAZ-2">[2]</a></sup> but it is used throughout the world as an <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a> in landscaping and as <a href="/wiki/Floriculture" title="Floriculture">cut foliage</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1275" data-file-width="1920" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg/220px-Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg/330px-Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg/440px-Pittosporum_tobira_Fruits.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>It is an evergreen <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> which can reach 10 m (33 ft) tall by 3 m (10 ft) broad,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RHSAZ_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RHSAZ-2">[2]</a></sup> and can become treelike. It can also be trimmed into a <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedge</a>. The leaves are oval in shape with edges that curl under and measure up to 10 cm (4 in) in length. They are leathery, hairless, and darker and shinier on the upper surfaces. The <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a> is a cluster of fragrant flowers occurring at the ends of branches. The flower has five white petals each about a centimetre long. The fruit is a hairy, woody capsule about 1 cm wide divided into three valves. Inside are black seeds in a bed of resinous pulp.</p>
<p>The binomial qualifier <i>tobira</i> derives from the Japanese name for the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RHSLG_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-RHSLG-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>This shrub is a common, drought-tolerant and fairly hardy landscaping plant. Many cultivars have been developed, including dwarf forms and the popular 'Variegata', which has <a href="/wiki/Variegation" title="Variegation">variegated</a> leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamps_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-4">[4]</a></sup> It is used for hedges, living privacy screens, and indoor and outdoor <a href="/wiki/Houseplant" title="Houseplant">planter boxes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamps_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-4">[4]</a></sup> The stems, leaves, and dried fruits are used in <a href="/wiki/Floristry" title="Floristry">flower arrangements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamps_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the cultivar 'Variegatum'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> have both gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">Royal Horticultural Society</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a>.</p>
<p>Common pests of this plant include various <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leafhopper" title="Leafhopper">leafhoppers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Icerya_purchasi" title="Icerya purchasi">cotton cushiony scale</a> (<i>Icerya purchasi</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Root-knot_nematode" title="Root-knot nematode">root-knot nematodes</a> (<i>Meloidogyne</i> spp.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamps_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-4">[4]</a></sup> It can be attacked by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Planchonia_arabidis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Planchonia arabidis (page does not exist)">pit-making pittosporum scale</a> (<i>Planchonia arabidis</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is vulnerable to the fungal plant pathogen <i><a href="/wiki/Phanerochaete_salmonicolor" title="Phanerochaete salmonicolor">Erythricium salmonicolor</a></i>, which causes <a href="/wiki/Gall" title="Gall">galls</a> and the dieback disease known as pink limb blight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamps_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
