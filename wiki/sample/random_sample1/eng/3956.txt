<p><b>Don Quijote</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ドン・キホーテ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Discount_store" title="Discount store">discount chain store</a> that has over 160 locations throughout <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> as well as three stores in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donki_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-donki-2">[2]</a></sup> It carries a wide range of products, from basic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groceries" title="Groceries">groceries</a> to <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> to <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">clothing</a>. The store is well known in Japan and is often referred to by its shortened name <b>Donki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ドンキ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. Distinctly, Don Quijote tends to keep very late hours for Japanese retailing (to 3 or 5am, or even 24 hours) and it packs its goods from ceiling to floor in a distinct merchandising strategy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Quijote_(store)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg/170px-Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg/255px-Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg/340px-Don_Quijote_Roppongi_Store_20060517.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>Don Quijote opened its first store in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suginami,_Tokyo" title="Suginami, Tokyo">Suginami, Tokyo</a> in September 1980 under its original name, Just Co.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Originally a retail store, Just Co. quickly switched to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesale" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> in 1982.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The company opened its first "Don Quijote" named store in <a href="/wiki/Fuch%C5%AB,_Tokyo" title="Fuchū, Tokyo">Fuchu</a>, Tokyo in March 1989. With the name change, the store also changed its primary business from wholesale to retail. It was not until 1995, six years later, that Just Co. followed suit and it changed its corporate to Don Quijote Co., Ltd as well. In June 1998, the company was listed on the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Stock_Exchange" title="Tokyo Stock Exchange">Tokyo Stock Exchange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donki_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-donki-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As one of the few discount stores in Japan, the end of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_asset_price_bubble" title="Japanese asset price bubble">Japanese economic bubble</a> did not have disastrous effects for Don Quijote. Instead, the sudden economic uncertainty caused the Japanese public to become more thrifty and therefore helped to boost sales at its stores during the early 1990s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2005, idol group <a href="/wiki/AKB48" title="AKB48">AKB48</a> opened its theater on the eighth floor of the Don Quijote Akihabara Outlet in Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-japantimes20100824_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-japantimes20100824-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Don Quijote purchased <a href="/wiki/Marukai_Corporation_U.S.A." title="Marukai Corporation U.S.A.">Marukai Corporation U.S.A.</a> including stores in Hawaii and California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theme_song">Theme song</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Quijote_(store)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theme song">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Don Quijote is known for the distinctive song that plays in its stores. The song is called "Miracle Shopping" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ミラクルショッピング</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> sung by Maimi Tanaka <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">田中マイミ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, a Don Quijote store employee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> "Miracle Shopping" was released as a <a href="/wiki/Maxi_single" title="Maxi single">maxi single</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arson">Arson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Quijote_(store)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2004, four stores in the <a href="/wiki/Kant%C5%8D_region" title="Kantō region">Kantō</a> area were damaged or destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">arson</a> attacks. Three store employees, Morio Oshima, 39, Mai Koishi, 20, and Maiko Sekiguchi, 19, died in the first arson incident. In 2007, Noriko Watanabe, 49, was found guilty of setting the fires and sentenced to <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life imprisonment</a>. Don Quijote received harsh criticism at the time for poor store layout that made it difficult to find exits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roller_coaster">Roller coaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Quijote_(store)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roller coaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Don_Quijote_%28store%29&amp;params=35_39_44.73_N_139_44_5.11_E_region:JP_type:landmark_scale:1000"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°39′44.73″N</span> <span class="longitude">139°44′5.11″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.6624250°N 139.7347528°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.6624250; 139.7347528</span></span></span></a></span></span></span></p>
<p>In 2005, Don Quijote began building a "half-pipe" <a href="/wiki/Roller_coaster" title="Roller coaster">roller coaster</a> on the roof of its eight story <a href="/wiki/Roppongi" title="Roppongi">Roppongi</a> store. Roppongi is a heavily populated area in the core of Tokyo, and many residents and businesses were upset with the idea of having a roller coaster in their neighborhood because of the spectacle, noise and crowds it will likely create. The project was completed in 2006 but due to increasing pressure from concerned groups in the area it has never been operated. As of 2016, the structure still exists. Don Quijote has not announced what future plans they have for it.　<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overseas_Assets">Overseas Assets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Quijote_(store)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Overseas Assets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Don Quijote purchased the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marukai" title="Marukai">Marukai</a> Japanese chain stores in USA in 2013, splitting it into Marukai and Tokyo Central Markets chains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
