<p>A <b>magnifying glass</b> (called a <b>hand lens</b> in laboratory contexts) is a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)#Types_of_simple_lenses" title="Lens (optics)">convex lens</a> that is used to produce a <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">magnified</a> <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">image</a> of an object. The <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a> is usually mounted in a frame with a handle (see image).</p>
<p>A <b>sheet magnifier</b> consists of many very narrow concentric ring-shaped lenses, such that the combination acts as a single lens but is much thinner. This arrangement is known as a <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a>.</p>
<p>A magnifying glass can also be used to focus light, such as to concentrate the sun's radiation to create a hot spot at the <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focus</a> for fire starting.</p>
<p>The magnifying glass is an icon of <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a>, particularly that of <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnifying_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest evidence of a magnifying device was a joke in <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Clouds" title="The Clouds">The Clouds</a></i> from 424 BC, where magnifying lenses to start kindling were sold in a pharmacy, and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s "lens", a glass globe filled with water, used to cauterize wounds. (<a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca</a> wrote that it could be used to read letters "no matter how small or dim").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a> described the properties of a magnifying glass in 13th-century <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyeglasses" title="Eyeglasses">Eyeglasses</a> were developed in 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnification">Magnification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnifying_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Magnification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">magnification</a> of a magnifying glass depends upon where it is placed between the user's eye and the object being viewed, and the total distance between them. The <i>magnifying power</i> is equivalent to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_magnification" title="Angular magnification">angular magnification</a> (this should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Optical_power" title="Optical power">optical power</a>, which is a different quantity). The magnifying power is the ratio of the sizes of the images formed on the user's retina with and without the lens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For the "without" case, it is typically assumed that the user would bring the object as close to one eye as possible without it becoming blurry. This point, known as the <i>near point</i>, <a href="/wiki/Presbyopia" title="Presbyopia">varies with age</a>. In a young child, it can be as close as 5¬†cm, while, in an elderly person it may be as far as one or two metres. Magnifiers are typically characterized using a "standard" value of 0.25¬†m.</p>
<p>The highest magnifying power is obtained by putting the lens very close to one eye and moving the eye and the lens together to obtain the best <a href="/wiki/Defocus_aberration" title="Defocus aberration">focus</a>. The object will then typically also be close to the lens. The magnifying power obtained in this condition is <i>MP</i><sub>0</sub>¬†=¬†(0.25¬†m)Œ¶¬†+¬†1, where Œ¶ is the optical power in <a href="/wiki/Dioptre" title="Dioptre">dioptres</a>, and the factor of 0.25¬†m represents the assumed near point (¬º¬†m from the eye). This value of the magnifying power is the one normally used to characterize magnifiers. It is typically denoted "<i>m</i>√ó", where <i>m</i>¬†=¬†<i>MP</i><sub>0</sub>. This is sometimes called the <i>total power</i> of the magnifier (again, not to be confused with optical power).</p>
<p>However, magnifiers are not always used as described above because it is more comfortable to put the magnifier close to the object (one focal length away). The eye can then be a larger distance away, and a good image can be obtained very easily; the focus is not very sensitive to the eye's exact position. The magnifying power in this case is roughly <i>MP</i>¬†=¬†(0.25¬†m)Œ¶.</p>
<p>A typical magnifying glass might have a <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a> of 25¬†cm, corresponding to an optical power of 4 dioptres. Such a magnifier would be sold as a "2√ó" magnifier. In actual use, an observer with "typical" eyes would obtain a magnifying power between 1 and 2, depending on where lens is held.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives">Alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnifying_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg/220px-Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg/330px-Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg/440px-Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Loupe-triplet-30x-0a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 30√ó Hastings triplet magnifier</div>

<p>Magnifying glasses typically have low magnifying power: 2√ó‚Äì6√ó, with the lower-power types being much more common. At higher magnifications, the image quality of a simple magnifying glass becomes poor due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_aberrations" title="Optical aberrations">optical aberrations</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">spherical aberration</a>. When more magnification or a better image is required, other types of hand magnifier are typically used. A <a href="/wiki/Coddington_magnifier" title="Coddington magnifier">Coddington magnifier</a> provides higher magnification with improved image quality. Even better images can be obtained with a multiple-lens magnifier, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hastings_triplet" title="Hastings triplet">Hastings triplet</a>. High power magnifiers are sometimes mounted in a cylindrical or conical holder with no handle. This is called a <a href="/wiki/Loupe" title="Loupe">loupe</a>.</p>
<p>Such magnifiers can reach up to about 30√ó, and at these magnifications the aperture of the magnifier becomes very small and it must be placed very close to both the object and the eye. For more convenient use or for magnification beyond about 30√ó, one must instead use a <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>.</p>
