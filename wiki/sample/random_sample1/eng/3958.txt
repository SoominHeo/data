<p>The <b>Geoemydidae</b> (formerly known as <b>Bataguridae</b>) are one of the largest and most diverse <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttwg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ttwg-4">[4]</a></sup> in the order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testudines" title="Testudines">Testudines</a> (turtles), with about 70 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. The family includes the <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> pond and river turtles and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropical" title="Neotropical">Neotropical</a> wood turtles.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoemydidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geoemydidae are turtles of various sizes (from about 10 to 80 cm in length) with often a high degree of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>. They usually have webbed toes, and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_girdle" title="Pelvic girdle">pelvic girdles</a> articulate with their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastron" title="Plastron">plastrons</a> flexibly. Their necks are drawn back vertically. Their <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapaces</a> have 24 marginal <a href="/wiki/Scute" title="Scute">scutes</a>. The plastron is composed of 12 scutes and has no mesoplastron; the pectoral and abdominal scutes contact the marginal scutes.</p>
<p>Some other features include a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Articulation_(anatomy)" title="Articulation (anatomy)">articulation</a> between the fifth and sixth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervical_vertebra" title="Cervical vertebra">cervical vertebrae</a>, the lack of a hyomandibular branch of the <a href="/wiki/Facial_nerve" title="Facial nerve">facial nerve</a>, and an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Epipterygoid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Epipterygoid (page does not exist)">epipterygoid</a> bone in the skull.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoemydidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geoemydidae live in tropics and subtropics of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the only genus in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> is <i><a href="/wiki/Rhinoclemmys" title="Rhinoclemmys">Rhinoclemmys</a></i>. Their habitats include freshwater ecosystems, coastal marine areas, and tropical forests. Most are <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivorous</a>, but some are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivorous</a> species. In <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a>, the males are usually much more active than females. A relatively small number of eggs per <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is common, produced several times a year. Some species have a temperature-dependent <a href="/wiki/Sex-determination_system" title="Sex-determination system">sex determination</a> system, while others possess different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_chromosome" title="Sex chromosome">sex chromosomes</a>.</p>
<p>About 70% of the extant species have been reported to be in <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> or vulnerable condition.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_evolution">Systematics and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoemydidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditional systematics placed the geoemydids in the family <a href="/wiki/Emydidae" title="Emydidae">Emydidae</a> as the subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batagurinae" title="Batagurinae">Batagurinae</a>. In the 1980s, the subfamily was elevated to the family status and renamed to Geoemydidae according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Code of Zoological Nomenclature">ICZN</a> rules.</p>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_systematics" title="Molecular systematics">molecular</a> data support their close relationship to the family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testudinidae" title="Testudinidae">Testudinidae</a>.</p>
<p>The intrafamilial taxonomy is not well established yet, due to the large number and diversity of species. The family is usually divided into two subfamilies and 19 <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttwg_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ttwg-4">[4]</a></sup> Several species are known to give viable <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>, which makes the systematics even more complicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buskirketal2005_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-buskirketal2005-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoemydidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of the early 2013, six species of the family Geoemydidae are on the <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> Appendix I, and 30 more are on the treaty's Appendix II. A joint China-US proposal for a March 2013 CITES participants' conference seeks to add 15 more Geoemydidae species to the convention's Appendix II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
