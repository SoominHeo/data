<p><i><b>Dollhouse</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> television series created by writer and director <a href="/wiki/Joss_Whedon" title="Joss Whedon">Joss Whedon</a> under <a href="/wiki/Mutant_Enemy_Productions" title="Mutant Enemy Productions">Mutant Enemy Productions</a>. It premiered on February 13, 2009, on the Fox network and was officially canceled on November 11, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety-cancel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety-cancel-3">[3]</a></sup> The final episode aired on January 29, 2010. Production wrapped in December 2009, with a total of 27 episodes produced including the original pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The show revolves around a corporation running numerous underground establishments (known as "Dollhouses") around the globe that program individuals referred to as Actives (or Dolls) with temporary personalities and skills. Wealthy clients hire Actives from Dollhouses at great expense for various purposes, including heists, sexual encounters, assassinations, expert counsel, and all manner of unique experiences. The series primarily follows the Active known as Echo, played by <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Dushku" title="Eliza Dushku">Eliza Dushku</a>, on her journey towards self-awareness. Dushku also served as series producer.</p>
<p><i>Dollhouse</i> initially received mixed reviews and underwhelming ratings, but improved progressively enough to be renewed for a second season. After the second season finale, the series was cancelled.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dollhouse_episodes" title="List of Dollhouse episodes">List of Dollhouse episodes</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Rossum Corporation" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rossum_(disambiguation)" title="Rossum (disambiguation)">Rossum</a>.</div>
<p>The story follows Echo (<a href="/wiki/Eliza_Dushku" title="Eliza Dushku">Eliza Dushku</a>), a "doll" or "Active" for the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> "Dollhouse", one of several fictional facilities, called "Houses", run by a company which hires out human beings to wealthy clients. These "engagements" range from romantic interludes to high-risk criminal enterprises. Each Active has their original memories wiped and exists in a childlike <a href="/wiki/Tabula_rasa" title="Tabula rasa">blank state</a> until programmed via the insertion of new memories and personalities for each mission. Actives such as Echo are ostensibly volunteers who have surrendered their minds and bodies to the organization for five-year stints, during which their original personalities are saved on hard drives, in exchange for vast amounts of money and a solution to any other problematic circumstances in their lives.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Ghost"><a href="#endnote_Ghost">E-1</a></sup> Echo is unique however in that she remembers small amounts even after personality "wipes", and gradually develops an increasingly cognizant self-awareness and personality. This concept allows the series to examine the notions of identity and personhood.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Omega"><a href="#endnote_Omega">E-12</a></sup></p>
<p>Within The House, opinions on such matters are divided. Dollhouse director Adelle DeWitt (<a href="/wiki/Olivia_Williams" title="Olivia Williams">Olivia Williams</a>) sees her role as merely giving people what they need; programmer Topher Brink (<a href="/wiki/Fran_Kranz" title="Fran Kranz">Fran Kranz</a>) is initially entirely scientific and amoral, apart from brief flashes of moral quandary; while Echo's mentor in The House or "handler", Boyd Langton (<a href="/wiki/Harry_Lennix" title="Harry Lennix">Harry Lennix</a>), an ex-cop with an unknown past, expresses concern with the ethical and theological implications of the Dollhouse's technology, using his inside role as an opportunity to limit any collateral damage. Raising intriguing questions about personality and selfhood are other dolls Victor (<a href="/wiki/Enver_Gjokaj" title="Enver Gjokaj">Enver Gjokaj</a>) and Sierra (<a href="/wiki/Dichen_Lachman" title="Dichen Lachman">Dichen Lachman</a>), who despite being continually re-wiped, begin to fall in love and retain those feelings whether wiped or imprinted with other personalities.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Needs"><a href="#endnote_Needs">E-8</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, FBI agent Paul Ballard (<a href="/wiki/Tahmoh_Penikett" title="Tahmoh Penikett">Tahmoh Penikett</a>) learns of Echo's original personality, Caroline Farrell, through messages, photographs, and videos he receives anonymously. Agent Ballard becomes obsessed with rumors of the Dollhouse and risks his career trying to prove its existence. It is insinuated that Ballard has developed feelings for Echo prior to even meeting her which leads him to continue his investigation even after being taken off the case. Meanwhile, Ballard has been casually dating his neighbor, Mellie. While discussing the investigation over takeout Mellie corrects Ballard when he refers to bringing ‘her’ in, to say ‘them’ in instead. Ballard tries to explain his slip away but Mellie does not look completely convinced. Mellie’s character up to this point on the show has been portrayed as the somewhat insecure neighbor with a crush on Ballard. At the end of this episode it is revealed that Mellie is a ‘sleeper’ doll. She has been planted by the Dollhouse to spy on Ballard. Mellie is unaware of her role in the Dollhouse and believes herself to be a young woman falling in love with an FBI agent. She is in fact a Doll known as November.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup></p>
<p>Ballard finally chases down a lead allowing him to ‘meet’ Caroline/Echo. During the encounter Echo is terrified of Ballard because she believes she is the personality she has been programmed with. Echo is whisked away by her handler, leaving Ballard with only Joel Minor, the man who paid for the encounter, to question. Minor points out the apparent connection that Ballard feels for Echo and cites it as the reason that Ballard is so driven to investigate the Dollhouse.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup></p>
<p>As Echo continues to evolve and learns to work beyond the limits of each temporary personality imprint or default "<a href="/wiki/Tabula_rasa" title="Tabula rasa">tabula rasa</a>" programming, she runs the risk of being sent to "the Attic", a permanent resting place for "broken" dolls and Dollhouse employees who cause problems. She is an object of fascination for the escaped doll Alpha (<a href="/wiki/Alan_Tudyk" title="Alan Tudyk">Alan Tudyk</a>)—a genius and serial killer who has been driven mad by being implanted with the memories of dozens of people. Alpha, the season 1 "<a href="/wiki/Big_Bad" title="Big Bad">Big Bad</a>" returns at the end of the first season to kidnap Caroline.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Briar_Rose"><a href="#endnote_Briar_Rose">E-11</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Omega"><a href="#endnote_Omega">E-12</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Epitaph_One" title="Epitaph One">Epitaph One</a>", the final episode of season one, which was not aired as part of the show's original run on US television, depicts a post-apocalyptic future where the mind-wiping technology of the Dollhouse has developed to the extent that vast numbers of people can be remotely wiped and have new personalities implanted, which has brought about the end of civilization. Many of the series' main characters' futures are shown.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Epitaph_One"><a href="#endnote_Epitaph_One">E-13</a></sup> As the second season begins, the show's focus shifts to depict the dangers of abusing the mind-wiping technology. Each character in the L.A. Dollhouse is forced to confront their own moral complicity in an increasingly downward spiral from moral grey areas to the realization that what the Dollhouse is doing is ultimately immoral and extremely dangerous. The Dollhouse's corporate sponsor is a medical research entity known as the Rossum Corporation, whose ultimate goal is revealed to be gaining control over national governments and even innocent people with no association with the Dollhouse. Through these abilities, the leaders of Rossum can rule the world and also be immortal, jumping from body to body at will. Attempting to stop the further spread of the mind-wiping technology, the L.A. Dollhouse vows to take down Rossum and its mysterious founder, whom only Echo's original personality, Caroline, has met.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Hollow_Men"><a href="#endnote_The_Hollow_Men">E-25</a></sup> They also learn that there is no person named "Rossum"—the company founder took the name from the play "<a href="/wiki/R.U.R." title="R.U.R.">R.U.R.</a>", which is short for Rossum's Universal Robots". This 1921 science fiction play by Karel Čapek is the origin of the word "robot".</p>
<p>The final episode of the series is set in the year 2020, and takes place shortly after the events that took place in "Epitaph One". Despite its best efforts, the L.A. Dollhouse has been unsuccessful in stopping the mind-wiping technology from spreading out of control. Rossum executives use multiple bodies to live in decadence while the peoples of the world are enslaved. A now mentally unstable Topher, architect of much of the technology, devises a way of restoring everyone's original personalities and eliminating Rossum's power, but at great sacrifice to himself and others. The series concludes with the world's personalities restored, while the Earth still lies in ruins, and those with Active architecture sheltering inside the Dollhouse for one year in order to keep the memories they have acquired since their original personalities were restored some years ago, rather than being wiped and defaulting back to their memories from before the Dollhouse got hold of them.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Epitaph_2"><a href="#endnote_Epitaph_2">E-26</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The series stars <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Dushku" title="Eliza Dushku">Eliza Dushku</a>, who worked with Whedon on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer_(TV_series)" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer (TV series)">Buffy the Vampire Slayer</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Angel_(1999_TV_series)" title="Angel (1999 TV series)">Angel</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Craft_and_Sarah_Fain" title="Elizabeth Craft and Sarah Fain">Elizabeth Craft and Sarah Fain</a> were the <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">showrunners</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Jane_Espenson" title="Jane Espenson">Jane Espenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Minear" title="Tim Minear">Tim Minear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_S._DeKnight" title="Steven S. DeKnight">Steven S. DeKnight</a> served as <a href="/wiki/Television_producer#Types_of_television_producers" title="Television producer">consulting producers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_Party_Is_TV_Fan_Heaven_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW_Party_Is_TV_Fan_Heaven-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tim_Minear_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tim_Minear-6">[6]</a></sup> In addition to Joss Whedon, the writing staff included Tim Minear, <a href="/wiki/Jed_Whedon" title="Jed Whedon">Jed Whedon</a> (Joss' brother), <a href="/wiki/Maurissa_Tancharoen" title="Maurissa Tancharoen">Maurissa Tancharoen</a> (Jed's wife), <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Chambliss" title="Andrew Chambliss">Andrew Chambliss</a>, Tracy Bellomo, Elizabeth Craft and Sarah Fain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Whedon directed a number of his own episodes, as he has done for many of the shows he created. Tim Minear and <i>Buffy</i> producer <a href="/wiki/David_Solomon_(TV_producer)" title="David Solomon (TV producer)">David Solomon</a> also directed. Fox used a <a href="/wiki/Viral_marketing" title="Viral marketing">viral marketing</a> campaign to promote <i>Dollhouse</i> in May 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Dollhouse</i> was produced by <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox_Television" title="20th Century Fox Television">20th Century Fox Television</a>, Whedon's <a href="/wiki/Mutant_Enemy_Productions" title="Mutant Enemy Productions">Mutant Enemy Productions</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimesblog_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimesblog-9">[9]</a></sup> and Dushku's Boston Diva Productions, and was granted an initial thirteen-episode production commitment by Fox, with a reported license fee in the range of $1.5 to 2 million per episode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hollywood_Insider_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hollywood_Insider-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13_episodes_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-13_episodes-11">[11]</a></sup> Fox decided to forego the usual practice of ordering a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilot_episode" title="Pilot episode">pilot episode</a> of the series, opting to instead put funds towards the construction of the elaborate set and cultural context of the television series. The set was described as a "life-size Dollhouse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pilot_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-12">[12]</a></sup> On July 22, 2008, Joss Whedon announced that the first episode shot, "Echo", would be pushed to be the second, saying that this "idea to do a new first episode wasn't the network's. It was mine".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whedonesque_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-whedonesque-13">[13]</a></sup> Despite several reshoots, "Echo" was later pulled from the run entirely;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lights_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-lights-14">[14]</a></sup> the staff of the show has since noted, during a panel on the series at the Paley Festival, a television festival held at the <a href="/wiki/Paley_Center_for_Media" title="Paley Center for Media">Paley Center for Media</a> in New York City, that portions of the episode were used in subsequent episodes throughout the series' first season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Dollhouse</i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/J._J._Abrams" title="J. J. Abrams">J. J. Abrams</a>' <a href="/wiki/Fringe_(TV_series)" title="Fringe (TV series)"><i>Fringe</i></a>, aired during its first season with half the commercials and promo spots of most current network dramas, adding about 6 minutes to the shows' run times, as part of a new Fox initiative called "Remote-Free TV".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Remote_Free_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Remote_Free-16">[16]</a></sup> Fox charged a premium price for this advertising space, but did not completely recoup the money that they spent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Remote_Free_leaving_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Remote_Free_leaving-17">[17]</a></sup> Fox later canceled Remote-Free TV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Originally, Whedon announced he was planning to shoot a separate <a href="/wiki/Webisode" title="Webisode">webisode</a> for every <i>Dollhouse</i> episode produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webisodes_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-webisodes-19">[19]</a></sup> The webisodes did not materialize, however. There was originally a five-year plan for the show, with Whedon plotting how its characters would evolve through that point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5-year_run_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-5-year_run-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>Anya Colloff and Amy McIntyre Britt, who previously worked with Joss Whedon on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer_(TV_series)" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer (TV series)"><i>Buffy the Vampire Slayer</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Angel_(1999_TV_series)" title="Angel (1999 TV series)"><i>Angel</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Firefly_(TV_series)" title="Firefly (TV series)"><i>Firefly</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Serenity_(film)" title="Serenity (film)"><i>Serenity</i></a>, were the show's casting directors.</p>
<p>On March 26, 2008, it was officially announced that <a href="/wiki/Tahmoh_Penikett" title="Tahmoh Penikett">Tahmoh Penikett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dichen_Lachman" title="Dichen Lachman">Dichen Lachman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran_Kranz" title="Fran Kranz">Fran Kranz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Enver_Gjokaj" title="Enver Gjokaj">Enver Gjokaj</a> had been cast in four principal roles for the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ausiello_Report_March_26.2C_2008_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ausiello_Report_March_26.2C_2008-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watch_with_Kristin_March_26.2C_2008_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watch_with_Kristin_March_26.2C_2008-22">[22]</a></sup> On April 3, 2008, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Williams" title="Olivia Williams">Olivia Williams</a> would be playing the role of Adelle DeWitt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HR2008-04-03_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HR2008-04-03-23">[23]</a></sup> Two weeks later, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Harry_Lennix" title="Harry Lennix">Harry Lennix</a> had also joined the cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eonlinepilot_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-eonlinepilot-24">[24]</a></sup> On the same day, <a href="/wiki/Joss_Whedon" title="Joss Whedon">Joss Whedon</a> announced on <a href="/wiki/Whedonesque.com" title="Whedonesque.com">whedonesque.com</a> that <a href="/wiki/Miracle_Laurie" title="Miracle Laurie">Miracle Laurie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amy_Acker" title="Amy Acker">Amy Acker</a> were to complete the cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whedonesque-2008-04-17_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-whedonesque-2008-04-17-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brennan_Elliott" title="Brennan Elliott">Brennan Elliott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Muhney" title="Michael Muhney">Michael Muhney</a> auditioned for the part of Paul Ballard but lost out to Penikett. <a href="/wiki/Ian_Anthony_Dale" title="Ian Anthony Dale">Ian Anthony Dale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Campbell_(Canadian_actor)" title="Paul Campbell (Canadian actor)">Paul Campbell</a> auditioned for Victor, but Gjokaj got the part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unaired_.22Epitaph_One.22">Unaired "Epitaph One"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: Unaired "Epitaph One"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 9, 2009, Whedon rebutted speculation that Fox was set to cancel the show. Producer Tim Minear explained that the "missing" 13th episode (titled "<a href="/wiki/Epitaph_One" title="Epitaph One">Epitaph One</a>") would be on the DVD release of the season. The reason Minear gave for that episode being dropped from the broadcast run was that the Fox <i>network</i> was counting the original first episode ("Echo"), which went unaired, as part of the original 13-episode order; in contrast, the Fox <i>production company</i> was required by contract to have a minimum of 13 completed episodes for international and DVD releases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Live_Feed_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Live_Feed-27">[27]</a></sup> According to both Minear and Whedon, the producers felt that the original first episode, having been subsequently scrapped entirely and having had its footage reused for other episodes throughout the season, should not be counted as a completed episode as part of their own 13-episode orders for international and DVD distribution but rather as a DVD extra, and thus Whedon produced a new 13th episode on a lower budget to fulfill the contractual requirements for the international broadcasts. The episode was screened at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic-Con_International" title="Comic-Con International">Comic-Con</a> on July 24, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Epitaph One" had its world premiere in Singapore on June 17, 2009, through Season Pass, an on-demand service offered by SingTel mio TV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the episode aired on the UK <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sci_Fi_Channel_(United_Kingdom)" title="Sci Fi Channel (United Kingdom)">Sci Fi Channel</a> on August 11, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_season_and_cancellation">Second season and cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second season and cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite low ratings in its first season, <i>Dollhouse</i> was renewed for a second season<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scifiwire_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-scifiwire-31">[31]</a></sup> of thirteen episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thrfeed-renew_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-thrfeed-renew-32">[32]</a></sup> Among other factors, fan response to the show was seen as a reason for the renewal; Fox's president of entertainment stated that "if we'd canceled Joss's show I'd probably have 110 million e-mails this morning from the fans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scifiwire_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-scifiwire-31">[31]</a></sup> As part of the deal, there was a cut in the show's budget, though Whedon stated that this would not affect the quality of the episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The second season also had changes visually, the show moved from being shot on <a href="/wiki/35_mm_film" title="35 mm film">35 mm film</a> to <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">high-definition video</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> With the addition of new cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Wiegand" title="Lisa Wiegand">Lisa Wiegand</a>, Whedon wanted the show to look darker. Other visual changes included more <a href="/wiki/Hand-held_camera" title="Hand-held camera">hand-held camera</a> work and the addition of snap zooms (an effect that moves in or pulls back very quickly, which was used extensively in <i><a href="/wiki/Firefly_(TV_series)" title="Firefly (TV series)">Firefly</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The series continued in its 9–10 pm Friday timeslot, with the season premiere on September 25, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Season 2 of <i>Dollhouse</i> began filming on July 22, 2009, so Fox pushed back <i>Dollhouse</i>'s return to the 25th to afford Whedon and the <i>Dollhouse</i> production team sufficient time to produce enough hours to kick off the season with at least three or four consecutive episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alexis_Denisof" title="Alexis Denisof">Alexis Denisof</a> joined the cast in a recurring role as Senator Daniel Perrin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> as did <a href="/wiki/Summer_Glau" title="Summer Glau">Summer Glau</a>, who was originally scheduled to appear in just two episodes, a number that was later extended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Hogan_(Canadian_actor)" title="Michael Hogan (Canadian actor)">Michael Hogan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Bamber" title="Jamie Bamber">Jamie Bamber</a>, both former castmates of series regular <a href="/wiki/Tahmoh_Penikett" title="Tahmoh Penikett">Tahmoh Penikett</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">Battlestar Galactica</a></i>, each had roles as guest stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Michele Fazekas and Tara Butters (creators of <i><a href="/wiki/Reaper_(TV_series)" title="Reaper (TV series)">Reaper</a></i>) joined the writing staff for season 2 as replacements for former showrunners <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Craft_and_Sarah_Fain" title="Elizabeth Craft and Sarah Fain">Elizabeth Craft and Sarah Fain</a> (who left <i>Dollhouse</i> to join the writing staff of <i><a href="/wiki/Lie_to_Me" title="Lie to Me">Lie to Me</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fox announced in October 2009 that it would not be airing any episodes of <i>Dollhouse</i> during November <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweeps" title="Sweeps">sweeps</a>, and that the series would return in December, airing episodes back-to-back instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On November 11, 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> announced that the show had been cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety-cancel_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-variety-cancel-3">[3]</a></sup> Fox passed on ordering more episodes of the show;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-futon-critic_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-futon-critic-43">[43]</a></sup> although it did air the entirety of the 13-episode order. After airing the back-to-back episodes in December, the final three episodes aired during January 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_cast">Main cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Main cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Echo_(Dollhouse)" title="Echo (Dollhouse)">Echo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Eliza_Dushku" title="Eliza Dushku">Eliza Dushku</a>) is an Active and the protagonist of the series. She is the most popular Active in the Dollhouse, and has shown skills that transcend the limitations of her parameters during the course of her engagements. Prior to having her mind wiped, Echo was a college graduate and political <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activist</a> named Caroline Farrell.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Echoes"><a href="#endnote_Echoes">E-7</a></sup> Caroline is originally a political activist who accidentally uncovers the Rossum Corporation's illegal activities; her boyfriend is killed during an attempt to infiltrate a Rossum laboratory, and Rossum attempts to have her recruited to Adelle DeWitt's Dollhouse.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Echoes"><a href="#endnote_Echoes">E-7</a></sup> While on the run from Rossum, Caroline becomes a terrorist devoted to bringing the corporation down, until she is finally captured.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup> Throughout the series, Echo becomes increasingly self-aware while in her blank state, and later even vows to discover and restore her original self.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Vows"><a href="#endnote_Vows">E-14</a></sup> On achieving full self-awareness, Echo develops a romantic interest in Paul Ballard. Having learned more about Caroline's potentially murky past, she decides to be her own person.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Meet_Jane_Doe"><a href="#endnote_Meet_Jane_Doe">E-20</a></sup> While condemned to the Attic, she discovers Rossum's weakness and emerges to ally with DeWitt and the LA Dollhouse against Rossum.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Attic"><a href="#endnote_The_Attic">E-23</a></sup> While she is uncertain what having become her own person will mean for her, she agrees to be reunited with Caroline's personality to gain crucial information over Rossum.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup></li>
<li>Boyd Langton (<a href="/wiki/Harry_Lennix" title="Harry Lennix">Harry Lennix</a>) is introduced at the onset of the first season as a former cop and the Dollhouse <a href="/wiki/Agent_handling" title="Agent handling">handler</a> assigned to Echo. He has doubts about the Dollhouse's ethics, but largely keeps quiet about them. Later in the season, he's promoted to head of security.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Spy"><a href="#endnote_A_Spy">E-9</a></sup> His own "morally compromised" past is not originally revealed, but it is known he has considerable skills and contacts in disposing of dead bodies.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup> When Echo returns to the Dollhouse after a three-month absence, he allies himself with Paul and Topher in keeping her self-awareness a secret from the Rossum execs.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Love_Supreme"><a href="#endnote_A_Love_Supreme">E-21</a></sup> After regaining Caroline's memories, she discovers that Boyd is one of the two founders of the Rossum Corporation and seemingly installed himself in the Dollhouse to become Echo's handler.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup> After attacking Echo, Langton's mind is wiped after Topher finishes a remote wiping device. In a doll state, Langton is fitted with explosives and given a grenade and is commanded to blow up the Rossum building, which he does, killing himself in the process.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Hollow_Men"><a href="#endnote_The_Hollow_Men">E-25</a></sup></li>
<li>Topher Brink (<a href="/wiki/Fran_Kranz" title="Fran Kranz">Fran Kranz</a>) is the scientist who operates Dollhouse's technology and uses it to imprint new personalities on the Actives. Cynical, ego-driven and seemingly amoral, Topher's knowledge of human behavior allows him to specially craft the various personalities of the Actives for their various missions. In the second season, Topher faces several moral dilemmas, the first when he is required by Rossum to revive a serial killer.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belle_Chose"><a href="#endnote_Belle_Chose">E-16</a></sup> When ordered to permanently surrender the Active Sierra to her real-life rapist, Nolan Kinnard, Topher instead returns her as Priya (original personality of the Doll Sierra), who then kills Kinnard in self-defense. Topher and Boyd help Priya cover up the murder, and Priya agrees to return to the Dollhouse as Sierra.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup> When the LA Dollhouse comes under the control of Matthew Harding, Topher becomes concerned about Rossum's ambitions concerning the Dollhouse technology. He informs Adelle that he has figured out Rossum's plan as well as the technique to imprint any human being remotely, but she betrays him and surrenders it to her superiors.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Meet_Jane_Doe"><a href="#endnote_Meet_Jane_Doe">E-20</a></sup> Following this, Topher aligns with Boyd and Ballard's "conspiracy" when he learns of Echo's full self-awareness.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Love_Supreme"><a href="#endnote_A_Love_Supreme">E-21</a></sup> Topher develops strong romantic feelings for Bennett Halverson, whose genius technological achievements he admired long before he met her in person. Bennett appears to reciprocate Topher's feelings, but she is killed before they have the chance to develop a more serious relationship. As the Dollhouse unravels and things spiral out of control, he develops an immense guilty conscience, blaming himself (as inventor of the technology) for the events that unfold. Ultimately he creates technology to reverse the process that has wiped the minds of the general population, but sacrifices his own life in order to deploy it.</li>
<li>Paul Ballard (<a href="/wiki/Tahmoh_Penikett" title="Tahmoh Penikett">Tahmoh Penikett</a>) is an FBI special agent assigned to the Dollhouse case at the onset of the series; most in the Bureau view the case as a joke, but he makes discovering and rescuing Caroline/Echo an obsession. The Dollhouse assigns active November to spy on him by becoming his girlfriend, Mellie. After breaking into the Dollhouse, he works for them under the condition that November is released,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Omega"><a href="#endnote_Omega">E-12</a></sup> and later takes the role of Echo's handler. The two of them vow to set the Actives free.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Vows"><a href="#endnote_Vows">E-14</a></sup> When Echo is left out on the streets, Paul finds her and helps conceal her from the Dollhouse for three months while she develops a full self-awareness. She becomes romantically and sexually attracted to him, but he abstains from sex with her. The two re-enter the Dollhouse to continue their crusade.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Meet_Jane_Doe"><a href="#endnote_Meet_Jane_Doe">E-20</a></sup> They later form an alliance with Boyd and Topher, but Paul is wiped and left braindead by Alpha.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Love_Supreme"><a href="#endnote_A_Love_Supreme">E-21</a></sup> Topher is able to partially restore Ballard by converting him into an Active, removing one brain function, and imprinting him with the scan Alpha took.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Attic"><a href="#endnote_The_Attic">E-23</a></sup> However, in doing so, it was necessary for Paul's feelings of love toward Echo to be erased.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup> In the final episode of Dollhouse set ten years in the future, Paul is killed in combat, and his memories and personality are imprinted into Echo.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Epitaph_2"><a href="#endnote_Epitaph_2">E-26</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Victor_(Dollhouse)" title="Victor (Dollhouse)">Victor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Enver_Gjokaj" title="Enver Gjokaj">Enver Gjokaj</a>) is an Active who was originally introduced as Lubov, Paul Ballard's informant inside the Russian mob, before being revealed to be a Doll.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Stage_Fright"><a href="#endnote_Stage_Fright">E-3</a></sup> The character is also regularly hired out by Adelle DeWitt herself to be her lover, whom she truly appears to love. In his mind-wiped state, Victor is inexplicably attracted to Sierra despite numerous attempts to wipe away his memories of and feelings for her.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Spy"><a href="#endnote_A_Spy">E-9</a></sup> Before entering the Dollhouse, Victor was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">War in Afghanistan</a> veteran named Anthony Ceccoli suffering <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">posttraumatic stress disorder</a> (of which he is cured by the Dollhouse). He is restored to this personality in "<a href="/wiki/Stop-Loss_(Dollhouse)" title="Stop-Loss (Dollhouse)">Stop-Loss</a>",<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Stop-Loss"><a href="#endnote_Stop-Loss">E-22</a></sup> and aligns with Priya, Echo, and the senior staff of the LA Dollhouse against Rossum.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Attic"><a href="#endnote_The_Attic">E-23</a></sup> In the series finale set ten years in the future, Anthony and Priya share a tense relationship due to his reliance on imprinting technology which Priya considers a dangerous obsession. Priya later realizes he compromised himself only to protect her and their child, and they reconcile near the end of the series.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Epitaph_2"><a href="#endnote_Epitaph_2">E-26</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sierra_(Dollhouse)" title="Sierra (Dollhouse)">Sierra</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dichen_Lachman" title="Dichen Lachman">Dichen Lachman</a>) is introduced as the newest Active in the Dollhouse; her original mind wipe occurs in the first episode of the first season. She is instinctively drawn to Echo, but lacks her growing self-awareness. Sierra is a painter named Priya Tsetsang prior to having her mind wiped and becoming an Active.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup> Unlike the other Actives, Sierra was committed to the Dollhouse against her will by a powerful man, Nolan Kinnard, after she rejected his advances. He then hires her out periodically for sexual encounters.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Needs"><a href="#endnote_Needs">E-8</a></sup> During Season 1, she is also raped by her handler in her blank state, only adding to her trauma.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup> When Sierra is sent to her original rapist and captor permanently, Topher sends Sierra as her original personality Priya, and she kills Kinnard in self-defense. Boyd and Topher dispose of the body and Priya agrees to return to the Dollhouse to be with Victor, whom she loves.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup> Priya is later awakened to help retrieve Anthony/Victor when he is captured and enslaved by Rossum,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Stop-Loss"><a href="#endnote_Stop-Loss">E-22</a></sup> and the two unite with Echo and the senior staff of the LA Dollhouse against Rossum.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Attic"><a href="#endnote_The_Attic">E-23</a></sup> Ten years after the events of the series, Priya is shown to have a child with Anthony whom she raises alone, as she mistrusts him and his obsession with imprinting technology. Near the end of the series, Priya realizes Anthony's unwavering devotion to her and introduces their son to him, reuniting the family.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Epitaph_2"><a href="#endnote_Epitaph_2">E-26</a></sup></li>
<li>Adelle DeWitt (<a href="/wiki/Olivia_Williams" title="Olivia Williams">Olivia Williams</a>) is the highest-ranking official at the Los Angeles Dollhouse. She claims to believe that the aims of the Dollhouse are truly benevolent; however, she displays an increasingly cynical attitude in Season 2, eventually joining the conspiracy against Rossum.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Attic"><a href="#endnote_The_Attic">E-23</a></sup> Although DeWitt is the head of her Dollhouse (as the L.A. Dollhouse is just one of more than twenty worldwide)<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup> she answers to a number of superiors at the Rossum Corporation. It is revealed that she has been hiring out Victor as her lover, though she has concealed these activities from others in the Dollhouse by inventing a client referred to as "Miss Lonelyhearts".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Spy"><a href="#endnote_A_Spy">E-9</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurring_cast">Recurring cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recurring cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Whiskey (<a href="/wiki/Amy_Acker" title="Amy Acker">Amy Acker</a>) is originally introduced in the series as the Actives' general physician Dr. Claire Saunders. It is revealed in the season one finale that she is in fact an Active. Formerly the Dollhouse's most popular Doll, she was attacked by Alpha with a pair of scissors, causing extensive facial scarring. Shortly afterward, Alpha killed the actual Dr. Saunders, and Whiskey was imprinted with his personality and skill-set to serve as his replacement before the events of the first episode. Saunders has trouble adjusting to the realization that she is merely Topher's creation, and objects to being wiped or restored as that would be equivalent to dying. She takes leave of the Dollhouse for some time to find herself.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Vows"><a href="#endnote_Vows">E-14</a></sup> Later, she begins a relationship with Boyd and he brings her back to the Dollhouse for the confrontation with Rossum. Boyd is later revealed to be one of Rossum's founders and has been working against the group all along. Dr. Saunders shoots Bennett through the forehead in front of Topher,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup> and she is speculated to have been a sleeper active.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whiskey_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-whiskey-45">[45]</a></sup> In "<a href="/wiki/The_Hollow_Men_(Dollhouse)" title="The Hollow Men (Dollhouse)">The Hollow Men</a>", Whiskey is imprinted as Rossum co-founder Clyde 2.0 and does battle with Echo. Between then and flashfoward episode "Epitaph One", Whiskey is re-imprinted with Dr. Saunders' personality as well as programmer skills. Later, after a time when her scars have been healed, she is seen having reverted to Whiskey, acting as a guardian for the Dollhouse, pointing people towards Safe Haven. When the Dollhouse is breached in "Epitaph One", she lets out a toxic gas which incapacitates the intruders and leaves her own fate uncertain.</li>
<li>Laurence Dominic (<a href="/wiki/Reed_Diamond" title="Reed Diamond">Reed Diamond</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lights_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-lights-14">[14]</a></sup> head of security at the Dollhouse during most of the first season, takes his job very seriously but views the Dolls as more like pets than humans. He attempts to kill Echo, and also suggests she be retired as an Active and put into "the Attic".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Believer"><a href="#endnote_Believer">E-5</a></sup> Later, while under the influence of a drug, he attempts to apologize to Echo for his actions.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Echoes"><a href="#endnote_Echoes">E-7</a></sup> Dominic is revealed to be an <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">NSA</a> agent who is monitoring but not exposing the Dollhouse for unknown purposes, but he may have been Senator Perrin's NSA contact who Perrin comments has "gone dark." Upon discovery, DeWitt has Topher extract his persona from his body and then sends him to the Attic.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Spy"><a href="#endnote_A_Spy">E-9</a></sup> While in the Attic he becomes a defender of other imprisoned minds against the shadowy killer "Arcane". He and Echo work together when she arrives in the Attic.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Attic"><a href="#endnote_The_Attic">E-23</a></sup></li>
<li>November (<a href="/wiki/Miracle_Laurie" title="Miracle Laurie">Miracle Laurie</a>) is originally introduced to the series as Mellie, Paul Ballard's neighbor, romantic interest, and confidante, but is in fact a "<a href="/wiki/Sleeper_agent" title="Sleeper agent">sleeper</a>" Active.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup> Adelle can switch November to a combat-ready personality using verbal codes.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup> In "Omega", November's original persona and memories are restored and she is released from her contract early with full payment at Ballard's request in exchange for his joining the Dollhouse's staff. She returns to her life as Madeline Costley.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Omega"><a href="#endnote_Omega">E-12</a></sup> After being approached by Senator Daniel Perrin with evidence of the criminal and sexual acts she was made to perform as an Active, she agrees to testify before Congress about the Dollhouse.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Public_Eye"><a href="#endnote_The_Public_Eye">E-18</a></sup> Unfortunately, this turns out to be a trap; she is denounced by Perrin (himself an Active) as a liar with faked evidence "disproving" the existence of the Dollhouse. She is subsequently sent to the less scrupulous Washington D.C. Dollhouse where her mind is forcibly erased and she is re-enslaved.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Left_Hand"><a href="#endnote_The_Left_Hand">E-19</a></sup> After Paul rescues her, Adelle chooses to reawaken her as Mellie rather than Madeline during the Dollhouse's confrontation with Rossum, due to her devotion to Paul and Madeline's disloyalty.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup> When Mellie's sleeper state is later activated to kill Paul during an invasion of the Rossum headquarters, she commits suicide to prevent herself from harming Paul.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Hollow_Men"><a href="#endnote_The_Hollow_Men">E-25</a></sup></li>
<li>Ivy (<a href="/wiki/Liza_Lapira" title="Liza Lapira">Liza Lapira</a>) is Topher's assistant. While highly skilled and seeing herself as Topher's apprentice, Topher treats her more as a <a href="/wiki/Gofer" title="Gofer">gofer</a>, assigning her menial tasks such as fetching him snacks. She later teams up with Adelle, Boyd, Priya, Anthony, Echo, Paul, and Topher to take down Rossum. Not wanting to see her get hurt in the battle, Topher tells her to leave and not become like him. Ivy then escapes from the Dollhouse.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup></li>
<li>Joe Hearn (<a href="/wiki/Kevin_Kilner" title="Kevin Kilner">Kevin Kilner</a>) is Sierra's handler in the first six episodes, in addition to being the handler of the previous Sierra. Joe Hearn is introduced as a less-dedicated counterpart to Boyd Langton.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Stage_Fright"><a href="#endnote_Stage_Fright">E-3</a></sup> He strongly dislikes Echo for her individualism and worries about her influence on Sierra. DeWitt eventually learns that Hearn has raped Sierra in her blank state a number of times, and has him killed by activating November's combat-ready personality while he's on assignment to assassinate "Mellie".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Man"><a href="#endnote_Man">E-6</a></sup></li>
<li>Dr. Nolan Kinnard (<a href="/wiki/Vincent_Ventresca" title="Vincent Ventresca">Vincent Ventresca</a>) is a wealthy psychiatrist, Rossum Corp VIP, and art collector. He meets Sierra while she is still aspiring artist Priya Tsetsang. As a means of high-profile courtship he buys one of her paintings and invites her to a party where it is exhibited. She spurns his advances, and in retaliation he drugs her with psychotropic medications to mimic the symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>, then turns her over to the Dollhouse.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup> When the Dollhouse temporarily restores her original personality, she confronts Kinnard and he gloats that he can have her any time he wants, even making her beg.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Needs"><a href="#endnote_Needs">E-8</a></sup> He eventually requests that Sierra be sent to him permanently, a demand that Rossum forces DeWitt to obey. A remorseful Topher imprints Sierra with her original personality, and during her confrontation with Kinnard he produces a knife, leading to her stabbing him. Topher and Boyd dispose of the body.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup></li>
<li>Alpha (<a href="/wiki/Alan_Tudyk" title="Alan Tudyk">Alan Tudyk</a>),<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Briar_Rose"><a href="#endnote_Briar_Rose">E-11</a></sup> born Carl William Craft,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Omega"><a href="#endnote_Omega">E-12</a></sup> is a rogue Active who escapes from the Dollhouse. Prior to the events of the series, an accident causes a "composite event" in which 48 personalities are simultaneously imprinted in Alpha, along with all the associated memories and skill sets. In his escape, he kills or maims several Dolls and Dollhouse staff members (including Echo's previous handler) but leaves Echo unscathed.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Target"><a href="#endnote_The_Target">E-2</a></sup> After his escape from the Dollhouse, Alpha begins to send anonymous packages to Paul Ballard that hint at the existence of the Dollhouse and Echo's former identity.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Ghost"><a href="#endnote_Ghost">E-1</a></sup> Alpha reveals himself after posing as an Environmental Systems Consultant involved in the construction of the Los Angeles Dollhouse facility, Stephen Kepler, whom Ballard has tracked down. He leads Ballard into the Dollhouse, takes control of the security and automated systems, and leaves with Echo.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Briar_Rose"><a href="#endnote_Briar_Rose">E-11</a></sup> Though Echo escapes him, he remains at large.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Omega"><a href="#endnote_Omega">E-12</a></sup> On learning of Echo's full self-actualisation and her romantic attraction to Paul Ballard, he imprints Paul's personality into himself and leaves the real Ballard brain-dead.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_A_Love_Supreme"><a href="#endnote_A_Love_Supreme">E-21</a></sup> In the series finale, Alpha is shown to have defected to Echo's side. When the wiping technology is to be reversed, Alpha isolates himself from the group in fear that his pre-wiped personality as a serial killer would lead the others to danger.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Epitaph_2"><a href="#endnote_Epitaph_2">E-26</a></sup></li>
<li>Daniel Perrin (<a href="/wiki/Alexis_Denisof" title="Alexis Denisof">Alexis Denisof</a>), introduced during season two,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Vows"><a href="#endnote_Vows">E-14</a></sup> is a third-generation United States Senator who was kidnapped by the Rossum Corporation for the purpose of being turned into an Active. His mind was subsequently heavily altered via fake memories implants regarding his wife (really his handler) and his personality, turning him from a drunken slacker known for partying, into a serious-minded politician and reformer. Though his conditioning was undone and he escaped along with Echo, it was ultimately restored and, as per Rossum's orders, he "debunks" the Dollhouse myth.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Left_Hand"><a href="#endnote_The_Left_Hand">E-19</a></sup></li>
<li>Matthew Harding (<a href="/wiki/Keith_Carradine" title="Keith Carradine">Keith Carradine</a>) is the Rossum Corp executive who oversees Adelle. He does not delude himself about Rossum's goals or the Dollhouse's purpose, and sees the Actives as property. He insists Adelle surrender Sierra to Kinnard,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Belonging"><a href="#endnote_Belonging">E-17</a></sup> and keep her nose out of the Senator Perrin engagement.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Public_Eye"><a href="#endnote_The_Public_Eye">E-18</a></sup> For three months, he becomes the head of the LA Dollhouse while it develops the remote wipe technology for Rossum. Adelle is able to wrestle power back off of him by showing her supplication to Rossum in handing over the complete remote imprint schematics designed by Topher.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Meet_Jane_Doe"><a href="#endnote_Meet_Jane_Doe">E-20</a></sup></li>
<li>Bennett Halverson (<a href="/wiki/Summer_Glau" title="Summer Glau">Summer Glau</a>), introduced in "The Public Eye", is the D.C. Dollhouse's programmer. Even Topher regards her as a genius. Prior to the events of the series (and becoming Echo), Caroline is Bennett's best friend. Although viewers were initially led to believe that Caroline betrayed Bennett and left her for dead, it is later revealed that she was attempting to disavow Bennett's involvement with the bombing attack Caroline and Bennett mounted against Rossum – something for which Bennett does not forgive Caroline. When Bennett attempts to kill Echo by imprinting Daniel Perrin with an assassin persona, she is thwarted by Topher, who had initially developed an attraction towards her.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_The_Left_Hand"><a href="#endnote_The_Left_Hand">E-19</a></sup> Eventually, she is kidnapped by Paul and Anthony, taking her back to the Dollhouse in order to fix Caroline's wedge, but she is shot in the head by Dr. Saunders directly after sharing her first kiss with Topher.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Getting_Closer"><a href="#endnote_Getting_Closer">E-24</a></sup> She is last seen onscreen in a tape Topher plays in which she lectures about neuroscience, helping him develop the device to reverse the wiping of the human race.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_books">Comic books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Comic books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="758" data-file-width="525" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c3/Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg/170px-Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c3/Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg/255px-Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c3/Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg/340px-Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dollhouse-epitaphs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of <i>Dollhouse Epitaphs</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the 2010 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">San Diego Comic-Con International</a>, it was announced that a comic book had been written by <a href="/wiki/Jed_Whedon" title="Jed Whedon">Jed Whedon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurissa_Tancharoen" title="Maurissa Tancharoen">Maurissa Tancharoen</a>. The book, titled <i>Dollhouse Epitaphs</i>, features a new storyline to bridge the gap between the main series and "<a href="/wiki/Epitaph_One" title="Epitaph One">Epitaph One</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Epitaph_Two:_Return" title="Epitaph Two: Return">Epitaph Two: Return</a>". It was drawn by <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richards" title="Cliff Richards">Cliff Richards</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Dark_Horse_Comics" title="Dark Horse Comics">Dark Horse Comics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book was a 24-page one-off limited edition, only available in the season two DVD or Blu-ray Disc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Early copies were released at the 2010 San Diego Comic-Con International for those who pre-ordered either the DVD or Blu-ray Disc at the event. Both Jed Whedon and Maurissa Tancharoen were available to sign the copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was later revealed at New York Comic-Con 2010 that there will be more comics that take place in the <i>Dollhouse</i> universe. A one-shot was released on March 30, 2011, and a miniseries began with the first issue released on July 13, 2011. The one-shot, which reprints the Season 2 exclusive with additional material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> is written by Jed Whedon and Maurissa Tancharoen and the miniseries is written by Andrew Chambliss. The comics are set in a future Los Angeles after the Dollhouse technology has reduced the city to ruins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The miniseries was later published in a trade paperback collection released on April 11, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viral_marketing_campaign">Viral marketing campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Viral marketing campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="337" data-file-width="312" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg/170px-Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg/255px-Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5f/Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dollhouse_Season_1_Poster.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Promotional posters featuring <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Dushku" title="Eliza Dushku">Eliza Dushku</a> as <a href="/wiki/Echo_(Dollhouse)" title="Echo (Dollhouse)">Echo</a> surrounded by blank <a href="/wiki/Mannequin" title="Mannequin">mannequins</a> were used to advertise <i>Dollhouse</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting" title="Fox Broadcasting">Fox</a> prior to the series' premiere.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On February 12, 2009, Fox launched <i>Dollplay,</i> a <a href="/wiki/Alternate_reality_game" title="Alternate reality game">participation drama</a> centered around <i>Dollhouse</i>. It involved using interactive webisodes and a user forum to drive a viral marketing campaign. The campaign asked users on the Fox <i>Dollhouse</i> website to "Save Hazel!" Hazel was a character trapped inside the Dollhouse in real-time. The campaign was called "Dollplay" according to the official Fox press release and was created by <a href="/wiki/The_company_P" title="The company P">the company P</a> "a radical production outfit from Sweden".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another viral marketing campaign was launched in November 2009 when the series was on hiatus. The campaign gave background on the Rossum Corporation, the technology company behind the Dollhouse, and offered clues as to how the apocalyptic future begins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollhouse_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Season one of <i>Dollhouse</i> had mixed reviews, with <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> giving it a rating of 57 out of a possible 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Ellen Gray of <i><a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Daily_News" title="Philadelphia Daily News">Philadelphia Daily News</a></i> gave a positive review, remarking that "<i>Dollhouse</i> is less about the ninja kicks and witty banter than it is about instant transformations, and about making the audience care about a character who's likely to behave differently every time we see her. That Dushku mostly pulls this off is a happy surprise, as is <i>Dollhouse</i>, which has survived <i>Firefly</i>-like trials of its own to get this far."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon.com</a> reviewer Heather Havrilesky was also positive, commenting that the show's combination of mystery, sly dialogue, and steady flow of action results in a "provocative, bubbly new drama that looks as promising as anything to hit the small screen over the course of the past year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternately, Tom Shales of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> wrote that the premise was "admittedly intriguing", but described the series as a "pretentious and risible jumble" and that Echo did not "inspire much concern or interest in the audience". He commented that the actors seemed to struggle due to the decor being so "outlandish", stating that it "simply isn't worth the trouble".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Brian Lowry of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> also wrote "<a href="/wiki/Eliza_Dushku" title="Eliza Dushku">Dushku</a>'s grasp of this vague, personality-changing character is a bit of a muddle. What's left, then, is a series with a hollow center that doesn't initially make you care about its mentally malleable protagonist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Robert Bianco of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> had a more nonchalant view of the series, describing <i>Dollhouse</i> as not boring or ordinary, and that the end result is a show "that <a href="/wiki/Joss_Whedon" title="Joss Whedon">Joss Whedon</a>'s most devoted fans will debate and embrace, and a mass audience just won't get".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many critics felt that the series' first season improved as it progressed. <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> Reviewer Eric Goldman believed the show became much stronger and more compelling with the episodes "Needs" and "A Spy in the House of Love". He opined of the later episodes that, "As a whole this show is definitely working better as we get away from Echo's missions of the week, and from focusing so much on just Echo and letting there be more of a true ensemble feel, with the time split amongst the Dolls."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Sarah Hughes of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> was unimpressed with the first five episodes but also found that the later episodes became "as involving and addictive as Whedon's best work".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep090515_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-indep090515-68">[68]</a></sup> Maureen Ryan of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> liked <i>Dollhouse</i>'s "unsettling" tone and found the show to be "unexpectedly moving and complex" during the second half of the first season. She called the second season renewal "a good day for unconventional television".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ct090515_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-ct090515-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
