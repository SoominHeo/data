<p>A <i><b>dong</b></i> or <b>neighborhood</b> is a submunicipal level administrative unit of a <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_South_Korea" title="List of cities in South Korea">city</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and of those <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_South_Korea" title="List of cities in South Korea">cities</a> which are not divided into wards throughout <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. The unit is often translated as neighborhood and has been used in both administrative divisions of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_South_Korea">In South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dong_(administrative_division)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>dong</i> is the smallest level of urban government to have its own office and staff in South Korea. There are two types of <i>dong</i>: <b>legal-status neighborhood</b>(법정동) and <b>administrative neighborhood</b>(행정동). In some cases, a single <b>legal-status neighborhood</b> is divided into several <b>administrative neighborhoods</b>. In such cases, each administrative <i>dong</i> has its own office and staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncyKorea_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EncyKorea-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doosan_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doosan-7">[7]</a></sup> Administrative dongs are usually distinguished from one another by number (as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Myeongjang-dong" title="Myeongjang-dong">Myeongjang</a> 1-dong and Myeongjang 2-dong).</p>
<p>The primary division of a <i>dong</i> is the <i>tong</i> (통/<span lang="ko">統</span>), but divisions at this level and below are seldom used in daily life. Some <i>dong</i> are subdivided into <i>ga</i> (가/<span lang="ko">街</span>), which are not a separate level of government, but only exist for use in addresses. Many major thoroughfares in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suwon" title="Suwon">Suwon</a>, and other cities are also subdivided into <i>ga</i>. Basically <i>ga</i> stands for a historical reason: in <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Korea under Japanese rule</a>, some districts living Japanese are subdivided as <i>machi</i> (<span lang="ja">町</span>, <span lang="ja">まち</span>) and after Independence of Korea, these <b>machi</b> are renamed as <i>ga</i>.</p>
