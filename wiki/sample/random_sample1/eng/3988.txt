<p>The <b>Winter <a href="/wiki/Paralympic_Games" title="Paralympic Games">Paralympic Games</a></b> is an international <a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">multi-sport event</a> where athletes with physical disabilities compete. This includes athletes with mobility disabilities, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amputations" title="Amputations">amputations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>. The Winter Paralympic Games are held every four years directly following the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Olympic_Games" title="Winter Olympic Games">Winter Olympic Games</a>. The Winter Paralympics are also hosted by the city that hosted the Winter Olympics. The <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a> (IPC) oversees the Winter Paralympics. Medals are awarded in each event: with gold medals for first place, silver for second and bronze for third, following the tradition that the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> started in 1904.</p>
<p>The Winter Paralympics began in <a href="/wiki/1976_Winter_Paralympics" title="1976 Winter Paralympics">1976</a> in <a href="/wiki/%C3%96rnsk%C3%B6ldsvik" title="Örnsköldsvik">Örnsköldsvik</a>, Sweden. Those Games were the first Paralympics (Summer or Winter) that featured athletes other than wheelchair athletes. The Games have expanded and grown to be (along with the <a href="/wiki/Summer_Paralympic_Games" title="Summer Paralympic Games">Summer Games</a>) part of the largest international sporting event after the Olympic Games. Given their expansion the need for a very specific classification system has arisen. This system has also given rise to controversy and opened the door for cheating. Winter Paralympians have also been convicted of steroid use and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_dysreflexia" title="Autonomic dysreflexia">cheating</a> unique to Paralympic athletes, which has tainted the integrity of the Games.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_Paralympic_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of the Winter Paralympics are similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summer_Paralympics" title="Summer Paralympics">Summer Paralympics</a>. Injured soldiers returning from World War II sought sports as an avenue to healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phistory_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-phistory-1">[1]</a></sup> Organized by Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Guttmann" title="Ludwig Guttmann">Ludwig Guttmann</a>, sports competitions between British convalescent hospitals began in 1948 and continued until 1960 when a parallel Olympics was held in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> after the <a href="/wiki/1960_Summer_Olympics" title="1960 Summer Olympics">1960 Summer Olympics</a>. Over 400 wheelchair athletes competed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1960_Paralympic_Games" title="1960 Paralympic Games">1960 Paralympic Games</a>, which became known as the first Paralympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phistory_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-phistory-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sepp Zwicknagl, a pioneer of snow sports for disabled athletes, was a double-leg amputee Austrian skier who experimented skiing using prosthetics. His work helped pioneer technological advances for people with disabilities who wished to participate in winter sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-origins-2">[2]</a></sup> Advances were slow and it was not until 1974 that the first official world ski competition for physically impaired athletes, featuring downhill and a cross-country skiing, was held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-origins-2">[2]</a></sup> The first Winter Paralympics were held in <a href="/wiki/1976_Winter_Paralympics" title="1976 Winter Paralympics">1976</a> at <a href="/wiki/%C3%96rnsk%C3%B6ldsvik" title="Örnsköldsvik">Örnsköldsvik</a>, Sweden from February 21–28. Alpine and Nordic skiing for amputees and visually impaired athletes where the main events but ice sledge racing was included as a demonstration event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-origins-2">[2]</a></sup> There were 198 participating athletes from 16 countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and it was the first time athletes with impairments other than wheelchair athletes were permitted to compete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in <a href="/wiki/1988_Summer_Paralympics" title="1988 Summer Paralympics">1988</a> the Summer Paralympics were held in the same host city as the <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympic_Games" title="Summer Olympic Games">Summer Olympic Games</a>. This was due to an agreement reached between the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC) and the <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a> (IPC). The <a href="/wiki/1992_Winter_Paralympics" title="1992 Winter Paralympics">1992 Winter Paralympics</a> were the first Winter Games to use the same facilities as the <a href="/wiki/1992_Winter_Olympics" title="1992 Winter Olympics">Winter Olympics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cheating">Cheating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_Paralympic_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cheating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cheating_at_the_Paralympic_Games" title="Cheating at the Paralympic Games">Cheating at the Paralympic Games</a></div>
<p>Athletes have cheated by over-representing impairment to have a competitive advantage, and the use of performance-enhancing drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> German skier Thomas Oelsner became the first Winter Paralympian to test positive for steroids in <a href="/wiki/2002_Winter_Paralympics" title="2002 Winter Paralympics">2002</a>. He had won two gold medals in the alpine events but was stripped of his medals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> One concern now facing Paralympic officials is the technique of boosting blood pressure, known as <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_dysreflexia" title="Autonomic dysreflexia">autonomic dysreflexia</a>. The increase in blood pressure can improve performance by 15% and is most effective in the endurance sports such as <a href="/wiki/Paralympic_cross-country_skiing" title="Paralympic cross-country skiing">cross-country skiing</a>. To increase blood pressure athletes will deliberately cause trauma to limbs below a spinal injury. This trauma can include breaking bones, strapping extremities in too tightly and using high-pressured compression stockings. The injury is painless to the athlete but affects the body and impacts the athlete's blood pressure, as can techniques like allowing the bladder to overfill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disability_categories">Disability categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_Paralympic_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disability categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IPC has established six disability categories applying to both the Summer and Winter Paralympics. Athletes with one of these physical disabilities are able to compete in the Paralympics though not every sport can allow for every disability category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-categories_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-categories-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Amputee</b>: Athletes with a <a href="/wiki/Amputation" title="Amputation">partial or total loss</a> of at least one <a href="/wiki/Limb_(anatomy)" title="Limb (anatomy)">limb</a>.</li>
<li><b>Cerebral Palsy</b>: Athletes with non-progressive <a href="/wiki/Brain_damage" title="Brain damage">brain damage</a>, for example <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> or similar disabilities affecting muscle control, <a href="/wiki/Sense_of_balance" title="Sense of balance">balance</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_(physiology)" title="Coordination (physiology)">coordination</a>.</li>
<li><b>Intellectual Disability</b>: Athletes with a significant <a href="/wiki/Developmental_disability" title="Developmental disability">impairment in intellectual functioning</a> and associated limitations in adaptive behavior.</li>
<li><b>Wheelchair</b>: Athletes with <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord_injury" title="Spinal cord injury">spinal cord injuries</a> and other disabilities which require them to compete in a <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair" title="Wheelchair">wheelchair</a>.</li>
<li><b>Visually Impaired</b>: Athletes with <a href="/wiki/Visual_impairment" title="Visual impairment">vision impairment</a> ranging from partial vision, sufficient to be judged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_blindness" title="Legal blindness">legally blind</a>, to total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Les Autres</b></i>: Athletes with a physical disability that does not fall strictly under one of the other five categories, such as <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">dwarfism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital</a> <a href="/wiki/Deformity" title="Deformity">deformities</a> of the limbs such as that caused by <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a> (the name for this category is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> for "the others").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-categories_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-categories-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classifications">Classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_Paralympic_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Within the six disability categories the athletes still need to be divided according to their level of impairment. The classification systems differ from sport to sport. The systems are designed to open up Paralympic sports to as many athletes as possible, who can participate in fair competitions against athletes with similar levels of ability. The closest equivalents in able-bodied competitions are age classifications in junior sports, and weight divisions in wrestling, boxing, and weightlifting. Classifications vary in accordance with the different skills required to perform the sport. The biggest challenge in the classification system is how to account for the wide variety and severity of disabilities. As a result, there will always be a range of impairment within a classification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-classdescribed_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-classdescribed-10">[10]</a></sup> What follows is a list of the Winter Paralympic sports and a general description of how they are classified.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Alpine_skiing_at_the_Winter_Paralympics" title="Alpine skiing at the Winter Paralympics">Alpine skiing</a></b>: There are two events in alpine skiing: slalom and giant slalom. Alpine skiing accommodates athletes with the following physical limitations: spinal injury, Cerebral Palsy, amputation, <i>Les Autres</i> and blindness/visual impairment. There are eleven classifications, seven for standing athletes, three for sitting athletes, and three for visually impaired athletes. The divisions are defined by the degree of the athletes' function and the need for assistive equipment (prosthesis, ski poles, etc.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Snowboard Cross is technically now included in this category, though competition will take place with only limited classifications (see below).</p>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paralympic_Biathlon" title="Paralympic Biathlon">Biathlon</a></b>: Biathlon is a combination of cross-country skiing with target shooting. It requires physical stamina and accurate shooting. The events are open to athletes with physical disabilities and visual impairments. There are fifteen classes in which athletes will be placed depending on their level of function. Twelve divisions are for athletes with a physical impairment and three divisions are for athletes with a visual impairment. The athletes compete together and their finishing times are entered into a formula with their disability class to determine the athletes' over all finish order. Visually impaired athletes are able to compete through the use of acoustic signals. The signal intensity varies depending upon whether or not the athlete is on target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Paralympic_cross-country_skiing" title="Paralympic cross-country skiing">Cross-country skiing</a></b>: Cross-country skiing, also known as Nordic skiing is open to athletes with Cerebral Palsy, amputations, the need for a wheelchair, visual impairment and intellectual impairment. There are fifteen classifications, three for visually impaired athletes, nine for standing athletes and three for seated athletes. The divisions are determined in a similar fashion to alpine skiing with attention given to the athletes' level of function and need for assistive devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Ice_sledge_hockey_at_the_Winter_Paralympics" title="Ice sledge hockey at the Winter Paralympics">Ice Sledge Hockey</a></b>: Ice sledge hockey is open only to male competitors with a physical disability in the lower part of their body. The game is played using international hockey rules with some modifications. Athletes sit on sledges with two blades that allow the puck to go beneath the sledge. They also use two sticks, which have a spike-end for pushing and a blade-end for shooting. The athletes are classified into three groups: group 1 is for athletes with no sitting balance or with major impairment in both upper and lower limbs, group 2 is for athletes with some sitting balance and moderate impairment in their extremities and athletes in group 3 have good balance and mild impairment in their upper and lower limbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Wheelchair_curling_at_the_Winter_Paralympics" title="Wheelchair curling at the Winter Paralympics">Wheelchair curling</a></b>: Wheelchair curling is a coed team event for athletes with permanent lower limb disabilities that require them to use a wheelchair in their daily lives. Athletes with Cerebral Palsy or Multiple Sclerosis can also play if they use a wheelchair. Delivery of the stone can be by hand release or the use of a pole. There are no classifications in this event except the requirement that all athletes participating must have need for a wheelchair for daily mobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Para-snowboarding_classification" title="Para-snowboarding classification">Para-snowboarding</a></b>: On 2 May 2012, the International Paralympic Committee officially sanctioned "para-snowboarding" (commonly known as <i>adaptive snowboarding</i>) as a medal event in the 2014 Winter Paralympic Games under Alpine Skiing. There will be men's and women's standing snowboard-cross competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The IPC currently recognizes two broader sport classes, one for competitors with lower-limb impairments and one for those with upper-limb impairments. Visually impaired classes are not currently recognized and the sport's debut in the 2014 Sochi Paralympics will feature events for only athletes with lower-limb impairments, who will be permitted to wear a prosthesis. The events will be run in a time trial format (one rider on course at a time), and results within each broad class calculated <i>without</i> factors that adjust raw times based on disability classification (for example, a hypothetical athlete with a single above-knee amputation <i>will not receive any adjustment</i> to his or her start-to-finish time, even though the lack of a knee and functional quadriceps in one leg can result in an impairment much greater than a hypothetical athlete with a single below-knee amputation but two functional quadriceps). However, as the sport develops, the classes will be expanded and/or refined in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
