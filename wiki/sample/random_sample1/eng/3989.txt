<div class="hatnote" role="note">This is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">family name</a> is <a href="/wiki/Tong_(surname)" title="Tong (surname)">Tong</a>.</div>
<p><b>Tong Guan</b> (1054–1126), <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> <b>Daofu</b> (道夫), was a court <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">eunuch</a>, military general, political adviser, and state councillor to <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Huizong_of_Song" title="Emperor Huizong of Song">Emperor Huizong</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>. In the <i><a href="/wiki/Water_Margin" title="Water Margin">Water Margin</a></i>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Great_Classical_Novels" title="Four Great Classical Novels">Four Great Classical Novels</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">Chinese literature</a>, Tong Guan is featured as an antagonist and enemy of the 108 <a href="/wiki/Mount_Liang" title="Mount Liang">Liangshan</a> outlaws. He appears mainly in the later chapters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tong_Guan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tong Guan began his military career under the mentorship of a leading eunuch general of the 1080s and became one of the few eunuch generals of the Song dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite being a eunuch, it was written by many that Tong Guan had strong personal character and was in peak physical condition, with a long beard that was considered unusual for eunuchs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> After Tong Guan gained his first victory in battle in 1104, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Huizong_of_Song" title="Emperor Huizong of Song">Emperor Huizong</a> granted him a valuable document of his own calligraphy written in his unique "slender gold" style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> After Tong Guan gained a greater reputation by commanding a series of victorious battles in the northwest against the <a href="/wiki/Tangut_people" title="Tangut people">Tanguts</a>, he became the favourite general and advisor of Emperor Huizong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1111, he went on a diplomatic mission to the <a href="/wiki/Khitan_people" title="Khitan people">Khitan</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Liao_dynasty" title="Liao dynasty">Liao dynasty</a> to the northeast, and after this his career steadily advanced. The following year, he was promoted to the top of the military command structure and by 1116 he was the first eunuch in the Song dynasty to gain entry into the top echelons of central administration as one of the chief policy-makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> Tong Guan was involved in all of the elite social gatherings of the time, as his name appeared as a guest on numerous lists for organised parties and banquets hosted by Emperor Huizong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> He was also one of only ten palace eunuchs who had their biographies as painters compiled and written for the court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the year 1118, Tong Guan suggested to Emperor Huizong that a military alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_people" title="Jurchen people">Jurchens</a> would be favourable in crushing the Liao dynasty once and for all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_165_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_165-2">[2]</a></sup> Emperor Huizong agreed, despite some protest by other ministers at court. In a secret alliance and mission of envoys across the borders, Tong Guan played a leading role in the agreement that was reached between the Jurchens and the Song dynasty to divide the Liao dynasty's territory (while the Song would ultimately obtain their coveted prize: the <a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Prefectures" title="Sixteen Prefectures">Sixteen Prefectures</a>). In 1120, at the age of 66, Tong Guan was put in command of an army to begin the assault on the Liao dynasty's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liao_Nanjing" title="Liao Nanjing">southern capital, Yanjing</a>. However, the campaign was halted for a time when word came to Tong Guan's camp that a revolt had broken out within the Song Empire, <a href="/wiki/Fang_La" title="Fang La">Fang La</a>'s rebellion in <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang" title="Zhejiang">Zhejiang</a>. His army was forced to march several hundred miles south to Zhejiang in order to suppress this rebellion. After successfully quelling this rebellion, his army marched back north but was routed in battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> Shortly after this, the Jurchens defeated the Liao dynasty at Yanjing and occupied the city. The city of Yanjing was turned over to Song forces only after a substantial payment was made to the Jurchens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> Due to his losses and inability to take Yanjing, when Tong Guan returned to Kaifeng, he was forced to retire from his post as commander.</p>
<p>Although he was earlier forced to retire, in 1124, Tong Guan was called back into military service by Emperor Huizong, who trusted no other general more than Tong Guan to head the mission across the northern border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_166_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_166-1">[1]</a></sup> However, in the last month of 1125, Tong Guan fled across the border back to Kaifeng to deliver the ill-fated news that the Jurchens had begun an <a href="/wiki/Jin%E2%80%93Song_Wars" title="Jin–Song Wars">invasion of the Song dynasty</a>. Tong Guan was made the leader of Emperor Huizong's personal bodyguard after the emperor abdicated the throne and fled from Kaifeng. Tong Guan was later blamed for much of the disaster that befell the Song dynasty when the Jurchens conquered northern China. Emperor Huizong's successor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Qinzong_of_Song" title="Emperor Qinzong of Song">Emperor Qinzong</a>, had Tong Guan executed.</p>
