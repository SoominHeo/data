<p><b>Cave diving</b> is <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">underwater diving</a> in water-filled <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>. It may be done as an extreme sport, a way of exploring flooded caves for scientific investigation, or for the search for and recovery of divers lost while diving for one of these reasons. The equipment used varies depending on the circumstances, and ranges from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-diving" title="Free-diving">breath hold</a> to <a href="/wiki/Surface-supplied_diving" title="Surface-supplied diving">surface supplied</a>, but almost all cave diving is done using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba_equipment" title="Scuba equipment">scuba equipment</a>, often in specialised configurations. Recreational cave diving is generally considered to be a type of <a href="/wiki/Technical_diving" title="Technical diving">technical diving</a> due to the lack of a free surface during large parts of the dive, and often involves <a href="/wiki/Decompression_(diving)" title="Decompression (diving)">decompression</a>.</p>
<p>In the United Kingdom it originated, and to a large degree remains, an extension of the more common activity of <a href="/wiki/Caving" title="Caving">caving</a>, and in the United States an extension of the more common sport of scuba diving. Compared to caving and scuba diving, there are relatively few practitioners of cave diving. This is due in part to the specialized equipment and skill sets required, and in part because of the high potential risks due to the specific environment.</p>
<p>Despite these risks, water-filled caves attract scuba divers, <a href="/wiki/Caving" title="Caving">cavers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Speleology" title="Speleology">speleologists</a> due to their often unexplored nature, and present <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">divers</a> with a technical diving challenge. Underwater caves have a wide range of physical features, and can contain <a href="/wiki/Stygofauna" title="Stygofauna">fauna</a> not found elsewhere.</p>

<p>The procedures of cave diving have much in common with procedures used for other types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penetration_diving" title="Penetration diving">penetration diving</a>. They differ from open water procedures mainly in the emphasis on navigation, gas management, operating in confined spaces, and that the diver is physically constrained from direct ascent to the surface during much of the dive.</p>
<p>As most cave diving is done in an environment where there is no free surface with breathable air allowing an above-water exit, it is critically important to be able to find the way out before the breathing gas runs out. This is ensured by the use of a continuous guideline between the dive team and outside of the flooded cave, and diligent planning and monitoring of gas supplies. Two basic types of guideline are used: permanent lines, and temporary lines. Permanent lines may include a main line starting near the entrance/exit, and side lines or branch lines, and are <a href="/wiki/Line_marker" title="Line marker">marked</a> to indicate the direction to the nearest exit. Temporary lines include exploration lines and jump lines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Decompression procedures may take into account that the cave diver usually follows a very rigidly constrained and precisely defined route, both into and out of the cave, and can reasonably expect to find any equipment such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_cylinders&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drop cylinders (page does not exist)">drop cylinders</a> temporarily stored along the guideline while making the exit. In some caves, changes of depth of the cave along the dive route will constrain decompression depths, and gas mixtures and decompression schedules can be tailored to take this into account.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skills">Skills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Skills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most open-water diving skills apply to cave diving, and there are additional skills specific to the environment, and to the chosen equipment configuration.</p>
<ul>
<li>Good buoyancy control, trim and finning technique help preserve visibility in areas with silt deposits.</li>
<li>The ability to navigate in total darkness using the guideline to find the way out is a safety critical emergency skill. Line management skills required for cave diving include laying and recovering <a href="/wiki/Distance_line" title="Distance line">guide lines</a> using a reel, tie-offs, the use of a jump line to cross gaps or find a lost guide line in silted out conditions, identifying the direction along the guideline leading to the exit, and the skills of dealing with a break in a guideline.</li>
<li>Emergency skills for dealing with gas supply problems are complicated by the possibility of the emergency occurring in a confined space and low visibility or darkness.</li>
</ul>

<p>Cave diving training includes equipment selection and configuration, guideline protocols and techniques, gas management protocols, communication techniques, propulsion techniques, emergency management protocols, and psychological education.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Cave diver training also stresses the importance of risk management and cave conservation ethics. Most training systems offer progressive stages of education and certification.</p>
<ul>
<li>Cavern training covers the basic skills needed to enter the overhead environment. Training will generally consist of gas planning, propulsion techniques needed to deal with the silty environments in many caves, reel and handling, and communication. Once certified as a cavern diver, a diver may undertake cavern diving with a cavern or cave certified "buddy," as well as continue into cave diving training.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Introduction into cave training builds on the techniques learned during cavern training and includes the training needed to penetrate beyond the cavern zone and working with permanent guidelines that exist in many caves. Once intro to cave certified, a diver may penetrate much further into a cave, usually limited by 1/3 of a single cylinder, or in the case of a basic cave certification, 1/6 of double cylinders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An intro cave diver is usually not certified to do complex navigation.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apprentice" title="Apprentice">Apprentice</a> cave training serves as the transition from intro to full certification and includes the training needed to penetrate deep into caves working from permanent guide lines as well as limited exposure to side lines that exist in many caves. Training covers complex dive planning and decompression procedures used for longer dives. Once apprentice certified, a diver may penetrate much further into a cave, usually limited by 1/3 of double cylinders. An apprentice diver is also allowed to do a single jump or gap (a break in the guideline from two sections of mainline or between mainline and sideline) during the dive. An apprentice diver typically has one year to finish full cave or must repeat the apprentice stage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Full cave training serves as the final level of basic training and includes the training needed to penetrate deep into the cave working from both permanent guidelines and sidelines, and may plan and complete complex dives deep into a system using decompression to stay longer. Once cave certified, a diver may penetrate much further into a cave, usually limited by 1/3 of double cylinders. A cave diver is also certified as competent to do multiple jumps or gaps (a break in the guideline from two sections of mainline or between mainline and sideline) during the dive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cave diving is one of the most challenging and potentially dangerous kinds of diving or caving and presents many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diving_hazards_and_precautions" title="Diving hazards and precautions">hazards</a>. Cave diving is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penetration_diving" title="Penetration diving">penetration diving</a>, meaning that in an emergency a diver cannot swim vertically to the surface due to the cave's ceilings, and so must swim the entire way back out. The underwater navigation through the cave system may be difficult and exit routes may be at considerable distance, requiring the diver to have sufficient <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> to make the journey. The dive may also be deep, resulting in potential <a href="/wiki/Deep_diving" title="Deep diving">deep diving</a> risks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Visibility can vary from nearly unlimited to low, or non-existent, and can go from one extreme to the other in a single dive. While a less-intensive kind of diving called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavern_diving" title="Cavern diving">cavern diving</a> does not take divers beyond the reach of natural light (and typically no deeper than 100 ft, and penetration not further than 200 ft), true cave diving can involve penetrations of many thousands of feet, well beyond the reach of sunlight. The level of darkness experienced creates an environment impossible to see in without an artificial form of light. Caves often contain sand, mud, clay, silt, or other sediment that can further reduce underwater visibility in seconds when stirred up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Caves can carry strong water currents. Most caves emerge on the surface as either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">springs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Siphon#Geology" title="Siphon">siphons</a>. Springs have out flowing currents, where water is coming up out of the Earth and flowing out across the land's surface. Siphons have in-flowing currents where, for example, an above-ground river is going underground. Some caves are complex and have some tunnels with out-flowing currents, and other tunnels with in-flowing currents. If currents are not properly managed, they can cause serious problems for the diver.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Cave diving has been perceived as one of the more deadly sports in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> This perception may be exaggerated because the majority of divers who have lost their lives in caves have either not undergone specialized training or have had inadequate equipment for the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> Some cave divers have suggested that cave diving is in fact statistically much safer than recreational diving due to the much larger barriers imposed by experience, training, and equipment cost,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> but there is no definitive statistical evidence for this claim.</p>
<p>There is no reliable worldwide database listing all cave diving fatalities. Such fractional statistics as are available, however, suggest that very few divers have ever died while following accepted protocols and while using equipment configurations recognized as acceptable by the cave diving community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> In the very rare cases of exceptions to this rule there have typically been unusual circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> Much the same can be said for other modes and applications of diving. There is little doubt that competence, good preparation and appropriate equipment reduce the risk of all hazardous activities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Most cave divers recognize five general rules or contributing factors for safe cave diving, which were popularized, adapted and became generally accepted from <a href="/wiki/Sheck_Exley" title="Sheck Exley">Sheck Exley</a>'s 1979 publication <i>Basic Cave Diving: A Blueprint for Survival</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> In this book, Exley included accounts of actual cave diving accidents, and followed each one with a breakdown of what factors contributed to the accident. Despite the unique circumstances of each individual accident, Exley found that at least one of a small number of major factors contributed to each one. This technique for breaking down accident reports and finding common causes among them is now called <i>Accident Analysis</i>, and is taught in introductory cave diving courses. Exley outlined a number of these resulting cave diving rules, but today these five are the most recognized:</p>
<ul>
<li>Training: A safe cave diver does not intentionally exceed the scope of his or her training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> Cave diving is normally taught in stages, each successive stage focusing on more complex aspects of cave diving. Each stage of training is intended to be reinforced with actual cave diving experience to develop competence before starting training at a more complex level. Accident analysis of cave diving fatalities has shown<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that academic training without sufficient real world experience is not always enough in the event of an underwater emergency. By systematically building experience<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the diver can develop the confidence, motor skills and reflexes to remain calm and apply the appropriate procedures in an emergency. An inexperienced diver is more likely to panic than an experienced diver when confronted with a similar situation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Distance_line" title="Distance line">Guide line</a>: A continuous guide line is maintained at all times between the leader of a dive team and a fixed point selected outside the cave entrance in open water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> Often this line is tied off a second time as a backup directly inside the cavern zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GUEguideline2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GUEguideline2-2">[2]</a></sup> As the dive leader lays the guideline he takes great care to ensure there is appropriate tension on the line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GUEguideline2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GUEguideline2-2">[2]</a></sup> and that it does not go into <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_underwater_diving_terminology#line_trap" title="Glossary of underwater diving terminology">line traps</a>. Other team members remain between the lead diver and the exit, in easy reach of the line at all times. If a <a href="/wiki/Silt_out" title="Silt out">silt out</a> occurs, divers can find the line and follow it back to the cave entrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GUEguideline2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-GUEguideline2-2">[2]</a></sup> Failure to use a continuous guide line to open water is cited as the most frequent cause of fatality among untrained, non-certified divers who venture into caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Depth rules: Gas consumption, nitrogen narcosis and decompression obligation increase with depth, and the effects of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis" title="Nitrogen narcosis">nitrogen narcosis</a> may be more critical in a cave due to the high task loading and presence of combinations of hazards. Cave divers are advised not to dive to depths exceeding the planned depth and the applicable range of their equipment and the breathing gases in use, and to keep in mind this effective difference between open water depth and cave depth. Excessive depth is frequently cited as a contributory factor in fatal incidents involving fully trained cave divers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scuba_gas_planning" title="Scuba gas planning">Breathing gas management</a>: The breathing gas supply must last the diver until out of the overhead environment. There are several strategies for gas management. The most common protocol is the '<a href="/wiki/Rule_of_thirds_(diving)" title="Rule of thirds (diving)">rule of thirds</a>,' in which one third of the initial gas supply is used for ingress, one third for egress, and one third to support another team member in the case of an emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bozanic_1977_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bozanic_1977-3">[3]</a></sup> This is a very simple method, but is not always sufficient. UK practice is to adhere to the rule of thirds, but with an added emphasis on keeping depletion of the separate air systems "balanced," so that the complete loss of any single gas supply will still leave the diver with sufficient gas to return safely. The rule of thirds makes no allowance for increased air consumption that the stress caused by the loss of an air system may induce. Dissimilar tank sizes among the divers are also not allowed for by the rule of thirds, and a sufficient reserve should be calculated for each dive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mount_2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mount_2008-4">[4]</a></sup> UK practice is to assume that each diver is completely independent, as in a typical UK sump there is usually nothing that a buddy can do to assist a diver in trouble. Most UK cave divers <a href="/wiki/Solo_diving" title="Solo diving">dive solo</a>. US sump divers follow a similar protocol.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The rule of thirds was devised as an approach to diving Florida's caves<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> - they typically have high outflow currents, which help to reduce air consumption when exiting. In a cave system with little or no outflow it is prudent to reserve more air than is provided by the rule of thirds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gurr_and_Mount_2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gurr_and_Mount_2008-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lights: Each cave diver should have at least three independent sources of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> One is considered the primary and is intended for general use during the dive. The others are considered backup lights and may be lower powered as they are not intended for exploration. Each light must have an expected burn time of at least the planned duration of the dive. If any diver loses light function so that they have fewer than three working lights, protocol requires that the dive is turned for all members of the dive team and exit started immediately.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Cave divers are taught to remember the five key components with the <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">mnemonic</a>: "<i><b>T</b>he <b>G</b>ood <b>D</b>ivers <b>A</b>lways <b>L</b>ive</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years new contributing factors were considered after reviewing accidents involving solo diving, diving with incapable dive partners, video or photography in caves, complex cave dives and cave diving in large groups. With the establishment of technical diving, the use of mixed gases—such as <a href="/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)" title="Trimix (breathing gas)">trimix</a> for bottom gas, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">nitrox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for decompression—reduces the margin for error. Accident analysis suggests that breathing the wrong gas for the depth or not analyzing the breathing gas properly has also led to cave diving accidents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Cave diving requires a variety of specialized procedures, and divers who do not correctly apply these procedures may significantly increase the risk to the members of their team. The cave diving community works hard to educate the public on the risks they assume when they enter water-filled caves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Warning signs with the likenesses of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grim_Reaper" title="Grim Reaper">Grim Reaper</a> have been placed just inside the openings of many popular caves in the US, and others have been placed in nearby parking lots and local dive shops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSS_sign_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSS_sign-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many cave diving sites around the world include open-water basins, which are popular open-water diving sites. The management of these sites try to minimize the risk of untrained divers being tempted to venture inside the cave systems. With the support of the cave diving community, many of these sites enforce a "no-lights rule" for divers who lack cave training—they may not carry any lights into the water with them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is easy to venture into an underwater cave with a light and not realize how far away from the entrance (and daylight) one has swum; this rule is based on the theory that, without a light, divers will not venture beyond daylight.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the early phases of cave diving the analysis shows that 90% of accidents were <i>not</i> trained cave divers; from the 2000s on the trend has reversed to 80% of accidents involving trained cave divers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Modern cave divers' capability and available technology allows divers to venture well beyond traditional training limits<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and into actual exploration. The result is an increase of cave diving accidents, in 2011 the yearly average of 2.5 fatalities a year tripled.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2012 fatalities reached the highest annual rate to that date at over 20.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As response to the increase in fatalities during the years 2010 onwards, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Diving_Research_and_Exploration_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Diving Research and Exploration Organization (page does not exist)">International Diving Research and Exploration Organization</a> (IDREO) was created in order to "bring awareness of the current safety situation of Cave Diving" by listing current worldwide accidents by year and promoting a community discussion and analysis of accidents through a "Cave Diver Safety Meeting" held annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAP_1995_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAP_1995-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Equipment used by cave divers ranges from fairly standard recreational scuba configurations, to more complex arrangements which allow more freedom of movement in confined spaces, extended range in terms of depth and time, allowing greater distances to be covered in acceptable safety, and equipment which helps with navigation, in what are usually dark, and often silty and convoluted spaces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Scuba configurations which are more often found in cave diving than in open water diving include independent or manifolded twin cylinder rigs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side_mount_diving" title="Side mount diving">side-mount</a> harnesses, <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder#Open_circuit" title="Diving cylinder">sling cylinders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rebreathers" title="Rebreathers">rebreathers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backplate_and_wing" title="Backplate and wing">backplate and wing</a> harnesses. <a href="/wiki/William_Stone_(caver)" title="William Stone (caver)">Bill Stone</a> designed and used <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a> composite tanks for exploration of the San Agustín and <a href="/wiki/Sistema_Huautla" title="Sistema Huautla">Sistema Huautla</a> caves in Mexico to decrease the weight for dry sections and vertical passages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StoneAAUS1986_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-StoneAAUS1986-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StoneAerospace_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-StoneAerospace-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_underwater_diving_terminology#stage_cylinder" title="Glossary of underwater diving terminology">Stage cylinders</a> are cylinders which are used to provide gas for a portion of the penetration. They may be deposited on the bottom at the guideline on preparation dives, to be picked up for use during the main dive, or may be carried by the divers and dropped off at the line during the penetration to be retrieved on the way out.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One of the high risk <a href="/wiki/List_of_diving_hazards_and_precautions" title="List of diving hazards and precautions">hazards</a> of cave diving is getting lost in the cave.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The use of <a href="/wiki/Distance_line" title="Distance line">guidelines</a> is the standard mitigation for this risk.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Guidelines may be permanent or laid and recovered during the dive, using <a href="/wiki/Distance_line" title="Distance line">cave reels</a> to deploy and recover the line. Permanent branch lines may be laid with a gap between the start of the branch line and the nearest point on the main line. <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_underwater_diving_terminology#Gap_spool" title="Glossary of underwater diving terminology">Gap spools</a> with a relatively short line are commonly used to make the <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_underwater_diving_terminology#jump" title="Glossary of underwater diving terminology">jump</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Line_marker" title="Line marker">Line arrows</a> are used to point towards the nearest exit, and <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_underwater_diving_terminology#cookie" title="Glossary of underwater diving terminology">cookies</a> are used to indicate use of a line by a team of divers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Silt screws are short lengths of rigid tube (usually plastic) with one sharpened end and a notch or slot at the other end to secure the line, which are pushed into the silt or detritus of the cave floor as a place to tie off a guideline when no suitable natural tie-off points are available.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diver_propulsion_vehicle" title="Diver propulsion vehicle">Diver propulsion vehicles</a>, or Scooters, are sometimes used to extend the range by reducing the work load on the diver and allowing faster travel in open sections of cave. Reliability of the diver propulsion vehicle is very important, as a failure could compromise the ability of the diver to exit the cave before running out of gas. Where this is a significant risk, divers may tow a spare scooter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dive_light" title="Dive light">Dive lights</a> are critical safety equipment, as it is dark inside caves. Each diver generally carries a primary light, and at least one backup light. A minimum of three lights is recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup> The primary light should last the planned duration of the dive, as should the backup lights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exley_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-exley-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The cave diving community is a global one, partly due to the highly specialised nature with the resulting small numbers of practitioners at a local level. Cave diving practice can differ markedly by locality.</p>
<p>One such difference is the use of a floating <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> guide line. Most cave divers in the U.S. balk at the use of any sort of floating guide line, 6 mm nylon line is the norm in UK and is regularly anchored to stones, lead weights, or whatever is needed these belays are laid clear of mud and silt. In continental Europe, thinner yet slightly buoyant line is typical. Cave diving practices in some localities may be different than those in other parts of the world because those caves require specialized techniques.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Consistency in signs and warnings also differs around the world. For example, warnings signs are rare in the UK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques-Yves_Cousteau" title="Jacques-Yves Cousteau">Jacques-Yves Cousteau</a>, co-inventor of the first commercially successful open circuit <a href="/wiki/Scuba_set" title="Scuba set">scuba equipment</a>, was the world's first open circuit scuba cave diver.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, many cave divers penetrated caves prior to the advent of scuba with surface supplied breathing apparatus through the use of umbilical hoses and compressors. Scuba diving in all its forms, including cave diving, has advanced in earnest since Cousteau introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqua-Lung" title="Aqua-Lung">Aqua-Lung</a> in 1943.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Two regions have had particular influence on cave diving techniques and equipment due to their very different cave diving environments. These are the United Kingdom, and USA, mainly Florida.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_history">UK history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: UK history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cave_Diving_Group" title="Cave Diving Group">Cave Diving Group</a> (CDG) was established informally in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in 1935 to organise training and equipment for the exploration of flooded caves in the <a href="/wiki/Mendip_Hills" title="Mendip Hills">Mendip Hills</a> of Somerset. The first dive was made by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Sheppard_(cave_diver)" title="Jack Sheppard (cave diver)">Jack Sheppard</a> on 4 October 1936,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheppard_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheppard-11">[11]</a></sup> using a home-made drysuit surface fed from a modified bicycle pump, which allowed Shepard to pass Sump 1 of <a href="/wiki/Swildon%27s_Hole" title="Swildon's Hole">Swildon's Hole</a>. Swildon's is an upstream feeder to the <a href="/wiki/Wookey_Hole_Caves" title="Wookey Hole Caves">Wookey Hole</a> resurgence system. The difficulty of access to the sump in Swildon's prompted operations to move to the resurgence, and the larger cave there allowed use of conventional "hard hat" equipment which was secured from the <a href="/wiki/Siebe_Gorman" title="Siebe Gorman">Siebe Gorman</a> company. The left photograph on the <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">standard diving dress</a> page will give some indication of the scale of operations this entailed. In UK cave diving, the term "<a href="/wiki/Sherpa_people" title="Sherpa people">Sherpa</a>" is used without a drop of irony for the people who carry the diver's gear although this has gone out of fashion; support is now more normally used, and before the development of SCUBA equipment such undertakings could be monumental operations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Diving in the spacious third chamber of Wookey Hole led to a rapid series of advances, each of which was dignified by being given a successive number, until an air surface was reached at what is now known as "Chamber 9." Some of these dives were broadcast live on <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> radio, which must have been a quite surreal experience for both diver and audience.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The number of sites where <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">standard diving dress</a> could be used is clearly limited and there was little further progress before the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> reduced the caving community considerably. However, the rapid development of underwater warfare through the war made a lot of surplus <a href="/wiki/Diving_equipment" title="Diving equipment">equipment</a> available. The CDG re-formed in 1946 and progress was rapid. Typical equipment at this time was a <a href="/wiki/Frogman" title="Frogman">frogman</a> rubber <a href="/wiki/Diving_suit" title="Diving suit">diving suit</a> for insulation (water temperature in the UK is typically 4 °C), an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soda_lime" title="Soda lime">soda lime</a> absorbent canister and counter-lung comprising a <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> air system and an "AFLOLAUN," meaning "Apparatus For Laying Out Line And Underwater Navigation." The AFLOLAUN consisted of lights, <a href="/wiki/Distance_line" title="Distance line">line-reel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a>, notebook (for the survey), batteries, and more.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Progress was typically by "bottom walking", as this was considered less dangerous than swimming (note the absence of buoyancy controls). The use of oxygen put a depth limit on the dive, which was considerably mitigated by the extended dive duration. This was the normal diving equipment and methods until approximately 1960 when new techniques using wetsuits (which provide both insulation and buoyancy ), twin open-circuit SCUBA air systems the development of side mounting cylinders, helmet-mounted lights and free-swimming with fins. The increasing capacity and pressure rating of air bottles also extended dive durations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farr_1991_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farr_1991-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_History">US History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: US History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, cave diving greatly increased in popularity among divers in the United States. However, there were very few experienced cave divers and almost no formal classes to handle the surge in interest. The result was a large number of divers trying to cave dive without any formal training. This resulted in more than 100 fatalities over the course of the decade. The state of Florida came close to banning SCUBA diving around the cave entrances. The cave diving organizations responded to the problem by creating training programs and certifying instructors, in addition to other measures to try to prevent these fatalities. This included posting signs, adding no-lights rules, and other enforcements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Sheck_Exley" title="Sheck Exley">Sheck Exley</a> was a pioneering cave diver who first explored many underwater cave systems in Florida, and many throughout the US and the world. On February 6, 1974, Exley became the first chairman of the Cave Diving Section of the <a href="/wiki/National_Speleological_Society" title="National Speleological Society">National Speleological Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cave_diving_section_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cave_diving_section-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1980s, cave diving education has greatly reduced diver fatalities, and it is now rare for an agency trained diver to perish in an underwater cave. Also in the 1980s, refinements were made to the equipment used for cave diving, most importantly better lights with smaller batteries. In the 1990s, cave diving equipment configurations became more standardized, due mostly to the adaptation and popularization of the "Hogarthian Rig", developed by several North Florida cave divers, named in honor of William "Hogarth" Main, which promotes equipment choices that "keep it simple and streamlined".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Today<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, the cave community is most focused on training, exploration, public awareness, and cave <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">conservation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Documentary films made by <a href="/wiki/Wesley_C._Skiles" title="Wesley C. Skiles">Wesley C. Skiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jill_Heinerth" title="Jill Heinerth">Jill Heinerth</a> have contributed to the increasing popularity of cave diving in the early 21st century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Cave diving in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> is happening in earnest following discovery of Pawod Underwater Cave in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mactan_Island" title="Mactan Island">Mactan Island</a>, off the big island of Cebu in the Central Philippines. Pawod was discovered in 2001 by Dr. Alfonso Amores, a cave diver certified in the underwater cave systems of North Central Florida. The discovery opened cave diving for the locals who erstwhile could not afford the expensive course in Florida. In 2012, Jake Miranda, Dr. Alfonso Amores and Bernil H. Gastardo founded the Filipino Cave Divers (FCD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fcd_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fcd-14">[14]</a></sup> A frenzy of explorations opened the freshwater caves of Casili, <a href="/wiki/Balamban" title="Balamban">Balamban</a> in Cebu Island and Enchanted River Underwater Cave in <a href="/wiki/Hinatuan,_Surigao_del_Sur" title="Hinatuan, Surigao del Sur">Hinatuan, Surigao del Sur</a> in the big southern island of <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a>. Several more caves in Mindanao have since been discovered and explored - Bababu Lake (<a href="/wiki/Dinagat_Island" title="Dinagat Island">Dinagat Island</a>), Pamutuanan Cave (<a href="/wiki/Lianga,_Surigao_del_Sur" title="Lianga, Surigao del Sur">Lianga, Surigao del Sur</a>) and Campomento Cave (<a href="/wiki/Lanuza,_Surigao_del_Sur" title="Lanuza, Surigao del Sur">Lanuza, Surigao del Sur</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Timor.2C_Indonesia">West Timor, Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: West Timor, Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are a number of freshwater filled cave sites located near and within <a href="/wiki/Kupang" title="Kupang">Kupang</a>, the principal city in <a href="/wiki/West_Timor" title="West Timor">West Timor</a> and the provincial capital of <a href="/wiki/East_Nusa_Tenggara" title="East Nusa Tenggara">East Nusa Tenggara</a>. At least 2 sites have been described: <i>Gua Oehani</i> (translates as the Oehani Cave) and <i>Gua Kristal</i> (translates as the Crystal Cave).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lengs_1966_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lengs_1966-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowan_2001_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cowan_2001-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 1999, there was no requirement to hold cave diving certification to dive either of these sites. However, competence equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Cave_Divers_Association_of_Australia" title="Cave Divers Association of Australia">CDAA</a> Advanced Cave and Cave/Advanced Cave appears to be respectively appropriate for these sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowan_2011b_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cowan_2011b-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg/170px-Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg/255px-Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg/340px-Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cave_diving_at_Piccaninnie_Ponds.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diving in Piccaninnie Ponds</div>

<p>Australia has many spectacular water filled caves and <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkholes</a>, but unlike the UK, most Australian cave divers come from a general ocean-diving background.</p>
<p>The "air-clear" water conditions experienced in the sinkholes and caves found in the Lower South East (now called the <a href="/wiki/Limestone_Coast" title="Limestone Coast">Limestone Coast</a>) of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> (SA) has attracted many visiting divers with the first cave and sinkhole dives taking place in the very late 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_1965_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1965-18">[18]</a></sup> Until the mid-1980s divers generally used single <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinders</a> and homemade torches, and <a href="/wiki/Distance_line" title="Distance line">reels</a>, resulting in most of their explorations being limited. Mixed-gas and <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> technologies can now be used in many sites. The area is usually known within the cave diving community as the <i><a href="/wiki/Mount_Gambier,_South_Australia" title="Mount Gambier, South Australia">Mount Gambier region</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A series of incidents between 1969 and 1974 in the former Lower South East of SA in which 11 divers died (including a triple and a quadruple fatality) in the following four karst features - <i>Kilsbys Hole</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Piccaninnie_Ponds_Conservation_Park" title="Piccaninnie Ponds Conservation Park">Piccaninnie Ponds</a></i>, <i>Death Cave</i> (also known as <i>Alleyns Cave</i>) and <i>The Shaft</i> - created much public comment and led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Cave_Divers_Association_of_Australia" title="Cave Divers Association of Australia">Cave Divers Association of Australia</a> Inc. (CDAA) in September 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_1977_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis_1977-19">[19]</a></sup> The introduction of a testing program by the CDAA in 1974, which involved the assessment of prospective cave divers' cave diving ability led to a reduced fatality rate. In 1989, this testing system was replaced by a training system which consists of three levels of qualification - <i>Deep Cavern</i>, <i>Cave</i> and <i>Advanced Cave</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDAA_training_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDAA_training-20">[20]</a></sup> Five further deaths have occurred since 1974; two died at <i>Piccaninnie Ponds</i> in 1984, one person died at <i>Kilsbys Hole</i> in 2010, and two people died in separate incidents at <i>Tank Cave</i> in 2011 including noted cave diver <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Milowka" title="Agnes Milowka">Agnes Milowka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horne_1987_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horne_1987-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Age_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Age-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S_M_Herald_2011_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-S_M_Herald_2011-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dougherty_2011_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dougherty_2011-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1980s, the <a href="/wiki/Nullarbor_Plain" title="Nullarbor Plain">Nullarbor Plain</a> was recognized as a major cave-diving area, with one cave, <i>Cocklebiddy</i>, being explored for more than 6 kilometres, involving the use of large sleds to which were attached numerous diving cylinders and other paraphernalia, and which were then laboriously pushed through the cave by the divers. In more recent years divers have been utilizing compact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diver_Propulsion_Vehicle" title="Diver Propulsion Vehicle">diver-towing powered scooters</a>, but the dive is still technically extremely challenging.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A number of other very significant caves have also been discovered during the past 20 years or so; the 10+ (Lineal) kilometre long <i>Tank Cave</i> near <a href="/wiki/Millicent,_South_Australia" title="Millicent, South Australia">Millicent</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Limestone_Coast" title="Limestone Coast">Limestone Coast</a>, other very large features on the Nullarbor and the adjacent Roe Plain as well as a number of specific sites elsewhere, and nowadays the cave diving community utilizes many techniques, equipment and standards from the U.S. and elsewhere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The CDAA is the major cave diving organisation in Australia and is responsible for the administration of cave diving at many sites. All cave diving in the <a href="/wiki/Limestone_Coast" title="Limestone Coast">Limestone Coast</a> as well as at some New South Wales sites and the Nullarbor requires divers to be members of the CDAA, whether in the capacity of a visitor or a trained and assessed member. A number of other organisations participate in cave diving activities within Australia. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Speleological_Federation" title="Australian Speleological Federation">Australian Speleological Federation</a> Cave Diving Group which was formed in 2005 coordinates projects focused on exploration and mapping at sites throughout Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASF-CDG_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASF-CDG-25">[25]</a></sup> The following diving training organisations offer courses in various aspects of cave diving via instructors either resident in Australia or visiting from overseas - <a href="/wiki/Global_Underwater_Explorers" title="Global Underwater Explorers">Global Underwater Explorers</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Nitrox_and_Technical_Divers" title="International Association of Nitrox and Technical Divers">International Association of Nitrox and Technical Divers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technical_Diving_International" title="Technical Diving International">Technical Diving International</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In the north west of <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, close to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Porto_Conte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Porto Conte (page does not exist)">Porto Conte</a> bay, <a href="/wiki/Alghero" title="Alghero">Alghero</a> territory, there are more than 300 caves above and below water, with about 30 large, and many smaller, underwater sea caves in the limestone cliffs of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Capo_Caccia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Capo Caccia (page does not exist)">Capo Caccia</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Punta_Giglio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Punta Giglio (page does not exist)">Punta Giglio</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Nereo_Cave" title="Nereo Cave">Nereo Cave</a></i> is the most important and is considered the largest in the Mediterranean Sea. On the east side of Sardinia there are many underwater cave systems starting from the <a href="/wiki/Gennargentu" title="Gennargentu">Gennargentu</a> Mountains, with underwater rivers. There is a fresh water cave of more than 110 m (360 ft) depth in this area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cala_Golone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cala Golone (page does not exist)">Cala Golone</a> is a departure point for cave diving in Sardinia's largest cave systems which are usually reached by sea. <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bue_Marino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bue Marino (page does not exist)">Bue Marino</a></i> is the longest known, and there are several others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jordbrugrotta" title="Jordbrugrotta">Jordbrugrotta</a> (also known as <b>Pluragrotta</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rana,_Norway" title="Rana, Norway">Rana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRK_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRK-26">[26]</a></sup> It is the deepest cave in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRK_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-NRK-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cave divers occasionally visit Jordbrugrotta which is the most dived cave in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. Most of the other approximately 200 caves in Rana are not suitable for diving, and formation of caves has been limited due most of the rock being granite. Another diveable cave nearby is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Litj%C3%A5ga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Litjåga (page does not exist)">Litjåga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEKDiveUSA_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-TEKDiveUSA-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEKDiveUSA_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-TEKDiveUSA-27">[27]</a></sup> Damming of <a href="/wiki/Kallvatnet" title="Kallvatnet">Kallvatnet</a> made diving in Jordbrugrotta possible. There have been multiple cases of fatalities and injuries among visiting cave divers, but the accident rate is not out of proportion with the number of divers. The cave's passages were formed by the river <a href="/wiki/Plura_(river)" title="Plura (river)">Plura</a>'s flow through porous <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_2014_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-HS_2014-28">[28]</a></sup> Rock formations include <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEKDiveUSA_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-TEKDiveUSA-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRK_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-NRK-26">[26]</a></sup> Visibility in the cave is good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YLE_Kiviranta_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-YLE_Kiviranta-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>UK requirements are generally that all people wishing to take up cave diving must be competent cavers before they start cave diving. This is primarily because most British cave dives are at the far end of dry caves. There are individuals that begin cave diving directly from the <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diving" title="Recreational diving">recreational diving</a>, but they represent a minority in the UK, and represent only a few percent of the <a href="/wiki/Cave_Diving_Group" title="Cave Diving Group">Cave Diving Group</a> (CDG).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bahamas">Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Bahamas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The caves and caverns of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bahama" title="Grand Bahama">Grand Bahama</a> contain an immense underwater cavern with a vast flooded <a href="/wiki/Labyrinth" title="Labyrinth">labyrinth</a> of caverns, caves and submerged tunnels that honeycomb the entire island of Grand Bahama and the surrounding sea bed. The inland caves are not abundant with life, but do contain creatures living in the caves other than the migrating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray_snapper" title="Gray snapper">gray snappers</a>. Residents of these caves include a type of blind cave fish and <a href="/wiki/Remipedia" title="Remipedia">remipedia</a> that don't pose any threat to cave divers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The caves in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> were formed during the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. With much of the Earth's water held in the form of glacial ice, the sea level fell hundreds of feet, leaving most of the Bahama banks, which are now covered in water, high and dry. Rain falling on the most porous limestone slowly filtered down to sea level forming a lens where it contacted the denser salt water of the ocean permeating the spongy lime stone. The water at the interface was acidic enough to dissolve away the limestone and form the caves. Then, as more ice formed and the sea level dropped even further, the caves became dry and rainwater dripping through the ceiling over thousands of years created the incredible crystal forests of stalagmites which now decorate the caves. Finally, when the ice melted and the sea level rose, the caves were reclaimed by the sea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominican_Republic">Dominican Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Dominican Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There is a growing number of known water filled caves in the Dominican Republic, spread all over the island. Regions with underwater caves include Santo Domingo, Pedernales, Cabrera, Bayahibe and others.</p>
<p>Active exploration is being conducted by the Dominican Republic Speleological Society which is working together with local institutions as well as international scientists to further explore all the cave systems possibilities and focusing in the preservation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The longest known cave in the island is <i>El Toro</i> which is about 6,000 ft (1,800 m) in length.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The best known caves in the island are <i>Cueva Taina</i>, <i>El Tildo</i>, <i>El Chicho</i> and <i>El Dudu</i>, which have easy access to the water and with a good level of safety outside of the water as they are in private properties or national parks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Cave diving is playing a very important role in science in the DR,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in the last 3 years the DRSS team together with international scientists and "Museo del Hombre Dominicano"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> has found a new species of cave bacteria, a number of new and extinct bat species, the first evidence of extinct crocodiles in the DR, fossil snakes, birds, sloths and remains of long extinct monkeys and other ancient cave life.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> DRSS has cataloged over 120 new springs all over the island in which many have caverns and cave systems attached.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><b>Safety concerns in Dominican Republic</b></p>
<p>An important safety issue on the island is untrained cave diving, principally led by unprofessional dive stores and guides risking their customers' lives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unprofessional and unsafe practices of dive guides may be reported to the relevant dive agency and to the Dominican Republic Speleological Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drss_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-drss-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico<span id="Yucatan_Peninsula.2C_Mexico"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>Yucatán Peninsula</b></p>
<p>While there is great potential for cave diving in the continental karst throughout Mexico, the majority of cave diving in Mexico occurs in the Yucatán Peninsula. While there are thousands of deep pit cenotes throughout the Yucatán Peninsula including in the states of <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Yucatán">Yucatán</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a>, the extensive sub-horizontal flooded cave networks for which the peninsula is known are essentially limited to a 10 km wide strip of the Caribbean coastline in the state of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> extending south from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cancun" title="Cancun">Cancun</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tulum_Municipality" title="Tulum Municipality">Tulum Municipality</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sian_Ka%27an_Biosphere_Reserve&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve (page does not exist)">Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve</a>, although some short segments of underwater cave have been explored on the north-west coast (Yucatán State).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the Yucatán Peninsula, any surface opening where groundwater can be reached is called <a href="/wiki/Cenote" title="Cenote">cenote</a>, which is a Spanish form of the Maya word <i>d’zonot</i>. The cave systems formed as normal caves underwater, but upper sections drained becoming air filled during past low sea levels. During this vadose, or air filled state, abundant <a href="/wiki/Speleothem" title="Speleothem">speleothem</a> deposits formed. The caves and the vadose speleothem were reflooded and became hydraulically reactivated as rising sea levels also raised the water table. These caves are therefore polygenetic, having experienced more than one cycle of formation below the water table. Polygenetic coastal cave systems with underwater speleothem are globally common, with notable examples being on the Balearic Islands (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mallorca" title="Mallorca">Mallorca</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menorca" title="Menorca">Menorca</a>) of Spain, the islands of the Bahamas, Bermuda, Cuba, and many more.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The underwater speleothems in the Yucatán Peninsula are fragile. If a diver accidentally breaks a formation, it will not reform as long as the cave is underwater so active cave conservation diving techniques are necessary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Quintana Roo caves are extremely complex with <a href="/wiki/Anastomosis" title="Anastomosis">anastomotic</a> interconnected passages. When diving through the caves, they appear to have many offshoots and junctions, requiring careful navigation with permanent tees or the implementation of jumps in the guideline.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The beginning of the 1980s brought the first cave divers from the U.S. to the Yucatán Peninsula, Quintana Roo to explore cenotes such as <i>Carwash</i>, <i>Naharon</i> and <i>Maya Blue</i>, and to central Mexico where resurgence rivers such as <i>Rio Mante</i>, and sinkholes such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zacaton" title="Zacaton">Zacaton</a></i> were documented.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the Yucatán, the 1980s ended with the discoveries of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dos_Ojos" title="Dos Ojos">Dos Ojos</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nohoch_Nah_Chich" title="Nohoch Nah Chich">Nohoch Nah Chich</a></i> cave systems which led into a long competition of which exploration team had the longest recorded underwater cave system in the world at the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The beginning of the 1990s led into the discovery of underwater caves such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aereolito&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aereolito (page does not exist)">Aereolito</a></i> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a>, ultimately leading to the 5th biggest underwater cave in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the mid-1990s a push into the central Yucatán Peninsula discovered a large number of deep sinkholes, or pit cenotes, such as <i>Sabak Ha</i> and <i>Utzil</i>, and deep caves such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chacdzinikche&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chacdzinikche (page does not exist)">Chacdzinikche</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dzibilchaltun" title="Dzibilchaltun">Dzibilchaltun</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kankirixche&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kankirixche (page does not exist)">Kankirixche</a></i> that have since been explored and mapped. These deep caves of the central Yucatán remain largely unexplored due to the number of cenotes in the State of Yucatán, and the depths, which require technical diving techniques or rebreathers. At the end of the last millennium closed circuit rebreathers were used to explore these caves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the end of the 1990s, <i>The Pit</i> in the <i>Dos Ojos</i> cave system located 5.8 km from the Caribbean coast had been discovered, and by 2008 it had been dived to 119 m deep. At that time, technical diving and rebreather equipment and techniques became commonplace.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the turn of the millennium the longest underwater cave system at that time, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ox_Bel_Ha" title="Ox Bel Ha">Ox Bel Ha</a></i> was established by cave diving explorers whose combined efforts and information helped join segments of previously explored caves. The use of hand held GPS technology and aerial and satellite images for reconnaissance during exploration became common. New technology such as improved rebreathers and diver propulsion vehicles (DPVs) became available and were utilized for longer penetration dives. In January 2013, <i>Ox Bel Ha</i> included 242 km of underwater passage (see <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo_Speleological_Survey" title="Quintana Roo Speleological Survey">QRSS</a> for current statistics).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Active exploration continues in the new millennium. Most cave diving exploration is now conducted on the basis of "mini projects" lasting 1 – 7 days, and occurring many times a year, and these may include daily commutes from home to jungle dive base camps located within 1 hour from road access.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Starting in 2006 a number of large previously explored and mapped cave systems have been shown to be connected using sidemount and often no-mount cave diving techniques to pass through tight cave passages, indicating the largest known connected underwater cave system on the planet, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sac_Actun" title="Sac Actun">Sac Actun</a></i>, which is 220 km long (see <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo_Speleological_Survey" title="Quintana Roo Speleological Survey">QRSS</a> for current statistics).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many cave maps have been published by the Quintana Roo Speleological Survey (QRSS).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="USA">USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>Central and Northern Florida</b></p>
<p>The largest and most active cave diving community in the United States is in north-central <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. The North Floridan <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">Aquifer</a> expels groundwater through numerous first-magnitude <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">springs</a>, each providing an entrance to the aquifer's labyrinthine cave system. These high-flow springs have resulted in Florida cave divers developing special techniques for exploring them, since some have such strong currents that it is impossible to swim against them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The longest known underwater cave system in the USA, The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Sinks" title="Leon Sinks">Leon Sinks</a></i> cave system, near Tallahassee, Florida, has multiple interconnected sinks and springs spanning two counties (Leon &amp; Wakulla).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAUSKernagis08_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAUSKernagis08-31">[31]</a></sup> One main resurgence of the system, <i><a href="/wiki/Wakulla_Springs" title="Wakulla Springs">Wakulla Springs</a></i>, is explored exclusively by a very successful and pioneering project called the <a href="/wiki/Woodville_Karst_Plain_Project" title="Woodville Karst Plain Project">Woodville Karst Plain Project</a> (WKPP), although other individuals and groups like the US Deep Cave Diving Team, have explored portions of <i>Wakulla Springs</i> in the past.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One deep underwater cave in the USA is <i>Weeki Wachee Spring</i>. Due to its strong outflow, divers have had limited success penetrating this first magnitude spring until 2007, when drought conditions eased the out-flowing water allowing team divers from <a href="/wiki/Karst_Underwater_Research" title="Karst Underwater Research">Karst Underwater Research</a> to penetrate to depths of 400 feet (120 m)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StPete2009_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-StPete2009-32">[32]</a></sup> The current deepest known underwater cave in the USA as of 2013 is Phantom Springs Cave located in west Texas. Phantom Springs has been explored down to 462 feet (141 m) in water filled cave passages. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_2013_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_2013-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Florida caves are formed from geologically young <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> with moderate porosity. The absence of speleothem decorations which can only form in air filled caves, indicates that the flooded Florida caves have a single genetic phase origin, having remained water filled even during past low sea levels. In plan form, the caves are relatively linear with a limited number of side passages allowing for most of the guidelines to be simple paths with few permanent tees. It is common practice for cave divers in Florida to joint a main line with a secondary line using a jump reel when exploring side passages, in order to maintain a continuous guideline to the surface.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cave_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Brazil there is cavern diving in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapada_da_Diamantina" title="Chapada da Diamantina">Chapada da Diamantina</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Bahia" title="Bahia">Bahia</a> state; <a href="/wiki/Bonito,_Mato_Grosso_do_Sul" title="Bonito, Mato Grosso do Sul">Bonito</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso_do_Sul" title="Mato Grosso do Sul">Mato Grosso do Sul</a> state; and <a href="/wiki/Mariana,_Minas_Gerais" title="Mariana, Minas Gerais">Mariana</a>, where there is also cave diving (visiting <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mina_da_Passagem&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mina da Passagem (page does not exist)">Mina da Passagem</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">Minas Gerais</a> state. For cave diving in Mariana a cave diver certification will be required.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
