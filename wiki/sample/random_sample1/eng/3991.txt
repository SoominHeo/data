<p><i><b>Panthera leo spelaea</b></i> or <i><b>P. spelaea</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taxon_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-taxon-2">[note 1]</a></sup> commonly known as the <b>European</b> or <b>Eurasian cave lion</b>, is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>. It is known from <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> and many examples of <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_art" title="Prehistoric art">prehistoric art</a>.</p>

<p><i>P. leo spelaea</i> has been proposed as a separate <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> <i>Panthera spelaea</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> One authority considered the cave lion to be more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tiger</a> based on a comparison of <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a> shapes, which would have resulted in the formal name <i>Panthera tigris spelaea</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Past DNA studies indicated that among extant felids, the cave lion was most closely related to the modern lion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burger_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burger-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett.2C_2009_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett.2C_2009-7">[6]</a></sup> and that it formed a single population with the <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringian</a> cave lion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett.2C_2009_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett.2C_2009-7">[6]</a></sup> which has sometimes been considered a distinct form. Therefore, the cave lion ranged from Europe to Alaska over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bering_land_bridge" title="Bering land bridge">Bering land bridge</a> until the late Pleistocene. A recent DNA study indicates that it is a separate species which diverged from a common ancestor shared with the lion 1.9 million years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnett2016_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-barnett2016-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Analysis of skulls and mandibles of a lion that inhabited <a href="/wiki/Sakha_Republic" title="Sakha Republic">Yakutia</a> (Russia), <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> (United States), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yukon_Territory" title="Yukon Territory">Yukon Territory</a> (Canada) during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(reference_date)" title="Epoch (reference date)">epoch</a> suggested that it was a new <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> different from the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> lions, <i>Panthera leo vereshchagini</i>, known as the East Siberian- or <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringian</a> cave lion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BurgerJ-Molecular-phylogeny_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BurgerJ-Molecular-phylogeny-8">[7]</a></sup> It differed from <i>Panthera leo spelaea</i> by its larger size and from the <a href="/wiki/American_lion" title="American lion">American lion</a> (<i>Panthera leo atrox</i>) by its smaller size and by <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a> proportions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BurgerJ-Molecular-phylogeny_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BurgerJ-Molecular-phylogeny-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> However, recent genetic research, using ancient DNA from Beringian lions found no evidence for separating <i>Panthera leo vereshchagini</i> from the European cave lion; indeed, DNA signatures from lions from Europe and Alaska were indistinguishable, suggesting one large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panmictic" title="Panmictic">panmictic</a> population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett.2C_2009_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett.2C_2009-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2015 two frozen cave lion cubs, estimated to be at least 10,000 years old, were discovered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakutia" title="Yakutia">Yakutia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Research on the specimens, named Uyan and Dina, indicated the cubs were likely barely a week old at the time of their deaths, as their baby teeth had not fully erupted. Further evidence shows the cubs were, like modern lions, hidden at a den site until they were old enough to join the pride. Researchers think that the cubs were trapped and killed by a landslide, and that without air, the cubs were preserved in such good condition. A second expedition to the site where the cubs were found is planned for 2016, in hopes of finding either the remains of a third cub or possibly the cubs' mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>P. leo spelaea</i> evolved from the earlier <i><a href="/wiki/Panthera_leo_fossilis" title="Panthera leo fossilis">Panthera leo fossilis</a></i>, which first appeared in Europe about 700,000 years ago. Genetic evidence indicates this lineage was isolated from extant lions after its dispersal to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burger_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burger-6">[5]</a></sup> <i>P. l. spelaea</i> lived from 370,000 to 10,000 years ago, during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a>. Apparently, it became extinct about 12,400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-14_years_BP" title="C-14 years BP"><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">14<br/></span>C</span> years</a> ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett.2C_2009_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett.2C_2009-7">[6]</a></sup> as the <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrm_glaciation" title="Würm glaciation">Würm glaciation</a> receded.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">Mitochondrial DNA</a> sequence data from fossil remains show the <a href="/wiki/American_lion" title="American lion">American lion</a> (<i>P. l. atrox</i>) represents a sister lineage to <i>P. l. spelaea</i>, and likely arose when an early <i>P. l. spelaea</i> population became isolated south of the <a href="/wiki/Cordilleran_Ice_Sheet" title="Cordilleran Ice Sheet">North American continental ice sheet</a> about 0.34 Mya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett.2C_2009_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett.2C_2009-7">[6]</a></sup> The following cladogram shows the genetic relationship between <i>P. spelaea</i> and other pantherines according to Barnett et al. (2016),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> besides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linnaeus" title="Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> (1758):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn2008-15">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>This subspecies was one of the largest lions. The skeleton of an adult male, which was found in 1985 near <a href="/wiki/Siegsdorf" title="Siegsdorf">Siegsdorf</a> (Germany), had a shoulder height of around 1.2 m (3.9 ft) and a head-body length of 2.1 m (6.9 ft) without the tail. This is similar to the size of a very large modern lion. The size of this male has been exceeded by other specimens of this subspecies. Therefore, this cat may have been around 8%-10% bigger than modern lions and smaller than the earlier cave lion subspecies <i><a href="/wiki/Panthera_leo_fossilis" title="Panthera leo fossilis">Panthera leo fossilis</a></i> or the relatively larger <a href="/wiki/American_lion" title="American lion">American lion</a> (<i>Panthera leo atrox</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The cave lion is known from <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_paintings" title="Cave paintings">cave paintings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> carvings, and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> <a href="/wiki/Figurine" title="Figurine">figurines</a>. These representations indicate cave lions had rounded, protruding ears, tufted tails, possibly faint tiger-like stripes, and at least some had a "ruff" or primitive mane around their necks, possibly indicating males. Other <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">archaeological artifacts</a> indicate they were featured in Paleolithic <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious rituals</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, a well-preserved cave lion specimen was unearthed near the <a href="/wiki/Maly_Anyuy_River" title="Maly Anyuy River">Maly Anyuy River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Chukotka Autonomous Okrug">Chukotka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, which still retained some clumps of hair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> A 2016 study identified the hair on the basis of DNA as being cave lion hair, and comparison to the hair of an African lion revealed that cave lion hair was similar in color to that of modern lions, though slightly lighter. In addition to slightly different coloration, cave lions had a very thick and dense undercoat comprising closed and compressed yellowish-to-white wavy downy hair with a smaller mass of darker coloured guard hairs, as an adaptation to the ice age climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeobiology">Palaeobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panthera_leo_spelaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Palaeobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="414" data-file-width="656" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg/220px-Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg/330px-Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg/440px-Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hoehlenloewe_CaveLion_hharder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cave lion with a reindeer, by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Harder" title="Heinrich Harder">Heinrich Harder</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These active carnivores probably preyed upon the large herbivorous animals of their time, including <a href="/wiki/Equidae" title="Equidae">horses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">bison</a> and even injured old or young <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoths</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arduini_.25_Teruzzi.2C_1993_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arduini_.25_Teruzzi.2C_1993-19">[18]</a></sup> which would have been killed by a powerful bite from the sharp teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lessem.2C_1999_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lessem.2C_1999-20">[19]</a></sup> Some paintings of them in caves show several hunting together, which suggests the hunting strategy of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Lion#Hunting_and_diet" title="Lion">lionesses</a>. Isotopic analyses of bone <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> samples extracted from fossils suggest reindeer and cave bear cubs were prominent in the diets of northwestern European cave lions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curry_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curry-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bocherens_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bocherens-22">[21]</a></sup> There was a suggestion of a shift in dietary preferences subsequent to the disappearance of the <a href="/wiki/Cave_hyena" title="Cave hyena">cave hyena</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bocherens_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bocherens-22">[21]</a></sup> The last cave lions seem to have focused on reindeer, up to the brink of local extinction or extirpation of both species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bocherens_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bocherens-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panthera_leo_spelaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1240" data-file-width="2558" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg/220px-Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg/330px-Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg/440px-Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ice_age_fauna_of_northern_Spain_-_Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cave lions and other Ice Age fauna in northern Spain, by <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Ant%C3%B3n" title="Mauricio Antón">Mauricio Antón</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>P. leo spelaea</i> populations were widespread in parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and northwestern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, Germany, and Spain <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> all the way across the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yukon_Territory" title="Yukon Territory">Yukon Territory</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkistan" title="Turkistan">Turkistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Panthera_youngi" title="Panthera youngi">Panthera youngi</a></i> reached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Archipelago" title="Japanese Archipelago">Japanese Archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JapanMammals_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-JapanMammals-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>P. leo spelaea</i> received its vernacular names because large quantities of its remains have been found in caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arduini_.25_Teruzzi.2C_1993_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arduini_.25_Teruzzi.2C_1993-19">[18]</a></sup> It had a wide habitat tolerance, but probably preferred conifer forests and grasslands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hublin.2C_1984_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hublin.2C_1984-26">[25]</a></sup> where medium-sized to large herbivores occurred. Fossil footprints of lions, which were found together with those of <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeer</a>, demonstrate the lions once occurred even in subpolar climates. The presence of fully articulated adult cave lion skeletons, deep in <a href="/wiki/Cave_bear" title="Cave bear">cave bear</a> dens, indicates these lions may have occasionally entered dens to prey on hibernating cave bears, with some dying in the attempt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
