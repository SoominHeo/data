<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Symmetric digital subscriber line</h1>

<p><b>Symmetric digital subscriber line</b> (<b>SDSL</b>) refers to <a href="/wiki/Digital_subscriber_line" title="Digital subscriber line">digital subscriber line</a> (DSL) technologies, that is, technologies for transmission of digital data over the copper wires of the telephone network where the <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downstream_(computer_science)" title="Downstream (computer science)">downstream</a> direction, from the network to the subscriber, is identical to the bandwidth in the <a href="/wiki/Upstream_(networking)" title="Upstream (networking)">upstream</a> direction, from the subscriber to the network. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetric" title="Symmetric">symmetric</a> bandwidth can be considered to be the opposite of the asymmetric bandwidth offered by <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_digital_subscriber_line" title="Asymmetric digital subscriber line">asymmetric digital subscriber line</a> (ADSL) technologies, where the upstream bandwidth is lower than the downstream bandwidth. SDSL is generally marketed at business customers, while ADSL is marketed at private as well as business customers.</p>
<p>More specifically, SDSL can be understood as:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>in the wider sense</i>, an umbrella term for all DSL variant which offer symmetric bandwidth, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDSL" title="IDSL">IDSL</a>, which offers 144 kbit/s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDSL" title="HDSL">HDSL</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDSL2" title="HDSL2">HDSL2</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.SHDSL" title="G.SHDSL">G.SHDSL</a>, which offers up to 22.784 Mbit/s over four pairs of copper wires, as well as the SDSL variant below</li>
<li><i>in the narrow sense</i>, a particular proprietary and non-standardized DSL variant for operation at 1.544 Mbit/s or 2.048 Mbit/s over a single pair of copper wires, without support for analog calls on the same line</li>
<li>a term used by <a href="/wiki/ETSI" title="ETSI">ETSI</a> to refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.SHDSL" title="G.SHDSL">G.SHDSL</a></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proprietary_SDSL_technology">Proprietary SDSL technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symmetric_digital_subscriber_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proprietary SDSL technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>SDSL is a rate-adaptive digital subscriber line (DSL) variant with <a href="/wiki/T-carrier" title="T-carrier">T1</a>/<a href="/wiki/E-carrier" title="E-carrier">E1</a>-like data rates (T1: 1.544 <a href="/wiki/Data_rate_units#Megabit_per_second" title="Data rate units">Mbit/s</a>, E1: 2.048 Mbit/s). It runs over one pair of copper wires, with a maximum range of 10,000 feet (3,000 m). It cannot co-exist with a conventional voice service on the same pair as it takes over the entire bandwidth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization_efforts">Standardization efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symmetric_digital_subscriber_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standardization efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SDSL is a <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_protocol" title="Proprietary protocol">proprietary</a> technology that was never standardized. As such it usually only interoperates with devices from the same vendor. It is the predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Single-pair_high-speed_digital_subscriber_line" title="Single-pair high-speed digital subscriber line">G.SHDSL</a> which was standardized in February 2001 by <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">ITU-T</a> with recommendation G.991.2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> SDSL is often confused with G.SHDSL and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_bit_rate_Digital_Subscriber_Line" title="High bit rate Digital Subscriber Line">HDSL</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, G.SHDSL was standardized by <a href="/wiki/ETSI" title="ETSI">ETSI</a> using the name 'SDSL'. This ETSI variant is compatible with the ITU-T G.SHDSL standardized regional variant for Europe.</p>
<p>As there is a standardised successor available, SDSL installations today are considered <a href="/wiki/Legacy_system" title="Legacy system">legacy</a>. Most new installations use G.SHDSL equipment instead of SDSL.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Target_audience">Target audience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symmetric_digital_subscriber_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Target audience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SDSL typically falls between ADSL and T1/E1 in price and was mainly targeted at small and medium businesses which don't need the service guarantees of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_relay" title="Frame relay">frame relay</a> or the higher performance of a <a href="/wiki/Leased_line" title="Leased line">leased line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
