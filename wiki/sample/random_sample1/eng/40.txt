<p><b>99942 Apophis</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>, previously known by its <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">provisional designation</a> <b><span class="nowrap">2004 MN<sub>4</sub></span></b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object#Near-Earth_asteroids" title="Near-Earth object">near-Earth asteroid</a> that caused a brief period of concern in December 2004 because initial observations indicated a probability of up to 2.7% that it would hit <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> on April 13, 2029. Additional observations provided improved predictions that eliminated the possibility of an <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact on Earth</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> in 2029. However, until 2006, a possibility remained that during the 2029 close encounter with Earth, Apophis would pass through a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_keyhole" title="Gravitational keyhole">gravitational keyhole</a>, a small region no more than about 965 kilometres (600 miles) wide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keyhole_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-keyhole-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neil_deGrasse_Tyson_-_Death_By_Giant_Meteor_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neil_deGrasse_Tyson_-_Death_By_Giant_Meteor-9">[9]</a></sup> that would set up a future impact exactly seven years later, on April 13, 2036. This possibility kept it at Level 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Torino_scale" title="Torino scale">Torino impact hazard scale</a> until August 2006, when the probability that Apophis would pass through the keyhole was determined to be very small. By 2008, the keyhole had been determined to be less than 1 km wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keyhole_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-keyhole-8">[8]</a></sup> During the short time when it had been of greatest concern, Apophis set the record for highest rating on the Torino scale, reaching level 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews146_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews146-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The diameter of Apophis is, as of the most recent 2013 observations, approximately 325 metres (1,066 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esanews1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-esanews1-2">[2]</a></sup> Preliminary observations by <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_Deep_Space_Communications_Complex" title="Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex">Goldstone</a> radar in January 2013 effectively ruled out the possibility of an Earth impact by Apophis in 2036.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2013-017_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA2013-017-11">[11]</a></sup> By May 6, 2013 (April 15, 2013 observation arc), the probability of an impact on April 13, 2036 had been eliminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup> As of October 8, 2014, using observations through February 26, 2014, the odds of an impact on April 12, 2068, as calculated by the JPL <a href="/wiki/Sentry_(monitoring_system)" title="Sentry (monitoring system)">Sentry risk table</a> is 1 in 149,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup> As of December 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, of objects not recently observed, there were about five asteroids with a more notable <a href="/wiki/Palermo_Technical_Impact_Hazard_Scale" title="Palermo Technical Impact Hazard Scale">Palermo Technical Impact Hazard Scale</a> than Apophis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-risk-table_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-risk-table-12">[12]</a></sup> On average, an asteroid the size of Apophis (325 metres) can be expected to impact Earth about every 80,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth-impact_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Earth-impact-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apophis was discovered on June 19, 2004, by <a href="/wiki/Roy_A._Tucker" title="Roy A. Tucker">Roy A. Tucker</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_J._Tholen" title="David J. Tholen">David J. Tholen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fabrizio_Bernardi" title="Fabrizio Bernardi">Fabrizio Bernardi</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Kitt_Peak_National_Observatory" title="Kitt Peak National Observatory">Kitt Peak National Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">[1]</a></sup> On December 21, 2004, Apophis passed 0.0963 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (14,410,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>; 8,950,000 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">Precovery</a> observations from March 15, 2004, were identified on December 27, and an improved orbit solution was computed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2004-Y70_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC2004-Y70-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews148_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews148-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">Radar</a> astrometry in January 2005 further refined its orbit solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arecibo_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arecibo-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews149_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews149-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>When first discovered, the object received the provisional designation <span class="nowrap">2004 MN<sub>4</sub></span>, and news and scientific articles about it referred to it by that name. When its orbit was sufficiently well calculated, it received the permanent number 99942 (on June 24, 2005). Receiving a permanent number made it eligible for naming, and it received the name "Apophis" on July 19, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naming_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-naming-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Apep" title="Apep">Apophis</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> name of an enemy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian" title="Ancient Egyptian">Ancient Egyptian</a> sun-god <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>: <a href="/wiki/Apep" title="Apep">Apep</a>, the Uncreator, an evil serpent that dwells in the eternal darkness of the <a href="/wiki/Duat" title="Duat">Duat</a> and tries to swallow Ra during his nightly passage. Apep is held at bay by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_(mythology)" title="Set (mythology)">Set</a>, the Ancient Egyptian god of storms and the desert. <a href="/wiki/David_J._Tholen" title="David J. Tholen">David J. Tholen</a> and Tucker—two of the co-discoverers of the asteroid—are reportedly fans of the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_SG-1" title="Stargate SG-1">Stargate SG-1</a></i>. One of the show's persistent villains is an alien named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apophis_(Stargate)" title="Apophis (Stargate)">Apophis</a>. He is one of the principal threats to the existence of civilization on Earth through the first few seasons, thus likely why the asteroid was named after him. In the fictional world of the show, the alien's backstory was that he had lived on Earth during ancient times and had posed as a god, thereby giving rise to the myth of the Egyptian god of the same name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naming_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-naming-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based upon the observed brightness, Apophis's diameter was initially estimated at 450 metres (1,480 ft); a more refined estimate based on spectroscopic observations at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_Telescope_Facility" title="Infrared Telescope Facility">Infrared Telescope Facility</a> in Hawaii by Binzel, Rivkin, Bus, and Tokunaga (2005) is 350 metres (1,150 ft). NASA's impact risk page lists the diameter at 330 metres (1,080 ft) and lists a mass of 4<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">10</span></sup> kg based on an assumed density of 2.6 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup> The mass estimate is more approximate than the diameter estimate, but should be accurate to within a factor of three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2029 approach, Apophis's brightness will peak at <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> 3.4,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODyS2029_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEODyS2029-19">[19]</a></sup> with a maximum angular speed of 42° per hour. The maximum apparent <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">angular diameter</a> will be ~2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a>, so that it will be barely <a href="/wiki/Optical_resolution" title="Optical resolution">resolved</a> by ground-based telescopes not equipped with <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Close_approaches">Close approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Close approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apophis_pass.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Apophis_pass.svg/220px-Apophis_pass.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Apophis_pass.svg/330px-Apophis_pass.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Apophis_pass.svg/440px-Apophis_pass.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apophis_pass_zoom.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Apophis_pass_zoom.svg/220px-Apophis_pass_zoom.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Apophis_pass_zoom.svg/330px-Apophis_pass_zoom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Apophis_pass_zoom.svg/440px-Apophis_pass_zoom.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apophis_pass_zoom.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The white bar indicates uncertainty in the range of positions (as known in February 2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews149_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews149-17">[17]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">Minor Planet Center</a> confirmed the June discovery of Apophis, an April 13, 2029 close approach was flagged by NASA's automatic <a href="/wiki/Sentry_(monitoring_system)" title="Sentry (monitoring system)">Sentry</a> system and <a href="/wiki/NEODyS" title="NEODyS">NEODyS</a>, a similar automatic program run by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pisa" title="University of Pisa">University of Pisa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Valladolid" title="University of Valladolid">University of Valladolid</a>. On that date, it will become as bright as magnitude 3.4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODyS2029_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NEODyS2029-19">[19]</a></sup> (visible to the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a> from rural as well as darker suburban areas, visible with <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> from most locations).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The close approach will be visible from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. During the close approach in 2029 Earth will <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturb</a> Apophis from an <a href="/wiki/Aten_asteroid" title="Aten asteroid">Aten</a> class orbit with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> of 0.92 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Apollo_asteroid" title="Apollo asteroid">Apollo</a> class orbit with a semi-major axis of 1.1 AU.</p>
<p>After Sentry and NEODyS announced the possible impact, additional observations decreased the uncertainty in Apophis's trajectory. As they did, the probability of an impact event temporarily climbed, peaking at 2.7% (1 in 37).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews164_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews164-21">[21]</a></sup> This probability, combined with its size, caused Apophis to be assessed at level 4 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torino_Scale" title="Torino Scale">Torino Scale</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews146_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews146-10">[10]</a></sup> and 1.10 on the <a href="/wiki/Palermo_Technical_Impact_Hazard_Scale" title="Palermo Technical Impact Hazard Scale">Palermo Technical Impact Hazard Scale</a>, scales scientists use to represent how dangerous a given asteroid is to Earth. These are the highest values for which any object has been rated on either scale. The chance that there would be an impact in 2029 was eliminated by December 27, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews148_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews148-15">[15]</a></sup> The danger of a 2036 passage was lowered to level 0 on the Torino Scale in August 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayback2006-08_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wayback2006-08-22">[22]</a></sup> With a cumulative Palermo Scale rating of −3.2,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup> the risk of impact from Apophis is less than one thousandth the background hazard level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 13, 2029, Apophis will pass Earth within the orbits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geosynchronous" title="Geosynchronous">geosynchronous</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">communication satellites</a>, but will come no closer than 19,400 miles (31,200 km) above Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2013-017_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA2013-017-11">[11]</a></sup> The 2029 pass will be much closer than had first been predicted. The pass in late March 2036 will be no closer than about 23 million kilometres (14<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup> mi)—and will most likely miss Earth by something closer to 56 million kilometres (35<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup> mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beatty2013_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beatty2013-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_and_2011_observations">2005 and 2011 observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2005 and 2011 observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In July 2005, former <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> astronaut <a href="/wiki/Rusty_Schweickart" title="Rusty Schweickart">Rusty Schweickart</a>, as chairman of the <a href="/wiki/B612_Foundation" title="B612 Foundation">B612 Foundation</a>, formally asked NASA to investigate the possibility that the asteroid's post-2029 orbit could be in <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonance</a> with Earth, which would increase the probability of future impacts. Schweickart also asked NASA to investigate whether a <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponder</a> should be placed on the asteroid to enable more accurate tracking of how its orbit is affected by the <a href="/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect" title="Yarkovsky effect">Yarkovsky effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schweickart_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schweickart-24">[24]</a></sup> On January 31, 2011, astronomers took the first new images of Apophis in more than 3 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tholen_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tholen-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_refinement">2013 refinement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2013 refinement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The close approach in 2029 will substantially alter the object's orbit, prompting Jon Giorgini of JPL to say: "If we get radar ranging in 2013 [the next good opportunity], we should be able to predict the location of <span class="nowrap">2004 MN<sub>4</sub></span> out to at least 2070."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science-26">[26]</a></sup> Apophis passed within 0.0966 AU (14,450,000 km; 8,980,000 mi) of Earth in 2013, allowing astronomers to refine the trajectory for future close passes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esanews1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-esanews1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODys_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEODys-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refine2013_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-refine2013-28">[28]</a></sup> Just after the closest approach on 9 January 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODys_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-NEODys-27">[27]</a></sup> the asteroid peaked at an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a>  of about 15.7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODyS2013_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEODyS2013-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_Deep_Space_Communications_Complex" title="Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex">Goldstone</a> observed Apophis during that approach from January 3 through January 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstone_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstone-30">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> observed Apophis once it entered Arecibo's declination window after February 13, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstone_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstone-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A NASA assessment as of 21 February 2013 that does not use the 2013 radar measurements gave an impact probability of 2.3 in a million for 2068.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news178_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-news178-31">[31]</a></sup> As of 6 May 2013, using observations through April 15, 2013, the odds of an impact on 12 April 2068 as calculated by the JPL Sentry risk table had increased to 3.9 in a million (1 in 256,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>The original NASA report on December 23, 2004, mentioned impact chances of "around 1 in 300" in 2029, which was widely reported in the media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews146_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews146-10">[10]</a></sup> The actual NASA estimates at the time were 1 in 233; these resulted in a Torino scale rating of 2, the first time any asteroid had received a rating above 1.</li>
<li>Later that day, based on a total of 64 observations, the estimates were changed to 1 in 62 (1.6%), resulting in an update to the initial report and an upgrade to a Torino scale rating of 4.</li>
<li>On December 25, 2004, the chances were first reported as 1 in 42 (2.4%) and later that day (based on 101 observations) as 1 in 45 (2.2%). At the same time, the asteroid's estimated diameter was lowered from 440 m to 390 m and its mass from 1.2×10<sup>11</sup> kg to 8.3×10<sup>10</sup> kg.</li>
<li>On December 26, 2004 (based on a total of 169 observations), the impact probability was still estimated as 1 in 45 (2.2%), the estimates for diameter and mass were lowered to 380 m and 7.5×10<sup>10</sup> kg, respectively.</li>
<li>On December 27, 2004 (based on a total of 176 observations), the impact probability was raised to 1 in 37 (2.7%); diameter was increased to 390 m, and mass to 7.9×10<sup>10</sup> kg.</li>
<li>On December 27, 2004, in the afternoon, a <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">precovery</a> increased the span of observations to 287 days, which eliminated the 2029 impact threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews148_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews148-15">[15]</a></sup> The cumulative impact probability was estimated to be around 0.004%, a risk lower than that of asteroid <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_VD17" title="2004 VD17">2004 VD<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">17</span></span></a></span>, which once again became the greatest-risk object. A 2053 approach to Earth still poses a minor risk of impact, and Apophis was still rated at level one on the Torino scale for this orbit.</li>
<li>On December 28, 2004, at 12:23 GMT and (based on a total of 139 observations), produced a value of one on the Torino scale for 2044-04-13.29 and 2053-04-13.51.</li>
<li>By 01:10 GMT on December 29, 2004, the only pass rated 1 on the Torino scale was for 2053-04-13.51 based on 139 observations spanning 287.71 days (2004-Mar-15.1104 to 2004-Dec-27.8243). (As of February 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the 2053 risk is only 1 in 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>By 19:18 GMT on December 29, 2004, this was still the case based upon 147 observations spanning 288.92 days (2004-Mar-15.1104 to 2004-Dec-29.02821), though the close encounters have changed and been reduced to 4 in total.</li>
<li>By 13:46 GMT on December 30, 2004, no passes were rated above 0, based upon 157 observations spanning 289.33 days (2004-Mar-15.1104 to 2004-Dec-29.44434). The most dangerous pass was rated at 1 in 7,143,000.</li>
<li>By 22:34 GMT on December 30, 2004, 157 observations spanning 289.33 days (2004-Mar-15.1104 to 2004-Dec-29.44434). One pass at 1 (Torino scale) 3 other passes.</li>
<li>By 03:57 GMT on January 2, 2005, 182 observations spanning 290.97 days (2004-Mar-15.1104 to 2004-Dec-31.07992) One pass at 1 (Torino scale) 19 other passes.</li>
<li>By 14:49 GMT on January 3, 2005, observations spanning 292.72 days (2004-Mar-15.1104 to 2005-Jan-01.82787) One pass at 1 (Torino scale) 15 other passes.</li>
<li>Extremely precise <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> observations at <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> in January 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arecibo_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arecibo-16">[16]</a></sup> refine the orbit further and show that the April 2029 close approach will occur at only 5.7 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_radii" title="Earth radii">Earth radii</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasanews149_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-nasanews149-17">[17]</a></sup> approximately one-half the distance previously estimated.</li>
<li>By February 6, 2005, Apophis (<span class="nowrap">2004 MN<sub>4</sub></span>) had a 1 in 13,000 chance of impacting in April 2036.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayback2005-02_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wayback2005-02-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Radar observation on August 7, 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arecibo_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Arecibo-16">[16]</a></sup> refines the orbit further and eliminates the possibility of an impact in 2035. Only the pass in 2036 remains at Torino Scale 1 (with a 1 in 5,560 chance of impact).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayback2005-10_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wayback2005-10-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>In October 2005 it is predicted that the asteroid will pass just below the altitude of <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_satellite" title="Geosynchronous satellite">geosynchronous satellites</a>, which are at approximately 35,900 kilometres (22,300 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Such a close approach by an asteroid is estimated to occur every 800 years or so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neo_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-neo-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Radar observation at Arecibo Observatory on May 6, 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arecibo_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Arecibo-16">[16]</a></sup> slightly lowered the Palermo scale rating, but the pass in 2036 remained at Torino Scale 1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayback2006-07_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wayback2006-07-36">[36]</a></sup> despite the impact probability dropping by a factor of four.</li>
<li>Additional observations through 2006 resulted in Apophis being lowered to Torino Scale 0 by August 5, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayback2006-08_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wayback2006-08-22">[22]</a></sup> (The impact probability was 1 in 45,000.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayback2006-08_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wayback2006-08-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>In April 2008, <a href="/wiki/Nico_Marquardt" title="Nico Marquardt">Nico Marquardt</a> published a research paper in which he calculated the probability of Apophis to collide with a <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_satellite" title="Geosynchronous satellite">geosynchronous satellite</a> during its flyby on April 13, 2029 and the consequences of this event to the likelihood of an Earth-collision 2036. Afterwards, the German newspaper <a href="/wiki/Bild" title="Bild">Bild</a> published an article stating a 100-times higher probability of an Earth-collision in the year 2036 than Marquardt calculated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Nearly all international press reported the news with false data caused by the review from Bild even though Marquardt denied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This estimate was allegedly confirmed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a> and NASA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> but in an official statement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAkid_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAkid-41">[41]</a></sup> NASA denied the wrong statement. The release went on to explain that since the angle of Apophis's approach to the Earth's equator means the asteroid will not travel through the belt of current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_orbit" title="Equatorial orbit">equatorial</a> geosynchronous satellites, there is currently no risk of collision; and the effect on Apophis' orbit of any such impact would be insignificant.</li>
<li>On April 16, 2008, NASA News Release 08-103 reaffirmed that its estimation of a 1 in 45,000 chance of impact in 2036 remains valid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAkid_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAkid-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>As of October 7, 2009, refinements to the <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">precovery</a> images of Apophis by the University of Hawaii's <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Astronomy" title="Institute for Astronomy">Institute for Astronomy</a>, the 90-inch <a href="/wiki/Bok_Telescope" title="Bok Telescope">Bok Telescope</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> have generated a refined path that reduces the odds of an April 13, 2036 impact to about 1 in 250,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2009_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2009-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>2009-Apr-29: An animation is released<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> that shows how unmeasured physical parameters of Apophis bias the entire statistical uncertainty region. If Apophis is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion"><i>retrograde</i> rotator</a> on the small, less-massive end of what is possible, the measurement uncertainty region will get pushed back such that the center of the distribution encounters Earth's orbit. This would result in an impact probability much higher than computed with the Standard Dynamical Model. Conversely, if Apophis is a small, less-massive <i>prograde</i> rotator, the uncertainty region is advanced along the orbit. Only the remote tails of the probability distribution could encounter Earth, producing a negligible impact probability.</li>
<li>Criticism of older published impact probabilities rests on the fact that important physical parameters such as mass and spin that affect its precise trajectory have not yet been accurately measured and hence there are no associated probability distributions. The Standard Dynamical Model used for making predictions simplifies calculations by assuming Earth is a point mass; this can introduce up to 2.9 Earth radii of prediction error for the 2036 approach, and Earth's oblateness must be considered for the 2029 passage to predict a potential impact reliably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neo_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-neo-35">[35]</a></sup> Additional factors that can greatly influence the predicted motion in ways that depend on unknown details, are the spin of the asteroid,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> its precise mass, the way it reflects and absorbs sunlight, radiates heat, and the gravitational pull of other asteroids passing nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neo_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-neo-35">[35]</a></sup> Small uncertainties in the masses and positions of the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> can cause up to a 23 Earth radii of prediction error for Apophis by 2036.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neo_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-neo-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>A statistical impact risk analysis of the data up to January 2013 calculated that the odds of the 2036 impact at 7.07 in a billion, effectively ruling it out. The same study looked at the odds of an impact in 2068, which were calculated at 2.27 in a million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 9 January 2013, the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA) announced the <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">Herschel Space Observatory</a> made new thermal infrared observations of the asteroid as it approached Earth. The initial data shows the asteroid to be bigger than first estimated because it is now expected to be less reflective than originally thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esanews1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-esanews1-2">[2]</a></sup> The Herschel Space Observatory observations increased the diameter estimate by 20% from 270 to 325 metres, which translates into a 75% increase in the estimates of the asteroid's volume or mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esanews1_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-esanews1-2">[2]</a></sup> Goldstone single-pixel observations of Apophis have ruled out the potential 2036 Earth impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2013-017_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA2013-017-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beatty2013_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beatty2013-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plait2013_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plait2013-46">[46]</a></sup> Apophis will then come no closer than about 14 million miles—and more likely miss us by something closer to 35 million miles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beatty2013_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beatty2013-23">[23]</a></sup> The radar astrometry is more precise than was expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beatty2013_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Beatty2013-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>The Sentry Risk Table estimates that Apophis would make atmospheric entry with 750 <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">megatons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskneo_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-riskneo-3">[3]</a></sup> The impacts that created <a href="/wiki/Meteor_Crater" title="Meteor Crater">Meteor Crater</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Tunguska_event" title="Tunguska event">Tunguska event</a> are estimated to be in the 3–10 megaton range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sandia_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sandia-47">[47]</a></sup> The 1883 eruption of <a href="/wiki/Krakatoa" title="Krakatoa">Krakatoa</a> was the equivalent of roughly 200 megatons and the biggest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bomb</a> ever exploded, the <a href="/wiki/Tsar_Bomba" title="Tsar Bomba">Tsar Bomba</a>, was around 57 megatons. In comparison, the <a href="/wiki/Chicxulub_crater" title="Chicxulub crater">Chicxulub impact</a> has been estimated to have released about as much energy as 100,000,000 megatons (100 teratons).</p>

<p>The exact effects of any impact would vary based on the asteroid's composition, and the location and angle of impact. Any impact would be extremely detrimental to an area of thousands of square kilometres, but would be unlikely to have long-lasting global effects, such as the initiation of an <a href="/wiki/Impact_winter" title="Impact winter">impact winter</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Assuming Apophis is a 325-metre-wide (1,066 ft) stony asteroid, if it were to impact into <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>, Apophis would create a 4.3-kilometre (14,000 ft) <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact crater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth-impact_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Earth-impact-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/B612_Foundation" title="B612 Foundation">B612 Foundation</a> made estimates of Apophis's path if a 2036 Earth impact were to occur, as part of an effort to develop viable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_deflection_strategies" title="Asteroid deflection strategies">deflection strategies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The result was a narrow corridor a few kilometres wide, called the "path of risk", extending across southern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, across the north <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> (relatively close to the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>), then right between <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, crossing northern <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, ending in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>, just before reaching <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Using the computer simulation tool NEOSim, it was estimated that the hypothetical impact of Apophis in countries such as Colombia and Venezuela, which were in the path of risk, could have more than 10 million casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, the exact location of the impact would be known weeks or even months in advance, allowing any nearby inhabited areas to be completely evacuated and significantly decreasing the potential loss of life and property. A deep-water impact in the Atlantic or Pacific oceans would produce an incoherent short-range <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> with a potential destructive radius (inundation height of &gt;2 m) of roughly 1,000 kilometres (620 mi) for the most of North America, Brazil and Africa, 3,000 kilometres (1,900 mi) for Japan and 4,500 kilometres (2,800 mi) for some areas in Hawaii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2007, The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_Society" title="Planetary Society">Planetary Society</a>, a <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>-based space advocacy group, organized a $50,000 competition to design an unmanned space probe that would 'shadow' Apophis for almost a year, taking measurements that would "determine whether it will impact Earth, thus helping governments decide whether to mount a deflection mission to alter its orbit". The society received 37 entries from 20 countries on 6 continents.</p>
<p>The commercial competition was won by a design called 'Foresight' created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceWorks_Enterprises,_Inc." title="SpaceWorks Enterprises, Inc.">SpaceWorks Enterprises, Inc.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-competition_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-competition-52">[52]</a></sup> SpaceWorks proposed a simple orbiter with only two instruments and a radio beacon at a cost of ~140 million USD, launched aboard a <a href="/wiki/Minotaur_(rocket_family)" title="Minotaur (rocket family)">Minotaur IV</a> between 2012 and 2014, to arrive at Apophis five to ten months later. It would then rendezvous with, observe, and track the asteroid. Foresight would orbit the asteroid to gather data with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-spectral_image" title="Multi-spectral image">multi-spectral imager</a> for one month. It would then leave orbit and fly in formation with Apophis around the Sun at a range of two kilometres (1.2 miles). The spacecraft would use laser ranging to the asteroid and radio tracking from Earth for ten months to accurately determine the asteroid's orbit and how it might change.</p>
<p>Pharos, the winning student entry, would be an orbiter with four science instruments (a multi-spectral imager, <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared_spectroscopy" title="Near-infrared spectroscopy">near-infrared spectrometer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser_rangefinder" title="Laser rangefinder">laser rangefinder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>) that would rendezvous with and track Apophis. Earth-based tracking of the spacecraft would then allow precise tracking of the asteroid. The Pharos spacecraft would also carry four instrumented probes that it would launch individually over the course of two weeks. <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">Accelerometers</a> and temperature sensors on the probes would measure the seismic effects of successive probe impacts, a creative way to explore the interior structure and dynamics of the asteroid.</p>
<p>Second place, for $10,000, went to a European team led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deimos_Space" title="Deimos Space">Deimos Space</a> S.L. of <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, in cooperation with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EADS_Astrium" title="EADS Astrium">EADS Astrium</a>, Friedrichshafen, Germany; <a href="/wiki/University_of_Stuttgart" title="University of Stuttgart">University of Stuttgart</a>, Germany; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A0_di_Pisa" title="Università di Pisa">Università di Pisa</a>, Italy. Juan L. Cano was <a href="/wiki/Principal_investigator" title="Principal investigator">principal investigator</a>.</p>
<p>Another European team took home $5,000 for third place. Their team lead was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EADS_Astrium_Ltd" title="EADS Astrium Ltd">EADS Astrium Ltd</a>, United Kingdom, in conjunction with EADS Astrium SAS, France; IASF-Roma, INAF, Rome, Italy; <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a>, UK; Rheinisches Institut für Umweltforschung, Germany; <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory_of_Belgium" title="Royal Observatory of Belgium">Royal Observatory of Belgium</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Telespazio" title="Telespazio">Telespazio</a>, Italy. The principal investigator was Paolo D'Arrigo.</p>
<p>Two teams tied for second place in the Student Category: <a href="/wiki/Monash_University" title="Monash University">Monash University</a>, Clayton Campus, Australia, with Dilani Kahawala as principal investigator; and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>, with Jeremy Hollander as principal investigator. Each second place team won $2,000. A team from <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Polytechnic_University" title="Hong Kong Polytechnic University">Hong Kong Polytechnic University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_University_of_Science_and_Technology" title="Hong Kong University of Science and Technology">Hong Kong University of Science and Technology</a>, under the leadership of Peter Weiss, received an honorable mention and $1,000 for the most innovative student proposal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Don_Quijote_mission">Don Quijote mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Don Quijote mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apophis is one of two asteroids that were considered by the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> as the target of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Quijote_(space_probe)" title="Don Quijote (space probe)">Don Quijote mission concept</a> to study the effects of impacting an asteroid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_deflection_strategies">Proposed deflection strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=99942_Apophis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Proposed deflection strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid-impact_avoidance" title="Asteroid-impact avoidance">Asteroid-impact avoidance</a></div>
<p>Studies by NASA, ESA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and various research groups in addition to the Planetary Society contest teams,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> have described a number of proposals for deflecting Apophis or similar objects, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_tractor" title="Gravitational tractor">gravitational tractor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_deflection_strategies" title="Asteroid deflection strategies">kinetic impact</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_bomb" title="Nuclear bomb">nuclear bomb</a> methods.</p>
<p>On December 30, 2009, <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Perminov" title="Anatoly Perminov">Anatoly Perminov</a>, the director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">Russian Federal Space Agency</a>, said in an interview that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roscosmos" title="Roscosmos">Roscosmos</a> will also study designs for a possible deflection mission to Apophis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 16, 2011, researchers at China's <a href="/wiki/Tsinghua_University" title="Tsinghua University">Tsinghua University</a> proposed launching a mission to knock Apophis onto a safer course using an impactor spacecraft in a retrograde orbit, steered and powered by a solar sail. Instead of moving the asteroid on its potential resonant return to Earth, Shengping Gong and his team believe the secret is shifting the asteroid away from entering the gravitational keyhole in the first place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 15, 2016, Sabit Saitgarayev, of the <a href="/wiki/Makeyev_Rocket_Design_Bureau" title="Makeyev Rocket Design Bureau">Makeyev Rocket Design Bureau</a>, announced intentions to use <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBMs</a> to target relatively small <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">near-Earth objects</a>. Although the report stated that likely targets would be between the 20 to 50 metres in size, it was also stated that 99942 Apophis would be an object subject to tests by the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
