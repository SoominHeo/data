<p><b>Fauna</b> is all of the <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> life of any particular region or time. The corresponding term for <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> is <i><a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a></i>. Flora, fauna and other forms of life such as <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> are collectively referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biota_(taxonomy)" title="Biota (taxonomy)">biota</a>. <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">Zoologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontologists</a> use <i>fauna</i> to refer to a typical collection of animals found in a specific time or place, e.g. the "<a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a> fauna" or the "<a href="/wiki/Burgess_Shale" title="Burgess Shale">Burgess Shale</a> fauna". <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">Paleontologists</a> sometimes refer to a sequence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faunal_stage" title="Faunal stage">faunal stages</a>, which is a series of rocks all containing similar fossils.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Fauna</i> comes from the Greek names <a href="/wiki/Fauna_(deity)" title="Fauna (deity)">Fauna</a>, a Roman goddess of earth and fertility, the Roman god <a href="/wiki/Faunus" title="Faunus">Faunus</a>, and the related forest spirits called <a href="/wiki/Faun" title="Faun">Fauns</a>. All three words are cognates of the name of the Greek god <a href="/wiki/Pan_(god)" title="Pan (god)">Pan</a>, and <i>panis</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> equivalent of fauna. <i>Fauna</i> is also the word for a book that catalogues the animals in such a manner. The term was first used by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> from Sweden in the title of his 1745<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> work <i>Fauna Suecica</i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryofauna">Cryofauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cryofauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Cryofauna</i> are animals that live in, or very close to, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryptofauna">Cryptofauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cryptofauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Cryptofauna</i> are the fauna that exist in protected or concealed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microhabitats" title="Microhabitats">microhabitats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infauna">Infauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Infauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Infauna</i> are <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic</a> organisms that live within the bottom substratum of a body of water, especially within the bottom-most oceanic sediments, rather than on its surface. Bacteria and microalgae may also live in the interstices of bottom sediments. In general, infaunal animals become progressively smaller and less abundant with increasing water depth and distance from shore, whereas bacteria show more constancy in abundance, tending toward one million cells per milliliter of interstitial seawater.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epifauna">Epifauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Epifauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Epifauna, also called <i>epibenthos</i>, are aquatic animals that live on the bottom substratum as opposed to within it, that is, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> fauna that live on top of the sediment surface at the seafloor...</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macrofauna">Macrofauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Macrofauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Macrofauna</i> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> or soil organisms which are retained on a 0.5 mm sieve. Studies in the deep sea define macrofauna as animals retained on a 0.3 mm sieve to account for the small size of many of the taxa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megafauna">Megafauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Megafauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">Megafauna</a></div>
<p><i>Megafauna</i> are large animals of any particular region or time. For example, <a href="/wiki/Australian_megafauna" title="Australian megafauna">Australian megafauna</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meiofauna">Meiofauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Meiofauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meiobenthos" title="Meiobenthos">Meiobenthos</a></div>
<p><i>Meiofauna</i> are small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> that live in both marine and fresh water <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">environments</a>. The term <i>Meiofauna</i> loosely defines a group of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> by their size, larger than microfauna but smaller than macrofauna, rather than a taxonomic grouping. One environment for meiofauna is between grains of damp sand (see <a href="/wiki/Mystacocarida" title="Mystacocarida">Mystacocarida</a>).</p>
<p>In practice these are metazoan animals that can pass unharmed through a 0.5 – 1 mm <a href="/wiki/Mesh" title="Mesh">mesh</a> but will be retained by a 30–45 μm mesh,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but the exact dimensions will vary from <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">researcher</a> to researcher. Whether an organism passes through a 1 mm mesh also depends upon whether it is alive or dead at the time of sorting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesofauna">Mesofauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mesofauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Soil_mesofauna" title="Soil mesofauna">Soil mesofauna</a></div>
<p><i>Mesofauna</i> are macroscopic soil invertebrates such as <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>. Mesofauna are extremely diverse; considering just the springtails (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collembola" title="Collembola">Collembola</a>), as of 1998, approximately 6,500 species had been identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microfauna">Microfauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fauna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Microfauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Microfauna" title="Microfauna">Microfauna</a></div>
<p><i>Microfauna</i> are microscopic or very small animals (usually including <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoans</a> and very small animals such as <a href="/wiki/Rotifer" title="Rotifer">rotifers</a>).</p>
