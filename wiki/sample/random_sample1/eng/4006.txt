<p><b>Animal fibers</b> are <a href="/wiki/Natural_fiber" title="Natural fiber">natural fibers</a> that consist largely of particular proteins. Instances are <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a>/<a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>) and feathers. The animal fibers used most commonly both in the manufacturing world as well as by the <a href="/wiki/Spinning_(textiles)" title="Spinning (textiles)">hand spinners</a> are wool from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep">domestic sheep</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>. Also very popular are <a href="/wiki/Alpaca#Fiber" title="Alpaca">alpaca fiber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mohair" title="Mohair">mohair</a> from <a href="/wiki/Angora_goat" title="Angora goat">Angora goats</a>. Unusual fibers such as <a href="/wiki/Angora_wool" title="Angora wool">Angora wool</a> from rabbits and <a href="/wiki/Chiengora" title="Chiengora">Chiengora</a> from dogs also exist, but are rarely used for mass production.</p>
<p>Not all animal fibers have the same properties, and even within a species the fiber is not consistent. <a href="/wiki/Merino" title="Merino">Merino</a> is a very soft, fine <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_sheep" title="Cotswold sheep">Cotswold</a> is coarser, and yet both merino and Cotswold are types of <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>. This comparison can be continued on the microscopic level, comparing the diameter and structure of the fiber. With animal fibers, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_fiber" title="Natural fiber">natural fibers</a> in general, the individual fibers look different, whereas all <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">synthetic fibers</a> look the same. This provides an easy way to differentiate between natural and synthetic fibers under a microscope.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Silk">Silk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Silk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silk_raw_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1917" data-file-width="2856" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Silk_raw_01.jpg/220px-Silk_raw_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Silk_raw_01.jpg/330px-Silk_raw_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Silk_raw_01.jpg/440px-Silk_raw_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silk_raw_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Raw silk</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">Silk</a> is a "natural" <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>, some forms of which can be <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">woven</a> into <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>. The best-known type of silk is obtained from <a href="/wiki/Pupa#Cocoon" title="Pupa">cocoons</a> made by the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of the silkworm <i><a href="/wiki/Bombyx_mori" title="Bombyx mori">Bombyx mori</a></i> reared in captivity. Rearing of silks is called <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">sericulture</a>. Degummed fibers from <i>B. mori</i> are 5-10 μm in diameter. The shimmering appearance for which silk is prized comes from the fibers' triangular <a href="/wiki/Prism_(geometry)" title="Prism (geometry)">prism</a>-like cross-sectional structure which allows silk cloth to refract incoming light at different angles.</p>
<p>The length of the silk fiber depends on how it has been prepared. Since the cocoon is made of one strand, if the cocoon is unwound carefully the fibers can be very long.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spider_silk" title="Spider silk">Spider silk</a> is the strongest natural fiber known. The strongest dragline silk is five times stronger than steel and three times tougher than <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a>. It is also highly elastic, the silk of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ogre-faced_spider" title="Ogre-faced spider">ogre-faced spider</a> can be stretched six times its original length without damage. As of 2005, there is no synthetic material in production that can match spider silk, but it is actively being sought by the U.S. military for such applications as body armour, parachutes and rope. Genetically engineered goats have been raised to produce spider silk in their milk at a cost of around $1,500 per gram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wool">Wool</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chantier_de_fouilles_%C3%A0_Morigny-Champigny_en_juin_2012_23.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Wool</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">Wool</a> is the fiber derived from the fur of animals of the <a href="/wiki/Caprinae" title="Caprinae">Caprinae</a> family, principally <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, but the hair of certain species of other mammals such as goats, alpacas, and <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a> may also be called wool.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alpaca">Alpaca</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alpaca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpaca_wool" title="Alpaca wool">Alpaca fiber</a> is that of an <a href="/wiki/Alpaca" title="Alpaca">alpaca</a>. It is warmer than sheep's wool and lighter in weight. It is soft, fine, glossy, and luxurious. The thickness of quality fiber is between 12-29 micrometers. Most alpaca fiber is white, but it also comes in various shades of brown and black.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angora">Angora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Angora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Angora_wool" title="Angora wool">Angora wool</a> or Angora fiber refers to the down coat produced by the <a href="/wiki/Angora_rabbit" title="Angora rabbit">Angora rabbit</a>. There are many types of Angora rabbits - English, French, German and Giant. Angora is prized for its softness, thin fibers of around 12-16 micrometers for quality fiber, and what knitters refer to as a halo (fluffiness). The fiber <a href="/wiki/Felt" title="Felt">felts</a> very easily. Angora fiber comes in white, black, and various shades of brown.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bison">Bison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1470" data-file-width="2500" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg/220px-Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg/330px-Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg/440px-Monida_and_Yellowstone_Stage_Company_bison_gloves_and_wool_blanket_-_Paugh_Regional_History_Hall_-_Museum_of_the_Rockies_-_2013-07-08.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">Bison</a> is the soft undercoat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Bison" title="American Bison">American Bison</a>. The coat of the bison contains two different types of fiber. The main coat is made up of coarse fibers (average 59 micrometers) called guard hairs, and the downy undercoat (average 18.5 micrometers). This undercoat is shed annually and consists of fine, soft fibers which are very warm and protect the animal from harsh winter conditions. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cashmere">Cashmere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cashmere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cashmere_wool" title="Cashmere wool">Cashmere wool</a> is wool obtained from the Cashmere goat. Cashmere is characterized by its luxuriously soft fibers, with high napability and loft. In order for a natural goat fiber to be considered Cashmere, it must be under 18.5 micrometers in diameter and be at least 3.175 centimeters long. It is noted as providing a natural light-weight insulation without bulk. Fibers are highly adaptable and are easily constructed into fine or thick yarns, and light to heavy-weight fabrics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mohair">Mohair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mohair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mohair" title="Mohair">Mohair</a> is a silk-like fabric or yarn made from the hair of the <a href="/wiki/Angora_goat" title="Angora goat">Angora goat</a>. It is both durable and resilient. It is notable for its high luster and sheen, and is often used in fiber blends to add these qualities to a textile. Mohair also takes dye exceptionally well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sheep.27s_wool">Sheep's wool</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sheep's wool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wool has two qualities that distinguish it from hair or fur: it has scales which overlap like shingles on a roof and it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crimp_(wool)" title="Crimp (wool)">crimped</a>; in some fleeces the wool fibers have more than 20 bends per inch. Wool varies in diameter from below 17 micrometers to over 35 micrometers. The finer the wool, the softer it will be, while coarser grades are more durable and less prone to <a href="/wiki/Pill_(textile)" title="Pill (textile)">pilling</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qiviut">Qiviut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Qiviut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Qiviut" title="Qiviut">Qiviut</a> is the fine underwool of the <a href="/wiki/Muskox" title="Muskox">muskox</a>. Qiviut fibers are long (about 5 to 8 cm), fine (between 15 and 20 micrometers in diameter), and relatively smooth. It is approximately eight times warmer than sheep's wool and does not felt or shrink.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiber_from_other_animals">Fiber from other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animal_fiber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fiber from other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hand spinners also use fiber from animals such as llamas, camels, yak, and possums. These fibers are generally used in clothing.</p>
<p>Hair from animals such as <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> is also an animal fiber. <a href="/wiki/Horsehair" title="Horsehair">Horsehair</a> is used for <a href="/wiki/Brush" title="Brush">brushes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">bows</a> of musical instruments and many other things. The best artists brushes are made from <a href="/wiki/Siberian_weasel" title="Siberian weasel">Siberian weasel</a>, many other fibers are used including <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">ox</a> hair and <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">hog</a> bristle. <a href="/wiki/Camel-hair_brush" title="Camel-hair brush">Camel-hair brushes</a> are usually made from <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrel</a>, cheaper ones from <a href="/wiki/Pony" title="Pony">pony</a>, but never <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camels</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chiengora" title="Chiengora">Chiengora</a> is <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> hair.</p>
