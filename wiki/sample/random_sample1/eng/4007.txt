<p>Sleep can follow a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiological" title="Physiological">physiological</a> or <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavioral</a> definition. In the physiological sense, sleep is a state characterized by reversible unconsciousness, special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_activity" title="Brain activity">brainwave</a> patterns, sporadic eye movement, loss of <a href="/wiki/Muscle_tone" title="Muscle tone">muscle tone</a> (possibly with some exceptions; see below regarding the sleep of birds and of aquatic mammals), and a compensatory increase following deprivation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the behavioral sense, sleep is characterized by non-responsiveness to external stimuli, the adoption of a typical posture, and the occupation of a sheltered site, all of which is usually repeated on a 24-hour basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The physiological definition applies well to birds and mammals, but in other animals (whose brain is not as complex), the behavioral definition is more often used. In very simple animals, behavioral definitions of sleep are the only ones possible, and even then the behavioral repertoire of the animal may not be extensive enough to allow distinction between sleep and wakefulness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolau_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicolau-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sleep as a phenomenon appears to have very old evolutionary roots. The <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">C. elegans</a></i> is the most primitive organism in which sleep-like states have been observed. Here, a <i><a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">lethargus</a></i> phase occurs in short periods preceding each <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moult</a>, a fact which may indicate that sleep primitively is connected to developmental processes. Raizen <i>et al.'</i>s results<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> furthermore suggest that sleep is necessary for changes in the neural system.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrophysiological" title="Electrophysiological">electrophysiological</a> study of sleep in small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> is complicated. However, even such simple animals as <a href="/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster" title="Drosophila melanogaster">fruit flies</a> appear to sleep, and systematic disturbance of that state leads to <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive disabilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There are several methods of measuring cognitive functions in fruit flies. A common method is to let the flies choose whether they want to fly through a tunnel that leads to a light source, or through a dark tunnel. Normally, flies are attracted to light. But if <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> is placed in the end of the dark tunnel, and something the flies dislike is placed in the end of the light tunnel, the flies will eventually learn to fly towards darkness rather than light. Flies deprived of sleep require a longer time to learn this and also forget it more quickly. If an <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a> is experimentally kept awake longer than it is used to, then its coming rest period will be prolonged. In <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a> that rest period is characterized by the <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a> being folded down and by a decreased sensitivity to external stimuli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Sleep has been described in <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a>, too, characterized by passivity and increased thresholds for sensory stimuli as well as changes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EEG" title="EEG">EEG</a> pattern, markedly differing from the patterns found in crayfish when they are awake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_in_fish">Sleep in fish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sleep in fish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sleep_in_fish" title="Sleep in fish">Sleep in fish</a></div>
<p>Sleep in <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> is not extensively studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some species that always live in shoals or that swim continuously (because of a need for ram ventilation of the gills, for example) are suspected never to sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There is also doubt about certain blind species that live in <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Other fish seem to sleep, however. For example, <a href="/wiki/Zebrafish" title="Zebrafish">zebrafish</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tilapia" title="Tilapia">tilapia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tench" title="Tench">tench</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brown_bullhead" title="Brown bullhead">brown bullhead</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swell_shark" title="Swell shark">swell shark</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> become motionless and unresponsive at night (or by day, in the case of the swell shark); <a href="/wiki/Spanish_hogfish" title="Spanish hogfish">Spanish hogfish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thalassoma_bifasciatum" title="Thalassoma bifasciatum">blue-headed wrasse</a> can even be lifted by hand all the way to the surface without evoking a response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A 1961 observational study of approximately 200 species in European public aquaria reported many cases of apparent sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On the other hand, sleep patterns are easily disrupted and may even disappear during periods of migration, spawning, and parental care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, the electrical activity in the brain has been registered when the animals have been asleep. However, the EEG pattern in reptilian sleep differs from what is seen in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolau_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicolau-4">[4]</a></sup> In reptiles, sleep time increases following <a href="/wiki/Sleep_deprivation" title="Sleep deprivation">sleep deprivation</a>, and stronger stimuli are needed to awaken the animals when they have been deprived of sleep as compared to when they have slept normally. This suggests that the sleep which follows deprivation is compensatorily deeper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>In 2016, a study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> report the existence of REM- and NREM-like sleep stages in the Australian dragon <i>Pogona vitticeps</i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_in_birds">Sleep in birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sleep in birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are significant similarities between sleep in <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and sleep in mammals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> which is one of the reasons for the idea that sleep in higher animals with its division into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/REM_sleep" title="REM sleep">REM</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NREM_sleep" title="NREM sleep">NREM sleep</a> has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolved" title="Evolved">evolved</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Warm-blooded" title="Warm-blooded">warm-bloodedness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Birds compensate for sleep loss in a manner similar to mammals, by deeper or more intense SWS (<a href="/wiki/Slow-wave_sleep" title="Slow-wave sleep">slow-wave sleep</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Birds have both REM and NREM sleep, and the EEG patterns of both have similarities to those of mammals. Different birds sleep different amounts, but the associations seen in mammals between sleep and variables such as body mass, brain mass, relative brain mass, <a href="/wiki/Basal_metabolic_rate" title="Basal metabolic rate">basal metabolism</a> and other factors (see below) are not found in birds. The only clear explanatory factor for the variations in sleep amounts for birds of different species is that birds who sleep in environments where they are exposed to <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a> have less deep sleep than birds sleeping in more protected environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>A peculiarity that birds share with aquatic mammals, and possibly also with certain species of <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> (opinions differ about that last point), is the ability for unihemispheric sleep. That is the ability to sleep with one <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere" title="Cerebral hemisphere">cerebral hemisphere</a> at a time, while the other hemisphere is awake (<a href="/wiki/Unihemispheric_slow-wave_sleep" title="Unihemispheric slow-wave sleep">Unihemispheric slow-wave sleep</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> When only one hemisphere is sleeping, only the contralateral eye will be shut; that is, when the right hemisphere is asleep the left eye will be shut, and vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The distribution of sleep between the two hemispheres and the amount of unihemispheric sleep are determined both by which part of the brain has been the most active during the previous period of wake<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>—that part will sleep the deepest—and it is also determined by the risk of attacks from predators. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ducks" title="Ducks">Ducks</a> near the perimeter of the flock are likely to be the ones that first will detect predator attacks. These ducks have significantly more unihemispheric sleep than those who sleep in the middle of the flock, and they react to threatening stimuli seen by the open eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Opinions partly differ about sleep in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migratory_bird" title="Migratory bird">migratory birds</a>. The controversy is mainly about whether they can sleep while flying or not. Theoretically, certain types of sleep could be possible while flying, but technical difficulties preclude the recording of brain activity in birds while they are flying.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_in_mammals">Sleep in mammals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sleep in mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_duration">Sleep duration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sleep duration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>Different <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> sleep different amounts. Some, such as <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, sleep 18–20 hours per day, while others, including <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">giraffes</a>, sleep only 3–4 hours per day. There can be big differences even between closely related species. There can also be differences between laboratory and field studies: for example, researchers in 1983 reported that captive <a href="/wiki/Sloth" title="Sloth">sloths</a> slept nearly 16 hours a day, but in 2008, when miniature neurophysiological recorders were developed that could be affixed to wild animals, sloths in nature were found to sleep only 9.6 hours a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mother_cubs.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sleeping <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a></div>

<p>As with birds, the main rule for mammals (with certain exceptions, see below) is that they have two essentially different stages of sleep: REM and NREM sleep (see above). Mammals' feeding habits are associated with their sleep length. The daily need for sleep is highest in <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a>, lower in <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a> and lowest in <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a>. Humans sleep less than many other omnivores but otherwise not unusually much or unusually little in comparison with other mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_.282005.29_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel_.282005.29-32">[32]</a></sup> Many herbivores, like <a href="/wiki/Ruminantia" title="Ruminantia">Ruminantia</a> (such as cattle), spend much of their wake time in a state of drowsiness,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (March 2016)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which perhaps could partly explain their relatively low need for sleep. In herbivores, an inverse correlation is apparent between body mass and sleep length; big mammals sleep less than smaller ones. This correlation is thought to explain about 25% of the difference in sleep amount between different mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_.282005.29_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel_.282005.29-32">[32]</a></sup> Also, the length of a particular sleep cycle is associated with the size of the animal; on average, bigger animals will have sleep cycles of longer durations than smaller animals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title="This text contradicts material elsewhere on this page. (March 2016)">contradictory</span></a></i>]</sup> Sleep amount is also coupled to factors like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basal_metabolism" title="Basal metabolism">basal metabolism</a>, brain mass, and relative brain mass.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Mammals born with well-developed regulatory systems, such as the horse and giraffe, tend to have less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/REM_sleep" title="REM sleep">REM sleep</a> than the species which are less developed at birth, such as cats and rats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This appears to echo the greater need for REM sleep among newborns than among adults in most mammal species.</p>

<p>Reasons given for the wide variations include the fact that mammals "that nap in hiding, like bats or rodents tend to have longer, deeper snoozes than those on constant alert." Lions, which have little fear of predators also have relatively long sleep periods, while elephants have to eat most of the time to support their huge bodies. Little brown bats conserve their energy except for the few hours each night when their insect prey are available, and platypuses eat a high energy <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustacean</a> diet and, therefore, probably do not need to spend as much time awake as many other mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WinksJennifer_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-WinksJennifer-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_in_monotremes">Sleep in monotremes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sleep in monotremes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Monotreme" title="Monotreme">monotremes</a>, egg-laying mammals, are considered to represent one of the evolutionarily oldest groups of mammals, they have been subject to special interest in the study of mammalian sleep. As early studies of these animals could not find clear evidence for REM sleep, it was initially assumed that such sleep did not exist in monotremes, but developed after the monotremes branched off from the rest of the mammalian evolutionary line, and became a separate, distinct group. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EEG" title="EEG">EEG</a> recordings of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_stem" title="Brain stem">brain stem</a> in monotremes show a firing pattern that is quite similar to the patterns seen in REM sleep in higher mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In fact, the largest amount of REM sleep known in any animal is found in the <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The average sleep time in a 24-hour period of a platypus is said to be as long as 14 hours; though this may be because of their high-calorie <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustacean</a> diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WinksJennifer_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-WinksJennifer-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>Among others, <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a> belong to the aquatic mammals. Seals are grouped in <a href="/wiki/Earless_seal" title="Earless seal">earless seals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eared_seal" title="Eared seal">eared seals</a>, which have solved the problem of sleeping in water differently. Eared seals, like whales, show unihemispheric sleep. The sleeping half of the brain does not awaken when they surface to breathe. When one half of a seal's brain shows slow-wave sleep, the flippers and whiskers on its opposite side are immobile. While in the water, these seals have almost no REM sleep and may go a week or two without it. As soon as they move onto land they switch to bilateral REM sleep and NREM sleep comparable to land mammals, surprising researchers with their lack of "recovery sleep" after missing so much REM.</p>

<p>Earless seals sleep bihemispherically like most mammals, under water, hanging at the water surface or on land. They hold their breath while sleeping under water, and wake up regularly to surface and breathe. They can also hang with their nostrils above water and in that position have REM sleep, but they do not have REM sleep underwater.</p>
<p>REM sleep has been observed in the <a href="/wiki/Pilot_whale" title="Pilot whale">pilot whale</a>, a species of dolphin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Whales do not seem to have REM sleep, nor do they seem to have any problems because of this. One reason REM sleep might be difficult in marine settings is the fact that REM sleep causes muscular <a href="/wiki/Atony" title="Atony">atony</a>; that is to say, a functional paralysis of skeletal muscles that can be difficult to combine with the need to breathe regularly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_.282005.29_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel_.282005.29-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unihemispheric_sleep">Unihemispheric sleep</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unihemispheric sleep">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unihemispheric_slow-wave_sleep" title="Unihemispheric slow-wave sleep">Unihemispheric slow-wave sleep</a></div>
<p>Unihemispheric sleep refers to sleeping with only a single <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere" title="Cerebral hemisphere">cerebral hemisphere</a>. The phenomenon has been observed in <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_mammal" title="Aquatic mammal">aquatic mammals</a>, as well as in several reptilian species (the latter being disputed: many reptiles behave in a way which could be construed as unihemispheric sleeping, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EEG" title="EEG">EEG</a> studies have given contradictory results). Reasons for the development of unihemispheric sleep are likely that it enables the sleeping animal to receive stimuli—threats, for instance—from its environment, and that it enables the animal to fly or periodically surface to breathe when immersed in water. Only NREM sleep exists unihemispherically, and there seems to exist a continuum in unihemispheric sleep regarding the differences in the hemispheres: in animals exhibiting unihemispheric sleep, conditions range from one hemisphere being in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_sleep" title="Deep sleep">deep sleep</a> with the other hemisphere being awake to one hemisphere sleeping lightly with the other hemisphere being awake. If one hemisphere is selectively deprived of sleep in an animal exhibiting unihemispheric sleep (one hemisphere is allowed to sleep freely but the other is awoken whenever it falls asleep), the amount of deep sleep will selectively increase in the hemisphere that was deprived of sleep when both hemispheres are allowed to sleep freely.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurobiological" title="Neurobiological">neurobiological</a> background for unihemispheric sleep is still unclear. In experiments on <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> in which the connection between the left and the right halves of the brain stem has been severed, the brain hemispheres show periods of a desynchronized EEG, during which the two hemispheres can sleep independently of each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In these cats, the state where one hemisphere slept NREM and the other was awake, as well as one hemisphere sleeping NREM with the other state sleeping REM were observed. Interestingly, the cats were never seen to sleep REM sleep with one hemisphere while the other hemisphere was awake. This is in accordance with the fact that REM sleep, as far as is currently known, does not occur unihemispherically.</p>
<p>The fact that unihemispheric sleep exists has been used as an argument for the necessity of sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It appears that no animal has developed an ability to go without sleep altogether.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_in_hibernating_animals">Sleep in hibernating animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleep_(non-human)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sleep in hibernating animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Animals that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hibernate" title="Hibernate">hibernate</a> are in a state of <a href="/wiki/Torpor" title="Torpor">torpor</a>, differing from sleep. Hibernation markedly reduces the need for sleep, but does not remove it. Some hibernating animals end their hibernation a couple of times during the winter so that they can sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1945046_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1945046-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
