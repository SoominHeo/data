<p><b>Est</b> is one of <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>'s 13 administrative <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Burkina_Faso" title="Regions of Burkina Faso">regions</a>. It was created on 2 July 2001. The region's capital is <a href="/wiki/Fada_N%27gourma" title="Fada N'gourma">Fada N'gourma</a>. Five <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Burkina_Faso" title="Provinces of Burkina Faso">provinces</a> make up the region - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnagna" title="Gnagna">Gnagna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gourma_Province" title="Gourma Province">Gourma</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Komondjari" title="Komondjari">Komondjari</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kompienga" title="Kompienga">Kompienga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tapoa_Province" title="Tapoa Province">Tapoa</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2010, the population of the region was 1,369,233 with 50.97 per cent females. The population in the region was 8.70 per cent of the total population of the country. The child mortality rate was 98, infant mortality rate was 98 and the mortality of children under five was 186. As of 2007, the literacy rate in the region was 28.5 per cent, compared to a national average of 28.3 per cent. The coverage of cereal need compared to the total production of the region was 108.00 per cent.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Est_Region_(Burkina_Faso)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG/200px-Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG/300px-Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG/400px-Fada_N_Gourma_2.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Most of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burkino_Faso" title="Burkino Faso">Burkino Faso</a> is a wide plateau formed by riverine systems and is called falaise de Banfora. There are three major rivers, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Volta" title="Red Volta">Red Volta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Volta" title="Black Volta">Black Volta</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_Volta" title="White Volta">White Volta</a>, which cuts through different valleys. The climate is generally hot, with unreliable rains across different seasons. Gold and quartz are common minerals found across the country, while <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> deposits are also common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> The dry season is usually from October to May and rains are common during the wet season from June to September. The soil texture is porous and hence the yield is also poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The average elevation is around 200 m (660 ft) to 300 m (980 ft) above mean sea level. Among West African countries, Burkino Faso has the largest elephant population and the country is replete with game reserves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-geo1-3">[3]</a></sup> The southern regions are more tropical in nature and have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">Savannah</a> and forests. The principal river is the Black Volta, that originates in the southern region and drains into <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>. The areas near the rivers usually have flies like tsete and similium, which are carriers of sleep sickness and river blindness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> The average rainfall in the region is around 100 cm (39 in) compared to northern regions that receive only 25 cm (9.8 in) rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-geo1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Est_Region_(Burkina_Faso)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2010, the population of the region was 1,369,233 with 50.97 per cent females. The population in the region was 8.70 per cent of the total population of the country. The child mortality rate was 98, infant mortality rate was 98 and the mortality of children under five was 186.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-census_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-census-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2007, among the working population, there were 62.30 per cent employees, 3.30 per cent under employed, 32.60 per cent inactive people, 34.40 per cent not working and 1.80 unemployed people in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emp_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-emp-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Est_Region_(Burkina_Faso)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2007, there were 960.2 km (596.6 mi) of highways, 369.8 km (229.8 mi) of regional roads and 514 km (319 mi) of county roads. The first set of car traffic was 29, first set of two-wheeler traffic was 2,610 and the total classified road network was 1,844.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-road_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-road-6">[6]</a></sup> The total corn produced during 2015 was 85,485 tonnes, cotton was 53,645 tonnes, cowpea was 44,098 tonnes, ground nut was 38,078 tonnes, millet was 87,588 tonnes, rice was 34,907 tonnes and sorghum was 193,161 tonnes. The coverage of cereal need compared to the total production of the region was 108.00 per cent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prod_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-prod-7">[7]</a></sup> As of 2007, the literacy rate in the region was 28.5 per cent, compared to a national average of 28.3 per cent. The gross primary enrollment was 50.4 per cent, pos-primary was 15.7 per cent and gross secondary school enrollment was 3.7. There were 424 boys and 137 girls enrolled in the primary and post-secondary level. There were 43 teachers in primary &amp; post-secondary level, while there were 489 teachers in post-primary and post-secondary level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-school_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-school-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Burkina Faso got independence from French Colonial Empire during 1960. It was originally called Upper Volta. There have been military coups till 1983 when Captain Thomas Sankara took control and implemented radical left wing policies. He was outsed by Blaise Compaore, who continued for 27 years till 2014, when a popular uprising ended his rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As per Law No.40/98/AN in 1998, Burkina Faso adhered to decentralization to provide administrative and financial autonomy to local communities. There are 13 administrative regions, each governed by a Governor. The regions are subdivided into 45 provinces, which are further subdivided into 351 communes. The communes may be urban or rural and are interchangeable. There are other administrative entities like department and village. An urban commune has typically 10,000 people under it. If any commune is not able to get 75 per cent of its planned budget in revenues for 3 years, the autonomy is taken off. The communes are administered by elected Mayors. The communes are stipulated to develop economic, social and cultural values of its citizens. A commune has financial autonomy and can interact with other communes, government agencies or international entities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
