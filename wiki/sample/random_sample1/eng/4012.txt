<p>The <b>eastern lesser bamboo lemur</b> (<i>Hapalemur griseus</i>), also known as the <b>gray bamboo lemur</b> and the <b>gray gentle lemur</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemur</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, with three known subspecies. As its name suggests, the eastern lesser bamboo lemur feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemurs</a> of the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hapalemur" title="Hapalemur">Hapalemur</a></i> have more manual dexterity and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand%E2%80%93eye_coordination" title="Hand–eye coordination">hand–eye coordination</a> than most lemurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They are vertical climbers and jump from stalk to stalk in thick bamboo forests.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_lesser_bamboo_lemur">Eastern lesser bamboo lemur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_lesser_bamboo_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eastern lesser bamboo lemur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eastern lesser bamboo lemur (<i>Hapalemur griseus griseus</i>), also known as the gray bamboo lemur, eastern gray bamboo lemur, or gray gentle lemur, was the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">original species</a> described in 1795 by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Friedrich_Link" title="Johann Heinrich Friedrich Link">Johann Heinrich Friedrich Link</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoM3_pg322-325_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoM3_pg322-325-5">[5]</a></sup> It is grey in colour, sometimes with a red patch on its head. It averages 284 mm (11 in) in length with a tail of 37 mm (1.5 in). Based on data from more than one hundred transect surveys which took place between 2004 and 2009, the population is estimated to be declining. There is an estimated 818 individuals in <a href="/wiki/Ranomafana_National_Park" title="Ranomafana National Park">Ranomafana National Park</a> and the decline in numbers is due to hunting and habitat loss. It is listed by the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (<a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>) on <a href="/wiki/CITES#Appendix_1" title="CITES">Appendix 1</a> and the IUCN considers it to be <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gilbert.27s_bamboo_lemur">Gilbert's bamboo lemur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_lesser_bamboo_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gilbert's bamboo lemur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gilbert's bamboo lemur (<i>H. g. gilberti</i>), also known as Gilbert's gentle lemur or Beanamalao bamboo lemur, was described as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> in 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rabarivola2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rabarivola2007-7">[7]</a></sup> but was raised to species status in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mittermeier2008_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mittermeier2008-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2010, it was returned to subspecies status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoM3_pg326-327_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoM3_pg326-327-9">[9]</a></sup> Its exact distribution is not certain but it is known from a small area of east-central Madagascar from its type locality of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beanamalao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beanamalao (page does not exist)">Beanamalao</a>, from a small area north of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nesivolo_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nesivolo river (page does not exist)">Nesivolo river</a> and possibly from an area south of the <a href="/wiki/Mangoro_River" title="Mangoro River">Mangoro River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Onive_River" title="Onive River">Onive River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mittermeier2008_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mittermeier2008-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucngilbert_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucngilbert-10">[10]</a></sup> This subspecies lives in dense bamboo stands and areas of bamboo vines and is threatened by habitat loss and degradation. The IUCN considered this subspecies to be <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> and it is listed by the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES) on Appendix 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucngilbert_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucngilbert-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranomafana_bamboo_lemur">Ranomafana bamboo lemur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_lesser_bamboo_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ranomafana bamboo lemur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ranomafana bamboo lemur (<i>H. g. ranomafanensis</i>), or Ranomafana gentle lemur, is the third sub-species and is found in two widely separated populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoM3_pg328-331_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoM3_pg328-331-11">[11]</a></sup> The exact distribution is not known but in the west of the island it is found in the forests of <a href="/wiki/Tsingy_de_Bemaraha_Strict_Nature_Reserve" title="Tsingy de Bemaraha Strict Nature Reserve">Tsingy de Bemaraha</a>, probably as far north as the <a href="/wiki/Betsiboka_River" title="Betsiboka River">Betsiboka River</a>. The eastern population can be found in forests south of the Mangoro River and the Onive River within Ranomafana National Park. It lives in stands of dense bamboo and bamboo vines within tropical moist lowland and montane forest with three-quarters of its diet being bamboo. It will also eat fig leaves, flowers, fungi, grass stems, small fruits and sugar cane. Due to habitat loss the IUCN has categorised this species as <a href="/wiki/Data_deficient" title="Data deficient">data deficient</a>. It is listed by the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES) on Appendix 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
