<p><b>Simultaneous death</b> is a problem of <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a> which occurs when two people (sometimes referred to as <b>commorientes</b>) die at, or very near, the same time, and at least one of them is entitled to part or all of the other's estate on their death. This is usually the result of an un-natural death occurring from events such as an accident, a homicide, or a murder-suicide.</p>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, if there was any evidence whatsoever that one party had survived the other, even by a few moments, then the estates would be distributed in that order, though the decedents could write (or have written) a clause in the will that requires their property to be distributed as though each had predeceased the other.</p>
<p>Some wills now include <i>Titanic clauses</i> (named for the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>, which caused many simultaneous deaths among testators and executors). These clauses lay out explicit instructions for dealing with simultaneous death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simultaneous_death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In order to alleviate problems of proving simultaneous death, many states in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> have enacted the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Simultaneous_Death_Act" title="Uniform Simultaneous Death Act">Uniform Simultaneous Death Act</a>, which provides that each spouse will be treated as though they predeceased the other if they die within 120 hours of one another, unless a specific clause in the will deals with this particular possibility.</p>
<p>However, the Act also states that the 120-hour rule is not applicable if the end result would be that the estate is <a href="/wiki/Intestacy" title="Intestacy">intestate</a>, and would therefore be escheated to the state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simultaneous_death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common law provision that, without evidence, there can be no presumption as to which of the commorientes died first, was superseded by the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Property_Act_1925" title="Law of Property Act 1925">Law of Property Act 1925</a>, Section 184. Under this statute, where the order of death of two persons is uncertain, the elder of the two is deemed to have died first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This can cause difficulties where for example the elder person had children prior to marriage. The rules can be ousted if inappropriate by an explicit provision in a will. Wills generally have a <i>survivorship clause</i>, typically of 30 days, so that both partner's estates are dealt with as though they were already widowed at the point of death; in cases of <a href="/wiki/Intestacy" title="Intestacy">intestacy</a>, the survivorship clause is set at 28 days.</p>
<p>However it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Revenue_and_Customs" title="Her Majesty's Revenue and Customs">Her Majesty's Revenue and Customs</a>'s longstanding practice to apply a concessionary treatment for inheritance tax purposes in such cases which reduces the burden on surviving family members.</p>
