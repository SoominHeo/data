<p>The <b>winter melon</b>, also called <b>ash gourd</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMPND_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMPND-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>white gourd</b>, <b>winter gourd</b>, <b>tallow gourd</b> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMPND_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MMPND-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>Chinese preserving melon</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMPND_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-MMPND-2">[2]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vine</a> grown for its very large <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>, eaten as a <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetable</a> when mature.</p>
<p>It is the only member of the genus <i><b>Benincasa</b></i>. The fruit is fuzzy when young. The immature melon has thick white flesh that is sweet when eaten. By maturity, the fruit loses its hairs and develops a <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">waxy</a> coating, giving rise to the name <b>wax gourd</b>, and providing a long <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a>. The melon may grow as large as 80 cm in length. Although the fruit is referred to as a "melon," the fully grown crop is not sweet. Native to <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, the winter melon is widely grown throughout Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Winter melon is also a <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> for members of the Inodorus <a href="/wiki/Cultivar_group" title="Cultivar group">cultivar group</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Muskmelon" title="Muskmelon">muskmelon</a> (<i>Cucumis melo</i> L), more commonly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casaba" title="Casaba">casaba</a> or <a href="/wiki/Honeydew_(melon)" title="Honeydew (melon)">honeydew</a> melons.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_melon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The winter melon requires very warm weather to grow but can be stored for many months much like <a href="/wiki/Winter_squash" title="Winter squash">winter squash</a>. Ash gourds of the Indian subcontinent have a white coating with rough texture. South East Asian varieties have a smooth waxy texture. It is one of the few vegetables available during winter in areas of <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>, hence its Chinese name literally means 'winter melon'. The winter melon can typically be stored for 12 months. In India, ash gourd is recognized for its medicinal properties in the Ayurvedic system of medicine.</p>
<p>In Vietnamese cuisine, it is called <i>bí đao</i>, which is usually used to make soup or stew. When cooked with pork short ribs, the resulting soup is traditionally thought to help produce more milk for breastfeeding mothers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Chinese cuisine the melons are used in stir fry or usually combined with pork or pork/beef bones to make <a href="/wiki/Melon_soup" title="Melon soup">winter melon soup</a>, often served in the scooped out melon, carved by scraping off the waxy coating. It is also chopped and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candied" title="Candied">candied</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_candy_box" title="Chinese candy box">wintermelon candy</a> (táng dōng guā) to be commonly eaten at <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">New Year festivals</a>, or as filling for <a href="/wiki/Sweetheart_cake" title="Sweetheart cake">Sweetheart cake</a> (lǎopó bǐng). It has also been used as the base filling in Chinese and Taiwanese <a href="/wiki/Mooncake" title="Mooncake">mooncakes</a> for the Moon Festival.</p>
<p>Winter melon is candied (referred to plainly as <i>kundol</i>) and is used as a pastry filling for <a href="/wiki/Bakpia" title="Bakpia">bakpia</a> (<i>hopia</i> in the Philippines). It is also an ingredient in some savory soups (sabaw) and stir-fries (guisado). It is one of the vegetables mentioned in the Filipino folk song "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bahay_Kubo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bahay Kubo (page does not exist)">Bahay Kubo</a>."</p>
<p>In Indian cuisine the vegetable is traditionally used to prepare a wide variety of dishes. In northern India it is used to prepare a candy called <i><a href="/wiki/Petha" title="Petha">Petha</a></i>. In <a href="/wiki/South_Indian_cuisine" title="South Indian cuisine">South Indian cuisine</a>, it is traditionally used to make a variety of curries, including a liquefied dish with curds or buttermilk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulusu_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pulusu-5">[5]</a></sup> The juice of raw ash gourd is used by the <a href="/wiki/Mizo_people" title="Mizo people">Mizo community</a> of North-East India as a natural remedy to treat mild to severe dysentery.</p>
<p>Occasionally, it is used to produce a <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">fruit drink</a> with a very distinctive taste. It is usually sweetened with caramelized sugar. In Southeast Asia, the drink is widely marketed as winter melon tea or <a href="/wiki/Winter_melon_punch" title="Winter melon punch">winter melon punch</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Shoot" title="Shoot">shoots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tendril" title="Tendril">tendrils</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> of the plant may also be eaten as <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">greens</a>.</p>
