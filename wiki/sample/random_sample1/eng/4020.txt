<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chinese constellations</h1>

<p>Traditional <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">Chinese astronomy</a> has a system of dividing the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a> into <a href="/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy)" title="Asterism (astronomy)">asterisms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a>, known as "officials" (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%9F" title="wikt:星">星</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AE%98" title="wikt:官">官</a> <i>xīng guān</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Chinese asterisms are generally smaller than the constellations of Hellenistic tradition. The <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (13th-century) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzhou_planisphere" title="Suzhou planisphere">Suzhou planisphere</a> shows a total of 283 asterisms, comprising a total of 1,565 <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_star_names" title="Traditional Chinese star names">individual stars</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The asterisms are divided into four groups, the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Eight_Mansions" title="Twenty-Eight Mansions">Twenty-Eight Mansions</a> along the ecliptic, and the Three Enclosures of the northern sky. The southern sky was added as a fifth group in the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> based on European star charts, comprising an additional 23 asterisms.</p>
<p>The Three Enclosures (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B8%89" title="wikt:三">三</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9E%A3" title="wikt:垣">垣</a>, <i>Sān Yuán</i>) are centered on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Celestial_Pole" title="North Celestial Pole">North Celestial Pole</a> and include those stars which could be seen year-round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-needy-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Eight_Mansions" title="Twenty-Eight Mansions">Twenty-Eight Mansions</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BA%8C%E5%8D%81%E5%85%AB" title="wikt:二十八">二十八</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AE%BF" title="wikt:宿">宿</a>, <i>Èrshíbā Xiù</i>) form an <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">ecliptic coordinate system</a> used for those stars not visible (from China) during the whole year, based on the movement of the moon over a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">lunar month</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_constellations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_star_maps" title="Chinese star maps">Chinese star maps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Song_of_the_Sky_Pacers" title="Song of the Sky Pacers">Song of the Sky Pacers</a></div>
<p>The Chinese system developed independently from the Greco-Roman system since at least the 5th century BC, although there may have been earlier mutual influence, suggested by parallels to ancient <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">Babylonian astronomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The system of twenty-eight lunar mansions is very similar (although not identical) to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">Indian</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Nakshatra" title="Nakshatra">Nakshatra</a></i> system, and it is not currently known if there was mutual influence in the history of the Chinese and Indian systems.</p>
<p>The oldest extant <a href="/wiki/Chinese_star_maps" title="Chinese star maps">Chinese star maps</a> date to the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>. Notable among them are the 8th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Treatise_on_Astrology_of_the_Kaiyuan_Era" title="Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era">Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dunhuang_Star_Chart" title="Dunhuang Star Chart">Dunhuang Star Chart</a>.</i> It contains collections of earlier Chinese astronomers (<a href="/wiki/Shi_Shen" title="Shi Shen">Shi Shen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gan_De" title="Gan De">Gan De</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu_Xian_(astronomer)" title="Wu Xian (astronomer)">Wu Xian</a>) as well as of <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">Indian astronomy</a> (which had reached China in the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism" title="Silk Road transmission of Buddhism">early centuries AD</a>). Gan De was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a> era (5th century BC) astronomer who according to the testimony of the Dunhuang Star Chart enumerated 810 stars in 138 asterisms. The Dunhuang Star Chart itself has 1,585 stars grouped into 257 asterisms.</p>
<p>The number of asterisms, or of stars grouped into asterisms, never became fixed, but remained in the same order of magnitude (for the purpose of comparison, the star catalogue compiled by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> in the 2nd century had 1,022 stars in 48 constellations). The 13th-century Suzhou star chart has 1,565 stars in 283 asterisms, the 14th-century Korean <a href="/wiki/Cheonsang_Yeolcha_Bunyajido" title="Cheonsang Yeolcha Bunyajido">Cheonsang Yeolcha Bunyajido</a> has 1,467 stars in 264 asterisms, and the celestial globe made by Flemish Jesuit <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Verbiest" title="Ferdinand Verbiest">Ferdinand Verbiest</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a> in 1673 has 1,876 stars in 282 asterisms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The southern sky was unknown to the ancient Chinese and is consequently not included in the traditional system. With <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_Pires_de_Andrade" title="Fernão Pires de Andrade">European contact</a> in the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Xu_Guangqi" title="Xu Guangqi">Xu Guangqi</a> , an astronomer of the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>, introduced another 23 asterisms based on European star charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The "Southern Sky" (近南極星區) asterisms are now also treated as part of the traditional Chinese system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_constellations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chinese word for "star, heavenly body" is 星 <i><span lang="pny" xml:lang="pny">xīng</span></i>. The character 星 is phonosemantic, its ideographic portion is 晶 (the character for <i><span lang="pny" xml:lang="pny">jīng</span></i> "bright radiant"), in origin depicting three twinkling stars (three times the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E6%97%A5" title="日">"sun" radical 日</a>).</p>
<p>The modern Chinese term for "constellation" referring to the IAU system is <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">星座</span> (<i><span lang="pny" xml:lang="pny">xīng zuò</span></i>, 座 being a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Chinese_classifiers" title="List of Chinese classifiers">classifier</a> for large immovable objects), while the term <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">星官</span> <i><span lang="pny" xml:lang="pny">xīng guān</span></i> remains reserved for the traditional system. The character 官 means "public official" (hence the English translation "officials" for the Chinese asterisms), but it is historically a variant glyph of 宮 <i><span lang="pny" xml:lang="pny">gōng</span></i> "temple, palace", in origin a pictogram of a large building.</p>
<p>The generic term for "asterism" is <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">星群</span> (<i><span lang="pny" xml:lang="pny">xīng qún</span></i>, lit. "group of stars").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_Enclosures">Three Enclosures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_constellations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Three Enclosures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Three Enclosures are the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Forbidden_enclosure" title="Purple Forbidden enclosure">Purple Forbidden enclosure</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B4%AB" title="wikt:紫">紫</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BE%AE" title="wikt:微">微</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9E%A3" title="wikt:垣">垣</a>, <i>Zǐ Wēi Yuán</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Palace_enclosure" title="Supreme Palace enclosure">Supreme Palace enclosure</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%AA" title="wikt:太">太</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BE%AE" title="wikt:微">微</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9E%A3" title="wikt:垣">垣</a>, <i>Tài Wēi Yuán</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Heavenly_Market_enclosure" title="Heavenly Market enclosure">Heavenly Market enclosure</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A9" title="wikt:天">天</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B8%82" title="wikt:市">市</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9E%A3" title="wikt:垣">垣</a>, <i>Tiān Shì Yuán</i>).</p>
<p>Purple Forbidden enclosure covers the northernmost area of the night sky. The Supreme Palace enclosure lies to its east and north, while the Heavenly Market enclosure lies to its west and south.</p>
<p>The Three Enclosures are separated by named by <a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">synecdoche</a> for the asterisms separating them, designated 垣 <i>yuán</i> "low wall, fence; enclosure" (not to be confused with the lunar mansion "<a href="/wiki/Wall_(Chinese_constellation)" title="Wall (Chinese constellation)">"Wall" 壁</a>):</p>
<ul>
<li>Supreme Palace Left Wall 太微左垣 (Virgo / Coma Berenices)</li>
<li>Supreme Palace Right Wall 太微右垣 (Leo / Virgo)</li>
<li>Heavenly Market Left Wall 天市左垣 (Hercules / Serpens / Ophiuchus / Aquila)</li>
<li>Heavenly Market Right Wall 天市右垣 (Serpens / Ophiuchus / Hercules)</li>
</ul>
<p>The Purple Forbidden Enclosure occupies the northernmost area of the night sky. From the viewpoint of the ancient Chinese, the Purple Forbidden Enclosure lies in the middle of the sky and is circled by all the other stars. It covers the modern constellations Ursa Minor, Draco, Camelopardalis, Cepheus, Cassiopeia, Auriga, Boötes, and parts of Ursa Major, Canes Venatici, Leo Minor, Hercules.</p>
<p>The Supreme Palace Enclosure covers the modern constellations Virgo, Coma Berenices and Leo, and parts of Canes Venatici, Ursa Major and Leo Minor.</p>
<p>The Heavenly Market Enclosure covers the modern constellations Serpens, Ophiuchus, Aquila and Corona Borealis, and parts of Hercules.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Twenty-Eight_Mansions">The Twenty-Eight Mansions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_constellations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Twenty-Eight Mansions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Southern_Asterisms_.28.E8.BF.91.E5.8D.97.E6.A5.B5.E6.98.9F.E5.8D.80.29">The Southern Asterisms (近南極星區)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_constellations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Southern Asterisms (近南極星區)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sky around the south <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">celestial pole</a> was unknown to ancient Chinese. Therefore, it was not included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Enclosures" title="Three Enclosures">Three Enclosures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Eight_Mansions" title="Twenty-Eight Mansions">Twenty-Eight Mansions</a> system. However, by the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xu_Guangqi" title="Xu Guangqi">Xu Guangqi</a> introduced another 23 asterisms based on the knowledge of European star charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These asterisms were since incorporated into the traditional Chinese star maps.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_Chinese_star_names">Traditional Chinese star names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_constellations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Traditional Chinese star names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_star_names" title="Traditional Chinese star names">Traditional Chinese star names</a></div>
<p>Ancient Chinese astronomers designated names to the visible stars systematically, roughly more than one thousand years before <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bayer" title="Johann Bayer">Johann Bayer</a> did it in a similar way. Basically, every star is assigned to an asterism. Then a number is given to the individual stars in this asterism. Therefore, a star is designated as "Asterism name" + "Number". The numbering of the stars in an asterism, however, is not based on the apparent magnitude of this star, but rather its position in the asterism. (The Bayer system does use this Chinese method sometimes, most notably with the stars in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper" title="Big Dipper">Big Dipper</a>, which are all about the same magnitude.)</p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Altair" title="Altair">Altair</a> is named 河鼓二 in Chinese. 河鼓 is the name of the asterism (literally the Drum at the River). 二 is the number designation (two). Therefore, it literally means "the Second Star of the Drum at the River". (Bayer might have called Altair "Beta Tympani Flumine" if he had been cataloguing Chinese constellations.)</p>
<p>Some stars also have traditional names, often related to <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Altair" title="Altair">Altair</a> is more commonly known as 牛郎星 or 牵牛星 (the Star of the Cowherd) in Chinese, after the mythological story of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowherd_and_Weaver_Girl" title="Cowherd and Weaver Girl">Cowherd and Weaver Girl</a>.</p>
<p>These designations are still used in modern Chinese astronomy. All stars for which the traditional names are used in English are routinely translated by their traditional Chinese designations, rather than translations of their catalogue names.</p>
