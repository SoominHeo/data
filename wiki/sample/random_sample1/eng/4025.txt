<p><b>Mobilization</b>, in military terminology, is the act of assembling and readying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troops" title="Troops">troops</a> and supplies for <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>. The word <i>mobilization</i> was first used, in a military context, to describe the preparation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_army" title="Russian army">Russian army</a> during the 1850s and 1860s. Mobilization theories and techniques have continuously changed since then. The opposite of mobilization is <a href="/wiki/Demobilization" title="Demobilization">demobilization</a>.</p>
<p>Mobilization became an issue with the introduction of conscription, and the introduction of the railways in the 19th Century. Mobilization institutionalized the mass levy of forces that was first introduced during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, and that had changed the character of war. A number of technological and societal changes promoted the move towards a more organized way of assembling armies. These included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> to provide rapid communication, the railways to provide rapid movement and concentration of troops, and <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> to provide a trained <a href="/wiki/Reservist" title="Reservist">reserve</a> of soldiers in case of war.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Empire">Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> was able to mobilize at various times sizeable forces, which including poorly trained <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> totaled between 6% (81-83 BCE) to, in emergency and for short periods, as much as 10% (210s BCE) of total Roman population (which included as many as 75% of males aged 17–45).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LigtNorthwood2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-LigtNorthwood2008-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a> is estimated to have mobilized about 11% of its free population in <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> (1861-1865).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LigtNorthwood2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-LigtNorthwood2008-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> mobilized about 6-7% of its total population in the years 1760 and 1813.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LigtNorthwood2008_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-LigtNorthwood2008-1">[1]</a></sup> Sweden mobilized 7.7% in 1709.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LigtNorthwood2008_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-LigtNorthwood2008-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Armies in the seventeenth century possessed an average of 20,000 men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onorato_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Onorato-2">[2]</a></sup> A military force of this size requires around 40,000 pounds of food per day, shelter, as well as all the necessary munitions, transportation (typically horses or mules), tools, and representative garments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onorato_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Onorato-2">[2]</a></sup> Without efficient transportation, mobilizing these average-sized forces was extremely costly, time-consuming, and potentially life-threatening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onorato_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Onorato-2">[2]</a></sup> Soldiers could traverse the terrain to get to war fronts, but they had to carry their supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onorato_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Onorato-2">[2]</a></sup> Many armies decided to forage for food<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onorato_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Onorato-2">[2]</a></sup>— however, foraging restricted movement because it is based on the presumption that the land the army moves over possesses significant agricultural production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onorato_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Onorato-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, due to new policies (like conscription), greater populations, and greater nation wealth, the nineteenth century army was composed of an average of 100,000 men. For example, in 1812 Bonaparte led an army of 600,000 hyped young drafts to Moscow while feeding off plentiful agricultural products introduced by the turn of the century, such as potatoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite the advantages of mass armies, mobilizing forces of this magnitude took much more time than it had in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Italian unification illustrated all of the problems in modern army mobilization for Prussia. It was not until Italian Unification that Prussia began to realize the future of mobilizing mass armies when Napoleon III transported 130,000 soldiers to Italy by use of the railroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-4">[4]</a></sup> French caravans that carried the supplies for the French and Piedmontese armies were incredibly slow and the arms inside these caravans were sloppily organized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vincennes_845_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vincennes_845-5">[5]</a></sup> These armies were in luck, however, in that their Austrian adversaries experienced similar problems with sluggish supply caravans (one of which apparently covered less than three miles per day).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vincennes_845_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vincennes_845-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not only did Prussia take note of the problems in transporting supplies to armies but it also took note of the lack of communication between troops, officers, and generals. Austria’s army was primarily composed of Slavs but it contained many other ethnicities as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DN_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-DN-6">[6]</a></sup> Austrian military instruction during peacetime utilized nine different languages, accustoming Austrian soldiers to taking orders only in the language they natively know.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DN_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-DN-6">[6]</a></sup> Conversely, in an effort to augment the efficacy of the new “precision rifle” developed by the monarchy, officers were forced to only speak German when giving orders to their men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DN_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-DN-6">[6]</a></sup> Even one Austrian officer commented at Solferino that his troops could not even comprehend the command, “Halt.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DN_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-DN-6">[6]</a></sup> This demonstrates the <i>communicative</i> problems that arose quickly with the advent of the mass army.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobilization_in_World_War_I">Mobilization in World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mobilization in World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schlieffen_Plan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="745" data-file-width="963" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Schlieffen_Plan.jpg/220px-Schlieffen_Plan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Schlieffen_Plan.jpg/330px-Schlieffen_Plan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Schlieffen_Plan.jpg/440px-Schlieffen_Plan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schlieffen_Plan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the <a href="/wiki/Schlieffen_Plan" title="Schlieffen Plan">Schlieffen Plan</a> and planned French counter-offensives</div>

<p>Intricate plans for mobilization contributed greatly to the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, since in 1914, under the laws and customs of warfare then observed (not to mention the desire to avoid compromising national security), general mobilization of one nation's military forces was invariably considered an <a href="/wiki/Casus_belli" title="Casus belli">act of war</a> by that country's likely enemies.</p>
<p>In 1914, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> was the only <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Power" title="Great Power">Great Power</a> without <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a>. The other Great Powers (<a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a>) all relied on compulsory military service to supply each of their armies with the millions of men they believed they would need to win a major war. France enacted the “<a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_France" title="Conscription in France">Three Year Law</a>” (1913) to extend the service of conscripted soldiers to match the size of the German army, as the French population of 40 million was smaller than the German population of 65 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The British and the Germans also started a race to build up a stronger navy, sparked by the German enactment of the <a href="/wiki/German_Naval_Laws" title="German Naval Laws">Second Naval Law</a>. Each of the Great Powers could only afford to keep a fraction of these men in uniform in peacetime, the rest were <a href="/wiki/Military_reserve" title="Military reserve">reservists</a> with limited opportunities to train. Maneuvering formations of millions of men with limited military training required intricate plans with no room for error, confusion, or discretion after mobilization began. These plans were prepared under the assumption of <a href="/wiki/Scenario" title="Scenario">worst-case scenarios</a>.</p>
<p>For example, German military leaders did not plan to mobilize for war with Russia whilst assuming that France would not come to her ally's aid, or vice versa. The <a href="/wiki/Schlieffen_Plan" title="Schlieffen Plan">Schlieffen Plan</a> therefore dictated not only mobilization against both powers, but also the order of attack—France would be attacked first regardless of the diplomatic circumstances. To bypass the fortified Franco-German frontier, the German forces were to be ordered to march through Belgium. Whether or not Russia had committed the first provocation, the German plan agreed to by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_II,_German_Emperor" title="William II, German Emperor">Emperor William II</a> called for the attack on Russia to take place only after France was defeated.</p>

<p>Similarly, the Russian <a href="/wiki/Stavka" title="Stavka">Stavka</a>'s war planning assumed that war against either <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> would mean war against the other power. Although the plan allowed flexibility as to whether the main effort would be made against <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> or <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>, in either case units would be mobilized on the frontiers of both Powers. On July 28, 1914, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsar_Nicholas_II" title="Tsar Nicholas II">Tsar Nicholas II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (William's cousin) ordered partial mobilization against <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> only. While war with <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> seemed inevitable, Nicholas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Willy_and_Nicky" title="Willy and Nicky">engaged in a personal dialogue with the German Emperor</a> in an attempt to avoid war with Germany. However, Nicholas was advised that attempts to improvise a partial mobilization would lead to chaos and probable defeat if, as pessimists on the Russian side expected, no amount of diplomacy could convince the Germans to refrain from attacking Russia whilst she was engaged with Germany's ally. On July 29, 1914, the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> ordered full mobilization, then changed his mind after receiving a telegram from Kaiser Wilhelm. Partial mobilization was ordered instead.</p>
<p>The next day, the Tsar's <a href="/wiki/Foreign_minister" title="Foreign minister">foreign minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Sazonov" title="Sergey Sazonov">Sergey Sazonov</a> once more persuaded Nicholas of the need for general mobilization, and the order was issued that day, July 30. In response, Germany declared war on Russia.</p>
<p>Germany mobilized under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helmuth_Johann_Ludwig_von_Moltke" title="Helmuth Johann Ludwig von Moltke">von Moltke the Younger</a>'s revised version of the <a href="/wiki/Schlieffen_Plan" title="Schlieffen Plan">Schlieffen Plan</a>, which assumed a two-front war with Russia and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Like Russia, Germany decided to follow its two-front plans despite the one-front war. Germany declared war on France on August 3, 1914, one day after issuing an ultimatum to <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> demanding the right of German troops to pass through as part of the planned pincer action of the military. Finally, Britain declared war on Germany for violating Belgian neutrality.</p>
<p>Thus the entangling alliances of the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Alliance_(1882)" title="Triple Alliance (1882)">Triple Alliance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Entente" title="Triple Entente">Triple Entente</a> directed the intricate plans for mobilization. This brought all of the Great Powers of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> into the Great War without actually utilizing the provisions of either alliance.</p>

<p>The mobilization was like a holiday for many of the inexperienced soldiers; for example, some Germans wore flowers in the muzzles of their rifles as they marched. Trains brought soldiers to the front lines of battle. The Germans timetabled the movements of 11,000 trains as they brought troops across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhine_River" title="Rhine River">Rhine River</a>. The French mobilized around 7,000 trains for movement. <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">Horses</a> were also mobilized. The British had 165,000 horses prepared for <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>, the Austrians 600,000, the Germans 715,000, and the Russians over a million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Britain's <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> including <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> were compelled to go to war when Britain did. However, it was largely left up to the individual Dominions to recruit and equip forces for the war effort. Canadian, Australian and New Zealand mobilizations all involved the creation of new field forces for overseas service rather than using the existing regimental structures as a framework. In the case of Canada, the Militia Minister, Sir <a href="/wiki/Sam_Hughes" title="Sam Hughes">Sam Hughes</a>, created the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Expeditionary_Force" title="Canadian Expeditionary Force">Canadian Expeditionary Force</a> from whole cloth by sending telegrams to 226 separate reserve unit commanders asking for volunteers to muster at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valcartier" title="Valcartier">Valcartier</a> in Quebec. The field force served separately from the Militia (Canada's peace time army); in 1920 the <a href="/wiki/Otter_Commission" title="Otter Commission">Otter Commission</a> was compelled to sort out which units would perpetuate the units that served in the trenches—the CEF or the prewar Militia. A unique solution of perpetuations was instituted, and mobilization during the Second World War did not repeat Sir Sam Hughes' model, which has been described by historians as being more closely akin to ancient Scottish clans assembling for battle than a modern, industrialized nation preparing for war.</p>
<p>"Colonials" served under British command though, perhaps owing to the limited autonomy granted to the Dominions regarding their respective mobilizations, the Dominions eventually compelled the British government to overrule the objections of some British commanders and let the Dominion forces serve together instead of being distributed amongst various British divisions. The "colonials" would go on to be acknowledged by both the British and German high commands as being elite British units. In May 1918, when command of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Corps" title="Australian Corps">Australian Corps</a> passed from <a href="/wiki/William_Birdwood" title="William Birdwood">William Birdwood</a> to <a href="/wiki/John_Monash" title="John Monash">John Monash</a>, it became the first British Empire formation commanded totally free of British officers.</p>
<p>On May 23, 1915, Italy entered World War I on the Allied side. Despite being the weakest of the big four Allied powers, the Italians soon managed to populate its army from 560 to 693 infantry battalions in 1916; the army had grown in size from a million to one and a half million soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On August 17, 1916, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> entered the war on the Allied side, mobilizing an army of 23 divisions. Romania was quickly defeated however by Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Bulgaria. Bulgaria went so far as to ultimately mobilize 1,2 million men, more than a quarter of its population of 4.3 million people, a greater share of its population than any other country during the war.</p>
<p>The production of supplies gradually increased throughout the war. In Russia, the expansion of industry allowed a 2,000 percent increase in the production of artillery shells - by November 1915, over 1,512,000 artillery shells were being produced per month. In France, a massive mobilization by the female population to work in factories allowed the rate of shell production to reach 100,000 shells a day by 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both sides also began drawing on larger amounts of soldiers. The British Secretary of State for War, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horatio_Kitchener,_1st_Earl_Kitchener" title="Horatio Kitchener, 1st Earl Kitchener">Lord Kitchener</a>, appealed for hundreds of thousands of soldiers, which was met with an enthusiastic response. 30 new British divisions were created. The response by volunteers allowed the British to put off the introduction of conscription until 1916. New Zealand followed suit, with Canada also eventually introducing conscription.</p>
<p>On April 6, 1917, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> entered the war on the Allied side. At the entrance, the U.S. only could mobilize its army of 107,641 soldiers, ranked only seventeenth in size worldwide at the time. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> quickly mobilized, adding 5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dreadnoughts" title="Dreadnoughts">dreadnoughts</a> to the Allied navy. However, conscription quickly ensued. By March 1918, 318,000 U.S. soldiers had been mobilized to France. Eventually, a force of 3 million U.S. soldiers joined in the war effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobilization_in_World_War_II">Mobilization in World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mobilization in World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Poland</a> partly mobilized its troops on August 24, 1939, and fully mobilized on August 30, 1939, following the increased confrontations with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a> since <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Polish_military_alliance" title="Anglo-Polish military alliance">March 1939</a>. On September 1, 1939 <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> invaded <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, which prompted both France and Britain to declare war on Germany. However, they were slow to mobilize, and by the time Poland had been overrun by the Axis powers, only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phony_War" title="Phony War">minor operations</a> had been carried out by the French at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saar_River" title="Saar River">Saar River</a>.</p>
<p>Canada actually carried out a partial mobilization on 25 August 1939. in anticipation of the growing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diplomatic_crisis" title="Diplomatic crisis">diplomatic crisis</a>. On September 1, 1939, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_Active_Service_Force&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canadian Active Service Force (page does not exist)">Canadian Active Service Force</a> (a corps-sized force of two divisions) was mobilized even though war was not declared by Canada until September 10, 1939. Only one division went overseas in December 1939, and the government hoped to follow a "limited liability" war policy. When France was invaded in May 1940, the Canadian government realized that would not be possible and mobilized three additional divisions, beginning their overseas employment in August 1940 with the dispatch of the 2nd Canadian Division (some units of which were deployed to Iceland and Newfoundland for garrison duty before moving to the UK). Canada also enacted the National Resources Mobilization Act in 1940, which among other things compelled men to serve in the military, though conscripts mobilized under the NRMA did not serve overseas until 1944. Conscripts did, however, serve in the Aleutian Islands in 1943 though the anticipated Japanese defense never materialized due to the evacuation of the enemy garrison before the landings. Service in the Aleutians was not considered "overseas" as technically the islands were part of North America.</p>
<p>The United Kingdom mobilized 22% of its total population for direct military service, more than any other nation of WWII era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Axelrod2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Axelrod2007-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobilization_during_the_Cold_War">Mobilization during the Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mobilization during the Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a></div>
<p>Full scale mobilizations were not enacted by the two Super Powers, or their allies, during the Cold War, as the era was characterized as "war by proxy". While many leading nations participated in military actions, some of significant size, national mobilization in the historical sense never occurred. Examples of this would be US involvement in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam</a>, though their enemies may be said to have mobilized, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> between the UK and Argentina.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_mobilization">Economic mobilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economic mobilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Economic mobilization is the preparation of resources for usage in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_emergency" title="National emergency">national emergency</a> by carrying out changes in the organization of the national economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is reorganizing the functioning of the national economy to use resources most effectively in support of the total war effort. Typically, the available resources and productive capabilities of each nation determined the degree and intensity of economic mobilization. Thus, effectively mobilizing economic resources to support the war effort is a complex process, requiring superior coordination and productive capability on a national scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
