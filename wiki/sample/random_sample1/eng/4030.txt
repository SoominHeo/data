<p>The <b>East Indies</b> or <b>Indies</b> are the lands of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> In a more restricted sense, the Indies can be used to refer to the islands of Southeast Asia, especially the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">Malay Archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The name "Indies" is derived from the river <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus</a> and is used to connote parts of Asia that came under <a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">Indian cultural influence</a> (except <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> which is in the <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_cultural_sphere" title="East Asian cultural sphere">Chinese cultural sphere</a>).</p>
<p>Dutch-held colonies in the area were known for about 300 years as the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_independence" title="Indonesian independence">Indonesian independence</a>, while Spanish-held colonies were known as the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish East Indies</a> before the US conquest and later Philippines' independence. The East Indies may also include the former French-held <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>, former British territories <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, and former Portuguese <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a>. It does not, however, include the former Dutch New Guinea <a href="/wiki/Western_New_Guinea" title="Western New Guinea">western New Guinea</a> (West Papua), which is geographically considered to be part of <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>.</p>
<p>The inhabitants of the East Indies are almost never called East Indians, distinguishing them both from inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> (which is also called the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>) and from the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> who are often called "American Indians." In colonial times they were just "natives". However, the peoples of the East Indies comprise a wide variety of cultural diversity, and the inhabitants do not consider themselves as belonging to a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic" title="Ethnic">ethnic</a> group. <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> are the most popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_world_religions" title="Major world religions">religions</a> throughout the region, while <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folk_religion" title="Chinese folk religion">Chinese folk religion</a> and various other traditional beliefs and practices are also prominent in some areas. The major languages in this area draw from a wide variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_families" title="Language families">language families</a>, and should not be confused with the term <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indic</a>, which refers only to a group of <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">Indo-Iranian languages</a> from <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.</p>
<p>The extensive East Indies are subdivided into two sections (from a European perspective), archaically called Hither India and <a href="/wiki/Farther_India" title="Farther India">Further India</a>. The first is the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_India" title="British India">British India</a>, the second is <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.</p>
<p>Regions of the East Indies are sometimes known by the colonial empire they once belonged to, hence, <i>British East Indies</i> refers to <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> means <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish East Indies</a> means the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.</p>
<p>Historically, the king of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Empire" title="Ethiopian Empire">Abyssinia</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>) was identified with "<a href="/wiki/Prester_John" title="Prester John">Prester John</a> of the Indies", since that part of the world was imagined to be one of "Three Indias".</p>

<div class="legend"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #F96; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">Indies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>)</div>
<div class="legend"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #F5DEB3; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Papua_(region)" title="West Papua (region)">Western New Guinea</a></div>
<div class="legend"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #1E90FF; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a></div>
<div class="legend"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ADD8E6; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> <b>Countries sometimes included in West Indies</b></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Exploration of these regions by <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> powers first began in the late 15th century and early 16th century led by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Portuguese</a> <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Exploration">explorers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-WDL-3">[3]</a></sup> The Portuguese described the entire region they discovered as the <i>Indies</i>. Eventually, the region would be broken up into a series of <i>Indies.</i> The East Indies, which was also called "Old Indies" or "Great Indies", consisting of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and the West Indies, also called "New Indies" or "Little Indies", consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>These regions were important sources of trading goods, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> after the establishment of European trading companies: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_East_India_Company" title="British East India Company">British East India Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a>, among others, in the 17th century.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> was initially thought to be the easternmost part of the <i>Indies</i> by explorer <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, who had grossly underestimated the westerly distance from Europe to Asia. Later, to avoid confusion, the New World came to be called the "West Indies", while the original Indies came to be called the "East Indies".</p>
<p>The designation <i>East Indian</i> was once primarily used to describe people of all of the East Indies, in order to avoid the potential confusion from the term <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">American Indian</a> who were once simply referred to as <i>Indians</i> (see the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_name_controversy" title="Native American name controversy">Native American name controversy</a> for more information).</p>
