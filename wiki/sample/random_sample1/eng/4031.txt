<p><i><b>Dōjinshi</b></i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">同人誌</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>, often transliterated as <i>doujinshi</i>)</span> is the Japanese term for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-published" title="Self-published">self-published</a> works, usually magazines, <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> or novels. Dōjinshi are often the work of amateurs, though some professional artists participate as a way to publish material outside the regular industry. Dōjinshi are part of a wider category of <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin" title="Dōjin">dōjin</a> including art collections, <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hentai" title="Hentai">hentai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a>. Groups of dōjinshi artists refer to themselves as a <i>sākuru</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">サークル</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin_circle" title="Dōjin circle">circle</a>)</span>. A number of such groups actually consist of a single artist: they are sometimes called <i>kojin sākuru</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">個人サークル</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>, personal circles)</span>.</p>
<p>Since the 1980s, the main method of distribution has been through regular dōjinshi conventions, the largest of which is called <a href="/wiki/Comiket" title="Comiket">Comiket</a> (short for "Comic Market") held in the summer and winter in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Big_Sight" title="Tokyo Big Sight">Big Sight</a>. At the convention, over 20 acres (81,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of dōjinshi are bought, sold, and traded by attendees. Dōjinshi creators who base their materials on other creators' works normally publish in small numbers to maintain a low profile so as to protect themselves against litigation. This makes a talented creator's or circle's dōjinshi a coveted commodity as only the fast or the lucky will be able to get them before they sell out.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term dōjinshi is derived from <i><a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin" title="Dōjin">dōjin</a></i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">同人</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>, literally "same person", used to refer to a person or persons with whom one shares a common goal or interest)</span> and <i>shi</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">誌</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>, a suffix generally meaning "periodical publication")</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pioneer among dōjinshi was <i>Meiroku Zasshi</i> (明六雑誌), published in the early <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a> (since 1874). Not a literary magazine in fact, <i>Meiroku Zasshi</i> nevertheless played a big role in spreading the idea of dōjinshi. The first magazine to publish dōjinshi novels was <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Garakuta_Bunko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Garakuta Bunko (page does not exist)">Garakuta Bunko</a></i> (我楽多文庫), founded in 1885 by writers <a href="/wiki/Ozaki_K%C5%8Dy%C5%8D" title="Ozaki Kōyō">Ozaki Kōyō</a> and Yamada Bimyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Dōjinshi publication reached its peak in the early <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_period" title="Shōwa period">Shōwa period</a>, and dōjinshi became a mouthpiece for the creative youth of that time. Created and distributed in small circles of authors or close friends, dōjinshi contributed significantly to the emergence and development of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shish%C5%8Dsetsu" title="Shishōsetsu">shishōsetsu</a></i> genre. During the postwar years, dōjinshi gradually decreased in importance as outlets for different literary schools and new authors. Their role was taken over by literary journals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Gunzo_(magazine)" title="Gunzo (magazine)">Gunzo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bungakukai" title="Bungakukai">Bungakukai</a></i> and others. One notable exception was <i>Bungei Shuto</i> (文芸首都 lit. <i>Literary Capital</i>), which was published from 1933 until 1969. Few dōjinshi magazines survived with the help of official literary journals. <i><a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">Haiku</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Waka_(poetry)" title="Waka (poetry)">tanka</a></i> magazines are still published today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It has been suggested that technological advances in the field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photocopying" title="Photocopying">photocopying</a> during the 1970s contributed to an increase in publishing dojinshi. During this time, manga editors were encouraging manga authors to appeal to a mass market, which may have also contributed to an increase in the popularity of writing dojinshi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galbraith_2011_211.E2.80.93232_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galbraith_2011_211.E2.80.93232-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1980s, the content of dōjinshi shifted from being predominantly original content to being mostly parodic of existing series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pedagogy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pedagogy-3">[3]</a></sup> Often called <i>aniparo</i>, this was often an excuse to feature certain characters in romantic relationships. Male authors focused on series like <i><a href="/wiki/Urusei_Yatsura" title="Urusei Yatsura">Urusei Yatsura</a></i>, and female authors focused on series like <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Tsubasa" title="Captain Tsubasa">Captain Tsubasa</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galbraith_2011_211.E2.80.93232_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Galbraith_2011_211.E2.80.93232-2">[2]</a></sup> This coincided with the rise in popularity of <a href="/wiki/Comiket" title="Comiket">Comiket</a>, the first event dedicated specifically to the distribution of dōjinshi, which had been founded in 1975.</p>
<p>As of February 1991, there were some dōjinshi creators who sold their work through supportive comic book stores. This practice came to light when three managers of such shops were arrested for having a <a href="/wiki/Lolicon" title="Lolicon">lolicon</a> dōjinshi for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orbaugh_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orbaugh-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the last decade, the practice of creating dōjinshi has expanded significantly, attracting thousands of creators and fans alike. Advances in personal publishing technology have also fueled this expansion by making it easier for dōjinshi creators to write, draw, promote, publish, and distribute their works. For example, some dōjinshi are now published on digital media. Furthermore, many dōjinshi creators are moving to online download and print-on-demand services, while others are beginning to distribute their works through American channels such as anime shop websites and specialized online direct distribution sites. In 2008, a <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Otaku" title="Otaku">otaku</a> industry was published, this estimated that gross revenue from sales of dōjinshi in 2007 were 27.73 billion yen, or 14.9% of total otaku expenditure on their hobby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perception">Perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>John Oppliger of <a href="/wiki/AnimeNation" title="AnimeNation">AnimeNation</a> stated that creating dōjinshi is largely popular with Japanese fans however not with Western fans. Oppliger claimed that because Japanese natives grow up with anime and manga "as a constant companion", Japanese fans "are more intuitively inclined" to create or expand on existing manga and anime in the form of dōjinshi .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Because Western fans experience a "more purely" visual experience as most Western fans cannot understand the Japanese language, the original language of most <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, and are "encouraged by social pressure to grow out of cartoons and comics during the onset of adolescence", most Western fans participate in utilizing and rearranging existing work into <a href="/wiki/Anime_music_video" title="Anime music video">anime music videos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Western cultures, dōjinshi is often perceived to be derivative of existing work, analogous to <a href="/wiki/Fan_fiction" title="Fan fiction">fan fiction</a> and almost completely pornographic. This is partly true: dōjinshi are often, though not always, parodies or alternative storylines involving the worlds of popular manga, game or <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> series, and can often feature overtly sexual material. However, there are also many non sexually explicit dōjinshi being created as well. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touhou" title="Touhou">Touhou</a> series for example, is notable for the large amount of dōjinshi being produced for it that are not pornographic in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-touhou_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-touhou-9">[9]</a></sup> Groups releasing adults-only themed materials during the annual Touhou only event Reitaisai in 2008 were estimated at roughly 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-touhou_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-touhou-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">Categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like their mainstream counterparts, dōjinshi are published in a variety of genres and types. However, due to the target audience, certain themes are more prevalent, and there are a few major division points by which the publications can be classified. It can be broadly divided into original works and <i>aniparo</i>—works which parody existing anime and manga franchises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fanfic" title="Fanfic">fanfics</a>, a very popular theme to explore is non-canonical pairings of characters in a given show (for dōjinshi based on mainstream publications). Many such publications contain <a href="/wiki/Yaoi" title="Yaoi">yaoi</a> or <a href="/wiki/Yuri_(genre)" title="Yuri (genre)">yuri</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hentai" title="Hentai">hentai</a> involving two or more males resp. females) motives, either as a part of non-canon pairings, or as a more direct statement of what can be hinted by the main show.</p>
<p>Another category of dōjinshi is <a href="/wiki/Furry_fandom" title="Furry fandom">furry</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kemono" title="Kemono">kemono</a>, often depicting homosexual male pairings of furries and, less often, <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> pairings. Furry dōjinshi shares some characteristics with the yaoi and yuri genres, with many furry dōjinshis depicting characters in erotic settings or circumstances and/or incorporating elements typical of <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> and manga, such as exaggerated drawings of eyes or facial expressions.</p>
<p>A major part of dōjinshi, whether based on mainstream publications or original, contains sexually explicit material, due to both the large demand for such publications and absence of restrictions official publishing houses have to follow. Indeed, often the main point of a given dōjinshi is to present an explicit version of a popular show's characters. Such works may be known to English speakers as "H-dōjinshi", in line with the former <a href="/wiki/Ecchi#Japanese_usage" title="Ecchi">Japanese use of letter H</a> to denote erotic material. The Japanese usage, however, has since moved towards the word <i>ero</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and so <i>ero manga</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">エロ漫画</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is the term almost exclusively used to mark dōjinshi with adult themes. Sometimes they will also be termed "for adults" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">成人向け</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>seijin muke</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> or <i>18-kin</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">18禁</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (an abbreviation of <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">18歳未満禁止</span></span> "forbidden to minors less than 18 years of age"). To differentiate, <i>ippan</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">一般</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, , "general", from the general public it is suitable for)</span> is the term used for publications absent of such content.</p>
<p>Most dōjinshi are commercially bound and published by dōjinshi-ka (dōjinshi authors) who self-publish through various printing services. <a href="/wiki/Copybooks" title="Copybooks">Copybooks</a>, however, are self-made using xerox machines or other copying methods. Few are copied by drawing by hand.</p>
<p>Not all category terms used by English-language fans of dōjinshi are derived from Japanese. For example, an <i>AU</i> dōjinshi is one set in an alternate universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elfgrove_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-elfgrove-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comiket">Comiket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comiket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Comiket" title="Comiket">Comiket</a> is the world's largest comic convention. It is held twice a year (summer and winter) in Tokyo, Japan. The first CM was held in December 1975, with only about 32 participating circles and an estimated 600 attendees. About 80% of these were female, but male participation in Comiket increased later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pedagogy_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pedagogy-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1982, there were fewer than 10,000 attendees, this increased to over 100,000 attendees as of 1989. This rapid increase in attendance enabled dōjinshi authors to sell thousands of copies of their works, earning a fair amount of money with their hobby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Attendance has since swelled to over half a million people. Many attendants come to exchange and/or sell their dōjinshi.</p>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Meiji_University" title="Meiji University">Meiji University</a> opened a <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin" title="Dōjin">dōjin</a> manga library, named “<a href="/wiki/Yoshihiro_Yonezawa" title="Yoshihiro Yonezawa">Yoshihiro Yonezawa</a> Memorial Library” to honour its <a href="/wiki/Alumnus" title="Alumnus">alumni</a> in its Surugadai campus. It contains Yonezawa's own dōjinshi collection, comprising 4137 boxes, and the collection of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tsuguo_Iwata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tsuguo Iwata (page does not exist)">Tsuguo Iwata</a>, another famous person in the sphere of dōjinshi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_issues">Copyright issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Copyright issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite being in direct conflict with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_copyright_law" title="Japanese copyright law">Japanese copyright law</a> as many dōjinshi are derivative works and dōjinshi artists rarely secure the permission of the original creator, Comiket is still permitted to be held twice a year and holds over half-a-million people attending each time it convenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lessig_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lessig-15">[15]</a></sup> However, the practice of dōjinshi can be beneficial to the commercial manga market by creating an avenue for aspiring manga artists to practice,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mehra_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mehra-16">[16]</a></sup> and talented dōjinshi creators are contacted by publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This practice has existed since the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonking_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bonking-18">[18]</a></sup> Salil Mehra, a law professor at <a href="/wiki/Temple_University" title="Temple University">Temple University</a>, hypothesizes that because dōjinshi market actually causes the manga market to be more productive, the law does not ban dōjinshi as the industry would suffer as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mehra_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mehra-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two notable instances of legal action over dōjinshi. In 1999, the author of an erotic <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Pokémon">Pokémon</a> manga was prosecuted by Nintendo. This created a media furor as well as an academic analysis in Japan of the copyright issues around dōjinshi. At this time, the legal analysis seemed to conclude that dōjinshi should be overlooked because they are produced by amateurs for one-day events and not sold in the commercial market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2006, an artist selling an imagined "final chapter" for the series <i><a href="/wiki/Doraemon" title="Doraemon">Doraemon</a></i>, which was never completed, was given a warning by the estate of author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fujiko_F._Fujio" title="Fujiko F. Fujio">Fujiko F. Fujio</a>. His creation apparently looked confusingly similar to a real Doraemon manga. He ceased distribution of his dōjinshi and sent compensation to the publisher voluntarily. The publisher noted at this time that dōjinshi were not usually a cause of concern for him. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Shinbun" title="Yomiuri Shinbun">Yomiuri Shinbun</a> noted, "Fanzines don't usually cause many problems as long as they are sold only at one-day exhibitions," but quoted an expert saying that due to their increasing popularity a copyright system should be set up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individuals">Individuals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Individuals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoshitoshi_Abe" title="Yoshitoshi Abe">Yoshitoshi Abe</a> has published some of his original works as dōjinshi, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Haibane_Renmei" title="Haibane Renmei">Haibane Renmei</a></i>. He cited the reason as, essentially, not wanting to answer to anyone about his work, especially because he saw it as so open ended.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ken_Akamatsu" title="Ken Akamatsu">Ken Akamatsu</a>, creator of manga such as <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Hina" title="Love Hina">Love Hina</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negima" title="Negima">Negima</a></i>, continues to make dōjinshi which he sells at Comiket under the pen-name Awa Mizuno.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kiyohiko_Azuma" title="Kiyohiko Azuma">Kiyohiko Azuma</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Azumanga_Daioh" title="Azumanga Daioh">Azumanga Daioh</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yotsuba%26" title="Yotsuba&amp;">Yotsuba&amp;</a></i> started out doing dōjinshi using the pen-name A-Zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doujin_listing_for_azuma_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-doujin_listing_for_azuma-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nanae_Chrono" title="Nanae Chrono">Nanae Chrono</a>, creator of the manga <i><a href="/wiki/Peacemaker_Kurogane" title="Peacemaker Kurogane">Peacemaker Kurogane</a></i>, has published multiple <i><a href="/wiki/Naruto" title="Naruto">Naruto</a></i> dōjinshi, most of a <a href="/wiki/Yaoi" title="Yaoi">yaoi</a> nature.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kazushi_Hagiwara" title="Kazushi Hagiwara">Kazushi Hagiwara</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Bastard!!" title="Bastard!!">Bastard!!</a></i>, and his group <i>Studio Loud in School</i> have published popular Bastard!!-related dōjinshi such as <i>Wonderful Megadeth!</i>, as well as various <a href="/wiki/Capcom" title="Capcom">Capcom</a>-related dōjinshi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Masaki_Kajishima" title="Masaki Kajishima">Masaki Kajishima</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Tenchi_Muyo!_Ryo-Ohki" title="Tenchi Muyo! Ryo-Ohki">Tenchi Muyo! Ryo-Ohki</a></i>, has long used the dōjinshi format to produce additional information about the series he has created, primarily <i>Tenchi Muyo! Ryo-Ohki</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tenchi_Muyo!_GXP" title="Tenchi Muyo! GXP">Tenchi Muyo! GXP</a></i>. These dōjinshi can either be completely filled with his work, or he will contribute a work to the dōjinshi title. Kajishima's dōjinshi works break down into one (or more) types of works: manga-style (where he illustrates a new story, usually with limited text), interviews, early drafts of scripts for the series (giving fans great insight into the creative process), storyboards drawn by Kajishima that ultimately were not animated, story notes (or short stories) giving further little details of various characters, situations, or places in Kajishima's World of Tenchi. As of this writing, Kajishima does two dōjinshi titles a year under the circle names "Kajishima Onsen" and "Kamidake Onsen". He has also used these to communicate with fans about his current projects, namely the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Knight%27s_Tale" title="Saint Knight's Tale">Saint Knight's Tale</a></i> spinoff anime featuring Tenchi's half-brother and the <i>GXP</i> novels.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monkey_Punch" title="Monkey Punch">Kazuhiko Katō</a>, also known as Monkey Punch, creator of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupin_III" title="Lupin III">Lupin III</a></i> began as a dōjinshi artist.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kodaka_Kazuma" title="Kodaka Kazuma">Kodaka Kazuma</a>, creator of <a href="/wiki/Kizuna:_Bonds_of_Love" title="Kizuna: Bonds of Love">Kizuna</a>, Rotten Teacher's Equation (Kusatta Kyōshi no Hōteishiki), Love Equation (Renai Hōteishiki) and Border among others, has published several parody yaoi dōjinshi as K2 Company of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_of_Tennis" title="Prince of Tennis">Prince of Tennis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist" title="Fullmetal Alchemist">Fullmetal Alchemist</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiger_and_Bunny" title="Tiger and Bunny">Tiger and Bunny</a></i>, as well as an original dōjinshi series called 'Hana to Ryuu' (Flower and Dragon).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rikdo_Koshi" title="Rikdo Koshi">Rikdo Koshi</a>, creator of the manga <i><a href="/wiki/Excel_Saga" title="Excel Saga">Excel Saga</a></i>, originally started out as a dōjinshi artist.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yun_Kouga" title="Yun Kouga">Yun Kouga</a>, a longtime published manga artist and creator of two well-known BL series, <i><a href="/wiki/Earthian" title="Earthian">Earthian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Loveless_(manga)" title="Loveless (manga)">Loveless</a></i> has published dōjinshi for series such as <i>Gundam Wing</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiger_and_Bunny" title="Tiger and Bunny">Tiger and Bunny</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sanami_Matoh" title="Sanami Matoh">Sanami Matoh</a>, creator of <a href="/wiki/Fake_(manga)" title="Fake (manga)">FAKE</a>, has published parody yaoi dōjinshi (mostly of One Piece) and original dōjinshi as East End Club.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maki_Murakami" title="Maki Murakami">Maki Murakami</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Gravitation_(manga)" title="Gravitation (manga)">Gravitation</a></i> and <i>Gamers' Heaven</i>. Her circle <b>Crocodile Ave.</b> created <i>Remix Gravitation</i> AKA <i>Rimigra</i> and <i>Megamix Gravitation</i>, which were extremely sexually graphic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Minami_Ozaki" title="Minami Ozaki">Minami Ozaki</a>, creator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boy%27s_love" title="Boy's love">boy's love</a> manga <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zetsuai" title="Zetsuai">Zetsuai</a></i>, is an extremely prolific dōjinshi creator. She authored numerous yaoi dōjinshi before her debut as a professional artist, most notably featuring characters from the soccer manga <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Tsubasa" title="Captain Tsubasa">Captain Tsubasa</a></i>. The main characters of her manga <i>Zetsuai</i> strongly resemble the main characters of her <i>Captain Tsubasa</i> dōjinshi. Ozaki continued to release dōjinshi about her own professional manga, often including sexual content that could not be published in <a href="/wiki/Margaret_(magazine)" title="Margaret (magazine)">Margaret</a>, the young girls-oriented manga magazine in which <i>Zetsuai</i> was serialized.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yukiru_Sugisaki" title="Yukiru Sugisaki">Yukiru Sugisaki</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/D.N.Angel" title="D.N.Angel">D.N.Angel</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Candidate_for_Goddess" title="The Candidate for Goddess">The Candidate for Goddess</a></i>, started as a dōjinka. She released dōjinshi about <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Fighters" title="King of Fighters">King of Fighters</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion_franchise" title="Neon Genesis Evangelion franchise">Evangelion</a></i>, etc.; all were gag dōjinshi.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rumiko_Takahashi" title="Rumiko Takahashi">Rumiko Takahashi</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Ranma_%C2%BD" title="Ranma ½">Ranma ½</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Inuyasha" title="Inuyasha">Inuyasha</a></i>, made dōjinshi before she became a professional artist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yoshihiro_Togashi" title="Yoshihiro Togashi">Yoshihiro Togashi</a>, creator of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YuYu_Hakusho" title="YuYu Hakusho">YuYu Hakusho</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_x_Hunter" title="Hunter x Hunter">Hunter x Hunter</a></i>, has authored dōjinshi such as <i>Church!</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hajime_Ueda" title="Hajime Ueda">Hajime Ueda</a>, the creator of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Q%E2%80%A2Ko-chan:_The_Earth_Invader_Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Q•Ko-chan: The Earth Invader Girl (page does not exist)">Q•Ko-chan</a></i> and the comic adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/FLCL" title="FLCL">FLCL</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nobuteru_Y%C5%ABki" title="Nobuteru Yūki">Nobuteru Yūki</a> sells dōjinshi based on his animated works under his pen-name "<a href="/wiki/The_Man_in_the_High_Castle" title="The Man in the High Castle">The Man in the High Castle</a>".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kana_Ueda" title="Kana Ueda">Kana Ueda</a>, creator of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoha_Strikers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nanoha Strikers (page does not exist)">Nanoha Strikers</a> futanari doujin. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Girl_lovers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Girl lovers (page does not exist)">Girl lovers</a> several as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teana_Lanster" title="Teana Lanster">Teana Lanster</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subaru_Nakajima" title="Subaru Nakajima">Subaru Nakajima</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signum_(Nanoha)" title="Signum (Nanoha)">Signum</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yagami_Hayate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yagami Hayate (page does not exist)">Yagami Hayate</a> and more.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yana_Toboso" title="Yana Toboso">Yana Toboso</a> used to be a Yaoi dōjinka before she authored <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuroshitsuji" title="Kuroshitsuji">Kuroshitsuji</a></i>, which explained why there are some notable BL hints throughout the series.</li>
<li>Sunao Minakata, the illustrator of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akuma_no_Riddle" title="Akuma no Riddle">Akuma no Riddle</a> is a regular doujinka, especially in girls' love theme. Usually makes Touhou dōjinshi and has collaborated with other known-for-Touhou-works-popular artists, such as Banpai Akira.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Online">Online</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C5%8Djinshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Online">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleedman" title="Bleedman">Bleedman</a>, creator of the online <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powerpuff_Girls_Doujinshi" title="Powerpuff Girls Doujinshi">Powerpuff Girls Doujinshi</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grim_Tales_From_Down_Below" title="Grim Tales From Down Below">Grim Tales From Down Below</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Snafu_Comics" title="Snafu Comics">Sugar Bits</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fred_Gallagher_(cartoonist)" title="Fred Gallagher (cartoonist)">Fred Gallagher</a>, creator of the <i><a href="/wiki/Megatokyo" title="Megatokyo">Megatokyo</a></i> series, as well as the in-development series <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Warmth_(manga)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Warmth (manga) (page does not exist)">Warmth</a></i>. His <i>Megatokyo</i> co-creator and former writer, <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Caston" title="Rodney Caston">Rodney "Largo" Caston</a>, can also be considered one, though Caston has since left the business.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Kim_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daniel Kim (artist) (page does not exist)">Daniel Kim</a>, creator of the <a href="/wiki/Cardcaptor_Sakura" title="Cardcaptor Sakura">Cardcaptor Sakura</a> parody <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tomoyo42%27s_Room&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tomoyo42's Room (page does not exist)">Tomoyo42's Room</a></i>, has written and illustrated several dōjinshi, all of which are hosted at the <a class="external text" href="http://manga.clone-army.org/" rel="nofollow">Clone Manga</a> collective.</li>
<li>Jesús García Ferrer (Jesulink) created <i><a href="/wiki/Raruto" title="Raruto">Raruto</a></i>, a Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/Naruto" title="Naruto">Naruto</a></i> dōjinshi published online.</li>
<li>Fabio Yabu produced the sentai spoof <i><a href="/wiki/Combo_Rangers" title="Combo Rangers">Combo Rangers</a></i>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kittyhawk_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kittyhawk (artist) (page does not exist)">Kittyhawk</a>, creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Sparkling_Generation_Valkyrie_Yuuki" title="Sparkling Generation Valkyrie Yuuki">Sparkling Generation Valkyrie Yuuki</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
