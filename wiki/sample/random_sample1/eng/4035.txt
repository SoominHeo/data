<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclides</a> are <b>isotones</b> if they have the same <a href="/wiki/Neutron_number" title="Neutron number">neutron number</a> <i>N</i>, but different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_number" title="Proton number">proton number</a> <i>Z</i>. For example, boron-12 and carbon-13 nuclei both contain 7 <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, and so are isotones . Similarly, <sup>36</sup>S, <sup>37</sup>Cl, <sup>38</sup>Ar, <sup>39</sup>K, and <sup>40</sup>Ca nuclei are all isotones of 20 because they all contain 20 neutrons. Despite its similarity to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> for "same stretching", the term was formed by the German physicist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=K._Guggenheimer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="K. Guggenheimer (page does not exist)">K. Guggenheimer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> by changing the "p" in "<a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>" from "p" for "proton" to "n" for "neutron".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The largest numbers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observationally_stable" title="Observationally stable">observationally stable</a> nuclides exist for isotones 50 (five; <sup>86</sup>Kr, <sup>88</sup>Sr, <sup>89</sup>Y, <sup>90</sup>Zr, <sup>92</sup>Mo) and 82 (six; <sup>138</sup>Ba, <sup>139</sup>La, <sup>140</sup>Ce, <sup>141</sup>Pr, <sup>142</sup>Nd, <sup>144</sup>Sm). <a href="/wiki/Neutron_number" title="Neutron number">Neutron numbers</a> for which there are no stable isotones are 19, 21, 35, 39, 45, 61, 89, 115, 123, and 127 or more. In contrast, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_number" title="Proton number">proton numbers</a> for which there are no stable isotopes are <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">43</a>, <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">61</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">83</a> or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is related to nuclear <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(physics)" title="Magic number (physics)">magic numbers</a>, the number of <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleons</a> forming complete <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_shell_model" title="Nuclear shell model">shells</a> within the nucleus, e.g. 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, and 126. No more than one stable nuclide has the same odd neutron number, except for 1 (<sup>2</sup>H and <sup>3</sup>He), 5 (<sup>9</sup>Be and <sup>10</sup>B), 7 (<sup>13</sup>C and <sup>14</sup>N), 55 (<sup>97</sup>Mo and <sup>99</sup>Ru), and 107 (<sup>179</sup>Hf and <sup>180m</sup>Ta). Odd neutron numbers for which there is a stable nuclide and a <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial</a> <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclide</a> are 27 (<sup>50</sup>V), 65 (<sup>113</sup>Cd), 81 (<sup>138</sup>La), 85 (<sup>147</sup>Sm), and 105 (<sup>176</sup>Lu). Neutron numbers for which there are two primordial nuclides are 88 (<sup>151</sup>Eu and <sup>152</sup>Gd) and 112 (<sup>187</sup>Re and <sup>190</sup>Pt).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">Isotopes</a> are nuclides having the same number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a>; e.g. carbon-12 and carbon-13.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isobar_(nuclide)" title="Isobar (nuclide)">Isobars</a> are nuclides having the same <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> (i.e. sum of protons plus neutrons); e.g. carbon-12 and boron-12.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">Nuclear isomers</a> are different excited states of the same type of nucleus. A transition from one isomer to another is accompanied by emission or absorption of a <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a>, or the process of <a href="/wiki/Internal_conversion" title="Internal conversion">internal conversion</a>. (Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">chemical isomers</a>.)</li>
</ul>
