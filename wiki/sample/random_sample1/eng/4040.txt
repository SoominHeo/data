<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chinese Eastern Railway</h1>

<p>The <b>Chinese Eastern Railway</b> or <b>CER</b> <span class="nowrap">(<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_characters" title="Traditional characters">trad</a>.</small> <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">東清鐵路</span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_characters" title="Simplified characters">simp</a>.</small> <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">东清铁路</span></span>, <i>Dōngqīng Tiělù</i>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Китайско-Восточная железная дорога</span> or <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">КВЖД</span>, <i>Kitaysko-Vostochnaya Zheleznaya Doroga</i> or <i>KVZhD</i>), also known as the <b>Chinese Far East Railway</b> and <b>Manchurian Railway</b>, is the historical name for a railway across <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> (northeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_(1912-1949)" title="Republic of China (1912-1949)">China</a>).</p>
<p>The line was built by <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Imperial Russia</a> using a concession from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing" title="Qing">Qing</a> China, and linked <a href="/wiki/Chita,_Zabaykalsky_Krai" title="Chita, Zabaykalsky Krai">Chita</a> with <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>. The T-shaped line consisted of three branches: the western branch, now the <a href="/wiki/Harbin%E2%80%93Manzhouli_Railway" title="Harbin–Manzhouli Railway">Harbin–Manzhouli Railway</a>, the eastern branch, now the <a href="/wiki/Harbin%E2%80%93Suifenhe_Railway" title="Harbin–Suifenhe Railway">Harbin–Suifenhe Railway</a>, and the southern branch, now part of the <a href="/wiki/Beijing%E2%80%93Harbin_Railway" title="Beijing–Harbin Railway">Beijing–Harbin Railway</a>, which intersected in <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a>. The railway and the concession, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Eastern_Railway_Zone" title="Chinese Eastern Railway Zone">Chinese Eastern Railway Zone</a>, were administered from the city, which grew into a major rail hub.</p>
<p>The southern branch of the CER, known in the West as the <a href="/wiki/South_Manchuria_Railway" title="South Manchuria Railway">South Manchuria Railway</a>, became the locus and partial <a href="/wiki/Casus_belli" title="Casus belli">casus belli</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, the 1929 <a href="/wiki/Sino-Soviet_conflict_(1929)" title="Sino-Soviet conflict (1929)">Sino-Soviet Conflict</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a>. The Soviet Union handed the CER to the People's Republic of China in 1952.</p>

<p>The Chinese Eastern Railway, a single-track line, provided a shortcut for the world's longest railroad, the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Siberian_Railway" title="Trans-Siberian Railway">Trans-Siberian Railway</a>, from near the <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Chita,_Zabaykalsky_Krai" title="Chita, Zabaykalsky Krai">Chita</a>, across northern inner <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> via <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a> to the Russian port of <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a>. This route drastically reduced the travel distance required along the originally proposed main northern route to Vladivostok, which lay completely on Russian soil but was not completed until a decade after the Manchurian "shortcut".</p>
<p>In 1896 <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> granted a construction <a href="/wiki/Concession_(territory)" title="Concession (territory)">concession</a> through northern Inner Manchuria under the supervision of Vice Minister of Public Works <a href="/wiki/Xu_Jingcheng" title="Xu Jingcheng">Xu Jingcheng</a>. Work on the CER began in July 1897 along the line <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tarskaya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tarskaya (page does not exist)">Tarskaya</a> (east of Chita) – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hailar,_China" title="Hailar, China">Hailar</a> – <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolsk-Ussuriski" title="Nikolsk-Ussuriski">Nikolsk-Ussuriski</a>, and accelerated drastically after Russia concluded a <a href="/wiki/Kwantung_Leased_Territory" title="Kwantung Leased Territory">25-year lease</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liaodong" title="Liaodong">Liaodong</a> from China in 1898. Officially, traffic on the line started in November 1901, but regular passenger traffic from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a> to <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a> across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans-Siberian_railway" title="Trans-Siberian railway">Trans-Siberian railway</a> did not commence until July 1903.</p>
<p>In 1898, construction of a 550-mile (880 km) <a href="/wiki/Branch_line" title="Branch line">spur line</a>, most of which later formed the <a href="/wiki/South_Manchuria_Railway" title="South Manchuria Railway">South Manchuria Railway</a>, began at Harbin, leading southwards through Eastern Manchuria, along the Liaodong Peninsula, to the ice-free deep-water port at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%BCshun" title="Lüshun">Lüshun</a>, which Russia was fortifying and developing into a first-class strategic naval base and marine coaling station for its Far East Fleet and Merchant Marine. This town was known in the west as Port Arthur, and the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a> (1904–1905) was fought largely over who would possess this region and its excellent harbor, as well as whether it would remain open to traders of all nations (<a href="/wiki/Open_Door_Policy" title="Open Door Policy">Open Door Policy</a>).</p>
<p>The Chinese Eastern Railway was essentially completed in 1902, a few years earlier than the <a href="/wiki/Circum-Baikal_Railway" title="Circum-Baikal Railway">stretch around Lake Baikal</a>. Until the Circumbaikal portion was completed (1904–1905; double-tracked, 1914), goods carried on the Trans-Siberian Railway had to be trans-shipped by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_ferry" title="Rail ferry">ferry</a> almost a hundred kilometers across the lake (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baikal,_Irkutsk_Oblast" title="Baikal, Irkutsk Oblast">Port Baikal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Babushkin_(town)" title="Babushkin (town)">Mysovaya</a>).</p>
<p>The Chinese Eastern Railway became important in international relations. After the <a href="/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War" title="First Sino-Japanese War">first Sino-Japanese War</a> of 1894-1895, Russia gained the right to build the Chinese Eastern Railway in Manchuria. They had a large army and occupied Northern Manchuria, which was of some concern to the Japanese. Russia pressed China for a "monopoly of rights" in Manchuria, but China reacted to this by an alliance with Japan and the United States against Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, Russia lost both the <a href="/wiki/Liaodong_Peninsula" title="Liaodong Peninsula">Liaodong Peninsula</a> and much of the South Manchurian branch to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The rail line from <a href="/wiki/Changchun" title="Changchun">Changchun</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%BCshun" title="Lüshun">Lüshun</a> - transferred to Japanese control - became the <a href="/wiki/South_Manchuria_Railway" title="South Manchuria Railway">South Manchuria Railway</a>.</p>
<p>During the 1917-1924 (<a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>) the Russian part of the CER came under the administration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Army" title="White Army">White Army</a>.</p>
<p>After 1924, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> administered the Northern CER jointly, while Japan maintained control of the southern spur line.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sino-Soviet_conflict_(1929)" title="Sino-Soviet conflict (1929)">Sino-Soviet conflict</a> of 1929 was fought over the administration of the Northern CER.</p>
<p>In 1935 the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> sold its rights to the CER to the <a href="/wiki/Manchukuo" title="Manchukuo">Manchukuo</a> government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>From August 1945, the CER again came under the joint control of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Somewhat reversing Russia's stinging losses in 1904-1905, after World War II the Soviet government insisted on occupying the <a href="/wiki/Liaodong_Peninsula" title="Liaodong Peninsula">Liaodong Peninsula</a> but allowed joint control over the Southern branch with China; all this together received the name of the "Chinese Changchun Railway" (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Кита́йская Чанчу́ньская желе́зная доро́га</span>).</p>
<p>In 1952, the Soviet Union transferred (free of charge) all of its rights to the Chinese Changchun Railway to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flags">Flags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Eastern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="306" data-file-width="533" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG/220px-Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG/330px-Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG/440px-Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harbin_Station_circa_1940.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1915-1925 CER flag at Harbin railway station</div>

<p>The flag of the Chinese Eastern Railway is a combination of Chinese and Russian flags. It has changed several times with the political changes of both owners. The first CER flag (1897-1915) was a combination of the triangular version of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Qing_dynasty" title="Flag of the Qing dynasty">flag of the Qing dynasty</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Russia" title="Flag of Russia">flag of Russia</a>, with <i>Great Qing East Provinces Railway</i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">大清東省鐵路</span>) in Chinese. The 1915-1925 flag replaced the flag of the Qing dynasty with a triangular version of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Races_Under_One_Union" title="Five Races Under One Union">five-colored flag</a>. The flag was changed again in 1925 and 1932, with the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Flag of the Soviet Union">flag of the Soviet Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Manchukuo" title="Flag of Manchukuo">flag of Manchukuo</a> added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trains">Trains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Eastern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The only train that covers the entire route is train #19/20 ‘Vostok’ (translated as the East) Moscow - Beijing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Trip from Moscow to Beijing takes 146 hours. Journey in the opposite direction lasts 143 hours. There is also train #653/654 Zabaikalsk – Manzhouli which one can use to cross Russian-Chinese border. The trip takes 25 min.</p>
