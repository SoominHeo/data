<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Logical equivalence</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, statements <var>p</var> and <var>q</var> are <b>logically equivalent</b> if they have the same logical content. This is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">semantic</a> concept; two statements are equivalent if they have the same <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth value</a> in every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_(logic)" title="Model (logic)">model</a> (Mendelson 1979:56). The logical equivalence of <var>p</var> and <var>q</var> is sometimes expressed as <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>p</mi>
<mo>≡<!-- ≡ --></mo>
<mi>q</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle p\equiv q}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="p \equiv q" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cf1b853ede16891d0e5cdef8c65cac3495f576b" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; margin-left: -0.079ex; width:5.448ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span>, E<i>pq</i>, or <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>p</mi>
<mo stretchy="false">⇔<!-- ⇔ --></mo>
<mi>q</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle p\Leftrightarrow q}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="p \Leftrightarrow q" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2a97f593c3c0349429c5681162aa00a206a69b2" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; margin-left: -0.079ex; width:5.963ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>. However, these symbols are also used for material equivalence; the proper interpretation depends on the context. Logical equivalence is different from material equivalence, although the two concepts are closely related.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Logical_equivalences">Logical equivalences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_equivalence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Logical equivalences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th><i>Equivalence</i></th>
<th><i>Name</i></th>
</tr>

<td>p∧<b>T</b> ≡ p<br/>

<td>Identity laws</td>

<tr>

<p>Logical equivalences involving conditional statements：<br/></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ol>
<li>p→q ≡ ¬p∨q</li>
<li>p→q ≡ ¬q→¬p</li>
<li>p∨q ≡ ¬p→q</li>
<li>p∧q ≡ ¬(p→¬q)</li>
<li>¬(p→q) ≡ p∧¬q</li>
<li>(p→q)∧(p→r) ≡ p→(q∧r)</li>
<li>(p→q)∨(p→r) ≡ p→(q∨r)</li>
<li>(p→r)∧(q→r) ≡ (p∨q)→r</li>
<li>(p→r)∨(q→r) ≡ (p∧q)→r</li>
</ol>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Logical equivalences involving biconditionals：<br/></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ol>
<li>p↔q ≡ (p→q)∧(q→p)</li>
<li>p↔q ≡ ¬p↔¬q</li>
<li>p↔q ≡ (p∧q)∨(¬p∧¬q)</li>
<li>¬(p↔q) ≡ p↔¬q</li>
</ol>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_equivalence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following statements are logically equivalent:</p>
<ol>
<li>If Lisa is in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, then she is in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. (In symbols, <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>f</mi>
<mo stretchy="false">→<!-- → --></mo>
<mi>e</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle f\rightarrow e}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="f \rightarrow e" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/963d0980c17b1deb61e27fb9986d1157809a650d" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:6.008ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>.)</li>
<li>If Lisa is not in Europe, then she is not in France. (In symbols, <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi mathvariant="normal">¬<!-- ¬ --></mi>
<mi>e</mi>
<mo stretchy="false">→<!-- → --></mo>
<mi mathvariant="normal">¬<!-- ¬ --></mi>
<mi>f</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \neg e\rightarrow \neg f}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\neg e \rightarrow \neg f" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f70aba5607d19b2101ea8f31c43ec6676542b985" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:9.129ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>.)</li>
</ol>
<p>Syntactically, (1) and (2) are derivable from each other via the rules of <a href="/wiki/Contraposition" title="Contraposition">contraposition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Double_negation" title="Double negation">double negation</a>. Semantically, (1) and (2) are true in exactly the same models (interpretations, valuations); namely, those in which either <i>Lisa is in France</i> is false or <i>Lisa is in Europe</i> is true.</p>
<p>(Note that in this example <a href="/wiki/Classical_logic" title="Classical logic">classical logic</a> is assumed. Some <a href="/wiki/Non-classical_logic" title="Non-classical logic">non-classical logics</a> do not deem (1) and (2) logically equivalent.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_material_equivalence">Relation to material equivalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_equivalence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to material equivalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Logical equivalence is different from material equivalence. The material equivalence of <i>p</i> and <i>q</i> (often written <i>p</i>↔<i>q</i>) is itself another statement, call it <i>r</i>, in the same <a href="/wiki/Formal_system" title="Formal system">object language</a> as <i>p</i> and <i>q</i>. <i>r</i> expresses the idea "<i>p</i> if and only if <i>q</i>". In particular, the truth value of <i>p</i>↔<i>q</i> can change from one model to another.</p>
<p>The claim that two formulas are logically equivalent is a statement in the <a href="/wiki/Metalanguage" title="Metalanguage">metalanguage</a>, expressing a relationship between two statements <i>p</i> and <i>q</i>. The claim that <i>p</i> and <i>q</i> are semantically equivalent does not depend on any particular model; it says that in every possible model, <i>p</i> will have the same truth value as <i>q</i>. The claim that <i>p</i> and <i>q</i> are syntactically equivalent does not depend on models at all; it states that there is a deduction of <i>q</i> from <i>p</i> and a deduction of <i>p</i> from <i>q</i>.</p>
<p>There is a close relationship between material equivalence and logical equivalence. Formulas <i>p</i> and <i>q</i> are syntactically equivalent if and only if <i>p</i>↔<i>q</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Theorem" title="Theorem">theorem</a>, while <i>p</i> and <i>q</i> are semantically equivalent if and only if <i>p</i>↔<i>q</i> is true in every model (that is, <i>p</i>↔<i>q</i> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_validity" title="Logical validity">logically valid</a>).</p>
