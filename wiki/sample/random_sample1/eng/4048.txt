<p><b>East Francia</b> (<a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Francia orientalis</i>) or the <b>Kingdom of the East Franks</b> (<i>regnum Francorum orientalium</i>) was a successor state of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>'s empire and precursor of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. Until 911 it was ruled by <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_dynasty" title="Carolingian dynasty">Carolingian dynasty</a>. It was created after the 840-43 civil war between Charlemagne's grandchildren which ended with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> which divided the former empire into three kingdoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> By the middle of the 10th century, the kingdom had become usually referred to as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Kingdom of Germany</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, therefore making it the earliest stage in the development of the modern German state.</p>
<p>The east–west division, enforced by the German-Latin language split, "gradually hardened into the establishment of separate kingdoms",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with East Francia becoming the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Kingdom of Germany</a> and West Francia the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">Kingdom of France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In August 843, after three years of civil war following the death of emperor <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a> on 20 June 840, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> was signed by his three sons and heirs. The division of lands was largely based on the Meuse, Scheldt, Saone and Rhone rivers. While the eldest son <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair I</a> kept the imperial title and the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Francia" title="Middle Francia">Middle Francia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a> received the <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_German" title="Louis the German">Louis the German</a> received the eastern portion of mostly Germanic-speaking lands of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony" title="Duchy of Saxony">Duchy of Saxony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austrasia" title="Austrasia">Austrasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alamania" title="Alamania">Alamania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Duchy of Bavaria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/March_of_Carinthia" title="March of Carinthia">March of Carinthia</a>.</p>
<p>The contemporary East Frankish <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Fuldenses" title="Annales Fuldenses">Annales Fuldenses</a></i> describes the kingdom being "divided in three" and Louis "acceding to the eastern part".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The West Frankish <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Bertiniani" title="Annales Bertiniani">Annales Bertiniani</a></i> describe the extent of Louis's lands: "at the assigning of portions, Louis obtained all the land beyond the Rhine river, but on this side of the Rhine also the cities of Speyer, Worms and Mainz with their counties".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The kingdom of <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a> went to Louis's younger half-brother <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a> and between their realms a kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Francia" title="Middle Francia">Middle Francia</a>, incorporating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regnum_Italicum" title="Regnum Italicum">Italy</a>, was given to their elder brother, the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair I</a>.</p>
<p>While West and Middle Francia contained "the traditional Frankish 'heartlands'", the East consisted mostly of lands only annexed to the Frankish empire in the eighth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldberg_1999.2C_41_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldberg_1999.2C_41-7">[7]</a></sup> These included the duchies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Alemannia" title="Duchy of Alemannia">Alemannia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony" title="Duchy of Saxony">Saxony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Thuringia" title="Duchy of Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, as well as the northern and eastern marches with the Danes and Slavs. The contemporary chronicler <a href="/wiki/Regino_of_Pr%C3%BCm" title="Regino of Prüm">Regino of Prüm</a> wrote that the "different people" (<i>diversae nationes populorum</i>) of East Francia, mostly Germanic- and Slavic-speaking, could be "distinguished from each other by race, customs, language and laws" (<i>genere moribus lingua legibus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldberg_1999.2C_41_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goldberg_1999.2C_41-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 869 <a href="/wiki/Lotharingia" title="Lotharingia">Lotharingia</a> was divided between West and East Francia under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Meersen" title="Treaty of Meersen">Treaty of Meersen</a>. The short lived Middle Francia turned out to be the theatre of Franco-German wars up until the 20th century.</p>
<p>All the Frankish lands were briefly reunited by <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Fat" title="Charles the Fat">Charles the Fat</a>, but in 888 he was deposed by nobles and in East Francia <a href="/wiki/Arnulf_of_Carinthia" title="Arnulf of Carinthia">Arnulf of Carinthia</a> was elected king.</p>
<p>The increasing weakness of royal power in East Francia meant that dukes of Bavaria, Swabia, Franconia, Saxony and Lotharingia from appointed nobles turned into hereditary rulers of their territories. Kings increasingly had to deal with regional rebellions.</p>
<p>In 911 Saxon, Franconian, Bavarian and Swabian nobles no longer followed the tradition of electing someone from Carolingian dynasty as a king to rule over them and on November 10, 911 elected one of their own as the new king. Because <a href="/wiki/Conrad_I_of_Germany" title="Conrad I of Germany">Conrad I</a> was one of the dukes, he found it very hard to establish his authority over them. Duke <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Fowler" title="Henry the Fowler">Henry of Saxony</a> was in rebellion against Conrad I until 915 and struggle against <a href="/wiki/Arnulf,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Arnulf, Duke of Bavaria">Arnulf, Duke of Bavaria</a> cost Conrad I his life. On his deathbed Conrad I chose Henry of Saxony as the most capable successor. This kingship changed from Franks to Saxons, who had suffered greatly during the conquests of Charlemagne. Henry, who was elected to kingship by only Saxons and Franconians at <a href="/wiki/Fritzlar" title="Fritzlar">Fritzlar</a>, had to subdue other dukes and concentrated on creating a state apparatus which was fully utilized by his son and successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otto_I" title="Otto I">Otto I</a>. By his death in July 936 Henry had prevented collapse of royal power as was happening in <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a> and left a much stronger kingdom to his successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otto_I" title="Otto I">Otto I</a>. After Otto I was crowned as the Emperor in Rome in 962 the era of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> began.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Francia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>orientalis Francia</i> originally referred to <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> and <i>orientales Franci</i> to its inhabitants, the ethnic <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> living east of the Rhine. The use of the term in a broader sense, to refer to the eastern kingdom, was an innovation of Louis the German's court. Since eastern Francia could be identified with old <a href="/wiki/Austrasia" title="Austrasia">Austrasia</a>, the Frankish heartland, Louis's choice of terminology hints at his ambitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Under his grandson, <a href="/wiki/Arnulf_of_Carinthia" title="Arnulf of Carinthia">Arnulf</a>, the terminology was largely dropped and the kingdom, when it was referred to by name, was simply <i>Francia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>When it was necessary, as in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Bonn" title="Treaty of Bonn">Treaty of Bonn</a> (921) with the West Franks, the "eastern" qualified appeared. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_Germany" title="Henry I of Germany">Henry I</a> refers to himself as <i>rex Francorum orientalium</i>, "king of the East Franks", in the treaty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> By the 12th century, the historian <a href="/wiki/Otto_of_Freising" title="Otto of Freising">Otto of Freising</a>, in using the Carolingian terminology had to explain that the "eastern kingdom of the Franks" (<i>orientale Francorum regnum</i>) was "now called the kingdom of the Germans" (<i>regnum Teutonicorum</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingship">Kingship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Francia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kingship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The regalia of the Carolingian empire had been divided by Louis the Pious on his deathbed between his two faithful sons, Charles the Bald and Lothair. Louis the German, then in rebellion, received nothing of the crown jewels or liturgical books associated with Carolingian kingship. Thus the symbols and rituals of East Frankish kingship were created from scratch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>From an early date the East Frankish kingdom had a more formalised notion of royal election than West Francia. Around 900, a liturgy (<i>ordo</i>) for the coronation of a king, called the early German <i>ordo</i>, was written for a private audience. It required the coronator to ask the "designated prince" (<i>princeps designatus</i>) whether he was willing to defend the church and the people and then to turn and ask the people whether they were willing to be subject to the prince and obey his laws. The latter then shouted, "<i>Fiat</i>, <i>fiat</i>!" (Let it be done!), an act that later became known as "Recognition". This is the earliest known coronation <i>ordo</i> with a Recognition in it, and it was subsequently incorporated in the influential <a href="/wiki/Pontificale_Romano-Germanicum" title="Pontificale Romano-Germanicum">Pontificale Romano-Germanicum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 888, King <a href="/wiki/Arnulf_of_Carinthia" title="Arnulf of Carinthia">Arnulf</a> convened a council at <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>. In attendance were the three archbishops of the East Frankish kingdom—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilbert_of_Cologne" title="Wilbert of Cologne">Wilbert of Cologne</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liutbert_of_Mainz" title="Liutbert of Mainz">Liutbert of Mainz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratbod_of_Trier" title="Ratbod of Trier">Ratbod of Trier</a>—and the West Frankish archbishops of Reims (<a href="/wiki/Fulk_(archbishop_of_Reims)" title="Fulk (archbishop of Reims)">Fulk</a>) and Rouen (John I) along with the bishops of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Beauvais" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Beauvais">Beauvais</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Diocese_of_Noyon" title="Ancient Diocese of Noyon">Noyon</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Walter_Ullmann" title="Walter Ullmann">Walter Ullmann</a>, the presence of the West Franks was on account of the "barren ecclesiastical thought" of the East, and the council proceeded to adopt West Frankish ideas of royal sacrality and <a href="/wiki/Anointing" title="Anointing">anointing</a>. It was "the first phase in the process of assimilation of the two halves of the Carolingian inheritance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1969.2C_124.E2.80.9327_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann_1969.2C_124.E2.80.9327-15">[15]</a></sup> In another church council at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribur" title="Tribur">Tribur</a> in 895, the prelates declared that Arnulf was chosen by God and not by men and Arnulf in turn swore to defend the church and its privileges from all its enemies. When Arnulf died in 899, his minor son, <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Child" title="Louis the Child">Louis IV</a>, was crowned, but not anointed, and placed under the tutelage of Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Hatto_I_(archbishop_of_Mainz)" title="Hatto I (archbishop of Mainz)">Hatto I of Mainz</a>. Louis's coronation was the first in German history. When Louis died in late September 911, Duke <a href="/wiki/Conrad_I_of_Germany" title="Conrad I of Germany">Conrad of Franconia</a> was elected to replace him on 10 November and he became the first German king to receive unction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1969.2C_124.E2.80.9327_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann_1969.2C_124.E2.80.9327-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church">Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Francia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three basic services monasteries could owe to the sovereign in the Frankish realms were military service, an annual donation of money or work, and prayers for the royal family and the kingdom. Collectively, these were known by the technical term <i>servitium regis</i> ("king's service").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> According to the evidence of the <i><a href="/wiki/Notitia_de_servitio_monasteriorum" title="Notitia de servitio monasteriorum">Notitia de servitio monasteriorum</a></i>, list of monasteries and the services they owed drawn up around 817, the burden of military and monetary service was more severe in west Francia than in east Francia. Only four monasteries listed as "beyond the Rhine" (<i>ultra Rhenum</i>) owed these services: <a href="/wiki/Lorsch_Abbey" title="Lorsch Abbey">Lorsch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schuttern_Abbey" title="Schuttern Abbey">Schuttern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mondsee_Abbey" title="Mondsee Abbey">Mondsee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tegernsee_Abbey" title="Tegernsee Abbey">Tegernsee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
