<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_signification">Etymology and signification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=-hou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and signification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its etymology and meaning are disputed, but most specialists think it comes from Saxon or Anglo-Saxon <i>hōh</i> "heel", sometimes <i>hō</i>, then "heel-shaped promontory", "rocky steep slope", "steep shore".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This toponymic appellative appears as a final <i>-hou</i> or associated with the Romance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">definite article</a> <i>le Hou</i>. It can be found everywhere in Normandy, but more in the western part of it.</p>
<p>The English toponymy uses this Saxon or Anglo-Saxon element the same way, but its result is phonetically <i>-hoo</i> or <i>-hoe</i>, sometimes <i>-(h)ow</i> or <i>-ho</i> e. g. : <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Northoo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Northoo (page does not exist)">Northoo</a> (Suffolk); <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Poddinghoo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Poddinghoo (page does not exist)">Poddinghoo</a> (Worcestershire); <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Millhoo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Millhoo (page does not exist)">Millhoo</a> (Essex); <a href="/wiki/Fingringhoe" title="Fingringhoe">Fingringhoe</a> (Essex); <a href="/wiki/Rainow" title="Rainow">Rainow</a> (Cheshire); <a href="/wiki/Soho" title="Soho">Soho</a> (London); etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As an independent element it is <i>Hoe, Hoo, Hooe, Ho</i> or <i>the Hoe</i>, e.g. the Hoe at <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> (Dorset) above the harbour.</p>
<p>In Normandy, it may have sometimes mixed up with <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>hólmr</i>, meaning a small island, and often found anglicised elsewhere as "holm". It can still be found in modern Scandinavian languages, e.g. Stockholm. The normal evolution of <i>hólmr</i> in Normandy is <i>-homme</i>, <i>-houme</i>, even <i>-onne</i> at the end of a toponym and le Homme, le Houlme, le Hom with the article.</p>
<p>The Norman toponym and <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> <i>hommet / houmet</i> also derives from this element.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">Parisian French</a>, the equivalent is <i>îlot</i>, which is cognate with the English "islet".</p>
