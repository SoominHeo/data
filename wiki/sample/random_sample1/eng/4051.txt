<p><b>Gamma Velorum</b> (γ Vel, γ Velorum) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiple_star_system" title="Multiple star system">multiple star system</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Vela_(constellation)" title="Vela (constellation)">Vela</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> +1.7, it is <a href="/wiki/List_of_brightest_stars" title="List of brightest stars">one of the brightest stars</a> in the night sky, and by far the closest and brightest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star" title="Wolf-Rayet star">Wolf-Rayet star</a>. It has the traditional name <b>Suhail al Muhlif</b> and the modern name <b>Regor</b>, but neither is approved by the International Astronomical Union.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance">Distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gamma_Velorum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gamma Velorum is close enough to have accurate parallax measurements as well as distance estimates by more indirect means. The <a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">Hipparcos</a> parallax for γ<sup>2</sup> implies a distance of 342 pc. A <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_parallax" title="Dynamical parallax">dynamical parallax</a> derived from calculations of the orbital parameters gives a value of 336 pc, similar to spectrophotometric derivations. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLTI" title="VLTI">VLTI</a> <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">interferometry</a> measurement of the distance gives a slightly larger value of 368<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+38<br/>
−13</span> pc. All these distances are somewhat less than the commonly assumed distance of 450 pc for the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vela_OB2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vela OB2 (page does not exist)">Vela OB2</a> association which is the closest grouping of young massive stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millour_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-millour-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gamma_Velorum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gamma Velorum system is composed of at least four stars. The brightest member, γ² Velorum or γ Velorum A, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary" title="Spectroscopic binary">spectroscopic binary</a> composed of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_supergiant" title="Blue supergiant">blue supergiant</a> of spectral class O7.5 (~30 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass"><i>M</i><sub>☉</sub></a>), and a massive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star" title="Wolf-Rayet star">Wolf-Rayet star</a> (~9 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>, originally ~35 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eldridge_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-eldridge-9">[9]</a></sup> The binary has an orbital period of 78.5 days and separation varying from 0.8 to 1.6 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a>. The Wolf-Rayet star is likely to end its life in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_Ib_supernova" title="Type Ib supernova">Type Ib supernova</a> explosion; it is one of the nearest <a href="/wiki/List_of_supernova_candidates" title="List of supernova candidates">supernova candidates</a> to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ass336_2_287_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ass336_2_287-15">[15]</a></sup> The Wolf Rayet star has traditionally been regarded as the primary since its emission lines dominate the spectrum, but the O star is visually brighter and also more luminous. For clarity, the components are now often referred to as WR and O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-north_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-north-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bright (apparent magnitude +4.2) γ¹ Velorum or γ Velorum B, is a spectroscopic binary with a period of 1.48 days. Only the primary is detected and it is a blue-white <a href="/wiki/Giant_star" title="Giant star">giant</a>. It is separated from the Wolf-Rayet binary by 41.2", easily resolved with <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffries_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-jeffries-8">[8]</a></sup> The pair are too close to be separated without optical assistance, and they appear to the naked eye as a single star of apparent magnitude 1.72 (at the average brightness of γ<sup>2</sup> of 1.83).</p>
<p>Gamma Velorum has several fainter companions that share a common motion and are likely to be members of the Vela OB2 association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffries_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-jeffries-8">[8]</a></sup> The magnitude +7.3 CD-46 3848 is a white <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">F0</a> star at is 62.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a> from the A component. At 93.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a> is another binary star, an F0 star of magnitude +9.2.</p>
<p>γ Velorum is associated with several hundred <a href="/wiki/Pre-main-sequence_star" title="Pre-main-sequence star">pre main sequence stars</a> within less than a degree. The ages of these stars would be at least 5 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffries_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-jeffries-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gamma_Velorum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Arabic name is al Suhail al Muḥlīf. al Muhlif refers to <i>the oath-taker</i>, and al Suhail is originally derived from a word meaning <i>the plain</i>. Suhail is used for at least three other stars: <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">Canopus</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Velorum" title="Lambda Velorum">λ Velorum</a> (al Suhail al Wazn); and <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Puppis" title="Zeta Puppis">ζ Puppis</a> (Suhail Hadar). <a href="/wiki/Suhail" title="Suhail">Suhail</a> is also a common Arabic male first name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-allen-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Chinese name for the star is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vela_(Chinese_astronomy)" title="Vela (Chinese astronomy)">天社一</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a>: tiān shè yī), which means "<i>The First Star of the Celestial Earth God's Temple</i>."</p>
<p>The name Regor ("Roger" spelled in reverse) was invented as a practical joke by the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> astronaut <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Gus Grissom</a> for his fellow astronaut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Chaffee" title="Roger Chaffee">Roger Chaffee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the exotic nature of its spectrum (bright <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a> in lieu of dark <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_line" title="Absorption line">absorption lines</a>) it is also dubbed the <i>Spectral Gem of Southern Skies</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hr_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-hr-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
