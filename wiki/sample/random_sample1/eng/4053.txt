<p>The <b>hog-nosed skunks</b> belong to the genus <i><b>Conepatus</b></i> and are members of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mephitidae" title="Mephitidae">Mephitidae</a> (skunks). They are native to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas">the Americas</a>. They have white backs and tails and black underparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Recent work has concluded that the western hog-nosed skunk or common hog-nosed skunk (formerly <i>Conepatus mesoleucus</i>) is the same species as the American hog-nosed skunk, and that <i>Conepatus leuconotus</i> is the correct name of the merged populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hog-nosed_skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_(Mephitis_suffocans).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="512" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_%28Mephitis_suffocans%29.jpg/200px-Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_%28Mephitis_suffocans%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_%28Mephitis_suffocans%29.jpg/300px-Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_%28Mephitis_suffocans%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_%28Mephitis_suffocans%29.jpg/400px-Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_%28Mephitis_suffocans%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brehms_Het_Leven_der_Dieren_Zoogdieren_Orde_4_Surilho_(Mephitis_suffocans).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The individual hog-nosed skunk <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> vary in size, but among them is included the largest of all skunks. All are characterized by comparatively short <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a>, especially on the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a>, and this appendage lacks the plumelike appearance observed in other skunks. The <a href="/wiki/Nose" title="Nose">nose</a> is prolonged into a distinct "snout", naked on the top and sides and evidently used for rooting in the earth after the manner of a <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pig</a>. In addition, the front feet are armed with long, heavy <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">claws</a>. The claws are well developed for digging up insect prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182-4">[4]</a></sup> and the front <a href="/wiki/Leg" title="Leg">legs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shoulder" title="Shoulder">shoulders</a> are provided with a strong <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscular</a> development for digging, as in a <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badger</a>. This likeness has led to the use in some places of the appropriate name "badger skunk" for these <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. The extent of the stripe on the hind of the skunk, and the color of the tail underside suggests a distinction between eastern and western species. The eastern species is a narrow <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stripe" title="wikt:stripe">stripe</a>, with black under the base of the tail. The western distinction is a wide stripe, with a predominantly <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a> tail. The hair on these skunks is coarse and harsh, lacking the qualities which render the coats of their northern relatives so valuable. They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the merge of the American hog-nosed skunks, the eastern hog-nosed skunk, <i>Conepatus leuconotus</i> is typically larger than the western hog-nosed skunk, <i>Conepatus mesoleucus</i>. Female eastern hog-nosed skunks range from 58–74 cm in length and 19–34 cm in height. They weigh between 2.0-4.0 kg. Male hog-nosed skunks range from 56–92 cm in length and 22–41 cm in height. They weigh on average between 3.0-4.5 kg. The western hog-nosed skunk ranged from 40–84 cm in length, 13–35 cm in height, and 1.1-2.7 kg. Males are larger than females and can occasionally reach 4.5 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182-4">[4]</a></sup> The teeth are smaller in <i>C. mesoleucus</i> than in <i>C. leuconotus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hog-nosed_skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eastern hog-nosed skunk is found only in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Texas" title="Southern Texas">Southern Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and Arizona<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028-5">[5]</a></sup> The Molina hog-nosed skunk, also known as Andes skunk, (<i>C. chinga</i>) is found in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Humboldt's hog-nosed skunk, also known as Patagonian hog-nosed skunk (<i>C. humboldtii</i>) finds its habitat in the open grassy areas in the <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonian regions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The western hog-nosed skunk (<i>C. mesoleucus</i>) is found in, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, Sierra Guadalupe, Coahuila, <a href="/wiki/Colima" title="Colima">Colima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028-5">[5]</a></sup> The striped hog-nosed skunk, <i>C. semistriatus</i> is found in <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_1981_1025.E2.80.931028-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hog-nosed_skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Where their range coincides with that of the common skunks, the local distribution of the two is practically the same. They live along the bottom-lands of watercourses, where <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> is abundant and the supply of food most plentiful, or in <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">canyons</a> and on rocky mountain slopes.</p>
<p>For their protection hog-nosed skunks create their own <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a>, generally within a bank, or beneath a <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a>, but do not hesitate to take possession of the deserted burrows of other animals, or of natural cavities among the rocks. Owing to their strictly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> habits, they are generally much less frequently seen than the common skunks, even in localities where they are numerous. Sightings are recorded from brush habitat and semiopen <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>. Habitats may also include rocky <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream beds</a> in desert-scrub and <a href="/wiki/Mesquite" title="Mesquite">mesquite</a> grassland.</p>
<p>Infrequent sightings of the American hog-nosed skunks raise concerns over the conservation status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kays_2002_180.E2.80.93182-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding_habits">Feeding habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hog-nosed_skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although both the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_skunk" title="Spotted skunk">spotted skunk</a> and common skunks live mainly on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, the hog-nosed skunks are even more <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a> in their feeding habits. The bare snout appears to be used constantly for the purpose of rooting out <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, grubs, and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of various kinds from the ground.</p>
