<p><i><b>Durumagi</b></i> is a variety of <i><a href="/wiki/Po_(clothing)" title="Po (clothing)">po</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Overcoat" title="Overcoat">overcoat</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hanbok" title="Hanbok">hanbok</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garment" title="Garment">garment</a>. It is usually the topmost layer of clothing that is worn over <i><a href="/wiki/Jeogori" title="Jeogori">jeogori</a></i> (jacket) and <i>baji</i> (pants). Durumagi means "closed all around", and is also known as <i>jumagui</i> (주막의, 周莫衣), <i>juchaui</i> (주차의, 周遮衣) or <i>juui</i> ( 주의, 周衣). Durumagi is worn not only to fend off the cold, but also for ceremonial purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-durumagi_doosan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-durumagi_doosan-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durumagi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of <i>durumagi</i> traces back to at least the <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> period, although Mongolian influence during the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> period caused changes in its appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-durumagi_nate_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-durumagi_nate-2">[2]</a></sup> Not only was the waist belt changed into a [hanbok|<i>goreum</i>], the traditional <i>po'</i>s short length and wide sleeves were lengthened and narrowed to the style of the Mongolian coat <i>xurumakci</i>, of which the name <i>durumagi</i> is said to be derived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-durumagi_mongol_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-durumagi_mongol-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>, the <i>durumagi</i> was less worn as an overcoat but more of a housecoat for the noble class, whereas it was worn outdoors by the commoners. In 1884, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Gojong of the Korean Empire">King Gojong</a> promulgated the unification of clothing for all social classes through reform laws. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gapsin_reforms_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gapsin_reforms-4">[4]</a></sup> However, this law was met with much resistance and it was only until ten years later, after the <a href="/wiki/Gabo_Reform" title="Gabo Reform">Gabo Reform</a> of 1894, that the <i>durumagi</i> became common as formal attire. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-durumagi_nate_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-durumagi_nate-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_and_characteristics">Types and characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durumagi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types and characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Different fabrics and materials are used in making <i>durumagi</i>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calico_(textile)" title="Calico (textile)">calico</a>, wool, cotton, and various silks for winter; <a href="/wiki/Ramie" title="Ramie">ramie</a>, fine <a href="/wiki/Ramie" title="Ramie">ramie</a> and silk <a href="/wiki/Gauze" title="Gauze">gauze</a> for summer; various silks and calico for spring and autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-durumagi_doosan_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-durumagi_doosan-1">[1]</a></sup> White, grey and navy blue are commonly used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-modern_durumagi_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-modern_durumagi-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various types include <i>hotedan durumagi</i> (홑단 두루마기, single layer), <i>gyup durumagi</i> (겹두루마기, double layer), <i>som durumagi</i> (솜두루마기, cotton), and <i><a href="/wiki/Kkachi_durumagi" title="Kkachi durumagi">kkachi durumagi</a></i> (까치두루마기, magpie) or <i>obangjang durumagi</i> (오방장 두루마기, five colors) for children. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kkachi_durumagi_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-kkachi_durumagi-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">Modern use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durumagi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The 'durumagi' is still considered an important part of traditional attire for formal occasions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but a variety of colors and designs are being used. Colorful <i>durumagis</i> were given as gifts to the world leaders of the 2005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APEC" title="APEC">APEC</a> Summit in <a href="/wiki/Busan" title="Busan">Busan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
