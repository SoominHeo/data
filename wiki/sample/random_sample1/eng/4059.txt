<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">Heron</a></div>
<p><b>Cranes</b> are a family, <b>Gruidae</b>, of large, long-legged and long-necked <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> in the group <a href="/wiki/Gruiformes" title="Gruiformes">Gruiformes</a>. There are fifteen species of crane in four genera. Unlike the similar-looking but unrelated <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>, cranes fly with necks outstretched, not pulled back. Cranes live on all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.</p>
<p>They are opportunistic feeders that change their diet according to the season and their own nutrient requirements. They eat a range of items from suitably sized small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>.</p>
<p>Cranes construct platform nests in shallow water, and typically lay two eggs at a time. Both parents help to rear the young, which remain with them until the next breeding season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoB_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoB-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some species and populations of cranes <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> over long distances; others do not migrate at all. Cranes are solitary during the breeding season, occurring in pairs, but during the non-breeding season they are gregarious, forming large flocks where their numbers are sufficient.</p>
<p>Most species of cranes have been affected by human activities and are at the least classified as threatened, if not critically endangered. The plight of the <a href="/wiki/Whooping_crane" title="Whooping crane">whooping cranes</a> of North America inspired some of the first <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> legislation to protect <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crane_(bird)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3525" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg/220px-Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg/330px-Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg/440px-Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sandhill_bare_areas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The bare area of skin on the face of a <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_crane" title="Sandhill crane">sandhill crane</a>. This can change colour or even expand in area when the bird is excited</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The cranes are large to very large birds, including the world's tallest flying bird. They range in size from the <a href="/wiki/Demoiselle_crane" title="Demoiselle crane">demoiselle crane</a>, which measures 90 cm (35 in) in length, to the <a href="/wiki/Sarus_crane" title="Sarus crane">sarus crane</a>, which can be up to 176 cm (69 in), although the heaviest is the <a href="/wiki/Red-crowned_crane" title="Red-crowned crane">red-crowned crane</a>, which can weigh 12 kg (26 lb) prior to migrating. They are long-legged and long-necked birds with streamlined bodies and large rounded wings. The males and females do not vary in external appearance, but on average males tend to be slightly larger than females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the cranes varies by habitat. Species inhabiting vast open wetlands tend to have more white in the plumage than do species that inhabit smaller wetlands or forested habitats, which tend to be more grey. These white species are also generally larger. The smaller size and colour of the forest species is thought to help them maintain a less conspicuous profile while nesting; two of these species (the <a href="/wiki/Common_crane" title="Common crane">common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_crane" title="Sandhill crane">sandhill cranes</a>) also daub their feathers with mud to further hide while nesting.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SarusTrachea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="525" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/SarusTrachea.jpg/220px-SarusTrachea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/SarusTrachea.jpg/330px-SarusTrachea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/SarusTrachea.jpg/440px-SarusTrachea.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SarusTrachea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The long coiled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" title="Vertebrate trachea">trachea</a> that produces the trumpeting calls of cranes (<a href="/wiki/Sandhill_crane" title="Sandhill crane">sandhill crane</a>, <i>Grus canadensis</i>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most species of crane have some areas of bare skin on the face; the only two exceptions are the <a href="/wiki/Blue_crane" title="Blue crane">blue</a> and demoiselle cranes. This skin is used in communication with other cranes, and can be expanded by contracting and relaxing muscles, and change the intensity of colour. Feathers on the head can be moved and erected in the blue, wattled and demoiselle cranes for signalling as well.</p>
<p>Also important to communication is the position and length of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" title="Vertebrate trachea">trachea</a>. In the two crowned-cranes the trachea is shorter and only slightly impressed upon the bone of the <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sternum</a>, whereas the trachea of the other species is longer and penetrates the sternum. In some species the entire sternum is fused to the bony plates of the trachea, and this helps amplify the crane's <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">calls</a>, allowing them to carry for several kilometres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sternum_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-sternum-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crane_(bird)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Gruiformes_by_population" title="List of Gruiformes by population">List of Gruiformes by population</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg/220px-Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg/330px-Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg/440px-Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anthropoides_virgo_-Mongolia_-flying-8.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Demoiselle_crane" title="Demoiselle crane">Demoiselle cranes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. Central Asian populations of this species migrate to Northern India in the winter</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The cranes have a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan distribution</a>, occurring across most of the world continents. They are absent from Antarctica and, mysteriously, South America. East Asia is the centre of crane diversity, with eight species, followed by Africa, which holds five resident species and wintering populations of a sixth. Australia, Europe and North America have two species. Of the four genera of crane, two, <i>Balearica</i> (two species) and <i>Bugeranus</i> (one species) are entirely restricted to Africa, and the third <i>Anthropoides</i> has one entirely African species and one species that is found in Africa, Asia and Europe. The remaining genus, <i>Grus</i>, contains the most species and is the most widespread genus, although only a single species occurs in Africa as a wintering migrant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most species of crane are dependent on wetlands and require large areas of open space. Most species of crane nest in shallow wetlands. Some species nest in wetlands but move their chicks up onto grasslands to feed (while returning to wetlands at night), whereas others remain in wetlands for the entirety of the breeding season. Even the two species of <i>Anthropoides</i> crane, which may nest and feed in grasslands (or even arid grasslands or deserts) require weedlands for roosting in during the night. The only two species that do not always roost in wetlands are the two African crowned-cranes (<i>Balearica</i>), which are the only cranes to roost in trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some crane species are sedentary, remaining in the same area throughout the year, others are highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, travelling thousands of kilometres each year from their breeding sites. A few species have both migratory and sedentary populations.</p>

<p>The cranes are <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> birds that vary in their sociality by season. During the breeding season they are territorial and usually remain on their territory all the time. In contrast in the non-breeding season they tend to be gregarious, forming large flocks to roost, socialise and in some species feed. Species that feed predominately on vegetable matter in the non-breeding season feed in flocks to do so, whereas those that feed on animals will usually feed in family groups, joining flocks only during resting periods, or in preparation for travel during migration. Large aggregations of cranes are important for safety when resting and also as places for young unmated birds to meet others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calls_and_communication">Calls and communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crane_(bird)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Calls and communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cranes are highly vocal and have a large vocabulary of specialized <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">calls</a>. The vocabulary begins soon after hatching with low, purring contact calls for maintaining contact with their parents, as well as food begging calls. Other calls used as chicks include alarm calls and "flight intention" calls, both of which are maintained into adulthood. The cranes' duet calls are most impressive. They can be used for individual recognition (see below external link).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crane_(bird)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The cranes as a family consume a wide range of food, ranging from animal to plant matter. When feeding on land they consume seeds, leaves, nuts and acorns, berries, fruit, insects, worms, snails, small reptiles, mammals and birds. In wetlands roots, rhizomes, tubers and other parts of emergent plants, other molluscs, small fish and amphibians are also consumed as well. The exact composition of the diet varies by location, season and availability. Within the wide range of items consumed there are some patterns; the shorter-billed species usually feed in drier uplands while the longer-billed species feed in wetlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cranes employ different foraging techniques for different food types. Tubers and rhizomes are dug for and a crane digging for them will remain in place for some time digging and then expanding a hole to find them. In contrast both to this and the stationary wait and watch hunting methods employed by many herons, they forage for insects and animal prey by slowly moving forwards with their heads lowered and probing with their bills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Where more than one species of crane exists in a locality, each species will adopt separate niches in order to minimise competition and niche overlap. At one important lake in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiangxi_Province" title="Jiangxi Province">Jiangxi Province</a> in China the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_crane" title="Siberian crane">Siberian cranes</a> feed on the mudflats and in shallow water, the <a href="/wiki/White-naped_crane" title="White-naped crane">white-naped cranes</a> on the wetland borders, the <a href="/wiki/Hooded_crane" title="Hooded crane">hooded cranes</a> on sedge meadows and the last two species also feed on the agricultural fields along with the common cranes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Cranes are perennially monogamous breeders, establishing long-term pair bonds that may last the lifetime of the birds. Pair bonds begin to form in the second or third years of life, but it may be several years before the first successful breeding season. Initial breeding attempts often fail, and in many cases newer pair bonds will dissolve (divorce) after unsuccessful breeding attempts. Pairs that are repeatedly successful at breeding will remain together for as long as they continue to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> In a study of <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_crane" title="Sandhill crane">sandhill cranes</a> in Florida, seven out of the 22 pairs studied remained together for an 11-year period. Of the pairs that separated 53% were due to the death of one of the pair, 18% due to divorce and the fate of 29% of pairs were unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesbitt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nesbitt-5">[5]</a></sup> Similar results had been found by acoustic monitoring (sonography / frequency analysis of duett and guard calls) in 3 breeding areas of common cranes in Germany over 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wessling_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wessling-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cranes are territorial and generally seasonal breeders. Seasonality varies both between and within species, dependent on local conditions. Migratory species begin breeding upon reaching their summer breeding grounds, between April and June. The breeding season of tropical species, however, is usually timed to coincide with the wet or monsoon seasons. Territory sizes also vary depending on location. Tropical species can maintain very small territories, for example sarus cranes in India can breed on territories as small as one hectare where the area is of sufficient quality and disturbance by humans is minimised. In contrast red-crowned crane territories may require 500 hectares, and pairs may defend even larger territories than that, up to several thousand hectares. Territory defence is usually performed by the male.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> Because of this females are much less likely to retain the territory than males in the event of the death of a partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesbitt_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nesbitt-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record of cranes leaves much to be desired. Apparently, the subfamilies were well distinct by the Late <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> (around 35 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>). The present genera are apparently some 20 mya old. <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">Biogeography</a> of known fossil and the living taxa of cranes suggests that the group is probably of (<a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasian</a>?) Old World origin. The extant diversity at the genus level is centered on (eastern) <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, making it all the more regrettable that no decent fossil record exists from there. On the other hand, it is peculiar that numerous fossils of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciconiiformes" title="Ciconiiformes">Ciconiiformes</a> are documented from there; these birds presumably shared much of their <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> with cranes back then already. Cranes are sister taxa to <a href="/wiki/Eogruidae" title="Eogruidae">Eogruidae</a>, a lineage of flightless birds; as predicted by the fossil record of true cranes, eogruids were native to the Old World.</p>

<p>The cranes' beauty and their spectacular mating dances have made them highly symbolic birds in many cultures with records dating back to ancient times. Crane mythology is widely spread and can be found in areas such as the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean</a>, South <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and in the Native American cultures of North America. In northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaidō">Hokkaidō</a>, the women of the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_people" title="Ainu people">Ainu people</a> performed a crane dance that was captured in 1908 in a photograph by <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Genthe" title="Arnold Genthe">Arnold Genthe</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, a crane dance has been performed in the courtyard of the Tongdosa Temple since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silla_Dynasty" title="Silla Dynasty">Silla Dynasty</a> (646 CE).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> epic poet <a href="/wiki/Valmiki" title="Valmiki">Valmiki</a> was inspired to write the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9Aloka" title="Śloka">śloka</a> couplet by the pathos of seeing a male <a href="/wiki/Sarus_crane" title="Sarus crane">sarus crane</a> shot while mating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, in pre-Islamic South Arabia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-l%C4%81t" title="Al-lāt">Allāt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uzza" title="Uzza">Uzza</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Man%C4%81t" title="Manāt">Manāt</a> were believed to be the three chief goddesses of Mecca, they were called the "three exalted cranes" (<i>gharaniq</i>, an obscure word on which 'crane' is the usual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloss_(transliteration)" title="Gloss (transliteration)">gloss</a>). See <a href="/wiki/Satanic_Verses" title="Satanic Verses">The Satanic Verses</a> for the best-known story regarding these three goddesses.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, several styles of kung fu take inspiration from the movements of cranes in the wild, the most famous of these styles being Wing Chun, Hung Gar (tiger crane), and the Shaolin Five Animals style of fighting. Crane movements are well known for their fluidity and grace.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> for crane is <i>Γερανος</i> (<i>Geranos</i>), which gives us the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranesbill" title="Cranesbill">cranesbill</a>, or hardy geranium. The crane was a bird of omen. In the tale of <a href="/wiki/Ibycus" title="Ibycus">Ibycus</a> and the cranes, a thief attacked Ibycus (a poet of the 6th century BCE) and left him for dead. Ibycus called to a flock of passing cranes, who followed the attacker to a theater and hovered over him until, stricken with guilt, he confessed to the crime.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> wrote that cranes would appoint one of their number to stand guard while they slept. The sentry would hold a stone in its claw, so that if it fell asleep it would drop the stone and waken. A crane holding a stone in its claw is a well-known symbol in <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>, and is known as a <i>crane in its vigilance</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> describes the migration of cranes in the <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Animals" title="History of Animals">History of Animals</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> adding an account of their fights with <a href="/wiki/Pygmy_peoples" title="Pygmy peoples">Pygmies</a> as they wintered near the source of the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>. He describes as untruthful an account that the crane carries a <a href="/wiki/Touchstone_(assaying_tool)" title="Touchstone (assaying tool)">touchstone</a> inside it that can be used to test for gold when vomited up. <i>(This second story is not altogether implausible, as cranes might ingest appropriate gizzard stones in one locality and regurgitate them in a region where such stone is otherwise scarce.)</i></p>
<p>Greek and Roman myths often portrayed the dance of cranes as a love of joy and a celebration of life, and the crane was often associated with both <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout Asia, the crane is a symbol of <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eternal_youth" title="Eternal youth">eternal youth</a>. In Japan, the crane is one of the mystical or holy creatures (others include the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_dragon" title="Japanese dragon">dragon</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_depictions_of_turtles_and_tortoises#Japan" title="Cultural depictions of turtles and tortoises">tortoise</a>) and symbolizes good fortune and longevity because of its fabled life span of a thousand years. The crane is a favourite subject of the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Origami" title="Origami">origami</a> or paper folding. An ancient Japanese legend promises that anyone who folds a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thousand_origami_cranes" title="Thousand origami cranes">thousand origami cranes</a> will be granted a wish by a crane. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the crane came to symbolize peace and the innocent victims of war through the story of schoolgirl <a href="/wiki/Sadako_Sasaki" title="Sadako Sasaki">Sadako Sasaki</a> and her thousand origami cranes. Suffering from <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> as a result of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">the atomic bombing of Hiroshima</a> and knowing she was dying, she undertook to make a thousand origami cranes before her death at the age of 12. After her death, she became internationally recognised as a symbol of the innocent victims of war and remains a heroine to many Japanese girls.</p>
