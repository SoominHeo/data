<p><b>Durga</b> is the form of <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">mother goddess</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shaktism" title="Shaktism">Shaktism</a>. She is also called <i>Renu</i> or <i>Renuga</i>, which means "wife of lord Shiva (or) mother of universe". There are many incarnations of Durga in <a href="/wiki/Shaktism" title="Shaktism">Shaktism</a> and nine appellations: Skandamata, Kushmanda, Shailaputri, Kaalratri, Brahmacharini, Katyayani, Chandraghanta and Siddhidatri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A list of 108 names that are used to describe her is very popularly in use by eastern Hindus and is called "Ashtottara Shatanamavali of Goddess Durga".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_development">Origins and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The historian Ramaprasad Chanda wrote the following treatise in 1916 about the ideological development of Goddess Durga from primitive goddess to her current form: <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"...it is possible to distinguish two different strata – one primitive and the other advanced. The primitive form of Durga (<a href="/wiki/Parvati" title="Parvati">Parvati</a>) is the result of <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretism</a> of a mountain-goddess worshiped by the dwellers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vindhya" title="Vindhya">Vindhyas</a>, a goddess worshiped by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abhiras" title="Abhiras">Abhiras</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Vegetation_deity" title="Vegetation deity">vegetation spirit</a> conceived as female and a war-goddess. As her votaries advanced in civilization the primitive war-goddess was transformed into the personification of the all-destroying time (<a href="/wiki/Kali" title="Kali">Kali</a>), the vegetation spirit into the primordial energy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adya_Sakti" title="Adya Sakti">Adya Sakti</a>) and the saviouress from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samsara" title="Samsara">samsara</a> (cycle of rebirths) and gradually brought into line with the <a href="/wiki/Historical_Vedic_religion" title="Historical Vedic religion">Brahmanic</a> mythology and philosophy...."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Devi Puranas state that Durga is the warrior manifestation of Goddess Adishakti. Durga means the Invincible One. Therefore, Goddess Adishakti is the Divine Mother of the Universe who had taken birth on Earth as Parvati to win and woo Shiva. Lalita Sahasranamam bestows Durga as a name of her indicating that Lalita is none other than a form of Maa Adishakti Parvati.</p>
<p>Durga is the supreme soul, otherwise called <a href="/wiki/Shakti" title="Shakti">Shakti</a> (primordial cosmic energy). As such, she is the original cause of all the present or past worldly occurrences. From Durga, all phenomenon of creation, existence and destruction is generated through the emergence of two triads of Supreme Powers:</p>
<ul>
<li>The masculine <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">Trimurti</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudra" title="Rudra">Rudra</a>)</li>
<li>The feminine <a href="/wiki/Tridevi" title="Tridevi">Tridevi</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saraswati" title="Saraswati">Saraswati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parvati" title="Parvati">Parvati</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Different terms all referring to the same concept referenced above are Adhyashakti, Paramatma Shakti or Ati Prakrutika Shakti. Durga is believed to create and control two types of power:</p>
<ul>
<li>Natural (Aatma Shakti, Prakrutika Shakti, Pancha Mahabhuta Shakti, etc.)</li>
<li>General( Jada Shakti, Tamashakti)</li>
</ul>
<p>Durga, as the mother of the Universe, provides man with the opportunity for salvation and enjoyment of life. <a href="/wiki/Vyasa" title="Vyasa">Vyasa</a>, the eminent sage and poet of the <a href="/wiki/Devi-Bhagavata_Purana" title="Devi-Bhagavata Purana">Devi-Bhagavata Purana</a>, has aptly stated "Rudrahinam Vishnuhinam na vadanti janastatha Shaktihinam Yathasarbe probodhanti Naradhamam". This verse translates loosely as "Powerless persons are despised as mean persons. So, by being devoted to the Supreme, we should be strong and powerful by her grace."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stories">Stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Shiva_Purana" title="Shiva Purana">Shiva Purana</a></i> gives an account of the origin of Durga. At the beginning of time, <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a> invoked Durga, the primordial energy from his left half to create. Together they created their eternal abode, Shivaloka, also known as Kashi. Thereafter, they created <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a>.</p>
<p>As per the Shiva Purana and <a href="/wiki/Devi_Mahatmya" title="Devi Mahatmya">Devi Mahatmya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahishasura" title="Mahishasura">Mahishasura</a>, son of demon Rambha, unleashed a reign of terror on earth. When the gods intervened, Mahishasura defeated them, banishing them from heaven.</p>
<p>The vanquished gods went to the <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">Trimurti</a> – Brahma, Vishnu and Maheshwara. As they narrated their woeful tale, an immense mass of light manifested from Vishnu's mouth. This was joined by similar rays that emerged from the enraged faces of gods. This mass of light transformed into a woman. The gods bestowed gifts of divine weapons to this woman who was <a href="/wiki/Adi_Parashakti" title="Adi Parashakti">Adi Parashakti</a> manifested as Durga, to slay Mahishasura.</p>
<p>Other sources<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> say that Durga did not arise from the Devas as she was a form of Goddess Adishakti. The Mother took birth on Earth as Parvati to be united with her lord, Shiva. After marriage, Shiva helps Parvati realize and gain control of her powers as Adi-shakti, the pure energy of the universe. Later on, she slays Mahishasura as Durga and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raktabija" title="Raktabija">Raktabija</a> as <a href="/wiki/Kali" title="Kali">Kali</a>.</p>
<p>Armed with celestial weapons gifted by the deities and decked with divine ornaments, Durga rode into the battle field and challenged the demons for battle. Mahishasura's entire army, led by demons like Chikshur, Chamar, Asiloma, Vidalaksha, Durdhara, Durmukha, Mahahanu and many more, attacked Durga simultaneously. But Durga slew all of them with unparalleled fearlessness. An enraged Mahishasura attacked Durga in the guise of a buffalo. But Durga bound him in this form with ropes. The buffalo then morphed into a lion and leapt on Durga, but she beheaded it with her sword. At this, Mahishasura began to fight with his sword. Durga pinned him down with a torrent of arrows. Mahishasura assumed the form of a giant elephant and tugged at Durga's mount, itself a lion. Durga lopped off the trunk of the elephant with her sword and freed her lion. The elephant turned into a buffalo and charged at Durga. Durga flung her trident and beheaded Mahishasura, finally killing him.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons">Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1086" data-file-width="720" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg/220px-Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg/330px-Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg/440px-Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Durga_Loro_Jonggrang_copy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Durga as Mahisashuramardini holding weapons</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><i>Chakra</i> – Given by Narayana, the Sudarshan Chakra that spins in the little index finger of the goddess symbolizes righteousness or dharma. The goddess uses this weapon to destroy evil and protect righteousness.</li>
<li><i>Conch</i> – Given by Varuna, this is seen in her first upper left hand. The conch stands for the cosmic sound of OM vibration. The sound destroys all evil negative forces and when one prays to her, the vibrations fill one with peace and tranquillity.</li>
<li><i>Bow and arrow</i> – Given by Surya, she holds them in her second left hand to destroy the difficulties arising in one`s path in life. Her blessings are needed for success in all spheres of life. All hindrances get removed by this powerful weapon.</li>
<li><i>Sword</i> – She holds it in her second right lower hand that depicts knowledge and keen intellect. She bestows one with the gift of knowledge to remove the veil of ignorance that binds a soul due to wrong deeds and karmic entanglements.</li>
<li><i>Spear</i> – This weapon helps to destroy the negative and evil forces and grants auspiciousness.</li>
<li><i>Club</i> – This is seen in her third right lower hand. This weapon destroys the power of the enemy, however powerful he may be. Durga's grace will help defeat one's enemies in life through the power of this weapon.</li>
<li><i>Trident</i> – Given by Shiva, Trishul or trident seen in her fourth left hand symbolizes the three qualities- satwa, rajas and tamas in a human. These qualities have to be balanced to lead a peaceful life. Her grace will give one the courage to fight the dark qualities arising in one due to the imbalance of the three qualities.</li>
<li><i>Thunderbolt</i> – Given by Indra, this weapon symbolizes firmness of spirit. Praying to Mother Durga will help shatter the problems encountered in life without losing confidence. She empowers her devotee with unshaken confidence and will.</li>
<li><i>Lotus</i> – It stands for awakening of spiritual consciousness in a soul. Divine Mother will help her devotees to understand the transience of life and seek the ultimate truth.</li>
</ul>

<p>The ten-day-long <a href="/wiki/Durga_Puja" title="Durga Puja">Durga Puja</a> is the biggest annual festival in <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odisha" title="Odisha">Odisha</a>, Assam, <a href="/wiki/Jharkhand" title="Jharkhand">Jharkhand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, where it is known as <a href="/wiki/Dashain" title="Dashain">Dashain</a>. Dashain is the longest national holiday of Nepal. In Dashain, Durga is worshipped in ten forms (<a href="/wiki/Kushmanda" title="Kushmanda">Kushmanda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chandraghanta" title="Chandraghanta">Chandraghanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brahmacharini" title="Brahmacharini">Brahmacharini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shailaputri" title="Shailaputri">Shailaputri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skandamata" title="Skandamata">Skandamata</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katyayani" title="Katyayani">Katyayani</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalaratri" title="Kalaratri">Kalaratri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahagauri" title="Mahagauri">Mahagauri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahakali" title="Mahakali">Mahakali</a> and Durga) with one form for each day in Nepal. It is celebrated likewise with much fervour in various parts of India, especially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> region, but is celebrated in various forms throughout India and the world.</p>

<p>The day of Durga's victory is celebrated as <a href="/wiki/Vijayadashami" title="Vijayadashami">Vijayadashami</a> (Bijoya in Bengali), Dashain (Nepali) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dussehra" title="Dussehra">Dussehra</a> (in Hindi) – these words literally mean "the victory on the Tenth (day)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Andhra Pradesh, she is also worshipped as Kanaka Durgammathalli, where there is also a temple for Kanaka Durga in Indrakeeladri, Vijayawada. She is also known by the name of Chandi Bhavani.</p>
<p>In Kashmir she is worshipped as Shaarika (the main temple is in Hari Parbat in <a href="/wiki/Srinagar" title="Srinagar">Srinagar</a>).</p>
<p>The actual period of the worship however may be on the preceding nine days <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navaratri" title="Navaratri">Navaratri</a> followed by the last day called <i>Vijayadashami</i> in or five days in Bengal (from the sixth to tenth day of the waxing-moon fortnight).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Nine aspects of Durga known as <a href="/wiki/Navadurga" title="Navadurga">Navadurga</a> are meditated upon, one by one during the nine-day festival by devout <a href="/wiki/Shakti" title="Shakti">Shakti</a> worshippers. Durga Puja also includes the worship of <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>, who is Durga's consort, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saraswati" title="Saraswati">Saraswati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganesha" title="Ganesha">Ganesha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kartikeya" title="Kartikeya">Kartikeya</a>, who are considered to be Durga's children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Worship of <a href="/wiki/Mother_Nature" title="Mother Nature">Mother Nature</a> is done, through nine types of plant (called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kala_Bo" title="Kala Bo">Kala Bou</a>), including plantain(banana) tree, which represent nine divine forms of Durga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In South India, especially Andhra Pradesh, Dussera Navaratri is also celebrated and the goddess is dressed each day as a different devi – Shailputri, Bramhacharini, Chandraghanta – for the nine days.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Telangana" title="Telangana">Telangana</a>, people celebrate <a href="/wiki/Bathukamma" title="Bathukamma">Bathukamma</a>, a flower festival during Navarathri, to honour the life-giver goddess Bathukamma (Durga) with the belief that if young girls pray with devotion, their wishes of getting good spouses will be fulfilled. On the other hand, married women seek Durga's blessings for prosperity and good year. Women folk arrange flowers in concentric layers to form conical shape and place Durga, made out turmeric on top. <a href="/wiki/Bathukamma" title="Bathukamma">Bathukamma</a> is a cultural icon of Telangana.</p>
<p>In North India, the tenth day is celebrated as <a href="/wiki/Vijayadashami" title="Vijayadashami">Vijayadashami</a>, the day <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a> emerged victorious in his battle against the demon, <a href="/wiki/Ravana" title="Ravana">Ravana</a>. Gigantic straw effigies of Ravana are burnt in designated open spaces (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>'s Ram Lila grounds), watched by thousands of families and little children.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mysore" title="Mysore">Mysore</a> in <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>, Durga is worshipped as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamundeshwari" title="Chamundeshwari">Chamundeshwari</a>, the patron goddess of the city during Dasara (dussehra) as it is believed that she saved all the people there from Mahishasura, the buffalo demon, who terrorised them. Hence, the city, the then larger region comprising other Kannada regions, got the name <i>Mysoru</i> after Mahishasura Mardhini(i.e. slayer of the demon <a href="/wiki/Mahishasura" title="Mahishasura">Mahishasura</a>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>, it is celebrated as the last day of Navaratri, during which the <a href="/wiki/Garba_(dance)" title="Garba (dance)">Garba dance</a> is performed to celebrate the victory of Durga over Mahishasura.</p>
<p>Durga is worshipped in her peaceful form as Maha Gauri, The Fair Lady. <a href="/wiki/Shantadurga" title="Shantadurga">Shantadurga</a> also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santeri" title="Santeri">Santeri</a>, the patron goddess of Goa. She is worshipped by all Goan Hindus.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tulja_Bhavani" title="Tulja Bhavani">Tulja Bhavani</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hedavde_Mahalaxmi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hedavde Mahalaxmi (page does not exist)">Hedavde Mahalaxmi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambabai" title="Ambabai">Ambabai</a> are worshipped as Mahishasura Mardini and considered the patron goddess of the land. Bhavani is known as Tulaja, Amba,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Renuka, Yamai, Saptshrungi and Jogai in different places of Maharashtra. She is the inspirational goddess of Raja <a href="/wiki/Shivaji" title="Shivaji">Shivaji</a>. As per legends, Bhavani appeared after Shivaji prayed to her and blessed him to be able to make Hindustan or the then India(ruled by the Mughals) independent – the kingdom he established eventually became the Hindu Pad Padshahi(sometimes also called the <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Maratha Empire</a>), which comprised all the land ruled by the Mughals and brought India back under Hindu sovereignty.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> also, the four-day-long <a href="/wiki/Durga_Puja" title="Durga Puja">Sharadiya Durga Puja</a> is the biggest religious festival for the Hindus and celebrated across the country with <i>Vijayadashami</i> being a national holiday.</p>
<p>The prominence of Durga Puja increased gradually during the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a> in Bengal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> After the Hindu reformists identified Durga with India, she became an icon for the <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian independence movement</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Durga_and_Ashtanayika">Durga and Ashtanayika</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Durga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Durga and Ashtanayika">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Hinduism, whenever a priest worships Durga, he has to utter a mantra which gives an account of Goddess Durga together with <a href="/wiki/Ashtanayika" title="Ashtanayika">Ashtanayika</a>(her eight eternal companions). The priest then deeply concentrates his mind upon Durga together with Ashtanayika.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some early Western accounts refer to a deity known as Deumus, Demus or Deumo. Western (Portuguese) sailors first came face to face with the <a href="/wiki/Murti" title="Murti">murti</a> of Deumus at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calicut" title="Calicut">Calicut</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Malabar_Coast" title="Malabar Coast">Malabar Coast</a> and they concluded it to be the deity of Calicut. Deumus is sometimes interpreted as an aspect of Durga in Hindu mythology and sometimes as <a href="/wiki/Deva_(Hinduism)" title="Deva (Hinduism)">deva</a>.</p>
<p>It is described that the ruler of Calicut (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zamorin" title="Zamorin">Zamorin</a>) had a <a href="/wiki/Murti" title="Murti">murti</a> of Deumus in his temple inside his royal palace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The temple was two paces wide in each of the four sides and three paces high, with a wooden door covered with gods carved in relief. At the centre of the temple, there was a metal idol of Deumus placed in a seat, which was also made of metal.</p>
<p>Western accounts also describe the ruler of Calicut worshiping an ultimate god called Tamerani("Tamburan"). The accounts also describes a misunderstood form of the "hook-swinging" ritual once commonly performed as part of some popular Hindu religious festivals.</p>
