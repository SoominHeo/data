<p>The <b>Dubai Metro</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مترو دبي</span>‎‎) is a <a href="/wiki/Automatic_train_operation" title="Automatic train operation">driverless, fully automated</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">metro</a> rail network in <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, United Arab Emirates. The <a href="/wiki/Red_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Red Line (Dubai Metro)">Red Line</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Green Line (Dubai Metro)">Green Line</a> are operational, with three further lines planned. These first two lines run underground in the city centre and on elevated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viaducts" title="Viaducts">viaducts</a> elsewhere (<a href="/wiki/Elevated_railway" title="Elevated railway">elevated railway</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> All trains and stations are air conditioned with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platform_edge_doors" title="Platform edge doors">platform edge doors</a> to make this possible. Architecture firm <a href="/wiki/Aedas" title="Aedas">Aedas</a> designed the metro's 45 stations, two depots and operational control centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Al_Ghurair_Investment" title="Al Ghurair Investment">Al Ghurair Investment</a> group were the metro's builders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first section of the Red Line, covering 10 stations, was ceremonially inaugurated at 9:09:09 pm on 9 September 2009, by <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Rashid_Al_Maktoum" title="Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum">Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum</a>, Ruler of Dubai,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with the line opening to the public at 6 am (UTC 04:00) on 10 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Dubai Metro is the first urban train network in the Arabian Peninsula<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and either the second in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_World" title="Arab World">Arab World</a> (after the <a href="/wiki/Cairo_Metro" title="Cairo Metro">Cairo Metro</a>) or the third (if the surface-level, limited-service <a href="/wiki/Baghdad_Metro" title="Baghdad Metro">Baghdad Metro</a> is counted). A major expansion of the Red Line to add 15 kilometres of track and extend it from Ibn Battuta to the Expo 2020 site was announced in April 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than 110,000 people, or nearly 10 percent of Dubai’s population, used the Metro in its first two days of operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Dubai Metro carried 10 million passengers from launch on 9 September 2009 to 9 February 2010 with 11 stations operational on the Red Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Engineering consultancy Atkins provided full multidisciplinary design and management of the civil works on Dubai Metro.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (June 2015)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Until 2016, The Dubai Metro was the world's longest fully automated driverless metro network with a route length of 75 kilometres (47 mi), which was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, its total route length have since been surpassed by the fully automated driverless <a href="/wiki/SkyTrain_(Vancouver)" title="SkyTrain (Vancouver)">Vancouver SkyTrain</a> as of December 2016.</p>

<p>Planning of the Dubai Metro began under the directive of Dubai's ruler <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Rashid_Al_Maktoum" title="Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum">Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum</a> who expected other projects to attract 15 million visitors to Dubai by 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The combination of a rapidly growing population (expected to reach 3 million by 2017) and severe traffic congestion necessitated the building of an urban rail system to provide additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> capacity, relieve motor traffic, and provide infrastructure for additional development.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In May 2005, a <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates_dirham" title="United Arab Emirates dirham">AED</a> 12.45 billion/US$3.4 billion design and build contract was awarded to the Dubai Rail Link (DURL) consortium made up of Japanese companies including <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Corporation" title="Mitsubishi Corporation">Mitsubishi Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obayashi_Corporation" title="Obayashi Corporation">Obayashi Corporation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kajima_Corporation" title="Kajima Corporation">Kajima Corporation</a> and Turkish firm <a href="/wiki/Yap%C4%B1_Merkezi" title="Yapı Merkezi">Yapı Merkezi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the Project Management ('The Engineer') and Construction Management services contract awarded to a French-American joint venture between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systra" title="Systra">Systra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parsons_Corporation" title="Parsons Corporation">Parsons Corporation</a>. The first phase (worth AED 15.5 billion/US$4.2 billion) covers 35 kilometres (22 mi) of the proposed network, including the <a href="/wiki/Red_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Red Line (Dubai Metro)">Red Line</a> between <a href="/wiki/Al_Rashidiya" title="Al Rashidiya">Al Rashidiya</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Ali_Free_Zone" title="Jebel Ali Free Zone">Jebel Ali Free Zone</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> set for completion by September 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Green Line (Dubai Metro)">Green Line</a> from <a href="/wiki/Al_Qusais" title="Al Qusais">Al Qusais</a> 2 to <a href="/wiki/Al_Jaddaf" title="Al Jaddaf">Al Jaddaf</a> 1. This was to be completed by June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gulfnews_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-gulfnews-16">[16]</a></sup> A second phase contract was subsequently signed in July 2006 and includes extensions to the initial routes. The Red Line partially opened at 9 minutes and 9 seconds past 9 pm on 9 September 2009 (9/9/9 9:9:9), inaugurated by Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid al Maktoum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gulfnews2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-gulfnews2-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_issues">Cost issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cost issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The construction cost of the Dubai Metro project has shot up by about 80 per cent from the original AED 15.5 billion/US$4.2 billion to AED 28 billion/US$7.8 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalCostUp_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NationalCostUp-18">[18]</a></sup> The authorities contradicted this, saying that the cost of the project did not overshoot. They attributed the increase in expenditure to the major changes in the scope and design of the project. The authorities also expect to generate AED 18 billion/US$4.9 billion in income over the next 10 years; but they speculate that the Metro would not be a profit-making enterprise, since the fares would be subsidised.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Delays">Delays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Delays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Work officially commenced on the construction of the metro on 21 March 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In February 2009, a top RTA Rail Agency official said the US$4.2 billion Dubai Metro project would be completed on schedule despite the global crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, only 10 out of 29 metro stations of the red line opened on 9 September 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gulfnews_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-gulfnews-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Construction of the 18 stations on the red line and another 18 on the green line restarted on 7 February 2010, according to contractors, after a settlement was reached with a Japanese-led consortium over disputed payments of about US$2 billion-US$3 billion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Construction of all 29 metro stations on the Red Line was declared complete on 28 April 2010 by the acting chief of the RTA Rail Agency.</p>
<p>Seven more stations on the Dubai Metro Red Line opened on 30 April 2010. Ten new trains were pressed into service, giving a total of 22 trains in service when the stations opened. The seven stations are, Emirates Station, Airport Terminal 1 Station, Dubai Internet City (TECOM) Station, Al Karama Station, Emirates Towers Station, Marina Station and Ibn Battuta Station. In addition to this, a further three stations were opened on 15 May 2010; Al Quoz Station, GGICO Station and World Trade Center Station. Furthermore, Business Bay Station, First Gulf Bank (Burj Al Arab/Gold and Diamond Park) Station, Sharaf DG (Al Barsha) Station, Nakheel (Emirates Golf Club) Station and Jumeirah Lakes Towers Station were opened on 15 October 2010. After much delay, Jebel Ali Station, the terminus of the Red Line on the Abu Dhabi side was opened on 11 March 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and Jebel Ali Industrial Station, renamed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Danube_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Danube (company) (page does not exist)">Danube</a> Station, was opened on 12 December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The final two stations, Al Jadaf and Creek, on the Green Line were opened on 1 March 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dubai_Metro_fully_functional.3B_Al_Jadaf.2C_Creek_stations_open_to_public_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dubai_Metro_fully_functional.3B_Al_Jadaf.2C_Creek_stations_open_to_public-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Dubai Metro is operated by <a href="/wiki/Serco" title="Serco">Serco</a> under contract to the Dubai <a href="/wiki/Roads_and_Transport_Authority_(Dubai)" title="Roads and Transport Authority (Dubai)">Roads and Transport Authority</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Red_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Red Line (Dubai Metro)">Red Line</a> trains run every 7 minutes off-peak (averaging 8.5 trains per hour), with a minimum headway of 3 minutes 45 seconds (16 trains per hour) during peak hours, with 44 trainsets in service. From 2010, when 51 trains were in service, the line had a peak-hour capacity of 11,675 passengers per hour in each direction. As of September 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the Red Line operates 60 trains (train registrations 5001-5045, 5065-5079) The theoretical maximum design capacity is 25,720 passengers per hour, which would require 106 trains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Green Line (Dubai Metro)">Green Line</a> had an initial capacity of 6,395 passengers per hour per direction, with 19 trains (train registrations 5046-5064) in service as of September 2014. The design capacity of this route is put at 13,380 passengers per hour with 60 trains in service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ridership">Ridership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ridership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 280,000 passengers used the Dubai Metro during the first week of its operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before launch, Dubai Municipality Public Transport Department expected the metro to provide transport for 12% of all trips in Dubai. After the first month of operation (on a limited network), the monthly total was 1,740,578 passengers, which equates to under 60,000 passengers/day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> After the opening of more stations in May 2010, ridership surged to 103,002 passengers/day and reached 130,000/day by the beginning of October 2010. When the Green Line opened on 9 September 2011, passengers on the Red Line was noted as 180,000/day. In 2013, passengers rose to 377,000/day, split 64% for the Red line and 36% for the Green Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> During the first half of 2015, RTA announced that 88,252,034 passengers have used the metro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metro_Stats_H1_2015_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metro_Stats_H1_2015-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p>One issue for the new system will be how to reliably and comfortably get riders to their final destination if it is not located at a metro station. The RTA has changed and added "feeder bus routes" which act as shuttle services to and from major locations in and around the station area. There are bus and taxi laybys constructed as well as drop off zones at each station for ease of passenger access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-rt-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition 268 km of light rail lines are also planned, these will serve as feeders to the Dubai Metro. The <a href="/wiki/Dubai_Tram" title="Dubai Tram">Dubai Tram</a> is one of the light rail plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-rt-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first two lines of the Dubai Metro have 70 kilometres (43 mi) of lines, and 47 stations (including nine underground stations).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GN_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-GN-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roads_and_Transport_Authority_(Dubai)" title="Roads and Transport Authority (Dubai)">Roads and Transport Authority's</a> masterplan includes 421 kilometres (262 mi) of metro lines up to 2030 to cater to the expected above 4.1 million population of the city. There are plans for 268 kilometres (167 mi) of light rail tracks to act as a feeder system for the Metro, although only the Al Sufouh Tramway is under construction as of January 2013. The fate of this entire network – which would reportedly be divided into Blue, Purple, Pink and Gold lines – is now dependent on an economic recovery and private investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed">Proposed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Proposed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, the RTA stated that there are no "immediate plans" to build the Blue and Purple lines "in the next five or six years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asc_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-asc-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the RTA laid out a three phase plan to expand the existing lines and build new ones: extending the Green Line by 12 stations and 24 kilometres (15 mi) to Academic City by 2020; expanding the overall system by 58 stations and 91 kilometres (57 mi) by 2025 and completing expansion with a total of 69 stations and 221 km over and above the present 47 stations and 70 kilometres (43 mi) that are present as of January 2013.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Purple Line (Dubai Metro)">Purple Line</a>: along <a href="/wiki/E_44_road_(United_Arab_Emirates)" title="E 44 road (United Arab Emirates)">Al Khail Road</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dol_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-dol-35">[35]</a></sup> There will be about eight stations, three with check in facilities. However, The Dubai Airports claimed that this was unfeasible as it did not pass through many localities. They however suggested opting for a "central terminal" similar to those in Europe where trains leave from inside the airport to the other airport with trains also leaving to the city. The RTA have taken this into consideration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blue_Line_(Dubai_Metro)" title="Blue Line (Dubai Metro)">Blue Line</a>: along <a href="/wiki/E_311_road_(United_Arab_Emirates)" title="E 311 road (United Arab Emirates)">Mohammed Bin Zayed Road</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dol_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-dol-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Pink Line</b>. The Pink Line is planned to run east-west with a terminus at Al Sufouh and is scheduled for completion by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-railwaygazette.com_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-railwaygazette.com-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Gold Line</b>: Announced as the 'Yellow Line' in April 2008 and confirmed in January 2013 as the 'Gold Line'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-rt-31">[31]</a></sup> One of the stations planned for the Gold Line is the Dubai Land Station, west of Meydaan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The Gold Line will connect Arabian Ranches, Deira, and Dubai Marina and is scheduled to open by 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-railwaygazette.com_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-railwaygazette.com-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>Red Line Extension: 15.5 kilometres (9.6 mi) and six new stations, terminating at the border with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_(emirate)" title="Abu Dhabi (emirate)">Abu Dhabi</a>. No dates for completion announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Green Line Extension: The line could be further extended by 11 km from <a href="/wiki/Al_Jaddaf" title="Al Jaddaf">Al Jaddaf</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_City" title="International City">International City</a> under the Green Line extension project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In 2014, the RTA approved the recent proposal of extending the red line from Al Rashidiya station to Mirdif City Center which will increase 3.5 kilometer with the new station. However, there is also a proposal to extend it further to Al Warqa’a which is currently being studied.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On the green line, the RTA has finalized the extension plan of 20.6 kilometer from Al Jaddaf to Academic City. The extension will go through Festival City, Lagoons, Ras Al Khor Industrial Area, International City, Silicon Oasis and Dubai Academic City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dubai Metro is composed of at-grade (G) elevated Type 1, Type 2 and Type 3 (T1, T2 and T3, respectively) underground stations (U) and underground transfer station types (UT). Type 1 is the regular at-grade concourse station, Type 2 is a regular elevated concourse station, and Type 3 is an elevated special track station with an extra track to hold a non operational train. Underground transfer stations will be accommodating both the Red and Green lines for easy transfers.</p>
<p>Besides these differences, there are five themes used in the interiors of the stations:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Heritage: Symbolizes the culture and history of the United Arab Emirates.</li>
<li>Earth: Marks the start of the Dubai modern and urban drive, which resembles the force and durability of earth and soil.</li>
<li>Air: Symbolizes the elation and joy that Dubai provides to residents and visitors.</li>
<li>Fire: Symbolizes the energy, vigor and the strong will displayed by Dubai leaders.</li>
<li>Water: Symbolizes the human values which Dubai seeks to ensure in its modern achievements.</li>
</ol>
<p>The Earth stations have a tan-brown colour effects; water has blue-white colour effects; fire has orange-red colour effects; and the air has green colour effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DURLARPM_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-DURLARPM-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DURLAGPM_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-DURLAGPM-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Officials are negotiating with international and local companies over <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> for 23 stations on the two lines. This <a href="/wiki/Corporate_branding" title="Corporate branding">corporate branding</a> is the first of its kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Handicapped_facilities">Handicapped facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Handicapped facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Platform_screen_doors" title="Platform screen doors">Platform screen doors</a> with corresponding flashing light signals are installed at every station for the safety of the passengers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telecommunication">Telecommunication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Telecommunication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> connectivity is proposed across all trains and stations which will be provided by <a href="/wiki/Du_(company)" title="Du (company)"><b>du</b></a> so passengers can use laptops, tablets as well as mobile to be connected with the internet. Mobile phone coverage is available across the entire network of the metro. Only some metro stations have Wi-Fi, but it's on a trial basis to see if the connection will be available everywhere.</p>
<p>Passengers also have emergency voice communication with train staff at the control room through a speaker system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tram_Integration">Tram Integration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tram Integration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 11 November 2014 onward, The <a href="/wiki/Dubai_Tram" title="Dubai Tram">Dubai Tram</a> integrated with Dubai Metro - Dubai Marina and Jumeirah Lake Towers station. Footbridges are connected and provide a direct link between the two adjacent Tram and Metro stations for a smooth and seamless interchange for passengers between the two systems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travelling">Travelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Travelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dubai Transport is divided into 4 tiers (5 zones). As of 2013 the cheapest ticket (not preloaded, and not in the "gold" class) with distance not more than 3 km cost 1.80 AED (about $0.54) - equivalent of Tier 0, and most costly single trip (Tier 3, exceed 2 zones, and paper not preloaded ticket also) 5.80 AED (about $1.77) and was not increased from opening. Dubai Metro fares are among the cheapest metro fares in the world. Tier 1 is one zone trip, where the travel exceeds 3 km, Tier 2 is neighboring 2 zones travel. Also (excluding Gold class) using cards there is "no more paying" - a free rest of day travel if cost exceeds 14 AED (about $3.81).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ticketing">Ticketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ticketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dubai Metro has a fixed fare based on 3 tiers. The tiers are</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Tiers</th>
<th>Number of Zones</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Tier 1</td>
<td>Within 1 zone or less than 3 zones, crossing 2 zones</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Tier 2</td>
<td>Starts in 1 zone and ends in neighboring zone</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Tier 3</td>
<td>Crosses 3 or more zones</td>
</tr>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nol_Card" title="Nol Card">Nol Card</a> are used by the passengers to check-in and check-out at the gates in their destination station. The fare will be automatically deducted based on the number of zones traveled. Passengers will be allowed to check-in when their card has more than minimum credit required.</p>
<p>Children below the age of 5 years or less than 90 cm and people with disabilities (personalized Nol Card required) will be eligible to travel the metro for free.</p>
<p>There is also a Nol Card available for students &amp; seniors, and they can get a student &amp; senior citizens' discount.</p>

<p>Japanese manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Kinki_Sharyo" title="Kinki Sharyo">Kinki Sharyo</a> built a total of 79 five-car trains (60 on Red Line, 19 on Green Line).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firsttrain_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-firsttrain-53">[53]</a></sup> They are designed to carry 643 seated and standing passengers, and unusually for a mass transit system, the trains have three classes of accommodation: Gold Class (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_class_commuter_travel" title="First class commuter travel">first class</a>), Women and Children class (a classification that is extended to a greater number of cars during the peak hours), and regular Silver Class (economy).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The first train (5001) was delivered to Dubai in March 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firsttrain_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-firsttrain-53">[53]</a></sup> The metro has driverless operation and uses <a href="/wiki/Third_rail" title="Third rail">third rail</a> current collection. Trained wardens accompany passengers to help with emergencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The four newer trains (5074, 5075, 5076, and 5077) are each painted with a different special livery, in which one of them (train 5077) representing the skyline of Dubai. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signalling">Signalling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dubai_Metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Signalling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To permit fully automated operation, <a href="/wiki/Thales_Rail_Signalling_Solutions" title="Thales Rail Signalling Solutions">Thales Rail Signalling Solutions</a> is supplying its <a href="/wiki/SelTrac" title="SelTrac">SelTrac</a> IS <a href="/wiki/Communications-based_train_control" title="Communications-based train control">communications-based train control</a> and NetTrac central control technology. This is configured for a minimum <a href="/wiki/Headway" title="Headway">headway</a> of 90 seconds (40 trains per hour). The top speed of the trains is estimated to be around 95 km/h, giving a round-trip time of 2 hours 23 minutes for the Red Line and 1 hours 23 minutes for the Green Line.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<li><span class="vevent">12 August 2014<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">2014-08-12</span>)</span>: <span class="summary">Commuters on a segment of the Dubai Metro’s Red Line were stranded after a train (registered 5075) stopped between Al Karama and Al Jafiliya stations during peak hours following a technical snag at around 7 pm. The passengers stranded in the train broke emergency door lever glass cover and opened the door as there was no air conditioning available and walked to Al Karama Metro Station by walking on the viaduct. Two hours later, the Metro services were fully restored.</span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
