<p>The total <b>solar eclipse of July 11, 2010</b>, occurred over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Ocean" title="Southern Pacific Ocean">southern Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a> occurs when the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> passes between <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, thereby totally or partly obscuring the image of the Sun for a viewer on Earth. A total solar eclipse occurs when the Moon's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">apparent diameter</a> is larger than the Sun's, blocking all direct sunlight, turning day into darkness. Totality occurs in a narrow path across Earth's surface, with the partial solar eclipse visible over a surrounding region thousands of kilometres wide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visibility">Visibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_2010&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Visibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SE2010Jul11T.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="270" data-file-width="250" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/SE2010Jul11T.gif/220px-SE2010Jul11T.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/SE2010Jul11T.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SE2010Jul11T.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Animated map of the eclipse's visibility over the southern Pacific Ocean</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The eclipse on this day was one of the most remote in recorded history. It was visible over much of the southern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, touching several <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a> in <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>, and Argentina's <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonian</a> plains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSM-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fred_Espenak" title="Fred Espenak">Fred Espenak</a>, a NASA astrophysicist, said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"One of the most unique things about this particular eclipse is that it crosses a unique and interesting archaeological site: Easter Island. On Easter Island there are <a href="/wiki/Moai" title="Moai">these great statues</a>... There's a lot of mystery about these statues, but in any case, this is the first total eclipse to hit the island in about 1,400 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The best one occurred in French Polynesia, where it was seen in 98 percent, At the spectacular eclipse, The diamond ring effect and the Baily's beads happened.</p>
<p>It ended at sunset over the southern tips of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> in South America, including the town of <a href="/wiki/El_Calafate" title="El Calafate">El Calafate</a>. The Sun's altitude was only 1° during the 2 minute 47 second total phase, but <a href="/wiki/Argentino_Lake" title="Argentino Lake">Argentino Lake</a> offered an adequate line-of-sight to the eclipse hanging just above the rugged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andes_mountains" title="Andes mountains">Andes</a> skyline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 58% partiality occurred at sunset in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago, Chile</a>, but it was not visible due to adverse weather conditions. In other cities such as <a href="/wiki/Valpara%C3%ADso" title="Valparaíso">Valparaíso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coquimbo" title="Coquimbo">Coquimbo</a>, clearer skies permitted the event to be witnessed in <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continental</a> Chile.</p>

The moon's diameter was over 5% larger than the sun's, represented by the <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_of_eclipse" title="Magnitude of eclipse">magnitude of eclipse</a> of the table above, making for a relatively long eclipse duration of 5 minutes, 20 seconds.</div>
</div>

<p>Total eclipse began 700 kilometers (430 mi) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> at approximately 18:15 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> and reached <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a> by 20:11 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup> The global sky photography project <a href="/wiki/The_World_At_Night" title="The World At Night">The World At Night</a> stationed photographers throughout the eclipse's visibility track. Eclipse chasers photographed the event on board a chartered airplane, cruise ships, numerous Pacific islands, and in Argentina's <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> region. Totality was observed for four minutes and 41 seconds (4:41) on <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSM_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSM-2">[2]</a></sup> where it was observed for the first time in 1,400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Approximately 4,000 observers visited Easter Island for this eclipse, including tourists, scientists, photographers, filmmakers and journalists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> prompting an increase in security at its important <a href="/wiki/Moai" title="Moai">moai</a> archeological sites. The eclipse occurred at the same time that the final game of the <a href="/wiki/2010_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2010 FIFA World Cup">2010 FIFA World Cup</a> was being played in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and many soccer fans in <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> watched the match instead of observing the partial eclipse with a high percentage of obscuring the sun by about 98 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The path of totality of this eclipse barely missed some significant inhabited islands, including passing just about 20 km north of the northern end of <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This eclipse was the first one to happen over <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a> in 350 years. An estimated 5,000 tourists visited various islands in the archipelago to observe the event. Nearly 120,000 pairs of special glasses were distributed for observers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Eclipse chasers were also able to observe the eclipse at El Calafate, near the southern tip of Argentina, before the sun set just two minutes later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSM_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-CSM-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several hours after the eclipse was observed in continental Chile, a magnitude 6.2 earthquake struck in the <a href="/wiki/Antofagasta_Region" title="Antofagasta Region">Antofagasta Region</a>. There were no major injuries or damage in the nearby cities of <a href="/wiki/Calama,_Chile" title="Calama, Chile">Calama, Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Pedro_de_Atacama" title="San Pedro de Atacama">San Pedro de Atacama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_eclipses">Related eclipses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_2010&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Related eclipses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>It was preceded two weeks earlier by the <a href="/wiki/June_2010_lunar_eclipse" title="June 2010 lunar eclipse">partial lunar eclipse on June 26, 2010</a>.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saros_cycle" title="Saros cycle">Saros cycle</a> 146, this eclipse repeats every 18 years 11 days, previously on total <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_30,_1992" title="Solar eclipse of June 30, 1992">solar eclipse of June 30, 1992</a> and next on total <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_22,_2028" title="Solar eclipse of July 22, 2028">solar eclipse of July 22, 2028</a>.</li>
<li>In a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tritos_cycle" title="Tritos cycle">tritos cycle</a>, it repeats every 10 years 11 months, previous as the total <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_11,_1999" title="Solar eclipse of August 11, 1999">solar eclipse of August 11, 1999</a> and followed by the annular <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_10,_2021" title="Solar eclipse of June 10, 2021">solar eclipse of June 10, 2021</a>.</li>
<li>The next total solar eclipse will occur on <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_November_13,_2012" title="Solar eclipse of November 13, 2012">November 13, 2012</a>. The path of totality ends about <span class="nowrap">500 km</span> west of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> but the eclipse will be partial on sundown from the coast, and <span class="nowrap">2000 km</span> east of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_eclipses_2008.E2.80.932011">Solar eclipses 2008–2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_2010&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar eclipses 2008–2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metonic_series">Metonic series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_2010&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Metonic series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
