<p>The <b>Douay–Rheims Bible</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/aʊ/ 'ou' in 'pout'">aʊ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) (also known as the <b>Rheims–Douai Bible</b> or <b>Douai Bible</b>, and abbreviated as <b>D–R</b> and <b>DV</b>) is a translation of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> into English made by members of the <a href="/wiki/English_College,_Douai" title="English College, Douai">English College, Douai</a>, in the service of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> portion was published in <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a>, France, in 1582, in one volume with extensive commentary and notes. The <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> portion was published in two volumes thirty years later by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Douai" title="University of Douai">University of Douai</a>. The first volume, covering <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a> through <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Job</a>, was published in 1609; the second, covering <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> to <a href="/wiki/2_Maccabees" title="2 Maccabees">2 Machabees</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">apocrypha</a> of the Vulgate was published in 1610. Marginal notes took up the bulk of the volumes and had a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polemics" title="Polemics">polemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patristics" title="Patristics">patristic</a> character. They offered insights on issues of translation, and on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> source texts of the Vulgate.</p>
<p>The purpose of the version, both the text and notes, was to uphold Catholic tradition in the face of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> which up till then had overwhelmingly dominated <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_Religious_Settlement" title="Elizabethan Religious Settlement">Elizabethan</a> religion and academic debate. As such it was an impressive effort by English Catholics to support the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>. The New Testament was reprinted in 1600, 1621 and 1633. The Old Testament volumes were reprinted in 1635 but neither thereafter for another hundred years. In 1589, <a href="/wiki/William_Fulke" title="William Fulke">William Fulke</a> collated the complete Rheims text and notes in parallel columns with those of the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Bible" title="Bishops' Bible">Bishops' Bible</a>. This work sold widely in England, being re-issued in three further editions to 1633. It was predominantly through Fulke's editions that the Rheims New Testament came to exercise a significant influence on the development of 17th century English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the text of the 1582/1610 bible employed a densely <a href="/wiki/Latinism" title="Latinism">latinate</a> vocabulary, to the extent of being in places unreadable. Consequently, this translation was replaced by a revision undertaken by bishop <a href="/wiki/Richard_Challoner" title="Richard Challoner">Richard Challoner</a>; the New Testament in three editions 1749, 1750, and 1752; the Old Testament (minus the Vulgate deuterocanonical), in 1750. Although retaining the title <i>Douay–Rheims Bible</i>, the Challoner revision was a new version, tending to take as its base text the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> rigorously checked and extensively adjusted for improved readability and consistency with the Clementine edition of the Vulgate. Subsequent editions of the Challoner revision, of which there have been very many, reproduce his Old Testament of 1750 with very few changes. Challoner's New Testament was, however, extensively revised by Bernard MacMahon in a series of Dublin editions from 1783 to 1810. These Dublin versions are the source of some Challoner bibles printed in the United States in the 19th century. Subsequent editions of the Challoner Bible printed in England most often follow Challoner's earlier New Testament texts of 1749 and 1750, as do most 20th-century printings and on-line versions of the Douay–Rheims bible circulating on the internet.</p>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Bible" title="Jerusalem Bible">Jerusalem Bible</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_American_Bible_Revised_Edition" title="New American Bible Revised Edition">New American Bible Revised Edition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revised_Standard_Version_Catholic_Edition" title="Revised Standard Version Catholic Edition">Revised Standard Version Catholic Edition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Revised_Standard_Version_Catholic_Edition" title="New Revised Standard Version Catholic Edition">New Revised Standard Version Catholic Edition</a> are the most commonly used in English-speaking Catholic churches, the Challoner revision of the Douay–Rheims is still often the Bible of choice of more traditional English-speaking Catholics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/University_of_Douai" title="University of Douai">University of Douai</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="828" data-file-width="618" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG/220px-Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG/330px-Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG/440px-Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cartulaire_douai_3_coll%C3%A9ges.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Colleges at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Douai" title="University of Douai">University of Douai</a></div>

<p>The English <a href="/wiki/Exile" title="Exile">exiles</a> for religious causes, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recusants" title="Recusants">recusants</a>, were not all Catholic. There were Catholic refugees on the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European mainland</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a>, and from the one, as from the other, there proceeded an English version of the Bible. The center of English Catholicism was the <a href="/wiki/English_College,_Douai" title="English College, Douai">English College</a> at <a href="/wiki/Douai" title="Douai">Douai</a> (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Douai" title="University of Douai">University of Douai</a>, France) founded in 1568 by <a href="/wiki/William_Allen_(cardinal)" title="William Allen (cardinal)">William Allen</a>, formerly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen%27s_College,_Oxford" title="Queen's College, Oxford">Queen's College, Oxford</a>, and Canon of York, and subsequently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a>, for the purpose of training priests to convert the English again to Catholicism. And it was here where the Catholic translation of the Bible into English was produced.</p>
<p>A run of a few hundred or more of the New Testament, in <a href="/wiki/Quarto" title="Quarto">quarto</a> form (not large folio), was published in the last months of 1582 (Herbert #177), during a temporary migration of the college to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rheims" title="Rheims">Rheims</a>; consequently, it has been commonly known as the Rheims New Testament. Though he died in the same year as its publication, this translation was principally the work of <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Martin_(scholar)" title="Gregory Martin (scholar)">Gregory Martin</a>, formerly Fellow of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s_College,_Oxford" title="St. John's College, Oxford">St. John's College, Oxford</a>, close friend of <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Campion" title="Edmund Campion">Edmund Campion</a>. He was assisted by others at Douai, notably Allen, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bristow" title="Richard Bristow">Richard Bristow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Worthington_(Douai)" title="Thomas Worthington (Douai)">Thomas Worthington</a>, who proofed and provided notes and annotations. The <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> is stated to have been ready at the same time but, for want of funds, it could not be printed until later, after the college had returned to Douai. It is commonly known as the Douay Old Testament. It was issued as two quarto volumes dated 1609 and 1610 (Herbert #300). Surprisingly these first New Testament and Old Testament editions followed the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Bible" title="Geneva Bible">Geneva Bible</a> not only in their quarto format but also in the use of Roman type.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8685" data-file-width="5645" height="385" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf/page1-250px-1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf/page1-375px-1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf/page1-500px-1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1582_Rhemes_New_Testament.pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title page of the 1582 Rheims New Testament, transcribed into Modern English "specially for the discovery of the corruptions of divers late translations, and for clearing the controversies in religion."</div>

</div>
<p>As a recent translation, the Rheims New Testament had an influence on the translators of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_James_Version_of_the_Bible" title="King James Version of the Bible">King James Version</a> (see below). Afterwards it ceased to be of interest in the Anglican church. Although the cities is now commonly spelled <a href="/wiki/Douai" title="Douai">Douai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a>, the Bible continues to be published as the <i>Douay–Rheims Bible</i> and has formed the basis of some later Catholic Bibles in English.</p>
<p>The title page runs: "The Holy Bible, faithfully translated into English out of the authentic Latin. Diligently conferred with the Hebrew, Greek and other Editions". The cause of the delay was "our poor state of banishment", but there was also the matter of reconciling the Latin to the other editions. William Allen went to Rome and worked, with others, on the revision of the Vulgate. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sixtine_Vulgate" title="Sixtine Vulgate">Sixtine Vulgate</a> edition was published in 1590. The definitive <a href="/wiki/Sixto-Clementine_Vulgate" title="Sixto-Clementine Vulgate">Clementine</a> text followed in 1592. Dr Worthington, responsible for many of the annotations for the 1609 and 1610 volumes, states in the preface: "we have again conferred this English translation and conformed it to the most perfect Latin Edition."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite, this preface, there is no evidence that the Clementine Vulgate was referenced in any manner in the production of the 1609 and 1610 Bibles, so it is unclear to which Edition he was referring (e.g. Genesis iii, 15 does not reflect either Vulgate).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Douay–Rheims Bible is a translation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_Vulgate" title="Latin Vulgate">Latin Vulgate</a>, which is itself a translation from <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Greek</a> texts. The Vulgate was largely created due to the efforts of Saint <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> (345–420), whose translation was declared to be the authentic Latin version of the Bible by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a>. While the Catholic scholars "conferred" with the Hebrew and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> originals, as well as with "other editions in diverse languages,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> their avowed purpose was to translate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_translation" title="Formal translation">after a strongly literal manner</a> from the Latin Vulgate, for reasons of accuracy as stated in their Preface and which tended to produce, in places, stilted syntax and Latinisms. The following short passage (<a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Ephesians" title="Epistle to the Ephesians">Ephesians</a> 3:6–12), is a fair example, admittedly without updating the spelling conventions then in use:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Gentils to be coheires and concorporat and comparticipant of his promis in Christ JESUS by the Gospel: whereof I am made a minister according to the gift of the grace of God, which is given me according to the operation of his power. To me the least of al the sainctes is given this grace, among the Gentils to evangelize the unsearcheable riches of Christ, and to illuminate al men what is the dispensation of the sacrament hidden from worldes in God, who created al things: that the manifold wisedom of God, may be notified to the Princes and Potestats in the celestials by the Church, according to the prefinition of worldes, which he made in Christ JESUS our Lord. In whom we have affiance and accesse in confidence, by the faith of him.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Other than when rendering the particular readings of the Vulgate Latin, the English wording of the Rheims New Testament follows more or less closely the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> version first produced by <a href="/wiki/William_Tyndale" title="William Tyndale">William Tyndale</a> in 1525, an important source for the Rheims translators having been identified as that of the revision of Tyndale found in an English and Latin diglot New Testament, published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miles_Coverdale" title="Miles Coverdale">Miles Coverdale</a> in Paris in 1538.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Furthermore, the translators are especially accurate in their rendition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">definite article</a> from Greek to English, and in their recognition of subtle distinctions of the Greek <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">past tense</a>, neither of which are capable of being represented in Latin. Consequently, the Rheims New Testament is much less of a new version, and owes rather more to the original languages, than the translators admit in their preface. Where the Rheims translators depart from the Coverdale text, they frequently adopt readings found in the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Bible" title="Geneva Bible">Geneva Bible</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> or those of the <a href="/wiki/Wycliffe%27s_Bible" title="Wycliffe's Bible">Wycliffe</a> bible, as this latter version had been translated from the Vulgate, and had been widely used by English Catholic churchmen unaware of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lollard" title="Lollard">Lollard</a> origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nevertheless, it was a translation of a translation of the Bible. Many highly regarded translations of the Bible routinely consult Vulgate readings, especially in certain difficult Old Testament passages; but nearly all modern Bible versions, Protestant and Catholic, go directly to original-language Hebrew, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a>, and Greek biblical texts as their translation base, and not to a secondary version like the Vulgate. The translators justified their preference for the Vulgate in their Preface, pointing to accumulated corruptions within the original language manuscripts available in that era, and asserting that <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> would have had access to better manuscripts in the original tongues that had not survived. Moreover, they could point to the Council of Trent’s decree that the Vulgate was, for Catholics, free of doctrinal error.</p>
<p>In their decision consistently to apply Latinate language, rather than everyday English, to render religious terminology, the Rheims–Douay translators continued a tradition established by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">Thomas More</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Gardiner" title="Stephen Gardiner">Stephen Gardiner</a> in their criticisms of the biblical translations of William Tyndale. Gardiner indeed had himself applied these principles in 1535 to produce a heavily revised version, which unfortunately has not survived, of Tyndale's translations of the Gospels of Luke and John. More and Gardiner had argued that Latin terms were more precise in meaning than their English equivalents, and consequently should be retained in Englished form to avoid ambiguity. However, David Norton observes that the Rheims–Douay version extends the principle much further. In the preface to the Rheims New Testament the translators criticise the Geneva Bible for their policy of striving always for clear and unambiguous readings; the Rheims translators proposed rather a rendering of the English biblical text that is faithful to the Latin text, whether or not, such a word-for-word translation results in hard to understand English, or transmits ambiguity from the Latin phrasings:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>we presume not in hard places to modifie the speaches or phrases, but religiously keepe them word for word, and point for point, for feare of missing or restraining the sense of the holy Ghost to our phantasie...acknowledging with S. Hierom, that in other writings it is ynough to give in translation, sense for sense, but that in Scriptures, lest we misse the sense, we must keep the very wordes.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This adds to More and Gardiner the opposite argument, that previous versions in standard English had improperly imputed clear meanings for obscure passages in the Greek source text where the Latin Vulgate had often tended to rather render the Greek literally, even to the extent of generating improper Latin constructions. In effect, the Rheims translators argue that, where the source text is ambiguous or obscure, then a faithful English translation should also be ambiguous or obscure, with the options for understanding the text discussed in a marginal note. Underlying this philosophy of translation (and unique to the original version of the Douay-Rheims for English Bibles) is the belief that the Bible is intentionally difficult to understand, and was never meant to be read, or understood, by the lay-person. The translators and Church at that time held that the sole purpose for the lay reading of Holy Scripture was to read those parts that were clearly understandable as examples of living a good life.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>so, that people must read them with licence of their spiritual superior, as in former times they were in like sort limited. such also of the Laitie, yea &amp; of the meaner learned Clergie, as were permitted to read holie Scriptures, did not presume to inteprete hard places, nor high Mysteries, much lesse to dispute and contend, but leaving the discussion thereof to the more learned, searched rather and noted the godlie and imitable examples of good life and so learned more humilitie, obedience...</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The translation was prepared with a definite <a href="/wiki/Polemic" title="Polemic">polemical</a> purpose in opposition to Protestant translations (which also had polemical motives). Prior to the Douay-Rheims, the only printed English language Bibles available had been Protestant translations. The Tridentine–Florentine <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">Biblical canon</a> was naturally used, with the <a href="/wiki/Deuterocanonical_books" title="Deuterocanonical books">Deuterocanonical books</a> incorporated into the Douay–Rheims Old Testament, and only 3 Esdras, 4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses in the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">Apocrypha</a> section.</p>
<p>The translators excluded the apocryphal Psalm 151, this unusual oversight given the otherwise "complete" nature of the book is explained in passing by the annotations to Psalm 150 that "S. Augustin in the conclusion of his ... Sermons upon the Psalms, explicateth a mysterie in the number of an hundred and fieftie[.]" which again reflects the Catholic Hermetic philosophy of the translators and the College of Douay that the Bible contains mysteries that are hidden to the common man. This philosophy is most evident in the annotations such as the aforementioned annotation to Psalm 150 and on the annotations to the Psalms generally "For the whole Sacred Bible is a Sealed Booke, and not rightly understood til the seal, or lock be opened[.]"</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible,_1589.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3022" data-file-width="3892" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible%2C_1589.jpg/220px-Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible%2C_1589.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible%2C_1589.jpg/330px-Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible%2C_1589.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible%2C_1589.jpg/440px-Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible%2C_1589.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Title_Page_of_the_Rheims_New_Testament_with_the_first_page_of_the_Gospel_According_to_Matthew_Compared_with_the_Bishop%27s_Bible,_1589.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title Page of the Rheims New Testament with the first page of the Gospel According to Matthew Compared with the Bishop's Bible, 1589, edited by William Fulke, to prove the Bishop's Bible of the New Testament was superior to the Rheims Roman Catholic New Testament translation.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In England the Protestant <a href="/wiki/William_Fulke" title="William Fulke">William Fulke</a> unintentionally popularized the Rheims New Testament through his collation of the Rheims text and annotations in parallel columns alongside the 1572 Protestant <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Bible" title="Bishops' Bible">Bishops' Bible</a>. Fulke's original intention through his first combined edition of the Rheims New Testament with the so-called Bishop's Bible was to prove that the Catholic-inspired text was inferior to the Protestant-influenced Bishop's Bible, then the official Bible of the Church of England. Fulke's work was first published in 1589; and as a consequence the Rheims text and notes became easily available without fear of criminal sanctions. Not only did Douay-Rheims influence Catholics, but also it had a substantive influence on the later creation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a>. The Authorized Version is distinguished from previous English Protestant versions by a greater tendency to employ Latinate vocabulary, and the translators were able to find many such terms (for example: <i>emulation</i> Romans 11:14) in the Rheims New Testament. Consequently, a number of the Latinisms of the Douay–Rheims, through their use in the King James Bible, have entered standard literary English. Douay-Rheims would go on through several reprintings on both sides of the continent.</p>
<p>The translators of the Rheims appended a list of these unfamiliar words;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> examples include "acquisition", "adulterate", "advent", "allegory", "verity", "calumniate", "character", "cooperate", "prescience", "resuscitate", "victim", and "evangelise". In addition the editors chose to transliterate rather than translate a number of technical Greek or Hebrew terms, such as "<a href="/wiki/Azymes" title="Azymes">azymes</a>" for unleavened bread, and "pasch" for <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>. Few of these have been assimilated into standard English. One that has is "<a href="/wiki/Holocaust_(sacrifice)" title="Holocaust (sacrifice)">holocaust</a>" for burnt offering.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challoner_Revision">Challoner Revision <span id="Challoner_Revision"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Challoner Revision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation">Translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original Douay–Rheims Bible was published during a time when Catholics were being persecuted in the United Kingdom and possession of the Douay–Rheims Bible was a crime. By the time possession was not a crime the English of the Douay–Rheims Bible was a hundred years out-of-date. It was thus substantially "revised" between 1749 and 1752 by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Challoner" title="Richard Challoner">Richard Challoner</a>, an English <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a>, formally appointed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_partibus_infidelium" title="In partibus infidelium">deserted see</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dojran" title="Dojran">Debra</a> (Doberus). Bishop Challoner was assisted by Father <a href="/wiki/Francis_Blyth" title="Francis Blyth">Francis Blyth</a>, a Carmelite Friar. Challoner's revisions borrowed heavily from the King James Version (being a convert from <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> and thus familiar with its style). The use of the Rheims New Testament by the translators of the King James Bible is discussed below. Challoner not only addressed the odd prose and much of the Latinisms, but produced a version which, while still called the Douay–Rheims, was little like it, notably removing most of the lengthy annotations and marginal notes of the original translators, the lectionary table of gospel and epistle readings for the Mass, and most notably the apocryphal books (all of which save Psalm 151 had been included in the original). At the same time he aimed for improved readability and comprehensibility, rephrasing obscure and obsolete terms and construction and, in the process, consistently removing ambiguities of meaning that the original Rheims–Douay version had intentionally striven to retain.</p>
<p>The same passage of Ephesians (3:6–12) in Challoner's revision gives a hint of the thorough stylistic editing he did of the text:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That the Gentiles should be fellow heirs and of the same body: and copartners of his promise in Christ Jesus, by the gospel, of which I am made a minister, according to the gift of the grace of God, which is given to me according to the operation of his power. To me, the least of all the saints, is given this grace, to preach among the Gentiles the unsearchable riches of Christ: and to enlighten all men, that they may see what is the dispensation of the mystery which hath been hidden from eternity in God who created all things: that the manifold wisdom of God may be made known to the principalities and powers in heavenly places through the church, according to the eternal purpose which he made in Christ Jesus our Lord: in whom we have boldness and access with confidence by the faith of him.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Challoner</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>For comparison, the same passage of Ephesians in the King James Bible and the 1534 Tyndale Version, which influenced the King James Bible:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That the Gentiles should be fellow heirs, and of the same body, and partakers of his promise in Christ by the gospel: whereof I was made a minister, according to the gift of the grace of God given unto me by the effectual working of his power. Unto me, who am less than the least of all saints, is this grace given, that I should preach among the Gentiles the unsearchable riches of Christ; and to make all men see what is the fellowship of the mystery, which from the beginning of the world hath been hid in God, who created all things by Jesus Christ: to the intent that now unto the principalities and powers in heavenly places might be known by the church the manifold wisdom of God, according to the eternal purpose which he purposed in Christ Jesus our Lord: in whom we have boldness and access with confidence by the faith of him.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— KJV</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That the gentiles should be inheritors also, and of the same body, and partakers of his promise that is in Christ, by the means of the gospel, whereof I am made a minister, by the gift of the grace of God given unto me, through the working of his power. Unto me the least of all saints is this grace given, that I should preach among the gentiles the unsearchable riches of Christ, and to make all men see what the fellowship of the mystery is which from the beginning of the world hath been hid in God which made all things through Jesus Christ, to the intent, that now unto the rulers and powers in heaven might be known by the congregation the manifold wisdom of God, according to that eternal purpose, which he purposed in Christ Jesu our Lord, by whom we are bold to draw near in that trust, which we have by faith on him.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Tyndale</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">Publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Challoner issued a New Testament edition in 1749. He followed this with an edition of the whole bible in 1750, making some 200 further changes to the New Testament. He issued a further version of the New Testament in 1752, in which differed in about 2,000 readings from the 1750 edition, and which remained the base text for further editions of the bible in Challoner's lifetime. In all three editions the extensive notes and commentary of the 1582/1610 original were drastically reduced, resulting in a compact one-volume edition of the Bible, which contributed greatly to its popularity. Gone also was the longer paragraph formatting of the text; instead, the text was broken up so that each verse was its own paragraph. The <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha#Clementine_Vulgate" title="Biblical apocrypha">three apocrypha</a>, which had been placed in an appendix to the second volume of the Old Testament, were dropped. Subsequent editions of the Challoner revision, of which there have been very many, reproduce his Old Testament of 1750 with very few changes.</p>
<p>Challoner's 1752 New Testament was extensively further revised by Bernard MacMahon in a series of Dublin editions from 1783 to 1810, for the most part adjusting the text away from agreement with that of the King James Bible, and these various Dublin versions are the source of many, but not all, Challoner versions printed in the United States in the 19th century. Editions of the Challoner Bible printed in England sometimes follow one or another of the revised Dublin New Testament texts, but more often tend to follow Challoner's earlier editions of 1749 and 1750 (as do most 20th-century printings, and on-line versions of the Douay–Rheims bible circulating on the internet).</p>
<p>This Challoner version, officially approved by the Church, remained the Bible of the majority of English-speaking Catholics well into the 20th century. It was first published in America in 1790 by Mathew Carey of Philadelphia. Several American editions followed in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, prominent among them an edition published in 1899 by the John Murphy Company of Baltimore. In 1941 the New Testament and Psalms of the Douay–Rheims Bible were again heavily revised to produce the New Testament (and in some editions, the Psalms) of the <a href="/wiki/Confraternity_Bible" title="Confraternity Bible">Confraternity Bible</a>. However, so extensive were these changes, that it was no longer identified as the Douay–Rheims.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_books">Names of books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Names of books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The names, numbers, and chapters of the Douay–Rheims Bible and the Challoner revision follow that of the Vulgate and therefore differ from those of the King James Version and its modern successors, making direct comparison of versions tricky in some places. For instance, the books called <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezra" title="Book of Ezra">Ezra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Nehemiah" title="Book of Nehemiah">Nehemiah</a> in the King James Version are called 1 and 2 Esdras in the Douay–Rheims Bible. The books called <a href="/wiki/1_Esdras" title="1 Esdras">1</a> and <a href="/wiki/2_Esdras" title="2 Esdras">2 Esdras</a> in the KJV are called 3 and 4 Esdras in the Douay, and were classed as apocrypha. A table illustrating the differences can be found <a href="/wiki/Books_of_the_Latin_Vulgate" title="Books of the Latin Vulgate">here</a>.</p>
<p>The names, numbers, and order of the books in the Douay–Rheims Bible follow those of the Vulgate except that the three apocryphal books are placed after the Old Testament in the Douay–Rheims Bible; in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_Vulgate" title="Clementine Vulgate">Clementine Vulgate</a> they come after the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>. These three apocrypha are omitted entirely in the Challoner revision.</p>
<p>The Psalms of the Douay–Rheims Bible follow the numbering of the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>, whereas those in the KJB follow that of <a href="/wiki/Masoretic_Text" title="Masoretic Text">Masoretic Text</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_the_King_James_Version">Influence on the King James Version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influence on the King James Version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Old Testament "Douay" translation of the Latin Vulgate arrived too late on the scene to have played any part in influencing the King James Bible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Rheims New Testament had, however, been available for twenty years. In the form of William Fulke's parallel version, it was readily accessible. Nevertheless, the official instructions to the King James Bible translators excluded the Rheims version from the list of previous English translations that should be consulted, probably deliberately.</p>
<p>The degree to which the King James Bible drew on the Rheims version has, therefore, been the subject of considerable debate; with James G Carleton in his book <i>The Part of the Rheims in the making of the English Bible</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> arguing for a very extensive influence, while Charles C Butterworth proposed that the actual influence was small, relative to those of the Bishops' Bible and the Geneva Bible.</p>
<p>Fortunately, much of this debate was resolved in 1969, when Ward Allen published a partial transcript of the minutes made by <a href="/wiki/John_Bois" title="John Bois">John Bois</a> of the proceedings of the General Committee of Review for the King James Bible (i.e., the supervisory committee which met in 1610 to review the work of each of the separate translation 'companies'). Bois records the policy of the review committee in relation to a discussion of 1 Peter 1:7 "we have not thought the indefinite sense ought to be defined"; which reflects the strictures expressed by the Rheims translators against concealing ambiguities in the original text. Allen shows that in several places, notably in the reading "manner of time" at Revelation 13:8, the reviewers incorporated a reading from the Rheims text specifically in accordance with this principle. More usually, however, the King James Version handles obscurity in the source text by supplementing their preferred clear English formulation with a literal translation as a marginal note. Bois shows that many of these marginal translations are derived, more or less modified, from the text or notes of the Rheims New Testament; indeed Rheims is explicitly stated as the source for the marginal reading at Colossians 2:18.</p>
<p>In 1995, Ward Allen in collaboration with Edward Jacobs further published a collation, for the four Gospels, of the marginal amendments made to a copy of the Bishops' Bible (now conserved in the Bodleian Library), which transpired to be the formal record of the textual changes being proposed by several of the companies of King James Bible translators. They found around a quarter of the proposed amendments to be original to the translators; but that three-quarters had been taken over from other English versions. Overall, about one-fourth of the proposed amendments adopted the text of the Rheims New Testament. "And the debts of the [KJV] translators to earlier English Bibles are substantial. The translators, for example, in revising the text of the synoptic Gospels in the Bishops' Bible, owe about one-fourth of their revisions, each, to the Geneva and Rheims New Testaments. Another fourth of their work can be traced to the work of Tyndale and Coverdale. And the final fourth of their revisions is original to the translators themselves".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Otherwise the English text of the King James New Testament can often be demonstrated as adopting latinate terminology also found in the Rheims version of the same text. In the majority of cases, these latinisms could also have been derived directly from the versions of Miles Coverdale or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wyclif_Bible" title="Wyclif Bible">Wyclif Bible</a> (i.e., the source texts for the Rheims translators), but they would have been most readily accessible to the King James translators in Fulke's parallel editions. This also explains the incorporation into the King James Bible from the Rheims New Testament of a number of striking English phrases, such as "publish and blaze abroad" at Mark 1:45.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Harvard-Dumberton_Oaks_Vulgate">Modern Harvard-Dumberton Oaks Vulgate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern Harvard-Dumberton Oaks Vulgate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard University Press and Swift Edgar &amp; Angela Kinney at Dumberton Oaks Library have used a version of Challoner's Douay–Rheims Bible as both the basis for the English text in a dual Latin-English Bible (The Vulgate Bible, six volumes), and, unusually, they have also used the English text of the Douay-Rheims in combination with the modern Biblia Sacra Vulgata to reconstruct (in part) the pre-Clementine Vulgate that was the basis for the Douay-Rheims for the Latin text. This is possible only because the Douay-Rheims, alone among English Bibles, and even in the Challoner revision, attempted a word-for-word translation of the underlying Vulgate. A noted example of the literalness of the translation is the differing versions of "Lord's Prayer," which has two versions in the Douay-Rheims: the Luke version uses "daily bread" (translating the Vulgate quotidianum) the version in Matthew reads "supersubstantial bread' (translating from the Vulgate supersubstantialem). Every other English Bible translation uses "daily" in both places, the underlying Greek word is the same in both places, Jerome translated the word in two different ways because then, as now, the actual meaning of the Greek word "epiousion" was unclear.</p>
