<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Brigandine</h1>

<p>A <b>brigandine</b> is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_armour" title="Body armour">body armour</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. It is a cloth garment, generally <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, lined with small oblong steel plates <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">riveted</a> to the fabric.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigandine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="534" data-file-width="516" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg/220px-Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg/330px-Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg/440px-Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saint_Michael_and_the_Dragon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Saint Michael and the Dragon</i> with sword &amp; buckler, wearing brigandine with plate armour for hand and legs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Protective clothing and armour have been used by armies from earliest recorded history; the King James Version of the Bible [Jeremiah 46:4] translates the Hebrew סריון<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>ÇiRYON</i> or שריון <i>SiRYoN</i> "coat of mail"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as "brigandine". Medieval brigandines were essentially a refinement of the earlier <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_plates" title="Coat of plates">coat of plates</a>, which developed in the late 12th century, typically of simpler construction made of larger plates. The Asian-originated armour reached <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> invasion in 1240 that destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">Kievan Rus'</a> and generated extensive damage to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a> in 1241. The new armour became very popular first in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, towards the end of the 13th century and after having proved effective was adopted by the medieval states from West Europe several decades later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later Brigandines first appeared towards the end of the 14th century, but survived beyond this transitional period between mail and plate, and came into wide use in the 15th century, remaining in use well into the 16th. 15th century brigandines are generally front-opening garments with the nails arranged in triangular groups of three, while 16th century brigandines generally have smaller plates with the rivets arranged in rows.</p>
<p>The brigandine has been confused with the <a href="/wiki/Hauberk" title="Hauberk">haubergeon</a>, while the name is often confused with the <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a>, a swift small sea vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigandine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The form of the brigandine is essentially the same as the civilian <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(clothing)" title="Doublet (clothing)">doublet</a>, though it is commonly sleeveless. However, depictions of brigandine armour with sleeves are known. The small armour plates were sometimes riveted between two layers of stout cloth, or just to an outer layer. Unlike armour for the torso made from large plates, the brigandine was flexible, with a degree of movement between each of the overlapping plates. Many brigandines appear to have had larger, somewhat 'L-shaped' plates over the central chest area. The rivets, or nails, attaching the plates to the fabric were often decorated, being <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilt</a>, or of <a href="/wiki/Latten" title="Latten">latten</a>, and sometimes embossed with a design. The rivets were also often grouped to produce a repeating decorative pattern. In more expensive brigandines the outer layer of cloth was usually of velvet. The contrast between a richly dyed velvet cloth and gilded rivet heads must have been impressive and, unsurprisingly, such armour was popular with high status individuals.</p>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Flak_jacket" title="Flak jacket">flak jackets</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistic_vest" title="Ballistic vest">ballistic vests</a> are based on the same principle: a protective cloth <a href="/wiki/Vest" title="Vest">vest</a> containing metal plates.</p>

<p>It was commonly worn over a <a href="/wiki/Gambeson" title="Gambeson">gambeson</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chainmail" title="Chainmail">mail shirt</a> and it was not long before this form of protection was commonly used by soldiers ranging in rank from <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archers</a> to <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a>. It was most commonly used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men-at-arms" title="Men-at-arms">Men-at-arms</a>. These wore brigandine, along with plate arm and leg protection, as well as a helmet. Even with the gambeson and the mail shirt, a wearer was not as well protected as when wearing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_armor" title="Plate armor">plate armor</a>. However, the brigandine was probably favored by soldiers who preferred the greater degree of mobility this armour afforded.</p>
<p>Brigandines were simple enough in design for a soldier to make and repair his own armor without needing the high skill of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armorer" title="Armorer">armorer</a>.</p>
<p>A common myth is that brigandines were so-named because they were a popular choice of protection for bandits and outlaws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-edge-5">[5]</a></sup> This is untrue. Originally the term "brigand" referred to a foot soldier. A brigandine was simply a type of armour worn by a foot soldier. It had nothing to do with its alleged ability to be concealed by bandits. In fact, brigandines were highly fashionable and were ostentatiously displayed by wealthy aristocrats both in European and in Asian courts.</p>

<p>A similar type of armor was the <b><a href="/wiki/Jack_of_plate" title="Jack of plate">jack of plates</a></b> or <b>coat of plates</b>, commonly referred to simply as a "jack" (although this could also refer to any outer garment). <div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jack_of_plate,_English_or_Scottish,_c1590,_Royal_Armoury,_Leeds.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jack of plates, English or Scottish, c1590</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like the brigandine, the jack was made of small iron plates between layers of felt and canvas. The main difference is in the method of construction: a brigandine is <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">riveted</a> whereas a jack is sewn. Jacks were often made from recycled pieces of older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_armor" title="Plate armor">plate armor</a>, including damaged brigandines and <a href="/wiki/Cuirass" title="Cuirass">cuirasses</a> cut into small squares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jack remained in use as late as the 16th century and was often worn by <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Border_Reiver" title="Border Reiver">Border Reivers</a>. Although they were obsolete by the time of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> many were taken to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim_Fathers" title="Pilgrim Fathers">Pilgrim Fathers</a> as they provided excellent protection from <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">arrows</a>; one dating back to 1607 was recently found at <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_.22coat_of_ten_thousand_nails.22">Indian "coat of ten thousand nails"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigandine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: Indian "coat of ten thousand nails"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3500" data-file-width="2736" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg/150px-Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg/225px-Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg/300px-Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Armor_coat_Rajasthan01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Indian brigandine enforced by <a href="/wiki/Mirror_armour" title="Mirror armour">mirror plates</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Indian equivalent of the brigandine was the Chihal'Ta Hazar Masha, or "coat of ten thousand nails": a padded <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacket" title="Jacket">jacket</a> covered in <a href="/wiki/Velvet" title="Velvet">velvet</a> and containing steel plates which was used until the early 19th century. The skirt was split to the waist, allowing the soldier to ride a horse. Matching <a href="/wiki/Vambrace" title="Vambrace">vambraces</a> and boots containing metal plates were also used. It was derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> armor used by the <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">Saracen</a> armies. These were often elaborately decorated with gold lace, silk and satin and are highly prized by European collectors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tipu_Sultan" title="Tipu Sultan">Tipu Sultan</a> wore armor of this type during his wars against the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a> used similar armor during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(disambiguation)" title="Russo-Turkish War (disambiguation)">Russo-Turkish Wars</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Two complete suits of armor are preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_Museum" title="Hermitage Museum">Hermitage Museum</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad" title="Leningrad">Leningrad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_dingjia">Chinese dingjia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigandine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chinese dingjia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Qing_military_uniforms.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1527" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Qing_military_uniforms.JPG/150px-Qing_military_uniforms.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Qing_military_uniforms.JPG/225px-Qing_military_uniforms.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Qing_military_uniforms.JPG/300px-Qing_military_uniforms.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Qing_military_uniforms.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Qing military uniform, made to look like earlier Dingjia armour</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A type of armour very similar in design to brigandine, known as <i>dingjia</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese</a>: 釘甲; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a>: Dīng jiǎ) was used in medieval China. It consisted of rectangular plates of metal, riveted between the fabric layers with the securing rivet heads visible on the outside.</p>
<p>Russian orientalist and weapon expert Mikhail Gorelik states that it was invented in the 8th century as parade armour for the Emperor's guards by reinforcing a thick cloth robe with overlaping iron plates, but did not come into wide use until the 13th century, when it became widespread in the newborn <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol Empire</a> under the name of <i>hatangu degel</i> ("robe which is as strong as iron"). He also argues that Eastern European kuyaks and, supposedly, Western European brigandines originate from this armour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorelik_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gorelik-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dingjia brigantines were still used in China as late as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing" title="Qing">Qing</a> periods. It was favoured by officers for its rich, expensive look and protection. Later examples, however, often lacked iron plates and were merely a military uniform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_kuyak">Russian kuyak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigandine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Russian kuyak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1005" data-file-width="757" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg/150px-01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg/225px-01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg/300px-01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:01_098_Book_illustrations_of_Historical_description_of_the_clothes_and_weapons_of_Russian_troops.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kazakh_guard_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2889" data-file-width="1417" height="449" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Kazakh_guard_1.jpg/220px-Kazakh_guard_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Kazakh_guard_1.jpg/330px-Kazakh_guard_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Kazakh_guard_1.jpg/440px-Kazakh_guard_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kazakh_guard_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reenactor in Central Asian brigandine</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Moskovy, there was a type of armour known as the <i>kuyak</i>. It is thought to have Mongolian origins<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorelik_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gorelik-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and be analogous to the Central Asian,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Indian and Chinese brigandine armours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The word "kuyak" is itself a derivative from Mongol <i>huyag</i>, which means "armour" (of any type). No known intact examples of this type of armour survived the tumultuous history of Russia, but historical depictions, textual descriptions and photos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> remained.</p>
<p>The descriptions, while not offering any in-depth details of the kuyak's construction, suggest a textile body armour reinforced with iron plates (usually not specifying directly the placement thereof, only mentioning the "nails" - rivets, which attached the plates to the layer of cloth), often with long armoured <a href="/wiki/Faulds_(armour)" title="Faulds (armour)">faulds</a>, sleeves and/or pauldrons, sometimes covered in expensive textiles like <a href="/wiki/Sateen" title="Sateen">sateen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velvet" title="Velvet">velvet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Damask" title="Damask">damask</a> and decorated with fur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some kuyaks had large "mirror" plates or "shields" attached to the outside. Some descriptions also mention <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotton_wool" title="Cotton wool">cotton wool</a> padding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were also brigandine helmets called "kuyak hats" that used the same principle of construction as the kuyak body armour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_kikko_armour">Japanese kikko armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigandine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Japanese kikko armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kikko_vest.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Kikko_vest.JPG/150px-Kikko_vest.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Kikko_vest.JPG/225px-Kikko_vest.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Kikko_vest.JPG/300px-Kikko_vest.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kikko_vest.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Edo period <i>manchira</i> vest, with leather plates sewn in between layers of cloth</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kikko" title="Kikko">Kikko</a> is the Japanese form of brigandine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Kikko are hexagonal plates made from iron or hardened leather and sewn to cloth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1-21">[21]</a></sup> These plates were either hidden by a layer of cloth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> or left exposed. Kikko were used only relatively recently, during the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-google1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kikko comes in many forms including, coats, vests, gloves, arm and thigh protectors, and helmet neck guards. Kikko armor was worn as a stand alone defense or under other types of armor as additional protection.</p>
