<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">House of Dunkeld</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>House of Dunkeld</b>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a> Dùn Chailleann (meaning <i>Fort of the Caledonii</i> or <i>of the Caledonians</i>), is a historiographical and genealogical construct to illustrate the clear succession of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Scotland" title="List of monarchs of Scotland">Scottish kings</a> from 1034 to 1040 and from 1058 to 1290. The line is also variously referred to by historians as "The Canmores", and "MacMalcolm".</p>
<p>It is dynastically sort of a continuation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cen%C3%A9l_nGabr%C3%A1in" title="Cenél nGabráin">Cenél nGabráin</a> of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="Dál Riata">Dál Riata</a>, "race of Fergus", as "house" an originally Celtic concept to express one of the two rivalling leader clans of early medieval Scotland, whose founding father is king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fergus_Mor_of_Dalriada" title="Fergus Mor of Dalriada">Fergus Mor of Dalriada</a>. This Ferguside royal clan had rivalled the crown (of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalriada" title="Dalriada">Dalriada</a>, then that of <a href="/wiki/Alba" title="Alba">Alba</a>) against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cen%C3%A9l_Loairn" title="Cenél Loairn">Cenél Loairn</a>, the later <a href="/wiki/House_of_Moray" title="House of Moray">House of Moray</a> for the preceding four or more centuries. The <i>Cenél nGabráin</i> were represented by the so-called <a href="/wiki/House_of_Alpin" title="House of Alpin">House of Alpin</a> before Dunkeld. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Iain_Moncreiffe" title="Iain Moncreiffe">Iain Moncreiffe</a> made the case that <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%ADn%C3%A1n_of_Dunkeld" title="Crínán of Dunkeld">Crínán of Dunkeld</a> actually belonged to a Scottish sept of Irish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cen%C3%A9l_Conaill" title="Cenél Conaill">Cenél Conaill</a> royal dynasty, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="Uí Néill">Uí Néill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This of course would not exclude his descendants from also being a (female line) continuation of the Cenél nGabráin through <a href="/wiki/Beth%C3%B3c" title="Bethóc">Bethóc</a>.</p>
<p>Genealogically the Dunkeld dynasty is based on <a href="/wiki/Duncan_I_of_Scotland" title="Duncan I of Scotland">Duncan I of Scotland</a> being of a different agnatic clan to his predecessor and maternal grandfather <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_II_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm II of Scotland">Malcolm II of Scotland</a>. However, sociohistorically, the reign of Duncan's son <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_III_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm III of Scotland">Malcolm III of Scotland</a>, which happens to coincide with the start of the centuries-long period of strong influence from the southern neighbour, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, has been seen as a more important place to start.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_and_features">Time and features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Dunkeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Time and features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the time of House of Dunkeld, succession to the Scottish throne evolved towards <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a> instead of the Irish-Celtic tradition of <a href="/wiki/Tanistry" title="Tanistry">tanistry</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> traditions (whether they were <a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineal</a> or not). Although the contemporaries did not have a common name for these monarchs, they were a family who formed a hereditary kingship.</p>
<p>Distinctive characteristics of the developments of society during this dynasty:</p>
<ul>
<li>Scotland was more influenced by outlanders than it had been earlier, or was to be under the kings of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Bruce" title="House of Bruce">House of Bruce</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">House of Stuart</a>. The kingdom was between two established powers: the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. The common goal uniting the usual policies of kings of this dynasty was to balance between the two neighbours, sometimes allying to have some outside support (<a href="/wiki/France_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="France in the Middle Ages">France in the Middle Ages</a>). Wars were fought in both fronts, but also alliances and treaties were made with both.</li>
<li>Many Norman lords and institutions were brought to Scotland, especially after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a>; by the end of the period, both had been in Scotland for centuries. The tribal polity evolved to a medieval feudal society, adopting legal traditions from <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, and the elite evolved to become broadly "<a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a>" in custom, a change strengthened by the immigration of (Anglo-Saxon) English, Normans and French.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Dunkeld dynasty rose to rule in a time when the kingdom was fragmented, under increasing outside threats, and some monarchs started to initiate more centralized government.</p>
<p>The Dunkelds came to power after the two centuries of civil unrest under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Alpin" title="House of Alpin">House of Alpin</a>. The first king of this new dynasty was <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_III_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm III of Scotland">Malcolm III of Scotland</a> who determined that succession would be to the eldest son, not according to the rules of <a href="/wiki/Tanistry" title="Tanistry">tanistry</a>. This political decision reduced the conflicts inside the Royal family. The Dunkelds consolidated Scotland's' union and independence as a kingdom, despite several skirmishes with the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The fall of the House of Dunkeld began in 1286, when <a href="/wiki/Alexander_III_of_Scotland" title="Alexander III of Scotland">Alexander III</a> died in a horse riding accident. The king had no living sons, only one three-year-old granddaughter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Scotland_(Maid_of_Norway)" title="Margaret of Scotland (Maid of Norway)">Margaret</a>, princess of Norway. Fearing the influence of king <a href="/wiki/Eric_II_of_Norway" title="Eric II of Norway">Eric II of Norway</a>, her father, and another endless civil war, the Scottish nobles appealed to <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a>. Margaret was betrothed to the future <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">Edward II of England</a>, but died shortly afterwards on her arrival to Scotland.</p>
<p>The dynasty ended at a time when immigrant-originated feudal families had reached material level almost as high as the kings and the beneficiaries of feudal system desired to better guarantee their positions, rights and properties - even at the cost of national independence.</p>
<p>Following the end of the Dunkelds, Scotland fell in the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_Scottish_Independence" title="Wars of Scottish Independence">First Scottish War of Independence</a> against England.</p>
