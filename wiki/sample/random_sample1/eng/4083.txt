<p><i><b>Doomsday</b></i> is a 2008 <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-language</a> <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a> film written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Marshall" title="Neil Marshall">Neil Marshall</a>. The film takes place in the future in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, which has been quarantined because of a deadly virus. When the virus is found in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, political leaders send a team led by Major Eden Sinclair (<a href="/wiki/Rhona_Mitra" title="Rhona Mitra">Rhona Mitra</a>) to Scotland to find a possible cure. Sinclair's team runs into two types of survivors: marauders and medieval knights. <i>Doomsday</i> was conceived by Marshall based on the idea of futuristic soldiers facing medieval knights. In producing the film, he drew inspiration from various movies, including <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max" title="Mad Max">Mad Max</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_from_New_York" title="Escape from New York">Escape from New York</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/28_Days_Later" title="28 Days Later">28 Days Later</a></i>.</p>
<p>Marshall had a budget three times the size of his previous two films, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Descent" title="The Descent">The Descent</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dog_Soldiers_(film)" title="Dog Soldiers (film)">Dog Soldiers</a></i>, and the director filmed the larger-scale <i>Doomsday</i> in Scotland and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. The film was released on 14 March 2008 in the United States and Canada and in the United Kingdom on 9 May 2008. <i>Doomsday</i> did not perform well at the box office, and critics gave the film mixed reviews.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, a killer virus, known as the "The Reaper Virus", has infected the country of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. Unable to contain the outbreak or cure the infected, The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> government built a massive 60-foot wall that isolated Scotland from the rest of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>. The quarantine was deemed a success; however, the extreme method employed by the government destroyed diplomatic and economic relations between the UK and the rest of the world. This led to massive unemployment, civil unrest and extreme economic turmoil, causing the UK to collapse into a dystopia.</p>
<p>In 2035, 27 years after the quarantine, during a raid, police discover several people infected with Reaper Virus. The Prime Minister John Hatcher shares with domestic security chief Captain Nelson satellite footage of survivors in Scotland. Believing a cure may exist, Hatcher orders Nelson to send a team into Scotland to find medical researcher Dr. Kane, who was working on a cure when Scotland was quarantined. Nelson chooses Major Eden Sinclair to lead the team.</p>
<p>Sinclair's team crosses the wall and goes to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, Dr. Kane's last known location. In Glasgow, while searching the local hospital for survivors, Sinclair and her team are ambushed by a group of marauders. Sgt. Norton and Dr. Stirling manage to escape, while the team suffers heavy casualties. Sinclair and Dr. Talbot are captured by what turns out to be a huge gang of brutal cannibals. Sinclair is interrogated by the gang's leader, Sol, who plans to use her as leverage to cross the wall and invade England. It's the only way to change the UK back to its formal glory. He then leaves for the sacrificial ceremony to join the rest of the gang, who grill Dr. Talbot and eat him. With the help of Cally, another prisoner, Sinclair escapes and kills Viper, Sol's dedicated partner. After meeting with Norton and Stirling, they escape on a train. Cally reveals that she and Sol are Kane's children. Unsatisfied with Kane's rule, Sol left to create his own army.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Hatcher plans secretly to isolate the central London area because of the infection. However, an infected man breaks into his office and tries to kill him. Nelson shoots and kills the man, but his blood splatters on Hatcher. Michael Canaris, Hatcher's right-hand man, quarantines him and takes his place as de facto Prime Minister. Hatcher later commits suicide.</p>
<p>After leaving the train, Sinclair's group are arrested by soldiers armed with archaic weapons and armor. They are taken to a medieval castle and imprisoned. Their leader, Marcus Kane, tells Sinclair the truth: there is no cure, only people with a natural immunity. Originally a medical researcher, he was devastated when his family was left behind during the quarantine. After losing his wife, Kane became a twisted, sadistic leader. He sentences Sinclair and her group to death, pitting Sinclair against Telamon, his executioner, in a small fighting ground to entertain his followers. Sinclair subdues and kills Telamon. During the duel, the rest of the group escapes, retrieves their equipment and rescue Sinclair.</p>
<p>Sinclair, Norton, Stirling and Cally escape to a fallout shelter entrance on horseback. They locate an underground facility in the forest and find an intact <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Continental_GT" title="Bentley Continental GT">2007 Bentley Continental GT</a>, which they fuel up. Kane's medieval knights arrive and kill Norton. Sinclair and the others drive the Bentley back to the quarantine wall. On the way, they are intercepted by Sol's gang, who seeks to avenge Viper's death. After a high-speed chase, Sol and many of his men are killed and Sinclair's group escape.</p>
<p>The car is tracked down by a government gunship. Canaris arrives and reveals he will let the virus continue as a form of population control and profit. Cally's blood is to be used as a vaccine for the virus. Cally and Stirling board the gunship, while Sinclair chooses to stay behind. Nelson flies into the quarantine zone to speak with Sinclair and she gives him a recording of the conversation, the evidence to bring down Canaris. The recording is later broadcast to the rest of the country. Sinclair retrieves Sol's head and returns to his gang. Looking at the head, the marauders cheer, accepting Sinclair as their new leader.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Rhona_Mitra" title="Rhona Mitra">Rhona Mitra</a> as Major Eden Sinclair of the Department of Domestic Security, selected to lead a team to find a cure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitra_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-4">[4]</a></sup> The heroine was inspired by the character <a href="/wiki/Snake_Plissken" title="Snake Plissken">Snake Plissken</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hot_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-hot-5">[5]</a></sup> Mitra worked out and fight trained for eleven weeks for the film. Marshall described Mitra's character as a soldier who has been rendered cold from her military indoctrination and her journey to find the cure for the virus is one of redemption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The character was originally written to have "funny" lines, but the director scaled back on the humor to depict Sinclair as more "hardcore".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdcom_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvdcom-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bob_Hoskins" title="Bob Hoskins">Bob Hoskins</a> as Bill Nelson, Eden Sinclair's boss. Marshall sought to have Hoskins emulate his "bulldog" role from the 1980 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Good_Friday" title="The Long Good Friday">The Long Good Friday</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdcom_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dvdcom-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McDowell" title="Malcolm McDowell">Malcolm McDowell</a> as Marcus Kane, a former scientist who now lives as a feudal lord in an abandoned castle, having medieval army under his command and controlling parts of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orange_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-orange-8">[8]</a></sup> McDowell described his character as a <a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> According to Marshall, Kane is based on <a href="/wiki/Kurtz_(Heart_of_Darkness)" title="Kurtz (Heart of Darkness)">Kurtz</a> from <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Darkness" title="Heart of Darkness">Heart of Darkness</a></i>. The director originally sought to bring <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a> out of retirement to play Kane but was unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orange_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-orange-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Siddig" title="Alexander Siddig">Alexander Siddig</a> as Prime Minister John Hatcher. Marshall originally wrote Hatcher as a sympathetic character misguided by Canaris, but revised the character to be more like Canaris in embracing political manipulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdcom_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-dvdcom-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_O%27Hara" title="David O'Hara">David O'Hara</a> as Michael Canaris, a senior official within the British government whose position is never stated, who acts as Hatcher's puppeteer. Canaris was depicted to have a <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascist</a> background, speaking lines that paralleled <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>'s mindset of cleansing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdcom_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-dvdcom-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Craig_Conway_(actor)" title="Craig Conway (actor)">Craig Conway</a> as Sol, Kane's son and the leader of the marauders. He has a biohazard sign tattooed on his back and a large scar across his chest. Even though he is Kane's son, he distanced himself from him and formed his own army. He was a young child in the original 2008 quarantine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdcom_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-dvdcom-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lee-Anne_Liebenberg" title="Lee-Anne Liebenberg">Lee-Anne Liebenberg</a> as Viper, the wild woman who is Sol's trusty companion and second-in-command.</li>
</ul>
<p>Also cast as part of Eden Sinclair's team were <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Lester" title="Adrian Lester">Adrian Lester</a> as Sergeant Norton, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Robson" title="Chris Robson">Chris Robson</a> as Miller, and Leslie Simpson as Carpenter. The names Miller and Carpenter were nods to directors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Miller_(producer)" title="George Miller (producer)">George Miller</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Carpenter" title="John Carpenter">John Carpenter</a>, whose films influenced Marshall's <i>Doomsday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdcom_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-dvdcom-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sean_Pertwee" title="Sean Pertwee">Sean Pertwee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Darren_Morfitt" title="Darren Morfitt">Darren Morfitt</a> portrayed the team's medical scientists, Dr Talbot and Dr Stirling, respectively. <a href="/wiki/MyAnna_Buring" title="MyAnna Buring">MyAnna Buring</a> portrayed Kane's daughter Cally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-credits_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-credits-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emma_Cleasby" title="Emma Cleasby">Emma Cleasby</a> played Eden's mother at the start of the film.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception">Conception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Director <a href="/wiki/Neil_Marshall" title="Neil Marshall">Neil Marshall</a> lived near the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian's Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a>, a Roman fortification built to defend England against Scotland's tribes. The director fantasised about what conditions would cause the Wall to be rebuilt and imagined a lethal virus would work. Marshall had also visualised a mixture of medieval and futuristic elements: "I had this vision of these futuristic soldiers with high-tech weaponry and body armour and helmets—clearly from the future—facing a medieval knight on horseback." The director favoured the English/Scottish border as the location for a rebuilt wall, finding the location more plausible than a lengthy boundary between the United States and Canada. Additionally, Scotland is the home to multiple castles, which fit Marshall's medieval aspect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfq_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfq-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lethal virus in <i>Doomsday</i> differs from contemporary films like <i><a href="/wiki/28_Days_Later" title="28 Days Later">28 Days Later</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/28_Weeks_Later" title="28 Weeks Later">28 Weeks Later</a></i> by being an authentic plague that actually devastates the population, instead of infecting people so they become aggressive cannibals or zombies. Marshall intended the virus as the backdrop to the story, having survivors scavenge for themselves and set up a primitive society. The director drew from tribal history around the world to design the society. Though the survivors are depicted as brutal, Marshall sought to have "shades of gray" by characterising some people in England as selfishly manipulative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfq_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfq-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The director intended <i>Doomsday</i> as a tribute to post-apocalyptic films from the 1970s and 1980s, explaining, "Right from the start, I wanted my film to be an homage to these sorts of movies, and deliberately so. I wanted to make a movie for a new generation of audience that hadn't seen those movies in the cinema—hadn't seen them at all maybe—and to give them the same thrill that I got from watching them. But kind of contemporise it, pump up the action and the blood and guts." Cinematic influences on <i>Doomsday</i> include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max" title="Mad Max">Mad Max</a></i> (1979), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Max_2:_The_Road_Warrior" title="Mad Max 2: The Road Warrior">The Road Warrior</a></i> (1981), and <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome" title="Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome">Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</a></i> (1985): Marshall drew inspiration from the punk style of the films and also shaped Rhona Mitra's character after <a href="/wiki/Max_Rockatansky" title="Max Rockatansky">Max Rockatansky</a> as a police officer with a history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Escape_from_New_York" title="Escape from New York">Escape from New York</a></i> (1981): The director drew from the concepts of gang warfare and the experience of being walled-in. Rhona Mitra's character has an eye patch like <a href="/wiki/Snake_Plissken" title="Snake Plissken">Snake Plissken</a>(the eye patch is not in the last part of the film), though the director sought to create a plot point for the eye of Mitra's character to reinforce its inclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Excalibur_(film)" title="Excalibur (film)">Excalibur</a></i> (1981): Marshall enjoyed <a href="/wiki/John_Boorman" title="John Boorman">John Boorman</a>'s artistry in the film and sought to include its medieval aspects in <i>Doomsday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Warriors_(film)" title="The Warriors (film)">The Warriors</a></i> (1979): The director enjoyed the tough and violent films of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Hill_(filmmaker)" title="Walter Hill (filmmaker)">Walter Hill</a>, including the "visual style of the gang warfare".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition, during the scene where Sol addresses the crowd in Glasgow, a Baseball Furies gang member can be seen in the crowd.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/No_Blade_of_Grass_(film)" title="No Blade of Grass (film)">No Blade of Grass</a></i> (1970): Marshall perceived the film as a predecessor to <i>28 Days Later</i> and <i>28 Weeks Later</i>, though he sought to make <i>Doomsday</i> less straight-faced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Omega_Man" title="The Omega Man">The Omega Man</a></i> (1971): The director was inspired by the "empty city" notion of the film and drew upon its dark and gritty nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/A_Boy_and_His_Dog" title="A Boy and His Dog">A Boy and His Dog</a></i> (1974): Marshall created a homage to the 1974 film's ending by including a scene of a human being cooked in <i>Doomsday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Waterworld" title="Waterworld">Waterworld</a></i> (1995): The director enjoyed the gritty atmosphere and how people scavenge to survive and adapt in their new world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Gladiator_(2000_film)" title="Gladiator (2000 film)">Gladiator</a></i> (2000): Like in <i>Gladiator</i>, Marshall sought to put Mitra's character through a trial by combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_Men" title="Children of Men">Children of Men</a></i> (2006): With the film coming out during the development of <i>Doomsday</i>, the director realised the similarity of the premises and sought to make his film "more bloody and more fun".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-picks_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-picks-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Marshall also cited <i><a href="/wiki/Metalstorm:_The_Destruction_of_Jared-Syn" title="Metalstorm: The Destruction of Jared-Syn">Metalstorm</a></i> (1983),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recalls_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-recalls-13">[13]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Zulu_(1964_film)" title="Zulu (1964 film)">Zulu</a></i> (1964),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wow_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-wow-14">[14]</a></sup> and works of director <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a> like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fisher_King_(film)" title="The Fisher King (film)">The Fisher King</a></i> (1991) as influences in producing <i>Doomsday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shock1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-shock1-15">[15]</a></sup> Marshall acknowledged that his creation is "so outrageous you've got to laugh". He reflected, "I do think it's going to divide audiences... I just want them to be thrilled and enthralled. I want them to be overwhelmed by the imagery they've seen. And go back and see it again."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wild_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-wild-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rogue_Pictures" title="Rogue Pictures">Rogue Pictures</a> signed Marshall to direct <i>Doomsday</i> in October 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogue_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogue-17">[17]</a></sup> and in November 2006, actress Rhona Mitra was signed to star in <i>Doomsday</i> as the leader of the elite team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitra_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-4">[4]</a></sup> Production was budgeted at <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>17 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-from_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-from-2">[2]</a></sup> an amount that was triple the combined total of Marshall's previous two films, <i><a href="/wiki/Dog_Soldiers_(film)" title="Dog Soldiers (film)">Dog Soldiers</a></i> (2002) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Descent" title="The Descent">The Descent</a></i> (2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wild_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-wild-16">[16]</a></sup> The increase in scale was a challenge to the director, who had been accustomed to small casts and limited locations. Marshall described the broader experience: "There's fifty or more speaking parts; I'm dealing with thousands of extras, logistical action sequences, explosions, car chases — the works."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfq_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-cfq-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Production began in February 2007 in South Africa,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoskins_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoskins-18">[18]</a></sup> where the majority of filming took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-from_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-from-2">[2]</a></sup> South Africa was chosen as a primary filming location for economic reasons, costing a third of estimated production in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Shooting in South Africa lasted 56 days out of 66 days, with the remaining ten taking place in Scotland. Marshall said of South Africa's appeal, "The landscape, the rock formations, I thought it was about as close to Scotland as you're likely to get, outside of Ireland or Wales."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-location_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-location-20">[20]</a></sup> In Scotland, secondary filming took place in the city of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, including Haghill in the city's East End, and at <a href="/wiki/Blackness_Castle" title="Blackness Castle">Blackness Castle</a> in West Lothian,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-black-21">[21]</a></sup> the latter chosen when filmmakers were unable to shoot at <a href="/wiki/Doune_Castle" title="Doune Castle">Doune Castle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-location_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-location-20">[20]</a></sup> The entire shoot, involving thousands of extras, included a series of complex fight scenes and pyrotechnical displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wild_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-wild-16">[16]</a></sup> The director sought to minimise the use of computer-generated elements in <i>Doomsday</i>, preferring to subscribe to "old-school filmmaking".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfq_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-cfq-11">[11]</a></sup> In the course of production, several sequences were dropped due to budgetary concerns, including a scene in which helicopter gunships attacked a medieval castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sayer_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-sayer-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>A massive car chase scene was filmed for <i>Doomsday</i>, described by Marshall to be one part <i>Mad Max</i>, one part <i><a href="/wiki/Bullitt" title="Bullitt">Bullitt</a></i> (1968), and one part "something else entirely different".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-23">[23]</a></sup> Marshall had seen the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DBS_V12" title="Aston Martin DBS V12">Aston Martin DBS V12</a> used in the James Bond film <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(2006_film)" title="Casino Royale (2006 film)">Casino Royale</a></i> (2006) and sought to implement a similarly "sexy" car. Since the car company did not do <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">product placement</a>, the filmmakers purchased three new <a href="/wiki/Bentley" title="Bentley">Bentley</a> <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Continental_GT" title="Bentley Continental GT">Continental GTs</a> for US$150,000 each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wild_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-wild-16">[16]</a></sup> The film also contains the director's trademark gore and violence from previous films, including a scene where a character is cooked alive and eaten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recalls_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-recalls-13">[13]</a></sup> The production was designed by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Bowles" title="Simon Bowles">Simon Bowles</a> who had worked previously with Marshall on "Dog Soldiers" and "The Descent". Paul Hyett, the prosthetic make-up designer who worked on <i>The Descent</i>, contributed to the production, researching diseases including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">sexually transmitted diseases</a> to design the make-up for victims of the Reaper virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vision_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-vision-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_effects">Visual effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Visual effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doomsday_Bentley.jpg"><img alt="A black car flies through the air out of the side of a graffiti-covered bus that is exploding." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="298" data-file-width="697" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ee/Doomsday_Bentley.jpg/300px-Doomsday_Bentley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ee/Doomsday_Bentley.jpg/450px-Doomsday_Bentley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ee/Doomsday_Bentley.jpg/600px-Doomsday_Bentley.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doomsday_Bentley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Bentley was filmed crashing through the bus without use of pyrotechnics due to fire hazards, and visual effects were later added to show the explosion.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The visual effects for <i>Doomsday</i> stemmed from the 1980s stunt-based films, involving approximately 275 visual effects shots. While filmmakers did not seek innovative visual effects, they worked with budget restrictions by creating set extensions. With most shots taking place in daylight, the extensions involved matte paint and 2D and 3D solutions. The visual effects crew visited Scotland to take reference photos so scenes that were filmed in Cape Town, South Africa could instead have Scottish backgrounds. Several challenges for the visual effects crew included the illustration of cow overpopulation in line with a decimated human population and the convincing creation of the rebuilt Hadrian Wall in different lights and from different distances. The most challenging visual effects shot in <i>Doomsday</i> was the close-up in which a main character is burned alive. The shot required multiple enhancements and implementations of burning wardrobe, burning pigskin, and smoke and fire elements to look authentic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vfxworld_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-vfxworld-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neil Marshall's car chase sequence also involved the use of visual effects. A scene in which the Bentley crashes through a bus was intended to implement pyrotechnics, but fire marshals in the South African nature reserve, the filming location for the scene, forbade their use due to dry conditions. A miniature mock-up was created and visual effects were applied so the filming of the mock-up would overlay the filming of the actual scene without pyrotechnics. Other visual effects that were created were the Thames flood plain and a remote Scottish castle. A popular effect with the visual effects crew was the "rabbit explosion" scene, depicting a rabbit being shot by guns on automatic sensors. The crew sought to expand the singular shot, but Neil Marshall sought to focus on one shot to emphasize its comic nature and avoid drawing unnecessary sympathy from audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vfxworld_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-vfxworld-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marshall originally intended to include 1980s <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synth</a> music in his film, but he found it difficult to combine the music with the intense action. Instead, composer <a href="/wiki/Tyler_Bates" title="Tyler Bates">Tyler Bates</a> composed a score using heavy orchestra music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sayer_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-sayer-22">[22]</a></sup> The film also included songs from the bands <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_the_Ants" title="Adam and the Ants">Adam and the Ants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fine_Young_Cannibals" title="Fine Young Cannibals">Fine Young Cannibals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siouxsie_and_the_Banshees" title="Siouxsie and the Banshees">Siouxsie and the Banshees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Goes_to_Hollywood" title="Frankie Goes to Hollywood">Frankie Goes to Hollywood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kasabian" title="Kasabian">Kasabian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-credits_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-credits-10">[10]</a></sup> The song "<a href="/wiki/Two_Tribes" title="Two Tribes">Two Tribes</a>" by Frankie Goes to Hollywood was the only song to remain in the film from the first draft of the screenplay. "<a href="/wiki/Spellbound_(Siouxsie_and_the_Banshees_song)" title="Spellbound (Siouxsie and the Banshees song)">Spellbound</a>" by Siouxsie and the Banshees was a favorite song of the director, who sought to include it. Marshall also hoped to include the song "Into the Light" by the Banshees, but it was left out due to the producer disliking it and the cost being too high to license it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatrical_run">Theatrical run</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Theatrical run">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For its theatrical run, the film was originally intended to be distributed by <a href="/wiki/Focus_Features" title="Focus Features">Focus Features</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rogue_Pictures" title="Rogue Pictures">Rogue Pictures</a>, but the company transferred <i>Doomsday</i> among other films to <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Pictures</a> for larger-scale distribution and marketing beginning in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <i>Doomsday</i> was commercially released on 14 March 2008 in the United States and Canada in 1,936 theatres, grossing US$4,926,565 in its opening weekend and ranking seventh in the box office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-bom-28">[28]</a></sup> which <i><a href="/wiki/Box_Office_Mojo" title="Box Office Mojo">Box Office Mojo</a></i> reported as a "failed" opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Its theatrical run in the United States and Canada lasted 28 days, ending on 10 April 2008, having grossed US$11,008,770.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-bom-28">[28]</a></sup> The film opened in the United Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> on 9 May 2008, grossing a total of US$2,027,749 in its entire run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The film's performance in the UK was considered a "disappointing run".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanks_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanks-31">[31]</a></sup> The film premiered in Italy in August 2008, grossing an overall US$500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Worldwide, <i>Doomsday</i> has grossed US$22,211,426.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-bom-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Reception"></span><span id="Critical_reception"></span><span id="Critics"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"The director of top horror flicks <i>The Descent</i> and <i>Dog Soldiers</i> was given more money for his latest effort, but many thought he wasted it on a collection of flashy set pieces without much interlinking plot in between."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> on UK's general consensus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanks_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-tanks-31">[31]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p><i>Doomsday</i> was not screened for critics in advance of its commercial opening in cinemas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The film received mixed reviews from critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a></i> reported that 50% of critics gave the film positive write-ups, based on a sample of 68, with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">average</a> score of 5.2/10. The website's consensus reads, "<i>Doomsday</i> is a pale imitation of previous futuristic thrillers, minus the cohesive narrative and charismatic leads."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doomsday_Movie_Reviews.2C_Pictures_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doomsday_Movie_Reviews.2C_Pictures-34">[34]</a></sup> At <i><a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a></i>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the film has received an average score of 51, based on 14 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doomsday_2008:_Reviews_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doomsday_2008:_Reviews-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alison Rowat of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Herald_(Glasgow)" title="The Herald (Glasgow)">The Herald</a></i> perceived <i>Doomsday</i> as "decidedly everyday" for a thriller, with Marshall's script having too many unanswered questions and characters not fully developed despite a decent cast. Rowat said, "In his previous films, Marshall made something out of nothing. Here he does the opposite". The critic acknowledged the attempted homages and the B-movie approach but thought that "there has to be something more".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Steve Pratt of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Northern_Echo" title="The Northern Echo">The Northern Echo</a></i> weighed in, "As a writer, Marshall leaves gaping holes in the plot while as a director he knows how to extract maximum punch from car chases, beatings and fights without stinting on the gore as body parts are lopped off with alarming frequency and bodies squashed to a bloody pulp."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Philip Key of the <i><a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Daily_Post" title="Liverpool Daily Post">Liverpool Daily Post</a></i> described the film, "<i>Doomsday</i> is a badly thought-out science fiction saga which leaves more questions than answers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alonso Duralde of <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a> described <i>Doomsday</i>: "It's ridiculous, derivative, confusingly edited and laden with gore, but it's the kind of over-the-top grindhouse epic that wears down your defenses and eventually makes you just go with it." Duralde believed that Mitra's character would have qualified as a "memorable fierce chick" if the film was not so silly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> David Hiltbrand of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="The Philadelphia Inquirer">The Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i> rated <i>Doomsday</i> at 2.5 out of 4 stars and thought that the film was better paced than most fantasy-action films, patiently building up its action scenes to the major "fireworks" where other films would normally be exhausted early on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reviewer <a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> found the production of <i>Doomsday</i> to be a mess, complaining, "The action sequences might be more tense if they weren't obfuscated by rapid-fire editing, and the backstory is muddled and not all that interesting." Berardinelli also believed the attempted development of parallel storylines to be too much for the film, weakening the eventual payoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Dennis Harvey of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> said Neil Marshall's "flair for visceral action" made up for <i>Doomsday'</i>s lack of originality and that the film barely had a dull moment. He added, "There's no question that <i>Doomsday</i> does what it does with vigor, high technical prowess and just enough humor to avoid turning ridiculous." Harvey considered the conclusion relatively weak, and found the quality of the acting satisfactory for the genre, while reserving praise for the "stellar" work of the stunt personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Peter Hartlaub of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> also praised the film's stunts, noting that it was reminiscent of "the beauty of the exploitation film era". Hartlaub said of the effect, "Hire a couple of great stuntmen and a halfway sober cinematographer, and you didn't even need a screenwriter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matt Zoller Seitz of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> saw Rhona Mitra's character as a mere impersonation of <a href="/wiki/Snake_Plissken" title="Snake Plissken">Snake Plissken</a> and considered the film's major supporting characters to be "lifeless". Seitz described his discontent over the lack of innovation in the director's attempted homages of older films: "<i>Doomsday</i> is frenetic, loud, wildly imprecise and so derivative that it doesn’t so much seem to reference its antecedents as try on their famous images like a child playing dress-up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_response">Scottish response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Scottish response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scotland's tourism agency <a href="/wiki/VisitScotland" title="VisitScotland">VisitScotland</a> welcomed <i>Doomsday</i>, hoping that the film would attract tourism by marketing Scotland to the rest of the world. The country's national body for film and television, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Screen" title="Scottish Screen">Scottish Screen</a>, had contributed £300,000 to the production of <i>Doomsday</i>, which provided economic benefits for the cast and crew who dwelt in Scotland. A spokesperson from Scottish Screen anticipated, "It's likely to also attract a big audience who will see the extent to which Scotland can provide a flexible and diverse backdrop to all genres of film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cannibal_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-cannibal-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast, several parties have expressed concern that <i>Doomsday</i> presents negativity in England's latent view of Scotland based on their history. <a href="/wiki/Angus_MacNeil" title="Angus MacNeil">Angus MacNeil</a>, member of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>, said of the film's impact: "The complimentary part is that people are thinking about Scotland as we are moving more and more towards independence. But the film depicts a country that is still the plaything of London. It is decisions made in London that has led to it becoming a quarantine zone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cannibal_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-cannibal-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Doomsday</i> was not nominated nor considered as a possible contender at the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Scotland" title="BAFTA Scotland">BAFTA Scotland</a> awards despite being one of the largest productions in Scotland in recent<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> memory; <span class="nowrap">£2 million</span> was spent on local services. Director Neil Marshall applied for membership with the organisation to add "fresh blood", but <i>Doomsday</i> was not mentioned during jury deliberations. According to a spokesperson from the organisation, the film was not formally submitted for consideration, and no one directly invited the filmmakers to discuss a possible entry. Several of BAFTA Scotland's jury members believed that the criteria and procedures for a Scottish film were unclear and could have been more formalised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haunted_house">Haunted house</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Haunted house">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Doomsday</i> was used as inspiration in building a haunted house for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halloween_Horror_Nights_(Orlando)" title="Halloween Horror Nights (Orlando)">Halloween Horror Nights</a> in Orlando.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doomsday_(2008_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Doomsday</i> was the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> title released by <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Pictures</a> after the studio's initial support of the now-folded <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">HD DVD</a> format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The unrated version was released on DVD and Blu-ray on 29 July 2008 in the United States, containing an audio commentary and bonus materials covering the film's post-apocalyptic scenario, visual effects, and destructive vehicles and weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> assessed the unrated DVD's video quality, writing, "For the most part, it's a crisp disc that's leaps above standard def." The audio quality was considered up to par with the film's loud scenes, though IGN found volume irregularity between the loud scenes and the quiet scenes. IGN called the commentary "a pretty straight-up behind-the-scenes take on the movie and a bit over-congratulatory". It found the "most fascinating" featurette to be about visual effects, while deeming the other featurettes skippable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
