<p><b>Lefteye flounders</b> are a family, <b>Bothidae</b>, of <a href="/wiki/Flounder" title="Flounder">flounders</a>. They are called "lefteye flounders" because most species lie on the sea bottom on their right sides, with both eyes on their left sides. A <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">helpful reminder</a> when trying to recall the family name for this fish is that "Bothidae (Both o' dey) eyes are on the same side o' dey head."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-1">[1]</a></sup> The family is also distinguished by the presence of spines on the snout and near the eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lefteye flounders vary considerably in size between the more than 160 species, ranging from 4.5Â cm (1.8Â in) to 1.5Â m (4.9Â ft) in length. They include such economically important species as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paralichthys_olivaceus" title="Paralichthys olivaceus">Japanese halibut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
