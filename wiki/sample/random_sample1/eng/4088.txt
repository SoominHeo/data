<p>The <b>Dongxiang</b> people (autonym: <i><a href="/wiki/Sart" title="Sart">Sarta</a></i> or <i>Santa</i> (撒尔塔); <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">东乡族</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">東鄉族</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Dōngxiāngzú</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Xiao%27erjing" title="Xiao'erjing">Xiao'erjing</a>: دْوݣسِيْاݣذُ) are one of <a href="/wiki/List_of_ethnic_groups_in_China" title="List of ethnic groups in China">56 ethnic groups</a> officially recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>. Most of the Dongxiang live in the <a href="/wiki/Linxia_Hui_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Linxia Hui Autonomous Prefecture">Linxia Hui Autonomous Prefecture</a> and surrounding areas of <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> Province in northwestern China. According to the 2010 census, their population numbers 621,500.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_development">Origin and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongxiangs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dongxiang are closely related to other Mongolic peoples like the Monguor and Bonan. Scholars speculate that their identity as an independent ethnic group arose through contact with Central Asians, due to whom the Dongxiang converted to <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a> in the 13th century.</p>
<p>They are believed to be descendants of Mongolian troops posted in the Hezhou area by <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a> (1162-1227 AD) during his journey westward, mixed with Sarts, which are merchants from Central Asia. Another possibility is that they could be a mixture of many peoples including Mongolian, Han, and Tibetan groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American Asiatic Association published an account of the Dongxiang's origins in the "Asia, Volume 40". A Muslim Mongol, Ma Chuanyuan, who was the supermagistrate of five districts, was interviewed, and gave a story on his people's origins. The conversion to Islam by a clan descended from Genghis Khan angered their relatives, who drove them all the way to Eastern <a href="/wiki/Linxia_Hui_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Linxia Hui Autonomous Prefecture">Linxia</a>. This occurred at the twilight of the Yuan dynasty. East Linxia was described as a land of "thorns and yellow earth". The author estimated a number of 100,000 Mongolian Muslims. They spoke Mongolian but were all illiterate. The account described them as a community of one hundred thousand, Mongol by race, Islam by religion and Chinese by culture. The majority of them were monolingual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dongxiang were also known as Santa (San-t'a) people, it was reported that many of them served in the army of the <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui</a> General <a href="/wiki/Ma_Fuxiang" title="Ma Fuxiang">Ma Fuxiang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was even said that Ma Fuxiang himself was of Santa descent, who had assimilated into the Hui community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their autonym, <i>sarta</i>, may also provide a contradictory clue to their origin: a similar word <i><a href="/wiki/Sart" title="Sart">Sart</a></i> was formerly used in Central Asia to refer to Arab traders<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, later to the local (mostly) Turkic-speaking city dwellers. Their official name of Dōngxiāng meaning "eastern villages" stems from the fact that their settlements are east of the major Han Chinese settlements.</p>
<p>Like other Muslims in China, the Dongxiang served extensively in the Chinese military. It was said that they and the <a href="/wiki/Salar_people" title="Salar people">Salars</a> were given to "eating rations", a reference to military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dongxiang, Baoan, and Hui troops served under Generals <a href="/wiki/Ma_Fulu" title="Ma Fulu">Ma Fulu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ma_Fuxiang" title="Ma Fuxiang">Ma Fuxiang</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion" title="Boxer Rebellion">Boxer Rebellion</a>, defeating the invading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eight_Nation_Alliance" title="Eight Nation Alliance">Eight Nation Alliance</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Langfang" title="Battle of Langfang">Battle of Langfang</a>. Ma Fulu along with 100 Dongxiang and Hui troops died in fierce combat at <a href="/wiki/Zhengyangmen" title="Zhengyangmen">Zhengyang Gate</a> in Beijing against the Alliance forces as they fought to the death to hold the Alliance at bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dongxiang, Baoan, Hui, Salar, and Tibetan troops served under <a href="/wiki/Ma_Biao_(general)" title="Ma Biao (general)">Ma Biao</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a> against the Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscegenation">Miscegenation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongxiangs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Miscegenation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dongxiang have <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetans" title="Tibetans">Tibetan</a> surnames.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Dongxiang with Han Chinese surnames such as Wang, Kang, Zhang, Gao, and Huang claim descent from Han Chinese. Those with surnames such as Ma and Mu are descended from Hui.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the Muslim <a href="/wiki/Confucius#Descendants" title="Confucius">descendants of Confucius</a> are descended from the marriage of Ma Jiaga (马甲尕), a Muslim woman, and Kong Yanrong (孔彦嵘), 59th generation descendant of Confucius in the year 1480 and their descendants are found among the Hui and Dongxiang peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Dongxiang have said that, in the rare instances that they do <a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">marry with other people</a>, it is only with Hui and Han, but not Tibetans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>A town called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangwangchuan" title="Tangwangchuan">Tangwangchuan</a> (唐汪川) in <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> had a multiethnic populace, the Tang (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">唐</span>) and Wang (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">汪</span>) families being the two major families. The Tang and Wang families were originally of non-Muslim Han Chinese extraction, but by the 1900s some branches of the families became Muslim by "intermarriage or conversion", while other branches of the families remained non-Muslim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> People in the area have changed their ethnicity by marrying members of other groups or converting to their religion. The Tang and Wang families are now composed of all three different ethnic groups, with <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui</a>, and Dongxiang people. The Dongxiang and Hui are Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Tangwangchuan and Hanjiaji were notable for being the lone towns with a multiethnic community, with both non-Muslims and Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cuisines of various ethnicities have spread across boundaries in the area of Hehuang, with different groups such as Mongolians, Tibetan, Dongxiang, and Hui eating each other's cuisines, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutton" title="Mutton">mutton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milk_tea" title="Milk tea">milk tea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongxiangs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The base of the economy of Dongxiang is agriculture. The main products are potatoes, maize and wheat. They are also recognized craftsmen, specializing in the elaboration of traditional carpets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_and_education">Language and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongxiangs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dongxiang speak the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dongxiang_language" title="Dongxiang language">Dongxiang language</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_languages" title="Mongolic languages">Mongolic</a> family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Dongxiang people also have a rich tradition of oral literature, and use the Arabic alphabet.</p>
<p>As a result of the <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">language shift</a>, some 20,000 people in several villages in the northeastern <a href="/wiki/Dongxiang_Autonomous_County" title="Dongxiang Autonomous County">Dongxiang County</a> now speak the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Tangwang_language" title="Tangwang language">Tangwang language</a>": a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creolized</a> version of <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a> with a strong Dongxiang influence, in particular in its grammar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-lee-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Government statistics show that the Dongxiang are among the poorest and least literate of China's minorities, with most Dongxiang having completed only an average of 1.1 years of schooling, a problem aggravated by the lack of a written language.</p>
<p>In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a> provided US$30,000 in grant money for a pilot project to promote bilingual education in Dongxiang and Mandarin, in an effort to reduce school drop-out rates. The project is credited with the publication of a Dongxiang-Chinese <a href="/wiki/Bilingual_dictionary" title="Bilingual dictionary">bilingual dictionary</a> as well as recent rises in test scores.</p>
