<p>A <b>dungkhag</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dzongkha_language" title="Dzongkha language">Dzongkha</a>: <span lang="dz" xml:lang="dz">དྲུང་ཁག</span>་ <i>drungkhak</i>) is a sub-district of a <a href="/wiki/Dzongkhag" title="Dzongkhag">dzongkhag</a> (district) of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>. The head of a dungkhag is a <i>Dungpa</i>. As of 2007, nine of the twenty dzongkhags had from one to three dungkhags, with sixteen dungkhags in total.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungkhag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under Bhutan's first government Act of decentralization, the Dzongkhag Yargay Tshogdu Chathrim of 2002 Dungpas were given a non-voting seat on the Dzongkhag Yargay Tshogdu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DYT02_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DYT02-1">[1]</a></sup> Under the Local Government Act of 2007, dungkhags provided general administration and coordination for two or more gewogs. As a result, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gewog" title="Gewog">gewogs</a> within a given district were directly subordinate to dungkhags while others are directly subordinate to <a href="/wiki/Dzongkhag" title="Dzongkhag">dzongkhags</a>. Dungkhag Administrations guided and supported their constituent Gewog Administrations and implemented the decisions of the Dzongkhag Tshogdu. Dungpas were administrative executives that reported directly to the Dzongkhag administration. The Dungpa was empowered to attend the meetings of the Gewog Tshogdes as a non-voting member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGA07_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LGA07-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to administrative functions, <a href="/wiki/Dungkhag_Court" title="Dungkhag Court">Dungkhag Courts</a> were codified under the Judicial Act of 2007 and <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bhutan" title="Constitution of Bhutan">Constitution of 2008</a> as a court of first instance within the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_system_of_Bhutan" title="Judicial system of Bhutan">judicial system of Bhutan</a>. These courts are subordinate to <a href="/wiki/Dzongkhag_Court" title="Dzongkhag Court">Dzongkhag Courts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JA07_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-JA07-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoBE_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoBE-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_of_Bhutan_2009" title="Local Government Act of Bhutan 2009">Local Government Act of 2009</a>, the administrative status of dungkhags is eschewed, and Dungpas are not specified to attend meetings of the Dzongkhag Tshogdu. The repeal of the Local Government Act of 2007 means that although dungkhags are no longer legal administrative divisions, they remain <a href="/wiki/Royal_Court_of_Justice" title="Royal Court of Justice">judicial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_Bhutan" title="Law enforcement in Bhutan">law enforcement</a> jurisdictions under the Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoBE_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CoBE-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGA09_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LGA09-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_dungkhags">List of dungkhags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungkhag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: List of dungkhags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The internal territorial divisions of Bhutan, including dungkhags and their constituent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gewog" title="Gewog">gewogs</a>, are subject to alteration by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Bhutan" title="Government of Bhutan">Government of Bhutan</a> through creation, transfer, and merger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoBE_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CoBE-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LGA09_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-LGA09-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, in 2002, there were 199 gewogs in the 20 dzongkhags;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9FYP_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-9FYP-6">[6]</a></sup> by 2005, there were 205.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geohive_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-geohive-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2007, Lhamozingkha Dungkhag was formally transferred from <a href="/wiki/Sarpang_District" title="Sarpang District">Sarpang Dzongkhag</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dagana_District" title="Dagana District">Dagana Dzongkhag</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sarpang1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sarpang1-8">[8]</a></sup> affecting the town of Lhamozingkha and three constituent gewogs – Lhamozingkha, Deorali and Nichula (Zinchula) – that formed the westernmost part of Sarpang and became the southernmost part of Dagana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sarpang2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sarpang2-9">[9]</a></sup> Such changes are reflected in the list below.</p>
<p>As of 2007, there were 16 dungkhags among nine of twenty Bhutanese <a href="/wiki/Dzongkhag" title="Dzongkhag">dzongkhags</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Statoids_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Statoids-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPP_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BPP-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
