<p><b>Dongting Lake</b> is a large, shallow <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a> province, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It is a flood basin of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze River</a>. Hence the lake's size depends on the season. The provinces of <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a> and Hunan are named after their location relative to the lake: <i>Hubei</i> means "North of the Lake" and <i>Hunan</i>, "South of the Lake".</p>
<p>Dongting Lake is famous in Chinese culture as the place of origin of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_boat_racing" title="Dragon boat racing">dragon boat racing</a>. It is the site of <a href="/wiki/Junshan_Island" title="Junshan Island">Junshan Island</a> and is a home to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finless_Porpoise" title="Finless Porpoise">Finless Porpoise</a>, which is endangered in China.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongting_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dongting_Plain.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1668" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Dongting_Plain.png/300px-Dongting_Plain.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Dongting_Plain.png/450px-Dongting_Plain.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Dongting_Plain.png/600px-Dongting_Plain.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dongting_Plain.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Dongting Plain</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the July–September period, flood water from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze</a> flows into the lake, enlarging it greatly. The lake's area, which normally is 2,820 square kilometers or 1,090 square miles (data before 1998), may increase to 20,000 square kilometers (7,700 sq mi) in flood season, when vast amounts of water and sediment from the Chang Jiang flow into the lake. The lake is also fed by four major rivers: the <a href="/wiki/Xiang_River" title="Xiang River">Xiang</a> (湘), <a href="/wiki/Zi_River" title="Zi River">Zi</a> (资), <a href="/wiki/Yuan_River" title="Yuan River">Yuan</a> (沅) and <a href="/wiki/Lishui_River" title="Lishui River">Li</a> (澧) rivers. Small rivers also flow in, the most famous one being <a href="/wiki/Miluo_River" title="Miluo River">Miluo River</a> where a famous poet <a href="/wiki/Qu_Yuan" title="Qu Yuan">Qu Yuan</a> committed suicide. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Xiao_River" title="Xiao River">Xiao River</a> (瀟) flows into the Xiang near <a href="/wiki/Yongzhou" title="Yongzhou">Yongzhou</a>, before the Xiang flows into the lake. Ocean-going vessels can travel through the Xiang to reach <a href="/wiki/Changsha" title="Changsha">Changsha</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongting_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest rice paddies yet discovered in the world were in the Liyang plain, which was then on the western edge of Dongting lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The state of Chu occupied the region in the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Eastern Zhou period</a>, and its territory there was taken over by <a href="/wiki/Qin_(state)" title="Qin (state)">Qin (state)</a> in the 3rd century BCE. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a>, Yunmeng Marsh (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">云梦大泽</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Yúnmèng dàzé</span></i> literally "Great Marsh of Cloud Dream"), which lies to the north of Dongting Lake in Hubei Province, served as the main flood-basin of the Yangtze. The rich sediment of the marsh attracted farmers. Embankments were built, keeping the river out, and the Dongting Lake area south of the Yangtze gradually became the river's main flood-basin. The Han state was actively involved in the colonization of the region, maintaining dikes in Liyang to protect farmland from flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At that time, Dongting Lake was China's largest freshwater lake. Because of its size, it gained the name <i>Eight-hundred-li-Dongting</i> (八百里洞庭). Nowadays, it is the second-largest, after <a href="/wiki/Poyang_Lake" title="Poyang Lake">Poyang Lake</a> (鄱阳湖), as much of the lake has been <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">turned into farmland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The area is well known in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_history" title="Chinese history">Chinese history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">literature</a>. "Dongting" literally means "Grotto Court", and the lake was named for the huge hall or cavern, which was believed to exist beneath the lake, where the spirits of the Sage-King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shun_(Chinese_leader)" title="Shun (Chinese leader)">Shun</a>'s wives <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xiang_River_goddesses" title="Xiang River goddesses">Ehuang and Nüying</a> were said to be the rulers of this grotto, which was claimed to have underground passages opening to all parts of the empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Dragon boat racing is said to have begun on the eastern shores of Dongting Lake as a search for the body of <a href="/wiki/Qu_Yuan" title="Qu Yuan">Qu Yuan</a>, the Chu poet (340–278 BC), and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon-king" title="Dragon-king">dragon-king</a> is said to live at the bottom of the lake.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Junshan_Island" title="Junshan Island">Junshan</a> was also named after the goddess-wives of Shun, who lived there after his death by drowning, while they mourned him and sought for his body all the way from the source of the Xiang River, in which he drowned, and then on down to where it could have drifted, into the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurck20009-10_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurck20009-10-5">[5]</a></sup> Junshan Island, formerly a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daoist" title="Daoist">Daoist</a> retreat, is a famous one-kilometer island with 72 peaks in the middle of the lake. The island is also famous for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junshan_Yinzhen_tea" title="Junshan Yinzhen tea">Junshan Yinzhen tea</a>. The basin of Dongting Lake and its surrounding area is famous for its scenic beauty, which has been encapsulated in the phrase "Hunan of the <a href="/wiki/Xiaoxiang" title="Xiaoxiang">Xiao and Xiang</a> rivers" (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">瀟湘湖南</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Xiāo-Xiāng Húnán</span></i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The scenery of the <a href="/wiki/Jiuyi_Mountains" title="Jiuyi Mountains">Jiuyi Mountains</a> and of the Xiao and Xiang rivers below is often mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_poetry" title="Chinese poetry">Chinese poetry</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Tang_poetry#Late_Tang" title="Tang poetry">late Tang Dynasty poet</a> <a href="/wiki/Yu_Wuling" title="Yu Wuling">Yu Wuling</a> is supposed to have been fond of the scenery of Dongting Lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUeki_et_al.1999133.E2.80.934_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUeki_et_al.1999133.E2.80.934-6">[6]</a></sup> During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>, it became the fashion to paint this region's scenery in a set of eight scenes, usually entitled as <a href="/wiki/Eight_Views_of_Xiaoxiang" title="Eight Views of Xiaoxiang">Eight Views of Xiaoxiang</a>. The fashion spread to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, where eventually other famous places were substituted for the Xiao and Xiang rivers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One of the famous ponds based on the geography of the lake is at the <a href="/wiki/Daikaku-ji" title="Daikaku-ji">Daikaku-ji</a> in Kyoto.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dongting_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The agricultural colonization of the region began in ancient times, and by the 19th century much of the lake's shallower areas had been destroyed to create farmland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After 1949 a new round of wetland drainage destroyed much of what remained, leaving only a fraction of the original wetland intact, though some of that area has subsequently been returned to wetland conditions. Nonetheless, along with Poyang Lake, it remains one of the largest lakes in China, and is an important wintering area for migratory birds.</p>
<p>In 2007 fears were expressed that China's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finless_Porpoise" title="Finless Porpoise">Finless Porpoise</a>, a native of the lake, might follow the <a href="/wiki/Baiji" title="Baiji">baiji</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> river dolphin, into extinction. There have been calls for action to save the finless porpoise, of which there are about 1400 left living, with approximately 700 to 900 in the Yangtze, and approximately another 500 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Poyang" title="Lake Poyang">Poyang</a> and Dongting Lakes. The 2007 population levels were less than half the 1997 levels, and the population continues to drop at a rate of 7.3 per cent per year. Pressure on the finless porpoise population on Poyang Lake comes from the high numbers of ships passing through, as well as sand dredging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After flooding of the Yangtze River in late June 2007, approximately 2 billion mice were displaced from the islands of the lake. The mice invaded surrounding communities, damaging crops and dikes and forcing the government to construct walls and ditches to control the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lake was also featured on news services as having a problem with <a href="/wiki/Schistosoma" title="Schistosoma">schistosoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> infected mosquitoes.</p>
<p>Billions of mice were forced from their holes and were sent scurrying into local villages when officials opened the sluice gates on Dongting Lake in June 2007 to relieve flooding. Villagers killed an estimated 2 billion mice by beating them with shovels or using poison. The rotting mouse corpses should have been properly disposed of, and other animals—such as cats and dogs—were the unintended victims of the poisons.</p>
<p>A restoration project, the Sino-Norwegian Project of Biodiversity Protection Management, began in 2005. According to a 2007 article in the <a href="/wiki/China_Daily" title="China Daily">China Daily</a>, "[The Dongting Lake area] will be restored to a sustainable biodiversity environment within five to 10 years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
