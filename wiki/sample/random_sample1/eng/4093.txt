<p>The <b>University of Duisburg-Essen</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Universität Duisburg-Essen</i></span>) is a public <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, Germany and a member of the newly founded University Alliance Metropolis Ruhr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ua-ruhr.de_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ua-ruhr.de-3">[3]</a></sup> It was founded in 1654 and re-established on 1 January 2003 as a merger of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerhard_Mercator_University" title="Gerhard Mercator University">Gerhard Mercator University</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a> and the University of <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a>. Until 1994 the name of the Gerhard Mercator University was <i>Comprehensive University of Duisburg</i>.</p>
<p>With its 12 departments and more than 39,000 students, the University of Duisburg-Essen is among the 10 largest German universities. In the Times Higher Education World University Rankings 2016-17 the University of Duisburg-Essen was ranked 197th in the world and stands at 17th place in it's 150 Under 50 Rankings. In many disciplines the UDE ranks amongst the top 10 of German research universities. Over the past three years, research income has risen by 150 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many international students study at the University of Duisburg-Essen and give the cities of Duisburg and Essen an international atmosphere.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Duisburg-Essen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Origins; The University of Duisburg (1555)</b> Its origins date back to the 1555 decision of <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Duke</a> <i>Wilhelm V von Jülich-Kleve-Berg</i> to create a university for the unified duchies at the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Rhine" title="Lower Rhine">Lower Rhine</a>. To this end, it was necessary to obtain a permission of the emperor and the pope. Although the permission of the pope was granted in 1564 and of the emperor in 1566, the university was founded about ninety years later in 1654, after the acquisition of the duchy of Kleve by Frederick William, Elector of Brandenburg. It opened on 14 October 1655 by Johannes Claudberg as their first rector. The university had four faculties: Theology, Medicine, Law and Arts. During its period of activity it was one of the central and leading universities of the western provinces of Prussia.</p>
<p>Only a few decades later the university was in competition with the much better equipped Dutch universities. Since only about one third of the population in the western provinces of Prussia were member of the reformed church, most Lutheran and Catholic citizens in the second half of the 18th century, sent their sons to other universities.</p>
<p>The university declined rapidly and was on 18 October 1818 due to a Cabinet Order of Friedrich Wilhelm III. officially closed. At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bonn" title="University of Bonn">University of Bonn</a> was founded. Large parts of the Duisburg University Library were relocated to <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a> and then formed the basis of the newly formed <i>Bonn Library</i>. Also the sceptre of the University of Duisburg was given to the University of Bonn, and is available until today.</p>
<p>In 1891 the <i>Rheinisch-Westfälische Hüttenschule</i> was settled from Bochum to Duisburg, which was later developed to the <i>Königlich-Preußischen Maschinenbau- und Hüttenschule</i>, and was renamed in 1938 to <i>Public School of Engineering</i>.</p>
<p>After a decision of the federal state government in 1960 the teacher training college of Kettwig was settled to Duisburg and was named <i>Pedagogical University Ruhr</i>. In 1968, the university was founded again in Duisburg, related to the old one, bearing the name: <i>Comprehensive University of Duisburg.</i> Initially only small, the university was developed rapidly in the 1970s up to about 15,000 students. In 1972 the <i>Pedagogical University Ruhr</i> and the <i>Public School of Engineering</i>, which was renamed in 1971 to University of applied sciences Duisburg. Other schools were also relocated to Duisburg. The University of Duisburg was then called <i>Comprehensive University of Duisburg</i>. In 1994 the university was renamed <i>Gerhard Mercator University</i>.</p>
<p>In 2003, Gerhard Mercator University merged with the University of Essen to form the University of Duisburg-Essen, which is today one of the largest universities in Germany with about 30,000 students.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments">Recent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Duisburg-Essen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013 Duisburg-Essen University is ranked number 69 in the World's top 100 universities under 50 years old: ranked by Times Higher Education.</p>
<p>In March 2007 the three universities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Bochum" title="University of Bochum">Bochum</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dortmund_University_of_Technology" title="Dortmund University of Technology">Dortmund</a> and Duisburg and Essen founded the University Alliance Metropolis Ruhr, which is modelled on the University of California system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions">Institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Duisburg-Essen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The University of Duisburg-Essen today has twelve faculties, listed below:</p>
<ul>
<li>Faculty of <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">Humanities</a></li>
<li>Faculty of <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">Social Sciences</a></li>
<li>Department of Educational Sciences</li>
<li>Faculty of <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">Art and Design</a></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercator-Professorship_Award">Mercator-Professorship Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Duisburg-Essen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mercator-Professorship Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The University of Duisburg-Essen awards the <i>Mercator-Professur</i> to individuals who are well known for their social and scientific engagement. So far, recipients of the <i>Mercator-Professur</i> have been:</p>
<ul>
<li>1997: <a href="/wiki/Hans-Dietrich_Genscher" title="Hans-Dietrich Genscher">Hans-Dietrich Genscher</a>, politician (<a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">FDP</a>), former Foreign Minister and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vice_Chancellor_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vice Chancellor of the Federal Republic of Germany (page does not exist)">Vice Chancellor of the Federal Republic of Germany</a></li>
<li>1998: <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Lenz" title="Siegfried Lenz">Siegfried Lenz</a>, writer</li>
<li>1999: <a href="/wiki/Jan_Philipp_Reemtsma" title="Jan Philipp Reemtsma">Jan Philipp Reemtsma</a>, literary scholar.</li>
<li>2000: <a href="/wiki/Jutta_Limbach" title="Jutta Limbach">Jutta Limbach</a>, jurist and politician (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPD" title="SPD">SPD</a>)</li>
<li>2001: <a href="/wiki/Volker_Schl%C3%B6ndorff" title="Volker Schlöndorff">Volker Schlöndorff</a>, filmmaker.</li>
<li>2002: <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_Wickert" title="Ulrich Wickert">Ulrich Wickert</a>, journalist, chief anchor for <a href="/wiki/Tagesthemen" title="Tagesthemen">tagesthemen</a></li>
<li>2003: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Goeudevert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daniel Goeudevert (page does not exist)">Daniel Goeudevert</a> French writer, management consultant.</li>
<li>2004: <a href="/wiki/Walter_Kempowski" title="Walter Kempowski">Walter Kempowski</a>, writer.</li>
<li>2005: <a href="/wiki/Richard_von_Weizs%C3%A4cker" title="Richard von Weizsäcker">Richard von Weizsäcker</a>, politician (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">CDU</a>), former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="President of the Federal Republic of Germany">President of the Federal Republic of Germany</a>.</li>
<li>2006: <a href="/wiki/Necla_Kelek" title="Necla Kelek">Necla Kelek</a>, social scientist</li>
<li>2007: <a href="/wiki/Hanan_Ashrawi" title="Hanan Ashrawi">Hanan Ashrawi</a>, legislator, activist, and scholar</li>
<li>2008: <a href="/wiki/Christiane_N%C3%BCsslein-Volhard" title="Christiane Nüsslein-Volhard">Christiane Nüsslein-Volhard</a>, biologist, winner of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a></li>
<li>2009: <a href="/wiki/Peter_Scholl-Latour" title="Peter Scholl-Latour">Peter Scholl-Latour</a>, journalist and publicist</li>
<li>2010: <a href="/wiki/Alice_Schwarzer" title="Alice Schwarzer">Alice Schwarzer</a>, publisher and feminist</li>
<li>2011: <a href="/wiki/Udo_Di_Fabio" title="Udo Di Fabio">Udo Di Fabio</a>, former judge of the Federal Constitutional Court</li>
<li>2012: <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Huber" title="Wolfgang Huber">Wolfgang Huber</a>, Bishop, former Chairman of the Council of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">EKD</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Professors include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jette_Joop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jette Joop (page does not exist)">Jette Joop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kai_Krause" title="Kai Krause">Kai Krause</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Ames" title="Bruce Ames">Bruce Ames</a>.</p>
