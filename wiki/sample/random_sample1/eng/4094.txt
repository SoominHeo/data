<p>A <b>dual-clutch transmission</b>, (<b>DCT</b>) (sometimes referred to as a <b>twin-clutch transmission</b> or <b>double-clutch transmission</b>), is a type of <a href="/wiki/Automatic_transmission" title="Automatic transmission">automatic transmission</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electrohydraulic_manual_transmission" title="Electrohydraulic manual transmission">automated automotive transmission</a>. It uses two separate <a href="/wiki/Clutch" title="Clutch">clutches</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup> for odd and even <a href="/wiki/Gear_train" title="Gear train">gear sets</a>. It can fundamentally be described as two separate <a href="/wiki/Manual_transmission" title="Manual transmission">manual transmissions</a> (with their respective clutches) contained within one housing, and working as one unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> They are usually operated in a fully automatic mode, and many also have the ability to allow the driver to manually shift gears in semi-automatic mode,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup> albeit still using the transmission's electro-hydraulics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This type of transmission was invented by Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_K%C3%A9gresse" title="Adolphe Kégresse">Adolphe Kégresse</a> just before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, although he never developed a working model. The first development of the twin clutch or dual-clutch transmission started in the early part of 1980 under the guidance of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Webster" title="Harry Webster">Harry Webster</a> at <a href="/wiki/Automotive_Products" title="Automotive Products">Automotive Products</a> (AP), <a href="/wiki/Leamington_Spa" title="Leamington Spa">Leamington Spa</a> with prototypes built into the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta_(first_generation)" title="Ford Fiesta (first generation)">Ford Fiesta Mk1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_Ranger" title="Ford Ranger">Ford Ranger</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_205" title="Peugeot 205">Peugeot 205</a>. Initially, the control systems were based on purely analogue/discrete digital circuitry with patents filed in July 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> All of these early AP twin clutch installations featured a single dry clutch and multi-plate wet clutch. Following discussions with VW/Porsche, DCT work continued from Porsche in-house development, for Audi and Porsche racing cars later in the 1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> when computers to control the transmission became compact enough: the Porsche Doppelkupplungsgetriebe (English: dual-clutch gearbox) (PDK)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> used in the <a href="/wiki/Porsche_956" title="Porsche 956">Porsche 956</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Porsche_962" title="Porsche 962">962</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">Le Mans</a> race cars from 1983,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audi_Quattro_S1" title="Audi Quattro S1">Audi Sport Quattro S1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lufteknic_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lufteknic-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dual-clutch transmission eliminates the <a href="/wiki/Torque_converter" title="Torque converter">torque converter</a> as used in conventional epicyclic-geared <a href="/wiki/Automatic_transmission" title="Automatic transmission">automatic transmissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup> Instead, dual-clutch transmissions that are currently on the market primarily use two <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a>-bathed wet multi-plate clutches, similar to the clutches used in most motorcycles, though dry clutch versions are also available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsWet_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsWet-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first series production <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">road car</a> with a DCT was the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf_Mk4#R32" title="Volkswagen Golf Mk4">Volkswagen Golf Mk4 R32</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the largest number of sales of DCTs in Western Europe are by various marques of the German <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsVWAG_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsVWAG-10">[10]</a></sup> though this is anticipated to change as other transmission makers and vehicle manufacturers make DCTs available in series production automobiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2010, on BMW Canada's website for the 3 Series Coupe, it is described both as a 7-speed double-clutch transmission and as a 7-speed automatic transmission. It is actually a dual-clutch semi-automatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In DCTs where the two clutches are arranged <a href="/wiki/Concentric" title="Concentric">concentrically</a>, the larger outer clutch drives the odd numbered gears,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup> while the smaller inner clutch drives the even numbered gears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup> Shifts can be accomplished without interrupting <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> distribution to the driven roadwheels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup> by applying the engine's torque to one clutch at the same time as it is being disconnected from the other clutch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> Since alternate gear ratios can pre-select<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> an odd gear on one gear shaft while the vehicle is being driven in an even gear,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> (and vice versa). DCTs are the fastest shifting road car transmission available and are even able to shift faster than a professional race car driver using a manual transmission. DCTs can even shift more quickly than cars equipped with single-clutch automated-manual transmissions (AMTs), a.k.a. single-clutch semi-automatics. Also, with a DCT, shifts can be made more smoothly than with a single-clutch AMT, making a DCT more suitable for conventional road cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (October 2009)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clutch_types">Clutch types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clutch types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dual-clutch transmissions use two fundamentally different types of clutches: either two wet multi-plate clutches, bathed in oil (for cooling)—or two dry single-plate clutches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup> The wet clutch design is generally used for higher torque engines that can generate 350 <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metres</a> (258 <a href="/wiki/Pound-foot_(torque)" title="Pound-foot (torque)">lbf·ft</a>) and more (the wet multi-plate clutch DCT in the <a href="/wiki/Bugatti_Veyron" title="Bugatti Veyron">Bugatti Veyron</a> is designed to cope with 1,250 N·m (922 lbf·ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup>), whereas the dry clutch design is generally suitable for smaller vehicles with lower torque outputs up to 250 N·m (184 lbf·ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsWet_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsWet-7">[7]</a></sup> However, while the dry clutch variants may be limited in torque compared to their wet clutch counterparts, the dry clutch versions offer an increase in fuel efficiency,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> due to the lack of pumping losses of the transmission fluid in the clutch housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clutch_installation">Clutch installation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Clutch installation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are currently three variations of clutch installation. The original design used a concentric arrangement, where both clutches shared the same plane when viewed perpendicularly from the transmission input shaft, along the same centre line as the engine <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshaft</a>; when viewed head-on along the length of the input shaft, this makes one clutch noticeably larger than the other.</p>
<p>The second implementation uses two single-plate dry clutches—side-by-side from the perpendicular view, but again sharing the center line of the crankshaft.</p>
<p>A later variation uses two separate but identical size clutches. These are arranged side-by-side when viewed head-on (along the length of the input shaft and crankshaft centre line), and also share the same plane when viewed perpendicularly. This latter clutch arrangement (unlike the other two variations) is driven via a gear from the engine crankshaft.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BorgWarner">BorgWarner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: BorgWarner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/BorgWarner" title="BorgWarner">BorgWarner Inc.</a> supplies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> wet dual clutches<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> and electro-hydraulic control modules (<a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">mechatronics</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> for these dual-clutch transmissions, along with complete dual-clutch transmission and <a href="/wiki/Transaxle" title="Transaxle">transaxle</a> assemblies. BorgWarner, who call their technology "DualTronic",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW_History_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BW_History-15">[15]</a></sup> entered series production (excludes <a href="/wiki/Bugatti_Veyron" title="Bugatti Veyron">Bugatti Veyron</a>) for <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> who renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-Shift_Gearbox" title="Direct-Shift Gearbox">Direct-Shift Gearbox</a> (DSG) in 2003 in the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf_Mk4#R32" title="Volkswagen Golf Mk4">Volkswagen Golf Mk4 R32</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW_History_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-BW_History-15">[15]</a></sup> The company signed further agreements with three other (unnamed) European automotive manufacturers to incorporate their components in DCTs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

(CDUI), which is owned by twelve Chinese automakers. This joint venture is known as the BorgWarner United Transmission Systems Co., Ltd., and is located in <a href="/wiki/Dalian" title="Dalian">Dalian</a>, China. The company has produced various dual-clutch transmission modules beginning in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nissan_GT-R" title="Nissan GT-R">Nissan GT-R</a> uses BorgWarner components, including the dual-clutch module, clutch-control, shift-actuation systems, and synchronizer assemblies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EATON">EATON</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: EATON">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiat_Powertrain_Technologies">Fiat Powertrain Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fiat Powertrain Technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fiat_Powertrain_Technologies" title="Fiat Powertrain Technologies">Fiat Powertrain Technologies</a> developed a dual-clutch transmission with <a href="/wiki/Magneti_Marelli" title="Magneti Marelli">Magneti Marelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/BorgWarner" title="BorgWarner">BorgWarner</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_Twin_Clutch_Transmission" title="Euro Twin Clutch Transmission">Euro Twin Clutch Transmission</a>. Magneti Marelli produces the control system, which integrates BorgWarner's hydraulic actuation module into its own power and transmission control units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwauto.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-bwauto.com-20">[20]</a></sup> It can handle torque inputs of up to 350 N·m (260 lb·ft), making it the highest-torque dry clutch application.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its weight of 81 kg (179 lb), including oil and transmission control unit, its three-shaft architecture, especially axially, means it can be installed in several types of <a href="/wiki/B-segment" title="B-segment">B-</a> and <a href="/wiki/C-segment" title="C-segment">C-segment</a> vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FEV_GmbH">FEV GmbH</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: FEV GmbH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=FEV_GmbH&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="FEV GmbH (page does not exist)">FEV</a> developed a DCT with an integrated Electric Motor, providing hybrid capability. The 7H AMT Transmission provides 7 forward speeds with multiple usage of gears (the electric drive can use unused gears from the combustion engine path): this aims to substantially reduce the complexity of the unit as well as the package size and weight. Another spin-off from this design was the FEV xDCT that provides 10 forward speeds but with the mechanical complexity of a standard 6 speed DCT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Getrag">Getrag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Getrag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Getrag" title="Getrag">Getrag</a> has developed a range of DCT<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> transaxles, including 7DCL750, a seven-speed transaxle for mid-engine longitudinal applications, capable of taking more than 750 N·m (553 lbf·ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Getrag will provide its DCT in its first commercial applications, for the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Journey" title="Dodge Journey">Dodge Journey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volvo_S40" title="Volvo S40">Volvo S40</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volvo_V50" title="Volvo V50">V50</a>, from mid-2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Getrag has developed the 6DCT250 dry clutch DCT for use in front wheel drive transverse applications. With use of electro-mechanical actuation, rather than electro-hydraulic, the 6DCT250 transmission surpasses the conventional manual transmission in fuel consumption and CO2 emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2nd quarter of 2008, Getrag had signed an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> to supply its PowerShift DCTs for use in American markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> However, due to the global economic downturn, this was subsequently cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoBlogChrysler_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoBlogChrysler-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoWeekChrysler_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoWeekChrysler-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006 Getrag was also working with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bosch_GmbH" title="Robert Bosch GmbH">Bosch</a> to develop a DCT for use in <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">hybrid vehicles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 Getrag introduced a new 7-speed, wet clutch DCT, the 7DCT300,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> with a maximum torque of 300 Nm. The first application was the Renault Espace with a 1.6l, Turbo GDI engine.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volkswagen_Group">Volkswagen Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Volkswagen Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Direct-shift_gearbox" title="Direct-shift gearbox">Direct-shift gearbox</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a> produces, so far, the fastest DCTs in the world (8ms) under the <a href="/wiki/Direct-shift_gearbox" title="Direct-shift gearbox">direct-shift gearbox</a> (DSG) name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsVWAG_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsVWAG-10">[10]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Direkt-Schalt-Getriebe</i></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> It is used in all of their mainstream marques, including <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SEAT" title="SEAT">SEAT</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Auto" title="Škoda Auto">Škoda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Commercial_Vehicles" title="Volkswagen Commercial Vehicles">Volkswagen Commercial Vehicles</a>, and also its top-tier marque <a href="/wiki/Bugatti" title="Bugatti">Bugatti</a>. Audi originally used the direct-shift gearbox (DSG) name, but now uses the name "S tronic" for its DCTs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first ever worldwide series production DCT was the Volkswagen Group DQ250<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsVWAG_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsVWAG-10">[10]</a></sup> six-speed dual-clutch transmission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup> with dual concentric wet multi-plate clutches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup> It was produced at the Group's <a href="/wiki/Kassel" title="Kassel">Kassel</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Volkswagen_Group_factories" title="List of Volkswagen Group factories">plant</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-19"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup> under exclusive license from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borg-Warner" title="Borg-Warner">Borg-Warner</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> for use in transverse <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">powertrain</a> installations, of either <a href="/wiki/Front-wheel_drive" title="Front-wheel drive">front-wheel drive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">four-wheel drive</a> (4WD) layouts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMS_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-VMS-8">[8]</a></sup> The 4WD versions are fundamentally identical to the 2WD versions, but the 4WD versions use an additional bolt-on <a href="/wiki/Power_take-off" title="Power take-off">power take-off</a> unit to direct engine torque to the <a href="/wiki/Haldex_Traction" title="Haldex Traction">Haldex Traction</a> rear axle. This DQ250 variant is used in a wide range of models: Volkswagen Passenger Cars (<a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Polo" title="Volkswagen Polo">Polo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf" title="Volkswagen Golf">Golf/Rabbit/Golf Plus</a>, Scirocco, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Jetta" title="Volkswagen Jetta">Jetta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Eos" title="Volkswagen Eos">Eos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Passat" title="Volkswagen Passat">Passat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Touran" title="Volkswagen Touran">Touran</a>); Audi cars (<a href="/wiki/Audi_A3" title="Audi A3">A3</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Audi_TT" title="Audi TT">TT</a>); SEAT cars (<a href="/wiki/SEAT_Ibiza" title="SEAT Ibiza">Ibiza</a>, <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Le%C3%B3n" title="SEAT León">León</a>, <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Altea" title="SEAT Altea">Altea</a> and <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Toledo" title="SEAT Toledo">Toledo</a>); Škoda cars (<a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">Octavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Superb" title="Škoda Superb">Superb</a>); and Volkswagen commercial vehicles (<a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Caddy" title="Volkswagen Caddy">Caddy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Transporter_(T5)" title="Volkswagen Transporter (T5)">T5 Transporter</a>).</p>
<p>A second variant of the direct-shift gearbox went into series production in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup>—the DQ200. This unit has seven forward ratios,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup> but the notable difference over the original DQ250 is the change from wet to dry clutches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-20"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup> This variant uses two single-plate dry clutches, arranged in a tandem design (instead of concentrically) and therefore similar in size. The DQ200 is again for use in transverse applications, but is intended for use in smaller cars, with smaller displacement engines that generate relatively low torque outputs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-21"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsVWAG_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsVWAG-10">[10]</a></sup> When used in the latest Golf with the 90 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatts</a> (122 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_horsepower" title="Metric horsepower">PS</a>; 121 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a>) engine, this new 7-speed DSG uses roughly 6% (5.9l/100 km for the 7-speed DSG compared to 6.3l/100 km with the 6-speed manual gearbox) less fuel than the same engine with a manual transmission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup> and up to 20% less than a conventional automatic transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VWAG7_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-VWAG7-9">[9]</a></sup> The original DQ250 also remains available.</p>
<p>Volkswagen Group subsidiary Audi AG have also developed an all-new DCT—the DL501, for use in longitudinal powertrains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsVWAG_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsVWAG-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsAudi7_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsAudi7-49">[49]</a></sup> Like the original transverse DQ250, this DL501 utilises dual wet multi-plate clutches, but unlike the DQ250, this variant uses seven forward ratios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsVWAG_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsVWAG-10">[10]</a></sup> This DL501 variant made its debut in the <a href="/wiki/Audi_Q5" title="Audi Q5">Q5</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsAudi7_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsAudi7-49">[49]</a></sup> and also is used in the latest versions of the <a href="/wiki/Audi_A4#B8" title="Audi A4">A4 (B8)</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsAudi7_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsAudi7-49">[49]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Audi_S4#B8" title="Audi S4">S4 (B8)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsAudi7_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsAudi7-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It is also being considered for use in an all new <a href="/wiki/Audi_A6" title="Audi A6">A6</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsAudi7_49-4"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsAudi7-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Huracan" title="Lamborghini Huracan">Lamborghini Huracan</a> uses a double clutch ‘Lamborghini Doppia Frizione' (LDF) transmission, while the Aventador uses a robotized manual. After the facelift in 2012, the Audi R8 uses a seven-speed S tronic.</p>
<p>English specialist consulting engineering company <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_plc" title="Ricardo plc">Ricardo plc</a> produces the seven-speed DCT for the 736 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatts</a> (1,001 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_horsepower" title="Metric horsepower">PS</a>; 987 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower#Brake_horsepower" title="Horsepower">bhp</a>) 2005 <a href="/wiki/Bugatti_Veyron" title="Bugatti Veyron">Bugatti Veyron EB 16.4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTfactsRicardo_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-DCTfactsRicardo-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lamborghini_.28VW_GROUP.29">Lamborghini (VW GROUP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Lamborghini (VW GROUP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a>'s first dual clutch transmission is available in the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Hurac%C3%A1n" title="Lamborghini Huracán">Lamborghini Huracán</a> LP610-4 (2014–present). The name of the transmission is 7-speed LDF Dual Clutch "Doppia Frizione" with shift characteristics variable via Drive Select Mode and All-wheel drive with electrohydraulic multi-plate clutch.</p>
<p>The car has three modes changed using the steering-wheel-mounted "ANIMA," or mode selector. The three modes are strada, sport, and corsa, each with faster shifts. Launch control raises the rpm to 4200 rpm before dropping the clutch. The Huracán upshifts automatically at redline, but not before running into the limiter for a fraction of a second. Using the paddles to call for earlier shifts knocks a tenth to two tenths off the figures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kia">Kia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Kia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kia_Motors" title="Kia Motors">Kia Motors</a> has announced application of the all-new seven-speed dual-clutch transmission (DCT) to a production model for the first time, the new Kia cee’d GT Line - which made its global debut at the 85th Salon International de l’Automobile in Geneva on 3 March 2015..</p>
<p>Compared to the six-speed DCT currently used in the Europe market <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cee%27d" title="Cee'd">Cee'd</a> models, the new transmission is expected to deliver a fuel-economy improvement of seven percent and a five-percent improvement in 0–62 mph acceleration times. Like most new DCTs, the new transmission features dual dry clutches and two input shafts, allowing for seamless torque delivery between shifts as well as gear-jumping to the optimal ratio, should the driver ask for it. If that was to apply to the current <a href="/wiki/Kia_Forte" title="Kia Forte">Forte</a> K3 Koup or Pro<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cee%27d_GT&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cee'd GT (page does not exist)">Cee'd GT</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=GT_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="GT Line (page does not exist)">GT Line</a>, with its 204-hp/265 nm 1.6L Inline-4 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gamma_II&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gamma II (page does not exist)">Gamma II</a> T-GDI Turbo Engine and also 133-ps/300 nm 1.6L <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CRDi" title="CRDi">CRDi</a> Diesel engine., for example, that would raise its fuel economy from 24 mpg city/36 highway to about 26/39 while bringing its 7.4-second 0–60 run to about seven seconds flat. Kia claims it is 7 percent more efficient than the six-speed, while improving acceleration times by 5 percent.</p>
<p>NVH (<a href="/wiki/Noise,_vibration,_and_harshness" title="Noise, vibration, and harshness">Noise, vibration, and harshness</a>) has also been a key focus for the team behind the DCT’s development, with an external damper ensuring a high level of refinement for the new transmission of Kia.</p>
<p>The new transmission is the first of its type from the brand, and has been developed in-house by Kia’s research and development teams at its Namyang, Korea R&amp;D centre. More fuel efficient than Kia’s existing six-speed automatic transmission, the new DCT has been engineered with low fuel consumption and a sporty driving feel when in manual mode, and with comfort and smoothness when left in automatic mode. Throughout its development, Kia’s R&amp;D teams targeted a three to five per cent improvement in acceleration (0 to 100 kph) and five to seven per cent improvement to fuel efficiency, depending on model.</p>
<p>Innovative hollow double-gear input shaft allows quick shifts. The DCT is made up of two dry clutches, each fitted with electric motor-driven clutch actuator to improve responsiveness, and an innovative hollow double-gear input shaft. The hollow shaft itself allows the system to quickly engage even gears, while a solid shaft that runs through the middle operates odd gears. This crossed gear shifting enables continuous power delivery and more efficient packaging, two development cornerstones for the engineering teams behind the project.</p>
<p>The hollow shaft mechanism lets the DCT operate sequentially, the car anticipating the next gear that the driver is likely to need next under acceleration or braking, or jump immediately to any of its seven forward gears (and reverse). This is particularly useful under kickdown or heavy braking, allowing the driver to remain in control of the vehicle at all times.</p>
<p>The continuous power delivery of the DCT minimises the loss of torque and forward motion by the powertrain during gear shifts, resulting in more decisive acceleration and a smoother drive. This contributes towards the transmission’s boost to performance in all road conditions in its first application in the cee’d GT Line.</p>
<p>Combined with an increase in power from 128 to 133 ps, the DCT’s seven gears allow the GT Line’s diesel engine to operate at its most efficient speeds at all times. With the engine’s torque increased from 265 to 285 Nm, the new DCT is engineered to effectively manage outputs of up to 300 Nm, achieving a better balance between the engine’s improved, more effortless performance and potential for greater fuel economy.</p>
<p>Reduction of noise, vibration and harshness (NVH) has also been a focus for the team behind the DCT’s development, with an external damper ensuring a higher level of refinement for the new transmission. After the cee’d GT Line, which will go on sale across Europe in Q4 2015, the new seven-speed DCT will be made available on a range of other Kia models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BMW">BMW</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: BMW">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2008, <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> introduced a dual-clutch transmission for their <a href="/wiki/BMW_M3" title="BMW M3">M3</a> model, manufactured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GETRAG" title="GETRAG">GETRAG</a>, embedding a DualTronic dual-clutch module from <a href="/wiki/BorgWarner" title="BorgWarner">BorgWarner</a>, and dubbed "M double-clutch transmission" (M DCT ). The same transmission is used in the second generation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_E89" title="BMW E89">Z4</a> (the Sdrive35i and sDrive35is versions), and is an available option on the 335i (coupe, convertible), 335is and later 135i builds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> released the New <a href="/wiki/BMW_M3" title="BMW M3">M3</a> (<a href="/wiki/BMW_M3#F80_M3" title="BMW M3">F80</a>) and <a href="/wiki/BMW_M4" title="BMW M4">M4</a> (<a href="/wiki/BMW_M4#F82_M4" title="BMW M4">F82</a>) with the 7-speed (DCT) Dual Clutch Transmission in 2014.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiat_Chrysler_Automobiles">Fiat Chrysler Automobiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Fiat Chrysler Automobiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_Twin_Clutch_Transmission" title="Euro Twin Clutch Transmission">Euro Twin Clutch Transmission</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_Group_LLC" title="Chrysler Group LLC">Chrysler</a> received the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Powertrain_Technologies" title="Fiat Powertrain Technologies">Fiat Powertrain Technologies</a> C635 dual dry-clutch transmission for use on Chrysler's turbo inline-4 in the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Dart_(PF)" title="Dodge Dart (PF)">Dodge Dart</a> in 2013.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fiat_Powertrain_Technologies" title="Fiat Powertrain Technologies">Fiat Powertrain Technologies</a> (FPT) started production of "TCT"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.italiaspeed.com.2F2010_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.italiaspeed.com.2F2010-62">[62]</a></sup> dual dry clutch transmission (DDCT) in early 2010 at the <a href="/wiki/Verrone" title="Verrone">Verrone</a> plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fptpowertrain.com_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-fptpowertrain.com-63">[63]</a></sup> The C635 DDCT gearbox is used for B (supermini), C (compact car), and D segment (large family car) cars, and can handle torque up to 350 N·m (258 lbf·ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fptpowertrain.com_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-fptpowertrain.com-63">[63]</a></sup> Fiat's C635 DDCT gearbox was introduced in <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo_MiTo" title="Alfa Romeo MiTo">Alfa Romeo MiTo</a> in 2011.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ford_Motor_Company">Ford Motor Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Ford Motor Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ford_PowerShift_transmission" title="Ford PowerShift transmission">Ford PowerShift transmission</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> released a wet clutch "PowerShift"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-22"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> transmission on the 2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(international)" title="Ford Focus (international)">Ford Focus (international)</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4car_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-4car-64">[64]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_C-MAX" title="Ford C-MAX">Ford C-MAX</a>. This wet clutch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> DCT was designed with gearbox specialist <a href="/wiki/Getrag" title="Getrag">Getrag</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> under the GETRAG FORD Transmissions <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a>, founded in 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and is expected to feature in other Ford and Volvo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4car_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-4car-64">[64]</a></sup> models.</p>
<p>Ford announced the US-market version of the Mark VI <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta" title="Ford Fiesta">Ford Fiesta</a> for 2011 featured a dry clutch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eureka_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-5">[5]</a></sup> PowerShift transmission. Ford also announced the introduction of PowerShift transmission to North American market by 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEI_2-23"><a href="#cite_note-AEI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoBlogFord_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoBlogFord-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Volvo version will be built in Koping, Sweden at Volvo's transmission plant. The PowerShift gearbox will be introduced on the second generation Volvo S60,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoBlogFord_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-AutoBlogFord-67">[67]</a></sup> and then on to the V50<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4car_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-4car-64">[64]</a></sup> and C30<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4car_64-3"><a href="#cite_note-4car-64">[64]</a></sup> models. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XC60" title="XC60">XC60</a> is expected to get this Ford PowerShift gearbox along with other new models to make more sporty cars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Motors">General Motors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: General Motors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the company's restructuring plan, it revealed that dry dual-clutch transmission would have been available in the 2012 calendar year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> However, this plan was cancelled. The GM's dual-clutch gearbox was introduced on the <a href="/wiki/GMC_Granite" title="GMC Granite">GMC Granite</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Concept_car" title="Concept car">concept car</a>. Had it been released, the new <a href="/wiki/Front-wheel_drive" title="Front-wheel drive">front-wheel drive</a> transmission would have incorporated the latest innovations for improving fuel economy and performance. The transmission alone would have provide upward of 10 percent improvement in fuel economy over today’s conventional six-speed automatic transmissions. The co-developed transmission between <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">GM</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shanghai_Automotive_Industry_Corporation" title="Shanghai Automotive Industry Corporation">SAIC</a> features dry, dual-clutch technology. It would have provided shift comfort equal to a conventional fully automatic transmission, with superior quality, while reducing CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honda">Honda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Honda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Honda implemented the 8 speed DCT in the Acura ILX and TLX. It is also the first and only company to pair their DCT with a torque converter to smooth low speed crawling (a problem found in many traditional DCTs). The Acura NSX super car also uses a 9 speed DCT, which consists of launch gear and a cruising gear while 7 gears on the middle are close geared closer to each other.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorcycles">Motorcycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Motorcycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In October 2009, <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/Honda_VFR1200F" title="Honda VFR1200F">VFR1200F</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycle</a> with a 1,237 cc (75.5 cu in) <a href="/wiki/V4_engine" title="V4 engine">V4 engine</a> and optional DCT dubbed the <i>Next Generation Transmission</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> In May 2009, Honda CEO <a href="/wiki/Takeo_Fukui" title="Takeo Fukui">Takeo Fukui</a> mentioned that Honda was working on a new dual-clutch transmission system that could be matched with future hybrids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>2012 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_NC700" title="Honda NC700">NC700 series</a> have dual-clutch models available: NC700SD, NC700XD and <a href="/wiki/Honda_NC700D_Integra" title="Honda NC700D Integra">NC700D Integra</a>. It is the second generation from the six-speed dual-clutch transmission first used on the Honda VFR1200F.</p>
<p>2013 Honda introduced the CTX700DCT, a 670 cc dual clutch motorcycle selling as a 2014 model. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ridermagazine.com/road-tests/2014-honda-ctx700-road-test.htm/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<p>The 2016 CRF1000L Africa Twin is available in both 3rd generation DCT and standard transmissions. The NC line has moved to the 3rd generation as well.</p>
<p>In 2016, Honda announced that they are studying a DCT for use in sport bikes as well.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitsubishi">Mitsubishi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Mitsubishi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitsubishi_Fuso_Truck_and_Bus_Corporation">Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Fuso_Truck_and_Bus_Corporation" title="Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation">Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation</a> presented a world first with a double-clutch transmission for commercial vehicles. The new six-speed M038S6 "Duonic transmission" features wet clutches and incorporates the ability to creep in traffic for smoother operation. Although Duonic-equipped trucks will probably be driven mostly in fully automatic mode, the transmission can also be manually shifted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nissan">Nissan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Nissan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a> <a href="/wiki/Nissan_GT-R" title="Nissan GT-R">GT-R's</a> rear <a href="/wiki/Transaxle" title="Transaxle">transaxle</a> six-speed dual-clutch transmission also contains the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_(mechanics)" title="Differential (mechanics)">differential</a> for the car's <a href="/wiki/All-wheel_drive" title="All-wheel drive">all-wheel drive</a> (AWD) system. Much of the dual-clutch system was engineered by BorgWarner,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoChannel_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-AutoChannel-11">[11]</a></sup> partnered with Nissan's gearbox supplier Aichi Machine Industry, and weighs a total installed mass of 117 kg (258 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Code named the GR6, the transmission is quite durable in that it is able to hold in excess of 600 lb·ft (810 N·m) of torque. Nissan reports shift times of 150 milliseconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PSA_Peugeot_Citro.C3.ABn">PSA Peugeot Citroën</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: PSA Peugeot Citroën">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PSA has dual-clutch transmission, produced by Getrag, in its <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_4007" title="Peugeot 4007">Peugeot 4007</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C-Crosser" title="Citroën C-Crosser">Citroën C-Crosser</a> SUVs, both based on the <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Outlander" title="Mitsubishi Outlander">Mitsubishi Outlander</a>. However, dual-clutch transmissions are never offered in other models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Porsche_.28VW_GROUP.29">Porsche (VW GROUP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Porsche (VW GROUP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche AG</a> offers a series production<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> of two new longitudinally installed, <a href="/wiki/ZF_Friedrichshafen" title="ZF Friedrichshafen">ZF Friedrichshafen AG</a> designed and built with Porsche<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZF_911_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZF_911-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTstoryPorsche_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-DCTstoryPorsche-47">[47]</a></sup> "7DT" wet-clutch versions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> of its previously <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">race</a>-only<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lufteknic_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lufteknic-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTstoryPorsche_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-DCTstoryPorsche-47">[47]</a></sup> Porsche Doppelkupplungsgetriebe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTstoryPorsche_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-DCTstoryPorsche-47">[47]</a></sup> (PDK) transmission.</p>
<p>The first variant, the 7DT-45,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-4"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> is used on its 2009 997 Carrera and Carrera S models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZF_911_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-ZF_911-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePress_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePress-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary911_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary911-83">[83]</a></sup> This version is also offered on the 2009 Cayman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-5"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> Boxster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-6"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> It uses a <a href="/wiki/ZF_Sachs" title="ZF Sachs">ZF Sachs</a> ND2015 clutch pack,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-7"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> and has a torque handling capacity of 500 N·m (369 lbf·ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-8"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> A higher torque version of the same transmission, the 7DT-70,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-9"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> is also available for the 2010 911 Turbo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-10"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> This is rated at 780 N·m (575 lbf·ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-11"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> and uses a different ZF Sachs clutch—the ND2216.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-12"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second PDK variant, the 7DT-75,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-13"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> is available on the 2009 Panamera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-14"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCTstoryPorsche_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-DCTstoryPorsche-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_GlossaryPanamera_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_GlossaryPanamera-85">[85]</a></sup> This is constructed fundamentally differently from the 7DT-45/7DT-70 versions; in that the internal shafts are mounted above the input shaft, so as to achieve a lower centre of gravity for the Panamera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-15"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> It also uses just one oil circuit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-16"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> whereas the 7DT-45/7DT-70 use two separate circuits;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-17"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> with very different specifications of fluids needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-18"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both variants use seven forward speeds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZF_PDK_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZF_PDK-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePress_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePress-82">[82]</a></sup> and Porsche claim the Doppelkupplung PDK transmission will replace the outgoing conventional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiptronic" title="Tiptronic">Tiptronic</a> automatic transmissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZF_911_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-ZF_911-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePress_82-2"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePress-82">[82]</a></sup> However, other reliable industry sources state that Porsche still intends to use conventional automatics; with the eight-speed <a href="/wiki/ZF_8HP_transmission" title="ZF 8HP transmission">ZF 8HP</a> being cited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-19"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> Like all DCTs, the Porsche PDK transmission is fundamentally two separate manual transmissions in one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> With the 1st, 3rd, 5th, and 7th gears and reverse available on one shaft,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> and 2nd, 4th, and 6th gears available on the other shaft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PorschePDK_Glossary_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-PorschePDK_Glossary-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Porsche claims noteworthy improvements in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions of around 15% when comparing DCT installations against its former automatic transmission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-20"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> of which half can be directly attributed to the DCT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-21"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup> When compared directly with a manual transmission, 16% improvement can be directly attributed to the DCT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustAuto_ZF_43-22"><a href="#cite_note-JustAuto_ZF-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qoros">Qoros</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Qoros">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Getrag" title="Getrag">Getrag</a> received the first order for its dual clutch transmission 6DCT250 from <a href="/wiki/Qoros" title="Qoros">Qoros Automotive Co.</a>, Ltd, an independent JV carmaker in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Attributes of the transmission include easy shifting, reduced fuel consumption compared to conventional automatic transmissions, and flexibility and functionality in hybrid applications. Start of production is planned for 2013 in the Getrag plant <a href="/wiki/Nanchang" title="Nanchang">Nanchang</a>. In addition to the six speed manual transmissions Getrag will supply to Qoros, the dual clutch transmission 6DCT250 will be applied to the first car under Qoros brand, which will meet all European standards and be exported to western European markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renault">Renault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Renault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early part of 2010 <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> introduced a new six-speed dual dry clutch DCT. This is available on the <a href="/wiki/Renault_M%C3%A9gane" title="Renault Mégane">Mégane</a> with the dCi 110 DPF engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renault_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Renault-88">[88]</a></sup> The DCT system was built by Getrag. These DCT-equipped Meganes are the marque's first automatic cars to qualify for the Renault eco² signature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renault_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Renault-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Since 2012 facelift, <a href="/wiki/Renault_M%C3%A9gane" title="Renault Mégane">Renault Mégane</a> is the first French automobile with dual-clutch transmission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Railcar_use">Railcar use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dual-clutch_transmission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Railcar use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A different type of dual-clutch transmission has been used in some <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">railcars</a>. The two clutches are placed one on the gearbox input shaft and the other on the gearbox output shaft. To make a gear change, both clutches disengage simultaneously and a brake inside the gearbox engages. The gearchange occurs with all gears stationary, so no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchromesh" title="Synchromesh">synchronizing mechanism</a> is needed. After the gear change, both clutches re-engage. There is a significant break in power transmission, so this system is unsuitable for <a href="/wiki/Switcher" title="Switcher">shunting locomotives</a>.</p>
