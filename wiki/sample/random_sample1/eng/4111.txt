<p><i><b>Dracula</b></i> is an 1897 Gothic horror novel by Irish author <a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker" title="Bram Stoker">Bram Stoker</a>, famous for introducing the character of the <a href="/wiki/Vampire" title="Vampire">vampire</a> <a href="/wiki/Count_Dracula" title="Count Dracula">Count Dracula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The novel tells the story of Dracula's attempt to move from <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> to England so that he may find new blood and spread the undead curse, and of the battle between Dracula and a small group of men and women led by Professor <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Van_Helsing" title="Abraham Van Helsing">Abraham Van Helsing</a>.</p>
<p><i>Dracula</i> has been assigned to many <a href="/wiki/Literary_genre" title="Literary genre">literary genres</a> including <a href="/wiki/Vampire_literature" title="Vampire literature">vampire literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror fiction</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">gothic novel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Invasion_literature" title="Invasion literature">invasion literature</a>. Stoker did not invent the vampire but he defined its modern form, and the novel has spawned numerous theatrical, film, and television interpretations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2360" data-file-width="1484" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg/220px-Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg/330px-Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg/440px-Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stoker_Dracula_Notes_Personal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stoker's handwritten notes on the characters in the novel</div>
</div>

<p>The story is told in <a href="/wiki/Epistolary_novel" title="Epistolary novel">epistolary</a> format, as a series of letters, <a href="/wiki/Diary" title="Diary">diary</a> entries, newspaper articles, and ships' log entries, whose narrators are the novel's protagonists, and occasionally supplemented with newspaper clippings relating events not directly witnessed. The events portrayed in the novel take place chronologically and largely in England and <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> during the 1890s and all transpire within the same year between the 3rd of May and the 6th of November. A short note is located at the end of the final chapter written 7 years after the events outlined in the novel.</p>
<p>The tale begins with <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Harker" title="Jonathan Harker">Jonathan Harker</a>, a newly qualified English <a href="/wiki/Solicitor" title="Solicitor">solicitor</a>, visiting <a href="/wiki/Count_Dracula" title="Count Dracula">Count Dracula</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a> on the border of <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bukovina" title="Bukovina">Bukovina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldavia</a>, to provide legal support for a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> transaction overseen by Harker's employer. At first enticed by Dracula's gracious manners, Harker soon realizes that he is Dracula's prisoner. Wandering the Count's castle against Dracula's admonition, Harker encounters three female vampires, called "<a href="/wiki/Brides_of_Dracula" title="Brides of Dracula">the sisters</a>", from whom he is rescued by Dracula. After the preparations are made, Dracula leaves Transylvania and abandons Harker to the sisters. Harker barely escapes from the castle with his life.</p>
<p>Not long afterward, a Russian ship, the <i>Demeter</i>, having weighed anchor at <a href="/wiki/Varna" title="Varna">Varna</a>, runs aground on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Whitby" title="Whitby">Whitby</a> in the east coast of England. The captain's <a href="/wiki/Logbook" title="Logbook">log</a> narrates the gradual disappearance of the entire crew, until the captain alone remained, himself bound to the helm to maintain course. An animal resembling "a large dog" is seen leaping ashore. The ship's cargo is described as silver sand and 50 boxes of "mould", or earth, from Transylvania. It is later learned that Dracula successfully purchased multiple estates under the alias 'Count De Ville' throughout London and devised to distribute the 50 boxes to each of them utilizing transportation services as well as moving them himself. He does this to secure for himself "lairs" and the 50 boxes of earth would be used as his graves which would grant safety and rest during times of feeding and replenishing his strength.</p>
<p>Soon Dracula is indirectly shown to be stalking <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Westenra" title="Lucy Westenra">Lucy Westenra</a>, who is holidaying in Whitby. As time passes she begins to suffer from episodes of sleepwalking and dementia, as witnessed by her friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mina_Murray" title="Mina Murray">Mina Murray</a>, the fiancée of Jonathan Harker. Lucy receives three marriage proposals from Dr. <a href="/wiki/John_Seward" title="John Seward">John Seward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quincey_Morris" title="Quincey Morris">Quincey Morris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Holmwood" title="Arthur Holmwood">Arthur Holmwood</a> (the son of Lord Godalming who later obtains the title himself<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>). Lucy accepts Holmwood's proposal while turning down Seward and Morris, but all remain friends. Dracula communicates with Seward's patient <a href="/wiki/Renfield" title="Renfield">Renfield</a>, an insane man who wishes to consume insects, spiders, birds, and rats to absorb their "life force". Renfield is able to detect Dracula's presence and supplies clues accordingly.</p>
<p>When Lucy begins to waste away suspiciously, Seward invites his old teacher, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Van_Helsing" title="Abraham Van Helsing">Abraham Van Helsing</a>, who immediately determines the true cause of Lucy's condition. He refuses to disclose it but diagnoses her with acute blood-loss. Helsing prescribes numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_transfusions" title="Blood transfusions">blood transfusions</a> to which Dr. Seward, Helsing, Quincey and Arthur all contribute over time. Helsing also prescribes flowers to be placed throughout her room and weaves a necklace of withered <a href="/wiki/Allium" title="Allium">Garlic Blossoms</a> for her to wear as well. She however continues to waste away - appearing to lose blood every night. While both doctors are absent, Lucy and her mother are attacked by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a>; Mrs. Westenra, who has a heart condition, dies of fright. Van Helsing attempts to protect her with <a href="/wiki/Garlic#Spiritual_and_religious_uses" title="Garlic">garlic</a> but fate thwarts him each night, whether Lucy's mother removes the garlic from her room, or Lucy herself does so in her restless sleep. The doctors have found two small puncture marks about her neck, which Dr. Seward is at a loss to understand. Helsing then places a crucifix around her neck, but soon after she is discovered dead with the crucifix missing. Helsing discovers that one of the nurses stole it the night before.</p>
<p>Following Lucy's death, the newspapers report children being stalked in the night by a "bloofer lady" (i.e., "beautiful lady").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Van Helsing, knowing Lucy has become a vampire, confides in Seward, Lord Godalming, and Morris. The suitors and Van Helsing track her down and, after a confrontation with her, stake her heart, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behead" title="Behead">behead</a> her, and fill her mouth with garlic. Around the same time, Jonathan Harker arrives from <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, where Mina marries him after his escape, and he and Mina join the campaign against Dracula.</p>
<p>The vampire hunters stay at Dr. Seward's residence, holding nightly meetings and providing reports based on each of their various tasks. Mina discovers that each of their journals and letters collectively contain clues to which they can track him down. She tasks herself with collecting them, researching newspaper clippings, fitting the most relevant entries into chronological order and typing out copies to distribute to each of the party which they are to study. Jonathan Harker tracks down the shipments of boxed graves and the estates which Dracula has purchased in order to store them. Van Helsing conducts research along with Dr. Seward to analyze the behaviour of their patient Renfield who they learn is directly influenced by Dracula. They also research historical events, folklore, and superstitions from various cultures to understand Dracula's powers and weaknesses. Van Helsing also establishes a criminal profile on Dracula in order to better understand his actions and predict his movements. Arthur Holmwood's fortune assists in funding the entire operation and expenses. As they learn the various properties Dracula had purchased, the male protagonists team up to raid each property and are several times confronted by Dracula. As they discover each of the boxed graves scattered throughout London, they pry them open to place and seal wafers of <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_bread" title="Sacramental bread">sacramental bread</a> within. This act renders the boxes of earth completely useless to Dracula as he is unable to open, enter or further transport them.</p>
<p>After Dracula learns of the group's plot against him, he attacks Mina on three occasions, and feeds Mina his own blood to control her. This curses Mina with vampirism and changes her but does not completely turn her into a vampire. Van Helsing attempts to bless Mina through prayer and by placing a wafer of sacrament against her forehead, although it burns her upon contact leaving a wretched scar. Under this curse, Mina oscillates from consciousness to a semi-trance during which she perceives Dracula's surroundings and actions. Van Helsing is able to use hypnotism at the hour of dawn and put her into this trance to further track his movements. Mina, afraid of Dracula's link with her, urges the team not to tell her their plans out of fear that Dracula will be listening. After the protagonists discover and sterilize 49 boxes found throughout his lairs in London, they learn that Dracula has fled with the missing 50th box back to his castle in Transylvania. They pursue him under the guidance of Mina. They split up into teams once they reach Europe; Van Helsing and Mina team up to locate the castle of Dracula while the others attempt to ambush the boat Dracula is using to reach his home. Van Helsing raids the castle and destroys the vampire "sisters". Upon discovering Dracula being transported by <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Gypsies</a>, Harker shears Dracula through the throat with a <a href="/wiki/Kukri" title="Kukri">kukri</a> while the mortally wounded Quincey stabs the Count in the heart with a <a href="/wiki/Bowie_knife" title="Bowie knife">Bowie knife</a>. Dracula crumbles to dust, and Mina is freed from her curse of vampirism.</p>
<p>The book closes with a note left by Jonathan Harker seven years after the events of the novel, detailing his married life with Mina and the birth of their son, whom they name after all four members of the party, but address as "Quincey". Quincey is depicted sitting on the knee of Van Helsing as they recount their adventure.</p>

</div>
<p>The short story "Dracula's Guest" was posthumously published in 1914, two years after Stoker's death. It was, according to most contemporary critics, the deleted first (or second) chapter from the original manuscript<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the one which gave the volume its name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belford2002_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belford2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:325</sup> but which the original publishers deemed unnecessary to the overall story.</p>
<p>"Dracula's Guest" follows an unnamed Englishman traveller as he wanders around <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> before leaving for Transylvania. It is <a href="/wiki/Walpurgis_Night" title="Walpurgis Night">Walpurgis Night</a> and the young Englishman foolishly leaves his hotel, in spite of the coachman's warnings, and wanders through a dense forest alone. Along the way, he feels that he is being watched by a tall and thin stranger (possibly Count Dracula).</p>
<p>The short story climaxes in an old graveyard, where the Englishman encounters a sleeping female vampire called <i>Countess Dolingen</i> in a marble tomb with a large iron stake driven into it. This malevolent and beautiful vampire awakens from her marble <a href="/wiki/Bier" title="Bier">bier</a> to conjure a snowstorm before being struck by lightning and returning to her eternal prison. However, the Englishman's troubles are not quite over, as he is dragged away by an unseen force and rendered unconscious. He awakens to find a "gigantic" wolf lying on his chest and licking at his throat; however, the wolf merely keeps him warm and protects him until help arrives.</p>
<p>When the Englishman is finally taken back to his hotel, a telegram awaits him from his expectant host Dracula, with a warning about "dangers from snow and wolves and night".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deleted_ending">Deleted ending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deleted ending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A small section was removed from a draft of the final chapter, in which Dracula's castle falls apart as he dies, hiding the fact that vampires were ever there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>As we looked there came a terrible convulsion of the earth so that we seemed to rock to and fro and fell to our knees. At the same moment with a roar which seemed to shake the very heavens the whole castle and the rock and even the hill on which it stood seemed to rise into the air and scatter in fragments while a mighty cloud of black and yellow smoke volume on volume in rolling grandeur was shot upwards with inconceivable rapidity.</p>
<p>Then there was a stillness in nature as the echoes of that thunderous report seemed to come as with the hollow boom of a thunder-clap - the long reverberating roll which seems as though the floors of heaven shook. Then down in a mighty ruin falling whence they rose came the fragments that had been tossed skywards in the cataclysm.</p>
From where we stood it seemed as though the one fierce volcano burst had satisfied the need of nature and that the castle and the structure of the hill had sunk again into the void. We were so appalled with the suddenness and the grandeur that we forgot to think of ourselves.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Harker" title="Jonathan Harker">Jonathan Harker</a>: A solicitor sent to do business with Count Dracula; Mina's fiancé and prisoner in Dracula's castle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Count_Dracula" title="Count Dracula">Count Dracula</a>: A Transylvanian noble who has purchased a house in London.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mina_Harker" title="Mina Harker">Wilhelmina "Mina" Harker</a> (née Murray): A schoolteacher and Jonathan Harker's fiancée (later his wife).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucy_Westenra" title="Lucy Westenra">Lucy Westenra</a>: A 19-year-old aristocrat; Mina's best friend; Arthur's fiancée and Dracula's first victim.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Holmwood" title="Arthur Holmwood">Arthur Holmwood</a>: Lucy's suitor and later fiancé. He inherits the title of Lord Godalming upon his father's death.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Seward" title="John Seward">John Seward</a>: A doctor; one of Lucy's suitors and a former student of Van Helsing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abraham_Van_Helsing" title="Abraham Van Helsing">Abraham Van Helsing</a>: A Dutch doctor, lawyer and professor; John Seward's teacher.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quincey_Morris" title="Quincey Morris">Quincey Morris</a>: An American cowboy and explorer; and one of Lucy's suitors.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Renfield" title="Renfield">Renfield</a>: A patient at Seward's insane asylum who has come under the influence of Dracula.</li>
<li>"Weird Sisters": Three siren-like vampire women who serve Dracula. In some of the plays, films etc. that came after the novel they are referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Brides_of_Dracula" title="Brides of Dracula">Brides of Dracula</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1879 and 1898, Stoker was a business manager for the world-famous <a href="/wiki/Lyceum_Theatre,_London" title="Lyceum Theatre, London">Lyceum Theatre</a> in London, where he supplemented his income by writing a large number of sensational novels, his most famous being the vampire tale <i>Dracula</i> published on 26 May 1897.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belford2002_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Belford2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:269</sup> Parts of it are set around the town of <a href="/wiki/Whitby" title="Whitby">Whitby</a>, where he spent summer holidays.</p>
<p>Throughout the 1880s and 1890s, authors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Rider_Haggard" title="Henry Rider Haggard">H. Rider Haggard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a> wrote many tales in which fantastic creatures threatened the British Empire. <a href="/wiki/Invasion_literature" title="Invasion literature">Invasion literature</a> was at a peak, and Stoker's formula was very familiar by 1897 to readers of fantastic adventure stories, of an invasion of England by continental European influences. Victorian readers enjoyed <i>Dracula</i> as a good adventure story like many others, but it did not reach its iconic legendary status until later in the 20th century when film versions began to appear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="435" data-file-width="342" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg/175px-Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg/263px-Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/11/Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Henry_Irving_portrait.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Shakespearean actor and friend of Stoker's <a href="/wiki/Henry_Irving" title="Henry Irving">Sir Henry Irving</a> was a possible real-life inspiration for the character of Dracula. The role was tailor-made to his dramatic presence, gentlemanly mannerisms, and affinity for playing villain roles. Irving, however, never agreed to play the part on stage.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before writing <i>Dracula</i>, Stoker spent seven years researching European folklore and stories of vampires, being most influenced by <a href="/wiki/Emily_Gerard" title="Emily Gerard">Emily Gerard</a>'s 1885 essay "Transylvania Superstitions". Later he also claimed that he had a nightmare, caused by eating too much crab meat covered with mayonnaise sauce, about a "vampire king" rising from his grave.</p>
<p>Despite being the most widely known vampire novel, <i>Dracula</i> was not the first. It was preceded and partly inspired by <a href="/wiki/Sheridan_Le_Fanu" title="Sheridan Le Fanu">Sheridan Le Fanu</a>'s 1871 <i><a href="/wiki/Carmilla" title="Carmilla">Carmilla</a></i>, about a <a href="/wiki/Lesbian_vampire" title="Lesbian vampire">lesbian vampire</a> who preys on a lonely young woman, and by <i><a href="/wiki/Varney_the_Vampire" title="Varney the Vampire">Varney the Vampire</a></i>, a lengthy <a href="/wiki/Penny_dreadful" title="Penny dreadful">penny dreadful</a> serial from the mid-Victorian period by <a href="/wiki/James_Malcolm_Rymer" title="James Malcolm Rymer">James Malcolm Rymer</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Polidori" title="John Polidori">John Polidori</a> created the image of a vampire portrayed as an aristocratic man, like the character of Dracula, in his tale "<a href="/wiki/The_Vampyre" title="The Vampyre">The Vampyre</a>" (1819). (He wrote <i>Vampyre</i> during a summer which he spent with <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a></i> creator <a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">Mary Shelley</a>, her husband poet <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">Percy Bysshe Shelley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a> in 1816.)</p>
<p>The Lyceum Theatre where Stoker worked between 1878 and 1898 was headed by actor-manager <a href="/wiki/Henry_Irving" title="Henry Irving">Henry Irving</a>, who was Stoker's real-life inspiration for Dracula's mannerisms and who Stoker hoped would play Dracula in a stage version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warren_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-warren-9">[9]</a></sup> Irving never did agree to do a stage version, but Dracula's dramatic sweeping gestures and gentlemanly mannerisms drew their living embodiment from Irving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warren_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-warren-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Dead Un-Dead</i> was one of Stoker's original titles for <i>Dracula</i>, and the manuscript was entitled simply <i>The Un-Dead</i> up until a few weeks before publication. Stoker's notes for <i>Dracula</i> show that the name of the count was originally "Count Wampyr", but Stoker became intrigued by the name "Dracula" while doing research, after reading <a href="/wiki/William_Wilkinson_(diplomat)" title="William Wilkinson (diplomat)">William Wilkinson</a>'s book <i>An Account of the Principalities of Wallachia and Moldavia with Political Observations Relative to Them</i> (London 1820),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which he found in the Whitby Library and consulted a number of times during visits to Whitby in the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The name Dracula was the <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronym</a> (<i>Drăculea</i>) of the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Vlad_II_Dracul" title="Vlad II Dracul">Vlad II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wallachia" title="Wallachia">Wallachia</a>, who took the name "Dracul" after being invested in the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Dragon" title="Order of the Dragon">Order of the Dragon</a> in 1431. In the Romanian language, the word <i>dracul</i> (Romanian <i>drac</i> "dragon" + <i>-ul</i> "the") can mean either "the dragon" or, especially in the present day, "the devil".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Dracula</i> was copyrighted in the United States in 1899 with the publication by Doubleday &amp; McClure of New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> But when Universal Studios purchased the rights, it came to light that Bram Stoker had not complied with a portion of US copyright law, placing the novel into the public domain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StokerHolt2009_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-StokerHolt2009-14">[14]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom and other countries following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Literary_and_Artistic_Works" title="Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works">Berne Convention</a> on copyrights, the novel was under copyright until April 1962, fifty years after Stoker's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/F._W._Murnau" title="F. W. Murnau">F. W. Murnau</a>'s unauthorized film adaptation <i><a href="/wiki/Nosferatu" title="Nosferatu">Nosferatu</a></i> was released in 1922, and the popularity of the novel increased considerably, owing to the controversy caused when Stoker's widow tried to have the film removed from public circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Florence Stoker sued the film company and won; however, the company was bankrupt, and Stoker only recovered her legal fees and an order by the court for all copies of the film to be destroyed. Some copies survived and found their way into theatres. Eventually, Florence Stoker simply gave up the fight against public displays of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StokerHolt2009_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-StokerHolt2009-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_and_scholarly_criticism">Reaction and scholarly criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reaction and scholarly criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dracamer99.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="629" data-file-width="500" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Dracamer99.jpg/220px-Dracamer99.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Dracamer99.jpg/330px-Dracamer99.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Dracamer99.jpg/440px-Dracamer99.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dracamer99.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1899 first American edition, Doubleday &amp; McClure, New York.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Dracula</i> was not an immediate <a href="/wiki/Bestseller" title="Bestseller">bestseller</a> when it was first published in 1897, although reviewers were unstinting in their praise. The contemporary <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> ranked Stoker's powers above those of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">Mary Shelley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Brontë">Emily Brontë</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights" title="Wuthering Heights">Wuthering Heights</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to literary historians <a href="/wiki/Nina_Auerbach" title="Nina Auerbach">Nina Auerbach</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_J._Skal" title="David J. Skal">David J. Skal</a> in the Norton Critical Edition, the novel has become more significant for modern readers than it was for Victorian readers, most of whom enjoyed it just as a good adventure story. It reached its broad and iconic status only later in the 20th century when the movie versions appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A. Asbjørn Jøn has also noted that <i>Dracula</i> has had a significant impact on the image of the vampire in popular culture, folklore, and legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>It did not make much money for Stoker. The last year of his life, he was so poor that he had to petition for a compassionate grant from the Royal Literary Fund,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and his widow was forced to sell his notes and outlines of the novel at a <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">Sotheby's</a> auction in 1913, where they were purchased for a little over 2 pounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> But then F. W. Murnau's unauthorized adaptation of the story was released in theatres in 1922 in the form of <i><a href="/wiki/Nosferatu" title="Nosferatu">Nosferatu</a></i>. Stoker's widow took affront and, during the legal battle that followed, the novel's popularity started to grow.</p>
<p><i>Nosferatu</i> was followed by a highly successful stage adaptation, touring the UK for three years before arriving in the US where Stoker's creation caught Hollywood's attention and, after the American 1931 movie version was released, the book has never been out of print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, some Victorian fans were ahead of the time, describing it as "the sensation of the season" and "the most blood-curdling novel of the paralysed century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Sir Arthur Conan Doyle">Sir Arthur Conan Doyle</a> wrote to Stoker in a letter, "I write to tell you how very much I have enjoyed reading <i>Dracula</i>. I think it is the very best story of diablerie which I have read for many years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <i>Daily Mail</i> review of 1 June 1897 proclaimed it a classic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_horror" title="Gothic horror">Gothic horror</a>, "In seeking a parallel to this weird, powerful, and horrorful story our mind reverts to such tales as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mysteries_of_Udolpho" title="The Mysteries of Udolpho">The Mysteries of Udolpho</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fall_of_the_House_of_Usher" title="The Fall of the House of Usher">The Fall of the House of Usher</a></i> ... but Dracula is even more appalling in its gloomy fascination than any one of these."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly good reviews appeared when the book was published in the U.S. in 1899. The first American edition was published by <a href="/wiki/Doubleday_(publisher)" title="Doubleday (publisher)">Doubleday &amp; McClure</a> in New York.</p>
<p>In the last several decades, literary and cultural scholars have offered diverse analyses of Stoker's novel and the character of Count Dracula. C.F. Bentley reads Dracula as an embodiment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freudian" title="Freudian">Freudian</a> <a href="/wiki/Id,_ego_and_super-ego#Id" title="Id, ego and super-ego">id</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Carol A. Senf reads the novel as a response to the powerful New Woman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> while Christopher Craft sees Dracula as embodying latent homosexuality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Stephen D. Arata interprets the events of the novel as anxiety over <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racial_mixing" title="Racial mixing">racial mixing</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and Talia Schaffer construes the novel as an indictment of <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Franco_Moretti" title="Franco Moretti">Franco Moretti</a> reads Dracula as a figure of <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> though <a href="/wiki/Hollis_Robbins" title="Hollis Robbins">Hollis Robbins</a> suggests that Dracula's inability to participate in social conventions and to forge business partnerships undermines his power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Killing_Time_2014_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Killing_Time_2014-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Richard Noll reads <i>Dracula</i> within the context of 19th century <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">alienism</a> (<a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>) and <a href="/wiki/History_of_psychiatric_institutions" title="History of psychiatric institutions">asylum</a> medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> D. Bruno Starrs understands the novel to be a pro-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> pamphlet promoting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proselytization" title="Proselytization">proselytization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_and_geographical_references">Historical and geographical references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historical and geographical references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Dracula</i> is a work of fiction, but it does contain some historical references—though it is a matter of conjecture and debate as to how much historical connection was deliberate on Stoker's part.</p>
<p>Popular attention was drawn to the supposed connections between the historical <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvanian</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Vlad_the_Impaler" title="Vlad the Impaler">Vlad III Dracula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wallachia" title="Wallachia">Wallachia</a> and Bram Stoker's fictional Dracula, following the publication of <i>In Search of Dracula</i> by <a href="/wiki/Radu_Florescu" title="Radu Florescu">Radu Florescu</a> and Raymond McNally in 1972. During his main reign (1456–1462), "Vlad the Impaler" is said to have killed from 40,000 to 100,000 European civilians (political rivals, criminals, and anyone that he considered "useless to humanity"), mainly by impaling. The sources depicting these events are records by <a href="/wiki/Transylvanian_Saxons" title="Transylvanian Saxons">Saxon</a> settlers in neighbouring Transylvania who had frequent clashes with Vlad III. Vlad III is revered as a folk hero by <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanians</a> for driving off the invading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a>, of whom his <a href="/wiki/Impalement" title="Impalement">impaled</a> victims are said to have included as many as 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andreescu_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andreescu-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Historically, the name "Dracula" is derived from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" title="Chivalric order">Chivalric order</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Dragon" title="Order of the Dragon">Order of the Dragon</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund of Luxembourg</a> (then king of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>) to uphold <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and defend the Empire against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Turks</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vlad_II_Dracul" title="Vlad II Dracul">Vlad II Dracul</a>, father of Vlad III, was admitted to the order around 1431, after which Vlad II wore the emblem of the order and later, as ruler of Wallachia, his <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coinage</a> bore the dragon symbol, from which the name "Dracula" is derived. People of Wallachia only knew <i>voievod</i> (king) Vlad III as Vlad Țepeș (the Impaler). The name "Dracula" became popular in Romania after publication of Stoker's book. Contrary to popular belief, the name Dracula does not translate to "son of the devil" in <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, which would be "<i>pui de drac</i>".</p>
<p>Stoker came across the name Dracula in his reading on <a href="/wiki/History_of_Romania" title="History of Romania">Romanian history</a>, and chose this to replace the name (<i>Count Wampyr</i>) originally intended for his villain. Some Dracula scholars led by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Miller_(academic)" title="Elizabeth Miller (academic)">Elizabeth Miller</a> argue that Stoker knew little of the historic Vlad III except for the name "Dracula", whereas Stoker mentions the Dracula who fought against the Turks and was later betrayed by his brother, historical facts in the novel which unequivocally point to Vlad III:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Who was it but one of my own race who as Voivode crossed the Danube and beat the Turk on his own ground? This was a Dracula indeed! Woe was it that his own unworthy brother, when he had fallen, sold his people to the Turk and brought the shame of slavery on them! Was it not this Dracula, indeed, who inspired that other of his race who in a later age again and again brought his forces over the great river into Turkey-land; who, when he was beaten back, came again, and again, though he had to come alone from the bloody field where his troops were being slaughtered, since he knew that he alone could ultimately triumph! (Chapter 3, pp 19)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Count's identity is later speculated on by Professor Van Helsing:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He must, indeed, have been that Voivode Dracula who won his name against the Turk, over the great river on the very frontier of Turkey-land. (Chapter 18, p 145)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Many of Stoker's biographers and literary critics have found strong similarities to the earlier Irish writer <a href="/wiki/Sheridan_Le_Fanu" title="Sheridan Le Fanu">Sheridan Le Fanu</a>'s classic of the vampire genre <i><a href="/wiki/Carmilla" title="Carmilla">Carmilla</a></i>. In writing <i>Dracula</i>, Stoker may also have drawn on stories about the <a href="/wiki/Aos_S%C3%AD" title="Aos Sí">sídhe</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> some of which feature blood-drinking women. The folkloric figure of <a href="/wiki/Abhartach" title="Abhartach">Abhartach</a> has also been suggested as a source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curran_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-curran-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1983, McNally additionally suggested that Stoker was influenced by the history of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungarian</a> Countess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bathory" title="Elizabeth Bathory">Elizabeth Bathory</a>, who tortured and killed between 36 and 700 young women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It was later commonly believed that she committed these crimes to bathe in their blood, believing that this preserved her youth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her book <i>The Essential Dracula</i>, Clare Haword-Maden suggested that the castle of Count Dracula was inspired by <a href="/wiki/New_Slains_Castle" title="New Slains Castle">Slains Castle</a>, at which Bram Stoker was a guest of the 19th <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Erroll" title="Earl of Erroll">Earl of Erroll</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> According to Miller, he first visited Cruden Bay in 1893, three years after work had begun on <i>Dracula</i>. Haining and Tremaine maintain that, during this visit, Stoker was especially impressed by Slains Castle's interior and the surrounding landscape. Miller and Leatherdale question the stringency of this connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Possibly, Stoker was not inspired by a real edifice at all, but by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Carpathian_Castle" title="The Carpathian Castle">The Carpathian Castle</a></i> (1892) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne_Radcliffe" title="Anne Radcliffe">Anne Radcliffe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mysteries_of_Udolpho" title="The Mysteries of Udolpho">The Mysteries of Udolpho</a></i> (1794).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A third possibility is that he copied information about a castle at <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%A2ncovene%C8%99ti,_Mure%C8%99" title="Brâncovenești, Mureș">Vécs</a> from one of his sources on Transylvania, the book by Major E.C. Johnson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> A further option is that Stoker saw an illustration of Castle Bran (Törzburg) in the book on Transylvania by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Boner" title="Charles Boner">Charles Boner</a>, or read about it in the books by Mazuchelli or Crosse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the scenes in <a href="/wiki/Whitby" title="Whitby">Whitby</a> and London are based on real places that Stoker frequently visited, although he distorts the geography for the sake of the story in some cases. One scholar has suggested that Stoker chose <a href="/wiki/Whitby" title="Whitby">Whitby</a> as the site of Dracula's first appearance in England because of the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Whitby" title="Synod of Whitby">Synod of Whitby</a>, given the novel's preoccupation with timekeeping and calendar disputes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Killing_Time_2014_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Killing_Time_2014-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daniel Farson, Leonard Wolf, and Peter Haining have suggested that Stoker received much historical information from <a href="/wiki/%C3%81rmin_V%C3%A1mb%C3%A9ry" title="Ármin Vámbéry">Ármin Vámbéry</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> professor whom he met at least twice. Miller argues, "there is nothing to indicate that the conversation included Vlad, vampires, or even Transylvania", and "furthermore, there is no record of any other correspondence between Stoker and Vámbéry, nor is Vámbéry mentioned in Stoker's notes for Dracula."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dracula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Dracula_in_popular_culture" title="Dracula in popular culture">Dracula in popular culture</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bela_Lugosi_as_Dracula,_anonymous_photograph_from_1931,_Universal_Studios.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bela_Lugosi" title="Bela Lugosi">Bela Lugosi</a> as <a href="/wiki/Dracula_(1931_English-language_film)" title="Dracula (1931 English-language film)">Dracula</a> (1931)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The story of <i>Dracula</i> has been the basis for numerous films and plays. Stoker himself wrote the first theatrical adaptation, which was presented at the Lyceum Theatre under the title <i>Dracula, or The Undead</i> shortly before the novel's publication and performed only once. The first motion picture to feature Dracula was <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula%27s_Death" title="Dracula's Death">Dracula's Death</a></i>, produced in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> in 1921. The now-<a href="/wiki/Lost_film" title="Lost film">lost</a> film, however, was not an adaptation of Stoker's novel, but featured an original story. In 1922, German director <a href="/wiki/F._W._Murnau" title="F. W. Murnau">F. W. Murnau</a> directed <i><a href="/wiki/Nosferatu" title="Nosferatu">Nosferatu</a></i>. Prana Film, the production company, had been unable to obtain permission to adapt the story from Bram's widow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florence_Stoker" title="Florence Stoker">Florence Stoker</a>, so screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Galeen" title="Henrik Galeen">Henrik Galeen</a> was told to alter numerous details to avoid legal trouble. Galeen transplanted the action of the story from 1890s England to 1830s Germany and reworked several characters, dropping some (such as Lucy and all three of her suitors), and renaming others (Dracula became <a href="/wiki/Count_Orlok" title="Count Orlok">Orlok</a>, Jonathan Harker became Thomas Hutter, Mina became Ellen, and so on). This attempt failed to avoid prosecution, however; Florence Stoker sued Prana Film, and all prints of the film were ordered destroyed. The film did survive the court-ordered purge, and subsequent rereleases have typically undone some of the changes, most notably restoring the original character names (a practice also followed by <a href="/wiki/Werner_Herzog" title="Werner Herzog">Werner Herzog</a> in his 1979 remake of Murnau's film <i><a href="/wiki/Nosferatu_the_Vampyre" title="Nosferatu the Vampyre">Nosferatu the Vampyre</a></i>).</p>
<p>Following <i>Nosferatu</i>, Florence Stoker licensed the story to playwright <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Deane" title="Hamilton Deane">Hamilton Deane</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Dracula_(1924_play)" title="Dracula (1924 play)">1924 stage play adaptation</a> toured England for several years before settling down in London. In 1927, American stage producer <a href="/wiki/Horace_Liveright" title="Horace Liveright">Horace Liveright</a> hired <a href="/wiki/John_L._Balderston" title="John L. Balderston">John L. Balderston</a> to revise Deane's script in advance of its American premiere. Balderston significantly compressed the story, most notably consolidating or removing several characters. The Deane play and its Balderston revisions introduced an expanded role and history for Renfield, who now replaced Jonathan Harker as Dracula's solicitor in the first part of the story; combined <a href="/wiki/Mina_Harker" title="Mina Harker">Mina Harker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Westenra" title="Lucy Westenra">Lucy Westenra</a> into a single character (named Lucy); and omitted both <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Holmwood" title="Arthur Holmwood">Arthur Holmwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quincey_Morris" title="Quincey Morris">Quincey Morris</a> entirely. When the play premiered in New York, it was with <a href="/wiki/Bela_Lugosi" title="Bela Lugosi">Bela Lugosi</a> in the title role, and with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_van_Sloan" title="Edward van Sloan">Edward van Sloan</a> as <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Van_Helsing" title="Abraham Van Helsing">Abraham Van Helsing</a>, roles which both actors (as well as <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Bunston" title="Herbert Bunston">Herbert Bunston</a> as <a href="/wiki/John_Seward" title="John Seward">Dr. Seward</a>) reprised for the English-language version of the 1931 <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> <a href="/wiki/Dracula_(1931_English-language_film)" title="Dracula (1931 English-language film)">film production</a>. The 1931 film was one of the most commercially successful adaptations of the story to date; it and the Deane/Balderston play that preceded it set the standard for film and television adaptations of the story, with the alterations to the novel becoming standard for later adaptations for decades to come. Universal Studios continued to feature the character of Dracula in many of their <a href="/wiki/Universal_Monsters" title="Universal Monsters">horror films</a> from the 1930s and 1940s.</p>

<p>In 1958, British film company <a href="/wiki/Hammer_Film_Productions" title="Hammer Film Productions">Hammer Film Productions</a> followed the success of its <i><a href="/wiki/The_Curse_of_Frankenstein" title="The Curse of Frankenstein">The Curse of Frankenstein</a></i> from the previous year with <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula_(1958_film)" title="Dracula (1958 film)">Dracula</a></i>, released in the US as <i>The Horror of Dracula</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Terence_Fisher" title="Terence Fisher">Terence Fisher</a>. Fisher's production featured <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lee" title="Christopher Lee">Christopher Lee</a> as Dracula and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cushing" title="Peter Cushing">Peter Cushing</a> as Van Helsing, but it diverged considerably from both the original novel and from the Deane/Balderston adaptation. It was an international hit for Hammer Film, however, and both Lee and Cushing reprised their roles multiple times over the next decade and a half, concluding with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_the_7_Golden_Vampires" title="The Legend of the 7 Golden Vampires">The Legend of the 7 Golden Vampires</a></i> (with Cushing but not Lee) in 1974. Christopher Lee also took on the role of Dracula in <i><a href="/wiki/Count_Dracula_(1970_film)" title="Count Dracula (1970 film)">Count Dracula</a></i>, a 1970 Spanish-Italian-German coproduction notable for its adherence to the plot of the original novel. (For instance, it was the first film version of the story to include the character of Quincey Morris.) Playing the part of Renfield in that version was <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Kinski" title="Klaus Kinski">Klaus Kinski</a>, who later played Dracula himself in 1979's <i>Nosferatu the Vampyre</i>.</p>
<p>In 1977, the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> made <i><a href="/wiki/Count_Dracula_(1977_film)" title="Count Dracula (1977 film)">Count Dracula</a></i>, a 155-minute adaptation for television starring <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jourdan" title="Louis Jourdan">Louis Jourdan</a>. Later film adaptations include <a href="/wiki/John_Badham" title="John Badham">John Badham</a>'s 1979 <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula_(1979_film)" title="Dracula (1979 film)">Dracula</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Frank_Langella" title="Frank Langella">Frank Langella</a> and inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Dracula_(1977_play)" title="Dracula (1977 play)">1977 Broadway revival</a> of the Deane/Hamilton play, and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a>'s 1992 <i><a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker%27s_Dracula" title="Bram Stoker's Dracula">Bram Stoker's Dracula</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Gary_Oldman" title="Gary Oldman">Gary Oldman</a>. The character of Count Dracula has remained popular over the years, and many films have used the character as a villain, while others have named him in their titles, including <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula%27s_Daughter" title="Dracula's Daughter">Dracula's Daughter</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brides_of_Dracula" title="The Brides of Dracula">The Brides of Dracula</a></i>. As of 2009, an estimated 217 films feature Dracula in a major role,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> a number second only to <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> (223 films).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> A large number of these appearances are not adaptations of Stoker's novel, but merely feature the character in an unrelated story.</p>
