<p><i><b>Dragonlance</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Shared_universe" title="Shared universe">shared universe</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Laura_Hickman" title="Laura Hickman">Laura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Hickman" title="Tracy Hickman">Tracy Hickman</a>, and expanded by Tracy Hickman and <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Weis" title="Margaret Weis">Margaret Weis</a> under the direction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TSR,_Inc." title="TSR, Inc.">TSR, Inc.</a> into a series of fantasy novels. The Hickmans conceived <i>Dragonlance</i> while driving in their car on the way to TSR for a job interview. At TSR Tracy Hickman met Margaret Weis, his future writing partner, and they gathered a group of associates to play the <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a>. The adventures during that game inspired a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragonlance_modules_(DL_series)" title="Dragonlance modules (DL series)">series of gaming modules</a>, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_novels" title="List of Dragonlance novels">series of novels</a>, licensed products such as board games, and lead miniature figures.</p>
<p>In 1984, TSR published the first <i>Dragonlance</i> novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Autumn_Twilight" title="Dragons of Autumn Twilight">Dragons of Autumn Twilight</a></i>. It began the <i>Chronicles</i> trilogy, a core element of the <i>Dragonlance</i> world. While the authoring team of Tracy Hickman and Margaret Weis wrote the setting's central books, numerous other authors contributed novels and short stories to the setting. Over 190 novels have used the <i>Dragonlance</i> setting, and have been accompanied by supplemental <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> <a href="/wiki/Campaign_setting" title="Campaign setting">campaign setting</a> material for over a decade. In 1997, <a href="/wiki/Wizards_of_the_Coast" title="Wizards of the Coast">Wizards of the Coast LLC</a> purchased TSR, and licensed <i>Dragonlance</i> to <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Press,_Inc" title="Sovereign Press, Inc">Sovereign Press, Inc</a> in 2001 to produce game materials; this licensing agreement expired in 2007.</p>
<p>The fictional <i>Dragonlance</i> world of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krynn" title="Krynn">Krynn</a> contains numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_major_Dragonlance_characters" title="List of major Dragonlance characters">characters</a>, an extensive timeline, and a detailed <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_locations" title="List of Dragonlance locations">geography</a>. The history of Krynn consists of five ages. The novels and related game products are primarily set in the fourth age, The Age of Despair. Since February 2009, the fifth age, the Age of Mortals, has been used. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_characters#Heroes_of_the_Lance" title="List of Dragonlance characters">Heroes of the Lance</a>, created by Weis and Hickman, are the popular protagonists of the <i>Chronicles</i> trilogy. Along with <i>D&amp;D'</i>s world of the <i><a href="/wiki/Forgotten_Realms" title="Forgotten Realms">Forgotten Realms</a></i>, <i>Dragonlance</i> is one of the most popular shared worlds in fiction.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World">World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Dragonlance</i> world is described in dozens of books and novels. The setting contains numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_major_Dragonlance_characters" title="List of major Dragonlance characters">characters</a>, an extensive timeline, and a detailed <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_locations" title="List of Dragonlance locations">geography</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Krynn">Krynn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Krynn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Dragonlance</i> is set on the world of Krynn. The majority of the novels take place in the various regions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ansalon" title="Ansalon">Ansalon</a>, a small continent, though some have taken place on the continent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taladas" title="Taladas">Taladas</a>, located northeast of Ansalon. The world's major gods are the High God and his children: good <a href="/wiki/Paladine" title="Paladine">Paladine</a>, neutral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilean" title="Gilean">Gilean</a>, and evil <a href="/wiki/Takhisis" title="Takhisis">Takhisis</a>. The gods are opposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaos_(Dragonlance)" title="Chaos (Dragonlance)">Chaos</a>, who seeks to destroy Krynn. Depending on the time period, the evil <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_dragon" title="Chromatic dragon">chromatic</a> and the good <a href="/wiki/Metallic_dragon" title="Metallic dragon">metallic dragons</a> are rare or plentiful. <a href="/wiki/Human_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Human (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">Humans</a> are Krynn's most common humanoid race, but <a href="/wiki/Elf_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Elf (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">elves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Dwarf (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">dwarves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kender_(fantasy)" title="Kender (fantasy)">kender</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnome_(Dragonlance)" title="Gnome (Dragonlance)">gnomes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Minotaur_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)#Minotaurs_in_Dragonlance" title="Minotaur (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">minotaurs</a> occupy the world as well. Clerics derive magical powers from their gods, and wizards derive their power from the three moon gods, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solinari" title="Solinari">Solinari</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunitari" title="Lunitari">Lunitari</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuitari" title="Nuitari">Nuitari</a>. Hickman had previously served as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_Movement" title="Latter Day Saint Movement">Mormon</a> missionary in <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> for two years, and uses <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> in <i>Dragonlance</i> spells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> During Krynn's various wars, armies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draconian_(Dragonlance)" title="Draconian (Dragonlance)">draconians</a> are used as troops. Draconians are created by corrupting a dragon egg, thereby creating a reptilian humanoid. The eggs of good dragons create evil draconians, and vice versa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_history">Fictional history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fictional history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of the world of Krynn, and thus the settings for both the novels and gaming supplements, is roughly split into five separate ages. The first age is the time of creation, when the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_deities" title="List of Dragonlance deities">gods</a> are born and Krynn is formed. The Age of Dreams, the second age, is marked by the rapid growth of the world's first great civilizations and the appearance of myriad new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Character_race_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Character race (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">races</a>. This era is also marked by three great wars between dragons and their minions. Following the Third Dragon War, in the Age of Might, the <a href="/wiki/Cataclysm_(Dragonlance)" title="Cataclysm (Dragonlance)">Cataclysm</a> obliterates the great empire of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Istar_(Dragonlance)" title="Istar (Dragonlance)">Istar</a> and changes almost the entire surface of Krynn. A three-hundred year depression follows this event, in what is called the Age of Despair. This period also marks the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Lance" title="War of the Lance">War of the Lance</a>. When Dragonlance was first introduced to <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i>, events such as the Lost Wars happened during The Age of Despair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dragonlances">Dragonlances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dragonlances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dragonlances are weapons first created in the Third Dragon War, designed with the purpose of killing evil <a href="/wiki/Dragon_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Dragon (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">dragons</a>, and are the only weapons with which mortals can kill dragons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolf201023_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolf201023-3">[3]</a></sup> Dragonlances have this power because of the way in which they are created, which requires the use of "two god-blessed artifacts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolf201023_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolf201023-3">[3]</a></sup> The weapons clearly draw upon Christian iconography, as the two mythical figures shown defeating dragons, <a href="/wiki/Michael_(archangel)" title="Michael (archangel)">Archangel Michael</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a>, are often portrayed wielding lances to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolf201023_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolf201023-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dragonlances are rare and not commonly traded. There are lesser dragonlances, which are made when only one of the artifacts is used to create them, and greater dragonlances, which are made when both artifacts are used to make them. Greater dragonlances are blessed with the power of Good, unlike lesser dragonlances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dragonlances_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dragonlances-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Heroes of the Lance are the protagonists of the <i>Chronicles</i> trilogy, the first series of <i>Dragonlance</i> books. They were created by Weis and Hickman, then fleshed out as <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player characters</a> in gaming sessions of <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> at Hickman's apartment. One player at this initial gaming session was game designer Terry Phillips, who was playing as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raistlin" title="Raistlin">Raistlin</a>. According to Hickman in the foreword to <i>The Soulforge</i>, "[we] were just settling in to the game when I turned to my good friend Terry Phillips and asked what his character was doing. Terry spoke...and the world of Krynn was forever changed. His rasping voice, his sarcasm and bitterness all masking an arrogance and power that never needed to be stated suddenly were real. Everyone in the room was both transfixed and terrified. To this day Margaret [Weis] swears that Terry wore the black robes to the party that night."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several other Heroes of the Lance were played by various people. Authors Gary and Janet Pack played the half-elf <a href="/wiki/Tanis_Half-Elven" title="Tanis Half-Elven">Tanis Half-Elven</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kender_(fantasy)" title="Kender (fantasy)">kender</a> <a href="/wiki/Tasslehoff_Burrfoot" title="Tasslehoff Burrfoot">Tasslehoff Burrfoot</a>, respectively. Author <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Niles" title="Douglas Niles">Douglas Niles</a> played the dwarf <a href="/wiki/Flint_Fireforge" title="Flint Fireforge">Flint Fireforge</a>. TSR employee Harold Johnson played the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_Solamnia" title="Knights of Solamnia">Solamnic knight</a> <a href="/wiki/Sturm_Brightblade" title="Sturm Brightblade">Sturm Brightblade</a>. The rest of the Heroes are the barbarians <a href="/wiki/Goldmoon" title="Goldmoon">Goldmoon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riverwind" title="Riverwind">Riverwind</a>, elf <a href="/wiki/Laurana_Kanan" title="Laurana Kanan">Laurana Kanan</a>, and humans <a href="/wiki/Caramon_Majere" title="Caramon Majere">Caramon Majere</a> (Raistlin's brother) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tika_Waylan" title="Tika Waylan">Tika Waylan</a>. Weis played <a href="/wiki/Paladine" title="Paladine">Fizban the Fabulous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Archer-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the beginning, Margaret Weis had problems depicting <a href="/wiki/Tanis_Half-Elven" title="Tanis Half-Elven">Tanis Half-Elven</a> in the novels. Tracy Hickman finally told her "He's <a href="/wiki/James_T._Kirk" title="James T. Kirk">James T. Kirk</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Starship_Enterprise" title="Starship Enterprise">Starship <i>Enterprise</i></a>." After that explanation, Margaret had no more difficulty writing about Tanis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirk_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirk-8">[8]</a></sup> Other noteworthy antagonists, and sometimes protagonists, are the <a href="/wiki/Death_knight_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Death knight (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">Death Knight</a> <a href="/wiki/Lord_Soth" title="Lord Soth">Lord Soth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitiara_Uth_Matar" title="Kitiara Uth Matar">Kitiara Uth Matar</a>, the half-sister of Raistlin and Caramon, and leader of one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragonarmies_of_Ansalon" title="Dragonarmies of Ansalon">Dragonarmies of Ansalon</a>. According to Hickman, Lord Soth is the most unpredictable character to write about, saying "Every time that character made an appearance in one of our books he would try to run off with the story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">Publication history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Publication history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg/220px-Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg/330px-Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg/440px-Tracy_Hickman_and_Margaret_Weis_-_GenCon_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Hickman developed his world creation technique by writing and self-publishing with his wife Laura the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dungeons_%26_Dragons_modules" title="List of Dungeons &amp; Dragons modules">adventure modules</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rahasia" title="Rahasia">Rahasia</a></i> (1979) and <i><a href="/wiki/Desert_of_Desolation#Pharaoh" title="Desert of Desolation">Pharaoh</a></i> (1980), and writing TSR's <a href="/wiki/Ravenloft_(module)" title="Ravenloft (module)"><i>Ravenloft</i> module</a> (1983). He was unemployed in 1982, and TSR offered him a job based on his submission of several modules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> That year, while driving from Utah to Wisconsin to start a job with TSR, Hickman and his wife created the <i>Dragonlance</i> universe concept. During the trip, Hickman and his wife discussed two ideas they had had for several years: an entire world used to support a storyline, and a world dominated by dragons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Archer-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their ideas were well received by TSR, whose marketing department felt they had enough dungeons, but not enough <a href="/wiki/Dragon_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Dragon (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">dragons</a>. Hickman suggested a series of twelve modules, each featuring a different dragon. TSR employee <a href="/wiki/Harold_Johnson_(game_designer)" title="Harold Johnson (game designer)">Harold Johnson</a> suggested that Hickman should try to get additional support from other TSR staff members and, after a period of months, Hickman had the support of <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Grubb" title="Jeff Grubb">Jeff Grubb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Elmore" title="Larry Elmore">Larry Elmore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_E._Moore" title="Roger E. Moore">Roger Moore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Niles" title="Douglas Niles">Doug Niles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Williams_(author)" title="Michael Williams (author)">Michael Williams</a>, and others with whom they discussed ideas for the project. Meanwhile, Weis was editing and writing various <i><a href="/wiki/Endless_Quest" title="Endless Quest">Endless Quest</a></i> books for TSR. The <i>Dragonlance</i> group decided that novels should accompany the game modules; TSR reluctantly agreed and hired a writer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Archer-7">[7]</a></sup> Hickman became the design coordinator for <i>Project Overlord</i>, the cover name for what would later be known as the Dragonlance saga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dragon_.23120_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dragon_.23120-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>TSR decided to create a franchise, including modules, board games, lead figures, and—for the first time—novels. Weis had been hired as an editor; with Hickman, she began working with the author hired to write the novels. They weren't satisfied with the author, and decided they should be the ones to write the books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> They collaborated over a weekend, writing the prologue for the first five chapters of the first novel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Autumn_Twilight" title="Dragons of Autumn Twilight">Dragons of Autumn Twilight</a></i>, based on the module <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Despair" title="Dragons of Despair">Dragons of Despair</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-courier_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-courier-11">[11]</a></sup> TSR liked their treatment and gave them the assignment, firing the author. After two years of development, TSR released <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Autumn_Twilight" title="Dragons of Autumn Twilight">Dragons of Autumn Twilight</a></i> as a supplement to the <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Advanced Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup> TSR had doubts about the finished novel's sales potential, and attempted to order thirty thousand copies, ultimately ordering the minimum print run of fifty thousand. The success of the novel prompted TSR to publish more copies to meet demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Dragonlance</i> eventually received ancillary products such as novels, calendars, computer games, and books of artwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.26Dfaq_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-D.26Dfaq-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_development">Further development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Further development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid to late 1980s, a rift developed between Weis and Hickman, and TSR. The pair were feeling under-appreciated by the company, and when TSR turned down their <i><a href="/wiki/Darksword" title="Darksword">Darksword</a></i> series of novels, they went to <a href="/wiki/Bantam_Books" title="Bantam Books">Bantam Books</a>. Bantam made them an offer, which they accepted, and they stopped writing <i>Dragonlance</i> novels for TSR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> They returned to write <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Summer_Flame" title="Dragons of Summer Flame">Dragons of Summer Flame</a></i> for TSR in 1995, thinking it would be their final <i>Dragonlance</i> novel. At the time, <i>Dragonlance</i> gaming had been converted to the <a href="/wiki/SAGA_System" title="SAGA System">SAGA System</a>, with limited success, and that, combined with TSR's general financial troubles, put the setting's future in doubt. Wizards of the Coast bought the troubled TSR in 1997, and Weis and Hickman then proposed the <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_novels#The_War_of_Souls" title="List of Dragonlance novels">War of Souls</a></i> trilogy, which was published in 2000–2002. All three novels made the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_bestseller_list" title="New York Times bestseller list"><i>New York Times</i> bestseller list</a>, and the setting was commercially revitalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Archer-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1998, the original <i>Dragonlance</i> trilogy had sold well over three million copies worldwide and spawned dozens of sequels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dragon_.23243_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dragon_.23243-14">[14]</a></sup> The central books of the <i>Dragonlance</i> series were written by the authoring team of Weis and Hickman; however, many other writers have made contributions, including <a href="/wiki/Richard_A._Knaak" title="Richard A. Knaak">Richard A. Knaak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Niles" title="Douglas Niles">Douglas Niles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_E._Moore" title="Roger E. Moore">Roger E. Moore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Perrin" title="Don Perrin">Don Perrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Rabe" title="Jean Rabe">Jean Rabe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_B._Thompson_(novelist)" title="Paul B. Thompson (novelist)">Paul B. Thompson</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tonya_C._Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tonya C. Cook (page does not exist)">Tonya C. Cook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Williams_(author)" title="Michael Williams (author)">Michael Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Varian_Berberick" title="Nancy Varian Berberick">Nancy Varian Berberick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Pierson" title="Chris Pierson">Chris Pierson</a>.</p>
<p>In 2001, Wizards of the Coast licensed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovereign_Press_(role-playing_game_publisher)" title="Sovereign Press (role-playing game publisher)">Sovereign Press</a> to publish further <i>Dragonlance</i> game materials. This began with the newly revised <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance_Campaign_Setting" title="Dragonlance Campaign Setting">Dragonlance Campaign Setting</a></i> in 2003, which used the new <a href="/wiki/Editions_of_Dungeons_%26_Dragons#Dungeons_.26_Dragons_3rd_edition" title="Editions of Dungeons &amp; Dragons"><i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> 3rd Edition</a> rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Archer-7">[7]</a></sup> On April 23, 2007, Weis announced Wizards of the Coast had not renewed Sovereign's license, and that <i>Dragonlance</i> RPG game supplements and accessories would only be released through the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Dragonlance</i> is one of the most popular <a href="/wiki/Shared_universe" title="Shared universe">shared worlds</a>, worlds in which writers other than those that created them place adventures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The first <i>Dragonlance</i> trilogy, <i>Chronicles</i>, launched the <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> line of novels, with many of its characters spun off into other novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies-17">[17]</a></sup> Along with <i><a href="/wiki/Forgotten_Realms" title="Forgotten Realms">Forgotten Realms</a></i>, <i>Dragonlance</i> is TSR's most popular series of novels. According to <i>The 1990s</i> by Marc Oxoby, what is most notable about the series is that "what may at one time been considered disposable, escapist literature" found "unprecedented popularity" in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxoby_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxoby-18">[18]</a></sup> All of the <i>Dragonlance</i> novels remained in print during the decade, turning Weis and Hickman into literary stars and boosting sales of their non-<i>Dragonlance</i> novels. Although the series was initially published in paperback, its success led to hardcover printings. The hardcover version of <i>Dragons of Summer Flame</i> had an "impressive" first printing of 200,000 books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxoby_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxoby-18">[18]</a></sup> Every <i>Dragonlance</i> novel by Weis and Hickman since 1995 has been released in hardcover, and some previous novels have been re-released in hardcover collector's editions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>Dragonlance</i> made TSR one of the most successful publishers of science fiction and fantasy in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dragon_Lady_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dragon_Lady-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 2008, there were more than 190 novels in the <i>Dragonlance</i> franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-courier_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-courier-11">[11]</a></sup> Weis and Hickman's <i>Dragonlance</i> novels have made over twenty bestseller lists, with sales in excess of 22 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dragon_Lady_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dragon_Lady-20">[20]</a></sup> The pair's novels have been translated into German, Japanese, Danish, Finnish, Spanish, French, Italian, Hebrew, Portuguese, Greek and Turkish and have sold well in the United States, Britain, and Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not all critics have praised <i>Dragonlance</i> and its creators. According to author <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hunt_(author)" title="Stephen Hunt (author)">Stephen Hunt</a>, Wendy Bradley of <i><a href="/wiki/Interzone_(magazine)" title="Interzone (magazine)">Interzone</a></i> magazine does not think highly of their work. Hunt feels that it is unusual for authors to receive such loathing among "fantasy's literary mafia", saying, "Behind every critic's scorn laden insult, there lays [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] that unsaid thought at the end: 'But I could have written that!'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Visions of Wonder</i>, edited by <a href="/wiki/David_G._Hartwell" title="David G. Hartwell">David G. Hartwell</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Milton_T._Wolf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Milton T. Wolf (page does not exist)">Milton T. Wolf</a>, and published by the <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction_Research_Association" title="Science Fiction Research Association">Science Fiction Research Association</a>, argues that <i>Dragonlance</i> is published under the "omnivore theory" of publishing. In this theory, the readership is made up of teenagers, and completely replaces itself every three to five years. This allows publishers to release subpar novels and still reach a small yet profitable audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels">Novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_novels" title="List of Dragonlance novels">List of Dragonlance novels</a></div>
<p>The main storyline of the original <i>Dragonlance</i> series has been written by Margaret Weis and Tracy Hickman, with multiple books written by other authors, covering years between and sometimes during the main events.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance_Chronicles" title="Dragonlance Chronicles">Chronicles</a></i> trilogy relates the events since the meeting of the Companions until the end of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Lance" title="War of the Lance">War of the Lance</a> and the defeat of the Dragon armies of Ansalon.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance_Legends" title="Dragonlance Legends">Legends</a></i> trilogy covers the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Lady%27s_War" title="Blue Lady's War">Blue Lady's War</a>, as well as the past adventures of <a href="/wiki/Raistlin_Majere" title="Raistlin Majere">Raistlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caramon_Majere" title="Caramon Majere">Caramon Majere</a>, culminating in Raistlin's attempt to achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godhood" title="Godhood">godhood</a>. The books feature <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a>, and focus on events in <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_locations#Istar" title="List of Dragonlance locations">Istar</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Cataclysm_(Dragonlance)" title="Cataclysm (Dragonlance)">Cataclysm</a>, as well as the ensuing Dwarfgate Wars.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Second_Generation" title="The Second Generation">The Second Generation</a></i> is a single compilation book which picks the most important tales from the <i>Tales</i> series and details the children of the Companions, all of whom become players in the later story. This book is considered to be part of the main storyline, as it must be read to understand the events that happened between the War of the Lance and the Chaos War. This novel develops characters that would later be seen in the <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_Souls" title="War of Souls">War of Souls</a></i> trilogy.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Summer_Flame" title="Dragons of Summer Flame">Dragons of Summer Flame</a></i> covers the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaos_War_(Dragonlance)" title="Chaos War (Dragonlance)">Chaos War</a>, also known as the Second Cataclysm. The gods and mortals join forces to defeat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaos_(Dragonlance)" title="Chaos (Dragonlance)">Chaos</a> in his attempt to destroy Krynn. The war ends with the withdrawal of Chaos and the gods of Krynn in a divine agreement to keep the world safe.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_novels#Dragons_of_a_New_Age" title="List of Dragonlance novels">Dragons of a New Age</a></i> describes the rise of the Dragon Overlords and introduces the Fifth Age of Dragonlance. It leads into the <i>War of Souls</i> trilogy.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_War_of_Souls" title="The War of Souls">The War of Souls</a></i> trilogy begins as a strange storm courses through Krynn, heralding the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Souls" title="War of Souls">War of Souls</a>. The end of the war brings the return of the gods, <a href="/wiki/Takhisis" title="Takhisis">Takhisis</a>'s death, and the departure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paladine_(Dragonlance)" title="Paladine (Dragonlance)">Paladine</a> as head of the good gods in order to maintain the balance between Good and Evil.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_novels#Dark_Disciple" title="List of Dragonlance novels">The Dark Disciple</a></i> trilogy follows the death of <a href="/wiki/Takhisis" title="Takhisis">Takhisis</a> and the departure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paladine_(Dragonlance)" title="Paladine (Dragonlance)">Paladine</a>, when the lesser gods strive to maintain dominance.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_novels#The_Lost_Chronicles_Trilogy" title="List of Dragonlance novels">Lost Chronicles</a></i> trilogy is a companion to the original <i>Chronicles</i>. Each book of the trilogy fills in sections of the story previously left untold. It tells the story surrounding the recovery of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_artifacts#Hammer_of_Kharas" title="List of Dragonlance artifacts">Hammer of Kharas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> how the Companions retrieve the dragon orb from Ice Wall, how <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitiara_Uth_Matar" title="Kitiara Uth Matar">Kitiara Uth Matar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Soth" title="Lord Soth">Lord Soth</a> became allies, and how <a href="/wiki/Raistlin_Majere" title="Raistlin Majere">Raistlin Majere</a> took the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wizards_of_High_Sorcery#Alignment" title="Wizards of High Sorcery">Black Robes</a> in <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_locations#Neraka" title="List of Dragonlance locations">Neraka</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign_setting">Campaign setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Campaign setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Dragonlance_modules_and_sourcebooks" title="List of Dragonlance modules and sourcebooks">List of Dragonlance modules and sourcebooks</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dragonlance_Adventures_1987_book_cover.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="300" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/32/Dragonlance_Adventures_1987_book_cover.jpg/220px-Dragonlance_Adventures_1987_book_cover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/32/Dragonlance_Adventures_1987_book_cover.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dragonlance_Adventures_1987_book_cover.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance_Adventures" title="Dragonlance Adventures">Dragonlance Adventures</a></i>, the first <i>Dragonlance</i> campaign setting sourcebook</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TSR,_Inc." title="TSR, Inc.">TSR</a> created <i>Dragonlance</i> as a campaign setting for the <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Advanced Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> (<i>AD&amp;D</i>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roleplaying_game" title="Roleplaying game">roleplaying game</a> in 1982, publishing the first of a series of <a href="/wiki/Adventure_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Adventure (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">modules</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Despair" title="Dragons of Despair">Dragons of Despair</a></i>, in March 1984. They published the first world-spanning sourcebook, <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance_Adventures" title="Dragonlance Adventures">Dragonlance Adventures</a></i>, in 1987. When <i>AD&amp;D</i> was updated to the 2nd edition in 1989, the <i>Dragonlance</i> campaign setting was updated as well. However, in 1996, <i>Dragonlance</i> was converted to use the new <a href="/wiki/SAGA_System" title="SAGA System">SAGA System</a>, which uses cards to determine the effects of actions, with the publication of the <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance:_Fifth_Age" title="Dragonlance: Fifth Age">Dragonlance: Fifth Age</a></i> roleplaying game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup> When the 3rd edition of <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> was released, Dragonlance was again updated with a new sourcebook (<i>Dragonlance Campaign Setting</i>), although no new adventures were published by <a href="/wiki/Wizards_of_the_Coast" title="Wizards of the Coast">Wizards of the Coast</a>. Wizards of the Coast also turned over all responsibility for maintaining the <i>Dragonlance</i> setting in the 3rd edition to Margaret Weis's home company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovereign_Press_(role-playing_game_publisher)" title="Sovereign Press (role-playing game publisher)">Sovereign Press</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragonlance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonlance:_Dragons_of_Autumn_Twilight" title="Dragonlance: Dragons of Autumn Twilight">Dragonlance: Dragons of Autumn Twilight</a></i>, an animated movie based on the first <i>Dragonlance</i> book of the same name, was released <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">direct-to-video</a>. The animation was produced by <a href="/wiki/Toonz_India_Ltd" title="Toonz India Ltd">Toonz Animation</a>, and featured the voices of <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Lawless" title="Lucy Lawless">Lucy Lawless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiefer_Sutherland" title="Kiefer Sutherland">Kiefer Sutherland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Rosenbaum" title="Michael Rosenbaum">Michael Rosenbaum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Trachtenberg" title="Michelle Trachtenberg">Michelle Trachtenberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A number of <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> are also set in the Dragonlance world: including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Dungeons_and_Dragons:_Heroes_of_the_Lance" title="Advanced Dungeons and Dragons: Heroes of the Lance">Heroes of the Lance</a></i> (1988), <i><a href="/wiki/Dragons_of_Flame_(video_game)" title="Dragons of Flame (video game)">Dragons of Flame</a></i> (1989), <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Lance_(video_game)" title="War of the Lance (video game)">War of the Lance</a></i> (1989), <i><a href="/wiki/DragonStrike_(video_game)" title="DragonStrike (video game)">Dragonstrike</a></i> (1990), <i><a href="/wiki/Shadow_Sorcerer" title="Shadow Sorcerer">Shadow Sorcerer</a></i> (1991), <i><a href="/wiki/Champions_of_Krynn" title="Champions of Krynn">Champions of Krynn</a></i> (1990), <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Knights_of_Krynn" title="Death Knights of Krynn">Death Knights of Krynn</a></i> (1991), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Queen_of_Krynn" title="The Dark Queen of Krynn">The Dark Queen of Krynn</a></i> (1992).<br/>
The <a href="/wiki/MUSH" title="MUSH">MUSH</a> game <i>DragonLance</i> is based on Krynn during the final stage of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Lance" title="War of the Lance">War of the Lance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series has inspired mention in music as well, including "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wishmaster_(song)" title="Wishmaster (song)">Wishmaster</a>", a song by <a href="/wiki/Nightwish" title="Nightwish">Nightwish</a> based partially on the master and apprentice relationship between <a href="/wiki/Raistlin_Majere" title="Raistlin Majere">Raistlin Majere</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalamar" title="Dalamar">Dalamar</a>. The Swedish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_band" title="Metal band">metal band</a> <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_Tears" title="Lake of Tears">Lake of Tears</a> also recorded a song called "Raistlin and the Rose" on their 1997 album <i>Crimson Cosmos</i>, while the German group <a href="/wiki/Blind_Guardian" title="Blind Guardian">Blind Guardian</a> wrote "The Soulforged", another song inspired by Raistlin's story, which appeared on the band's 2002 album <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(Blind_Guardian_album)" title="A Night at the Opera (Blind Guardian album)">A Night at the Opera</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Also Danish/American band Pyramaze recorded in their 2008 album <i>Immortal</i> song "Caramon's Poem".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Another German metal band, Evertale, released <i>The Chronicles Chapter I</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extended_Play" title="Extended Play">EP</a> in 2008 and the full album <i>Of Dragons And Elves</i> in 2013 - both releases were composed entirely of songs inspired by and relating to Dragonlance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In late 2011, Holysoft Studios Ltd. released the first part of a German audio adaption of the Chronicles Trilogy, with subsequent releases of the later trilogies being announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
