<p><b>Ornithomimus</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/-/ affix">-</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/-/ affix">-</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> "bird mimic") is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ornithomimosauria" title="Ornithomimosauria">ornithomimid</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> from the Late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">Period</a> of what is now <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. <i>Ornithomimus</i> was a swift bipedal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a> which fossil evidence indicates was covered in <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>, equipped with a small toothless beak that may indicate an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous">omnivorous</a> diet. It is usually classified into two species: the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, <i><b>Ornithomimus velox</b></i>, and a referred species, <i><b>Ornithomimus edmontonicus</b></i>. <i>O. velox</i> was named in 1890 by <a href="/wiki/Othniel_Charles_Marsh" title="Othniel Charles Marsh">Othniel Charles Marsh</a> on the basis of a foot and partial hand from the late <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a>-age <a href="/wiki/Denver_Formation" title="Denver Formation">Denver Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Another seventeen species have been named since, though most of them have subsequently been assigned to new genera or shown to be not directly related to <i>Ornithomimus velox</i>. The best material of species still considered part of the genus has been found in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, representing the species <i>O. edmontonicus</i>, known from several skeletons from the early Maastrichtian <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Canyon_Formation" title="Horseshoe Canyon Formation">Horseshoe Canyon Formation</a>. Additional species and specimens from other formations are sometimes classified as <i>Ornithomimus</i>, such as <i><b>Ornithomimus samueli</b></i> (alternately classified in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Dromiceiomimus" title="Dromiceiomimus">Dromiceiomimus</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Struthiomimus" title="Struthiomimus">Struthiomimus</a></i>) from the earlier, <a href="/wiki/Campanian" title="Campanian">Campanian</a>-age <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_Park_Formation" title="Dinosaur Park Formation">Dinosaur Park Formation</a> of Alberta.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1092" data-file-width="956" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg/170px-Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg/255px-Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg/340px-Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_edmontonicus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Specimen of <i>Ornithomimus sp.</i> found in 1995 with quill knobs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Tyrrell_Museum" title="Royal Tyrrell Museum">Royal Tyrrell Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Ornithomimosauria" title="Ornithomimosauria">ornithomimids</a>, species of <i>Ornithomimus</i> are characterized by feet with three weight-bearing toes, long slender arms, and long necks with birdlike, elongated, toothless, beaked skulls. They were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biped" title="Biped">bipedal</a> and superficially resembled <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostriches</a>. They would have been swift runners. They had very long limbs, hollow bones, and large brains and eyes. The brains of ornithomimids in general were large for non-avialan dinosaurs, but this may not necessarily be a sign of greater intelligence; some paleontologists think that the enlarged portions of the brain were dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinesthetic" title="Kinesthetic">kinesthetic</a> coordination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ageofdinosaursdromiceiomimus_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ageofdinosaursdromiceiomimus-3">[3]</a></sup> The bones of the hands are remarkably sloth-like in appearance, which led <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn" title="Henry Fairfield Osborn">Henry Fairfield Osborn</a> to suggest that they were used to hook branches during feeding.</p>
<p><i>Ornithomimus</i> differ from other ornithomimids, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Struthiomimus" title="Struthiomimus">Struthiomimus</a></i>, in having shorter torsos, long slender forearms, very slender, straight hand and foot claws and in having hand bones (metacarpals) and fingers of similar lengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dinosauria2004_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dinosauria2004-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two <i>Ornithomimus</i> species today seen as possibly valid differ in size. In 2010 <a href="/wiki/Gregory_S._Paul" title="Gregory S. Paul">Gregory S. Paul</a> estimated the length of <i>O. edmontonicus</i> at 3.8 m (12 ft), its weight at 170 kilograms (370 lb). One of its specimens, CMN 12228, preserves a <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">femur</a> (thigh bone) 46.8 centimetres (18.4 in) long. <i>O. velox</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of <i>Ornithomimus</i>, is based on material of a smaller animal. Whereas the <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> of <i>O. edmontonicus</i>, CMN 8632, preserves a second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metacarpal" title="Metacarpal">metacarpal</a> eighty-four millimetres long, the same element with <i>O. velox</i> measures only fifty-three millimetres.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feathers_and_skin">Feathers and skin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Feathers and skin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ornithomimus</i>, like many dinosaurs, was long thought to have been scaly. However, beginning in 1995, several specimens of <i>Ornithomimus</i> have been found preserving evidence of <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>.</p>
<p>In 1995, 2008 and 2009, three <i>Ornithomimus edmontonicus</i> specimens with evidence of feathers were found; two adults with carbonized traces on the lower arm, indicating the former presence of <a href="/wiki/Pennaceous_feather" title="Pennaceous feather">pennaceous feather</a> shafts, and a juvenile with impressions of feathers, of which were up to five centimetres in length, in the form of hair-like filaments covering the rump, legs and neck was also discovered. The fact that the feather imprints were found in <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, previously thought to not be able to support such impressions, raised the possibility of finding similar structures with more careful preparation of future specimens. A study describing the fossils in 2012 concluded that <i>O. edmontonicus</i> was covered in plumaceous feathers at all growth stages, and that only adults had pennaceous wing-like structures, suggesting that wings may have evolved for mating displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2014, Christian Foth and others argued that the evidence was insufficient to conclude that the forelimb feathers of <i>Ornithomimus</i> were necessarily pennaceous, citing the fact that the monofilamentous wing feathers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassowaries" title="Cassowaries">cassowaries</a> would likely leave similar traces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1052" data-file-width="2074" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png/220px-%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png/330px-%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png/440px-%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%22Ornithomimus%22_sp._by_Tom_Parker.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Life restoration of the plumage pattern suggested by specimens preserving feathers and skin (Tom Parker, 2015)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A fourth feathered specimen of <i>Ornithomimus</i>, this time from the lower portion of the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_Park_Formation" title="Dinosaur Park Formation">Dinosaur Park Formation</a>, was described in October, 2015 by Aaron van der Reest, Alex Wolfe, and Phil Currie. It was the first <i>Ornithomimus</i> specimen to preserve the feathers along the tail. The feathers, though crushed and distorted, bore numerous similarities with those of the modern <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostrich</a>, both in their structure and distribution on the body. Skin impressions were also preserved in the 2015 specimen, which indicated that from mid-thigh to the feet, there was bare skin devoid of scales, and that a flap of skin connect the upper thigh to the torso. This latter structure is similar to that found in modern birds, including ostriches, but was positioned higher above the knee in <i>Ornithomimus</i> than in birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanderreestetal2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanderreestetal2015-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_discovery">History of discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_species_named">First species named</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First species named">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_velox.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="786" data-file-width="993" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Ornithomimus_velox.jpg/220px-Ornithomimus_velox.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Ornithomimus_velox.jpg/330px-Ornithomimus_velox.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Ornithomimus_velox.jpg/440px-Ornithomimus_velox.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_velox.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Holotype material of <i>O. velox</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The history of <i>Ornithomimus</i> classification, and the classification of ornithomimids in general, has been complicated. The <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, <i>Ornithomimus velox</i>, was first named by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O.C._Marsh" title="O.C. Marsh">O.C. Marsh</a> in 1890, based on <a href="/wiki/Syntype" title="Syntype">syntypes</a> YPM 542 and YPM 548, a partial hindlimb and forelimb found on 30 June 1889 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Lyman_Cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Lyman Cannon (page does not exist)">George Lyman Cannon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Denver_Formation" title="Denver Formation">Denver Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. The generic name means "bird mimic", derived from Greek ὄρνις, <i>ornis</i>, "bird", and μῖμος, <i>mimos</i>, "mimic", in reference to the bird-like foot. The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> means "swift" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Simultaneously, Marsh named two other species: <i>Ornithomimus tenuis</i>, based on specimen USNM 5814, and <i>Ornithomimus grandis</i>. Both consist of fragmentary fossils found by <a href="/wiki/John_Bell_Hatcher" title="John Bell Hatcher">John Bell Hatcher</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> of which it is today understood they represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyrannosauroid" title="Tyrannosauroid">tyrannosauroid</a> material. At first Marsh assumed <i>Ornithomimus</i> was an <a href="/wiki/Ornithopod" title="Ornithopod">ornithopod</a> but this changed when Hatcher found specimen USNM 4736, a partial ornithomimid skeleton, in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, which Marsh named <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithomimus_sedens" title="Ornithomimus sedens">Ornithomimus sedens</a></i> in 1892. On that occasion also <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithomimus_minutus" title="Ornithomimus minutus">Ornithomimus minutus</a></i> was created based on specimen YPM 1049, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metatarsus" title="Metatarsus">metatarsus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> since recognized as belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Alvarezsauridae" title="Alvarezsauridae">Alvarezsauridae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holtz2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holtz2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A sixth species, <i>Ornithomimus altus</i>, was named in 1902 by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Lambe" title="Lawrence Lambe">Lawrence Lambe</a>, based on specimen CMN 930, hindlimbs found in 1901 in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> but this was in 1916 renamed to a separate genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Struthiomimus" title="Struthiomimus">Struthiomimus</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn" title="Henry Fairfield Osborn">Henry Fairfield Osborn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1920 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Whitney_Gilmore" title="Charles Whitney Gilmore">Charles Whitney Gilmore</a> named <i>Ornithomimus affinis</i> for <i>Dryosaurus grandis</i> Lull 1911,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> based on indeterminate material. In 1930 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Loris_Russell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Loris Russell (page does not exist)">Loris Russell</a> renamed <i>Struthiomimus brevetertius</i> Parks 1926 and <i>Struthiomimus samueli</i> Parks 1928 into <i>Ornithomimus brevitertius</i> and <i>Ornithomimus samueli</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The same year <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Perry_Hay" title="Oliver Perry Hay">Oliver Perry Hay</a> renamed <i>Aublysodon mirandus</i> Leidy 1868 into <i>Ornithomimus mirandus</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> today seen as a <i><a href="/wiki/Nomen_dubium" title="Nomen dubium">nomen dubium</a></i>. In 1933 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Arthur_Parks" title="William Arthur Parks">William Arthur Parks</a> created a <i>Ornithomimus elegans</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> today seen as either belonging to <i><a href="/wiki/Chirostenotes" title="Chirostenotes">Chirostenotes</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Elmisaurus" title="Elmisaurus">Elmisaurus</a></i>. That same year, Gilmore named <i>Ornithomimus asiaticus</i> for material found in <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1933, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mortram_Sternberg" title="Charles Mortram Sternberg">Charles Mortram Sternberg</a> named the species <i>Ornithomimus edmontonicus</i> for a nearly complete skeleton from the <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Canyon_Formation" title="Horseshoe Canyon Formation">Horseshoe Canyon Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, specimen CMN 8632.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reclassification_by_Dale_Russell">Reclassification by Dale Russell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reclassification by Dale Russell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimid_skull.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2483" data-file-width="3277" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Ornithomimid_skull.jpg/220px-Ornithomimid_skull.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Ornithomimid_skull.jpg/330px-Ornithomimid_skull.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Ornithomimid_skull.jpg/440px-Ornithomimid_skull.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimid_skull.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skull and neck of <i>Ornithomimus</i> sp. (RTMP 95.110.1)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At first it had been common to name each newly discovered ornithomimid as a species of <i>Ornithomimus</i>. In the sixties, this tendency was still strong as is shown by the fact that <a href="/wiki/Oskar_Kuhn" title="Oskar Kuhn">Oskar Kuhn</a> renamed <i>Megalosaurus lonzeensis</i> Dollo 1903 from <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> into <i>Ornithomimus lonzeensis</i> (today understood to be an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abelisauroidea" title="Abelisauroidea">abelisauroid</a> claw),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Dale_Russell" title="Dale Russell">Dale Russell</a> in 1967 renamed <i>Struthiomimus currellii</i> Parks 1933 and <i>Struthiomimus ingens</i> Parks 1933 into <i>Ornithomimus currellii</i> and <i>Ornithomimus ingens</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> At the same time it was usual that workers referred to the entire ornithomimid material as simply "Struthiomimus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> To solve this confusion by scientifically testing the separation between <i>Ornithomimus</i> and <i>Struthiomimus</i>, in 1972 Dale Russell published a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphometry" title="Morphometry">morphometric</a> study showing that statistical differences in some proportions could be used to distinguish the two. He concluded that <i>Struthiomimus</i> and <i>Ornithomimus</i> were valid genera. In the latter Russell recognised two species: the type species <i>Ornithomimus velox</i> and <i>Ornithomimus edmontonicus</i> even though he had trouble reliably distinguishing it from <i>O. velox</i>. <i>Struthiomimus currellii</i> he considered a younger synonym of <i>Ornithomimus edmontonicus</i>. However, Russell also interpreted the data as indicating that many specimens could not be referred to either <i>Ornithomimus</i> or <i>Struthiomimus</i>. Therefore, he created two new genera. The first was <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeornithomimus" title="Archaeornithomimus">Archaeornithomimus</a></i> to which <i>Ornithomimus asiaticus</i> and <i>Ornithomimus affinis</i> were assigned, becoming an <i>Archaeornithomimus asiaticus</i> and an <i>Archaeornithomimus affinis</i>. The second genus was <i><a href="/wiki/Dromiceiomimus" title="Dromiceiomimus">Dromiceiomimus</a></i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">Emu</a> mimic" from the old generic name for the emu, <i>Dromiceius</i>. Russell assigned several former <i>Ornithomimus</i> species named during the 20th century, including <i>O. brevitertius</i> and <i>O. ingens</i>, to the new genus as <i>Dromiceimimus brevitertius</i>. He renamed <i>Ornithomimus samueli</i> into a second <i>Dromiceiomimus</i> species: <i>Dromiceiomimus samueli</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell1972_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-russell1972-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Misassigned_to_Ornithomimus">Misassigned to <i>Ornithomimus</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Misassigned to Ornithomimus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibiae</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Navesink_Formation" title="Navesink Formation">Navesink Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> were named <i><a href="/wiki/Coelosaurus_antiquus" title="Coelosaurus antiquus">Coelosaurus antiquus</a></i> ("antique hollow lizard") by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Leidy" title="Joseph Leidy">Joseph Leidy</a> in 1865. The tibiae were first attributed to <i>Ornithomimus</i> in 1979 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_Baird&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Donald Baird (page does not exist)">Donald Baird</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_R._Horner" title="John R. Horner">John R. Horner</a> as <i>Ornithomimus antiquus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Normally, this would have made <i>Ornithomimus</i> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junior_synonym" title="Junior synonym">junior synonym</a> of <i>Coelosaurus</i>, but Baird and Horner discovered that the name "Coelosaurus" was preoccupied by a <a href="/wiki/Nomen_dubium" title="Nomen dubium">dubious</a> taxon based on a single vertebra, named <i>Coelosaurus</i> by an anonymous author now known to be <a href="/wiki/Richard_Owen" title="Richard Owen">Richard Owen</a> in 1854.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weishampel2004eastcoast_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-weishampel2004eastcoast-24">[24]</a></sup> Baird referred several other specimens from New Jersey and <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> to <i>O. antiquus</i>. Beginning in 1997, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_M._Sullivan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert M. Sullivan (page does not exist)">Robert M. Sullivan</a> regarded <i>O. velox</i> and <i>O. edmontonicus</i> as junior synonyms of <i>O. antiquus</i>. Like Russell, he considered the former two species indistinguishable from each other, and noted that they both shared distinctive features with <i>O. antiquus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sullivan1997_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan1997-25">[25]</a></sup> However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Weishampel" title="David Weishampel">David Weishampel</a> (2004) considered "C." <i>antiquus</i> to be indeterminate among ornithomimosaurs, and therefore a <i><a href="/wiki/Nomen_dubium" title="Nomen dubium">nomen dubium</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weishampel2004eastcoast_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-weishampel2004eastcoast-24">[24]</a></sup> An SVP 2012 abstract agreed with Weishampel by noting that <i>Coelosaurus</i> differs from <i>Gallimimus</i> and <i>Ornithomimus</i> in the features of the tibiae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1988 <a href="/wiki/Gregory_S._Paul" title="Gregory S. Paul">Gregory S. Paul</a> renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Gallimimus" title="Gallimimus">Gallimimus</a> bullatus</i> into <i>Ornithomimus bullatus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This has found no acceptance among other workers and presently the name is not used by Paul himself.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_interpretations">Present interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Present interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_skull.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1142" data-file-width="1807" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Ornithomimus_skull.png/220px-Ornithomimus_skull.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Ornithomimus_skull.png/330px-Ornithomimus_skull.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Ornithomimus_skull.png/440px-Ornithomimus_skull.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_skull.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
T scan of <i>O. edmontonicus</i> skull RTMP 1995.110.0001, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taphonomically" title="Taphonomically">taphonomically</a> deformed bones reconstructed on the right</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Even after Russell's study, various researchers have found reasons to lump some or all of these species back into <i>Ornithomimus</i> in various combinations. In 2004, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Makovicky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Makovicky (page does not exist)">Peter Makovicky</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yoshitsugu_Kobayashi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yoshitsugu Kobayashi (page does not exist)">Yoshitsugu Kobayashi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phil_Currie" title="Phil Currie">Phil Currie</a> studied Russell's 1972 proportional statistics to re-analyze ornithomimid relationships in light of new specimens. They concluded that there was no justification to separate <i>Dromiceiomimus</i> from <i>Ornithomimus</i>, sinking <i>Dromiceiomimus</i> as a synonym of <i>O. edmontonicus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dinosauria2ornithomimid_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-dinosauria2ornithomimid-28">[28]</a></sup> However, they did not include the type species of <i>Ornithomimus</i>, <i>O. velox</i>, in this analysis. The same team further supported the synonymy between <i>Dromiceiomimus</i> and <i>O. edmontonicus</i> in a 2006 lecture at the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Vertebrate_Paleontology" title="Society of Vertebrate Paleontology">Society of Vertebrate Paleontology</a> annual meeting,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-svp2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-svp2006-29">[29]</a></sup> and their opinion has been followed by most later authors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longrich2008_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-longrich2008-30">[30]</a></sup> Makovicky's team also considered <i>Dromiceiomimus samueli</i> to be a junior synonym of <i>O. edmontonicus</i>, though Longrich later suggested it may belong to a distinct, unnamed species from the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_Park_Formation" title="Dinosaur Park Formation">Dinosaur Park Formation</a> which have yet to be described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longrich2008_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-longrich2008-30">[30]</a></sup> Longrich called the species <i>Ornithomimus samueli</i> in a faunal list for the Dinosaur Park Formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from <i>O. edmontonicus</i> dating to the early <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a>, two other species are presently considered to be possibly valid, both from the late Maastrichtian. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithomimus_sedens" title="Ornithomimus sedens">O. sedens</a></i> was named by Marsh in 1891 from partial remains found in the <a href="/wiki/Lance_Formation" title="Lance Formation">Lance Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, only one year after the description of <i>O. velox</i>. Dale Russell, in his 1972 revision of ornithomimids, could not determine which genus it actually belonged to, though he speculated that it may be intermediate between <i>Struthiomimus</i> and <i>Dromiceiomimus</i>. In 1985 he considered it a species of <i>Ornithomimus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell1985_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-russell1985-32">[32]</a></sup> Although it has since been referred to mainly as <i>Struthiomimus sedens</i>, based on complete specimens from <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> (as well as some fragments from Alberta and <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>), these yet have to be described and compared to the <i>O. sedens</i> holotype.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longrich2008_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-longrich2008-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The other is the original type species: <i>O. velox</i>, at first known from very limited remains. Additional specimens referred to <i>O. velox</i> have been described from the Denver Formation and from the <a href="/wiki/Ferris_Formation" title="Ferris Formation">Ferris Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wyomingornithomimus_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-wyomingornithomimus-33">[33]</a></sup> One specimen attributed to <i>O. velox</i> (MNA P1 1762A) from the <a href="/wiki/Kaiparowits_Formation" title="Kaiparowits Formation">Kaiparowits Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, was described in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell1985_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-russell1985-32">[32]</a></sup> Re-evaluation of this specimen by Lindsay Zanno and colleagues in 2010, however, cast doubt on its assignment to <i>O. velox</i>, and possibly even to <i>Ornithomimus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zannoetal2010_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-zannoetal2010-34">[34]</a></sup> This conclusion was supported by a 2015 re-description of <i>O. velox</i>, which found that only the holotype specimen was confidently referable to that species. The authors of this study tentatively referred to the Kaiparowits specimen as <i>Ornithomimus sp.</i>, along with all of the specimens from the Dinosaur Park Formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-velox_redescription_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-velox_redescription-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="481" data-file-width="671" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg/220px-Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg/330px-Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg/440px-Ornithomimus_-_claw.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In 1890 Marsh assigned <i>Ornithomimus</i> to the <a href="/wiki/Ornithomimosauria" title="Ornithomimosauria">Ornithomimosauria</a>, a classification that is still common. Modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cladistic" title="Cladistic">cladistic</a> studies indicate a derived position in the ornithomimids; these however have only included <i>O. edmontonicus</i> in their analyses. The relationships between <i>O. edmontonicus</i>, <i>O. velox</i> and <i>O. sedens</i> have not been published.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="876" data-file-width="700" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg/170px-Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg/255px-Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg/340px-Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dakotaraptor_RPR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An <i>Ornithomimus</i> being restrained while preyed upon by <i><a href="/wiki/Dakotaraptor" title="Dakotaraptor">Dakotaraptor</a></i> (Emily Willoughby, 2015)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The diet of <i>Ornithomimus</i> is still debated. As theropods, ornithomimids might have been carnivorous but their body shape would also have been suited for a partly or largely herbivorous lifestyle. Suggested food includes insects, crustaceans, fruit, leaves, branches, eggs, and the meat of lizards and small mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ageofdinosaursdromiceiomimus_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ageofdinosaursdromiceiomimus-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Ornithomimus</i> had legs that seem clearly suited for rapid locomotion, with the <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibia</a> about 20% longer than the <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">femur</a>. The large eye sockets suggest a keen visual sense, and also suggest the possibility that they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoDP_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoDP-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2001 study conducted by Bruce Rothschild and other paleontologists, 178 foot bones referred to <i>Ornithomimus</i> were examined for signs of <a href="/wiki/Stress_fracture" title="Stress fracture">stress fracture</a>, but none were found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rothschild-dino_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-rothschild-dino-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
