<p><i><b>Dryosaurus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/oʊ/ or /ə/ 'o' in 'omit'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">DRY</span></span></b>-o-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">SAWR</span></span></b>-əs</i></a></span>; meaning 'tree lizard', <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i><b>δρυς/drys</b></i> meaning 'tree, oak' and <i><b>σαυρος/sauros</b></i> meaning 'lizard'; the name reflects the forested habitat, not a vague oak-leaf shape of its cheek teeth as is sometimes assumed) is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Ornithopod" title="Ornithopod">ornithopod</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaur</a> that lived in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Jurassic" title="Late Jurassic">Late Jurassic</a> period. It was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iguanodont" title="Iguanodont">iguanodont</a> (formerly classified as a <a href="/wiki/Hypsilophodont" title="Hypsilophodont">hypsilophodont</a>). Fossils have been found in the western <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and were first discovered in the late 19th century. <i><a href="/wiki/Valdosaurus" title="Valdosaurus">Valdosaurus canaliculatus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dysalotosaurus" title="Dysalotosaurus">Dysalotosaurus lettowvorbecki</a></i> were both formerly considered to represent species of <i>Dryosaurus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H.C3.BCbner.26Rauhut_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-H.C3.BCbner.26Rauhut-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDonald_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDonald-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dryosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Driozaur_(Dryosaurus)_Baltow_(1).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="2800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Driozaur_%28Dryosaurus%29_Baltow_%281%29.JPG/220px-Driozaur_%28Dryosaurus%29_Baltow_%281%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Driozaur_%28Dryosaurus%29_Baltow_%281%29.JPG/330px-Driozaur_%28Dryosaurus%29_Baltow_%281%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Driozaur_%28Dryosaurus%29_Baltow_%281%29.JPG/440px-Driozaur_%28Dryosaurus%29_Baltow_%281%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><i>Dryosaurus</i> had a long neck, long, slender legs and a long, stiff tail. Its arms, however, with five fingers on each hand, were short. Known specimens were about 8 to 14 feet (2.4 to 4.3 m) long and weighed 170 to 200 pounds (77 to 91 kg). However, the adult size is unknown, as no known adult specimens of the genus have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JRFetal09_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-JRFetal09-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Dryosaurus</i> had a horny beak and <a href="/wiki/Cheek_teeth" title="Cheek teeth">cheek teeth</a> and, like other ornithopods, was a <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivore</a>. Some scientists suggest that it had cheek-like structures to prevent the loss of food while the animal processed it in the mouth.</p>
<p>A quick and agile runner with strong legs, <i>Dryosaurus</i> used its stiff tail as a counterbalance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoDP_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoDP-5">[5]</a></sup> It probably relied on its speed as a main defense against <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivorous</a> dinosaurs.</p>

<p>The teeth of <i>Dryosaurus</i> were, according to museum curator John Foster, characterized by "a strong median ridge on the lateral surface."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster-dryosaurus_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-foster-dryosaurus-6">[6]</a></sup><i>Dryosaurus</i> subsisted primarily on low growing vegetation in ancient floodplains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster-dryosaurus_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-foster-dryosaurus-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_development">Growth and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dryosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i>Dryosaurus</i> hatchling found at Dinosaur National Monument in Utah confirmed that <i>Dryosaurus</i> followed similar patterns of craniofacial development to other vertebrates; the eyes were proportionally large while young and the muzzle proportionally short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster-dryosaurus_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-foster-dryosaurus-6">[6]</a></sup> As the animal grew, its eyes became proportionally smaller and its snout proportionally longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster-dryosaurus_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-foster-dryosaurus-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dryosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dryosaurus_limb.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1666" data-file-width="1033" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Dryosaurus_limb.png/170px-Dryosaurus_limb.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Dryosaurus_limb.png/255px-Dryosaurus_limb.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Dryosaurus_limb.png/340px-Dryosaurus_limb.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dryosaurus_limb.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pelvis, leg, and tooth</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1876, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Wendell_Williston" title="Samuel Wendell Williston">Samuel Wendell Williston</a> in <a href="/wiki/Albany_County,_Wyoming" title="Albany County, Wyoming">Albany County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> discovered the remains of small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euornithopoda" title="Euornithopoda">euornithopods</a>. In 1878, Professor <a href="/wiki/Othniel_Charles_Marsh" title="Othniel Charles Marsh">Othniel Charles Marsh</a> named these as a new species of <i><a href="/wiki/Laosaurus" title="Laosaurus">Laosaurus</a></i>, <i>Laosaurus altus</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> <i>altus</i>, meaning "tall" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, refers to it being larger than <i>Laosaurus celer</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1894, Marsh made the taxon a separate genus, <i>Dryosaurus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The generic name is derived from the Greek δρῦς, <i>drys</i>, "tree, oak", referring to a presumed forest-dwelling life mode. Later it was often assumed to have been named after an oak-leaf shape of its cheek teeth, which, however, is absent. The <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> remains <i>Laosaurus altus</i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Combinatio_nova" title="Combinatio nova">combinatio nova</a></i> is <i>Dryosaurus altus</i>.</p>
<p>The holotype, <b>YPM 1876</b>, was found in a layer of the Upper Brushy Basin Member of the <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Formation" title="Morrison Formation">Morrison Formation</a>, dating from the <a href="/wiki/Tithonian" title="Tithonian">Tithonian</a>. It consists of a partial skeleton including a rather complete skull and lower jaws. Several other fossils from Wyoming have been referred to <i>Dryosaurus altus</i>. They include specimens YPM 1884: the rear half of a skeleton; AMNH 834: a partial skeleton lacking the skull from the <i><a href="/wiki/Bone_Cabin_Quarry" title="Bone Cabin Quarry">Bone Cabin Quarry</a></i>; and CM 1949: a rear half of a skeleton dug up in 1905 by William H. Utterback. From 1922 onwards in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Earl_Douglass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Earl Douglass (page does not exist)">Earl Douglass</a> discovered <i>Dryosaurus</i> remains at the <i><a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_National_Monument" title="Dinosaur National Monument">Dinosaur National Monument</a></i>. These include CM 11340: the front half of a skeleton of a very young individual; CM 3392: a skeleton with skull but lacking the tail; CM 11337: a fragmentary skeleton of a juvenile; and DNM 1016: a left ilium dug up by technician Jim Adams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other fossils were found in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. In <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lily_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lily Park (page does not exist)">Lily Park</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moffat_County" title="Moffat County">Moffat County</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Leroy_Kay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Leroy Kay (page does not exist)">James Leroy Kay</a> and Albert C. Lloyd in 1955 recovered CM 21786, a skeleton lacking skull and neck. From 'Scheetz’ Quarry 1, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uravan" title="Uravan">Uravan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montrose_County" title="Montrose County">Montrose County</a>, in 1973 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Malcolm_Galton" title="Peter Malcolm Galton">Peter Malcolm Galton</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_A._Jensen" title="James A. Jensen">James Alvin Jensen</a> described specimen BYU ESM-171R found by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rodney_Dwayne_Scheetz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rodney Dwayne Scheetz (page does not exist)">Rodney Dwayne Scheetz</a> and consisting of some vertebrae, a left lower jaw, a left forelimb and two hindlimbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gregory_S._Paul" title="Gregory S. Paul">Gregory S. Paul</a> in 2010 suggested that the Utah material represented a separate species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paul2010-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from <i>Dryosaurus altus</i>, several other species have been named in the genus. The first of these was created accidentally when in 1903 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giuseppe_de_Stefano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giuseppe de Stefano (page does not exist)">Giuseppe de Stefano</a> renamed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crocodilus" title="Crocodilus">Crocodilus</a> phosphaticus</i> Thomas 1893 into <i>Dryosaurus phosphaticus</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> he had intended to call it <i><a href="/wiki/Dyrosaurus" title="Dyrosaurus">Dyrosaurus</a> phosphaticus</i>. This was only emended by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ric_Buffetaut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Éric Buffetaut (page does not exist)">Éric Buffetaut</a> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoecology">Paleoecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dryosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paleoecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Dryosaurus</i> <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> specimen <b>YPM 1876</b> was discovered in Reed’s YPM Quarry 5, in the Upper Brushy Basin Member, of the <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Formation" title="Morrison Formation">Morrison Formation</a>. In the Late Jurassic Morrison formation of Western North America, <i>Dryosaurus</i> remains have been recovered from stratigraphic zones 2-6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster-appendix_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-foster-appendix-14">[14]</a></sup> A spectacular digsite near Uravan, Colorado held hundreds of <i>D. altus</i> fossils which represented multiple stages of the animal's life cycle. This formation is a sequence of shallow marine and alluvial sediments which, according to <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a>, ranges between 156.3 million years old (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mega-annum" title="Mega-annum">Ma</a>) at its base,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR06_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR06-15">[15]</a></sup> to 146.8 million years old at the top,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAB98_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAB98-16">[16]</a></sup> which places it in the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxfordian_stage" title="Oxfordian stage">Oxfordian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kimmeridgian" title="Kimmeridgian">Kimmeridgian</a>, and early <a href="/wiki/Tithonian" title="Tithonian">Tithonian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faunal_stages" title="Faunal stages">stages</a> of the Late Jurassic period. In 1877 this formation became the center of the <a href="/wiki/Bone_Wars" title="Bone Wars">Bone Wars</a>, a fossil-collecting rivalry between early paleontologists <a href="/wiki/Othniel_Charles_Marsh" title="Othniel Charles Marsh">Othniel Charles Marsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">Edward Drinker Cope</a>. The Morrison Formation is interpreted as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiarid" title="Semiarid">semiarid</a> environment with distinct <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry seasons</a>. The Morrison Basin where dinosaurs lived, stretched from New Mexico to Alberta and Saskatchewan, and was formed when the precursors to the <a href="/wiki/Front_Range" title="Front Range">Front Range</a> of the Rocky Mountains started pushing up to the west. The deposits from their east-facing <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a> were carried by streams and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and deposited in <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swampy</a> lowlands, lakes, river channels and <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAR89_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAR89-17">[17]</a></sup> This formation is similar in age to the <a href="/wiki/Solnhofen_limestone" title="Solnhofen limestone">Solnhofen Limestone</a> Formation in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tendaguru" title="Tendaguru">Tendaguru Formation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.</p>

<p>The Morrison Formation records an environment and time dominated by gigantic sauropod dinosaurs such as <i><a href="/wiki/Camarasaurus" title="Camarasaurus">Camarasaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Barosaurus" title="Barosaurus">Barosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diplodocus" title="Diplodocus">Diplodocus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Apatosaurus" title="Apatosaurus">Apatosaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Brachiosaurus" title="Brachiosaurus">Brachiosaurus</a></i>. Dinosaurs that lived alongside <i>Dryosaurus</i> included the herbivorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithischians" title="Ornithischians">ornithischians</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Camptosaurus" title="Camptosaurus">Camptosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stegosaurus" title="Stegosaurus">Stegosaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Othnielosaurus" title="Othnielosaurus">Othnielosaurus</a></i>. Predators in this paleoenvironment included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropods" title="Theropods">theropods</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Saurophaganax" title="Saurophaganax">Saurophaganax</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Torvosaurus" title="Torvosaurus">Torvosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratosaurus" title="Ceratosaurus">Ceratosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Marshosaurus" title="Marshosaurus">Marshosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stokesosaurus" title="Stokesosaurus">Stokesosaurus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ornitholestes" title="Ornitholestes">Ornitholestes</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Allosaurus" title="Allosaurus">Allosaurus</a></i>, which accounting for 70 to 75% of theropod specimens and was at the top <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a> of the Morrison <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food web</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JRF03a_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-JRF03a-19">[19]</a></sup> Other vertebrates that shared this paleoenvironment included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Actinopterygii" title="Actinopterygii">ray-finned fishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphenodontia" title="Sphenodontia">sphenodonts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, terrestrial and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Crocodylomorpha" title="Crocodylomorpha">crocodylomorphans</a>, and several species of <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaur</a>. Early mammals were present in this region, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Docodonts" title="Docodonts">docodonts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multituberculates" title="Multituberculates">multituberculates</a>, symmetrodonts, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triconodonts" title="Triconodonts">triconodonts</a>. The flora of the period has been revealed by fossils of <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyta" title="Chlorophyta">green algae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equisetum" title="Equisetum">horsetails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">ginkgoes</a>, and several families of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>. Vegetation varied from <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>-lining forests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_fern" title="Tree fern">tree ferns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gallery_forest" title="Gallery forest">gallery forests</a>), to fern <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a> with occasional trees such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria" title="Araucaria">Araucaria</a></i>-like conifer <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyphyllum" title="Brachyphyllum">Brachyphyllum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KC06_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-KC06-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other sites that have produced <i>Dryosaurus</i> material include Bone Cabin Quarry, the Red Fork of the Powder River in Wyoming and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lily_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lily Park (page does not exist)">Lily Park</a> in Colorado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foster-dryosaurus_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-foster-dryosaurus-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
