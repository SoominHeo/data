<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dmanisi</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Dmanisi</b> (<a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka">დმანისი</span>, <span style="font-size: smaller;"><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Georgian" title="Romanization of Georgian">translit.</a>:</span> <span lang="ka-Latn" style="font-style: italic;">dmanisi</span>; <small>Georgian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Georgian" title="Help:IPA for Georgian">[dmɑnisi]</a></span>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Townlet" title="Townlet">townlet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological site</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kvemo_Kartli" title="Kvemo Kartli">Kvemo Kartli</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a> approximately 93 km southwest of the nation’s capital <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a> in the river valley of Mashavera. The hominin site is the earliest of its kind outside of Africa, dating back to 1.81 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaannum" title="Megaannum">Ma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garcia_2010_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia_2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmanisi_skull" title="Dmanisi skull">series of skulls</a> from Dmanisi, discovered in the early 2010s, led to the hypothesis that many separate species in the <i>Homo</i> genus were in fact a single lineage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Also known as Skull 5, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmanisi_skull" title="Dmanisi skull">D4500</a> is the fifth skull to be discovered in Dmanisi.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dmanisi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town of Dmanisi is first mentioned in the 9th century as a possession of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Tbilisi" title="Emirate of Tbilisi">emirate of Tbilisi</a>, though the area had been settled since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox Christian</a> cathedral – “<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dmanisi_Sioni_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dmanisi Sioni Cathedral (page does not exist)">Dmansis Sioni</a>” – was built there in the 6th century. Located on the confluence of trading routes and cultural influences, Dmanisi was of particular importance, growing into a major commercial center of medieval Georgia. The town was conquered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk Turks</a> in the 1080s, but was later liberated by the Georgian kings <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_the_Builder" title="David the Builder">David the Builder</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demetre_I_of_Georgia" title="Demetre I of Georgia">Demetrios I</a> between 1123 and 1125. The <a href="/wiki/Turco-Mongol" title="Turco-Mongol">Turco-Mongol</a> armies under <a href="/wiki/Timur" title="Timur">Timur</a> laid waste to the town in the 14th century. Sacked again by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkoman_invasions_of_Georgia" title="Turkoman invasions of Georgia">Turkomans</a> in 1486, Dmanisi never recovered and declined to a scarcely inhabited village by the 18th century. The castle was controlled by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Orbeliani" title="House of Orbeliani">House of Orbeliani</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_site">Archaeological site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dmanisi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeological site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GeorgieKaart_met_Dmanisi.png"><img alt="GeorgieKaart met Dmanisi.png" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="636" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/GeorgieKaart_met_Dmanisi.png/390px-GeorgieKaart_met_Dmanisi.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/GeorgieKaart_met_Dmanisi.png/585px-GeorgieKaart_met_Dmanisi.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/GeorgieKaart_met_Dmanisi.png 2x" width="390"/></a></div>
<p>Extensive archaeological studies began in the area in 1936 and continued in the 1960s. Beyond a rich collection of ancient and medieval artifacts and the ruins of various buildings and structures, unique remains of prehistoric animals and humans have been unearthed. Some of the animal bones were identified by the Georgian paleontologist A. Vekua with the teeth of the extinct rhino <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dicerorhinus_etruscus" title="Dicerorhinus etruscus">Dicerorhinus etruscus</a> etruscus</i> in 1983. This species dates back presumably to the early <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> epoch.</p>
<p>The discovery of primitive stone tools in 1984 led to increasing interest to the archaeological site. In 1991, a team of Georgian scholars was joined by the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> archaeologists from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%B6misch-Germanisches_Zentralmuseum" title="Römisch-Germanisches Zentralmuseum">Römisch-Germanisches Zentralmuseum</a>, and later the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> researchers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homo_erectus_georgicus"><i>Homo erectus georgicus</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dmanisi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Homo erectus georgicus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_erectus_georgicus" title="Homo erectus georgicus">Homo erectus georgicus</a></div>
<p>Early human (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominin" title="Hominin">hominin</a>) fossils, originally named <i>Homo georgicus</i> and now considered <i>Homo erectus georgicus</i>, were found at Dmanisi between 1991 and 2005. At 1.8 million years old, they are now believed to be a subspecies of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> and not a separate species of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo" title="Homo">Homo</a></i>. These fossils represent the earliest known human presence in the Caucasus.</p>
<p>Subsequently, four fossil skeletons were found, showing a species primitive in its skull and upper body but with relatively advanced spines and lower limbs. They are now thought<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to represent a stage soon after the transition from <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus" title="Australopithecus">Australopithecus</a></i> to <i>Homo erectus</i>.</p>
<p>Human habitation in the Caucasus goes back to the remotest antiquity. The hominin remains discovered in 1991 by <a href="/wiki/David_Lordkipanidze" title="David Lordkipanidze">David Lordkipanidze</a> at Dmanisi, <a href="/wiki/Kvemo_Kartli" title="Kvemo Kartli">Kvemo Kartli</a> (1.8 million years old) are the oldest found outside of Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a> remains have been found at Ortvale K’lde (1973) and elsewhere in the Caucasus (36,000–50,000 years old).</p>
<p>The Dmanisi hominin remains are still making an impact on the paleontological community. As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dmanisi&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmanisi_skull" title="Dmanisi skull">Dmanisi skull 5</a> is in the middle of a controversy: many hominin fossils formerly thought to be different species may not have been separate species at all. Several early members of the genus Homo were possibly one evolving <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(evolution)" title="Lineage (evolution)">lineage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
