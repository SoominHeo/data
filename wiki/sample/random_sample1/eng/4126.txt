<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Demi-glace</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Demi-glace</b> (English: "half glaze") is a rich <a href="/wiki/Brown_sauce_(meat_stock_based)" title="Brown sauce (meat stock based)">brown sauce</a> in <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French cuisine</a> used by itself or as a base for other sauces. The term comes from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word <i>glace</i>, which, used in reference to a sauce, means <i>icing</i> or <i>glaze</i>. It is traditionally made by combining equal parts of <a href="/wiki/Veal" title="Veal">veal</a> <a href="/wiki/Stock_(food)" title="Stock (food)">stock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Espagnole_sauce" title="Espagnole sauce">espagnole sauce</a>, the latter being one of the five <a href="/wiki/Sauce#French_cuisine" title="Sauce">mother sauces</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisine_classique" title="Cuisine classique">classical French cuisine</a>, and the mixture is then simmered and reduced by half.</p>
<p>Common variants of demi-glace use a 1:1 mixture of beef or chicken stock to sauce espagnole; these are referred to as "beef demi-glace" (<i>demi-glace au boeuf</i>) or "chicken demi-glace" (<i>demi-glace au poulet</i>). The term "demi-glace" by itself implies that it is made with the traditional veal stock.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demi-glace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic recipe for demi-glace is provided by the French chef <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Escoffier" title="Auguste Escoffier">Auguste Escoffier</a>, who is often considered to have established the method of French cooking as well as codified many of the standard French recipes. Although many recipes for demi-glace give the preparation for the espagnole first, and then the recipe for the brown stock, preparation should actually proceed in the reverse.</p>
<p>A basic brown stock should be prepared and when completed, left on the heat to remain very warm. At this point the espagnole is prepared, and when it is finished the brown stock is added in equal portions to the espagnole. Demi-glace keeps very well, about six months refrigerated or almost indefinitely frozen.</p>
<p>Due to the considerable effort involved in making the traditional demi-glace, it is common for chefs to substitute a simple jus lié of veal stock or to create a simulated version, which the American cookbook author <a href="/wiki/Julia_Child" title="Julia Child">Julia Child</a> referred to as a "semi-demi-glace". However, even today, many chefs who prepare French <a href="/wiki/Haute_cuisine" title="Haute cuisine">haute cuisine</a> use a demi-glace prepared in their own kitchen. Concentrates and mixes, mostly available to professional kitchens, are another way of avoiding the labor inherent in preparing the sauce.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demi-glace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Demi-glace was humorously featured in popular TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Exposure" title="Northern Exposure">Northern Exposure</a></i>, season 4, episode "The Big Feast". The recipe for the sauce was exaggerated to the point of boiling down the bones of forty cows to produce the super luxurious glace.</p>
