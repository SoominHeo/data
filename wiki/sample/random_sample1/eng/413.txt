<p>The <b>Opening Ceremony</b> of the <b><a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010 Winter Olympics</a></b> was held on February 12, 2010 beginning at 6:00 pm <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Time_Zone" title="Pacific Time Zone">PST</a> (02:00 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>, February 13) at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BC_Place_Stadium" title="BC Place Stadium">BC Place Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. This was the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_opening_ceremony" title="Olympic opening ceremony">Olympic opening ceremony</a> to be held indoors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter_Olympics_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winter_Olympics-1">[1]</a></sup> It was directed by <a href="/wiki/David_Atkins" title="David Atkins">David Atkins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xinhua_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The event was officially opened by <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General</a> <a href="/wiki/Micha%C3%ABlle_Jean" title="Michaëlle Jean">Michaëlle Jean</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GovernorGeneralFamily_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GovernorGeneralFamily-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> representative of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Canada" title="Monarchy of Canada">Queen of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The opening ceremony was dedicated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver_Organizing_Committee_for_the_2010_Olympic_and_Paralympic_Winter_Games" title="Vancouver Organizing Committee for the 2010 Olympic and Paralympic Winter Games">Vancouver Organizing Committee</a> (VANOC) to <a href="/wiki/Nodar_Kumaritashvili" title="Nodar Kumaritashvili">Nodar Kumaritashvili</a>, a Georgian <a href="/wiki/Luge" title="Luge">luger</a> who died earlier in the day in a training run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter_Olympics_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Winter_Olympics-1">[1]</a></sup> An audience of 61,600 were in attendance at the venue. There were an estimated 4,500 performers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter_Olympics_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Winter_Olympics-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olympics_begin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olympics_begin-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The production's director was <a href="/wiki/David_Atkins" title="David Atkins">David Atkins</a>, who directed the <a href="/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics_opening_ceremony" title="2000 Summer Olympics opening ceremony">Sydney 2000 Olympic</a> and <a href="/wiki/2006_Asian_Games" title="2006 Asian Games">2006 Doha Asian Games</a> ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xinhua_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OC15Feb2010_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OC15Feb2010-7">[7]</a></sup> The Opening and Closing Ceremonies had a combined budget of $48.5 million (it received $20 million (<span style="white-space: nowrap">CA$</span>) funding from the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Canadian_Heritage" title="Department of Canadian Heritage">Department of Canadian Heritage</a> while VANOC contributed the rest).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OC15Feb2010_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-OC15Feb2010-7">[7]</a></sup> Much of the instrumental music for the ceremony was written by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Pierce" title="Dave Pierce">Dave Pierce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Greenaway" title="Gavin Greenaway">Gavin Greenaway</a>, and <a class="external text" href="http://www.cpo-live.com/main/biography.php?id=12" rel="nofollow">Donovan Seidle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Music_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Music-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The music during the ceremony came from the live on-stage orchestra, conducted by Pierce.</p>
<p>On December 15, 2010, <a href="/wiki/John_Furlong_(sports_administrator)" title="John Furlong (sports administrator)">John Furlong</a>, the CEO of VANOC revealed that <a href="/wiki/Celine_Dion" title="Celine Dion">Celine Dion</a> had been contracted to sing the national anthem at the Opening Ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, when she became pregnant she cancelled the performance and was subsequently replaced by 16-year-old <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Yanofsky" title="Nikki Yanofsky">Nikki Yanofsky</a>. In addition, Furlong also revealed that "a famous Quebec composer" was contracted to provide musical elements to the show. That composer, whom he would not name, backed out of arrangement months before the Games over what he termed "philosophical differences." As a result, the artist refused to allow VANOC access to his music's rights and the organizing committee had to unwind part of the ceremony. There is speculation within the media that the Opening Ceremony's cultural show finale titled "We Are More," featuring slam poetry by <a href="/wiki/Shane_Koyczan" title="Shane Koyczan">Shane Koyczan</a>, served as a last minute replacement for the canceled segment featuring the Quebec composer's music. VANOC had also requested the <a href="/wiki/Cirque_du_Soleil" title="Cirque du Soleil">Cirque du Soleil</a> to perform several aerial stunts, however it opted not to as it was already stretched with many U.S. productions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Program">Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedication">Dedication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dedication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At 5:59 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Standard_Time" title="Pacific Standard Time">PST</a>), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_address" title="Public address">PA system</a> announced that the opening ceremony would be dedicated in memory of the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="Georgia at the 2010 Winter Olympics">Georgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Luge" title="Luge">luger</a> <a href="/wiki/Nodar_Kumaritashvili" title="Nodar Kumaritashvili">Nodar Kumaritashvili</a>, who had died in a training accident earlier that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_Ceremony_timeline_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Opening_Ceremony_timeline-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_section">Opening section</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opening section">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Giant video screens<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vancouver_Olympic_opening_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vancouver_Olympic_opening-11">[11]</a></sup> showed Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowboarder" title="Snowboarder">snowboarder</a> Johnny Lyall sliding down a mountain slope, with the dates and locations of previous Winter Olympic games were recalled in voiceover. As the 1988 games in Calgary were mentioned, Lyall passed through a row of torchbearers in the shape of the Canadian symbol: the <a href="/wiki/Maple_leaf" title="Maple leaf">maple leaf</a>. This concluded with Lyall leaping through a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_Rings" title="Olympic Rings">Olympic Rings</a>, while snow and ice exploded off them and into the stadium, and him welcoming the crowd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Despite_luger.27s_death_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Despite_luger.27s_death-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_anthem">National anthem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: National anthem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A guard of honour mounted by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">RCMP</a> marched the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Canada" title="Flag of Canada">Canadian Flag</a> to the flagpost. There a guard of honour composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> members raised the flag. <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Yanofsky" title="Nikki Yanofsky">Nikki Yanofsky</a> performed an arrangement of the national anthem, "<a href="/wiki/O_Canada" title="O Canada">O Canada</a>", singing in English and French.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> in whose traditional territories the games were held - the <a href="/wiki/Squamish_Nation" title="Squamish Nation">Squamish Nation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musqueam_Indian_Band" title="Musqueam Indian Band">Musqueam Indian Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27wat_First_Nation" title="Lil'wat First Nation">Lil'wat First Nation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tsleil-Waututh_First_Nation" title="Tsleil-Waututh First Nation">Tsleil-Waututh First Nation</a> - were recognized as heads of state and seated directly behind the Canadian Governor General and the Prime Minister. Four Coast Salish welcome poles were raised from the centre of the stadium, and greetings were given to the crowd (and the world) by members of the Four Host First Nations in their respective languages as well as English and French. The arms of the poles were raised in a traditional gesture of greeting to welcome the athletes and the world. Following the greetings, groups of dancers from other main culture-regions of aboriginal societies within Canada were introduced, including the Métis Nation and the Inuit, as well as the Peoples of the Northwest, the Peoples of the Plains and the Peoples of the East and took places around the welcome poles and a large drum surface between them, forming a welcome circle to prepare for the forthcoming Parade of the Nations and danced traditional welcoming dances as the athletes paraded in.</p>

<p>The participating countries marched in, with <a href="/wiki/Greece_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="Greece at the 2010 Winter Olympics">Greece</a> coming first, then the other nations ending with the host nation, <a href="/wiki/Canada_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="Canada at the 2010 Winter Olympics">Canada</a>. The names of the nations were announced first in French and followed by English, the official languages of the Olympics, which also happened to be the official languages of the host nation. The nations entered in alphabetic order of their country names in English because it is the more dominant of the two languages in Vancouver and in the province of British Columbia.</p>
<p>The team from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="Georgia at the 2010 Winter Olympics">Georgia</a> was greeted with a standing ovation out of respect for their colleague, <a href="/wiki/Nodar_Kumaritashvili" title="Nodar Kumaritashvili">Nodar Kumaritashvili</a>, who died in a luge accident earlier that day. The team left an empty space in the processional and left the stadium immediately following the procession. They had indicated they would not participate in the opening ceremony or withdraw completely, but decided against doing so. The team wore black scarves and armbands to honor Kumaritashvili while a black ribbon was affixed to the team's flag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-13">[13]</a></sup> Teams from some countries, including <a href="/wiki/Australia_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="Australia at the 2010 Winter Olympics">Australia</a> also wore black armbands in respect of Kumaritashvili.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_armband_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black_armband-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/China_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="China at the 2010 Winter Olympics">China</a>, which hosted the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">last Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, also got a standing ovation due to the large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Canadian" title="Chinese Canadian">Chinese population in Vancouver</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_Ceremony_timeline_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Opening_Ceremony_timeline-10">[10]</a></sup> as did the <a href="/wiki/United_States_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="United States at the 2010 Winter Olympics">United States</a>, which received the loudest ovation before Canada entered two minutes later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_Ceremony_timeline_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Opening_Ceremony_timeline-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Landscape_of_a_Dream">The Landscape of a Dream</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Landscape of a Dream">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cultural section of the Opening Ceremony was titled "The Landscape of a Dream" whose purpose was to celebrate the diverse geography and people of Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_Ceremony_timeline_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Opening_Ceremony_timeline-10">[10]</a></sup> It was directed by <a href="/wiki/David_Atkins" title="David Atkins">David Atkins</a> and the narration was provided by <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sutherland" title="Donald Sutherland">Donald Sutherland</a>, himself one of the Olympic flag bearers. It featured tributes to different regions of Canada.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hymns_of_the_North">Hymns of the North</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hymns of the North">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>A Tribute to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Canada" title="Northern Canada">Northern Canada</a> After Nelly Furtado and Bryan Adams had finished their performance, snow began to fall throughout the stadium. Performers, dressed in warm, Inuit-like clothes, walked about and mingled on the stage floor. Their leader took his staff and banged it on the ground, producing waves of light rippling on the floor. The northern lights and different constellations of animals appeared. A giant, sparkling puppet (one of the largest puppets ever created) of a spirit or <a href="/wiki/Kermode_bear" title="Kermode bear">Kermode bear</a> that rose from the stadium floor, and hovered over the performers, who were standing on a simulated ice floe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanCourier_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-VanCourier-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPNReilly_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESPNReilly-18">[18]</a></sup> After a few seconds, the ice began to break up, and the performers "floated" to the edge of the stage, where they disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voices.yahoo.com_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-voices.yahoo.com-19">[19]</a></sup> The ice breaking away gave way to a huge arctic sea, where simulated whales swam while breathing until next transition.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacred_Grove">Sacred Grove</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sacred Grove">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>Opened with brown Coast Salish welcome poles rising up, symbolizing welcome. Red salmon rose from the floor to the ceiling via the poles, representing salmon spawning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Foliage appeared on top of the poles, symbolizing a lush grove of trees. Dancers from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alberta_Ballet" title="Alberta Ballet">Alberta Ballet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ballet_BC" title="Ballet BC">Ballet BC</a> assembled around the grove as a quote from <i>My Heart Soars</i> by <a href="/wiki/Chief_Dan_George" title="Chief Dan George">Chief Dan George</a> was read. <a href="/wiki/Sarah_McLachlan" title="Sarah McLachlan">Sarah McLachlan</a>, herself a Vancouver resident though born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halifax_Regional_Municipality" title="Halifax Regional Municipality">Halifax</a>, performed her song "Ordinary Miracle" while the dancers danced in and around the grove. The ballet dancers also danced to "<a href="/wiki/Adagio_for_Strings" title="Adagio for Strings">Adagio for Strings</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Barber" title="Samuel Barber">Samuel Barber</a> as the grove turned into a starry sky. All turned white and the performers rose to the ceiling after this piece finished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Despite_luger.27s_death_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Despite_luger.27s_death-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhythms_of_the_Fall">Rhythms of the Fall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rhythms of the Fall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This tribute to the fiddling traditions of Canada as well as <a href="/wiki/French_Canadians" title="French Canadians">French Canadians</a>, particularly those of the Maritime Provinces, began with the appearance of the horned fiddler in a flying canoe, a reference to the story of the <a href="/wiki/Chasse-galerie" title="Chasse-galerie">Chasse-galerie</a>. As the canoe descended from the ceiling, the opening of the song "The Old Ways" was performed by <a href="/wiki/Loreena_McKennitt" title="Loreena McKennitt">Loreena McKennitt</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>Fiddler/aerialist Colin Maier duelled with his shadow that appears on the moon. The stage was here covered with big red maple leaves. The song played during this sequence is the start of "The Old Ways",</li>
<li>There were six lead "Hero Fiddlers" on the centre stage, each fiddling a certain style of Canadian fiddling: Andre Brunet (Quebec/French Canadian), <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Lapp" title="Daniel Lapp">Daniel Lapp</a> (Pacific Northwest), <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Noble" title="Sierra Noble">Sierra Noble</a> (Métis), Samantha Robichard (Acadian/Cape Breton), <a href="/wiki/April_Verch" title="April Verch">April Verch</a> (Prairies) and <a href="/wiki/Ashley_MacIsaac" title="Ashley MacIsaac">Ashley MacIsaac</a> (Cape Breton). Dancers around the stage performed the respective styles of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_stepdance" title="Canadian stepdance">Canadian stepdance</a> to each fiddler's style of fiddling.</li>
<li>A tap dancer, Brock Jellison, called a river to encircle the main podium.</li>
</ul>
<p>At some point in this section, <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Vollrath" title="Calvin Vollrath">Calvin Vollrath</a> who was sitting next to the orchestra, performed "Fiddle Nation", a tune he'd composed. This portion of the ceremony was concluded by <a href="/wiki/Ashley_MacIsaac" title="Ashley MacIsaac">Ashley MacIsaac</a> who performed his version of the traditional nineteenth century <a href="/wiki/Strathspey_(dance)" title="Strathspey (dance)">strathspey</a> "Devil in the Kitchen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thesession_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-thesession-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Who_Has_Seen_the_Wind">Who Has Seen the Wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Who Has Seen the Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"Who Has Seen the Wind" was a tribute to Canadian Prairies. A lone boy, Thomas Saulgrain (L'École Nationale de Cirque) stood in the centre of the floor in a square wheat. A voice quoted from the novel <a href="/wiki/Who_Has_Seen_the_Wind_(novel)" title="Who Has Seen the Wind (novel)">Who Has Seen the Wind (novel)</a> by <a href="/wiki/W._O._Mitchell" title="W. O. Mitchell">W. O. Mitchell</a> As the boy began running, he was lifted up and performed ballet on fly wire. A beautiful rendition of the song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Both_Sides,_Now_(song)" title="Both Sides, Now (song)">Both Sides, Now</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Joni_Mitchell" title="Joni Mitchell">Joni Mitchell</a> was played during this segment. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voices.yahoo.com_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-voices.yahoo.com-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>"Peaks of Endeavour" was a tribute to the Canadian Rockies and Western Canada. After the previous segment had ended, a storm was stimulated and clouds seemed to fall to the ground. As the clouds gave away, mountains, resembling the Rocky Mountains, rose from the floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voices.yahoo.com_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-voices.yahoo.com-19">[19]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/George_Vancouver" title="George Vancouver">George Vancouver</a> quote was read by Donald Sutherland, and skiers and snowboarders hung from wires to simulate going down the artificial mountains. Images of winter sports in action exploded onto the mountains, which finally gave way to sports victories. Inline skaters mimicked figure skaters and speed skaters, circling the artificial mountains as the videos of sports changed to the Vancouver skyline. Coloured lines of light representing flowing traffic surrounded the mountain.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="We_Are_More">We Are More</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: We Are More">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

(<a class="external text" href="http://www.vancouversun.com/news/More+Transcript+opening+ceremony+poem+Shane+Koyczan/2558526/story.html" rel="nofollow">transcript</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_Ceremony_timeline_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Opening_Ceremony_timeline-10">[10]</a></sup> Performers formed a giant maple leaf around Koyczan with red flares to end off his section.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_remarks.2FOfficial_Opening">Opening remarks/Official Opening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Opening remarks/Official Opening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The opening remarks began with <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Rogge" title="Jacques Rogge">Jacques Rogge</a>, President of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">IOC</a>, who offered sympathy for the loss of Georgian luge athlete, <a href="/wiki/Nodar_Kumaritashvili" title="Nodar Kumaritashvili">Nodar Kumaritashvili</a>. A welcome from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Furlong_(Canadian)" title="John Furlong (Canadian)">John Furlong</a>, Chair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver_Organizing_Committee_for_the_2010_Olympic_and_Paralympic_Winter_Games" title="Vancouver Organizing Committee for the 2010 Olympic and Paralympic Winter Games">the Vancouver Organizing Committee</a>, was then delivered. (<a class="external text" href="http://www.vancouver2010.com/olympic-spectator-guide/celebrations-and-ceremonies/ceremonies/opening-ceremonies/opening-ceremonies---john-furlong_274284dm.html" rel="nofollow">transcript</a>) A statement by Rogge followed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_armband_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Black_armband-14">[14]</a></sup> mixing English and French. <p><a href="/wiki/Rick_Hansen" title="Rick Hansen">Rick Hansen</a>, paraplegic athlete and paralympic medalist, carried the flame into BC Place stadium and lit the torch of speed skater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catriona_Le_May_Doan" title="Catriona Le May Doan">Catriona Le May Doan</a>, who in turn lit the torch of basketball All-Star <a href="/wiki/Steve_Nash" title="Steve Nash">Steve Nash</a>. Nash then lit skier <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Greene" title="Nancy Greene">Nancy Greene</a>'s torch who lit the torch of <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Gretzky" title="Wayne Gretzky">Wayne Gretzky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hockey_Hall_of_Fame" title="Hockey Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a> hockey player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_Ceremony_timeline_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Opening_Ceremony_timeline-10">[10]</a></sup> Le May Doan, Nash, Greene, and Gretzky then made their way to their four pre-determined locations on the stadium floor to await the raising of the cauldron.</p>
<p>Due to a malfunction of the Olympic cauldron's <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_machinery" title="Hydraulic machinery">hydraulic</a> system, only three of the four arms came up before it was lit. Le May Doan's designated arm of the cauldron had malfunctioned, so she stood by as the other three athletes lit the cauldron at the same time by touching the base of the arms with their respective torches. After the cauldron was lit, a series of red fireworks were shot off in a ring around the exterior of the stadium. Gretzky then jogged out of the stadium, where he was then driven by police escorts through the streets of downtown Vancouver to light a second, outdoor cauldron near the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Convention_Centre" title="Vancouver Convention Centre">Vancouver Convention Centre</a> located in the city's downtown waterfront district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBC_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-NBC-15">[15]</a></sup> Under IOC rules, the lighting of the Olympic cauldron must be witnessed by those attending the opening ceremony, implying that it must be lit at the location where the ceremony is taking place. Although another IOC rule states that the cauldron should be witnessed outside by the entire residents of the entire host city, this was not possible since the ceremony took place indoors. However, VANOC secretly built a second outdoor cauldron next to the West Building of the Vancouver Convention Centre, and Gretzky was secretly chosen to light this permanent cauldron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Quickly word spread through the downtown Vancouver area that Gretzky was indeed the final torchbearer, and very soon a crush of people came running after the police escort to cheer Gretzky on and hopefully catch a glimpse of him carrying the torch to the outdoor cauldron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics_closing_ceremony" title="2010 Winter Olympics closing ceremony">closing ceremony</a> of the games would begin with a tongue-in-cheek homage to the indoor cauldron malfunction, featuring mime Yves Dagenais repairing and finally raising the missing arm of the cauldron, and offering Le May Doan a chance to finally light her arm of the interior cauldron.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_comments">Media comments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Media comments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> arts critic <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ouzounian" title="Richard Ouzounian">Richard Ouzounian</a> gave the ceremony a negative review, blasting the proceedings as "an unimaginatively conceived and loosely executed spectacle that promised much and delivered little."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StarOuzounian_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-StarOuzounian-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a>'</i> Charles McGrath described the event as "like New Year’s Eve, but a tasteful, well-behaved New Year’s Eve", and that it was "authentically and unabashedly Canadian".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTMcGrath_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTMcGrath-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cleve Dheensaw of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Times-Colonist" title="Times-Colonist">Times-Colonist</a></i> (Victoria) described the ceremony as "moving and memorable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times-Colonist_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times-Colonist-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paul_Wells" title="Paul Wells">Paul Wells</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> described the event as "sometimes incomprehensible, but sometimes heart-stoppingly beautiful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macleans_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macleans-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>Also from <i>Maclean's</i>, Wayne Gretzky's trip on a truck through downtown Vancouver to the waterfront cauldron was described as a "<a href="/wiki/Redneck" title="Redneck">redneck</a> <a href="/wiki/Popemobile" title="Popemobile">Popemobile</a>" by writer <a href="/wiki/Scott_Feschuk" title="Scott Feschuk">Scott Feschuk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Citizen" title="Ottawa Citizen">Ottawa Citizen</a>'</i>s Mark Sutcliffe deemed the occasion "tasteful but dull, well-behaved and sometimes thoughtful. You've got Canada nailed." He also found the ceremony lacked a portrayal of contemporary urban Canadian society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>Nikki Yanofsky's performance of "O Canada" received mixed reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MG20100214_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-MG20100214-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> McGrath panned the performance as a "power ballad" while Ouzounian deemed it an "uncomfortable alliance of pop and jazz".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StarOuzounian_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-StarOuzounian-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTMcGrath_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTMcGrath-43">[43]</a></sup> Conversely, Wells praised the "gorgeously languid" singing while Alex Strachan of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canwest_News_Service" title="Canwest News Service">Canwest News Service</a> also had a generally positive reaction taking exception only to a "backbeat" in the arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macleans_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macleans-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanwestStrachan_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-CanwestStrachan-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trevor_W._Payne" title="Trevor W. Payne">Trevor Payne</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Jubilation_Gospel_Choir" title="Montreal Jubilation Gospel Choir">Montreal Jubilation Gospel Choir</a> took issue with the "arrangement and interpretation" of the anthem but also found that Yanofsky was "gifted" in terms of vocal quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MG20100214_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-MG20100214-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bilingualism">Bilingualism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Bilingualism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_Moore_(Canadian_politician)" title="James Moore (Canadian politician)">James Moore</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Canadian_Heritage" title="Minister of Canadian Heritage">Minister of Canadian Heritage</a>, and Quebec Premier <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charest" title="Jean Charest">Jean Charest</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> both expressed disappointment in the limited amount of French content during the ceremony. The Canadian <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Commissioner_of_Official_Languages" title="Office of the Commissioner of Official Languages">Commissioner of Official Languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Fraser" title="Graham Fraser">Graham Fraser</a>, was of the impression that the event was "developed, perceived and presented in English with a French song." Fraser's office received numerous complaints regarding the ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> VANOC, however, defended the case and said that they had made "a very deliberate focus and effort to ensure a strong celebration of Quebec culture and language."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> They also said that there was a significant amount of French in the opening ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Atkins" title="David Atkins">David Atkins</a> said that the ceremonies did celebrate francophone Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiculturalism">Multiculturalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Multiculturalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are critics saying that 41% of Metro Vancouver residents are visible minorities yet these groups are mostly absent from the opening ceremony. VANOC CEO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Furlong_(Canadian)" title="John Furlong (Canadian)">John Furlong</a> hints that VANOC would try to address this issue in the closing ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The international television audience varied from source. VANOC estimated more than a billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_unveils_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canada_unveils-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_ratings">North American ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_Winter_Olympics_opening_ceremony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: North American ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Canada–United States border">Canada-US border</a>, this opening ceremony drew high television ratings.</p>
<p>In Canada, this ceremony aired on the <a href="/wiki/CTV_Television_Network" title="CTV Television Network">CTV Television Network</a> and 10 other channels (all part of a <a href="/wiki/Canada%27s_Olympic_Broadcast_Media_Consortium" title="Canada's Olympic Broadcast Media Consortium">CTV-Rogers media consortium</a>), in a total of 11 languages. The broadcast drew an average of 13.3 million viewers across the country at any given moment, and 23 million Canadians, 69 percent of the national audience, watching at least a portion of the 3.5-hour ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorthAmericaRatings_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-NorthAmericaRatings-57">[57]</a></sup> It was, for a period, the most-watched television event in Canadian history; these numbers were surpassed on the final day of the Games by the <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_tournament#Gold_medal_game" title="Ice hockey at the 2010 Winter Olympics – Men's tournament">gold medal game of the Men's hockey tournament</a>, which drew 16.6 million viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>South of the border, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympics_on_NBC" title="Olympics on NBC">NBC</a> reported an average of 32.6 million viewers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorthAmericaRatings_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-NorthAmericaRatings-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beijing_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beijing-59">[59]</a></sup> making it the second-most watched non-United States Winter Olympics, behind the <a href="/wiki/1994_Winter_Olympics" title="1994 Winter Olympics">Lillehammer Games in 1994</a>, which drew 33.8 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beijing_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beijing-59">[59]</a></sup> and the third most-watched non-United States Olympics, behind the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics_opening_ceremony" title="2008 Summer Olympics opening ceremony">previous one</a>, the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">Summer Olympics in Beijing two years before</a> (34.2 million)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beijing_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beijing-59">[59]</a></sup> and Lillehammer, and with 67.5 million viewers watching at least a portion of it, it was the most watched non-United States Winter Olympics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorthAmericaRatings_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-NorthAmericaRatings-57">[57]</a></sup> and the second-most watched non-United States Olympics, behind Beijing, as that drew 69.9 million viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beijing_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-Beijing-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
