<p><b>Operation Demetrius</b> was a <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> operation in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> on 9–10 August 1971, during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>. It involved the mass <a href="/wiki/Arrest" title="Arrest">arrest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">internment</a> (imprisonment without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" title="Trial (law)">trial</a>) of 342 people suspected of being involved with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_organisations_known_as_the_Irish_Republican_Army" title="List of organisations known as the Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA), which was waging a campaign for a united Ireland against the British state. It was proposed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Government" title="Northern Ireland Government">Northern Ireland Government</a> and approved by the British Government. Armed soldiers launched <a href="/wiki/Police_raid" title="Police raid">dawn raids</a> throughout Northern Ireland, sparking four days of violence in which 20 civilians, two IRA members and two British soldiers were killed. All of those arrested were <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalists</a>, the vast majority of them Catholic. Due to faulty intelligence, many had no links with the IRA. <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">Ulster loyalist</a> paramilitaries were also carrying out acts of violence, which were mainly directed against Catholics and Irish nationalists, but no loyalists were included in the sweep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainsum_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cainsum-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The introduction of internment, the way the arrests were carried out, and the abuse of those arrested, led to mass protests and a sharp increase in violence. Amid the violence, about 7,000 people fled or were forced out of their homes. <a href="/wiki/Five_techniques" title="Five techniques">The interrogation techniques</a> used on the internees were described by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission_of_Human_Rights" title="European Commission of Human Rights">European Commission of Human Rights</a> in 1976 as <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECHR) ruled on appeal in 1978 that while the techniques were "inhuman and degrading", they did not constitute torture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was later revealed that the British Government had withheld information from the ECHR and that a policy of torture had in fact been authorized by British Government ministers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ministers-sanctioned_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ministers-sanctioned-3">[3]</a></sup> In December 2014 the Irish government asked the ECHR to revise its 1978 judgement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rte.ie_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rte.ie-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The policy of internment was to last until December 1975 and during that time 1,981 people were interned;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joint2005p110_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-joint2005p110-5">[5]</a></sup> 1,874 were nationalist, while 107 were loyalist. The first loyalist internees were detained in February 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainsum_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cainsum-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_planning">Background and planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Internment had been used a number of times during <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>'s (and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>'s) history, but had not yet been used during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>, which began in the late 1960s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalist" title="Ulster loyalist">Ulster loyalist</a> paramilitaries such as the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> (UVF) had been engaged in a low-level violent campaign since 1966. After the <a href="/wiki/1969_Northern_Ireland_riots" title="1969 Northern Ireland riots">August 1969 riots</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> was deployed on the streets to bolster the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> (RUC). Up until this point the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1922%E2%80%9369)" title="Irish Republican Army (1922–69)">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA) had been largely inactive. However, as the violence and political situation worsened, the IRA was divided over how to deal with it. It split into two factions, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA" title="Provisional IRA">Provisional IRA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_IRA" title="Official IRA">Official IRA</a>. In 1970–71, the Provisionals began to retaliate against the British Army and the RUC. The Officials' policy was one more defensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irishstory_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-irishstory-6">[6]</a></sup> During 1970–71 there were numerous clashes between state forces and the two wings of the IRA, between the IRAs and loyalists, and occasionally between the IRAs. Most loyalist attacks were directed against Catholic civilians, but they also clashed with state forces and the IRA on a number of occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irishstory_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-irishstory-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The idea of re-introducing internment for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_republican" title="Irish republican">Irish republican</a> militants came from the <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionist</a> <a href="/wiki/Executive_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Executive Committee of the Privy Council of Northern Ireland">government of Northern Ireland</a>, headed by <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Prime Minister of Northern Ireland">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Brian_Faulkner" title="Brian Faulkner">Brian Faulkner</a>. It was agreed to re-introduce internment at a meeting between Faulkner and UK Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a> on 5 August 1971. The <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">British cabinet</a> recommended "balancing action", such as the arrest of loyalist militants, the calling in of weapons held by (generally unionist) rifle clubs in Northern Ireland and an indefinite ban on parades (most of which were held by unionist/loyalist groups). However, Faulkner argued that a ban on parades was unworkable, that the rifle clubs posed no security risk and that there was no evidence of loyalist terrorism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theirishstory.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-theirishstory.com-7">[7]</a></sup> It was eventually agreed that there would be a six-month ban on parades but no interning of loyalists and that internment would go ahead on 9 August, in an operation carried out by the British Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irishstory_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-irishstory-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the initial list of those to be arrested, which was drawn up by <a href="/wiki/RUC_Special_Branch" title="RUC Special Branch">RUC Special Branch</a> and <a href="/wiki/MI5" title="MI5">MI5</a>, there were 450 names, but only 350 of these were found. Key figures on the list, and many who never appeared on them, had got wind of the swoop before it began. The list included leaders of the non-violent <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Civil_Rights_Association" title="Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association">Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association</a> such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ivan_Barr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ivan Barr (page does not exist)">Ivan Barr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Farrell_(activist)" title="Michael Farrell (activist)">Michael Farrell</a>. But, as <a href="/wiki/Tim_Pat_Coogan" title="Tim Pat Coogan">Tim Pat Coogan</a> noted,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>What they did not include was a single Loyalist. Although the UVF had begun the killing and bombing, this organisation was left untouched, as were other violent Loyalist satellite organisations such as <a href="/wiki/Tara_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Tara (Northern Ireland)">Tara</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Defence_Association" title="Shankill Defence Association">Shankill Defence Association</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestant_Volunteers" title="Ulster Protestant Volunteers">Ulster Protestant Volunteers</a>. Faulkner was urged by the British to include a few Protestants in the trawl but, apart from two republicans, he refused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a subsequent case brought to the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission_of_Human_Rights" title="European Commission of Human Rights">European Commission of Human Rights</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_government" title="Irish government">Irish government</a> against the government of the United Kingdom, it was conceded that <i>Operation Demetrius</i> was planned and implemented from the highest levels of the British government and that specially trained personnel were sent to Northern Ireland to familiarize the local forces in what became known as the '<a href="/wiki/Five_techniques" title="Five techniques">five techniques</a>', methods of interrogation described by opponents as "a euphemism for torture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_basis">Legal basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The internments were initially carried out under Regulations 11 and 12 of 1956 and Regulation 10 of 1957 (the Special Powers Regulations), made under the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Authorities_(Special_Powers)_Act_(Northern_Ireland)_1922" title="Civil Authorities (Special Powers) Act (Northern Ireland) 1922">Special Powers Act</a>. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Detention_of_Terrorists_(Northern_Ireland)_Order_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Detention of Terrorists (Northern Ireland) Order 1972 (page does not exist)">Detention of Terrorists Order</a> of 7 November 1972, made under the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_(Temporary_Provisions)_Act_1972" title="Northern Ireland (Temporary Provisions) Act 1972">Temporary Provisions Act</a>, was used after direct rule was instituted.</p>
<p>Internees arrested without trial pursuant to Operation Demetrius could not complain to the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission_of_Human_Rights" title="European Commission of Human Rights">European Commission of Human Rights</a> about breaches of Article 5 of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> (ECHR) because on 27 June 1957, the UK lodged a notice with the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> declaring that there was a "public emergency within the meaning of Article 15(1) of the Convention."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_and_immediate_aftermath">Operation and immediate aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operation and immediate aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Maidstone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="568" data-file-width="759" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/HMS_Maidstone.jpg/220px-HMS_Maidstone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/HMS_Maidstone.jpg/330px-HMS_Maidstone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/HMS_Maidstone.jpg/440px-HMS_Maidstone.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Maidstone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/HMS_Maidstone_(1937)" title="HMS Maidstone (1937)">HMS <i>Maidstone</i></a> (pictured here in Algiers in the Second World War), a prison ship which docked at <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> and where many internees were sent</div>

<p>Operation Demetrius began on Monday 9 August at about 4<small>AM</small>.</p>
<p>The operation was in two parts:</p>
<ul>
<li>(1) Arrest and movement of the detainees to one of three regional holding centers: Girdwood in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abercorn_Barracks" title="Abercorn Barracks">Ballykinler</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">County Down</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magilligan_(HM_Prison)" title="Magilligan (HM Prison)">Magilligan</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a></li>
<li>(2) The process of identification and questioning, leading either to release of the detainee or movement into detention at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crumlin_Road_(HM_Prison)" title="Crumlin Road (HM Prison)">Crumlin Road prison</a> or aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Maidstone_(1937)" title="HMS Maidstone (1937)">HMS <i>Maidstone</i></a>, a <a href="/wiki/Prison_ship" title="Prison ship">prison ship</a> in Belfast Harbour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-compton-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In the first wave of raids across Northern Ireland, 342 people were arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainchron_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cainchron-12">[12]</a></sup> Many of those arrested reported that they and their families were assaulted, verbally abused and threatened by the soldiers. There were claims of soldiers smashing their way into houses without warning and firing <a href="/wiki/Rubber_bullet" title="Rubber bullet">baton rounds</a> through doors and windows. Many of those arrested also reported being ill-treated during their three-day detention at the holding centres. They complained of being beaten, verbally abused, threatened, harassed by dogs, denied sleep, and starved. Some reported being forced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauntlet_(punishment)" title="Gauntlet (punishment)">run a gauntlet</a> of baton-wielding soldiers, being forced to run an 'obstacle course', having their heads forcefully shaved, being kept naked, being burnt with cigarettes, having a sack placed over their heads for long periods, having a rope kept around their necks, having the barrel of a gun pressed against their heads, being dragged by the hair, being trailed behind armoured vehicles while barefoot, and being tied to armoured trucks as a <a href="/wiki/Human_shield" title="Human shield">human shield</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennally1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennally1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennally2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennally2-14">[14]</a></sup> Some were hooded, beaten and then thrown from a helicopter. They were told they were hundreds of feet in the air, but were actually only a few feet from the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The operation sparked an immediate upsurge of violence, the worst since the <a href="/wiki/1969_Northern_Ireland_riots" title="1969 Northern Ireland riots">August 1969 riots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainchron_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-cainchron-12">[12]</a></sup> The British Army came under sustained attack from Irish nationalist rioters and gunmen, especially in Belfast. According to journalist <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Myers" title="Kevin Myers">Kevin Myers</a>: "Insanity seized the city. Hundreds of vehicles were hijacked and factories were burnt. Loyalist and IRA gunmen were everywhere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostlives80_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-lostlives80-16">[16]</a></sup> People blocked roads and streets with burning barricades to stop the British Army entering their neighbourhoods. In <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, barricades were again erected around <a href="/wiki/Free_Derry" title="Free Derry">Free Derry</a> and "for the next 11 months these areas effectively seceded from British control".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anphoblacht36th_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-anphoblacht36th-17">[17]</a></sup> Between 9 and 11 August, 24 people were killed or fatally wounded: 20 civilians (14 Catholics, 6 Protestants), two members of the Provisional IRA (shot dead by the British Army), and two members of the British Army (shot dead by the Provisional IRA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BallymurphyMassacre.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1012" data-file-width="1701" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/BallymurphyMassacre.jpg/220px-BallymurphyMassacre.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/BallymurphyMassacre.jpg/330px-BallymurphyMassacre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/BallymurphyMassacre.jpg/440px-BallymurphyMassacre.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A <a href="/wiki/Murals_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Murals in Northern Ireland">mural</a> commemorating those killed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballymurphy_Massacre" title="Ballymurphy Massacre">Ballymurphy Massacre</a> during Operation Demetrius</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Of the civilians killed, 17 were killed by the British Army and the other three were killed by unknown attackers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-18">[18]</a></sup> In West Belfast's Ballymurphy housing estate, 11 Catholic civilians were killed by <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion,_Parachute_Regiment" title="1st Battalion, Parachute Regiment">1st Battalion, Parachute Regiment</a> between 9 and 11 August in an episode that has become known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballymurphy_Massacre" title="Ballymurphy Massacre">Ballymurphy Massacre</a>. Another flashpoint was <a href="/wiki/Ardoyne" title="Ardoyne">Ardoyne</a> in North Belfast, where soldiers shot dead three people on 9 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-18">[18]</a></sup> Many Protestant families fled Ardoyne and about 200 burnt their homes as they left, lest they "fall into Catholic hands".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-coogan-19">[19]</a></sup> Protestant and Catholic families fled "to either side of a dividing line, which would provide the foundation for the permanent <a href="/wiki/Peace_lines" title="Peace lines">peaceline</a> later built in the area".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostlives80_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-lostlives80-16">[16]</a></sup> Catholic homes were burnt in Ardoyne and elsewhere too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-coogan-19">[19]</a></sup> About 7000 people, most of them Catholics, were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-coogan-19">[19]</a></sup> About 2500 Catholic refugees fled south of the border, where new <a href="/wiki/Refugee_camp" title="Refugee camp">refugee camps</a> were set up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-coogan-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 13 August, media reports indicated that the violence had begun to wane, seemingly due to exhaustion on the part of the IRA and security forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 15 August, the nationalist <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) announced that it was starting a campaign of <a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a> in response to the introduction of internment. By 17 October, it was estimated that about 16,000 households were withholding rent and rates for council houses as part of the campaign of civil disobedience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainchron_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-cainchron-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16 August, over 8,000 workers went on strike in Derry in protest at internment. <a href="/wiki/Joe_Cahill" title="Joe Cahill">Joe Cahill</a>, then <a href="/wiki/List_of_Irish_Republican_Army_chiefs_of_staff" title="List of Irish Republican Army chiefs of staff">Chief of Staff</a> of the Provisional IRA, held a press conference during which he claimed that only 30 Provisional IRA members had been interned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainchron_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-cainchron-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 August, in protest against internment, about 130 non-<a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionist</a> <a href="/wiki/Councillor" title="Councillor">councillors</a> announced that they would no longer sit on <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Local government in Northern Ireland">district councils</a>. The SDLP also withdrew its representatives from a number of public bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainchron_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-cainchron-12">[12]</a></sup> On 19 October, five Northern Ireland Members of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" title="House of Commons (United Kingdom)">Parliament</a> (MPs) began a 48-hour <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">hunger strike</a> against internment. The protest took place near <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a> in London. Among those taking part were <a href="/wiki/John_Hume" title="John Hume">John Hume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austin_Currie" title="Austin Currie">Austin Currie</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernadette_Devlin" title="Bernadette Devlin">Bernadette Devlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainchron_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-cainchron-12">[12]</a></sup> Protests would continue until internment was ended in December 1975.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_effects">Long-term effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Long-term effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bogside_(12),_August_2009.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bogside_%2812%29%2C_August_2009.JPG/220px-Bogside_%2812%29%2C_August_2009.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bogside_%2812%29%2C_August_2009.JPG/330px-Bogside_%2812%29%2C_August_2009.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bogside_%2812%29%2C_August_2009.JPG/440px-Bogside_%2812%29%2C_August_2009.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bogside_(12),_August_2009.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anti-internment mural in the <a href="/wiki/Bogside" title="Bogside">Bogside</a> area of Derry</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The backlash against internment contributed to the decision of the British Government under Prime Minister Edward Heath to suspend the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Executive Committee of the Privy Council of Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland Government</a> and replace it with <a href="/wiki/Direct_rule" title="Direct rule">direct rule</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" title="Her Majesty's Government">Westminster</a>, under the authority of a British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Secretary of State for Northern Ireland">Secretary of State for Northern Ireland</a>. This took place in 1972.</p>
<p>Following the suspension of the Northern Ireland Government and <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament</a>, internment was continued by the direct rule administration until 5 December 1975. During this time a total of 1,981 people were interned: 1,874 were from an Irish nationalist background, while 107 were from a unionist background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainsum_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cainsum-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historians generally view the period of internment as inflaming sectarian tensions in Northern Ireland, while failing in its goal of arresting key members of the IRA. Many of the people arrested had no links whatsoever with the IRA, but their names appeared on the list of those to be arrested through bungling and incompetence. The list's lack of reliability and the arrests that followed, complemented by reports of internees being abused far in excess of the usual state violence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theirishstory.com_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-theirishstory.com-7">[7]</a></sup> led to more nationalists identifying with the IRA and losing hope in non-violent methods. After Operation Demetrius, recruits came forward in huge numbers to join the Provisional and Official wings of the IRA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-coogan-19">[19]</a></sup> Internment also led to a sharp increase in violence. In the eight months before the operation, there were 34 conflict-related deaths in Northern Ireland. In the four months following it, 140 were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-coogan-19">[19]</a></sup> A serving officer of the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a> declared:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It (internment) has, in fact, increased terrorist activity, perhaps boosted IRA recruitment, polarised further the Catholic and Protestant communities and reduced the ranks of the much needed Catholic moderates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In terms of loss of life, 1972 was the most violent year of the Troubles. The fatal march on <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Sunday_(1972)" title="Bloody Sunday (1972)">Bloody Sunday</a> (30 January 1972) in <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, when 14 unarmed civil rights protesters were shot dead by British paratroopers, was an anti-internment march.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interrogation_of_internees">Interrogation of internees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interrogation of internees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All of those arrested were interrogated by the British Army and RUC. However, twelve internees were then chosen for further "deep interrogation", using <a href="/wiki/Sensory_deprivation" title="Sensory deprivation">sensory deprivation</a>. This took place at a secret interrogation centre, which was later revealed to be <a href="/wiki/Shackleton_Barracks" title="Shackleton Barracks">Shackleton Barracks</a>, outside <a href="/wiki/Ballykelly,_County_Londonderry" title="Ballykelly, County Londonderry">Ballykelly</a>. In October, a further two internees were chosen for deep interrogation. These fourteen became known as "the Hooded Men", or "the Guineapigs".</p>
<p>After undergoing the same treatment as the other internees, the men were <a href="/wiki/Hooding" title="Hooding">hooded</a>, handcuffed and flown to the base by helicopter. On the way, soldiers severely beat them and threatened to throw them from the helicopter. When they arrived they were stripped naked, photographed, and examined by a doctor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcguffin4_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcguffin4-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>For seven days, when not being interrogated, they were kept hooded and handcuffed in a cold cell and subjected to a continuous loud hissing noise. Here they were forced to stand in a <a href="/wiki/Stress_position" title="Stress position">stress position</a> for many hours and were repeatedly beaten on all parts of their body. They were deprived of sleep, food and drink. Some of them also reported being kicked in the genitals, having their heads banged against walls, being shot at with blank rounds, and being threatened with injections. The result was severe physical and mental exhaustion, severe anxiety, depression, hallucinations, disorientation and repeated loss of consciousness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcguffin4_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcguffin4-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcguffin6_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcguffin6-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The interrogation methods used on the men became known as the '<a href="/wiki/Five_techniques" title="Five techniques">five techniques</a>'. Training and advice regarding the five techniques came from senior intelligence officials in the British government. The <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECHR) defined the five techniques as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<ul>
<li>(a) wall-standing: forcing the detainees to remain for periods of some hours in a "stress position", described by those who underwent it as being "spreadeagled against the wall, with their fingers put high above the head against the wall, the legs spread apart and the feet back, causing them to stand on their toes with the weight of the body mainly on the fingers";</li>
<li>(b) hooding: putting a black or navy coloured bag over the detainees' heads and, at least initially, keeping it there all the time except during interrogation;</li>
<li>(c) subjection to noise: pending their interrogations, holding the detainees in a room where there was a continuous loud and hissing noise;</li>
<li>(d) deprivation of sleep: pending their interrogations, depriving the detainees of sleep;</li>
<li>(e) deprivation of food and drink: subjecting the detainees to a reduced diet during their stay at the centre and pending interrogations.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>The fourteen Hooded Men were the only internees subjected to the full five techniques. However, over the following months, some internees were subjected to at least one of the five techniques, as well as other interrogation methods. These allegedly included <a href="/wiki/Waterboarding" title="Waterboarding">waterboarding</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Electric_shock#Torture" title="Electric shock">electric shocks</a>, burning with matches and candles, forcing internees to stand over hot electric fires while beating them, beating and squeezing of the genitals, inserting objects into the anus, injections, <a href="/wiki/Foot_whipping" title="Foot whipping">whipping the soles of the feet</a>, and psychological abuse such as <a href="/wiki/Russian_roulette" title="Russian roulette">Russian roulette</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcguffin9_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcguffin9-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parker_Report">Parker Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Parker Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the interrogation techniques used on the internees became known to the public, there was outrage at the British government, especially from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_nationalist" title="Irish nationalist">Irish nationalists</a>. In answer to the anger from the public and Members of Parliament, on 16 November 1971, the British government commissioned a committee of inquiry chaired by <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Parker,_Baron_Parker_of_Waddington" title="Hubert Parker, Baron Parker of Waddington">Lord Parker</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England" title="Lord Chief Justice of England">Lord Chief Justice of England</a>) to look into the legal and moral aspects of the 'five techniques'.</p>
<p>The "Parker Report"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> was published on 2 March 1972 and found the five techniques to be illegal under domestic law:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>10. Domestic Law ...(c) We have received both written and oral representations from many legal bodies and individual lawyers from both England and Northern Ireland. There has been no dissent from the view that the procedures are illegal alike by the law of England and the law of Northern Ireland. ... (d) This being so, no Army Directive and no Minister could lawfully or validly have authorized the use of the procedures. Only Parliament can alter the law. The procedures were and are illegal.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>On the same day (2 March 1972), United Kingdom Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a> stated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[The] Government, having reviewed the whole matter with great care and with reference to any future operations, have decided that the techniques ... will not be used in future as an aid to interrogation... The statement that I have made covers all future circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ireland.v._UK_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ireland.v._UK-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As foreshadowed in the Prime Minister's statement, directives expressly forbidding the use of the techniques, whether alone or together, were then issued to the security forces by the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ireland.v._UK_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ireland.v._UK-27">[27]</a></sup> While these are still legally in force and the use of such methods by UK security forces is not officially condoned by the government, the five techniques were still being used by the British Army in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Commission_of_Human_Rights">European Commission of Human Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: European Commission of Human Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Irish Government, on behalf of the men who had been subject to the five techniques, took a case to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="European Commission on Human Rights">European Commission on Human Rights</a> (<i>Ireland v. United Kingdom</i>, 1976 Y.B. Eur. Conv. on Hum. Rts. 512, 748, 788–94 (Eur. Comm’n of Hum. Rts.)). The Commission stated that it</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...unanimously considered the combined use of the five methods to amount to torture, on the grounds that (1) the intensity of the stress caused by techniques creating sensory deprivation "directly affects the personality physically and mentally"; and (2) "the systematic application of the techniques for the purpose of inducing a person to give information shows a clear resemblance to those methods of systematic torture which have been known over the ages...a modern system of torture falling into the same category as those systems applied in previous times as a means of obtaining information and confessions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Court_of_Human_Rights">European Court of Human Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: European Court of Human Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Commissions findings were appealed. In 1978, in the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECHR) trial <i>Ireland v. the United Kingdom</i> (Case No. 5310/71),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> the court ruled:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>167. ... Although the five techniques, as applied in combination, undoubtedly amounted to inhuman and degrading treatment, although their object was the extraction of confessions, the naming of others and/or information and although they were used systematically, they did not occasion suffering of the particular intensity and cruelty implied by the word torture as so understood. ...</p>
<p>168. The Court concludes that recourse to the five techniques amounted to a practice of inhuman and degrading treatment, which practice was in breach of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights#Article_3_-_prohibition_of_torture" title="European Convention on Human Rights">Article 3 (art. 3)</a>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>On 8 February 1977, in proceedings before the ECHR, and in line with the findings of the Parker Report and UK Government policy, the Attorney-General of the United Kingdom stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Government of the United Kingdom have considered the question of the use of the 'five techniques' with very great care and with particular regard to Article 3 (art. 3) of the Convention. They now give this unqualified undertaking, that the 'five techniques' will not in any circumstances be reintroduced as an aid to interrogation.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_developments">Later developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Demetrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, declassified documents revealed the existence of the interrogation centre at Ballykelly. It had not been mentioned in any of the inquiries. Human rights group the <a href="/wiki/Pat_Finucane_Centre" title="Pat Finucane Centre">Pat Finucane Centre</a> accused the British Government of deliberately hiding it from the inquiries and the European Court of Human Rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In June 2014, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RT%C3%89" title="RTÉ">RTÉ</a> documentary entitled <i>The Torture Files</i> uncovered a letter from the UK Home Secretary <a href="/wiki/Merlyn_Rees" title="Merlyn Rees">Merlyn Rees</a> in 1977 to the then British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a>. It confirmed that a policy of 'torture' had in fact been authorized by the British Government's ministers—specifically the Secretary for Defence <a href="/wiki/Peter_Carington,_6th_Baron_Carrington" title="Peter Carington, 6th Baron Carrington">Peter Carrington</a>—in 1971, contrary to the knowledge of the Irish government or the ECHR. The letter states: "It is my view (confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Faulkner" title="Brian Faulkner">Brian Faulkner</a> before his death) that the decision to use methods of torture in Northern Ireland in 1971/72 was taken by ministers – in particular Lord Carrington, then secretary of state for defence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ministers-sanctioned_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ministers-sanctioned-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the 2014 revelations, the President of <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a>, called on the Irish government to bring the case back to the ECHR because the British government, he said, "lied to the European Court of Human Rights both on the severity of the methods used on the men, their long term physical and psychological consequences, on where these interrogations took place and who gave the political authority and clearance for it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On 2 December 2014 the Irish government announced that, having reviewed the new evidence and following requests from the survivors, it had decided to officially ask the ECHR to revise its 1978 judgement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rte.ie_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-rte.ie-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
