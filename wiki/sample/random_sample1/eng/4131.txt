<p><i><b>Phodopus</b></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> in the hamster <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">subfamily</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cricetinae" title="Cricetinae">Cricetinae</a>—a division of the larger family <a href="/wiki/Cricetidae" title="Cricetidae">Cricetidae</a>—is a lineage of small hamsters native to central Asia that display unusual adaptations to extreme temperatures. They are the only known hamsters that live in groups and, in some cases, rely on significant contributions by males to the raising of offspring. They are nocturnal and active throughout the year; they do not hibernate. Species of <i>Phodopus</i>, together with members of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Cricetulus" title="Cricetulus">Cricetulus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Allocricetulus" title="Allocricetulus">Allocricetulus</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tscherskia" title="Tscherskia">Tscherskia</a></i> are called "dwarf hamsters" because of their small size (roughly 7 to 10 centimetres or 2.8 to 3.9 inches) relative to other hamsters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neumann_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neumann-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like other hamsters, members of <i>Phodopus</i> have a round body shape, short tails and <a href="/wiki/Cheek_pouch" title="Cheek pouch">cheek pouches</a> in which they can store food. They all live in dry conditions with extreme temperatures. They inhabit the forests, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steppes" title="Steppes">steppes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-desert" title="Semi-desert">semi-deserts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>. Fossils of <i>Phodopus</i> have been found in Pleistocene and Pliocene deposits in Europe and Asia.</p>
<p><i>Phodopus</i> species are commonly sold as pets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanderlip_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanderlip-3">[3]</a></sup> They are also used as laboratory organisms for a variety of studies, especially involving seasonal endocrine variation. Some species are considered to be agricultural pests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW-sungorus_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-sungorus-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>All three species are widespread and probably abundant, and are assessed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> (LC) by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-sungorus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-sungorus-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-campbelli_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-campbelli-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-roborovskii_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-roborovskii-7">[7]</a></sup> However, their ecology and population dynamics are not well understood. Historical records indicate they were once common in places where they no longer occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup> Consequently, they are listed in the Kazakhstan Government Regulation of Approval of Rare and Endangered Animal and Plant Species Index (2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feoktistova_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feoktistova-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics">Systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Phodopus</i> is one of seven genera within the subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cricetinae" title="Cricetinae">Cricetinae</a>, a group of rodents distributed throughout <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3-Cricetinae_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-Cricetinae-10">[10]</a></sup> It contains hamsters distinguished from other hamsters by small size, short tails and fur covered feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup> Fossils assignable to the genus occur in the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> in Europe and in the Late Pliocene of Kazakhstan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyut.27kova_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyut.27kova-11">[11]</a></sup> despite the fact that molecular data suggest the lineage is no younger than 8.5 million years.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gerrit_Smith_Miller" title="Gerrit Smith Miller">G.S. Miller</a> first described the genus <i>Phodopus</i> in 1910, designating <i>Cricetulus bedfordiae</i> as its <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup> (<i>C. bedfordiae</i> is the species now called <i>P. roborovskii</i>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-roborovskii_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-roborovskii-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.1</sup> The genus name derives from ancient Greek <i>phodos</i> (φωδος, genitive of <i>phos</i> φως "blister") and <i>pous</i> (πους "foot") and refers to the large pad on the sole of each foot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-roborovskii_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-roborovskii-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.6</sup></p>

<p>Morphology has proved inadequate in providing characters for phylogenetic analyses of the subfamily Cricetinae, but the group appears to be monophyletic based on molecular analyses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neumann_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Neumann-2">[2]</a></sup> The genus <i>Phodopus</i> is one of three well-supported lineages in Cricetinae, the other two being the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Mesocricetus" title="Mesocricetus">Mesocricetus</a></i> and the Cricetus-related group (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cricetus" title="Cricetus">Cricetus</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tscherskia" title="Tscherskia">Tscherskia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Allocricetulus" title="Allocricetulus">Allocricetulus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Cricetulus" title="Cricetulus">Cricetulus</a></i>). Analysis of chromosomes supports these three lineages. <i>Phodopus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister</a> to all other Cricetinae (meaning that it is the first lineage to diverge in the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>). Using several molecular dating techniques, researchers have determined that the <i>Phodopus</i> lineage may have originated 8.5 to 12.2 million years ago (mya). Other genetic dating analyses suggest a somewhat earlier origination of 13.5–14.1 mya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steppan_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steppan-13">[13]</a></sup> Fossils assignable to the genus are unknown before about 2.5 million years ago, but failure to identify the remains accurately may contribute to the apparent lack of older fossils.</p>
<p>As shown in the cladogram, <i>Phodopus roborovskii</i> is sister to <i>Phodopus campbelli</i> + <i>Phodopus sungorus</i>. The validity of <i>P. campbelli</i> as a species has been controversial. Some biologists consider it to be a subspecies of <i>P. sungorus</i>. Neumann et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neumann_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Neumann-2">[2]</a></sup> determined that, at least for <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome b</a>, <i>P. campbelli</i> and <i>P. sungorus</i> do not display the level of genetic divergence characteristic of sister rodent species. Both <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> have 2n=28 chromosomes. However, evidence from hybridization with <i>P. sungorus</i> has suggested to some researchers that they are in fact separate species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sokolov_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sokolov-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>P. roborovskii</i>, on the other hand, has levels of genetic divergence from the other two taxa in the genus characteristic of genera among small mammals. <i>P. roborovskii</i> is also distinct from the other species morphologically (in its smaller body size and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelage" title="Pelage">pelage</a>, for example) and ecologically (preferring sandy, arid habitats). <i>P. roborovskii</i> has 2n=34 chromosomes. For these reasons, Neumann et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neumann_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Neumann-2">[2]</a></sup> suggest removing <i>P. sungorus</i> and <i>P. campbelli</i> to the genus <i>Cricetiscus</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biologists currently recognize three species of <i>Phodopus</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>P. campbelli</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-campbelli_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-campbelli-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>P. sungorus</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup> and <i>P. roborovskii</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-roborovskii_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-roborovskii-12">[12]</a></sup> The validity of <i>P. Campbelli</i> is in dispute, and it has sometimes been considered a subspecies of <i>P. sungorus</i>.</p>
<p>The species of <i>Phodopus</i> do not have stable common names, even among biologists. "Djungarian" (or "Dzhungarian" or "Zungarian") and "Siberian" have been applied to both <i>P. sungorus</i> and <i>P. campbelli</i> in the scientific literature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanHoosier_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-VanHoosier-16">[16]</a></sup> and all three species have been called "desert" hamsters as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanderlip_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vanderlip-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>P. sungorus</i> is known as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Djungarian [or Dzhungarian<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-sungorus_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-sungorus-5">[5]</a></sup> ] hamster</dd>
<dd>Russian white or Russian winter white hamster</dd>
<dd>winter white (dwarf) hamster</dd>
<dd>Siberian hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>striped hairy-footed hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-campbelli_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-campbelli-15">[15]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>striped desert hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilsonReeder_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-WilsonReeder-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p><i>P. campbelli</i> is known as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Djungarian [or Dzhungarian] hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-campbelli_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-campbelli-15">[15]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Campbell's (dwarf) hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-campbelli_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-campbelli-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Campbell's desert hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilsonReeder_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-WilsonReeder-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p><i>P. roborovskii</i> is known as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>desert hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-roborovskii_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-roborovskii-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-roborovskii_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-roborovskii-12">[12]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Roborovski (dwarf) hamster</dd>
<dd>Roborovski's desert hamster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilsonReeder_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-WilsonReeder-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Robo (dwarf) hamster</dd>
</dl>
<p>Below is a key to the four species of dwarf hamster commonly kept as pets. Some dwarf hamsters are albino, in which case they will be primarily white and the fur colour characteristics will not serve to distinguish them. The characteristics in brackets will allow identification using characters other than pelage, but the traits are generally comparative and are more difficult to assess. The key is based on Ross 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Has a visible tail. Foot pads not fur covered. <span class="nowrap">→ <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cricetulus_griseus" title="Cricetulus griseus">Cricetulus griseus</a></i></span></li>
<li>Has no visible tail, or at most a very short tail typical of hamsters. Foot pads fur covered.
<ul>
<li>Back without a dark stripe running from head to tail. Sides show a sharp transition from darker upper body fur to lighter under body fur. [Body size very small, head rounded when observed from above. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cusp_(dentistry)" title="Cusp (dentistry)">Cusps</a> and re-entrant folds of the upper and lower <a href="/wiki/Molar_(tooth)" title="Molar (tooth)">molars</a> directly opposite. Feet with one large pad on the bottom surface.] <span class="nowrap">→ <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phodopus_roborovskii" title="Phodopus roborovskii">Phodopus roborovskii</a></i></span></li>
<li>Back with a dark stripe running from head to tail. Sides show darker upper body fur intergrading into lighter under body fur, forming a wave-like (or arched) interface. (If the hamster is all white, but the eyes are dark (not red) it is most likely <i>P. sungorus</i> in its winter pelage.) [Body size typical for dwarf hamsters, head rectangular when observed from above. Cusps and re-entrant folds of the upper and lower molars directly alternate. Feet with three pads on the bottom surface.]
<ul>
<li>Dark patch on the crown of the head. Fur on the under body is white at the base. No yellow or buffy highlights on the sides where the darker upper body fur meets the lighter underbody fur. [Ears small.] <span class="nowrap">→ <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phodopus_sungorus" title="Phodopus sungorus">Phodopus sungorus</a></i></span></li>
<li>Dark patch on crown of head lacking. Fur on the under body is slate gray at the base. May have yellow or buffy highlights on the sides where the darker upper body fur meets the lighter under body fur. [Ears larger.] <span class="nowrap">→ <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phodopus_campbelli" title="Phodopus campbelli">Phodopus campbelli</a></i></span></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campbell.27s_dwarf_hamster">Campbell's dwarf hamster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campbell's dwarf hamster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_dwarf_hamster" title="Campbell's dwarf hamster">Campbell's dwarf hamster</a></div>
<p><i>Phodopus campbelli</i>: The lips and cheeks have white fur, and the region around the ears is grey. A dark <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsum_(anatomy)" title="Dorsum (anatomy)">dorsal</a> stripe runs from the base of the neck to the base of the tail. The fur on the throat, abdomen and legs is white, and the fur on top of the paws is silvery white. When the cheek pouches are full, they extend back to the shoulders. The average body mass is 23.4 grams (0.8 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-campbelli_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-campbelli-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Djungarian_hamster">Djungarian hamster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Djungarian hamster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Djungarian_hamster" title="Djungarian hamster">Djungarian hamster</a></div>

<p><i>Phodopus sungorus</i>: In the summer, the face is brown, which is slightly lighter than the fur around the mouth and ears. The rest of the head is dark brown with a black rim around each eye. A dark brown dorsal stripe runs from the back of the head to the base of the tail. The feet, tail, throat and abdomen are white, and the back is ash grey to dark brown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roborovski_hamster">Roborovski hamster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roborovski hamster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roborovski_hamster" title="Roborovski hamster">Roborovski hamster</a></div>
<p><i>Phodopus roborovskii</i> is the smallest species of <i>Phodopus</i> and of hamsters in general. The feet are unusually short, broad and densely hairy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The fur on the abdomen is grey, and the coat colour turns grey during moulting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flint_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flint-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations_to_extreme_environments">Adaptations to extreme environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Adaptations to extreme environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Phodopus</i> species display a variety of morphological, physiological and behavioral adaptations to seasonal temperature extremes and aridity. To survive the exceptional cold of winter, they have evolved spherical, compact bodies with excellent insulation, including both fur and fat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wynne-Edwards1999_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wynne-Edwards1999-20">[20]</a></sup> Water is scarce in both summer and winter, and these hamsters have developed an excellent ability to conserve water by maintaining low evaporative water loss rates and concentrating urine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wynne-Edwards1999_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wynne-Edwards1999-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>During periods of extreme cold (below -20 °C), <i>P. sungorus</i> adopts a characteristic hunched posture, with its head and forepaws tucked under its belly. It fluffs its fur evenly, increasing its insulating quality. During periods of high heat (greater than 30 °C), this species flattens itself on its belly, with all four limbs splayed to the side. It grooms its fur into clumps, creating "ventilation gaps" between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross-sungorus_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-sungorus-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sociality has been considered an adaptation to the extreme environment, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wynne-Edwards1999_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wynne-Edwards1999-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the wild, <i>Phodopus</i> primarily collect and eat seeds and dry fruits. They also eat other plant parts and insects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitats_and_conservation_status">Habitats and conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Habitats and conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Phodopus</i> species inhabit the mountainous forests, steppes, and semideserts of Mongolia and the adjacent areas of China, northeast Kazakhstan, and the southern part of the West Siberian lowlands of <a href="/wiki/Tuva" title="Tuva">Tuva</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dauria" title="Dauria">Dauria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcken_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcken-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> All three species are widespread, although some isolated local populations exist. All three species are probably abundant and are assessed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> (LC) in the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-sungorus_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-sungorus-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-campbelli_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-campbelli-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn-roborovskii_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-roborovskii-7">[7]</a></sup> They all occur to some extent in protected areas, at least in Mongolia, and are not thought to face significant threats to their existence. Because the status and dynamics of their populations are uncertain, some species are treated as species of concern (for example, the government of Kazakhstan requires monitoring of <i>P roborovskii</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feoktistova_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feoktistova-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kazakhstan_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kazakhstan-22">[22]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>Some species are clearly diminished in range and may have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extirpate" title="Extirpate">extirpated</a> as agricultural pests or by habitat destruction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions_with_humans">Interactions with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phodopus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Interactions with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since some populations and species of <i>Phodopus</i>, such as <i>P. campbelli</i>, inhabit areas with sparse human population and little potential for agriculture, they have little interaction with humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW-campbelli_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-campbelli-23">[23]</a></sup> Others, such as <i>P. sungorus</i>, have been perceived as significant agricultural pests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW-sungorus_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-sungorus-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dwarf hamsters were introduced into the pet trade in the mid-1990s. They are considered excellent pets, especially for novice pet owners, because they are easy to maintain (requiring no special diet or conditions), require little space (because of their small size), are generally not aggressive to people and tolerate living with other members of their species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanderlip_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vanderlip-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW-campbelli_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-campbelli-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Phodopus</i> species have developed into important laboratory organisms. They provide an excellent model system for the evolution of hormonal control of <a href="/wiki/Photoperiodism" title="Photoperiodism">photoperiod</a> and seasonal physiological and behavioral changes, sociality, biparental offspring care, and behavioral ecology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feoktistova_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Feoktistova-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wynne-Edwards_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wynne-Edwards-24">[24]</a></sup> Because <i>Phodopus</i> species have small chromosome numbers with easily distinguished chromosomes, they have been used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytogenetic" title="Cytogenetic">cytogenetic</a> and cancer research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanHoosier_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-VanHoosier-16">[16]</a></sup> Because of their extreme cold tolerance, they have been used to study <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulation</a>. One population of laboratory animals developed both <a href="/wiki/Glycosuria" title="Glycosuria">glycosuria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ketonuria" title="Ketonuria">ketonuria</a> independently and has been used as a model for <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes mellitus</a>.</p>
<p>The first laboratory use of <i>Phodopus</i> stemmed from a breeding colony established in Leningrad at the Zoological Institute of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Academy of Sciences of the Soviet Union">Academy of Sciences of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanHoosier_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-VanHoosier-16">[16]</a></sup> The animals became the seed stock from which other European lab colonies were started. The animals were said to have been trapped in Tuva and Siberia and were labeled as <i>P. sungorus campbelli</i>. Both <i>P. sungorus</i> and <i>P. campbelli</i> are popular research animals, especially as they differ enough in behavior and physiology to support fruitful comparison studies. More recently, <i>P. roborovskii</i> became an important research animal, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feoktistova_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Feoktistova-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
