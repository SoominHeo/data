<p>In <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a>, <b>score</b> refers to an abstract quantity associated with a player or team. Score is usually measured in the abstract unit of points, and events in the game can raise or lower the score of different parties. Most games with score use it as a quantitative indicator of success in the game, and in competitive games, a goal is often made of attaining a better score than one's opponents in order to win.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:190px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_the_Mask_animated_screenshot_3_optimized.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="188" height="334" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Tomb_of_the_Mask_animated_screenshot_3_optimized.gif" width="188"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_the_Mask_animated_screenshot_3_optimized.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In <i>Tomb of the Mask</i>, the player collects coins, which are converted to points counted atop the screen.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">video games</a> that features a scoring, points are usually an optional, side component of gaming. Players may achieve points through normal gameplay, but their score will often not have an immediate relevance to the game itself. Instead, playing to beat a "high score" set by the game program, another player or onself becomes an extra challenge, adding <a href="/wiki/Replay_value" title="Replay value">replay value</a>.</p>
<p>In modern gaming, the presence of a score is not ubiquitous as in the past. Instead during the era of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcade_games" title="Arcade games">arcade games</a>, when, because of the technical limitations of the time, games could not be "won" or "completed" but were instead endless cycles of continuous <a href="/wiki/Gameplay" title="Gameplay">gameplay</a>, points had a much greater relevance. Many modern games no longer even keep track of score, and many that do no longer feature an option to save or record high scores. However, some games (particularly <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing games</a>) have <a href="/wiki/Experience_point" title="Experience point">experience points</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skill_point" title="Skill point">skill points</a>, and use <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Treasure" title="Treasure">treasure</a>) which can all be used to buy/<a href="/wiki/Upgrade" title="Upgrade">upgrade</a> skills/objects, but these are not really considerable scores, being embellishments to their "growing" characters instead of the numerical evaluation of their gaming performance in a given match.</p>
<p>Sometimes the score of a game can have relevance to gameplay. In <a href="/wiki/Fighting_game" title="Fighting game">fighting games</a>, for example, scoring a very high number of points could result in <a href="/wiki/Unlockable_(gaming)" title="Unlockable (gaming)">unlockable</a> players or modes. In some games, reaching certain scores gives <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra_life" title="Extra life">extra lifes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring">Scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In puzzle games, scores are usually gained by solving the puzzles quickly. Higher scores can be gained by performing combos of puzzle solving. There is often a time bonus which can add extra points. The level number is often a multiplier on the points, so higher scores are possible on harder levels. Level multipliers can also be picked up in some games, to further multiply your points bonus.</p>
<p>In other games, points are typically gained from defeating monsters and enemies. When defeating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boss_(video_games)" title="Boss (video games)">boss</a>, a proportionally large number of points is usually rewarded. Extra points can be gained from gathering <a href="/wiki/Item_(gaming)" title="Item (gaming)">items</a>, such as power-ups or other pick-ups.</p>
<p>Usually, when a player gets a certain number of points, they may get an <a href="/wiki/1-up" title="1-up">extra life</a> or go on to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experience_level" title="Experience level">higher level</a>. Points can be often used as currency which can redeemed for rewards and player upgrades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_score">High score</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: High score">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>high score</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> is usually the highest logged point value. Many times a game will have a list of several high scores, called the <b>high score table.</b></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seawolfscreenshot.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="256" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b9/Seawolfscreenshot.png/220px-Seawolfscreenshot.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/b9/Seawolfscreenshot.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seawolfscreenshot.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Wolf_(video_game)" title="Sea Wolf (video game)">Sea Wolf</a></i>, the first video game to use the term "high score".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The concept of a high score first achieved cultural significance with the rise in popularity of <a href="/wiki/Pinball" title="Pinball">pinball</a> machines and <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game#Electro-mechanical_games" title="Arcade game">electro-mechanical</a> <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade games</a>. Players who achieve a high score are often greeted with a congratulatory message and are able to enter their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Initials" title="Initials">initials</a> or name into the machine. Their score and name will remain there until someone "knocks" them off the high score list by achieving a higher score. For this reason, high scores are inherently competitive and may sometimes involve <a href="/wiki/One-upmanship" title="One-upmanship">one-upmanship</a> against other players.</p>
<p>The high score has a close association to the "free game." When in an arcade, many games will offer a player a free chance at another game if they achieve a high score. This has declined in popularity in recent years, as players are often allowed to play for as long as they can without losing, but not given free games even if they achieve a high score.</p>
<p>The first video game to use the term "high score" was <a href="/wiki/Midway_Games" title="Midway Games">Midway</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Wolf_(video_game)" title="Sea Wolf (video game)">Sea Wolf</a></i> (1976). In these early video games, the player would attempt to reach a pre-determined high score within an allotted time period, after which they would win bonus playing time, since it was not possible to save the top score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Symonds_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Symonds-1">[1]</a></sup> Though the term "high score" was not used, the concept of reaching pre-determined scores to win bonus playing time was featured in earlier arcade video games such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taito_Corporation" title="Taito Corporation">Taito</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Racing_video_game" title="Racing video game">racing game</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speed_Race" title="Speed Race">Speed Race</a></i> (1974).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The high score concept changed in 1978 with the release of Taito's <a href="/wiki/Shoot_%27em_up" title="Shoot 'em up">shoot 'em up</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Invaders" title="Space Invaders">Space Invaders</a></i>, where high scores were determined by gamers playing for as long as they could stay alive as high scores kept rising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Symonds_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Symonds-1">[1]</a></sup> This was made possible due to being the first game to <a href="/wiki/Saved_game" title="Saved game">save</a> the player's score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN-10_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-10-3">[3]</a></sup> The astounding popularity of <i>Space Invaders</i> stemmed from players returning to beat the current high score, as players could now compete with each other over who had the highest score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Symonds_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Symonds-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1979, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Invaders_Part_II" title="Space Invaders Part II">Space Invaders Part II</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Fire" title="Star Fire">Star Fire</a></i> took this concept a step further by allowing players to enter initials next to their score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Symonds_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Symonds-1">[1]</a></sup>Since this data was stored in the machine's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_access_memory" title="Random access memory">random access memory</a> (RAM), it was deleted every time the machine lost power, which in practice would almost invariably happen every night as operators preferred to leave the machines unplugged when the arcade was closed to avoid incurring unnecessary power costs.</p>
<p>The popularity of the high score has made it nearly ubiquitous among modern video games. In fact, the high score has become a defining feature of many games. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_magazine" title="Video game magazine">Magazines</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Power" title="Nintendo Power">Nintendo Power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sega_Visions" title="Sega Visions">Sega Visions</a> would often publish high scores submitted by their readers. The high score became most popular when, starting in 1982, the <a href="/wiki/Twin_Galaxies" title="Twin Galaxies">Twin Galaxies</a> Scoreboard began to appear in the pages of Video Games Magazine, Joystik Magazine, Computer Games Magazine, VideoGiochi Magazine (Milano, Italy), Video Games Player Magazine and Electronic Fun Magazine. Later, under Twin Galaxies direction in the 1990s, all performances would have to be <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">videotaped</a> to verify the achievement.</p>
<p>The high score also exists in online games in various forms. The spread of the Internet has made it possible to compete with the rest of the world, rather than the players of a single machine or game. many modern games have the ability to post his/her high score to a central webpage. Online multiplayer games, especially <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first person shooters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Real-time_strategy" title="Real-time strategy">real time strategies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> often have <a href="/wiki/Rank_up" title="Rank up">ranking systems</a>. These new high score lists and ranking systems often are more complex than conventional high score lists. Some are based on tournaments, while others track game servers continuously, keeping statistics for all players.</p>
<p>Some games include default "high scores" that do not actually represent real players, but are displayed whenever the machine's memory is reset, often with generic initials such as "AAA." These scores often represent certain levels of achievement for a player to aspire to, ensuring that there is always something for players to compete with. Many video games also have default high scores built in, sometimes attributed to fictitious entities (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Commander_Keen" title="Commander Keen">Commander Keen</a></i>) or to members of the game's development team.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The high score's prominence in video game culture, and even mainstream society has led to various pieces of art and entertainment. There is a <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoon</a> titled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=High_Score_(webtoon)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="High Score (webtoon) (page does not exist)">High Score</a></i>. There is also a <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">book</a> entitled <i><a href="/wiki/High_Score!:_The_Illustrated_History_of_Electronic_Games" title="High Score!: The Illustrated History of Electronic Games">High Score!: The Illustrated History of Electronic Games</a></i>. A 2007 documentary, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_King_of_Kong:_A_Fistful_of_Quarters" title="The King of Kong: A Fistful of Quarters">The King of Kong: A Fistful of Quarters</a></i>, follows the attempts to beat the high score in <i>Donkey Kong</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/The_Frogger" title="The Frogger">an episode</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">TV series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Seinfeld" title="Seinfeld">Seinfeld</a></i>, George is astonished to find that the <i><a href="/wiki/Frogger" title="Frogger">Frogger</a></i> machine he played as a teen still retains his high score. With the owners wanting to get rid of it, George decides to keep the machine for posterity, the catch being that he has to move the game without unplugging it, because if he unplugs the game the high score will be erased. Unfortunately the machine is destroyed when he unsuccessfully tries to move it across the street in a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">spoof</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gameplay" title="Gameplay">gameplay</a>.</p>
<p>On September 24, 2005, Twin Galaxies issued Poster #59, which publicized a $1,000 prize to the first gamer who could break George Costanza's fictitious Frogger high score of 863,050 points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 1, 1982, the <a href="/wiki/Twin_Galaxies" title="Twin Galaxies">Twin Galaxies</a> Intergalactic Scoreboard issued a colorful poster that listed the world record high scores for more than two dozen arcade video games. The poster was distributed among arcades in the U.S., Canada and abroad. This was the first poster (#1) in a series of colorful posters that continues today, with poster #131 issued in October, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Friends" title="Friends">Friends</a></i> Chandler Bing puts in dirty words on all the high score positions on a PacMan machine. He then finds out that they are not blanked when the machine is reset so he has to break all his high scores to remove the offending words. (This is not possible on an actual PacMan machine; such machines only record one high score and do not allow the winning player to enter initials.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_media">In the media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In the media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Twin_Galaxies" title="Twin Galaxies">Twin Galaxies</a> Intergalactic Scoreboard, "high-score" attempts enjoyed as much press coverage as any other video-game-related topic reported in the media during the 1982-1985 period. Though the media was often focused on the amazing growth of the video game industry, it was equally as fascinated with the human side of gaming, as typified by the "player vs machine" showdowns that led to new world record high scores set on nearly a daily basis. In fact, Twin Galaxies reports that during that early era it was not unusual for there to be multiple new world records reported in the media on a single day.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Score_attack">Score attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Score attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some games feature a 'score attack' mode, which charges the player with gathering the highest score possible. Games with a mode like this include, for example, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rez" title="Rez">Rez</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gradius_IV_Fukkatsu" title="Gradius IV Fukkatsu">Gradius IV Fukkatsu</a></i>. Some games do not include this mode but keep a record of the score, in which the players can choose whether they score attack the game, or continue normally.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring_bugs">Scoring bugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Score_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scoring bugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many games, a good player can get the "maximum score" due to a limitation or bug in the game. A typical limit is 999,999 points or 999,999,999. In some eight-bit systems and games, getting a score of 255 as the maximum was common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
