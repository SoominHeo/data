<p><b>Deulkkae</b>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">들깨</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[tɯl.k͈ɛ]</a></span>), <i>Perilla frutescens</i> var. <i>frutescens,</i> or <b>perilla</b> is one of the two distinct varieties of <a href="/wiki/Perilla_frutescens" title="Perilla frutescens">Perilla frutescens</a>. The other variety, <i>Perilla frutescens</i> var. <i>crispa</i>, is called <a href="/wiki/Shiso" title="Shiso">shiso</a>.</p>
<p>The leaf of deulkkae is called <b>kkaennip</b>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">깻잎</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[k͈ɛn.nip̚]</a></span>), or <b>perilla leaf</b>.</p>
<p>Deulkkae is an <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual plant</a> native to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> highlands, and grown in <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">Southern China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> The plant was introduced into <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> before <a href="/wiki/Later_Silla" title="Later Silla">Unified Silla era</a>, when it was widely cultivated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, the name <i>kkae</i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">깨</span>) refers to both the <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a> and perilla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">Sesame</a> is called <i>chamkkae</i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">참깨</span>), which literally means "true <i>kkae</i>", while perilla is called <i>deulkkae</i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">들깨</span>), which literally means "wild <i>kkae</i>". Since <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a> is considered as the regular <i>kkae</i> in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean-speaking communities</a>, <i>kkae</i> has often been translated into <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a>. This (mis-)translation custom thus gave the nickname "wild sessame" to <i>deulkkae</i>.</p>
<p>The plant is called <i>egoma</i>(<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">エゴマ/荏胡麻</span>) in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, and <i>báizĭsū</i>(<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">白紫苏</span>) in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deulkkae is an <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual plant</a> growing 60–90 centimetres (24–35 in) tall, with hairy <a href="/wiki/Square" title="Square">square</a> <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stalks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are <a href="/wiki/Phyllotaxis" title="Phyllotaxis">opposite</a>, 7–12 centimetres (2.8–4.7 in) long and 5–8 centimetres (2.0–3.1 in) wide, with an broad oval shape, pointy ends, <a href="/wiki/Serration" title="Serration">serrated</a>(saw-toothed) margins, and long <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">leafstalks</a>. The leaves are <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a> with occasional touches of <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a> on the underside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> bloom on <a href="/wiki/Raceme" title="Raceme">racemes</a> at the end of <a href="/wiki/Branch" title="Branch">branches</a> and the main <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stalk</a> in August and September. The <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">calyx</a>, 3–4 millimetres (0.12–0.16 in) long, consist of upper three sepals and the hairy lower two. The <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">corolla</a> is 4–5 millimetres (0.16–0.20 in) long with its lower lip longer than the upper. Two of the four <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a> are long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fruit is a <a href="/wiki/Schizocarp" title="Schizocarp">schizocarp</a>, 2 millimetres (0.079 in) in <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, and with <a href="/wiki/Reticulate" title="Reticulate">reticulate</a> pattern on the outside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> Deulkkae seeds can be soft and hard, being <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_white" title="Shades of white">white</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_gray" title="Shades of gray">grey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_brown" title="Shades of brown">brown</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_brown" title="Shades of brown">dark brown</a> in colour<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and globular in shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-7">[7]</a></sup> 1000 seeds weight about 4 grams (0.14 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-7">[7]</a></sup> Deulkkae seeds contain about 38-45% <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In its natural state, the yield of deulkkae is not high. If the <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stem</a> is cut about 5 centimetres (2.0 in) above ground level in <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, a new <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stalk</a> grows and it produces more <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">Leaves</a> can be harvested from the <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stem</a> that cut off in <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, ass well as from the new stalk and its <a href="/wiki/Branch" title="Branch">branches</a>, throughout <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> are harvested in <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> are <a href="/wiki/Ripening" title="Ripening">ripe</a>. To collect deulkkae <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, the whole plant is harvested and the seeds are beat out of the plant, before spread for <a href="/wiki/Food_drying" title="Food drying">sundry</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">Culinary use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Culinary use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seeds">Seeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Seeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deulkkae_(perilla).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="699" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Deulkkae_%28perilla%29.jpg/220px-Deulkkae_%28perilla%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Deulkkae_%28perilla%29.jpg/330px-Deulkkae_%28perilla%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Deulkkae_%28perilla%29.jpg/440px-Deulkkae_%28perilla%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deulkkae_(perilla).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
deulkkae seeds</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>, deulkkae seeds are either toasted and grounded into powder called <i>dulkkaetgaru</i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">들깻가루</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[tɯl.k͈ɛt̚.k͈a.ɾu]</a></span>), or used to make <a href="/wiki/Perilla_oil" title="Perilla oil">perilla oil</a> called <i>deulgireum</i>(<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">들기름</span>, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[tɯl.gi.ɾɯm]</a></span>).</p>
<p>Toasted deulkkae powder is used as condiment when making <a href="/wiki/Guk" title="Guk">guk</a>(soup), <a href="/wiki/Namul" title="Namul">namul</a>(seasoned vegetable), <a href="/wiki/Korean_noodles" title="Korean noodles">guksu</a>(noodles), <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kamaboko" title="Kamaboko">eomuk</a>(fish cake). It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">desserts</a>: <a href="/wiki/Yeot" title="Yeot">Yeot</a> and several <a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">tteok</a>(rice cake) varieties can be coated in toasted deulkkae powder. In <a href="/wiki/Fusion_cuisine" title="Fusion cuisine">Korean-style western food</a>, toasted deulkkae powder is used in <a href="/wiki/Salad" title="Salad">salad dressing</a> as well as in <a href="/wiki/Dipping_sauce" title="Dipping sauce">dipping sauce</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_oil">Seed oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Seed oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Perilla_oil" title="Perilla oil">Perilla oil</a></div>
<p>Toasted and untoasted <a href="/wiki/Perilla_oil" title="Perilla oil">perilla oil</a> is used as cooking oil, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>. Toasted <a href="/wiki/Perilla_oil" title="Perilla oil">perilla oil</a> is also used as a condiment. The untoasted perilla oil was also used for non-culinary purposes: for lamps, oilpaper, etc. The <a href="/wiki/Press_cake" title="Press cake">press cake</a> remaining after pressing <a href="/wiki/Perilla_oil" title="Perilla oil">perilla oil</a> is used as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertiliser</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">animal feed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaves">Leaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deulkkae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Leaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>, <i>kkaennip</i>, the perilla leaves, is used fresh as <a href="/wiki/Ssam" title="Ssam">ssam</a> vegetable or <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickled</a> in <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">soy sauce</a> or <a href="/wiki/Doenjang" title="Doenjang">soybean paste</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Jangajji" title="Jangajji">jangajji</a>. It is used as <a href="/wiki/Namul" title="Namul">namul</a> vegetable, or as <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herb</a> in the preparation of many dishes, including meat dishes and <a href="/wiki/Fried_rice" title="Fried rice">fried rice</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a> made of perilla leaves are called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kkaenip_kimchi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kkaenip kimchi (page does not exist)">kkaenip kimchi</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Fusion_cuisine" title="Fusion cuisine">Korean-style western food</a>, perilla leaves are sometimes used to substitute <a href="/wiki/Basil" title="Basil">basil</a>.</p>
