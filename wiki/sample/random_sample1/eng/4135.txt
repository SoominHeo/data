<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Deuteragonist</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, the <b>deuteragonist</b> or <b>secondary main character</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">δευτεραγωνιστής</span>, <i>deuteragōnistḗs</i>, second actor) is the second most important character, after the <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> and before the <a href="/wiki/Tritagonist" title="Tritagonist">tritagonist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deut1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-deut1-1">[1]</a></sup> The deuteragonist may switch from being with or against the protagonist depending on the deuteragonist's own conflict/plot.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuteragonist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> began with simply one actor, the protagonist, and a <a href="/wiki/Greek_chorus" title="Greek chorus">chorus</a> of dancers. The playwright <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> introduced the deuteragonist; Aristotle says in his <i><a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Poetics</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Καὶ τό τε τῶν ὑποκριτῶν πλῆθος ἐξ ἑνὸς εἰς δύο πρῶτος Αἰσχύλος ἤγαγε καὶ τὰ τοῦ χοροῦ ἠλάττωσε καὶ τὸν λόγον πρωταγωνιστεῖν παρεσκεύασεν</span></p>
<p><i><span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">Kai to te tōn hypokritōn plēthos ex enos eis duo prōtos Aiskhilos ēgage kai ta tou khorou ēlattōse kai ton logon prōtagōnistein pareskeuasen</span></i></p>
Thus, it was Aeschylus who first raised the number of the actors from one to two. He also curtailed the chorus and made the dialogue be the leading part.
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Poetics</a> (1449a15)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deut2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-deut2-2">[2]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Aeschylus' efforts brought the dialogue and interaction between characters to the forefront and set the stage for other playwrights of the era, like <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a>, to produce many iconic plays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deut3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-deut3-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drama">Drama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuteragonist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because Ancient Greek drama involved only three actors (the protagonist, deuteragonist, and tritagonist) plus the chorus, each actor often played several parts. For instance, in Sophocles' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oedipus_the_King" title="Oedipus the King">Oedipus Rex</a></i>, the protagonist would be <a href="/wiki/Oedipus" title="Oedipus">Oedipus</a>, who is on stage in most acts, the deuteragonist would be <a href="/wiki/Jocasta" title="Jocasta">Jocasta</a> (Oedipus' mother and wife), as well as the Shepherd and Messenger. This would be because Jocasta is certainly a major role—acting opposite Oedipus many times and occupying a central part of the story—and because the Shepherd and Messenger are onstage when Jocasta is offstage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deut4_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-deut4-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuteragonist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Literarily, the deuteragonist often assumes the role of "<a href="/wiki/Sidekick" title="Sidekick">sidekick</a>" to the protagonist. In <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Huckleberry_Finn" title="The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn">The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn</a></i>, the protagonist is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huckleberry_Finn_(character)" title="Huckleberry Finn (character)">Huck</a> and the deuteragonist, his constant companion, is <a href="/wiki/Jim_(Huckleberry_Finn)" title="Jim (Huckleberry Finn)">Jim</a>. In this story the tritagonist would be <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sawyer" title="Tom Sawyer">Tom Sawyer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deut5_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-deut5-5">[5]</a></sup> Conversely, the deuteragonist could also be a particularly visible <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonist</a>, normally whom the actual antagonist hides behind. In some cases, the deuteragonist is a sidekick who is also used as a foil for the protagonist, in order to more greatly enhance the powers or strengths of the main character. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctor_Watson" title="Doctor Watson">Dr. John Watson</a>, for example, in the <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> series by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Sir Arthur Conan Doyle">Sir Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, is an educated and intelligent gentleman of professional standing, and yet his own intelligence is of too rigid a stance to embrace fully the kind of possibilities of which the more maverick <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> is capable.</p>
