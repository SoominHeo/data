<p><b>Dido</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">DY</span></span></b>-doh</i></a></span>, <small>Latin pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Latin" title="Help:IPA for Latin">[ˈdiːdoː]</a></span>) was, according to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> sources, the founder and first queen of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Carthage</a> (in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>). She is primarily known from the account given by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> poet <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>. In some sources she is also known as <b>Elissa</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>ee-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LISS</span></span></b>-ə</i></a></span>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_accounts">Early accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2012" data-file-width="2024" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg/250px-Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg/375px-Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg/500px-Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meister_des_Vergilius_Vaticanus_001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Aeneid, Book IV, Death of Dido. From the <a href="/wiki/Vergilius_Vaticanus" title="Vergilius Vaticanus">Vergilius Vaticanus</a> (Vatican Library, Cod. Vat. lat. 3225).</div>

<p>The person of Dido can be traced to references by Roman historians to lost writings of <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(historian)" title="Timaeus (historian)">Timaeus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Taormina" title="Taormina">Tauromenium</a> in Sicily (c. 356–260 BC).</p>
<p>Historians gave various dates, both for the foundation of Carthage and the foundation of Rome. <a href="/wiki/Appian" title="Appian">Appian</a> in the beginning of his <i>Punic Wars</i> claims that Carthage was founded by a certain Zorus and Carchedon, but <i>Zorus</i> looks like an alternative transliteration of the city name <i>Tyre</i> and <i>Carchedon</i> is just the Greek form of <i>Carthage</i>. Timaeus made Carchedon's wife Elissa the sister of King <a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_of_Tyre" title="Pygmalion of Tyre">Pygmalion of Tyre</a>. Archaeological evidence of settlement on the site of Carthage before the last quarter of the 8th century BC has yet to be found. Paucity of material for this period may be explained by rejection of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Dark_Ages" title="Greek Dark Ages">Greek Dark Age</a> theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> That the city is named <span class="script-phoenician" dir="rtl" style="direction: rtl; unicode-bidi: bidi-override; font-family: 'ALPHABETUM Unicode', 'MPH 2B Damase', Aegean, Code2001, 'Noto Sans Phoenician', FreeSans, 'Segoe UI Historic', sans-serif;" title="Phoenician alphabet">𐤒𐤓𐤕 𐤇𐤃𐤔𐤕</span> (<i>Qart-hadasht</i>, or "New City") at least indicates it was a colony.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The only surviving full account before Virgil's treatment is that of Virgil's contemporary <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Pompeius_Trogus" title="Gnaeus Pompeius Trogus">Gnaeus Pompeius Trogus</a> in his <i>Philippic histories</i> as rendered in a digest or epitome made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junianus_Justinus" title="Junianus Justinus">Junianus Justinus</a> in the 3rd century AD.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a> quoting or paraphrasing Trogus states (18.4–6), a king of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyre_(Lebanon)" title="Tyre (Lebanon)">Tyre</a> whom Justin does not name, made his very beautiful daughter Dido and son Pygmalion his joint heirs. But on his death the people took Pygmalion alone as their ruler though Pygmalion was yet still a boy. Dido married <a href="/wiki/Acerbas" title="Acerbas">Acerbas</a> her uncle who as priest of <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>—that is, <a href="/wiki/Melqart" title="Melqart">Melqart</a>—was second in power to King Pygmalion. Acerbas (Sicharbas, Zacherbas) can be equated with the Zikarbaal king of Byblos mentioned in the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Story_of_Wenamun" title="Story of Wenamun"><i>Tale of Wenamon</i></a>. Rumor told that Acerbas had much wealth secretly buried and King Pygmalion had Acerbas murdered in hopes of gaining this wealth. Dido, desiring to escape Tyre, expressed a wish to move into Pygmalion's palace, but then ordered the attendants whom Pygmalion sent to aid in the move, to throw all Acerbas' bags of gold into the sea apparently as an offering to his spirit. In fact these bags contained only sand. Dido then persuaded the attendants to join her in flight to another land rather than face Pygmalion's anger when he discovered what had supposedly become of Acerbas' wealth. Some senators also joined her in her flight.</p>
<p>The party arrived at Cyprus where the priest of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a> joined the expedition. There the exiles also seized about eighty young women who were prostituting themselves on the shore in order to provide wives for the men in the party.</p>
<p>Eventually Dido and her followers arrived on the coast of North Africa where Dido asked the <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berber</a> king <a href="/wiki/Iarbas" title="Iarbas">Iarbas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> for a small bit of land for a temporary refuge until she could continue her journeying, only as much land as could be encompassed by an oxhide. They agreed. Dido cut the oxhide into fine strips so that she had enough to encircle an entire nearby hill, which was therefore afterwards named <i>Byrsa</i> "hide". (This event is commemorated in modern mathematics: The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoperimetry" title="Isoperimetry">isoperimetric problem</a>" of enclosing the maximum area within a fixed boundary is often called the "Dido Problem" in modern <a href="/wiki/Calculus_of_variations" title="Calculus of variations">calculus of variations</a>.) That would become their new home. Many of the local <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a> joined the settlement and both <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a> and envoys from the nearby <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Utica,_Tunisia" title="Utica, Tunisia">Utica</a> urged the building of a city. In digging the foundations an ox's head was found, indicating a city that would be wealthy but subject to others. Accordingly, another area of the hill was dug instead where a horse's head was found, indicating that the city would be powerful in war.</p>
<p>But when the new city of Carthage had been established and become prosperous, Iarbas, a native king of the Maxitani or Mauritani (manuscripts differ), demanded Dido for his wife or he would make war on Carthage. Dido's envoys, fearing Iarbas, told Dido only that Iarbas' terms for peace were that someone from Carthage must dwell permanently with him to teach Phoenician ways and they added that of course no Carthaginian would agree to dwell with such savages. Dido condemned any who would feel that way when they should indeed give their lives for the city if necessary. Dido's envoys then explained that Iarbas had specifically requested Dido as wife. Dido was trapped by her words. But Dido preferred to stay faithful to her first husband and after creating a ceremonial funeral <a href="/wiki/Pyre" title="Pyre">pyre</a> and sacrificing many victims to his spirit in pretense that this was a final honoring of her first husband in preparation for marriage to Iarbas, Dido ascended the pyre, announced that she would go to her husband as they desired, and then slew herself with her sword. After this self-sacrifice Dido was deified and was worshipped as long as Carthage endured. In this account, the foundation of Carthage occurred 72 years before the foundation of Rome.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servius" title="Servius">Servius</a> in his commentary on Virgil's <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i> gives <i>Sicharbas</i> as the name of Dido's husband in early tradition.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Questions_of_historicity_and_dating">Questions of historicity and dating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Questions of historicity and dating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="1000" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg/220px-Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg/330px-Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg/440px-Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Affresco_romano_-_Enea_e_di.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dido and <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>, from a Roman fresco, <a href="/wiki/Pompeian_Styles" title="Pompeian Styles">Pompeian Third Style</a> (10 BC - 45 AD), <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, Italy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The oxhide story which explains the name of the hill must be of Greek origin since <i>Byrsa</i> means "oxhide" in Greek, not in <a href="/wiki/Punic_language" title="Punic language">Punic</a>. The name of the hill in Punic was probably just a derivation from <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">Semitic</a> <i>brt</i> "fortified place". But that does not prevent other details in the story from being Carthaginian tradition though still not necessarily historical. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Grant_(author)" title="Michael Grant (author)">Michael Grant</a> in <i>Roman Myths</i> (1973) claims:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"That is to say, Dido-Elissa was originally a goddess."</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>It has been conjectured that she was first converted from a goddess into a mortal queen in some Greek work of the later 5th century BC.</dd>
</dl>
<p>But others conjecture that Dido was indeed historical, as described in the following accounts.</p>
<p>It is not known who first combined the story of Dido with the tradition that connected Aeneas either with Rome or with earlier settlements from which Rome traced its origin.</p>
<p>A fragment of an epic poem by <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Naevius" title="Gnaeus Naevius">Gnaeus Naevius</a> who died at Utica in 201 BC includes a passage which might or might not be part of a conversation between Aeneas and Dido. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servius" title="Servius">Servius</a> in his commentary (4.682; 5.4) cites <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro" title="Marcus Terentius Varro">Varro</a> (1st century BC ) for a version in which Dido's sister Anna killed herself for love of Aeneas.</p>
<p>Evidence for the historicity of Dido (which is a question independent of whether or not she ever met Aeneas) can be associated with evidence for the historicity of others in her family, such as her brother Pygmalion and their grandfather Balazeros. Both of these kings are mentioned, as well as Dido, in the list of Tyrian kings given in <a href="/wiki/Menander_of_Ephesus" title="Menander of Ephesus">Menander of Ephesus</a>'s list of the kings of Tyre, as preserved in <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Against_Apion" title="Against Apion">Against Apion</a></i>, i.18. Josephus ends his quotation of Menander with the sentence "Now, in the seventh year of his [Pygmalion's] reign, his sister fled away from him and built the city of Carthage in Libya."</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nora_Stone" title="Nora Stone">Nora Stone</a>, found on Sardinia, has been interpreted by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Moore_Cross" title="Frank Moore Cross">Frank Moore Cross</a> as naming Pygmalion as the king of the general who was using the stone to record his victory over the local populace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On paleographic grounds, the stone is dated to the 9th century BC. (Cross's translation, with a longer discussion of the Nora stone, is found in the <a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_of_Tyre" title="Pygmalion of Tyre">Pygmalion</a> article). If Cross's interpretation is correct, this presents inscriptional evidence substantiating the existence of a 9th-century-BC king of Tyre named (in Greek) Pygmalion.</p>
<p>Several scholars have identified Baa‘li-maanzer, the king of Tyre who gave tribute to <a href="/wiki/Shalmaneser_III" title="Shalmaneser III">Shalmaneser III</a> in 841 BC, with <span class="script-phoenician" dir="rtl" style="direction: rtl; unicode-bidi: bidi-override; font-family: 'ALPHABETUM Unicode', 'MPH 2B Damase', Aegean, Code2001, 'Noto Sans Phoenician', FreeSans, 'Segoe UI Historic', sans-serif;" title="Phoenician alphabet">𐤁𐤏𐤋𐤏𐤑𐤅𐤓</span> <i>Ba‘al-‘azor</i> (Phoenician form of the name) or <i><a href="/wiki/Baal-Eser_II" title="Baal-Eser II">Baal-Eser/Balazeros</a></i> (Greek form of the name), Dido's grandfather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This lends credibility to the account in Josephus/Menander that names the kings of Tyre from <a href="/wiki/Abibaal" title="Abibaal">Abibaal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hiram_I" title="Hiram I">Hiram I</a> down to the time of Pygmalion and Dido.</p>
<p>Another possible reference to Balazeros is found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>. It was a common ancient practice of using the hypocoristicon or shortened form of the name that included only the divine element, so that the "Belus" that Virgil names as the father of Dido in the <i>Aeneid</i> may be a reference to her grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Baal-Eser_II" title="Baal-Eser II">Baal-Eser II</a>/Balazeros.</p>
<p>Even more important than the inscriptional and literary references supporting the historicity of Pygmalion and Dido are chronological considerations that give something of a mathematical demonstration of the veracity of the major feature of the Pygmalion/Dido saga, namely the flight of Dido from Tyre in Pygmalion's seventh year, and her eventual founding of the city of Carthage. Classical authors give two dates for the founding of Carthage. The first is that of <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Pompeius_Trogus" title="Gnaeus Pompeius Trogus">Pompeius Trogus</a>, mentioned above, that says this took place 72 years before the foundation of Rome. At least as early as the 1st century BC, and then later, the date most commonly used by Roman writers for the founding of Rome was 753 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This would place Dido's flight in 753 + 72 = 825 BC. Another tradition, that of the Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(historian)" title="Timaeus (historian)">Timaeus</a> (c. 345–260 BC), gives 814 BC for the founding of Carthage. Traditionally most modern scholars have preferred the 814 date. However, the publication of the Shalmaneser text mentioning tribute from <a href="/wiki/Baal-Eser_II" title="Baal-Eser II">Baal-Eser II</a> of Tyre in 841 BC caused a re-examination of this question, since the best texts of Menander/Josephus only allow 22 years from the accession of Baal-Eser/Balazeros until the seventh year of Pygmalion, and measuring back from 814 BC would not allow any overlap of Balazeros with the 841 tribute to Shalmaneser. With the 825 date for the seventh year of Pygmalion, however, Balazeros's last year would coincide with 841 BC, the year of the tribute. Additional evidence in favor of the 825 date is found in the statement of Menander, repeated by Josephus as corroborated from Tyrian court records (<i><a href="/wiki/Against_Apion" title="Against Apion">Against Apion</a></i> i.17,18), that Dido's flight (or the founding of Carthage) occurred 143 years and eight months after <a href="/wiki/Hiram_I" title="Hiram I">Hiram</a> of Tyre sent assistance to <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a> for the building of the Temple. Using the 825 date, this Tyrian record would then date the start of Temple construction in 969 or 968 BC, in agreement with the statement in 1 Kings 6:1 that Temple construction began in Solomon's fourth regnal year. Solomon's fourth year can be calculated as starting in the fall of 968 BC when using the widely accepted date of 931/930 BC for the division of the kingdom after the death of Solomon. These chronological considerations therefore definitely favor the 825 date over the 814 date for Dido's departure from Tyre. More than that, the agreement of this date with the timing of the tribute to Shalmaneser and the year when construction of the First Temple began provide evidence for the essential historicity of at least the existence of Pygmalion and Dido as well as their rift in 825 BC that eventually led to the founding of Carthage.</p>
<p>According to J. M. Peñuela, the difference in the two dates for the foundation of Carthage has an explanation if we understand that Dido fled Tyre in 825 BC, but eleven years elapsed before she was given permission by the original inhabitants to build a city on the mainland, years marked by conflict in which the Tyrians first built a small city on an island in the harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Additional information about Dido's activities after leaving Tyre are found in the <a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_of_Tyre" title="Pygmalion of Tyre">Pygmalion</a> article, along with a summary of later scholars who have accepted Peñuela's thesis.</p>
<p>If chronological considerations thus help to establish the basic historicity of Dido, they also serve to refute the idea that she could have had any liaison with <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>. Aeneas fought in the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>, which is conventionally dated anywhere from the 14th to the 12th centuries BC, far too early for Aeneas to have been alive in the time of Dido. Even with the date of 864 BC that historical revisionist <a href="/wiki/David_Rohl" title="David Rohl">David Rohl</a> gives for the end of the Trojan War,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Aeneas would have been about 77 years old when Dido fled Tyre in 825 BC and 88 when she began to build Carthage in 814 (following Peñuela's reconstruction), hardly consistent with the romantic intrigues between Dido and Aeneas imagined by <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Virgil.27s_Aeneid">Virgil's <i>Aeneid</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Virgil's Aeneid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3555" data-file-width="1600" height="444" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg/200px-Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg/300px-Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg/400px-Dido_Cochet_Louvre_ENT2000.10.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<i>Dido</i>, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Christophe_Cochet" title="Christophe Cochet">Christophe Cochet</a>, formerly at <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Marly" title="Château de Marly">Marly</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre" title="Musée du Louvre">Louvre</a>)</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s references in the <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i> generally agree with what Justin's epitome of Trogus recorded. Virgil names Belus as Dido's father, this Belus sometimes being called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belus_II" title="Belus II">Belus II</a> by later commentators to distinguish him from <a href="/wiki/Belus_(Egyptian)" title="Belus (Egyptian)">Belus</a> son of <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Libya_(mythology)" title="Libya (mythology)">Libya</a> in earlier <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>. If the story of Dido has a factual basis and is synchronized properly with history then this Belus should<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> stand for <a href="/wiki/Mattan_I" title="Mattan I">Mattan I</a>, father of the historical Pygmalion.</p>
<p>Virgil (1.343f) adds that the marriage between Dido and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sychaeus" title="Sychaeus">Sychaeus</a>, as Virgil calls Dido's husband, occurred while her father was still alive. Pygmalion slew Sychaeus secretly due to his wealth and Sychaeus appeared to Dido in a dream in which he told the truth about his death, urged her to flee the country, and revealed to her where his gold was buried. She left with those who hated or feared Pygmalion. None of these details contradict Justin's epitome, but Virgil very much changes the import and many details of the story when he brings Aeneas and his followers to Carthage.</p>
<p>(1.657f) Dido and Aeneas fall in love by the management of <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a>, acting in concert, though for different reasons. (4.198f) When the rumour of the love affair comes to King <a href="/wiki/Iarbas" title="Iarbas">Iarbas</a> the Gaetulian, "a son of Jupiter Ammon by a raped <a href="/wiki/Garamantes" title="Garamantes">Garamantian</a> nymph", Iarbas prays to his father, blaming Dido who has scorned marriage with him yet now takes Aeneas into the country as her lord. (4.222f) Jupiter dispatches <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a> to send Aeneas on his way and the pious Aeneas sadly obeys. Mercury tells Aeneas of all the promising Italian lands and orders Aeneas to get his fleet ready.</p>
<p>(4.450f)</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="843" data-file-width="963" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg/250px-Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg/375px-Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg/500px-Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guercino_Morte_di_Didone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Death of Dido</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a>, AD 1631.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dido can no longer bear to live. (4.474) She has her sister Anna build her a pyre under the pretence of burning all that reminded her of Aeneas, including weapons and clothes that Aeneas had left behind and (what she calls) their bridal bed (though, according to <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>, they were never officially married.) (4.584f) When Dido sees Aeneas' fleet leaving she curses him and his Trojans and proclaims endless hate between Carthage and the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a>, foreshadowing the <a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">Punic Wars</a>. (4.642) Dido ascends the pyre, lies again on the couch which she had shared with Aeneas, and then falls on a sword that Aeneas had given her. (4.666) Those watching let out a cry; Anna rushes in and embraces her dying sister; Juno sends <a href="/wiki/Iris_(mythology)" title="Iris (mythology)">Iris</a> from heaven to release Dido's spirit from her body. (5.1) From their ships, Aeneas and his crew see the glow of Dido's burning funeral pyre and can only guess what has happened. At least two scholars have argued that the inclusion of the pyre as part of Dido's suicide—otherwise unattested in epic and tragedy—alludes to the self-immolation that took the life of Carthage's last queen in 146 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>(6.450f) During his journey in the underworld Aeneas meets Dido and tries to excuse himself, but Dido does not deign to look at him. Instead she turns away from Aeneas to a grove where her former husband Sychaeus waits.</p>
<p>Virgil has included most of the motifs from the original: Iarbas who desires Dido against her will, a deceitful explanation for the building of the pyre, and Dido's final suicide. In both versions Dido is loyal to her original husband in the end. But whereas the earlier Elissa remained always loyal to her husband's memory, Virgil's Dido dies as a tortured and repentant woman who has fallen away from that loyalty.</p>
<p>Virgil consistently uses the form <i>Dido</i> as nominative, but derivates of <i>Elissa</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">oblique cases</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Roman_tradition">Later Roman tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later Roman tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Letter 7 of <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i>Heroides</i> is a feigned letter from Dido to Aeneas written just before she ascends the pyre. The situation is as in Virgil's <i>Aeneid</i>. In Ovid's <i>Fasti</i> (3.545f) Ovid introduced a kind of sequel involving Aeneas and Dido's sister Anna. See <a href="/wiki/Anna_Perenna" title="Anna Perenna">Anna Perenna</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Barcids" title="Barcids">Barcids</a>, the family to which <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> belonged, claimed descent from a younger brother of Dido according to <a href="/wiki/Silius_Italicus" title="Silius Italicus">Silius Italicus</a> in his <i>Punica</i> (1.71–7).</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Augustan_History" title="Augustan History">Augustan History</a></i> ("Tyrrani Triginta" 27, 30) claims that <a href="/wiki/Zenobia" title="Zenobia">Zenobia</a> queen of <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a> in the late third century was descended from <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra" title="Cleopatra">Cleopatra</a>, Dido and <a href="/wiki/Semiramis" title="Semiramis">Semiramis</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuing_tradition">Continuing tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Continuing tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Divine_Comedy" title="The Divine Comedy">The Divine Comedy</a></i> Dante sees the shade of Dido in the second circle of Hell, where she is condemned (on account of her consuming lust) to be blasted for eternity in a fierce whirlwind.</p>
<p>This legend inspired the Renaissance drama <i><a href="/wiki/Dido,_Queen_of_Carthage_(play)" title="Dido, Queen of Carthage (play)">Dido, Queen of Carthage</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a>.</p>
<p>Also from the 17th century is a <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a> inspired by the relationship between Dido and Aeneas. The ballad, often printed on a <a href="/wiki/Broadside_(music)" title="Broadside (music)">broadside</a>, is called "<a href="/wiki/The_Wandering_Prince_of_Troy" title="The Wandering Prince of Troy">The Wandering Prince of Troy</a>," and it interestingly alters the end of the relationship between the two lovers, rethinking Dido's final sentiment for Aeneas and rewriting Aeneas's visit to the underworld as Dido's choice to haunt him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The story of Dido and Aeneas remained popular throughout the post-Renaissance era and was the basis for many operas, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>1641: <i><a href="/wiki/Didone_(opera)" title="Didone (opera)">La Didone</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Cavalli" title="Francesco Cavalli">Francesco Cavalli</a></li>
<li>1656: <i>La Didone</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrea_Mattioli" title="Andrea Mattioli">Andrea Mattioli</a></li>
<li>1689: <i><a href="/wiki/Dido_and_Aeneas" title="Dido and Aeneas">Dido and Aeneas</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Purcell" title="Henry Purcell">Henry Purcell</a></li>
<li>1693: <i><a href="/wiki/Didon_(Desmarets)" title="Didon (Desmarets)">Didon</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Desmarets" title="Henry Desmarets">Henry Desmarets</a></li>
<li>1707: <i>Dido, Königin von Carthago</i> by <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Graupner" title="Christoph Graupner">Christoph Graupner</a></li>
<li>1724: <i>Didone abbandonata</i> by <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Sarro" title="Domenico Sarro">Domenico Sarro</a></li>
<li>1740: <i>Didone abbandonata</i> by <a href="/wiki/Baldassare_Galuppi" title="Baldassare Galuppi">Baldassare Galuppi</a></li>
<li>1747: <i>Didone abbandonata</i> by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Jommelli" title="Niccolò Jommelli">Niccolò Jommelli</a></li>
<li>1762: <i><a href="/wiki/Didone_abbandonata_(Sarti)" title="Didone abbandonata (Sarti)">Didone abbandonata</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Sarti" title="Giuseppe Sarti">Giuseppe Sarti</a></li>
<li>1770: <i>Didone abbandonata</i> by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Piccinni" title="Niccolò Piccinni">Niccolò Piccinni</a></li>
<li>1783: <i><a href="/wiki/Didon_(Piccinni)" title="Didon (Piccinni)">Didon</a></i> by Niccolò Piccinni</li>
<li>1823: <i>Didone abbandonata</i> by <a href="/wiki/Saverio_Mercadante" title="Saverio Mercadante">Saverio Mercadante</a></li>
<li>1860: <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Troyens" title="Les Troyens">Les Troyens</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1794 Germany, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_von_Stein" title="Charlotte von Stein">Charlotte von Stein</a> wrote her own drama named <i>Dido</i>, with an autobiographical element - as von Stein had been forsaken by her own lover, the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>, in a manner which she found reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>.</p>
<p>Even today, Dido appears in <a href="/wiki/Sid_Meier" title="Sid Meier">Sid Meier</a>'s strategy games <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_II" title="Civilization II">Civilization II</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_V" title="Civilization V">Civilization V</a></i>, as the leader of the Carthaginian civilization, although she appears alongside Hannibal in the former. In <a href="/wiki/Civilization_V" title="Civilization V">Civilization V</a>, she speaks Phoenician, with a modern Israeli accent.</p>
<p>Remembrance of the story of the bull's hide and the foundation of Carthage is preserved in mathematics in connection with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoperimetry" title="Isoperimetry">Isoperimetric problem</a> which is sometimes called Dido's Problem (and similarly the Isoperimetric theorem is sometimes called Dido's Theorem). It is sometimes stated in such discussion that Dido caused her thong to be placed as a half circle touching the sea coast at each end (which would add greatly to the perimeter) but the sources mention the thong only and say nothing about the sea.</p>
<p>Carthage was the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>'s greatest rival and enemy, and Virgil's Dido in part symbolises this. Even though no Rome existed in her day, Virgil's Dido curses the future progeny of the Trojans. In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> under the Fascist regime, her figure was demonized, perhaps not only as an anti-Roman figure but because she represented together at least three other "unpleasant" qualities: feminine virtue, "<a href="/wiki/Semitic_people" title="Semitic people">Semitic race</a>", and North African civilization. As an innocuous example: when <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s regime named the streets of new quarters in Rome with the characters of Virgil's <i>Aeneid</i>, only the name <i>Dido</i> did not appear.</p>
