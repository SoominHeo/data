<p>The <b>Dimitrov Constitution</b> was the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bulgaria" title="Constitution of Bulgaria">Constitution of Bulgaria</a> in effect from 1947 to 1971. <a href="/wiki/Georgi_Dimitrov" title="Georgi Dimitrov">Georgi Dimitrov</a>, after whom the document is named, guided the framing of the 1947 constitution on the model of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1936_constitution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="1936 constitution of the Soviet Union">1936 constitution of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup> The Dimitrov Constitution guaranteed citizens equality before the law; freedom from discrimination; a universal welfare system; freedom of speech, the press, and assembly; and inviolability of person, domicile, and correspondence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup> But those rights were qualified by a clause prohibiting activity that would jeopardize the attainments of "the national revolution of 9 September 1944."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup> Citizens were guaranteed employment but required to work in a socially useful capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup> The constitution also prescribed a planned national economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup> Private property was allowed, if its possession was not "to the detriment of the public good."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
