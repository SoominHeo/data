<p><b>Demetrios Vikelas</b> (also <i>Demetrius Bikelas</i>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Δημήτριος Βικέλας</span>; February 15, 1835<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> – July 20, 1908) was a Greek businessman and writer; he was the first president of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC), from 1894 to 1896.</p>
<p>After a childhood spent in Greece and <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>), he found fortune in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, where he married. He then moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, on account of his wife. Abandoning business, he dedicated himself to literature and history, and published numerous novels, short stories and essays, which earned him a distinguished reputation.</p>
<p>Because of his reputation and the fact that he lived in Paris, he was chosen to represent Greece in a congress called by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">Pierre de Coubertin</a> in June 1894, which decided to re-establish the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> and to organise them in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> in 1896, designating Vikelas to preside over the organisation committee. After the Games were over, he stepped down, remaining in Athens until his death in 1908.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vikelas was born in <a href="/wiki/Ermoupoli" title="Ermoupoli">Ermoupoli</a>, on the island of <a href="/wiki/Syros" title="Syros">Syros</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. His father was a merchant, originally from <a href="/wiki/Veria" title="Veria">Veria</a> (then part of the Ottoman Empire, today capital of the northern Greek province of <a href="/wiki/Imathia" title="Imathia">Imathia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Macedonia" title="Central Macedonia">Central Macedonia</a>) and his mother, Smaragda, was a member of the rich Melas family. He was educated at home by his mother,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_84_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_84-2">[2]</a></sup> possibly due to his fragile health.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When he was six, the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, and ten years after that to <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">Odessa</a>. There he started working for his father's business.</p>
<p>Already he showed signs of his literary potential. At the age of 17 he translated <i><a href="/wiki/Esther_(drama)" title="Esther (drama)">Esther</a></i>, a tragedy by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Racine" title="Jean Racine">Jean Racine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_84_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_84-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="London.2C_from_business_to_literature">London, from business to literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: London, from business to literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While aged 17, in 1852, he left home to live in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> with his uncles Leon and Vasileios Melas, where he worked for their business, Melas Bros, first as a bookkeeper and then as a partner. He also began to maintain a weekly correspondence with his mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_84_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_84-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>This correspondence, which was kept, is one of the most important in establishing his biography. He also kept a journal in which he recorded not only facts about his daily life but also advice from his uncle Leon and his thoughts on books he had read and plays he was able to attend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_84-85_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_84-85-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his day's work at his uncles' business, he took evening classes at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> (the only university in London which did not require students to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a>). There, he obtained a degree in <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> (the only subject which offered evening classes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_106-107_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_106-107-4">[4]</a></sup> He learned German and Italian. He also took part in fencing, horse-riding and rowing, although circumstances did not allow him to keep these up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_107_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_107-5">[5]</a></sup> He had also become very scholarly, and started to publish — an anthology of poems in 1862 and numerous articles in London periodicals, on the British press and the growing of cotton in Greece.</p>

<p>During the political events of 1863 in Greece, following the revolution which led to the eviction of <a href="/wiki/Otto_of_Greece" title="Otto of Greece">Otto</a> and the enthronement of <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Greece" title="George I of Greece">George I</a>, Vikelas led fundraising efforts in support of the provisional government. He also wrote letters to the main newspapers of the time to demand that Greece's rights be respected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_107-108_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_107-108-6">[6]</a></sup> He became definitively known in the British intellectual world in 1866 when he contacted authors and academics to gain their support for the <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Cretan</a> cause during the <a href="/wiki/Cretan_Revolt_(1866%E2%80%931869)" title="Cretan Revolt (1866–1869)">Cretan Revolt of 1866-1867</a>, for which he raised more fundraising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_108_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_108-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1866, he married Kalliope Geralopoulou, a young sister of Katerini, the wife of one of his uncles, also a member of a rich merchant family in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup> He also became a titular partner in his uncles' business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_108_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_108-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also met and became friends with <a href="/wiki/Charilaos_Trikoupis" title="Charilaos Trikoupis">Charilaos Trikoupis</a> - the son of the Greek ambassador to Britain <a href="/wiki/Spyridon_Trikoupis" title="Spyridon Trikoupis">Spyridon Trikoupis</a>, himself destined to become <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Greece" title="Prime Minister of Greece">Prime Minister of Greece</a>. At the time they met, Charilaos Trikoupis was just starting his diplomatic and political career as an attaché, then <i>chargé d'affaires</i>, of the Greek legation. The two men kept a busy correspondence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demetrius Vikelas continued to gain favour in Greece — in 1868 he published a 30-page statistical article on the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Greece" title="Kingdom of Greece">Kingdom of Greece</a> following a conference at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Statistical_Society" title="Royal Statistical Society">Royal Statistical Society</a>; in 1870 he founded a school for Greek children living in England. All his work — polemic, political, journalistic, historical or literary — had a double objective: to elevate the morals and level of intellect of his country but also to change its reputation with respect to the rest of the world. In his historical essay of 1874, <i>On the Byzantines</i>, he wrote that he wanted to restore the reputation of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_108_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_108-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1876, in the wake of the economic crisis that had started in 1873, and in order not to lose the profits of their work, Vikelas and his uncles dissolved their business (now called "Melas Bros - D. Vikelas"). He thus found himself in command of a comfortable fortune, which allowed him to fully dedicate his time to literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_109_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_109-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paris.2C_the_illness_of_his_wife.2C_and_literature">Paris, the illness of his wife, and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paris, the illness of his wife, and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1874, following the death of her father, Vikelas' wife Kalliope began to suffer from mental problems and showed a number of suicidal tendencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup> The couple tried travelling to ease the illness. In Paris, following another scare, doctors declared Kalliope mad and she stayed for seven and a half months in <a href="/wiki/Jules_Bernard_Luys" title="Jules Bernard Luys">Jules Bernard Luys</a>' asylum in <a href="/wiki/Ivry-sur-Seine" title="Ivry-sur-Seine">Ivry-sur-Seine</a>. True to his character, Vikelas recorded the progress of his wife's mental health daily during the twenty years which followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his journal, from 1872 Vikelas expressed the wish to move to Athens. In 1877, while Kalliope's condition was in remission, they took the opportunity to make the move. Vikelas started to build a home around the corner from the streets of <a href="/wiki/Panepistimiou_Street" title="Panepistimiou Street">Panepistimiou</a> (on which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Athens" title="University of Athens">University</a> was situated) and <a href="/wiki/Akadimias_Street" title="Akadimias Street">Akadimias</a>. However, his wife's health worsened again and he accompanied her to France where she again stayed in Ivry-sur-Seine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his stays in Paris, Vikelas embarked on translating <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> plays into Greek: <i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Othello" title="Othello">Othello</a></i> during his wife's first stay (1878), and <i><a href="/wiki/Macbeth" title="Macbeth">Macbeth</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i> during the second (1881). The public readings of his translations received an enthusiastic welcome in the literary community in Athens. He then also wrote his main literary work: <i>Loukis Laras</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_85_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_85-7">[7]</a></sup> The book first appeared in Athens as a series starting in 1879. The same year, it was translated into French and German. The French translation (which had its first republication in 1880) was included by the Education Minister <a href="/wiki/Jules_Ferry" title="Jules Ferry">Jules Ferry</a> in the list of works which could be given as prizes to good students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_109-110_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_109-110-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:201px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Julietteadam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="202" data-file-width="199" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/Julietteadam.jpg" width="199"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Julietteadam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Juliette_Adam" title="Juliette Adam">Juliette Adam</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Vikelas spent the following fifteen years in Paris, building up contacts with the surrounding intellectuals and literati of the French capital. Consequently, <a href="/wiki/Juliette_Adam" title="Juliette Adam">Juliette Adam</a> dedicated her anthology <i>Poètes grecs contemporains</i> ("Contemporary Greek poets"), published in 1881, to him, and he published in her <i>Nouvelle Revue</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basch_229_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basch_229-11">[11]</a></sup> He wrote for it, as before, numerous articles (on Byzantine history, Eastern issues and Greek political life), novels (a compendium in French and Greek came out in 1887) and even travel guides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_86_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_86-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the linguistic controversy in Greece between <i><a href="/wiki/Katharevousa" title="Katharevousa">Katharevousa</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimotiki" title="Dimotiki">Dimotiki</a></i>, Vikelas chose the middle ground, rejecting the excesses of the Dimotikists just as much as the fierce defenders of the more intellectual language. He suggested using <i>Katharevousa</i> for parliamentary proceedings, for example, but popular language for poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basch_231_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basch_231-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1877 and 1892, he travelled, since at the worst of her crises, his wife could not bear his presence. He returned to Greece, visited Scotland, Switzerland, Spain and Constantinople.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_86_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_86-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1892, he bought a new plot in Athens (between the streets of Kriezotou and Valaoriti) where he built a new residence which was also his final home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_86_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_86-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1893, he helped finance the construction of the Greek Orthodox Church in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_111_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_111-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1894, he received a request from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panellinios_Gymnastikos_Syllogos" title="Panellinios Gymnastikos Syllogos">Pan-Hellenic Gymnastic Club</a>, asking him to assist at a congress on amateurism convened the following month by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">Pierre de Coubertin</a>. After hesitation, he agreed to represent the association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_86_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_86-12">[12]</a></sup> Following the congress it was decided to recreate the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> and to organise them in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>.</p>
<p>Originally, it had been De Coubertin's idea to hold the first celebration of the modern <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympic_Games" title="Summer Olympic Games">Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1900, but Vikelas convinced him and the newly created <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> that they should be held in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, in order to symbolically link them to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Olympic_Games" title="Ancient Olympic Games">original Games</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As the constitution of the IOC at that time required the IOC president to be from the country which would host the next Games, Vikelas became the IOC's first president.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_return_to_Greece">Permanent return to Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Permanent return to Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With his responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/1896_Summer_Olympics" title="1896 Summer Olympics">1896 Summer Olympics</a>, Vikelas returned to Greece for just ten days in autumn 1894. On October the 14th, he received a telegram from doctor Luys informing him that the condition of his wife had worsened. She had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%92dema" title="Œdema">œdemas</a> in her thighs, calves and stomach. She could no longer feed herself. He urgently returned to Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_95_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_95-15">[15]</a></sup> It seems that she then died.</p>
<p>In November 1894, a number of young nationalist officers, advocates of the <a href="/wiki/Megali_Idea" title="Megali Idea">Megali Idea</a>, created a secret society, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethniki_Etairia" title="Ethniki Etairia">Ethniki Etairia</a>, whose aim was to revive the morale of the country and prepare the liberation of Greek peoples still under the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_49-50_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_49-50-16">[16]</a></sup> In September 1895, they recruited civilians, all linked to the organisation of the Olympic Games, including Vikelas himself, although he claimed only to have given in to friendly pressure, playing a solely financial role and then quickly resigning from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_149-150_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_149-150-17">[17]</a></sup> At this point he was still attracted by the possibility of rebuilding his country.</p>
<p>After the Games, which proved a success, Vikelas withdrew from the IOC, replaced as a member by the Count Alexander Mercati and as president by Coubertin. The defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Turkish_War_(1897)" title="Greco-Turkish War (1897)">Greco-Turkish War</a> which came soon after dealt a serious blow to his morale. He decided to leave Paris to move permanently to Athens. There he dedicated himself to popular education. In 1899 he founded the "Society for the Spread of Useful Books" in Athens, to help the country to recover from its defeat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1905, he represented the <a href="/wiki/National_and_Kapodistrian_University_of_Athens" title="National and Kapodistrian University of Athens">University of Athens</a> at the third <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Congress" title="Olympic Congress">Olympic Congress</a> and seventh <a href="/wiki/List_of_IOC_meetings" title="List of IOC meetings">IOC Meeting</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Revue_Olympique_132_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Revue_Olympique_132-19">[19]</a></sup> He also remained an active member of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_Olympic_Committee" title="Hellenic Olympic Committee">Hellenic Olympic Committee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olympic_Review_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olympic_Review-20">[20]</a></sup> He died in Athens on 20 July 1908 "from an afflicting illness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Revue_Olympique_131_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Revue_Olympique_131-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>He had been made a knight of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" title="Légion d'honneur">Legion of Honour</a> on 31 December 1891, and <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary doctor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">University of St Andrews</a> in November 1893 (the first Greek to receive this honour).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_111_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_111-14">[14]</a></sup> He was a member (from 1874, and Vice-President from 1894<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolianitis_111_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dolianitis_111-14">[14]</a></sup>) of the French "Association for the Promotion of Greek Studies", and of the Society for the Promotion of Hellenic Studies in London.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He left his immense library collection to the city of <a href="/wiki/Heraklion" title="Heraklion">Heraklion</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, founding the Vikelaia Municipal Library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heraklion_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heraklion-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though in fact he did not live much of his life in <a href="/wiki/Syros" title="Syros">Syros</a>, the island counts him among its most well-known sons. Today, the Sports Center (Stadium) in <a href="/wiki/Ermoupoli" title="Ermoupoli">Ermoupoli</a> bears Demetrios Vikelas' name. The stadium seats 2000 people, and has an Olympic-size <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">swimming pool</a>, four <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> courts, two gym halls, <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a> courts, <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>, floor football court and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">soccer</a> field.</p>
<p>Also the <a href="/wiki/Syros_Island_National_Airport" title="Syros Island National Airport">Syros Island National Airport</a> is named for him.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Olympic_Movement">The Olympic Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Olympic Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Olympic_Congress">First Olympic Congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First Olympic Congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">Pierre de Coubertin</a> had already attempted to restart the Olympic Games at the congress for the fifth anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Union_des_Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9s_Fran%C3%A7aises_de_Sports_Athl%C3%A9tiques" title="Union des Sociétés Françaises de Sports Athlétiques">Union des Sociétés Françaises de Sports Athlétiques</a> in 1892. While he may have raised the enthusiasm of the public, he didn't manage to establish a proper commitment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_87_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_87-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BASA-3K-7-422-5-1896_Summer_Olympics.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> at the first Olympic Games in Athens. Vikelas is seated in the center</div>
</div>

<p>He decided to reiterate his efforts at the congress in 1894 which followed, which would openly address the issue of amateur sports, but also with the sub-text of the recreation of the Olympic Games. Six of the seven points which would be debated pertained to amateurism (definition, disqualification, betting, etc.) and the seventh on the possibility of restoring the Games. Coubertin also sought to give an international dimension to his congress. He gained support from several personalities: the <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II_of_Belgium" title="Leopold II of Belgium">King of the Belgians</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">Prince of Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_of_Greece" title="Constantine I of Greece">Diadochus Constantine</a> (hereditary prince of Greece) and <a href="/wiki/William_Penny_Brookes" title="William Penny Brookes">William Penny Brookes</a>, the founder of the "Olympian Games" in Shropshire, England, and Ioannis Phokianos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_79-81_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_79-81-24">[24]</a></sup> Phokianos was a professor of mathematics and physics and a college principal. Phokianos was also one of the propagators of sport in Greece and the organiser of an Olympic Games sponsored by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelis_Zappas" title="Evangelis Zappas">Evangelis Zappas</a> in 1875. In 1888, Phokianos organised an elite and private Games as the founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panellinios_Gymnastikos_Syllogos" title="Panellinios Gymnastikos Syllogos">Pan-Hellenic Gymnastic Club</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_61_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_61-25">[25]</a></sup> Phokianos could not travel to Paris for financial reasons and because he was finalising the construction of his new college. He turned to one of the more eminent representatives of the Greek community in Paris, Demetrios Vikelas, to whom he wrote to ask him to take part in the congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Llewellyn_Smith_88_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Llewellyn_Smith_88-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<li><i>Loukis Laras</i> was his main work, a historical, patriotic and moral novel. An old rich Greek merchant in London reflects on the adventures of his youth. The novel was published as a series from 1879 in the literary Athenian magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Estia" title="Estia">Estia</a></i>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">Translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Vikelas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
