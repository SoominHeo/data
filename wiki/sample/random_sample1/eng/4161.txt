<p><i><b>Discworld</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Comic_fantasy" title="Comic fantasy">comic</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> <a href="/wiki/Book_series" title="Book series">book series</a> written by the English author <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett" title="Terry Pratchett">Terry Pratchett</a> (1948–2015), set on the fictional <a href="/wiki/Discworld_(world)" title="Discworld (world)">Discworld</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Flat_Earth" title="Flat Earth">flat disc</a> balanced on the backs of four elephants which in turn stand on the back of a giant turtle, Great A'Tuin. The books frequently parody or take inspiration from <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Howard" title="Robert E. Howard">Robert E. Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._P._Lovecraft" title="H. P. Lovecraft">H. P. Lovecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" title="Fairy tales">fairy tales</a>, often using them for satirical parallels with current cultural, political and scientific issues. The series is popular, with more than 80 million books sold in 37 languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Forty-one <i>Discworld</i> novels have been published. Pratchett, who suffered from <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, said that he would be happy for his daughter <a href="/wiki/Rhianna_Pratchett" title="Rhianna Pratchett">Rhianna</a> to continue the series when he is no longer able to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newstatesman_2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-newstatesman_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> However, Rhianna has stated she will only be involved in spin-offs, adaptations and tie-ins, and that there will be no more novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newstatesman_2015_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-newstatesman_2015-4">[4]</a></sup> The original British editions of the first 26 novels, up to <i><a href="/wiki/Thief_of_Time" title="Thief of Time">Thief of Time</a></i> (2001), had distinctive cover art by <a href="/wiki/Josh_Kirby" title="Josh Kirby">Josh Kirby</a>. The American editions, published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harper_Collins" title="Harper Collins">Harper Collins</a>, used their own cover art. Since Kirby's death in October 2001, the covers have been designed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kidby" title="Paul Kidby">Paul Kidby</a>. Companion publications include eleven short stories (some only loosely related to the Discworld), four popular science books, and a number of supplementary books and reference guides. In addition, the series has been adapted for graphic novels, for the theatre, as computer and board games, as music inspired by the series, and repeatedly for television.</p>
<p>Newly released <i>Discworld</i> books regularly topped <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(UK)" title="The Sunday Times (UK)">The Sunday Times</a></i> best-sellers list, making Pratchett the UK's best-selling author in the 1990s. <i>Discworld</i> novels have also won awards such as the <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Award" title="Prometheus Award">Prometheus Award</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Medal_(literary_award)" title="Carnegie Medal (literary award)">Carnegie Medal</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Read" title="The Big Read">Big Read</a>, four <i>Discworld</i> novels were in the top 100, and a total of fourteen in the top 200.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Very few of the <i>Discworld</i> novels have chapter divisions and instead feature interweaving storylines. Pratchett is quoted as saying that he "just never got into the habit of chapters",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp92_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp92-5">[5]</a></sup> later adding that "I have to shove them in the putative YA books because my editor screams until I do".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp93_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp93-6">[6]</a></sup> However, the first <i>Discworld</i> novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colour_of_Magic" title="The Colour of Magic">The Colour of Magic</a></i> was divided into "books", as is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyramids_(Discworld)" title="Pyramids (Discworld)">Pyramids</a></i>. Additionally, <i><a href="/wiki/Going_Postal" title="Going Postal">Going Postal</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Making_Money" title="Making Money">Making Money</a></i> both have chapters, a prologue, an epilogue, and brief teasers of what is to come in each chapter, in the style of <a href="/wiki/A._A._Milne" title="A. A. Milne">A. A. Milne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerome_K._Jerome" title="Jerome K. Jerome">Jerome K. Jerome</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes_and_motifs">Themes and motifs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Themes and motifs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Discworld</i> novels contain common themes and motifs that run through the series. Fantasy clichés are parodied in many of the novels, as are various subgenres of fantasy, such as <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a> (notably <i><a href="/wiki/Witches_Abroad" title="Witches Abroad">Witches Abroad</a></i>), witch and vampire stories (<i><a href="/wiki/Carpe_Jugulum" title="Carpe Jugulum">Carpe Jugulum</a></i>) and so on. Analogies of real-world issues, such as religion (<i><a href="/wiki/Small_Gods" title="Small Gods">Small Gods</a></i>), business and politics (<i><a href="/wiki/Making_Money" title="Making Money">Making Money</a></i>), are recurring themes, as are music genres such as opera (<i><a href="/wiki/Maskerade" title="Maskerade">Maskerade</a></i>) or rock music (<i><a href="/wiki/Soul_Music_(novel)" title="Soul Music (novel)">Soul Music</a></i>). Parodies of non-Discworld fiction also occur frequently, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Potter" title="Beatrix Potter">Beatrix Potter</a>, and several movies. Major historical events, especially battles, are sometimes used as the basis for both trivial and key events in <i>Discworld</i> stories (<i><a href="/wiki/Jingo_(novel)" title="Jingo (novel)">Jingo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pyramids_(novel)" title="Pyramids (novel)">Pyramids</a></i>), as are trends in science, technology, and pop culture (<i><a href="/wiki/Moving_Pictures_(novel)" title="Moving Pictures (novel)">Moving Pictures</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Men_at_Arms" title="Men at Arms">Men at Arms</a></i>). There are also humanist themes in many of the Discworld novels, and a focus on critical thinking skills in the Witches and Tiffany Aching series.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storylines">Storylines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Storylines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Discworld</i> stories stand alone as independent works set in the same fantasy universe. However, a number of novels and stories can be grouped together into grand <a href="/wiki/Story_arc" title="Story arc">story arcs</a> dealing with a set number of characters and events, and some books refer to earlier (or later) events. The main threads within the <i>Discworld</i> series are:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rincewind">Rincewind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rincewind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rincewind" title="Rincewind">Rincewind</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rincewind" title="Rincewind">Rincewind</a> was the first protagonist of <i>Discworld</i>; a wizard with no skill, no wizardly qualifications, and no interest in heroics. He is the archetypal coward but is constantly thrust into extremely dangerous adventures. In <i>The Last Hero</i>, he flatly states that he does <i>not</i> wish to join an expedition to explore over the edge of the Disc—but, being fully geared for the expedition at the time, clarifies by saying that any amount of protesting on his part is futile, as something will eventually occur that will bring him into the expedition anyway. As such, he not only constantly succeeds in staying alive, but also saves Discworld on several occasions, and has an instrumental role in the emergence of life on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roundworld" title="Roundworld">Roundworld</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_of_Discworld" title="Science of Discworld">Science of Discworld</a>).</p>
<p>Other characters in the Rincewind story arc include: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cohen_the_Barbarian" title="Cohen the Barbarian">Cohen the Barbarian</a>, an aging hero of the old fantasy tradition, out of touch with the modern world and still fighting despite his advanced age; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twoflower" title="Twoflower">Twoflower</a>, a naive tourist from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agatean_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agatean Empire (page does not exist)">Agatean Empire</a> (inspired by cultures of the Far East, particularly Japan and China); and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Luggage" title="The Luggage">The Luggage</a>, a magical, semi-sentient and exceptionally vicious multi-legged travelling accessory, made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sapient_pearwood" title="Sapient pearwood">sapient pearwood</a>. Rincewind appeared in eight Discworld novels as well as the four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_of_Discworld" title="Science of Discworld">Science of Discworld</a> supplementary books.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Death_(Discworld)" title="Death (Discworld)">Death (Discworld)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sto_Helit" title="Susan Sto Helit">Susan Sto Helit</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Death_(Discworld)" title="Death (Discworld)">Death</a> appears in every novel except <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wee_Free_Men" title="The Wee Free Men">The Wee Free Men</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Snuff_(Pratchett_novel)" title="Snuff (Pratchett novel)">Snuff</a></i>, although sometimes with only a few lines. As dictated by tradition, he is a seven-foot-tall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grim_Reaper" title="Grim Reaper">skeleton in a black robe</a> who sits astride a pale horse (called Binky). His dialogue is always depicted in <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;"><a href="/wiki/Small_caps" title="Small caps">small caps</a></span>, and without quotation marks, as several characters state that Death's voice seems to arrive in their heads without actually passing through their ears as sound.</p>
<p>As the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic_personifications_(Discworld)" title="Anthropomorphic personifications (Discworld)">anthropomorphic personification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)" title="Death (personification)">death</a>, Death has the job of guiding souls onward from this world into the next. Over millennia in the role, he has developed a fascination with humanity, even going so far as to create a house for himself in his personal dimension.</p>
<p>Characters that often appear with Death include his butler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_(Discworld)" title="Albert (Discworld)">Albert</a>; his granddaughter <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sto_Helit" title="Susan Sto Helit">Susan Sto Helit</a>; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_of_Rats" title="Death of Rats">Death of Rats</a>, the part of Death in charge of gathering the souls of rodents; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quoth_(Discworld)" title="Quoth (Discworld)">Quoth</a>, a talking raven (a parody of Edgar Allan Poe's "<a href="/wiki/The_Raven" title="The Raven">The Raven</a>", although it flat-out refuses to say "Nevermore"); and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditors_of_Reality" title="Auditors of Reality">Auditors of Reality</a>, personifications of the orderly <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">physical laws</a> and the closest thing Death has to a nemesis. Death or Susan appear as the main characters in five Discworld novels. He also appears in the short stories <i><a href="/wiki/Death_and_What_Comes_Next" title="Death and What Comes Next">Death and What Comes Next</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_Cruelty_(Discworld)" title="Theatre of Cruelty (Discworld)">Theatre of Cruelty</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Turntables_of_the_Night" title="Turntables of the Night">Turntables of the Night</a></i>.</p>
<p>Death also appears in the non-Discworld novel <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Omens" title="Good Omens">Good Omens</a></i>, written by Pratchett and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">Neil Gaiman</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Witches">Witches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Witches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Witches_(Discworld)" title="Witches (Discworld)">Witches (Discworld)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Witches_(Discworld)" title="Witches (Discworld)">Witches</a> in Pratchett's universe are largely stripped of their modern occultist associations (though Pratchett does frequently use his stories to lampoon such conceptions of witchcraft), and act as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbalist" title="Herbalist">herbalists</a>, adjudicators and wise women. That is not to say that witches on the Disc cannot use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discworld_magic" title="Discworld magic">magic</a>; they simply prefer not to, finding simple but cunningly applied psychology (often referred to as "headology", or sometimes "boffo") far more effective.</p>
<p>The principal witch in the series is <a href="/wiki/Granny_Weatherwax" title="Granny Weatherwax">Granny Weatherwax</a>, who at first glance seems to be a taciturn, bitter old crone, from the small mountain country of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lancre" title="Lancre">Lancre</a>. She largely despises people but takes on the role of their healer and protector because no one else can do the job as well as she can. Her closest friend is <a href="/wiki/Nanny_Ogg" title="Nanny Ogg">Nanny Ogg</a>, a jolly, personable witch with the "common touch" who enjoys a smoke and a pint of beer, often leading to her singing bawdy folk songs including the notorious "Hedgehog Song". The two take on apprentice witches, initially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magrat_Garlick" title="Magrat Garlick">Magrat Garlick</a>, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agnes_Nitt" title="Agnes Nitt">Agnes Nitt</a>, and then <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Aching" title="Tiffany Aching">Tiffany Aching</a>, who in turn go on to become accomplished witches in their own right, and, in Magrat's case, Queen of Lancre.</p>
<p>Other characters in the Witches series include: King <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Verence_II_of_Lancre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Verence II of Lancre (page does not exist)">Verence II of Lancre</a>, a onetime <a href="/wiki/Jester" title="Jester">Fool</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jason_Ogg" title="Jason Ogg">Jason Ogg</a>, Nanny Ogg's eldest son and local blacksmith; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shawn_Ogg" title="Shawn Ogg">Shawn Ogg</a>, Nanny's youngest son who serves as his country's entire army and civil service; and Nanny's murderous cat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greebo" title="Greebo">Greebo</a>. The witches have appeared in numerous Discworld books, but have featured as protagonists in seven. They have also appeared in the short story "<a href="/wiki/The_Sea_and_Little_Fishes" title="The Sea and Little Fishes">The Sea and Little Fishes</a>". Their stories frequently draw on ancient European folklore and fairy tales, as well as parody famous works of literature, particularly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_Watch">City Watch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: City Watch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ankh-Morpork_City_Watch" title="Ankh-Morpork City Watch">Ankh-Morpork City Watch</a></div>
<p>The stories featuring the <a href="/wiki/Ankh-Morpork_City_Watch" title="Ankh-Morpork City Watch">Ankh-Morpork City Watch</a> are urban-set, and frequently show the clashes that result when a traditional, magically run fantasy world such as the Disc comes into contact with modern technology and civilization. They revolve around the growth of the <a href="/wiki/Ankh-Morpork" title="Ankh-Morpork">Ankh-Morpork</a> City Watch from a hopeless gang of three to a fully equipped and efficient police force. The stories are largely <a href="/wiki/Police_procedural" title="Police procedural">police procedurals</a>, featuring crimes that have heavy political or societal overtones.</p>
<p>The main character is <a href="/wiki/Sam_Vimes" title="Sam Vimes">Sam Vimes</a>, a haggard, cynical, working-class street copper who, when we first meet him in <i><a href="/wiki/Guards!_Guards!" title="Guards! Guards!">Guards! Guards!</a></i>, is the drunken/alcoholic Captain of the 2-person Night Watch: lazy, cowardly, and none-too-bright Sergeant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_Colon" title="Fred Colon">Fred Colon</a>, and Corporal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobby_Nobbs" title="Nobby Nobbs">Nobby Nobbs</a>, a petty thief in his own right. Then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrot_Ironfoundersson" title="Carrot Ironfoundersson">Carrot Ironfoundersson</a>, a 6-foot-6-inch-tall (1.98 m) dwarf-by-adoption, comes down from the mountains to join the Watch and do real policing. The Night Watch manages to save the city from a dragon, we learn that Carrot is possibly the rightful heir to the throne of Ankh-Morpork, and the Patrician decides to allow Vimes to create a real police force.</p>
<p>Other main characters include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Angua_von_%C3%9Cberwald" title="Captain Angua von Überwald">Angua</a>, a werewolf; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detritus_(Discworld)" title="Detritus (Discworld)">Detritus</a>, a troll; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reg_Shoe#Constable_Reginald_Shoe" title="Reg Shoe">Reg Shoe</a>, a zombie and Dead Rights campaigner; Cuddy, a <a href="/wiki/Dwarfs_(Discworld)" title="Dwarfs (Discworld)">Dwarf</a> who appears in <i><a href="/wiki/Men_at_Arms" title="Men at Arms">Men at Arms</a></i>; Golem Constable Dorfl; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheery_Littlebottom" title="Cheery Littlebottom">Cheery Littlebottom</a>, the Watch's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensics" title="Forensics">forensics</a> expert, who is one of the first dwarves to be openly female (and who tried to rename herself "Cheri", but without success); Sam's wife, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lady_Sybil_Vimes" title="Lady Sybil Vimes">Lady Sybil Vimes (née Ramkin)</a>; Constable Visit-the-infidel-with-explanatory-pamphlets, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Havelock_Vetinari" title="Havelock Vetinari">Havelock Vetinari</a>, the Patrician of Ankh-Morpork. The City Watch have starred in eight Discworld stories, and have cameoed in a number of others, including <i><a href="/wiki/Making_Money" title="Making Money">Making Money</a></i>, the children's book <i><a href="/wiki/Where%27s_My_Cow%3F" title="Where's My Cow?">Where's My Cow?</a>,</i> and the short story "<a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_Cruelty_(Discworld)" title="Theatre of Cruelty (Discworld)">Theatre of Cruelty</a>".</p>
<p>Pratchett stated on numerous occasions that the presence of the City Watch makes Ankh-Morpork stories 'problematic', as stories set in the city that do not directly involve Vimes and the Watch often <i>require</i> a Watch presence to maintain the story—at which point, it becomes a Watch story by default.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wizards">Wizards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wizards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unseen_University" title="Unseen University">Unseen University</a></div>
<p>The Wizards of the <a href="/wiki/Unseen_University" title="Unseen University">Unseen University</a> (UU) have represented a strong thread through many of the <i>Discworld</i> novels, although the only books that they star in exclusively are The Science of the Discworld series and the novels <i><a href="/wiki/Unseen_Academicals" title="Unseen Academicals">Unseen Academicals</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Continent" title="The Last Continent">The Last Continent</a></i>. In the early books, the faculty of UU changed frequently, as rising to the top usually involved assassination. However, with the ascension of the bombastic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mustrum_Ridcully" title="Mustrum Ridcully">Mustrum Ridcully</a> to the position of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archchancellor_(Discworld)" title="Archchancellor (Discworld)">Archchancellor</a>, the hierarchy has settled and characters have been given the chance to develop. The earlier books featuring the wizards also frequently dealt with the possible invasion of the Discworld by the creatures from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dungeon_Dimensions" title="Dungeon Dimensions">Dungeon Dimensions</a>, Lovecraftian monsters that hunger for the magic and potential of the Discworld.</p>
<p>The wizards of UU employ the traditional "whizz-bang" type of magic seen in <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> games, but also investigate the rules and structure of magic in terms highly reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>. Prominent members include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ponder_Stibbons" title="Ponder Stibbons">Ponder Stibbons</a>, a geeky young wizard; <a href="/wiki/Hex_(Discworld)" title="Hex (Discworld)">Hex</a>, the Disc's first computer/semi-sentient thinking engine; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Librarian_(Discworld)" title="Librarian (Discworld)">the Librarian</a>, who was turned into an <a href="/wiki/Orangutan" title="Orangutan">orangutan</a> by magical accident; the Dean; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Bursar" title="The Bursar">Bursar</a>; the Chair of Indefinite Studies; the Lecturer in Recent Runes; and the Senior Wrangler. In later novels, Rincewind also joins their group, while the Dean leaves to become the Archchancellor of Brazeneck College in the nearby city of Pseudopolis.</p>
<p>The Wizards have featured prominently in nine <i>Discworld</i> books as well as starred in The Science of Discworld series and the short story "<a href="/wiki/A_Collegiate_Casting-Out_of_Devilish_Devices" title="A Collegiate Casting-Out of Devilish Devices">A Collegiate Casting-Out of Devilish Devices</a>".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tiffany_Aching">Tiffany Aching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tiffany Aching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Aching" title="Tiffany Aching">Tiffany Aching</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Aching" title="Tiffany Aching">Tiffany Aching</a> is a young apprentice witch and star of a series of Discworld books aimed at young adults. Her stories often parallel mythic heroes' quests, but also deal with Tiffany's difficulties as a young girl maturing into a responsible woman. She is aided in her task by the <a href="/wiki/Nac_Mac_Feegle" title="Nac Mac Feegle">Nac Mac Feegle</a>, a gang of blue-tattooed, 6-inch tall, hard-drinking, loud-mouthed pictsie creatures also called "The Wee Free Men" who serve as her guardians. Both <a href="/wiki/Granny_Weatherwax" title="Granny Weatherwax">Granny Weatherwax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanny_Ogg" title="Nanny Ogg">Nanny Ogg</a> have also appeared in her stories. She has appeared in five novels (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Wee_Free_Men" title="The Wee Free Men">The Wee Free Men</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Hat_Full_of_Sky" title="A Hat Full of Sky">A Hat Full of Sky</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wintersmith" title="Wintersmith">Wintersmith</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Shall_Wear_Midnight" title="I Shall Wear Midnight">I Shall Wear Midnight</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shepherd%27s_Crown" title="The Shepherd's Crown">The Shepherd's Crown</a></i>). Major characters in this series include Miss Tick, who discovered Tiffany, Annagramma Hawkin, Petulia Gristle, and Nac Mac Feegle chieftain Rob Anybody.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moist_von_Lipwig">Moist von Lipwig</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Moist von Lipwig">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moist_von_Lipwig" title="Moist von Lipwig">Moist von Lipwig</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Moist_von_Lipwig" title="Moist von Lipwig">Moist von Lipwig</a> is a professional criminal and con man to whom Havelock Vetinari gives a "second chance" after staging his execution, recognising the advantages his <a href="/wiki/Jack_of_all_trades,_master_of_none" title="Jack of all trades, master of none">jack-of-all-trades</a> abilities would have to the development of the city. After setting him in charge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ankh-Morpork_Post_Office" title="Ankh-Morpork Post Office">Ankh-Morpork Post Office</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Going_Postal" title="Going Postal">Going Postal</a></i>, to good result, Vetinari ordered him to clear up the city's corrupt financial sector in <i><a href="/wiki/Making_Money" title="Making Money">Making Money</a></i>. A third book, <i><a href="/wiki/Raising_Steam" title="Raising Steam">Raising Steam</a></i> published on 7 November 2013 features Lipwig's further exploits as a pioneer to the newly invented locomotive. Other characters in this series include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adora_Belle_Dearheart" title="Adora Belle Dearheart">Adora Belle Dearheart</a>, Lipwig's acerbic, chain-smoking wife; <a href="/wiki/Golems_(Discworld)#Gladys" title="Golems (Discworld)">Gladys</a>, a golem who develops a strange crush on Lipwig, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanley_Howler" title="Stanley Howler">Stanley Howler</a>, a mildly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autistic" title="Autistic">autistic</a> young man who was raised by peas and becomes the Disc's first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stamp_collector" title="Stamp collector">stamp collector</a>, and the very old Junior Postman Groat, who never got promoted to Senior Postman because there was never a Postmaster alive long enough to do so.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discworld_cultures"><i>Discworld</i> cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Discworld cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several other books can be grouped together as "Other cultures of Discworld" books. They may contain characters or locations from other arcs, typically not as protagonist or antagonist but as a supporting character or even a throwaway reference. These include <a href="/wiki/Pyramids_(novel)" title="Pyramids (novel)"><i>Pyramids</i></a> (Djelibeybi), <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Gods" title="Small Gods">Small Gods</a></i> (Omnia), and <a href="/wiki/Monstrous_Regiment_(novel)" title="Monstrous Regiment (novel)"><i>Monstrous Regiment</i></a> (Zlobenia and Borogravia).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Short_stories">Short stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Short stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are also a number of short stories by Pratchett based in the Discworld, including published miscellanea such as the fictional game origins of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thud_(game)" title="Thud (game)">Thud</a></i>. All are available in the anthology <i><a href="/wiki/A_Blink_of_the_Screen" title="A Blink of the Screen">A Blink of the Screen</a></i> (2012) as well as in the following locations:</p>
<ul>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Troll_Bridge" title="Troll Bridge">Troll Bridge</a>" – in <i>After The King: Stories in honour of J. R. R. Tolkien</i> (1992); reprinted in <i>The Mammoth Book of Comic Fantasy</i> edited by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Ashley_(writer)" title="Mike Ashley (writer)">Mike Ashley</a> (1998); available online<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_Cruelty_(Discworld)" title="Theatre of Cruelty (Discworld)">Theatre of Cruelty</a>" (1993); available online<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wzxcek_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-wzxcek-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/The_Sea_and_Little_Fishes" title="The Sea and Little Fishes">The Sea and Little Fishes</a>" – in <i><a href="/wiki/Legends_(book)" title="Legends (book)">Legends</a></i> (1998), anthology of novellas taking place within popular fantasy cycles edited by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Silverberg" title="Robert Silverberg">Robert Silverberg</a></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Death_and_What_Comes_Next" title="Death and What Comes Next">Death and What Comes Next</a>" (2002); available online<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wzxcek_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-wzxcek-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/A_Collegiate_Casting-Out_of_Devilish_Devices" title="A Collegiate Casting-Out of Devilish Devices">A Collegiate Casting-Out of Devilish Devices</a>" (2005); available online<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Seven of the short stories or short writings were also collected in a compilation of the majority of Pratchett's known short work named <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Once_More*_With_Footnotes" title="Once More* With Footnotes">Once More* With Footnotes</a></i> (2004).</p>
<p>Additionally, another short story "<a href="/wiki/Turntables_of_the_Night" title="Turntables of the Night">Turntables of the Night</a>" (1989) is set in England but features <a href="/wiki/Death_(Discworld)" title="Death (Discworld)">Death</a> as a character; it is available online and in both anthologies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Mapps.22">"Mapps"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title='Edit section: "Mapps"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although Terry Pratchett said, "There are no maps. You can't map a sense of humour,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> there are four "Mapps": <i><a href="/wiki/The_Streets_of_Ankh-Morpork" title="The Streets of Ankh-Morpork">The Streets of Ankh-Morpork</a></i> (1993), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Discworld_Mapp" title="The Discworld Mapp">The Discworld Mapp</a></i> (1995), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Tourist_Guide_to_Lancre" title="A Tourist Guide to Lancre">A Tourist Guide to Lancre</a></i> (1998), and <i><a href="/wiki/Death%27s_Domain" title="Death's Domain">Death's Domain</a></i> (1999). The first two were drawn by Stephen Player, based on plans by Pratchett and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Briggs" title="Stephen Briggs">Stephen Briggs</a>, the third is a collaboration between Briggs and Kidby, and the last is by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kidby" title="Paul Kidby">Paul Kidby</a>. All also contain booklets written by Pratchett and Briggs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twin_cities">Twin cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Twin cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several <i>Discworld</i> locations have been twinned with real world towns and cities. <a href="/wiki/Wincanton" title="Wincanton">Wincanton</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, UK, for example is twinned with <a href="/wiki/Ankh-Morpork" title="Ankh-Morpork">Ankh-Morpork</a>, and the town is the first to name streets after their fictional equivalents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_books">Science books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Science books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diaries">Diaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Diaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Discworld_Diary" title="Discworld Diary">Discworld Diary</a></div>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Discworld_Almanak" title="The Discworld Almanak">The Discworld Almanak – The Year of The Prawn</a></i> has a similar format and general contents to the diaries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reading_order">Reading order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Reading order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The books take place roughly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real-time_(media)" title="Real-time (media)">real time</a> and the characters' ages change to reflect the passing of years. The meetings of various characters from different narrative threads (e.g., Ridcully and <a href="/wiki/Granny_Weatherwax" title="Granny Weatherwax">Granny Weatherwax</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Lords_and_Ladies_(novel)" title="Lords and Ladies (novel)">Lords and Ladies</a></i>, Rincewind and Carrot in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Hero" title="The Last Hero">The Last Hero</a></i>) indicate that all the main storylines take place around the same period of time (end of the Century of the Fruitbat, beginning of the Century of the Anchovy). Thus, it could be seen that some of the separate books run concurrently rather than consecutively. The main exception to this rule is the stand-alone book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Gods" title="Small Gods">Small Gods</a></i>, which appears to take place at some point earlier than most of the other stories, though even this contains cameo appearances by Death and the Librarian.</p>
<p>Some main characters may make <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearances</a> in other books where they are not the primary focus; for example, City Watch members <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrot_Ironfoundersson" title="Carrot Ironfoundersson">Carrot Ironfoundersson</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Angua_von_%C3%9Cberwald" title="Captain Angua von Überwald">Angua</a> appear briefly in <i><a href="/wiki/Going_Postal" title="Going Postal">Going Postal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Making_Money" title="Making Money">Making Money</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Unseen_Academicals" title="Unseen Academicals">Unseen Academicals</a></i> (placing those books after <i><a href="/wiki/Guards!_Guards!" title="Guards! Guards!">Guards! Guards!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Men_at_Arms" title="Men at Arms">Men at Arms</a></i>). A number of characters, such as members of staff of <a href="/wiki/Unseen_University" title="Unseen University">Unseen University</a> and Lord Vetinari, appear prominently in many different storylines without having specific storylines of their own.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due in part to the complexity of the novels, <i>Discworld</i> has been difficult to adapt to film – Pratchett was fond of an anecdote of a producer attempting to pitch an adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Mort" title="Mort">Mort</a></i> in the early 1990s but was told to "lose the Death angle" by US backers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp-mort_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp-mort-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>A list of adaptations include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cosgrove_Hall" title="Cosgrove Hall">Cosgrove Hall</a> produced 6x30 minute animated adaptations of two books for <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> in 1996. These were made available on DVD and VHS in the US from Acorn Media, though they are now out of print. Both series are available on a DVD boxset in <a href="/wiki/DVD_region_code#Region_2" title="DVD region code">Region 2</a>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_the_Discworld" title="Welcome to the Discworld">Welcome to the Discworld</a></i> – an 8-minute animated television adaptation of a fragment of <i>Reaper Man</i> filmed by Cosgrove Hall before the TV series.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Soul_Music_(TV_series)" title="Soul Music (TV series)">Soul Music</a></i> – Starring <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lee" title="Christopher Lee">Christopher Lee</a> as Death, also featuring <a href="/wiki/Neil_Morrissey" title="Neil Morrissey">Neil Morrissey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Crowden" title="Graham Crowden">Graham Crowden</a>. First episode broadcast on 18 May 1997. The soundtrack to <i>Soul Music</i> was also released on CD.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Wyrd_Sisters_(TV_series)" title="Wyrd Sisters (TV series)">Wyrd Sisters</a></i> – Starring Christopher Lee as Death, also featuring <a href="/wiki/Annette_Crosbie" title="Annette Crosbie">Annette Crosbie</a>, <a href="/wiki/June_Whitfield" title="June Whitfield">June Whitfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jane_Horrocks" title="Jane Horrocks">Jane Horrocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Les_Dennis" title="Les Dennis">Les Dennis</a>. First episode broadcast on 28 December 1998.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Mort</i> (2001): A fan movie adaptation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mort_(novel)" title="Mort (novel)">the eponymous novel</a> by Orange Cow Production, 26 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Lords and Ladies</i> (2005): A fan movie adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Lords_and_Ladies_(novel)" title="Lords and Ladies (novel)">Lords and Ladies</a> by Almost No Budget Films was completed in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett%27s_Hogfather" title="Terry Pratchett's Hogfather">Terry Pratchett's Hogfather</a></i> (2006): In the UK, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky1" title="Sky1">Sky One</a> commissioned a £6 million 'made for television' adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett%27s_Hogfather" title="Terry Pratchett's Hogfather">Hogfather</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/David_Jason" title="David Jason">David Jason</a> playing the role of Albert. It was first broadcast in December 2006 and features Terry Pratchett in a brief cameo role as the Toymaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Run Rincewind Run!</i> (2007): A Snowgum Films original story created for Nullus Anxietas. Stars Troy Larkin as <a href="/wiki/Rincewind" title="Rincewind">Rincewind</a>, and features Terry Pratchett as himself.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett%27s_The_Colour_of_Magic" title="Terry Pratchett's The Colour of Magic">Terry Pratchett's The Colour of Magic</a></i> (2008; based on both <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colour_of_Magic" title="The Colour of Magic">The Colour of Magic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Light_Fantastic" title="The Light Fantastic">The Light Fantastic</a></i>): David Jason played 'Rincewind'. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_radio" title="BBC radio">BBC radio</a> adaptations of Discworld stories, including:</p>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/Eric_(novel)" title="Eric (novel)">Eric</a></i> (1990), a 4-part dramatised adaptation began airing on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> on 6 March 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage">Stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Briggs" title="Stephen Briggs">Stephen Briggs</a> published stage adaptations of 18 <i>Discworld</i> novels. Most of them were first produced by the Studio Theatre Club in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abingdon,_Oxfordshire" title="Abingdon, Oxfordshire">Abingdon</a>, Oxfordshire. They include adaptations of <i>The Truth</i>, <i>Maskerade</i>, <i>Mort</i>, <i>Wyrd Sisters</i> and <i>Guards! Guards!</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>Irana Brown directed her adaptation of <i>Lords and Ladies</i>, first performed in 1995 at the Winton Studio Theatre. Her adaptation was published in 2001 by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_French_Ltd." title="Samuel French Ltd.">Samuel French</a>, and is still being performed as of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li>A stage version of <i>Eric</i>, adapted by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Harrison_(writer)" title="Scott Harrison (writer)">Scott Harrison</a> and Lee Harris, was produced and performed by The Dreaming Theatre Company in July 2003 inside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clifford%27s_Tower" title="Clifford's Tower">Clifford's Tower</a>, the 700-year-old castle keep in <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> It was revived in 2004 in a tour of England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rankin" title="Robert Rankin">Robert Rankin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Antipope" title="The Antipope">The Antipope</a></i>.</li>
<li><i>Small Gods</i> was adapted for the stage by Ben Saunders and was performed in February 2011 at the Assembly Rooms Theatre, <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">Durham</a> by Ooook! Productions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and members of <a href="/wiki/Durham_Student_Theatre" title="Durham Student Theatre">Durham Student Theatre</a>. Ooook! Productions also adapted and staged<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Terry Pratchett's <i>Night Watch</i> (February 2012), <i>Thief of Time</i> (February 2013; adapted by Tim Foster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>), <i>Lords and Ladies</i> (February 2014, adapted by Irana Brown<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>), <i>Monstrous Regiment</i> (2015),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and <i>Soul Music</i> (February 2016; adapted by Imogen Eddleston).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>A stage version of <i>Monstrous Regiment</i> was produced by <a href="/wiki/Lifeline_Theatre" title="Lifeline Theatre">Lifeline Theatre</a> in Chicago, Illinois in June, July, and August 2014 with an adaptation written by one of Lifeline's ensemble members, Chris Hainsworth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchandise">Merchandise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Merchandise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Board_games">Board games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discworld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Board games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thud_(game)" title="Thud (game)">Thud</a></i> (2002) was created by puzzle compiler <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Truran" title="Trevor Truran">Trevor Truran</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Guards!_Guards!" title="Guards! Guards!">Guards! Guards!</a> A Discworld Boardgame</i> (2011) was created by designers Leonard Boyd &amp; David Brashaw (Backspindle Games) and published by Z-Man Games. The first copies went on sale on 8 July 2011 at the North American Discworld Convention, exactly 20 years after Leonard had conceived the first draft in 1991. The box cover and 90 Discworld character cards were illustrated by Stephen Player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Discworld:_Ankh-Morpork" title="Discworld: Ankh-Morpork">Discworld: Ankh-Morpork</a></i> (2011) was designed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Wallace_(game_designer)" title="Martin Wallace (game designer)">Martin Wallace</a> and released by Treefrog Games in three different editions, each with different content and different game boards; the collectible editions also have different numbering system (the number 8 is replaced by 7a).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> A follow-up game called <i>The Witches</i>, also by Wallace, was released by Treefrog in September 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
