<p><b>Dieppe</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[djɛp]</a></span>) is a coastal community in the <a href="/wiki/Arrondissement_of_Dieppe" title="Arrondissement of Dieppe">Arrondissement of Dieppe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seine-Maritime" title="Seine-Maritime">Seine-Maritime</a> <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normandy_(French_region)" title="Normandy (French region)">Normandy region</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. The population stood at 34,670 in 2006.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaport" title="Seaport">port</a> on the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Arques_(river)" title="Arques (river)">Arques river</a>, famous for its <a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">scallops</a>, and with a regular ferry service to <a href="/wiki/Newhaven,_East_Sussex" title="Newhaven, East Sussex">Newhaven</a> in England, Dieppe also has a popular <a href="/wiki/Pebble" title="Pebble">pebbled</a> <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a>, a 15th-century <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> of <a href="/wiki/James,_son_of_Zebedee" title="James, son of Zebedee">Saint-Jacques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Remigius" title="Saint Remigius">Saint-Remi</a>. The mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Scie_(river)" title="Scie (river)">Scie river</a> lies in the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Dieppe-Ouest" title="Canton of Dieppe-Ouest">Canton of Dieppe-Ouest</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hautot-sur-Mer" title="Hautot-sur-Mer">Hautot-sur-Mer</a>.</p>
<p>The inhabitants of the town of Dieppe are called <i>Dieppois</i> (m) and <i>Dieppoise</i> (f) in French.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dieppe belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_Caux" title="Pays de Caux">Pays de Caux</a>, lying along the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Alb%C3%A2tre" title="Côte d'Albâtre">Alabaster Coast</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. It is located on the Channel coast, north of Rouen at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Arques_(river)" title="Arques (river)">Arques river</a> and lies east of the mouth of the river <a href="/wiki/Scie_(river)" title="Scie (river)">Scie</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mentioned as <i>Deppae</i> in 1015–1029, <i>Dieppa</i> in 1030, then in the 12th century: <i>Deppa</i>, <i>Deupa</i> and <i>Diopa</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From Dutch <i>diep</i> &gt; deep, or Old English <i>deop</i> or Old Norse <i>djupr</i>, same meaning. The same adjective can be recognized in other place-names like Dieppedalle (f. e. <a href="/wiki/Saint-Vaast-Dieppedalle" title="Saint-Vaast-Dieppedalle">Saint-Vaast-Dieppedalle</a>) and Dipdal in Normandy, which is the same as <a href="/wiki/Deepdale" title="Deepdale">Deepdale</a> in Great Britain.</p>
<p>The stream running through Dieppe was called <i>Tella</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolingian" title="Carolingian">Carolingian</a> documents, before being called <i>Dieppe</i> in the 10th century. The name has stuck to the town, although the name of the stream changed again, to Béthune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:802px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1876" data-file-width="6800" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg/800px-France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg/1200px-France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg/1600px-France.Dieppe.City.Panorama.July2011.jpg 2x" width="800"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>First recorded as a small <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> settlement in 1030, Dieppe was an important prize fought over during the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a>. Dieppe housed the most advanced French school of <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a> in the 16th century. Two of France's best navigators, Michel le Vasseur and his brother Thomas le Vasseur, lived in Dieppe when they were recruited to join the expedition of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Goulaine_de_Laudonni%C3%A8re" title="René Goulaine de Laudonnière">René Goulaine de Laudonnière</a> which departed <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a> for <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> on April 20, 1564. The expedition resulted in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Caroline" title="Fort Caroline">Fort Caroline</a>, the first French colony in the New World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Another expedition two years before where Goulaine de Laudonnière was under command of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ribault" title="Jean Ribault">Jean Ribault</a>, a local Huguenot captain, had resulted in the foundation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlesfort" title="Charlesfort">Charlesfort</a>, now in South Carolina. Dieppe was the premier port of the kingdom in the 17th century. On July 23, 1632, 300 <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonists</a> heading to <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> departed from Dieppe. At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revocation_of_the_Edict_of_Nantes" title="Revocation of the Edict of Nantes">Revocation of the Edict of Nantes</a> in 1685, Dieppe lost 3,000 of its <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> citizens, who fled abroad.</p>
<p>Dieppe was an important target in <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">wartime</a>; the town was largely destroyed by an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">Anglo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> naval bombardment in 1694. It was rebuilt after 1696 in a typical French classical style by Ventabren, an architect, who gave it its unique feature for a sea port. It was popularised as a <a href="/wiki/Seaside_resort" title="Seaside resort">seaside resort</a> following the 1824 visit of the widowed <a href="/wiki/Duchess_of_Berry" title="Duchess of Berry">Duchess of Berry</a>, daughter-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Charles_X_of_France" title="Charles X of France">Charles X</a>. She encouraged the building of the recently renovated municipal <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a>, the <i>Petit-Théâtre</i> (1825), associated particularly with <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>.</p>
<p>During the later 19th century, Dieppe became popular with English artists as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_resort" title="Beach resort">beach resort</a>. Prominent literary figures such as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Symons" title="Arthur Symons">Arthur Symons</a> loved to keep up with the latest fads of <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> France here, and during "the season" sometimes stayed for weeks on end.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Dieppe was occupied by German naval and army forces after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_France" title="Fall of France">fall of France</a> in 1940. In order to allow a better defence of the coast against a possible <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> landing, the Germans destroyed the mauresque casino that was located near the beach area. The destruction of the casino had only begun at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Dieppe_Raid" title="Dieppe Raid">Dieppe Raid</a>.</p>
<p>The Dieppe Raid in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> was a costly battle for the Allies. On August 19, 1942, Allied soldiers, mainly drawn from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Canadian_Infantry_Division" title="2nd Canadian Infantry Division">2nd Canadian Infantry Division</a>, landed at Dieppe in the hope of occupying the town for a short time, gaining intelligence and drawing the <i><a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a></i> into open battle. The Allies suffered more than 1,400 deaths; 1,946 Canadian soldiers were captured—more prisoners than the army lost in the 11 months of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Western Front (World War II)">1944–45 NW Europe campaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, no major objectives on the ground were achieved; in the air, a major objective of drawing German air forces into open battle was realized.</p>
<p>French soldiers from the region, captured in the fighting of 1940, were returned to the area after the Dieppe Raid as repayment by the German occupation authorities, who felt that the conduct of the French civilians in Dieppe had been correct and had not hindered the defence of the port during the battle.</p>
<p>The port remained garrisoned by German forces until the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Battle of Normandy</a>. When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_Canadian_Army" title="1st Canadian Army">1st Canadian Army</a> approached at the end of August, the garrison withdrew, not desiring to enter into battle for the port.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Operation_Fusilade" title="Operation Fusilade">Dieppe was liberated</a> on September 1, 1944, by soldiers from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Canadian_Infantry_Division" title="2nd Canadian Infantry Division">2nd Canadian Infantry Division</a>. On September 3, the entire division paused for reorganization, and a victory parade was held; contingents representing all major units of the 2nd Division marched 10 abreast behind the massed pipes and drums of the division's highland regiments. A memorial service was held in the nearby Canadian military cemetery to honour those killed in the Dieppe Raid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dieppe,_New_Brunswick" title="Dieppe, New Brunswick">Dieppe</a>, a city in <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, Canada, received its present name in 1946, in honour of the commemoration of the 913 Canadian soldiers killed in the Dieppe Raid. The majority of its inhabitants are of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acadian" title="Acadian">Acadian</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>castle</b>, <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Dieppe" title="Château de Dieppe">Château de Dieppe</a>, which survived the 1694 bombardment, is now a museum and exhibition space, with a strong maritime collection. A rich collection of 17th- and 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> carvings, including lacy folding fans, for which Dieppe was known, and the furnishings and papers of <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>. The castle's interior courtyard is picturesque.</p>
<p>At the <b>Square du Canada</b>, near the castle in a park at the western end of the Esplanade, there is a monument erected by the town commemorating the long relationship between Dieppe and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The events recorded begin with the early 16th century, and culminate with the Dieppe Raid and the liberation of Dieppe by Canadians on September 1, 1944. The base of the monument is inscribed with the words "<i>nous nous souvenons</i>" ("we remember"). Above the monument, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Canada" title="Flag of Canada">Canadian Maple Leaf flag</a> is flown side-by-side with that of France.</p>
<p>Some of the Canadian soldiers who were killed are buried in the <a href="/wiki/Dieppe_Canadian_War_Cemetery" title="Dieppe Canadian War Cemetery">Dieppe Canadian War Cemetery</a>, in the <i>commune</i> of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Aubin-sur-Scie" title="Saint-Aubin-sur-Scie">Saint-Aubin-sur-Scie</a> south-west of Dieppe.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town has a railway station, operated by <a href="/wiki/SNCF" title="SNCF">SNCF</a>, with frequent departure for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gare_de_Rouen_Rue_Verte" title="Gare de Rouen Rue Verte">Rouen-Rive-Droite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gare_de_Paris-Saint-Lazare" title="Gare de Paris-Saint-Lazare">Paris-Saint-Lazare</a>. SNCF operates also buses to <a href="/wiki/Gisors" title="Gisors">Gisors</a>-Embranchement through <a href="/wiki/Serqueux,_Seine-Maritime" title="Serqueux, Seine-Maritime">Serqueux</a>.</p>
<p>Dieppe has a ferry port with direct services to the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Newhaven,_East_Sussex" title="Newhaven, East Sussex">Newhaven</a>, situated at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Ouse,_Sussex" title="River Ouse, Sussex">river Ouse</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a>. The twice-daily service to the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Newhaven" title="Port of Newhaven">Port of Newhaven</a> is operated by <a href="/wiki/DFDS_Seaways_France" title="DFDS Seaways France">DFDS Seaways</a>, under a concession subsidised by the French government. Services are normally operated using the <a href="/wiki/MS_C%C3%B4te_D%27Alb%C3%A2tre" title="MS Côte D'Albâtre">MS Côte D'Albâtre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferry_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferry-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieppe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current mayor of Dieppe is Sébastien Jumel. Member of the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">French Communist Party</a>, he was elected in 2008, and reelected in 2014.</p>
