<p>The <b>Battle of Dien Bien Phu</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Bataille de Diên Biên Phu</i></span>; Vietnamese: <span lang="vi" xml:lang="vi"><i>Chiến dịch Điện Biên Phủ</i></span>, "Campaign of Dien Bien Phu") was the climactic confrontation of the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a> between the <a href="/wiki/French_Union" title="French Union">French Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/French_Far_East_Expeditionary_Corps" title="French Far East Expeditionary Corps">French Far East Expeditionary Corps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">Viet Minh</a> <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a>-<a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">revolutionaries</a>. It was, from the French view before the event, a <a href="/wiki/Pitched_battle" title="Pitched battle">set piece battle</a> to draw out the Vietnamese and destroy them with superior firepower. The battle occurred between March and May 1954 and culminated in a comprehensive French defeat that influenced negotiations underway at Geneva among several nations over the future of <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>.</p>
<p>As a result of blunders in French decision-making, the French began an operation to insert, then support the soldiers at <a href="/wiki/%C4%90i%E1%BB%87n_Bi%C3%AAn_Ph%E1%BB%A7" title="Điện Biên Phủ">Điện Biên Phủ</a>, deep in the hills of northwestern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. Its purpose was to cut off <a href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">Viet Minh</a> supply lines into the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Laos" title="Kingdom of Laos">Kingdom of Laos</a>, a French ally, and tactically draw the Viet Minh into a major confrontation in order to cripple them. The Viet Minh, however, under General <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B5_Nguy%C3%AAn_Gi%C3%A1p" title="Võ Nguyên Giáp">Võ Nguyên Giáp</a>, surrounded and <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">besieged</a> the French. The Viet Minh brought in vast amounts of heavy artillery (including <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">anti-aircraft guns</a>). They moved these weapons through difficult terrain up the rear slopes of the mountains surrounding the French positions, dug tunnels through the mountain, and placed the artillery pieces overlooking the French encampment. This positioning of the artillery made it nearly impervious to French <a href="/wiki/Counter-battery_fire" title="Counter-battery fire">counter-battery fire</a>.</p>
<p>The Viet Minh opened fire with a massive artillery bombardment in March. After several days the French artillery commander, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Piroth" title="Charles Piroth">Charles Piroth</a>, unable to respond with any effective counter-battery fire, committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>. The Viet Minh occupied the highlands around Điện Biên Phủ and bombarded the French positions. Tenacious fighting on the ground ensued, reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a> of World War I. The French repeatedly repulsed Viet Minh assaults on their positions. Supplies and reinforcements were delivered by air, though as the key French positions were overrun, the French perimeter contracted and the air resupply on which the French had placed their hopes became impossible. As the Viet Minh anti-aircraft fire took its toll, fewer and fewer of those supplies reached the French. The garrison was overrun in May after a two-month siege, and most French forces surrendered. A few escaped to Laos. The French government resigned and the new Prime Minister, the left-of-centre <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Mend%C3%A8s_France" title="Pierre Mendès France">Pierre Mendès France</a>, supported French withdrawal from Indochina.</p>
<p>The war ended shortly after the Battle of Dien Bien Phu and the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">1954 Geneva Accords</a>. France agreed to withdraw its forces from all its colonies in <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a>, while stipulating that Vietnam would be temporarily divided at the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Vietnamese Demilitarized Zone">17th parallel</a>, with control of the north given to the Viet Minh as the <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> under <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>, and the south becoming the <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">State of Vietnam</a>, nominally under Emperor <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a>, preventing Ho Chi Minh from gaining control of the entire country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The refusal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> (the US-supported President of the first Republic of Vietnam (RVN)) to allow elections in 1956, as had been stipulated by the Geneva Conference, eventually led to the first phase of the <i>Second Indochina War.</i> This is better known as the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, which was waged largely by the United States after 1963. (see <a href="/wiki/War_in_Vietnam_(1959%E2%80%9363)" title="War in Vietnam (1959–63)">War in Vietnam (1959–63)</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_situation">Military situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1953, the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a> was not going well for France. A succession of commanders — <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Leclerc_de_Hauteclocque" title="Philippe Leclerc de Hauteclocque">Philippe Leclerc de Hauteclocque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_%C3%89tienne_Valluy" title="Jean Étienne Valluy">Jean Étienne Valluy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Blaizot" title="Roger Blaizot">Roger Blaizot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Carpentier" title="Marcel Carpentier">Marcel Carpentier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Lattre_de_Tassigny" title="Jean de Lattre de Tassigny">Jean de Lattre de Tassigny</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Salan" title="Raoul Salan">Raoul Salan</a> — had proven incapable of suppressing the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">Viet Minh</a> insurrection. They were fighting for independence. During their 1952–53 campaign, the Viet Minh had overrun vast swathes of <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, a French ally and Vietnam's western neighbor, advancing as far as <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang" title="Luang Prabang">Luang Prabang</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Plain_of_Jars" title="Plain of Jars">Plain of Jars</a>. The French were unable to slow the Viet Minh advance, who fell back only after outrunning their always-tenuous supply lines. In 1953, the French had begun to strengthen their defenses in the <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> delta region to prepare for a series of offensives against Viet Minh <a href="/wiki/Staging_area" title="Staging area">staging areas</a> in northwest Vietnam. They set up fortified towns and outposts in the area, including <a href="/wiki/Lai_Ch%C3%A2u" title="Lai Châu">Lai Châu</a> near the Chinese border to the north,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%A0_S%E1%BA%A3n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nà Sản (page does not exist)">Nà Sản</a> to the west of Hanoi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the Plain of Jars in northern Laos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1953, French Premier <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Mayer" title="René Mayer">René Mayer</a> appointed <a href="/wiki/Henri_Navarre" title="Henri Navarre">Henri Navarre</a>, a trusted colleague, to take command of <a href="/wiki/French_Union" title="French Union">French Union</a> Forces in Indochina. Mayer had given Navarre a single order—to create military conditions that would lead to an "honorable political solution".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d165_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-d165-19">[19]</a></sup> According to military scholar <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Davidson" title="Phillip Davidson">Phillip Davidson</a>,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>On arrival, Navarre was shocked by what he found. There had been no long-range plan since de Lattre's departure. Everything was conducted on a day-to-day, reactive basis. Combat operations were undertaken only in response to enemy moves or threats. There was no comprehensive plan to develop the organization and build up the equipment of the Expeditionary force. Finally, Navarre, the intellectual, the cold and professional soldier, was shocked by the "school's out" attitude of <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Salan" title="Raoul Salan">Salan</a> and his senior commanders and staff officers. They were going home, not as victors or heroes, but then, not as clear losers either. To them the important thing was that they were getting out of Indochina with their reputations frayed, but intact. They gave little thought to, or concern for, the problems of their successors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d165_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-d165-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="N.C3.A0_S.E1.BA.A3n_and_the_hedgehog_concept">Nà Sản and the hedgehog concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nà Sản and the hedgehog concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_N%C3%A0_S%E1%BA%A3n" title="Battle of Nà Sản">Battle of Nà Sản</a>.</div>
<p>Simultaneously, Navarre had been searching for a way to stop the Viet Minh threat to Laos. Colonel Louis Berteil, commander of Mobile Group 7 and Navarre's main planner,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> formulated the <i>hérisson</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Hedgehog_defence" title="Hedgehog defence">hedgehog</a>") concept. The French army would establish a fortified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airhead_(warfare)" title="Airhead (warfare)">airhead</a> by air-lifting soldiers adjacent to a key Viet Minh supply line to Laos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> They would cut off Viet Minh soldiers fighting in Laos and force them to withdraw. "It was an attempt to interdict the enemy's rear area, to stop the flow of supplies and reinforcements, to establish a redoubt in the enemy's rear and disrupt his lines".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hedgehog concept was based on French experiences at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_N%C3%A0_S%E1%BA%A3n" title="Battle of Nà Sản">Battle of Nà Sản</a>. In late November and early December 1952, Giáp attacked the French outpost at Nà Sản, which was essentially an "air-land base", a fortified camp supplied only by air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The French beat back Giáp's forces repeatedly, causing them to suffer very heavy losses. The French hoped that by repeating the strategy on a much larger scale, they would be able to lure Giáp into committing the bulk of his forces in a massed assault. This would enable superior French artillery, armor, and air support to decimate the exposed Viet Minh forces. The experience at Nà Sản convinced Navarre of the viability of the fortified airhead concept.</p>
<p>French staff officers disastrously failed to treat seriously several crucial differences between Điện Biên Phủ and Nà Sản: Firstly, at Nà Sản, the French commanded most of the high ground with overwhelming artillery support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> At Điện Biên Phủ, however, the Viet Minh controlled much of the high ground around the valley, their artillery far exceeded French expectations, and they outnumbered the French troops four-to-one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d224_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-d224-3">[3]</a></sup> Giáp compared Điện Biên Phủ to a "rice bowl," where his troops occupied the edge and the French the bottom. Secondly, Giáp made a mistake in Nà Sản by committing his forces to reckless frontal attacks before being fully prepared. At Điện Biên Phủ, Giáp spent months meticulously stockpiling ammunition and emplacing heavy artillery and anti-aircraft guns before making his move. Teams of Viet Minh volunteers were sent into the French camp to scout the disposition of the French artillery. Wooden artillery pieces were built as decoys and the real guns were rotated every few salvos to confuse French counter-battery fire. As a result, when the battle finally began, the Viet Minh knew exactly where the French artillery pieces were, while the French did not even know how many guns Giáp possessed. Thirdly, the aerial resupply lines at Nà Sản were never severed, despite Viet Minh anti-aircraft fire. At Điện Biên Phủ, Giáp amassed anti-aircraft batteries that quickly shut down the runway and made it extremely difficult and costly for the French to bring in reinforcements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude">Prelude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prelude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lead_up_to_Castor">Lead up to Castor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lead up to Castor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Cogny" title="René Cogny">René Cogny</a>, commander of the <a href="/wiki/Tonkin" title="Tonkin">Tonkin</a> Delta, proposed <a href="/wiki/%C4%90i%E1%BB%87n_Bi%C3%AAn_Ph%E1%BB%A7" title="Điện Biên Phủ">Điện Biên Phủ</a>, which had an old airstrip built by the Japanese during World War II, as a "mooring point".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In another misunderstanding, Cogny envisioned a lightly defended point from which to launch raids; however, Navarre believed that he intended a heavily fortified base capable of withstanding a siege. Navarre selected Điện Biên Phủ for the location of Berteil's "hedgehog" operation. When presented with the plan, every major subordinate officer protested: Colonel <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Nicot" title="Jean-Louis Nicot">Jean-Louis Nicot</a> (commander of the French Air transport fleet), Cogny, and generals Jean Gilles and Jean Dechaux (the ground and air commanders for <i>Operation Castor,</i> the initial airborne assault on Điện Biên Phủ). Cogny pointed out, presciently, that "we are running the risk of a new <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_N%C3%A0_S%E1%BA%A3n" title="Battle of Nà Sản">Nà Sản</a> under worse conditions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Navarre rejected the criticisms of his proposal and concluded a November 17 conference by declaring that the operation would begin three days later, on 20 November 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Navarre decided to go ahead with the operation, despite operational difficulties. These later became painfully obvious (but at the time may have been less apparent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> He had been repeatedly assured by his intelligence officers that the operation had very little risk of involvement by a strong enemy force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Navarre had previously considered three other ways to defend Laos: <a href="/wiki/Maneuver_warfare" title="Maneuver warfare">mobile warfare</a>, which was impossible given the terrain in Vietnam; a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Static_defense" title="Static defense">static defense</a> line stretching to Laos, which was not feasible given the number of troops at Navarre's disposal; or placing troops in the Laotian provincial capitals and supplying them by air, which was unworkable due to the distance from <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> to <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang" title="Luang Prabang">Luang Prabang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vientiane" title="Vientiane">Vientiane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Navarre believed that he was left only with the hedgehog option, which he characterized as "a mediocre solution".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The French National Defense Committee ultimately agreed that Navarre's responsibility did not include defending Laos. However, their decision (which was drawn up on 13 November) was not delivered to him until 4 December, two weeks after the Điện Biên Phủ operation began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d176_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-d176-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment_of_air_operations">Establishment of air operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Establishment of air operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on Dien Bien Phu order of battle, see <a href="/wiki/Operation_Castor" title="Operation Castor">Operation Castor</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Operations at Điện Biên Phủ began at 10:35 on the morning of 20 November 1953. In <i>Operation Castor</i>, the French dropped or flew 9,000 troops into the area over three days, including a bulldozer to prepare the airstrip. They were landed at three drop zones: "Natasha" (northwest), "Octavie" (southwest), and "Simone" (southeast) of Điện Biên Phủ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Viet Minh elite 148th Independent Infantry Regiment, headquartered at Điện Biên Phủ, reacted "instantly and effectively". Three of their four battalions, however, were absent that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Initial operations proceeded well for the French. By the end of November, six parachute battalions had been landed and the French were consolidating their positions.</p>
<p>It was at this time that Giáp began his counter-moves. He had expected an attack, but could not foresee when or where it would occur. Giáp realized that, if pressed, the French would abandon <a href="/wiki/Lai_Ch%C3%A2u_Province" title="Lai Châu Province">Lai Châu Province</a> and fight a <a href="/wiki/Pitched_battle" title="Pitched battle">pitched battle</a> at Điện Biên Phủ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson_p196_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson_p196-36">[36]</a></sup> On 24 November, Giáp ordered the 148th Infantry Regiment and the <a href="/wiki/316th_Division_(Vietnam)" title="316th Division (Vietnam)">316th Division</a> to attack Lai Chau, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/308_Division_(Vietnam)" title="308 Division (Vietnam)">308th</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=312th&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="312th (page does not exist)">312th</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=351st_Division&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="351st Division (page does not exist)">351st</a> divisions assault Điện Biên Phủ from <i><a href="/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_B%E1%BA%AFc" title="Việt Bắc">Việt Bắc</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson_p196_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson_p196-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in December, the French, under the command of Colonel <a href="/wiki/Christian_de_Castries" title="Christian de Castries">Christian de Castries</a>, began transforming their anchoring point into a fortress by setting up seven satellite positions. (Each was said to be named after a former mistress of de Castries, although the allegation is probably unfounded, as the eight names begin with letters from the first nine of the alphabet, excluding F.) The fortified headquarters was centrally located, with positions "Huguette" to the west, "Claudine" to the south, and "Dominique" to the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson.2C_p._199_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson.2C_p._199-37">[37]</a></sup> Other positions were "Anne-Marie" to the northwest, "Beatrice" to the northeast, "Gabrielle" to the north, and "Isabelle" 6 km (3.7 mi) to the south, covering the reserve airstrip.</p>
<p>The choice of de Castries as the on-scene commander at Điện Biên Phủ was, in retrospect, a bad one. Navarre had picked de Castries, a <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalryman</a> in the 18th-century tradition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-38">[38]</a></sup> because Navarre envisioned Điện Biên Phủ as a mobile battle. Điện Biên Phủ required someone adept at World War I-style <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a>, something for which de Castries was not suited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson.2C_p._199_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson.2C_p._199-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The arrival of the 316th Viet Minh Division prompted Cogny to order the evacuation of the Lai Chau garrison to Điện Biên Phủ, exactly as Giáp had anticipated. En route, they were virtually annihilated by the Viet Minh. "Of the 2,100 men who left Lai Chau on 9 December, only 185 made it to Điện Biên Phủ on 22 December. The rest had been killed, captured or deserted".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The Viet Minh troops converged on Điện Biên Phủ.</p>

<p>The French had committed 10,800 troops, with more reinforcements totaling nearly 16,000 men, to the defense of a monsoon-affected valley surrounded by heavily wooded hills that had not been secured. Artillery as well as ten <a href="/wiki/M24_Chaffee" title="M24 Chaffee">M24 Chaffee</a> light tanks and numerous aircraft were committed to the garrison. The garrison included French regular troops (notably elite paratroop units plus artillery), <a href="/wiki/French_Foreign_Legion" title="French Foreign Legion">Foreign Legionnaires</a>, Algerian and Moroccan <a href="/wiki/Tirailleur" title="Tirailleur">tirailleurs</a>, and locally recruited Indochinese infantry. All told, the Viet Minh had moved 50,000 regular troops into the hills surrounding the valley, totaling five divisions including the 351st Heavy Division, which was made up entirely of heavy artillery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d223_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-d223-5">[5]</a></sup> Artillery and AA (anti-aircraft) guns, which outnumbered the French artillery by about four to one,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d223_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-d223-5">[5]</a></sup> were moved into positions overlooking the valley. The French came under direct and sporadic Viet Minh artillery fire for the first time on 31 January 1954, and patrols encountered the Viet Minh in all directions. The French were surrounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beatrice">Beatrice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Beatrice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1048" data-file-width="712" height="368" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png/250px-Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png/375px-Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png/500px-Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dien_Bein_Phu_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The French disposition at Điện Biên Phủ, as of March 1954. The French took up positions on a series of fortified hills. The southernmost, Isabelle, was dangerously isolated. The Viet Minh positioned their five divisions (the 304th, 308th, 312th, 316th, and 351st) in the surrounding areas to the north and east. From these areas, the Viet Minh had a clear line of sight on the French fortifications and were able to accurately rain down artillery on the French positions.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Viet Minh assault began in earnest on 13 March 1954 with an attack on outpost "Beatrice". Viet Minh artillery opened a fierce bombardment of the fortification and French command was disrupted at 18:15 when a shell hit the French command post, killing Legionnaire commander Major Paul Pegot and his entire staff. A few minutes later, Colonel <a href="/wiki/Jules_Gaucher" title="Jules Gaucher">Jules Gaucher</a>, commander of the entire northern sector, was killed by Viet Minh artillery. The Viet Minh 312th Division then launched a massive infantry assault, using <a href="/wiki/Sapper" title="Sapper">sappers</a> to defeat French obstacles. French resistance at Beatrice collapsed shortly after midnight following a fierce battle. Roughly 500 French legionnaires were killed. The French estimated that Viet Minh losses totalled 600 dead and 1,200 wounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d236_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-d236-41">[41]</a></sup> The French launched a counter-attack against "Beatrice" the following morning, but it was quickly beaten back by Viet Minh artillery. The victory at "Beatrice" "galvanized the morale" of the Viet Minh troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d236_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-d236-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much to French disbelief, the Viet Minh had employed direct artillery fire, in which each gun crew does its own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artillery_spotting" title="Artillery spotting">artillery spotting</a> (as opposed to indirect fire, in which guns are massed farther away from the target, out of direct line of sight, and rely on a forward artillery spotter). Indirect artillery, generally held as being far superior to direct fire, requires experienced, well-trained crews and good communications, which the Viet Minh lacked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Navarre wrote that, "Under the influence of Chinese advisers, the Viet Minh commanders had used processes quite different from the classic methods. The artillery had been dug in by single pieces...They were installed in shell-proof dugouts, and fire point-blank from portholes...This way of using artillery and AA guns was possible only with the expansive ant holes at the disposal of the Vietminh and was to make shambles of all the estimates of our own artillerymen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Two days later, the French artillery commander, Colonel <a href="/wiki/Charles_Piroth" title="Charles Piroth">Charles Piroth</a>, distraught at his inability to bring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counterfire" title="Counterfire">counterfire</a> on the well-camouflaged Viet Minh batteries, went into his dugout and committed suicide with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_grenade" title="Hand grenade">hand grenade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> He was buried there in secret to prevent loss of morale among the French troops.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gabrielle">Gabrielle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gabrielle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following a five-hour cease fire on the morning of 14 March, Viet Minh artillery resumed pounding French positions. The air strip, already closed since 16:00 the day before due to a light bombardment, was now put permanently out of commission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Any further French supplies would have to be delivered by parachute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d237_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-d237-46">[46]</a></sup> That night, the Viet Minh launched an attack on "Gabrielle", held by an elite <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algerian</a> battalion. The attack began with a concentrated artillery barrage at 17:00. This was very effective and stunned the defenders. Two <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiments</a> from the crack 308th Division attacked starting at 20:00. At 04:00 the following morning, an artillery shell hit the battalion headquarters, severely wounding the battalion commander and most of his staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d237_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-d237-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>De Castries ordered a counterattack to relieve "Gabrielle". However, Colonel <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Langlais" title="Pierre Langlais">Pierre Langlais</a>, in forming the counterattack, chose to rely on the 5th Vietnamese Parachute Battalion, which had jumped in the day before and was exhausted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d238_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-d238-47">[47]</a></sup> Although some elements of the counterattack reached "Gabrielle", most were paralyzed by Viet Minh artillery and took heavy losses. At 08:00 the next day, the Algerian battalion fell back, abandoning "Gabrielle" to the Viet Minh. The French lost around 1,000 men defending Gabrielle, and the Viet Minh between 1,000 and 2,000 attacking the strongpoint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d238_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-d238-47">[47]</a></sup> The loss of the outpost "Beatrice" and now "Gabrielle", allowed almost pin point artillery to be rained down for the rest of the battle and cut off any air resupply using the airstrip, and this dictated the resulting events.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anne-Marie">Anne-Marie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anne-Marie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Anne-Marie" was defended by <a href="/wiki/Tai_peoples" title="Tai peoples">Tai</a> troops, members of a Vietnamese ethnic minority loyal to the French. For weeks, Giáp had distributed subversive <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> leaflets, telling the Tais that this was not their fight. The fall of "Beatrice" and "Gabrielle" had severely demoralized them. On the morning of 17 March, under the cover of fog, the bulk of the Tais left or defected. The French and the few remaining Tais on "Anne-Marie" were then forced to withdraw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lull">Lull</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lull">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>17 March through 30 March saw a lull in fighting. The Viet Minh further tightened the noose around the French central area (formed by the strongpoints "Huguette", "Dominique", "Claudine", and "Eliane"), effectively cutting off Isabelle and its 1,809 personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> During this lull, the French suffered from a serious crisis of command. "It had become painfully evident to the senior officers within the encircled garrison—and even to Cogny at Hanoi—that de Castries was incompetent to conduct the defense of Dien Bien Phu. Even more critical, after the fall of the northern outposts, he isolated himself in his bunker so that he had, in effect, relinquished his command authority".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d241_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-d241-50">[50]</a></sup> On 17 March, Cogny attempted to fly into Điện Biên Phủ to take command, but his plane was driven off by anti-aircraft fire. Cogny considered parachuting into the encircled garrison, but his staff talked him out of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d241_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-d241-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>De Castries' seclusion in his bunker, combined with his superiors' inability to replace him, created a leadership vacuum within the French command. On 24 March, an event took place which later became a matter of historical debate. Historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_Fall" title="Bernard Fall">Bernard Fall</a> records, based on Langlais' memoirs, that Colonel Langlais and his fellow paratroop commanders, all fully armed, confronted de Castries in his bunker on 24 March. They told him he would retain the appearance of command, but that Langlais would exercise it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> De Castries is said by Fall to have accepted the arrangement without protest, although he did exercise some command functions thereafter. Phillip Davidson stated that the "truth would seem to be that Langlais did take over effective command of Dien Bien Phu, and that Castries became 'commander emeritus' who transmitted messages to Hanoi and offered advice about matters in Dien Bien Phu".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jules_Roy" title="Jules Roy">Jules Roy</a>, however, makes no mention of this event, and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Windrow" title="Martin Windrow">Martin Windrow</a> argues that the "paratrooper putsch" is unlikely to have happened. Both historians record that Langlais and <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Bigeard" title="Marcel Bigeard">Marcel Bigeard</a> were known to be on good terms with their commanding officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French aerial resupply took heavy losses from Viet Minh machine guns near the landing strip. On 27 March, Hanoi air transport commander Nicot ordered that all supply deliveries be made from 2,000 m (6,600 ft) or higher; losses were expected to remain heavy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> De Castries ordered an attack against the Viet Minh machine guns 3 km (1.9 mi) west of Điện Biên Phủ. Remarkably, the attack was a complete success, with 350 Viet Minh soldiers killed and seventeen AA machine guns destroyed (French estimate), while the French lost 20 killed and 97 wounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="30_March_.E2.80.93_5_April_assaults">30 March – 5 April assaults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 30 March – 5 April assaults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Condor_(1954)" title="Operation Condor (1954)">Operation Condor (1954)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="744" data-file-width="1052" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg/350px-Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg/525px-Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg/700px-Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dien_Bien_Phu_zoom.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The central French positions at Điện Biên Phủ in late March 1954. The positions in Eliane saw some of the most intense combat of the entire battle.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The next phase of the battle saw more massed Viet Minh assaults against French positions in the central Điện Biên Phủ—at "Eliane" and "Dominique" in particular. Those two areas were held by five understrength battalions, composed of Frenchmen, Legionnaires, Vietnamese, North Africans, and Tais.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Giáp planned to use the tactics from the "Beatrice" and "Gabrielle" skirmishes.</p>
<p>At 19:00 on 30 March, the Viet Minh 312th Division captured "Dominique 1 and 2", making "Dominique 3" the final outpost between the Viet Minh and the French general headquarters, as well as outflanking all positions east of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d246_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-d246-57">[57]</a></sup> At this point, the French 4th Colonial Artillery Regiment entered the fight, setting its 105 mm howitzers to zero elevation and firing directly on the Viet Minh attackers, blasting huge holes in their ranks. Another group of French, near the airfield, opened fire on the Viet Minh with anti-aircraft machine guns, forcing the Viet Minh to retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d246_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-d246-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Viet Minh were more successful in their simultaneous attacks elsewhere. The 316th Division captured "Eliane 1" from its Moroccan defenders, and half of "Eliane 2" by midnight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d247_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-d247-58">[58]</a></sup> On the other side of Điện Biên Phủ, the 308th attacked "Huguette 7", and nearly succeeded in breaking through, but a French sergeant took charge of the defenders and sealed the breach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d247_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-d247-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just after midnight on 31 March, the French launched a counterattack against "Eliane 2", and recaptured half of it. Langlais ordered another counterattack the following afternoon against "Dominique 2" and "Eliane 1", using virtually "everybody left in the garrison who could be trusted to fight".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d247_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-d247-58">[58]</a></sup> The counterattacks allowed the French to retake "Dominique 2" and Eliane 1, but the Viet Minh launched their own renewed assault. The French, who were exhausted and without reserves, fell back from both positions late in the afternoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Reinforcements were sent north from "Isabelle", but were attacked en route and fell back to "Isabelle".</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="727" data-file-width="1048" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg/220px-French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg/330px-French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg/440px-French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:French_M24s_atr_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The French deployed a small number of <a href="/wiki/M24_Chaffee" title="M24 Chaffee">M24 Chaffee</a> light tanks during the battle that proved critical in repelling the enemy attacks.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Shortly after dark on 31 March, Langlais told Major <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Bigeard" title="Marcel Bigeard">Marcel Bigeard</a>, who was leading the defense at "Eliane", to fall back across the river. Bigeard refused, saying "As long as I have one man alive I won't let go of 'Eliane 4'. Otherwise, Dien Bien Phu is done for."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roy210_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-roy210-60">[60]</a></sup> The night of the 31st, the 316th Division attacked "Eliane 2". Just as it appeared the French were about to be overrun, a few French tanks arrived, and helped push the Viet Minh back. Smaller attacks on "Eliane 4" were also pushed back. The Viet Minh briefly captured "Huguette 7", only to be pushed back by a French counterattack at dawn on 1 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d253_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-d253-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fighting continued in this manner over the next several nights. The Viet Minh repeatedly attacked "Eliane 2", only to be beaten back. Repeated attempts to reinforce the French garrison by parachute drops were made, but had to be carried out by lone planes at irregular times to avoid excessive casualties from Viet Minh anti-aircraft fire. Some reinforcements did arrive, but not enough to replace French casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d253_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-d253-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trench_warfare">Trench warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Trench warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="813" data-file-width="1206" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg/220px-Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg/330px-Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg/440px-Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dien_Bien_Phu002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
French troops seeking cover in trenches.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 5 April, after a long night of battle, French fighter-bombers and artillery inflicted particularly devastating losses on one Viet Minh regiment, which was caught on open ground. At that point, Giáp decided to change tactics. Although Giáp still had the same objective—to overrun French defenses east of the river—he decided to employ <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">entrenchment and sapping</a> to try to achieve it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 10 April, the French attempted to retake "Eliane 1", which had been lost eleven days earlier. The loss posed a significant threat to "Eliane 4", and the French wanted to eliminate that threat. The dawn attack, which Bigeard devised, was preceded by a short, massive artillery barrage, followed by small unit <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_tactics" title="Infiltration tactics">infiltration attacks</a>, followed by mopping-up operations. "Eliane 1" changed hands several times that day, but by the next morning the French had control of the strongpoint. The Viet Minh attempted to retake it on the evening of 12 April, but were pushed back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>At this point, the morale of the Viet Minh soldiers was greatly lowered due to the massive casualties they had received. During a period of stalemate from 15 April to 1 May, the French intercepted enemy radio messages which told of whole units refusing orders to attack, and communist prisoners said that they were told to advance or be shot by the officers and non-commissioned officers behind them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Worse still, the Viet Minh lacked advanced medical care, with one stating that "Nothing strikes at combat morale like the knowledge that if wounded, the soldier will go uncared for".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> To avert the crisis of <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutiny</a>, Giáp called in fresh reinforcements from Laos.</p>
<p>During the fighting at "Eliane 1", on the other side of camp, the Viet Minh entrenchments had almost entirely surrounded "Huguette 1 and 6". On 11 April, the garrison of "Huguette 1" attacked, and was joined by artillery from the garrison of "Claudine". The goal was to resupply "Huguette 6" with water and ammunition. The attacks were repeated on the nights of the 14–15 and 16–17 April. While they did succeed in getting some supplies through, the French suffered heavy casualties, which convinced Langlais to abandon "Huguette 6". Following a failed attempt to link up, on 18 April, the defenders at "Huguette 6" made a daring break out, but only a few managed to make it to French lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The Viet Minh repeated the isolation and probing attacks against Huguette 1, and overran the fort on the morning of 22 April. With the fall of "Huguette 1", the Viet Minh took control of more than 90 percent of the airfield, making accurate parachute drops impossible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> This caused the landing zone to become perilously small, and effectively choked off much needed supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> A French attack against "Huguette 1" later that day was repulsed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isabelle">Isabelle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Isabelle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Isabelle" saw only light action until 30 March, when the Viet Minh succeeded in isolating it and beating back the attempt to send reinforcements north. Following a massive artillery barrage on 30 March, the Viet Minh began employing the same trench warfare tactics that they were using against the central camp. By the end of April, "Isabelle" had exhausted its water supply and was nearly out of ammunition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_attacks">Final attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Final attacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="405" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg/220px-Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg/330px-Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Victory_in_Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Viet Minh troops plant their flag over the captured French headquarters.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Viet Minh launched a massed assault against the exhausted defenders on the night of 1 May, overrunning "Eliane 1", "Dominique 3", and "Huguette 5", although the French managed to beat back attacks on "Eliane 2". On 6 May, the Viet Minh launched another massed attack against "Eliane 2". The attack included, for the first time, <a href="/wiki/Katyusha_rocket_launcher" title="Katyusha rocket launcher">Katyusha</a> rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d236_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-d236-41">[41]</a></sup> The French artillery fired a "TOT" (<a href="/wiki/Time_On_Target" title="Time On Target">Time On Target</a>) attack, so artillery rounds fired from different positions would strike on target at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> This barrage defeated the first assault wave. A few hours later that night, the Viet Minh detonated a mine shaft, blowing "Eliane 2" up. The Viet Minh attacked again, and within a few hours had overrun the defenders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 May, Giáp ordered an all-out attack against the remaining French units with over 25,000 Viet Minh against fewer than 3,000 garrison troops. At 17:00, de Castries radioed French headquarters in Hanoi and talked with Cogny.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>De Castries: "The Viets are everywhere. The situation is very grave. The combat is confused and goes on all about. I feel the end is approaching, but we will fight to the finish."<br/>
Cogny: "Of course you will fight to the end. It is out of the question to run up the white flag after your heroic resistance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-time-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>By nightfall, all French central positions had been captured. The last radio transmission from the French headquarters reported that enemy troops were directly outside the headquarters bunker and that all the positions had been overrun. The radio operator in his last words stated: "The enemy has overrun us. We are blowing up everything. <i>Vive la France</i>!" That night the garrison made a breakout attempt, in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Camar%C3%B3n" title="Battle of Camarón">Camarón</a> tradition. While some of the main body managed to break out, none succeeded in escaping the valley. However at "Isabelle", a similar attempt later the same night saw about 70 troops, out of 1,700 men in the garrison, escape to Laos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prisoners">Prisoners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Prisoners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 8 May, the Viet Minh counted 11,721 prisoners, of whom 4,436 were wounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-losses_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-losses-74">[74]</a></sup> This was the greatest number the Viet Minh had ever captured: one-third of the total captured during the entire war. The prisoners were divided into groups. Able-bodied soldiers were force-marched over 600 km (370 mi) to prison camps to the north and east,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> where they were intermingled with Viet Minh soldiers to discourage French bombing runs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Hundreds died of disease along the way. The wounded were given basic first aid until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> arrived, removed 858, and provided better aid to the remainder. Those wounded who were not evacuated by the Red Cross were sent into detention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of their jailers was <a href="/wiki/Georges_Boudarel" title="Georges Boudarel">Georges Boudarel</a>, a French academic and Communist militant. He was accused of torturing French prisoners for the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">Viet Minh</a> during the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a>.</p>
<p>Of 10,863 survivors held as prisoners, only 3,290 were officially repatriated four months later;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-losses_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-losses-74">[74]</a></sup> however, the losses figure may include the 3,013 prisoners of Vietnamese origin whose eventual fate is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_ramifications">Political ramifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Political ramifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The garrison constituted roughly a tenth of the total French Union manpower in Indochina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The defeat seriously weakened the position and prestige of the French as previously planned negotiations over the future of Indochina began.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">Geneva Conference</a> opened on 8 May 1954,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> the day after the surrender of the garrison. Ho Chi Minh entered the conference on the opening day with the news of his troops' victory in the headlines. The resulting agreement temporarily partitioned Vietnam into two zones: the North was administered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">communist</a> <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> while the South was administered by the French-supported <a href="/wiki/State_of_Vietnam" title="State of Vietnam">State of Vietnam</a>. The last units of the French Union forces withdrew from Indochina in 1956. This partition was supposed to be temporary, and the two zones were meant to be reunited through national elections in 1956. After the French withdrawal, the United States supported the southern government, under Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bao_Dai" title="Bao Dai">Bao Dai</a> and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Ngo Dinh Diem">Ngo Dinh Diem</a>, which opposed the Geneva agreement, and which claimed that Ho Chi Minh's forces from the North had been killing Northern loyalists and terrorizing people both north and south. The North was supported by both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC) and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (USSR). This arrangement proved tenuous and escalated into the Vietnam War (Second Indochina War), eventually bringing 500,000 American troops into South Vietnam.</p>
<p>France's defeat in Indochina, coupled with the German destruction of her armies just 14 years earlier, seriously damaged its prestige elsewhere in its colonial empire, as well as with its <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> allies, most importantly, the United States. Within her empire, the defeat in Indochina served to spur independence movements in other colonies, notably the North African territories from which many of the troops who fought at Điện Biên Phủ had been recruited. In 1954, six months after the battle at Điện Biên Phủ ended, the <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">Algerian War</a> started, and by 1956 both the Moroccan and Tunisian protectorates had gained independence. A French board of inquiry, the <a href="/wiki/Catroux_Commission" title="Catroux Commission">Catroux Commission</a>, later investigated the defeat.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_participation">American participation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: American participation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Vulture" title="Operation Vulture">Operation Vulture</a></div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Mutual_Defense_Assistance_Act" title="Mutual Defense Assistance Act">Mutual Defense Assistance Act</a>, the United States provided the French with material aid during the battle – aircraft (supplied by the <a href="/wiki/USS_Saipan_(CVL-48)" title="USS Saipan (CVL-48)">USS <i>Saipan</i></a>), weapons, mechanics, 24 <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a>/<a href="/wiki/Civil_Air_Transport" title="Civil Air Transport">CAT</a> pilots, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> maintenance crews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roy_p140_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roy_p140-81">[81]</a></sup> The United States, however, intentionally avoided overt direct intervention. In February 1954, following the French occupation of Điện Biên Phủ but prior to the battle, Democratic senator <a href="/wiki/Mike_Mansfield" title="Mike Mansfield">Michael Mansfield</a> asked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Defense_Secretary" title="United States Defense Secretary">United States Defense Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Erwin_Wilson" title="Charles Erwin Wilson">Charles Erwin Wilson</a> whether the United States would send naval or air units if the French were subjected to greater pressure there, but Wilson replied that "for the moment there is no justification for raising United States aid above its present level". President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> also stated, "Nobody is more opposed to intervention than I am".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roy_p140_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roy_p140-81">[81]</a></sup> On 31 March, following the fall of "Beatrice", "Gabrielle", and "Anne-Marie", a panel of U.S. Senators and House Representatives questioned the American <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff</a>, Admiral <a href="/wiki/Arthur_W._Radford" title="Arthur W. Radford">Arthur W. Radford</a>, about the possibility of American involvement. Radford concluded it was too late for the U.S. Air Force to save the French garrison. A proposal for direct intervention was unanimously voted down by the panel, which "concluded that intervention was a positive <a href="/wiki/Casus_belli" title="Casus belli">act of war</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United States did covertly participate in the battle. Following a request for help from <a href="/wiki/Henri_Navarre" title="Henri Navarre">Henri Navarre</a>, Radford provided two squadrons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-26_Invader" title="A-26 Invader">B-26 Invader</a> bomber aircraft to support the French. Subsequently, 37 American transport pilots flew 682 sorties over the course of the battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franco_Embassy_2005_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franco_Embassy_2005-83">[83]</a></sup> Earlier, in order to succeed the pre-Điện Biên Phủ <a href="/wiki/Operation_Castor" title="Operation Castor">Operation Castor</a> of November 1953, General <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chester_McCarty&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chester McCarty (page does not exist)">Chester McCarty</a> made available 12 additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-119_Flying_Boxcar" title="C-119 Flying Boxcar">C-119 Flying Boxcars</a> flown by French crews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franco_Embassy_2005_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Franco_Embassy_2005-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two of the American pilots, <a href="/wiki/James_B._McGovern,_Jr." title="James B. McGovern, Jr.">James McGovern, Jr.</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wallace_Buford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wallace Buford (page does not exist)">Wallace Buford</a>, were killed in action during the siege of Điện Biên Phủ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> On 25 February 2005, the seven still living American pilots were awarded the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honor" title="Legion of Honor">Legion of Honor</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jean-David_Levitte" title="Jean-David Levitte">Jean-David Levitte</a>, the French ambassador to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franco_Embassy_2005_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-Franco_Embassy_2005-83">[83]</a></sup> The role that the American pilots played in this battle had remained little known until 2004. The American historian Erik Kirsinger researched the case for more than a year to establish the facts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> French author <a href="/wiki/Jules_Roy" title="Jules Roy">Jules Roy</a> suggests that Admiral Radford discussed with the French the possibility of using <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> in support of the French garrison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Moreover, <a href="/wiki/John_Foster_Dulles" title="John Foster Dulles">John Foster Dulles</a> reportedly mentioned the possibility of lending atomic bombs to the French for use at Điện Biên Phủ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> and a similar source claims that the British Foreign Secretary Sir <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Eden" title="Anthony Eden">Anthony Eden</a> was aware of the possibility of the use of nuclear weapons in that region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Khe_Sanh">Khe Sanh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Khe Sanh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Khe_Sanh" title="Battle of Khe Sanh">Battle of Khe Sanh</a></div>
<p>In January 1968, during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Vietnamese_Army" title="North Vietnamese Army">North Vietnamese Army</a> (still under Giáp's command) initiated a <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> and artillery <a href="/wiki/Bombardment" title="Bombardment">bombardment</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> infantry and artillery base at <a href="/wiki/Khe_Sanh" title="Khe Sanh">Khe Sanh</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnam</a>. Historians are divided on whether this was a genuine attempt to repeat their success at Điện Biên Phủ by forcing the surrender of the Marine base, or else a diversion from the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">Tết Offensive</a>, or an example of the North Vietnamese Army keeping its options open. At Khe Sanh, a number of factors were significantly different from Điện Biên Phủ. Khe Sanh was much closer to an American supply base (45 km or 28 mi) compared to a French one at Điện Biên Phủ (200 km or 120 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Khe Sanh, the U.S. Marines held the high ground, and their artillery forced the North Vietnamese to use their own artillery from a much greater distance. By contrast, at Điện Biên Phủ, the French artillery (six 105 mm batteries and one battery of four 155 mm howitzers and mortars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>) were only sporadically effective;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rottman_p9_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rottman_p9-92">[92]</a></sup> Khe Sanh received 18,000 tons of aerial resupplies during the 77-day battle, whereas during the 167 days that the French forces at Điện Biên Phủ held out, they received only 4,000 tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rottman_p9_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rottman_p9-92">[92]</a></sup> And lastly, the US Air Force dropped 114,810 tons of bombs on the Vietnamese at Khe Sanh – roughly as much as on Japan in 1945 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Women_at_.C4.90i.E1.BB.87n_Bi.C3.AAn_Ph.E1.BB.A7">Women at Điện Biên Phủ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Women at Điện Biên Phủ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the flights operated by the French Air force to evacuate casualties had female flight nurses on board. A total of 15 women served on flights to Điện Biên Phủ. One, <a href="/wiki/Genevi%C3%A8ve_de_Galard" title="Geneviève de Galard">Geneviève de Galard</a>, was stranded there when her plane was destroyed by shellfire while being repaired on the airfield. She remained on the ground providing medical services in the field hospital until the surrender. She was later referred to as the "Angel of Điện Biên Phủ". However, historians disagree regarding this moniker, with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Windrow" title="Martin Windrow">Martin Windrow</a> maintaining that de Galard was referred to by this name by the garrison itself, but <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Kenney_(journalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Kenney (journalist) (page does not exist)">Michael Kenney</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_Fall" title="Bernard Fall">Bernard Fall</a> maintaining it was added by outside press agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French forces came to Điện Biên Phủ accompanied by two <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bordels_mobiles_de_campagne" title="Bordels mobiles de campagne">bordels mobiles de campagne</a></i>, ("mobile field brothels"), served by Algerian and Vietnamese women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> When the siege ended, the Viet Minh sent the surviving Vietnamese women for "re-education".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pringle2004_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pringle2004-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The battle was depicted in two films.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Jump_into_Hell" title="Jump into Hell">Jump into Hell</a></i> (1955) was an American film directed by <a href="/wiki/David_Butler_(director)" title="David Butler (director)">David Butler</a> that was shot in the US and released by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Dien_Bien_Phu_(film)" title="Dien Bien Phu (film)">Dien Bien Phu</a></i>, was a 1992 docudrama film — with several autobiographical parts — in conjunction with the Vietnamese army by Điện Biên Phủ veteran and French director <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Schoendoerffer" title="Pierre Schoendoerffer">Pierre Schoendoerffer</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Dien Bien Phu Falls is mentioned in the 1989 song "<a href="/wiki/We_Didn%27t_Start_the_Fire" title="We Didn't Start the Fire">We Didn't Start the Fire</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Billy_Joel" title="Billy Joel">Billy Joel</a>.</p>
