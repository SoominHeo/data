<p><i><b>D-War</b></i> (Korean: 디워, released in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> as <i><b>Dragon Wars: D-War</b></i>), is a 2007 South Korean-American <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a>-<a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure film</a> written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Shim_Hyung-rae" title="Shim Hyung-rae">Shim Hyung-rae</a>, and starring <a href="/wiki/Jason_Behr" title="Jason Behr">Jason Behr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Brooks" title="Amanda Brooks">Amanda Brooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Forster" title="Robert Forster">Robert Forster</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Elizabeth Peña">Elizabeth Peña</a>.</p>
<p>As of its release, it is the highest-budgeted Korean film of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kh_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kh-1">[1]</a></sup> The film was a box office success, grossing almost $100 million worldwide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-2">[2]</a></sup> but received generally negative reviews from critics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D-War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The story follows the adventures of Ethan Kendrick (<a href="/wiki/Jason_Behr" title="Jason Behr">Jason Behr</a>), charged in his childhood by the enigmatic Jack (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Forster" title="Robert Forster">Robert Forster</a>) to protect the Yuh Yi Joo, an individual born able to change an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imoogi" title="Imoogi">Imoogi</a> into a Celestial Dragon. To this end, Jack gives Ethan a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medallion" title="Medallion">medallion</a> formerly belonging to Haram and reveals that the Yuh Yi Joo is Sarah Daniels (<a href="/wiki/Amanda_Brooks" title="Amanda Brooks">Amanda Brooks</a>), whom Ethan will find in Los Angeles.</p>
<p>15 years after this revelation, a corrupt Imoogi identified as "Buraki" invades the city, bent on capturing Sarah; whereupon Ethan, now a television news anchor, rescues her, conveys the knowledge of her purpose, and attempts to save her from Buraki. During the resulting chase, Buraki's "Atrox Army" enters the city and engages the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">Los Angeles Police Department</a> in battle. Here, the Atrox Army is shown to consist of black-armored, humanoid knight warriors; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> called "Shaconnes"; small, winged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_dragon" title="Western dragon">Western dragons</a> called "Bulcos"; and immense, slow-moving reptiles carrying <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a>-launchers on their backs, identified in the dialogue as "Dawdlers". Despite losses, this army overwhelms the human forces, while Ethan and Sarah escape. They are subsequently captured by the Bulcos and taken to a menacing fortress in the midst of a darkened desert landscape. There, Ethan's medallion destroys the Atrox Army; but Buraki attempts to consume Sarah, whereupon he is attacked by the Good Imoogi. The two Imoogi, good and evil, engage each other in a duel that Buraki appears to win after seemingly snapping the Good Imoogi's neck. Having won the fight, Buraki again approaches Sarah; but having offered herself to him, she instead gives her power to the Good Imoogi who had just revived, who thereupon becomes the Celestial Dragon, continuing the duel until it finally destroys Buraki.</p>
<p>After Buraki is destroyed, Sarah dissolves into a <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">spirit</a> form, and the Celestial Dragon permits her to speak to Ethan, whom she promises to "love...for all eternity"; then withdraws Sarah into his body and ascends to the <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heavens</a>. Jack appears behind Ethan, reminding him that the two of them "have been given a great honor" to take part in this transformation, and vanishes, presumably never to be seen again. After whispering "Goodbye old man", Ethan walks away into the desert.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D-War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally titled <i>Dragon Wars</i> (and still referred to by this title in publicity material), <i>D-War</i> has a long production history in South Korea. The film was announced in 2002 by director <a href="/wiki/Shim_Hyung-rae" title="Shim Hyung-rae">Shim Hyung-rae</a> as his follow-up project to 1999's <i><a href="/wiki/Yonggary_(1999_film)" title="Yonggary (1999 film)">Yonggary</a></i>. A show reel appeared in early 2003<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, displaying the extensive amount of <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> used to create the various creatures. Despite heavy promotion via posters, press releases, and videos, principal photography did not begin until October 2004, continuing through December.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The budget was set at 30 million <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_won" title="South Korean won">won</a> (approximately US$35 million),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but ultimately went over budget in order to create the various creatures in the film, with some outside estimation as high as $US75 million dollars. The DVD release confirmed that it did indeed cost US$99 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kh_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-kh-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with <i>Yonggary</i>, Shim opted for a mostly American cast. Veteran actor Robert Forster landed a pivotal role and Jason Behr and Amanda Brooks were cast as the two young leads.</p>
<p>The next three years were spent creating the creature effects, all of which were done in house by Shim's Younggu-Art Movies company. The completed film premiered at the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Market" title="American Film Market">American Film Market</a> in early 2007. The film was released in South Korea on 1 August 2007. In the U.S., the film was released on 14 September 2007.</p>
<p>On 7 August 2007, South Korea's <a href="/wiki/Munhwa_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation">Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation</a> (MBC) <i>Morning Live TV Show</i> broadcast the film's final scene on TV without the permission of the studio, causing a controversy. A few days later the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Culture_and_Tourism_(South_Korea)" title="Ministry of Culture and Tourism (South Korea)">Ministry of Culture and Tourism</a> released a statement in which they said that the incident did not violate South Korean copyright laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D-War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film received mostly negative reviews upon release in the U.S. and was not screened in advance for many critics. Up to 22 September 2014, 29% of the review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> critics gave the film positive reviews, based on 35 reviews,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> while the film scored a 33 out of 100 at <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Derek Elley of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i>, reviewing it at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Film_Festival" title="Berlin Film Festival">Berlin Film Festival</a>'s market section, called it "visually entertaining, and superior to helmer Shim Hyung-rae's last monster movie (<i>Yonggary</i> in 1999)", while also saying the film had a "Z-grade, irony-free script," and "likely to end up the most expensive cult movie on DVD."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i>'s Frank Scheck said, "the CGI effects are undeniably impressive" but that "the laughable story line, risible dialogue and cheap humor ... seriously detract from the fun."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Luke Y. Thompson in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L.A._Weekly" title="L.A. Weekly">L.A. Weekly</a></i> derided the film as one "for connaisseurs of the 'totally preposterous crap' school of fantasy cinema... You know who you are: You have all the Warlock sequels on Laserdisc [and] the complete Leprechaun series on DVD" and says it's "funnier when it tries to be serious than when it goes for the gag."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within nine days of its South Korean release, <i>D-War</i> attracted five million viewers, setting a national box office record for an opening week.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The seemingly positive reaction from the Korean population, as indicated by the movie's box office success in Korea, was widely attributed to the film's appeal to Korean nationalism;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-10">[10]</a></sup> a logical impression drawn from Shim's message at the end of the Korean version of this film, "<i>D-War</i> and I will succeed in the world market without fail," accompanied by the Korean folk anthem, "<a href="/wiki/Arirang" title="Arirang">Arirang</a>." However, despite box office success, <i>D-Wars</i> was far from critically acclaimed by either Korean critics or Korea's general public. Korean film critic Kim Bong-sok said, "They want it to be successful in the U.S. because it's Korean, not because it's good" and called the film "immature and poorly made" and "below criticism". Other reactions from Korean critics have been similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>D-War</i> set a record of grossing US$20.3 million in South Korea in its first five days of release. As of 1 September, the film has grossed US$44 million in Korea and another US$10 million in other countries, totaling a worldwide gross of US$54 million as of 16 September. In North America, the film grossed US$5 million on 2,275 screens in its opening weekend. As of 25 November 2007, the film has grossed US$10,977,721 in North America,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> making it the highest-grossing Korean-made film released theatrically in North America.</p>
<p>Since then, <i>D-War</i> has been released theatrically in Malaysia and China, both with moderate critical reaction. China was the only one to live up to the South Korean release record, spawning 3,000,000 admissions and a premiere including pop idol Libing Chen. The movie was a box office failure in Japan.</p>
