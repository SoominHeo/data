<p>A <b>digital identity</b> is information on an entity used by <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer systems</a> to represent an external agent. That agent may be a person, organisation, application, or device. ISO/IEC 24760-1 defines identity as "set of attributes related to an entity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The information contained in a digital identity allows these questions to be answered without the involvement of human operators. Digital identities allow our access to computers and the services they provide to be automated, and make it possible for computers to mediate relationships.</p>
<p>The term "digital identity" has also come to denote aspects of civil and personal identity that have resulted from the widespread use of identity information to represent people in computer systems.</p>
<p>Digital identity is now often used in ways that require data about persons stored in computer systems to be linked to their civil, or national, identities. Furthermore, the use of digital identities is now so widespread that many discussions refer to "digital identity" as the entire collection of information generated by a person’s online activity. This includes usernames and passwords, online search activities, birth date, social security, and purchasing history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Especially where that information is publicly available, and can be used by others to discover that person's civil identity. In this wider sense, a digital identity is a version, or facet, of a person's social identity. This may also be referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Online_identity" title="Online identity">online identity</a>.</p>
<p>The legal and social effects of digital identity are complex and challenging. However, they are simply a consequence of the increasing use of computers, and the need to provide computers with information that can be used to identify external agents.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A critical problem in cyberspace is knowing with whom one is interacting. Currently there are no ways to precisely determine the identity of a person in digital space. Even though there are attributes associated to a person's digital identity, these attributes or even identities can be changed, masked or dumped and new ones created. Despite the fact that there are many <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">authentication</a> systems and digital identifiers that try to address these problems, there is still a need for a unified and verified identification system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus, there are issues of <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> and security related to digital identity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terms">Related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subject_and_entity">Subject and entity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subject and entity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A digital identity may also be referred to as a Digital Subject or Digital <a href="/wiki/Entity" title="Entity">entity</a> and is the digital representation of a set of claims made by one party about itself or another person, group, thing or concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attributes.2C_preferences_and_traits">Attributes, preferences and traits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Attributes, preferences and traits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Every digital identity has zero or more <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Identity_attributes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Identity attributes (page does not exist)">identity attributes</a>. Attributes are acquired and contain information about a subject, such as medical history, purchasing behaviour, bank balance, age and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Windley_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Windley-5">[5]</a></sup> Preferences retain a subject's choices such as favourite brand of shoes, preferred currency. Traits are features of the subject that are inherent, such as eye colour, nationality, place of birth. While attributes of a subject can change easily, traits change slowly, if at all.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_aspects">Technical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technical aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trust.2C_authentication_and_authorization">Trust, authentication and authorization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trust, authentication and authorization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In order to assign a digital representation to an entity, the attributing party must trust that the claim of an attribute (such as name, location, role as an employee, or age) is correct and associated with the person or thing presenting the attribute (see <a href="/wiki/Digital_identity#Authentication" title="Digital identity">Authentication</a> below). Conversely, the individual claiming an attribute may only grant selective access to its information, e.g. when one proves identity in a bar or PayPal authentication for payment at a web site. In this way, digital identity is better understood as a particular viewpoint within a mutually-agreed relationship than as an objective property. This contextual nature of digital identity is referred to as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Contextual_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Contextual identity (page does not exist)">contextual identity</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Authentication">Authentication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Authentication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">Authentication</a> is a key aspect of trust-based identity attribution, providing a codified assurance of the identity of one entity to another. Authentication methodologies include the presentation of a unique object such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_credit_card" title="Bank credit card">bank credit card</a>, the provision of confidential information such as a <a href="/wiki/Password" title="Password">password</a> or the answer to a pre-arranged question, the confirmation of ownership of an e-mail address, and more robust but relatively costly solutions utilising <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a> methodologies. In general, business-to-business authentication prioritises security while user to business authentication tends towards simplicity. Physical authentication techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iris_scan" title="Iris scan">iris scanning</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Handprinting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Handprinting (page does not exist)">handprinting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Speaker_recognition" title="Speaker recognition">voiceprinting</a> are currently being developed and in the hope of providing improved protection against <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a>. Those techniques fall into the area of Biometry (<a href="/wiki/Biometrics" title="Biometrics">biometrics</a>).</p>
<p>Whilst technological progress in authentication continues to evolve, these systems do not prevent aliases being used. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Strong_authentication" title="Strong authentication">strong authentication</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for online payment transactions within the European Union now links a verified person to an account, where such person has been identified in accordance with statutory requirements prior to account being opened. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Reliance_authentication" title="Reliance authentication">reliance authentication</a> makes use of pre-existing accounts, to piggy back further services upon those accounts, providing that the original source is reliable. The concept of reliability comes from various anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism funding legislation in the USA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> EU28,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Singapore and New Zealand<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> where second parties may place reliance on the customer due diligence process of the first party, where the first party is say a financial institution. An example of reliance authentication is PayPal's verification method.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Authorization">Authorization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Authorization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authorization_(computer_access_control)" title="Authorization (computer access control)">Authorization</a> is the determination of any entity that controls resources that the authenticated can access those resources. Authorization depends on authentication, because authorization requires that the critical attribute (i.e., the attribute that determines the authorizer's decision) must be verified. For example, authorization on a credit card gives access to the resources owned by Amazon, e.g., Amazon sends one a product. Authorization of an employee will provide that employee with access to network resources, such as printers, files, or software. For example, a database management system might be designed so as to provide certain specified individuals with the ability to retrieve information from a database but not the ability to change data stored in the database, while giving other individuals the ability to change data.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Consider the person who rents a car and checks into a hotel with a credit card. The car rental and hotel company may request authentication that there is credit enough for an accident, or profligate spending on room service. Thus a card may be refused when trying to book the balloon trip, though there is adequate credit to pay for the rental, the hotel, and the balloon trip. Then when the person leaves the hotel and returns the car, the actual charges are authorized (ironically of course too late for the balloon trip).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_identifiers">Digital identifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Digital identifiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Digital identity fundamentally requires digital identifiers—strings or tokens that are unique within a given scope (globally or locally within a specific domain, community, directory, application, etc.). Identifiers are the key used by the parties to an identification relationship to agree on the entity being represented. Identifiers may be classified as <i>omnidirectional</i> and <i>unidirectional</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Omnidirectional identifiers are intended to be public and easily discoverable, while unidirectional identifiers are intended to be private and used only in the context of a specific identity relationship.</p>
<p>Identifiers may also be classified as resolvable or non-resolvable. Resolvable identifiers, such as a <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail_address" title="E-mail address">e-mail address</a>, may be dereferenced into the entity they represent, or some current state data providing relevant attributes of that entity. Non-resolvable identifiers, such as a person's real-world name, or a subject or topic name, can be compared for equivalence but are not otherwise machine-understandable.</p>
<p>There are many different schemes and formats for digital identifiers. The most widely used is <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Identifier" title="Uniform Resource Identifier">Uniform Resource Identifier</a> (URI) and its internationalized version <a href="/wiki/Internationalized_Resource_Identifier" title="Internationalized Resource Identifier">Internationalized Resource Identifier</a> (IRI)—the standard for identifiers on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. <a href="/wiki/OpenID" title="OpenID">OpenID</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light-Weight_Identity" title="Light-Weight Identity">Light-Weight Identity</a> (LID) are two web authentication protocols that use standard HTTP URIs (often called URLs), for example.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_Object_Architecture">Digital Object Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Digital Object Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Digital Object Architecture (DOA)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> provides a means of managing digital information in a network environment. A digital object has a machine and platform independent structure that allows it to be identified, accessed and protected, as appropriate. A digital object may incorporate not only informational elements, i.e., a digitized version of a paper, movie or sound recording, but also the unique identifier of the digital object and other metadata about the digital object. The metadata may include restrictions on access to digital objects, notices of ownership, and identifiers for licensing agreements, if appropriate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Handle_System">Handle System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Handle System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Handle_System" title="Handle System">Handle System</a> is a general purpose distributed information system that provides efficient, extensible, and secure identifier and resolution services for use on networks such as the internet. It includes an open set of protocols, a <a href="/wiki/Namespace" title="Namespace">namespace</a>, and a reference implementation of the protocols. The protocols enable a <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computer system</a> to store identifiers, known as handles, of arbitrary resources and resolve those handles into the information necessary to locate, access, contact, authenticate, or otherwise make use of the resources. This information can be changed as needed to reflect the current state of the identified resource without changing its identifier, thus allowing the name of the item to persist over changes of location and other related state information. The original version of the Handle System technology was developed with support from the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensible_Resource_Identifiers">Extensible Resource Identifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extensible Resource Identifiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A new <a href="/wiki/OASIS_(organization)" title="OASIS (organization)">OASIS</a> standard for abstract, structured identifiers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XRI" title="XRI">XRI</a> (Extensible Resource Identifiers), adds new features to URIs and IRIs that are especially useful for digital identity systems. <a href="/wiki/OpenID" title="OpenID">OpenID</a> also supports XRIs, and XRIs are the basis for <a href="/wiki/I-name" title="I-name">i-names</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_aspects">Policy aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Policy aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are proponents of treating self-determination and freedom of expression of digital identity as a new <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human right</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some have speculated that digital identities could become a new form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juristic_person" title="Juristic person">legal entity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomies_of_identity">Taxonomies of identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Taxonomies of identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Digital identity attributes—or data—exist within the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontology_(computer_science)" title="Ontology (computer science)">ontologies</a>. A simple example of a taxonomy is "A <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cat</a> is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a>." An entity represented in this ontology as a "cat" is therefore invariably also considered an "animal." In establishing the contextual relationship of identity attributes to one another, taxonomies are able to represent identity in terms of pre-defined structures. This in turn allows computer applications to process identity attributes in a reliable and useful manner. <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> (eXtensible Markup Language) has become a de facto standard for the abstract description of structured data.</p>
<p>Taxonomies inevitably reflect <a href="/wiki/Cultural_relativism" title="Cultural relativism">culturally</a> and personally relative world views. Consider two possible elaborations of the above example:</p>
<ol>
<li>"A cat is a kind of animal. A domestic cat is a kind of cat and is a pet."</li>
<li>"A cat is a kind of animal. A domestic cat is a kind of cat and is edible by humans."</li>
</ol>
<p>Someone searching the first taxonomy for pets would find "domestic cat," whereas a search of the second taxonomy for foodstuffs would yield the same result! We can see that while each taxonomy is useful within a particular cultural context or set of contexts, neither represents a universally valid point of view on domestic cats.</p>
<p>The development of digital identity network solutions that can interoperate taxonomically-diverse representations of digital identity is a contemporary challenge. <a href="/wiki/Folksonomy" title="Folksonomy">Free-tagging</a> has emerged recently as an effective way of circumventing this challenge (to date, primarily with application to the identity of digital entities such as bookmarks and photos) by effectively flattening identity attributes into a single, unstructured layer. However, the organic integration of the benefits of both structured and fluid approaches to identity attribute management remains elusive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Networked_identity">Networked identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Networked identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Identity relationships within a digital network may include multiple <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Identity_entity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Identity entity (page does not exist)">identity entities</a>. However, in a decentralised network like the Internet, such extended identity relationships effectively require both (a) the existence of independent trust relationships between each pair of entities in the relationship and (b) a means of reliably integrating the paired relationships into larger relational units. And if identity relationships are to reach beyond the context of a single, federated ontology of identity (see <a href="#Taxonomies_of_identity">Taxonomies of identity</a> above), identity attributes must somehow be matched across diverse ontologies. The development of network approaches that can embody such integrated "compound" trust relationships is currently a topic of much debate in the <a href="/wiki/Blogosphere" title="Blogosphere">blogosphere</a>.</p>
<p>Integrated compound trust relationships allow, for example, entity A to accept an assertion or claim about entity B by entity C. C thus vouches for an aspect of B's identity to A.</p>
<p>A key feature of "compound" trust relationships is the possibility of selective disclosure from one entity to another of locally relevant information. As an illustration of the potential application of selective disclosure, let us suppose a certain Diana wished to book a hire car without disclosing irrelevant personal information (utilising a notional digital identity network that supports compound trust relationships). As an adult, UK resident with a current driving license, Diana might have the UK's <a href="/wiki/Driver_and_Vehicle_Licensing_Agency" title="Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency">Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency</a> vouch for her driving qualification, age and nationality to a car-rental company without having her name or contact details disclosed. Similarly, Diana's bank might assert just her banking details to the rental company. Selective disclosure allows for appropriate <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> of information within a network of identity relationships.</p>
<p>A classic form of networked digital identity based on international standards is the "White Pages".</p>
<p>An electronic white pages links various devices, like computers and telephones, to an individual or organization. Various attributes such as X.509v3 digital certificates for secure cryptographic communications are captured under a schema, and published in an <a href="/wiki/Lightweight_Directory_Access_Protocol" title="Lightweight Directory Access Protocol">LDAP</a> or <a href="/wiki/X.500" title="X.500">X.500</a> directory. Changes to the LDAP standard are managed by working groups in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IETF" title="IETF">IETF</a>, and changes in X.500 are managed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>. The ITU did significant analysis of gaps in digital identity interoperability via the FGidm, focus group on <a href="/wiki/Identity_management" title="Identity management">identity management</a>.</p>
<p>Implementations of X.500[2005] and LDAPv3 have occurred worldwide but are primarily located in major data centers with administrative policy boundaries regarding sharing of personal information. Since combined X.500 [2005] and LDAPv3 directories can hold millions of unique objects for rapid access, it is expected to play a continued role for large scale secure identity access services. LDAPv3 can act as a lightweight standalone server, or in the original design as a TCP-IP based Lightweight Directory Access Protocol compatible with making queries to a X.500 mesh of servers which can run the native OSI protocol.</p>
<p>This will be done by scaling individual servers into larger groupings that represent defined "administrative domains", (such as the country level digital object) which can add value not present in the original "White Pages" that was used to look up phone numbers and email addresses, largely now available through non-authoritative search engines.</p>
<p>The ability to leverage and extend a networked digital identity is made more practicable by the expression of the level of trust associated with the given identity through a common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identity_Assurance_Framework" title="Identity Assurance Framework">Identity Assurance Framework</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_issues_and_privacy">Security issues and privacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Security issues and privacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With automated face recognition, tagging, location tracking and widespread digital authentication systems many actions of a person become easily associated with identity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-3">[3]</a></sup> as a cause, sometimes <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> is lost and security is subverted. An identity system that builds on confirmed <a href="/wiki/Pseudonymity" title="Pseudonymity">pseudonyms</a> can provide privacy and enhance security for digital services and transactions. Cyberspace creates opportunities for identity theft. Exact copies of everything sent over a digital communications channel can be recorded. Thus, cyberspace needs a system that allows individuals to verify their identities to others without revealing to them the digital representation of their identities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anonymous.2Fpseudonymous_attribute_systems">Anonymous/pseudonymous attribute systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Anonymous/pseudonymous attribute systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An anonym is an authenticated attribute that is not linked to an identifier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camp_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Camp-3">[3]</a></sup> An anonymous identifier identifies the person once. If it is used more than once, becomes a pseudonym. A pseudonym is an identifier associated with attributes but with no permanent identifier.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Clare Sullivan presents the grounds for digital identity as an emerging legal concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identity_Cards_Act" title="Identity Cards Act">Identity Cards Act</a> confirms Sullivan's argument and unfolds the new legal concept involving database identity and transaction identity. Database identity refers to the collection of data that is registered about an individual within the databases of the scheme and transaction identity is a set of information that defines the individual's identity for transactional purposes. Although there is reliance on the verification of identity, none of the processes used are entirely trustworthy. The consequences of digital identity abuse and fraud are potentially serious, since in possible implications the person is held legally responsible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_aspects">Business aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Business aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Corporations have begun to recognize the Internet's potential to facilitate the tailoring of the online storefront to each individual customer. Purchase suggestions, personalised adverts and other tailored marketing strategies are a great success to businesses. Such tailoring however, depends on the ability to connect attributes and preferences to the identity of the visitor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
