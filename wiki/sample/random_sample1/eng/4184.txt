<p><b>Digital television</b> (<b>DTV</b>) is the transmission of audio and video by digitally processed and multiplexed signal, in contrast to the totally analog and channel separated signals used by <a href="/wiki/Analog_television" title="Analog television">analog television</a>. Digital TV can support more than one program in the same channel <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is an innovative service that represents the first significant evolution in television technology since <a href="/wiki/Color_television" title="Color television">color television</a> in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Several regions of the world are in different stages of adaptation and are implementing different broadcasting standards. Below are the different widely used digital television broadcasting standards (DTB):</p>
<ul>
<li>Digital Video Broadcasting (<a href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Broadcasting" title="Digital Video Broadcasting">DVB</a>) uses coded orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OFDM" title="OFDM">OFDM</a>) modulation and supports hierarchical transmission. This standard has been adopted in Europe, Australia and New Zealand.</li>
<li>Advanced Television System Committee (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATSC" title="ATSC">ATSC</a>) uses eight-level vestigial sideband (<a href="/wiki/8VSB" title="8VSB">8VSB</a>) for terrestrial broadcasting. This standard has been adopted by six countries: United States, Canada, Mexico, South Korea, Dominican Republic and Honduras.</li>
<li>Integrated Services Digital Broadcasting (<a href="/wiki/ISDB" title="ISDB">ISDB</a>) is a system designed to provide good reception to fixed receivers and also portable or mobile receivers. It utilizes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OFDM" title="OFDM">OFDM</a> and two-dimensional interleaving. It supports hierarchical transmission of up to three layers and uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPEG-2_video" title="MPEG-2 video">MPEG-2 video</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Audio_Coding" title="Advanced Audio Coding">Advanced Audio Coding</a>. This standard has been adopted in Japan and the Philippines. <a href="/wiki/ISDB-T_International" title="ISDB-T International">ISDB-T International</a> is an adaptation of this standard using <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">H.264/MPEG-4 AVC</a> that been adopted in most of South America and is also being embraced by Portuguese-speaking African countries.</li>
<li>Digital Terrestrial Multimedia Broadcasting (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DTMB" title="DTMB">DTMB</a>) adopts time-domain synchronous (TDS) OFDM technology with a pseudo-random signal frame to serve as the guard interval (GI) of the OFDM block and the training symbol. The DTMB standard has been adopted in the People's Republic of China, including Hong Kong and Macau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Digital Multimedia Broadcasting (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Multimedia_Broadcasting" title="Digital Multimedia Broadcasting">DMB</a>) is a digital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_transmission" title="Radio transmission">radio transmission</a> <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> developed in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as part of the national <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a> project for sending multimedia such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV" title="TV">TV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Datacasting" title="Datacasting">datacasting</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mobile_device" title="Mobile device">mobile devices</a> such as mobile phones, laptops and GPS navigation systems.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Digital TV's roots have been tied very closely to the availability of inexpensive, high performance computers. It wasn't until the 1990s that digital TV became a real possibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benton_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-benton-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s as Japanese consumer electronics firms forged ahead with the development of HDTV technology, and as the MUSE analog format proposed by NHK, a Japanese company, was seen as a pacesetter that threatened to eclipse U.S. electronics companies. Until June 1990, the Japanese MUSE standard—based on an analog system—was the front-runner among the more than 23 different technical concepts under consideration. Then, an American company, General Instrument, demonstrated the feasibility of a digital television signal. This breakthrough was of such significance that the FCC was persuaded to delay its decision on an ATV standard until a digitally based standard could be developed.</p>
<p>In March 1990, when it became clear that a digital standard was feasible, the FCC made a number of critical decisions. First, the Commission declared that the new ATV standard must be more than an enhanced <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">analog signal</a>, but be able to provide a genuine HDTV signal with at least twice the resolution of existing television images. Then, to ensure that viewers who did not wish to buy a new digital television set could continue to receive conventional television broadcasts, it dictated that the new ATV standard must be capable of being "simulcast" on different channels. The new ATV standard also allowed the new DTV signal to be based on entirely new design principles. Although incompatible with the existing NTSC standard, the new DTV standard would be able to incorporate many improvements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benton_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-benton-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The final standard adopted by the FCC did not require a single standard for scanning formats, aspect ratios, or lines of resolution. This outcome resulted from a dispute between the consumer electronics industry (joined by some broadcasters) and the computer industry (joined by the film industry and some public interest groups) over which of the two scanning processes—interlaced or progressive—is superior. Interlaced scanning, which is used in televisions worldwide, scans even-numbered lines first, then odd-numbered ones. Progressive scanning, which is the format used in computers, scans lines in sequences, from top to bottom. The computer industry argued that progressive scanning is superior because it does not "flicker" in the manner of interlaced scanning. It also argued that progressive scanning enables easier connections with the Internet, and is more cheaply converted to interlaced formats than vice versa. The film industry also supported progressive scanning because it offers a more efficient means of converting filmed programming into digital formats. For their part, the consumer electronics industry and broadcasters argued that interlaced scanning was the only technology that could transmit the highest quality pictures then (and currently) feasible, i.e., 1,080 lines per picture and 1,920 pixels per line. Broadcasters also favored interlaced scanning because their vast archive of interlaced programming is not readily compatible with a progressive format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benton_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-benton-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_information">Technical information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formats_and_bandwidth">Formats and bandwidth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formats and bandwidth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2487" data-file-width="1995" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg/220px-Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg/330px-Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg/440px-Digital_%26_Analog_TV_screen_quality_comparison-1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Digital television supports many different picture formats defined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_television_system" title="Broadcast television system">broadcast television systems</a> which are a combination of size and <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a> (width to height ratio).</p>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Digital_terrestrial_television" title="Digital terrestrial television">digital terrestrial television</a> (DTT) broadcasting, the range of formats can be broadly divided into two categories: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_definition_television" title="High definition television">high definition television</a> (HDTV) for the transmission of <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">high-definition video</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard-definition_television" title="Standard-definition television">standard-definition television</a> (SDTV). These terms by themselves are not very precise, and many subtle intermediate cases exist.</p>
<p>One of several different HDTV formats that can be transmitted over DTV is: 1280 × 720 <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a> in <a href="/wiki/Progressive_scan" title="Progressive scan">progressive scan</a> mode (abbreviated <i><a href="/wiki/720p" title="720p">720p</a></i>) or 1920 × 1080 pixels in <a href="/wiki/Interlaced_video" title="Interlaced video">interlaced video</a> mode (<i><a href="/wiki/1080i" title="1080i">1080i</a></i>). Each of these uses a <a href="/wiki/16:9" title="16:9">16:9</a> <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a>. (Some televisions are capable of receiving an HD resolution of 1920 × 1080 at a 60 Hz progressive scan frame rate — known as <a href="/wiki/1080p" title="1080p">1080p</a>.) HDTV cannot be transmitted over analog <a href="/wiki/Television_channel" title="Television channel">television channels</a> because of <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel capacity</a> issues.</p>
<p>Standard definition TV (SDTV), by comparison, may use one of several different formats taking the form of various aspect ratios depending on the technology used in the country of broadcast. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4:3" title="4:3">4:3</a> aspect-ratio broadcasts, the 640 × 480 format is used in <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a> countries, while 720 × 576 is used in <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">PAL</a> countries. For <a href="/wiki/16:9" title="16:9">16:9</a> broadcasts, the 720 × 480 format is used in NTSC countries, while 1024 × 576 is used in PAL countries. However, broadcasters may choose to reduce these resolutions to reduce <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">bit rate</a> (e.g., many DVB-T channels in the United Kingdom use a horizontal resolution of 544 or 704 pixels per line).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each <a href="/wiki/Commercial_broadcasting" title="Commercial broadcasting">commercial broadcasting</a> <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">terrestrial television</a> DTV channel in North America is permitted to be broadcast at a <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">bit rate</a> up to 19 <a href="/wiki/Megabit" title="Megabit">megabits</a> per second. However, the broadcaster does not need to use this entire bandwidth for just one broadcast channel. Instead the broadcast can use the channel to include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PSIP" title="PSIP">PSIP</a> and can also subdivide across several video <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subchannel" title="Subchannel">subchannels</a> (a.k.a. feeds) of varying quality and compression rates, including non-video <a href="/wiki/Datacasting" title="Datacasting">datacasting</a> services that allow one-way high-bit-rate streaming of data to computers like <a href="/wiki/National_Datacast" title="National Datacast">National Datacast</a>.</p>
<p>A broadcaster may opt to use a standard-definition (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SDTV" title="SDTV">SDTV</a>) digital signal instead of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a> signal, because current convention allows the bandwidth of a DTV channel (or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplex_(TV)" title="Multiplex (TV)">multiplex</a>") to be subdivided into multiple <a href="/wiki/Digital_subchannel" title="Digital subchannel">digital subchannels</a>, (similar to what most FM <a href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio stations</a> offer with <a href="/wiki/HD_Radio" title="HD Radio">HD Radio</a>), providing multiple feeds of entirely different <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programming</a> on the same channel. This ability to provide either a single HDTV feed or multiple lower-resolution feeds is often referred to as distributing one's "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bit_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bit budget (page does not exist)">bit budget</a>" or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multicasting" title="Multicasting">multicasting</a>. This can sometimes be arranged automatically, using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_multiplexer" title="Statistical multiplexer">statistical multiplexer</a> (or "stat-mux"). With some implementations, image resolution may be less directly limited by bandwidth; for example in <a href="/wiki/DVB-T" title="DVB-T">DVB-T</a>, broadcasters can choose from several different modulation schemes, giving them the option to reduce the transmission <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">bit rate</a> and make reception easier for more distant or mobile viewers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Receiving_digital_signal">Receiving digital signal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Receiving digital signal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several different ways to receive digital television. One of the oldest means of receiving DTV (and TV in general) is from terrestrial transmitters using an <a href="/wiki/Television_antenna" title="Television antenna">antenna</a> (known as an <i>aerial</i> in some countries). This way is known as <a href="/wiki/Digital_terrestrial_television" title="Digital terrestrial television">Digital terrestrial television</a> (DTT). With DTT, viewers are limited to channels that have a terrestrial transmitter in range of their antenna.</p>
<p>Other ways have been devised to receive digital television. Among the most familiar to people are <a href="/wiki/Digital_cable" title="Digital cable">digital cable</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_satellite" title="Digital satellite">digital satellite</a>. In some countries where transmissions of TV signals are normally achieved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwaves" title="Microwaves">microwaves</a>, digital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMDS" title="MMDS">MMDS</a> is used. Other standards, such as <a href="/wiki/Digital_multimedia_broadcasting" title="Digital multimedia broadcasting">Digital multimedia broadcasting</a> (DMB) and <a href="/wiki/DVB-H" title="DVB-H">DVB-H</a>, have been devised to allow handheld devices such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_phones" title="Mobile phones">mobile phones</a> to receive TV signals. Another way is <a href="/wiki/IPTV" title="IPTV">IPTV</a>, that is receiving TV via Internet Protocol, relying on <a href="/wiki/Digital_subscriber_line" title="Digital subscriber line">digital subscriber line</a> (DSL) or optical cable line. Finally, an alternative way is to receive digital TV signals via the open Internet (<a href="/wiki/Internet_television" title="Internet television">Internet television</a>), whether from a central streaming service or a P2P (peer-to-peer) system.</p>
<p>Some signals carry <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a> and specify use conditions (such as "may not be recorded" or "may not be viewed on displays larger than 1 m in diagonal measure") backed up with the force of law under the World Intellectual Property Organization Copyright Treaty (<a href="/wiki/WIPO_Copyright_Treaty" title="WIPO Copyright Treaty">WIPO Copyright Treaty</a>) and national <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a> implementing it, such as the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">Digital Millennium Copyright Act</a>. Access to encrypted channels can be controlled by a removable <a href="/wiki/Smart_card" title="Smart card">smart card</a>, for example via the Common Interface (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DVB-CI" title="DVB-CI">DVB-CI</a>) standard for Europe and via <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Point_Of_Deployment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Point Of Deployment (page does not exist)">Point Of Deployment</a> (POD) for IS or named differently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CableCard" title="CableCard">CableCard</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While poor signal quality on an analog TV is still viewable by the user, digital TV is less forgiving. When noise is introduced analog TV can be either annoying or not even perceptible, while digital TV will experience either annoying tiling or no picture at all.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction">Interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People can interact with a DTV system in various ways. One can, for example, browse the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_program_guide" title="Electronic program guide">electronic program guide</a>. Modern DTV systems sometimes use a return path providing feedback from the end user to the broadcaster. This is possible with a coaxial or fiber optic cable, a dialup modem, or Internet connection but is not possible with a standard antenna.</p>
<p>Some of these systems support <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">video on demand</a> using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_channel" title="Communication channel">communication channel</a> localized to a neighborhood rather than a city (terrestrial) or an even larger area (satellite).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1-segment_broadcasting">1-segment broadcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1-segment broadcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1seg" title="1seg">1seg</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/1seg" title="1seg">1seg</a> (1-segment) is a special form of <a href="/wiki/ISDB" title="ISDB">ISDB</a>. Each channel is further divided into 13 segments. The 12 segments of them are allocated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a> and remaining segment, the 13th, is used for narrow-band receivers such as mobile television or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_phone" title="Cell phone">cell phone</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline_of_transition">Timeline of transition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Timeline of transition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Digital_television_transition" title="Digital television transition">Digital television transition</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_analog_vs_digital">Comparison of analog vs digital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Comparison of analog vs digital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Analog_television" title="Analog television">Analog television</a></div>
<p>DTV has several advantages over analog TV, the most significant being that digital channels take up less bandwidth, and the bandwidth needs are continuously variable, at a corresponding reduction in image quality depending on the level of compression as well as the resolution of the transmitted image. This means that digital broadcasters can provide more digital channels in the same space, provide <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition television</a> service, or provide other non-television services such as multimedia or interactivity. DTV also permits special services such as multiplexing (more than one program on the same channel), electronic program guides and additional languages (spoken or subtitled). The sale of non-television services may provide an additional revenue source.</p>
<p>Digital and analog signals react to interference differently. For example, common problems with analog television include <a href="/wiki/Ghosting_(television)" title="Ghosting (television)">ghosting</a> of images, noise from weak signals, and many other potential problems which degrade the quality of the image and sound, although the program material may still be watchable. With digital television, the audio and video must be synchronized digitally, so reception of the digital signal must be very nearly complete; otherwise, neither audio nor video will be usable. Short of this complete failure, "blocky" video is seen when the digital signal experiences interference.</p>
<p>Analog TV started off with monophonic sound, and later evolved to stereophonic sound with two independent audio signal channels. DTV will allow up to 5 audio signal channels plus a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-woofer" title="Sub-woofer">sub-woofer</a> bass channel, with broadcasts similar in quality to movie theaters and DVDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compression_artifacts_and_allocated_bandwidth">Compression artifacts and allocated bandwidth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Compression artifacts and allocated bandwidth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DTV images have some picture defects that are not present on analog television or motion picture cinema, because of present-day limitations of bit rate and compression algorithms such as <a href="/wiki/MPEG-2" title="MPEG-2">MPEG-2</a>. This defect is sometimes referred to as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosquito_noise" title="Mosquito noise">mosquito noise</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the way the human visual system works, defects in an image that are localized to particular features of the image or that come and go are more perceptible than defects that are uniform and constant. However, the DTV system is designed to take advantage of other limitations of the human visual system to help mask these flaws, e.g. by allowing more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_artifacts" title="Compression artifacts">compression artifacts</a> during fast motion where the eye cannot track and resolve them as easily and, conversely, minimizing artifacts in still backgrounds that may be closely examined in a scene (since time allows).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_poor_reception">Effects of poor reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects of poor reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Changes in signal reception from factors such as degrading antenna connections or changing weather conditions may gradually reduce the quality of analog TV. The nature of digital TV results in a perfectly decodable video initially, until the receiving equipment starts picking up interference that overpowers the desired signal or if the signal is too weak to decode. Some equipment will show a garbled picture with significant damage, while other devices may go directly from perfectly decodable video to no video at all or lock up. This phenomenon is known as the digital <a href="/wiki/Cliff_effect" title="Cliff effect">cliff effect</a>.</p>
<p>For remote locations, distant channels that, as analog signals, were previously usable in a snowy and degraded state may, as digital signals, be perfectly decodable or may become completely unavailable. The use of higher frequencies will add to these problems, especially in cases where a clear line-of-sight from the receiving antenna to the transmitter is not available.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_old_analog_technology">Effect on old analog technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Effect on old analog technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Television sets with only analog tuners cannot decode digital transmissions. When analog broadcasting over the air ceases, users of sets with analog-only tuners may use other sources of programming (e.g. cable, recorded media) or may purchase set-top converter boxes to tune in the digital signals. In the United States, a <a href="/wiki/Coupon-eligible_converter_box" title="Coupon-eligible converter box">government-sponsored coupon</a> was available to offset the cost of an external converter box. Analog switch-off (of full-power stations) took place on December 11, 2006 in The Netherlands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> June 12, 2009 in the United States for full-power stations, and later for Class-A Stations on September 1, 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> July 24, 2011 in Japan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> August 31, 2011 in Canada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> February 13, 2012 in Arab states, May 1, 2012 in Germany, October 24, 2012 in the United Kingdom<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and Ireland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> October 31, 2012 in selected Indian cities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-india-switchover_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-india-switchover-20">[20]</a></sup> and December 10, 2013 in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Completion of analog switch-off is scheduled for December 31, 2017 in the whole of India,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-india-switchover_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-india-switchover-20">[20]</a></sup> December 2018 in Costa Rica and around 2020 for the Philippines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance_of_TV-audio_receivers">Disappearance of TV-audio receivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Disappearance of TV-audio receivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the conversion to digital TV, analog television broadcast audio for TV channels on a separate FM <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_signal" title="Carrier signal">carrier signal</a> from the video signal. This FM audio signal could be heard using standard radios equipped with the appropriate tuning circuits.</p>
<p>However, after the <a href="/wiki/Digital_television_transition" title="Digital television transition">transition of many countries to digital TV</a>, no portable radio manufacturer has yet developed an alternative method for portable radios to play just the audio signal of digital TV channels. (<a href="/wiki/DTV_radio" title="DTV radio">DTV radio</a> is not the same thing.)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The adoption of a broadcast standard incompatible with existing analog receivers has created the problem of large numbers of analog receivers being discarded during <a href="/wiki/Digital_television_transition" title="Digital television transition">digital television transition</a>. One superintendent of Public Works was quoted in 2009 as saying, "Some of the studies I’ve read in the trade magazines say up to a quarter of American households could be throwing a TV out in the next two years following the regulation change".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2009, an estimated 99 million analog TV receivers were sitting unused in homes in the US alone and, while some obsolete receivers are being retrofitted with converters, many more are simply dumped in <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a> where they represent a source of toxic metals such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> as well as lesser amounts of materials such as <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to one campaign group, a <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">CRT</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_monitor" title="Computer monitor">computer monitor</a> or TV contains an average of 8 pounds (3.6 kg) of lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> According to another source, the lead in glass of a CRT varies from 1.08 lb to 11.28 lb, depending on screen size and type, but the lead is in the form of "stable and immobile" lead oxide mixed into the glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It is claimed that the lead can have long-term negative effects on the environment if dumped as landfill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However, the glass envelope can be recycled at suitably equipped facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Other portions of the receiver may be subject to disposal as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazardous_material" title="Hazardous material">hazardous material</a>.</p>
<p>Local restrictions on disposal of these materials vary widely; in some cases <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second-hand_store" title="Second-hand store">second-hand stores</a> have refused to accept working color television receivers for resale due to the increasing costs of disposing of unsold TVs. Those <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrift_store" title="Thrift store">thrift stores</a> which are still accepting donated TVs have reported significant increases in good-condition working used television receivers abandoned by viewers who often expect them not to work after digital transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Michigan in 2009, one recycler estimated that as many as one household in four would dispose of or recycle a TV set in the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The digital television transition, migration to <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition television</a> receivers and the replacement of CRTs with flatscreens are all factors in the increasing number of discarded analog CRT-based television receivers.</p>
