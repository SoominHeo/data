<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Detoxification (alternative medicine)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Detoxification</b> (sometimes called <b>body cleansing</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a> treatment which aims to rid the body of unspecified "toxins" – accumulated substances that proponents claim have undesirable short-term or long-term effects on individual health. Activities commonly associated with detoxification include <a href="/wiki/Dieting" title="Dieting">dieting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a>, consuming exclusively or avoiding specific foods (such as fats, carbohydrates, fruits, vegetables, juices, herbs, or water), <a href="/wiki/Colon_cleansing" title="Colon cleansing">colon cleansing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelation_therapy" title="Chelation therapy">chelation therapy</a>, and the removal of <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(dentistry)" title="Amalgam (dentistry)">dental fillings</a>.</p>
<p>The concept has received criticism from scientists and health organizations for its unsound scientific basis and lack of evidence for the claims they make.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015rev_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-2015rev-1">[1]</a></sup> The "toxins" usually remain undefined, with little to no evidence of toxic accumulation in the patient. The British organisation <a href="/wiki/Sense_About_Science" title="Sense About Science">Sense About Science</a> has described some detox diets and commercial products as "a waste of time and money",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senseaboutscience.org.uk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-senseaboutscience.org.uk-2">[2]</a></sup> while the British Dietetic Association called the idea "nonsense" and a "marketing myth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bda-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suspicions of the inefficacy of purging became widespread by the 1830s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">Biochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a> appeared to support auto-intoxication theory in the 19th century, but by the early twentieth century detoxification-based approaches quickly fell out of favour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2015)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wanjek_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wanjek-6">[6]</a></sup> Even though abandoned by mainstream medicine, the idea has persisted in the popular imagination and amongst <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative-medicine</a> practitioners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst1997_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst1997-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StraightDope_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-StraightDope-9">[9]</a></sup> In recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> notions of body cleansing have undergone something of a resurgence, along with many other alternative medical approaches. Nonetheless, mainstream medicine continues to produce evidence that the field is unscientific and anachronistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst1997_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst1997-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detox_diets">Detox diets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Detox diets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Detox diets are <a href="/wiki/Dieting" title="Dieting">dietary plans</a> that claim to have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detoxifying" title="Detoxifying">detoxifying</a> effects. The general idea suggests that most food contains contaminants: ingredients deemed unnecessary for human life, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavor_enhancer" title="Flavor enhancer">flavor enhancers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">food colorings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservatives</a>. Scientists, dietitians, and doctors, while generally viewing "detox diets" as harmless (unless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutritional_deficiency" title="Nutritional deficiency">nutritional deficiency</a> results), often dispute the value and need of "detox diets", due to lack of supporting factual evidence or coherent rationale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senseaboutscience.org.uk_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-senseaboutscience.org.uk-2">[2]</a></sup> In cases where a person suffers from a disease, belief in the efficacy of a detox diet can result in delay or failure to seek effective treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.smh.com.au_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.smh.com.au-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Detox diets can involve consuming extremely limited sets of foods (only water or juice, for example, a form of <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCJuly08_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCJuly08-11">[11]</a></sup> known as <a href="/wiki/Juice_fasting" title="Juice fasting">juice fasting</a>), eliminating certain foods (such as fats) from the diet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> or eliminating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Processed_food" title="Processed food">processed foods</a> and alleged <a href="/wiki/Irritation" title="Irritation">irritants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Detox diets are often high in <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>. Proponents claim that this causes the body to burn accumulated stored fats, releasing fat-stored "toxins" into the blood, which can then be eliminated through the blood, skin, urine, feces and breath. Proponents claim that things such as an altered body-odor support the notion that detox diets have an effect. The mainstream medical view is that the body has mechanisms to rid itself of toxins, and a <a href="/wiki/Healthy_diet" title="Healthy diet">healthy diet</a> is best for the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebMD_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-WebMD-14">[14]</a></sup> Although a brief fast of a single day is unlikely to cause harm, prolonged fasting (as recommended by certain detox diets) can have dangerous health consequences or can even be fatal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quackwatch_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quackwatch-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colon_cleansing">Colon cleansing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Colon cleansing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Colon_cleansing" title="Colon cleansing">colon cleansing</a></div>
<p>Colon cleansing involves giving an <a href="/wiki/Enema" title="Enema">enema</a> (colonic) containing some salt, and sometimes coffee or <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a> to remove food that, according to proponents,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> remains in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colon</a>, producing nonspecific symptoms and general ill-health. However, the colon usually does not require any help cleaning itself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The practice can be potentially dangerous if incorrectly practised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quackwatch_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Quackwatch-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_metals">Heavy metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heavy metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chelation_therapy" title="Chelation therapy">chelation therapy</a></div>
<p>Practitioners may recommend detoxification as a treatment to address the notion that <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury poisoning</a> arises from consumption of contaminated fish and from <a href="/wiki/Dental_amalgam_controversy" title="Dental amalgam controversy">dental amalgam fillings</a> – <a href="/wiki/Quackwatch" title="Quackwatch">Quackwatch</a> states: "Removing good fillings is not merely a waste of money. In some cases, it results in tooth loss because when fillings are drilled out, some of the surrounding tooth structure will be removed with it.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quackwatch_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Quackwatch-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Detoxification.22_devices">"Detoxification" devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "Detoxification" devices'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Energy_medicine" title="Energy medicine">Energy medicine</a></div>
<p>Certain devices are promoted to allegedly remove toxins from the body. One version involves a foot-bath using a mild electric current, while another involves small adhesive pads applied to the skin (usually the foot). In both cases, the production of an alleged brown "toxin" appears after a brief delay. In the case of the foot bath, the "toxin" is actually small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rusted</a> iron leaching from the electrodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldacre_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldacre-17">[17]</a></sup> The adhesive pads change color due to <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> of the pads' ingredients in response to the skin's moisture. In both cases, the same color-changes occur irrespective of whether the water or patch even make contact with the skin (they merely require water—thus proving the color-change does not result from any body-detoxification process).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quackwatch_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Quackwatch-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unsound_scientific_basis">Unsound scientific basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Detoxification_(alternative_medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Unsound scientific basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2015 review of clinical evidence about detox diets concluded: "At present, there is no compelling evidence to support the use of detox diets for weight management or toxin elimination. Considering the financial costs to consumers, unsubstantiated claims and potential health risks of detox products, they should be discouraged by health professionals and subject to independent regulatory review and monitoring."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015rev_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-2015rev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Detoxification and body cleansing products and diets have been criticized for their unsound scientific basis, in particular their premise of nonexistent "toxins" and their appropriation of the legitimate medical concept of <a href="/wiki/Detoxification" title="Detoxification">detoxification</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Mayo_Clinic" title="Mayo Clinic">Mayo Clinic</a>, the "toxins" typically remain unspecified and there is little to no evidence of toxic accumulation in patients treated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayo_Clinic_Website_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayo_Clinic_Website-18">[18]</a></sup> According to a <a href="/wiki/British_Dietetic_Association" title="British Dietetic Association">British Dietetic Association</a> (BDA) Fact Sheet, "The whole idea of detox is nonsense. The body is a well-developed system that has its own builtin mechanisms to detoxify and remove waste and toxins."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bda-3">[3]</a></sup> It went on to characterize the idea as a "marketing myth",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bda-3">[3]</a></sup> while other critics have called the idea a "scam"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sbm_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-sbm-19">[19]</a></sup> and a "hoax".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quackwatch_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-quackwatch-20">[20]</a></sup> The organization <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sense_about_Science" title="Sense about Science">Sense about Science</a> investigated "detox" products, calling them a waste of time and money".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senseaboutscience.org.uk_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-senseaboutscience.org.uk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>resulting in a report that concluded the term is used differently by different companies, most offered no evidence to support their claims, and in most cases its use was the simple renaming of "mundane things, like cleaning or brushing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senseaboutscience.org.uk_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-senseaboutscience.org.uk-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The human body is naturally capable of maintaining itself, with several organs dedicated to cleansing the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Alan Boobis, a professor and toxicologist at <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a>, states:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The body’s own detoxification systems are remarkably sophisticated and versatile. They have to be, as the natural environment that we evolved in is hostile. It is remarkable that people are prepared to risk seriously disrupting these systems with unproven ‘detox’ diets, which could well do more harm than good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senseaboutscience.org.uk_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-senseaboutscience.org.uk-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Skeptical_movement" title="Skeptical movement">Scientific skeptic</a> author <a href="/wiki/Brian_Dunning_(author)" title="Brian Dunning (author)">Brian Dunning</a> investigated the subject in 2008 and concluded that "Anyone interested in detoxifying their body might think about paying a little more attention to their body and less attention to the people trying to get their money... Why is it that so many people are more comfortable self-medicating for conditions that exist only in advertisements, than they are simply taking their doctor's advice? It's because doctors are burdened with the need to actually practice medicine. They won't hide bad news from you or make up easy answers to please you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite unsound scientific basis, detoxification is popular, and detoxification products and regimes have become a profitable health trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015rev_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-2015rev-1">[1]</a></sup> As with some other alternative medicine treatments, efficacy has been attributed to <a href="/wiki/Astroturfing" title="Astroturfing">astroturfing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Placebo" title="Placebo">placebo</a> effect, <a href="/wiki/Psychosomatic_medicine" title="Psychosomatic medicine">psychosomatic</a> improvements, or natural recovery from illness that would have occurred without use of the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
