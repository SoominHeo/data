<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Deep South</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Deep South</b> is a cultural and geographic subregion in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a>. Historically, it is differentiated from the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_South" title="Upper South">Upper South</a>" as being the states most dependent on <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a>-type agriculture and considered slave societies during the pre–<a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> period. The Deep South was also commonly referred to as the <b>Lower South</b> or the <b>Cotton States,</b> for their production of cotton as the primary commodity crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the out-migrations from the Deep South during the first half of the 20th century, African-descended peoples, whose ancestry also often included the British Isles, comprised the majority of the population in numerous counties and some states. Today, the Deep South is usually delineated as being those states and areas where the cultural elements most often thought of as "Southern" exist in their most concentrated form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_belt_counties.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="983" data-file-width="1513" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Black_belt_counties.png/275px-Black_belt_counties.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Black_belt_counties.png/413px-Black_belt_counties.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Black_belt_counties.png/550px-Black_belt_counties.png 2x" width="275"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_belt_counties.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The location of the Black Belt (sociological sense) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The term "Deep South" is defined in a variety of ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>Most definitions include the states <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedict_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-freedict-4">[4]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> is sometimes included<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or else considered "in the Peripheral or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_South" title="Upper South">Rim South</a> rather than the Deep South."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The seven states that seceded from the United States before the firing on <a href="/wiki/Fort_Sumter" title="Fort Sumter">Fort Sumter</a> and the start of the American Civil War, and were the first to form the Confederate States of America. Ultimately the Confederacy included eleven states. In order of secession they are: South Carolina, Mississippi, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. The first six states to secede were those that held the largest number of slaves.</li>
<li>A large part of the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotton_Belt_(region)" title="Cotton Belt (region)">"Cotton Belt"</a>. This was considered to extend from eastern <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> to South Carolina and through the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf States</a> as far west as <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">East Texas</a>, and including those parts of western <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> and eastern Arkansas in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_embayment" title="Mississippi embayment">Mississippi embayment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup> Some of this is coterminous with the <a href="/wiki/Black_Belt_(U.S._region)" title="Black Belt (U.S. region)">Black Belt</a>, originally referring to upland areas of Alabama and Mississippi with fertile soil, which were developed for cotton under slave labor. The term came to be used for much of the Cotton Belt, which had a high percentage of African-American slave labor.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though often used in history books to refer to the seven states that originally formed the Confederacy, the term "Deep South" did not come into general usage until long after the Civil War ended. Up until that time, "Lower South" was the primary designation for those states. When "Deep South" first began to gain mainstream currency in print in the middle of the 20th century, it applied to the states and areas of Georgia, southern Alabama, Florida, Mississippi, north Louisiana, and East Texas, all historic areas of cotton plantations and slavery. This was the part of the South many considered the "most Southern".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (November 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Later, the general definition expanded to include all of South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, and Louisiana, and often taking in bordering areas of <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">East Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North Florida</a>. In its broadest application today, the Deep South is considered to be "an area roughly coextensive with the old cotton belt from eastern <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> through South Carolina west into East Texas, with extensions north and south along the Mississippi".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> is the largest city of the Deep South region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moskin2004_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-moskin2004-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtcom02_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-jtcom02-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="593" data-file-width="800" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg/400px-Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg/600px-Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg/800px-Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Census-2000-Data-Top-US-Ancestries-by-County.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
2000 <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">Census</a> Population Ancestry Map, with African-American ancestry in purple.</div>

<p>European Americans or whites in every part of the Deep South except for southern Louisiana have often identified as having predominately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_American" title="English American">English</a> ancestry, but numerous <a href="/wiki/Irish_Americans" title="Irish Americans">Irish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_Americans" title="Scotch-Irish Americans">Scots-Irish</a> immigrants and migrants also entered the region. They sometimes served as overseers on plantations. In the 1980 census, of those people who identified solely by one European national ancestry, most European Americans identified as being of English ancestry in every Southern state except Louisiana, where more people identified as having French ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>With regards to people in the Deep South who reported only a single European-American ancestry group in 1980, the census showed the following self-identification in each state in this region:</p>
<ul>
<li>Alabama - 857,864 persons out of a total of 2,165,653 people in the state identified as "English," making them 41% of the state and the largest national ancestry group at the time by a wide margin.</li>
<li>Georgia - 1,132,184 out of 3,009,484 people identified as "English," making them 37.62% of the state's total.</li>
<li>Mississippi - 496,481 people out of 1,551,364 people identified as "English," making them 32.00% of the total, the largest national group by a wide margin.</li>
<li>Florida - 1,132,033 people out of 5,159,967 identified "English" as their only ancestry group, making them 21.94% of the total.</li>
<li>Louisiana - 440,558 people out of 2,319,259 people identified only as "English," making them 19.00% of the total people and the second-largest ancestry group in the state at the time. Those who wrote only "French" were 480,711 people out of 2,319,259 people, or 20.73% of the total state population.</li>
<li>Texas - 1,639,322 people identified as "English" only out of a total of 7,859,393 people, making them 20.86% of the total people in the state and the largest ancestry group by a large margin.</li>
</ul>
<p>These figures to do not take into account people who identified as "English" and another ancestry group. When the two were added together, people who self identified as being of English with other ancestry, made up an even larger portion of southerners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> South Carolina was settled earlier than those states commonly classified as the Deep South. Its population in 1980 included 578,338 people out of 1,706,966 people in the state who identified as "English" only, making them 33.88% of the total population, the largest national ancestry group by a large margin.</p>
<p>The map to the right was prepared by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Census_Bureau" title="Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a> from the <a href="/wiki/2000_United_States_Census" title="2000 United States Census">2000 census</a>; it shows the predominant ancestry in each county as self-identified by residents themselves. Note: The Census said that areas with the largest "American"-identified ancestry populations were mostly settled by descendants of colonial English and others from the British Isles, French, Germans and later Italians. Those who are African-descended tended to identify as African American, although many of historically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-race" title="Mixed-race">mixed-race</a> families also have ancestors of British Isles or Northern European ancestry.</p>
<p>As of 2003<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Deep_South&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the majority of African-descended Americans in the South live in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Belt_(U.S._region)" title="Black Belt (U.S. region)">Black Belt</a> counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the 1870s to the early 1960s, conservative whites of the Deep South held control of state governments and overwhelmingly identified as and supported the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_Democratic_Party" title="History of the United States Democratic Party">Democratic Party</a>, Even former Whigs belonged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The most powerful leaders belonged to the party's moderate-to-conservative wing. At the same time, numerous <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_Republican_Party" title="History of the United States Republican Party">Republicans</a>, primarily African Americans, continued to be elected in local offices through the 1880s and into the 1890s. The Republicans also controlled many mountain districts on the fringe of the Deep South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the turn of the 20th century, all of the Southern states, starting with Mississippi in 1890, passed new constitutions and other laws that effectively <a href="/wiki/Disenfranchisement_after_the_Reconstruction_Era" title="Disenfranchisement after the Reconstruction Era">disenfranchised</a> the great majority of blacks and sometimes many poor whites as well. Blacks were excluded them from the political system entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The white Democratic-dominated state legislatures passed laws to impose <a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">white supremacy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Crow" title="Jim Crow">Jim Crow</a>, including racial segregation of public facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The region became known in politics as "<a href="/wiki/Solid_South" title="Solid South">Solid South</a>" for decades: while this disenfranchisement was enforced, all of the states in this region were one-party states dominated by white <a href="/wiki/Southern_Democrats" title="Southern Democrats">Southern Democrats</a>. Its representatives accrued outsized power in the Congress and the national Democratic Party, as they controlled all the seats apportioned to southern states based on total population but represented only white voters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valelly146-147_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-valelly146-147-20">[20]</a></sup> During this same period, the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lynchings_in_the_United_States" title="Lynchings in the United States">lynchings</a> of blacks by whites reached a peak in the region; the most deaths annually were in the years shortly before the turn of the century, when economic problems and stress were high in the region.</p>
<p>Major demographic changes ensued in the 20th century; during the two waves of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a>, a total of 6 million African Americans left the South for opportunities in the North, Midwest and West Coast. In some areas, white migration increased into the South, especially since the late 20th century. Beginning with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1964" title="United States presidential election, 1964">Goldwater–Johnson election</a> of 1964, a significant contingent of white conservative voters in the Deep South stopped supporting national Democratic Party candidates and switched to Republicans. They still voted for many Democrats at the state and local level into the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Republican Party in the South had been crippled by the disenfranchisement of blacks, and the national party was unable to relieve their injustices in the South. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and the administration of Democrat <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Deal_Coalition" title="New Deal Coalition">New Deal</a> measures aided African Americans across the country and in the poor rural South, as well as poor whites. In the post-World War II era, Democratic Party presidents and national politicians began to support desegregation and other elements of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, from President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a>'s desegregating the military, to <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>'s support for non-violent protests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> These efforts culminated in <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>'s important work in gaining Congressional approval for the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Act_of_1964" title="Civil Rights Act of 1964">Civil Rights Act of 1964</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voting_Rights_Act_of_1965" title="Voting Rights Act of 1965">Voting Rights Act of 1965</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Since then, upwards of 90% of African Americans in the South and the rest of the nation have voted for the Democratic Party, including .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> They voted 93% for Obama in 2012 and 88% for Clinton in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>White southern voters consistently voted for the Democratic Party for many years, in order to hold onto Jim Crow Laws. "The weird thing about Jim Crow politics is that white southerners with conservative views on taxes, moral values, and national security would vote for Democratic presidential candidates who didn't share their views. They did that as part of a strategy for maintaining white supremacy in the South." (Yglesias 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> When the leaders of the Democratic party started to leave office, they were replaced with people who did not share the views of their voters.</p>
<p>One opinion piece attributed the political and cultural changes, along with the easing of racial tensions, as the reason why southern voters began to vote for Republican national candidates, in line with their political ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Since then, white Southern voters have voted for Republican candidates in every presidential election except in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1976" title="U.S. presidential election, 1976">1976 election</a> when Georgia native <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> received the Democratic nomination, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1980" title="U.S. presidential election, 1980">1980 election</a> when Carter won Georgia, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1992" title="United States presidential election, 1992">1992 election</a> when Arkansas native and former governor <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> won both Georgia and Louisiana, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1996" title="United States presidential election, 1996">1996 election</a> when the incumbent president Clinton again won Louisiana. In 1995, Georgia Republican <a href="/wiki/Newt_Gingrich" title="Newt Gingrich">Newt Gingrich</a> was elected by representatives of a Republican-dominated House as <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House</a>.</p>
<p>Since the 1990s the white majority has continued to shift toward Republican candidates at the state and local levels. This trend culminated in 2014, when the Republicans swept every statewide office in the region <a href="/wiki/United_States_elections,_2014" title="United States elections, 2014">midterm elections</a>. As a result, the Republican party came to control all the state legislatures in the region, as well as all House seats that were not representing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majority-minority_districts" title="Majority-minority districts">majority-minority districts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Continuing demographic changes are likely to influence regional politics: many educated African Americans are migrating back to the South, and taking professional and middle-class jobs; there have been waves of immigration from Latino countries of Mexico, and Central and South America; and white northerners have also moved to the South for jobs or to retire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Your explanation here (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Presidential elections in which the Deep South diverged noticeably from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_South" title="Upper South">Upper South</a> occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1928" title="U.S. presidential election, 1928">1928</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1948" title="U.S. presidential election, 1948">1948</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1964" title="U.S. presidential election, 1964">1964</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1968" title="U.S. presidential election, 1968">1968</a>, and, to a lesser extent, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1952" title="U.S. presidential election, 1952">1952</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1956" title="U.S. presidential election, 1956">1956</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1992" title="United States presidential election, 1992">1992</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_2008" title="U.S. presidential election, 2008">2008</a>. Former Arkansas Governor <a href="/wiki/Mike_Huckabee" title="Mike Huckabee">Mike Huckabee</a> fared well in the Deep South in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_Republican_primaries" title="2008 Republican primaries">2008 Republican primaries</a>, losing only one state (South Carolina) while running (he had dropped out of the race before the Mississippi primary).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (November 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
