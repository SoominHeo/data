<p><b>Ronald Gordon King-Smith</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">OBE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Education" title="Master of Education">Hon.MEd</a> (27 March 1922 – 4 January 2011),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-1">[1]</a></sup> was a prolific English writer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_books" title="Children's books">children's books</a>, primarily using the pen name <b>Dick King-Smith</b>. He is best known for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sheep-Pig" title="The Sheep-Pig">The Sheep-Pig</a></i> (1983), or <i>Babe the Gallant Pig</i> in the US. It was <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">adapted</a> as the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Babe_(film)" title="Babe (film)">Babe</a></i> (1995) and translations have been published in fifteen languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was awarded an Honorary Master of Education degree by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_West_of_England" title="University of the West of England">University of the West of England</a> in 1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">Officer of the Order of the British Empire</a> (OBE) in the 2010 New Year Honours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Dick and Myrle had three children – two daughters (Juliet and Lizzie) and one son (Giles).</p>
<p>Dick King-Smith’s first book, <i>The Fox Busters</i>, was published in 1978.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dick_King-Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dick King-Smith grew up in the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a>, where his family ran several <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mills</a>, and was educated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaudesert_Park_School" title="Beaudesert Park School">Beaudesert Park School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_College" title="Marlborough College">Marlborough College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-1">[1]</a></sup> He was a soldier in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, serving with the <a href="/wiki/Grenadier_Guards" title="Grenadier Guards">Grenadier Guards</a> in Italy and a farmer for 20 years before he became a teacher at <a href="/wiki/Farmborough" title="Farmborough">Farmborough</a> Primary School and author. King-Smith's first book was <i>The Fox Busters</i>, published in 1978 while he was living and teaching in <a href="/wiki/Farmborough" title="Farmborough">Farmborough</a>. He was one of Britain's most prolific authors and wrote over a hundred books. In later life he lived in <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlton" title="Queen Charlton">Queen Charlton</a>, a small farming village near <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, contributing to the maintenance and conservation of the local area as the vice-president of the <a href="/wiki/Avon_Wildlife_Trust" title="Avon Wildlife Trust">Avon Wildlife Trust</a>. He married his first wife, Myrle, in 1943. They had three children Juliet, Giles and Liz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-1">[1]</a></sup> Myrle died in 2000, and King-Smith subsequently married Zona Bedding, a family friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He presented a feature on animals on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV-AM" title="TV-AM">TV-AM</a>'s children's programme <i><a href="/wiki/Rub-a-Dub-Dub_(TV_series)" title="Rub-a-Dub-Dub (TV series)">Rub a Dub Dub</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and also appeared regularly (in a similar capacity) on the similarly named Saturday morning TV series Rub-a-Dub Tub (1983).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>King-Smith died at his home near <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath, Somerset</a>, on 4 January 2011 at the age of 88. He was survived by three children, 14 grandchildren, four great-grandchildren and one great-great grandchild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dick_King-Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King-Smith and <i>The Sheep-pig</i> won the 1984 <a href="/wiki/Guardian_Children%27s_Fiction_Prize" title="Guardian Children's Fiction Prize">Guardian Children's Fiction Prize</a>, a once-in-a-lifetime book award judged by a panel of British children writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relaunch_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-relaunch-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
