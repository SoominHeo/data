<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dilophosauridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dilophosaurs were large bipedal predators with lengths of 4 to 7 metres and estimated adult weights of 300 to 500 kg. They are well known for their distinctive head crests, which were probably used for mating displays, or to intimidate rivals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dilophosaurs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dilophosaurs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dilophosauridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The family Dilophosauridae was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Alan_J._Charig" title="Alan J. Charig">Alan Charig</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_C._Milner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrew C. Milner (page does not exist)">Andrew Milner</a> in 1990 to contain only the <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type genus</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dilophosaurus" title="Dilophosaurus">Dilophosaurus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charig.26milner1990_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-charig.26milner1990-3">[3]</a></sup> Other genera, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Zupaysaurus" title="Zupaysaurus">Zupaysaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dracovenator" title="Dracovenator">Dracovenator</a></i>, have since been assigned to this family, though the group has never been given a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> definition and is not currently a clade. Some studies have suggested that there was a natural group of medium-sized crested <a href="/wiki/Theropoda" title="Theropoda">theropods</a> which included <i>Dilophosaurus</i> as well as <i>Dracovenator</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cryolophosaurus" title="Cryolophosaurus">Cryolophosaurus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sinosaurus" title="Sinosaurus">Sinosaurus</a></i>, though it has not been formally named Dilophosauridae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithetal2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-smithetal2007-4">[4]</a></sup> While traditionally assigned to the superfamily <a href="/wiki/Coelophysoidea" title="Coelophysoidea">Coelophysoidea</a>, these analyses suggest that dilophosaurids may have been more closely related to the group <a href="/wiki/Tetanurae" title="Tetanurae">Tetanurae</a>, comprising the more advanced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megalosaurs" title="Megalosaurs">megalosaurs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnosaur" title="Carnosaur">carnosaurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coelurosauria" title="Coelurosauria">coelurosaurs</a>.</p>
<p>The following <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a> outlines the relationships of <i>Dilophosaurus</i> and its close relatives as recovered by the 2007 analysis of Smith, Makovicky, Pol, Hammer, and Currie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithetal2007_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-smithetal2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dilophosauridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All dilophosaurids are known for large, distinctive, head crests. These crests have been thought to have had many different uses, including being used for display, to attract mates, or intimidate rivals. It has been suggested that the crests symbolized <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>, but this interpretation of the crests is controversial. Another distinctive cranial feature is a notch between the premaxilla and maxilla, giving dilophosaurids a crocodile-like appearance. This makes some people, for example <a href="/wiki/David_B._Norman" title="David B. Norman">David B. Norman</a>, suspect that their front teeth were too weak to bring down prey, and that they were scavengers. Tracks found in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> might show that dilophosaurids could swim, implying that they fed on fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xing12_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-xing12-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
