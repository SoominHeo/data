<p><b>Dingwall</b> (<a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>: <span lang="sco" xml:lang="sco"><i>Dingwal</i></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd"><i>Inbhir Pheofharain</i></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a town and former <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burgh</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Highland_(council_area)" title="Highland (council area)">Highland council area</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It has a population of 5,491. It was formerly an east-coast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harbour" title="Harbour">harbour</a> but now lies inland. <a href="/wiki/Dingwall_Castle" title="Dingwall Castle">Dingwall Castle</a> was once the biggest <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a>. On the town's present-day outskirts lies <a href="/wiki/Tulloch_Castle" title="Tulloch Castle">Tulloch Castle</a>, parts of which may date back to the 12th-century building. In 1411 the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dingwall" title="Battle of Dingwall">Battle of Dingwall</a> is said to have taken place between the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Mackay" title="Clan Mackay">Clan Mackay</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Donald" title="Clan Donald">Clan Donald</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dingwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its name, derived from the Scandinavian <i>Þingvöllr</i> (field or meeting-place of the <i><a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">thing</a></i>, or local assembly; compare <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Tingwall_(disambiguation)" title="Tingwall (disambiguation)">Tingwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thingwall" title="Thingwall">Thingwall</a> in the British Isles alone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> plus many others across northern Europe), preserves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> connections of the town; Gaels call it <i>Inbhir Pheofharain</i> (<small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA for Scottish Gaelic">[iɲiɾʲˈfjɔhəɾaiɲ]</a></span>), meaning "the mouth of the Peffery" or <i>Baile Chàil</i> meaning "cabbage town".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site of the <i><a class="external text" href="http://dingwallcc.org.uk/dingwall-thing/4589085522?preview=Y" rel="nofollow">Þingvöllr</a></i>, and of the medieval Moothill, lies beneath the Cromartie memorial.</p>
<p>Dingwall formerly served as the county town of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counties_of_Scotland" title="Counties of Scotland">county</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ross_and_Cromarty" title="Ross and Cromarty">Ross and Cromarty</a>. It lies near the head of the <a href="/wiki/Cromarty_Firth" title="Cromarty Firth">Cromarty Firth</a> where the valley of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peffery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peffery (page does not exist)">Peffery</a> unites with the alluvial lands at the mouth of the Conon, 14 miles (23 km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a>.</p>

<p>In the early Middle Ages Dingwall was reputed to have the largest castle north of Stirling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>King <a href="/wiki/Alexander_II_of_Scotland" title="Alexander II of Scotland">Alexander II</a> created Dingwall a <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burgh</a> in 1226, and <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV</a> renewed its charter. On the top of <a href="/wiki/Knockfarrel" title="Knockfarrel">Knockfarrel</a> (Gaelic: Cnoc Fhearghalaigh), a hill about three miles (5 km) to the west, stands a large and very complete <a href="/wiki/Vitrified_fort" title="Vitrified fort">vitrified fort</a> with ramparts.</p>
<p>The 18th-century town house, and some remains of the ancient mansion of the once powerful <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Ross" title="Earl of Ross">earls of Ross</a>, still exist. An <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisk</a>, 51 feet (16 m) high, was erected over the grave of <a href="/wiki/George_Mackenzie,_1st_Earl_of_Cromartie" title="George Mackenzie, 1st Earl of Cromartie">Sir George Mackenzie</a>, 1st <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Cromartie" title="Earl of Cromartie">Earl of Cromartie</a>, near the parish church of St Clement. It was affected by subsidence, becoming known as the "Leaning Tower", and was replaced by a much smaller replica in the early years of the 20th century. However even this is now marked by signs saying "Keep Out" on the grounds that it is a dangerous structure.</p>
<p>Dingwall suffered widespread flooding during storms in late October 2006, during which the weather cut off much of the Highlands north of Inverness, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A9_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A9 road (Great Britain)">A9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Far_North_Line" title="Far North Line">Far North Line</a> for a significant period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dingwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dingwall is the home of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> team <a href="/wiki/Ross_County_F.C." title="Ross County F.C.">Ross County</a>, who won promotion to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Premier_League" title="Scottish Premier League">Scottish Premier League</a> in 2012 and finished the 2012/13 season in fifth place. Despite the town's small population, Ross County attract sizeable crowds to <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Park,_Dingwall" title="Victoria Park, Dingwall">Victoria Park</a> thereby maintaining the UK's most northerly full-time squad. The team reached the <a href="/wiki/2010_Scottish_Cup_Final" title="2010 Scottish Cup Final">2010 Scottish Cup Final</a>, having knocked out <a href="/wiki/Celtic_F.C." title="Celtic F.C.">Celtic</a> in the previous round. Over 17,000 <i>Staggies</i> fans travelled to the match. Sunday 13 March 2016 Ross County won the Scottish League Cup in the final match against Hibernian by 1–2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amenities">Amenities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dingwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amenities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dingwall_railway_station" title="Dingwall railway station">Dingwall railway station</a> has lain on what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Far_North_Line" title="Far North Line">Far North Line</a> since circa 1865. It also serves the <a href="/wiki/Kyle_of_Lochalsh_Line" title="Kyle of Lochalsh Line">Kyle of Lochalsh Line</a>, with the junction between the two lines being located within the town. The station is served with around 26 trains a day, 14 of which go to Inverness. The town contains the shortest and most northerly canal in the UK, the <a href="/wiki/Dingwall_Canal" title="Dingwall Canal">Dingwall Canal</a>.</p>
<p>The majority of the High Street is pedestrianised. On the outskirts of Dingwall a new mini shopping mall, named DingMall, opened in November 2012.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Highland_Theological_College" title="Highland Theological College">Highland Theological College</a> is located within the town. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Highlands_and_Islands" title="University of the Highlands and Islands">University of the Highlands and Islands</a> and has been approved by the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a>, the United Free Church and other denominations as a training provider for those entering ministry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_burgh">Parliamentary burgh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dingwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parliamentary burgh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Dingwall was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentary_burgh" title="Parliamentary burgh">parliamentary burgh</a>, combined with <a href="/wiki/Dornoch" title="Dornoch">Dornoch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirkwall" title="Kirkwall">Kirkwall</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tain,_Scotland" title="Tain, Scotland">Tain</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wick_(Caithness)" title="Wick (Caithness)">Wick</a> in the Northern Burghs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constituency" title="Constituency">constituency</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">Parliament of Great Britain</a> from 1708 to 1801 and of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> from 1801 to 1918. <a href="/wiki/Cromarty" title="Cromarty">Cromarty</a> was added to the list in 1832.</p>
<p>The constituency was a <a href="/wiki/District_of_burghs" title="District of burghs">district of burghs</a> known also as <a href="/wiki/Tain_Burghs_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Tain Burghs (UK Parliament constituency)">Tain Burghs</a> until 1832, and then as <a href="/wiki/Wick_Burghs_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Wick Burghs (UK Parliament constituency)">Wick Burghs</a>. It was represented by one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a>. In 1918 the constituency was abolished and the Dingwall component was merged into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_constituency" title="County constituency">county constituency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ross_and_Cromarty_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Ross and Cromarty (UK Parliament constituency)">Ross and Cromarty</a>.</p>
