<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Signed zero</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Signed zero</b> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero" title="Zero">zero</a> with an associated <a href="/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs" title="Plus and minus signs">sign</a>. In ordinary arithmetic, the number 0 does not have a sign, so that −0, +0 and 0 are identical. However, in <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, some number representations allow for the existence of two zeros, often denoted by <b>−0</b> (<b>negative zero</b>) and <b>+0</b> (<b>positive zero</b>), regarded as equal by the numerical comparison operations but with possible different behaviors in particular operations. This occurs in the <i>sign and magnitude</i> and <i>ones' complement</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signed_number_representation" title="Signed number representation">signed number representations</a> for integers, and in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating-point_number" title="Floating-point number">floating-point number</a> representations. The number 0 is usually encoded as +0, but can be represented by either +0 or −0.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_754" title="IEEE 754">IEEE 754</a> standard for floating-point arithmetic (presently used by most computers and programming languages that support floating point numbers) requires both +0 and −0. Real arithmetic with signed zeros can be considered a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Extended_real_number_line" title="Extended real number line">extended real number line</a> such that 1/−0 = −<a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">∞</a> and 1/+0 = +∞; <a href="/wiki/Division_(mathematics)" title="Division (mathematics)">division</a> is only <a href="/wiki/Indeterminate_form" title="Indeterminate form">undefined</a> for <a href="/wiki/Plus-minus_sign" title="Plus-minus sign">±</a>0/±0 and ±∞/±∞.</p>
<p>Negatively signed zero echoes the <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">mathematical analysis</a> concept of approaching 0 from below as a <a href="/wiki/One-sided_limit" title="One-sided limit">one-sided limit</a>, which may be denoted by <i>x</i> → 0<sup>−</sup>, <i>x</i> → 0−, or <i>x</i> → ↑0. The notation "−0" may be used informally to denote a small negative number that has been <a href="/wiki/Rounding" title="Rounding">rounded</a> to zero. The concept of negative zero also has some theoretical applications in <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a> and other disciplines.</p>
<p>It is claimed that the inclusion of signed zero in IEEE 754 makes it much easier to achieve numerical accuracy in some critical problems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in particular when computing with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_numbers" title="Complex numbers">complex</a> elementary functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On the other hand, the concept of signed zero runs contrary to the general assumption made in most mathematical fields that negative zero is the same thing as zero. Representations that allow negative zero can be a source of errors in programs, if software developers do not take into account that while the two zero representations behave as equal under numeric comparisons, they yield different results in some operations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representations">Representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signed_zero&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The widely used <a href="/wiki/Two%27s_complement" title="Two's complement">two's complement</a> encoding does not allow a negative zero. In a 1+7-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signed_magnitude" title="Signed magnitude">sign-and-magnitude</a> representation for integers, negative zero is represented by the bit string <span style="white-space:nowrap">1000<span style="margin-left:0.25em">0000</span></span>. In an 8-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One%27s_complement" title="One's complement">one's complement</a> representation, negative zero is represented by the bit string <span style="white-space:nowrap">1111<span style="margin-left:0.25em">1111</span></span>. In all three encodings, positive zero is represented by <span style="white-space:nowrap">0000<span style="margin-left:0.25em">0000</span></span>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="125" data-file-width="618" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg/400px-IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg/600px-IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg/800px-IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IEEE_754_Single_Negative_Zero.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Negative zero by IEEE 754 representation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary32" title="Binary32">binary32</a></div>

<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_floating-point_standard" title="IEEE floating-point standard">IEEE 754</a> binary floating point numbers, zero values are represented by the biased exponent and <a href="/wiki/Significand" title="Significand">significand</a> both being zero. Negative zero has the sign bit set to one. One may obtain negative zero as the result of certain computations, for instance as the result of <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_underflow" title="Arithmetic underflow">arithmetic underflow</a> on a negative number, or <code>−1.0×0.0</code>, or simply as <code>−0.0</code>.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_754-2008" title="IEEE 754-2008">IEEE 754</a> decimal floating point encoding, a negative zero is represented by an exponent being any valid exponent in the range for the encoding, the true significand being zero, and the sign bit being one.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_handling">Properties and handling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signed_zero&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties and handling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IEEE 754 floating point standard specifies the behavior of positive zero and negative zero under various operations. The outcome may depend on the current <a href="/wiki/Floating_point#Rounding_modes" title="Floating point">IEEE rounding mode</a> settings.</p>

<p>Division of a non-zero number by zero sets the divide by zero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_(computing)" title="Flag (computing)">flag</a>, and an operation producing a NaN sets the invalid operation flag. An <a href="/wiki/Exception_handling" title="Exception handling">exception handler</a> is called if enabled for the corresponding flag.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons">Comparisons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signed_zero&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparisons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the IEEE 754 standard, negative zero and positive zero should compare as equal with the usual (numerical) comparison operators, like the <code>==</code> operators of <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_programming_language" title="Java programming language">Java</a>. In those languages, special programming tricks may be needed to distinguish the two values:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Type_punning" title="Type punning">Type punning</a> the number to an integer type, so as to look at the sign bit in the bit pattern;</li>
<li>using the ISO C <code>copysign()</code> function (IEEE 754 copySign operation) to copy the sign of the zero to some non-zero number;</li>
<li>using the ISO C <code>signbit()</code> macro (IEEE 754 isSignMinus operation) that returns whether the sign bit of a number is set;</li>
<li>taking the reciprocal of the zero to obtain either 1/(+0) = +∞ or 1/(−0) = −∞ (if the <a href="/wiki/Division_by_zero" title="Division by zero">division by zero</a> exception is not trapped).</li>
</ul>
<p>Note: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cast_(computer_programming)" title="Cast (computer programming)">Casting</a> to integral type will not always work, especially on two's complement systems.</p>
<p>However, some programming languages may provide alternative comparison operators that do distinguish the two zeros. This is the case, for example, of the <tt>equals</tt> method in Java's <code>Double</code> wrapper class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_uses">Scientific uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signed_zero&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientific uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Informally, one may use the notation "−0" for a negative value that was rounded to zero. This notation may be useful when a negative sign is significant; for example, when tabulating <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> temperatures, where a negative sign means <i>below freezing</i>.</p>
<p>In statistical mechanics, one sometimes uses <a href="/wiki/Negative_temperature" title="Negative temperature">negative temperatures</a> to describe systems with <a href="/wiki/Population_inversion" title="Population inversion">population inversion</a>, which can be considered to have a temperature greater than positive infinity, because the coefficient of energy in the population distribution function is −1/Temperature. In this context, a temperature of −0 is a (theoretical) temperature larger than any other negative temperature, corresponding to the (theoretical) maximum conceivable extent of population inversion, the opposite extreme to +0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
