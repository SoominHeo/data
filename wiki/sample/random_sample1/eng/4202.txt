<p><b>Sweat glands</b>, also known as <b>sudoriferous</b> or <b>sudoriparous glands</b>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">sudor</span></i>, meaning "sweat",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saunders03_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-saunders03-7">[7]</a></sup> are small tubular structures of the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> that produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweat" title="Sweat">sweat</a>. Sweat glands are a type of <a href="/wiki/Exocrine_gland" title="Exocrine gland">exocrine gland</a>, which are glands that produce and secrete substances onto an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epithelial" title="Epithelial">epithelial</a> surface by way of a <a href="/wiki/Duct_(anatomy)" title="Duct (anatomy)">duct</a>. There are two main types of sweat glands that differ in their structure, function, secretory product, mechanism of excretion, anatomic distribution, and distribution across species:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eccrine_sweat_gland" title="Eccrine sweat gland">Eccrine sweat glands</a> are distributed almost all over the human body, in varying densities. Its water-based secretion represents a primary form of <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">cooling</a> in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Apocrine_sweat_gland" title="Apocrine sweat gland">Apocrine sweat glands</a> are mostly limited to the <a href="/wiki/Axilla" title="Axilla">axilla (armpits)</a> and perianal areas in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255-8">[8]</a></sup> They are not significant for cooling in humans, but are the sole effective sweat glands in hoofed animals, such as the <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">donkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991181_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991181-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bullard70_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bullard70-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTES.C3.B8rensenPrasad1973173_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTES.C3.B8rensenPrasad1973173-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceruminous_glands" title="Ceruminous glands">Ceruminous glands</a> (which produce ear wax), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammary_glands" title="Mammary glands">mammary glands</a> (which produce <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciliary_glands" title="Ciliary glands">ciliary glands</a> in the eyelids are modified apocrine sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grayanat_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-grayanat-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ackerman2005_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ackerman2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gray946.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="450" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Gray946.png/220px-Gray946.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Gray946.png/330px-Gray946.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Gray946.png/440px-Gray946.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gray946.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Body of a sweat gland cut in various directions</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Generally, sweat glands consist of a <i>secretory unit</i> consisting of a base rolled into a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glomerulus" title="wikt:glomerulus">glomerulum</a>, and a <i>duct</i> that carries the sweat away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randall46_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-randall46-13">[13]</a></sup> The <i>secretory coil</i> or base, is set deep in the lower <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypodermis" title="Hypodermis">hypodermis</a>, and the entire gland is surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">adipose tissue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grayanat_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-grayanat-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caceci_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-caceci-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255-8">[8]</a></sup> In both sweat gland types, the secretory coils are surrounded by contractile myoepithelial cells that function to facilitate excretion of secretory product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984256_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984256-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEroschenko2008222.2C_226.2C_228_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEroschenko2008222.2C_226.2C_228-16">[16]</a></sup> The secretory activities of the gland cells and the contractions of myoepithelial cells are controlled by both the autonomic nervous system and by the circulating hormones. The distal or apical part of the duct that opens to the skin's surface is known as the <i>acrosyringium</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bolognia.2C_Jorizzo.2CSchaffer_2012_539.E2.80.93544_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bolognia.2C_Jorizzo.2CSchaffer_2012_539.E2.80.93544-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each sweat gland receives several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nerve_fibers" title="Nerve fibers">nerve fibers</a> that branch out into bands of one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axons" title="Axons">axons</a> and encircle the individual tubules of the secretory coil. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">Capillaries</a> are also interwoven among sweat tubules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy94_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennedy94-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The number of active sweat glands varies greatly among different people, though comparisons between different areas (ex. axillae vs. groin) show the same directional changes (certain areas always have more active sweat glands while others always have fewer).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandall2012_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandall2012-21">[21]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Gray" title="Henry Gray">Henry Gray</a>'s estimates, the palm has around 370 sweat glands per cm<sup>2</sup>; the back of the hand has 200 per cm<sup>2</sup>; the forehead has 175 per cm<sup>2</sup>; the breast, abdomen, and forearm have 155 per cm<sup>2</sup>; and the back and legs have 60–80 per cm<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grayanat_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-grayanat-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the finger pads, sweat glands are somewhat irregularly spaced on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal_papillae" title="Dermal papillae">epidermal ridges</a>. There are no pores between the ridges, though sweat tends to spill into them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandall2012_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandall2012-21">[21]</a></sup> The thick epidermis of the palms and soles causes the sweat glands to become spirally coiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grayanat_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-grayanat-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-primate mammals have eccrine sweat glands only on the palms and soles. Apocrine glands cover the rest of the body, though they are not as effective as humans' in temperature regulation (with the exception of horses').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prosimian" title="Prosimian">Prosimians</a> have a 1:20 ratio of follicles with apocrine glands versus follicles without.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991182_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991182-22">[22]</a></sup> They have eccrine glands between hairs over most of their body (while humans have them between the hairs on their <a href="/wiki/Scalp" title="Scalp">scalp</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991181_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991181-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The overall distribution of sweat glands varies among primates: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhesus_monkey" title="Rhesus monkey">rhesus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patas_monkey" title="Patas monkey">patas monkeys</a> have them on the chest; the <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_monkey" title="Squirrel monkey">squirrel monkey</a> has them only on the palms and soles; and the <a href="/wiki/Stump-tailed_macaque" title="Stump-tailed macaque">stump-tailed macaque</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_monkey" title="Japanese monkey">Japanese monkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baboon" title="Baboon">baboon</a> have them over the entire body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991183_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991183-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Domestic animals<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2013)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocrine_glands" title="Apocrine glands">apocrine glands</a> at the base of each hair follicle, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccrine_glands" title="Eccrine glands">eccrine glands</a> only in foot pads and snout. Their apocrine glands, like those in humans, produce an odorless oily milky secretion evolved not to evaporate and cool but rather coat and stick to hair so odor-causing bacteria can grow on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merck_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-merck-24">[24]</a></sup> Eccrine glands on their foot pads, like those on palms and soles of humans, did not evolve to cool either but rather increase friction and enhance grip.</p>
<p>Dogs and cats have apocrine glands that are specialized in both structure and function located at the eyelids (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moll%27s_glands" title="Moll's glands">Moll's glands</a>), ears (<a href="/wiki/Ceruminous_gland" title="Ceruminous gland">ceruminous glands</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_sac" title="Anal sac">anal sac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clitoral_hood" title="Clitoral hood">prepuce vulva</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perineum" title="Perineum">circumanal area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slatter03_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-slatter03-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apocrine">Apocrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Apocrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apocrine_sweat_gland" title="Apocrine sweat gland">Apocrine sweat gland</a></div>
<p>Apocrine sweat glands are found in the armpit, <a href="/wiki/Areola" title="Areola">areola</a> (around the nipples), perineum (between the anus and genitals), in the ear, and in the eyelids. The secretory portion is larger than that of eccrine glands (making them larger overall). Rather than opening directly onto the surface of the skin, apocrine glands secrete sweat into the pilary canal of the <a href="/wiki/Hair_follicle" title="Hair follicle">hair follicle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before puberty, the apocrine sweat glands are inactive;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mosby_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-mosby-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Puberty#Major_hormones" title="Puberty">hormonal changes in puberty</a> cause the glands to increase in size and begin functioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-braunfalco_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-braunfalco-27">[27]</a></sup> The substance secreted is thicker than eccrine sweat and provides nutrients for bacteria on the skin: the bacteria's decomposition of sweat is what creates the acrid odor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dorlands_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-dorlands-28">[28]</a></sup> Apocrine sweat glands are most active in times of stress and sexual excitement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americanheritage_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-americanheritage-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In mammals (including humans), apocrine sweat contains <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromone</a>-like compounds to attract other organisms within their species. Study of human sweat has revealed differences between men and women in apocrine secretions and bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eccrine">Eccrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eccrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eccrine_sweat_gland" title="Eccrine sweat gland">Eccrine sweat gland</a></div>
<p>Eccrine sweat glands are everywhere except the lips, <a href="/wiki/Ear_canal" title="Ear canal">ear canal</a>, prepuce, <a href="/wiki/Glans_penis" title="Glans penis">glans penis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labia_minora" title="Labia minora">labia minora</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clitoris" title="Clitoris">clitoris</a>. They are ten times smaller than apocrine sweat glands, do not extend as deeply into the dermis, and excrete directly onto the surface of the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurosumiShibasakiIto1984255-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKrstic2004466_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrstic2004466-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spearman73_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-spearman73-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKrstic2004464_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrstic2004464-4">[4]</a></sup> The proportion of eccrine glands decreases with age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007175.E2.80.93176_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007175.E2.80.93176-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The clear secretion produced by eccrine sweat glands is termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweat" title="Sweat">sweat</a> or <i>sensible perspiration</i>. Sweat is mostly water, but it does contain some electrolytes, since it is derived from <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. The presence of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> gives sweat a salty taste.</p>
<p>The total volume of sweat produced depends on the number of functional glands and the size of the surface opening. The degree of secretory activity is regulated by neural and hormonal mechanisms (men sweat more than women). When all of the eccrine sweat glands are working at maximum capacity, the rate of perspiration for a human being may exceed three liters per hour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and dangerous losses of fluids and electrolytes can occur.</p>
<p>Eccrine glands have three primary functions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Thermoregulation: sweat cools the surface of the skin and reduces body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007170_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007170-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>Excretion: eccrine sweat gland secretion can also provide a significant excretory route for water and electrolytes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERomich2009203_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERomich2009203-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Protection: eccrine sweat gland secretion aids in preserving the skin's <a href="/wiki/Acid_mantle" title="Acid mantle">acid mantle</a>, which helps protect the skin from colonization from bacteria and other pathogenic organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Apoeccrine">Apoeccrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Apoeccrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some human sweat glands cannot be classified as either apocrine or eccrine, having characteristics of both; such glands are termed <i>apoeccrine</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171-37">[37]</a></sup> They are larger than eccrine glands, but smaller than apocrine ones;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> their secretory portion has a narrow portion similar to secretory coils in eccrine glands as well as a wide section reminiscent of apocrine glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apoeccrine, found in the armpits and perianal region, have ducts opening onto the skin surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> They are presumed to have developed in puberty from the eccrine glands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007175_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007175-41">[41]</a></sup> and can comprise up to 50% of all axillary glands. Apoeccrine glands secrete more sweat than both eccrine and apocrine glands, thus playing a large role in axillary sweating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007176_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007176-42">[42]</a></sup> Apoeccrine glands are sensitive to cholinergic activity, though they can also be activated via adrenergic stimulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171-37">[37]</a></sup> Like eccrine glands, they continuously secrete a thin, watery sweat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007176_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007176-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Specialized sweat glands, including the <a href="/wiki/Ceruminous_gland" title="Ceruminous gland">ceruminous glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammary_gland" title="Mammary gland">mammary glands</a>, ciliary glands of the eyelids, and sweat glands of the <a href="/wiki/Nasal_vestibule" title="Nasal vestibule">nasal vestibulum</a>, are modified apocrine glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hogin06_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-hogin06-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKrstic2004466_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrstic2004466-5">[5]</a></sup> Ceruminous glands are near the ear canals, and produce cerumen (earwax) that mixes with the oil secreted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebaceous_glands" title="Sebaceous glands">sebaceous glands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERomich2009206_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERomich2009206-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hogin06_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-hogin06-43">[43]</a></sup> Mammary glands use apocrine secretion to produce milk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanlommel_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanlommel-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweat">Sweat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sweat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sweat glands are used to regulate temperature and remove waste by secreting water, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_salt" title="Sodium salt">sodium salts</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogenous_waste" title="Nitrogenous waste">nitrogenous waste</a> (such as urea) onto the skin surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERomich2009203_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERomich2009203-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEroschenko2008215_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEroschenko2008215-46">[46]</a></sup> The main electrolytes of sweat are <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> though the amount is small enough to make sweat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotonic" title="Hypotonic">hypotonic</a> at the skin surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESlegers1964271_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESlegers1964271-48">[48]</a></sup> Eccrine sweat is clear, odorless, and is composed of 98–99% water; it also contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NaCl" title="NaCl">NaCl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a>, urea, and <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> ranges from 4 to 6.8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draelos10_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-draelos10-49">[49]</a></sup> On the other hand, the apocrine sweat has a pH of 6 to 7.5; it contains water, proteins, carbohydrate waste material, lipids, and <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a>. The sweat is oily, cloudy, viscous, and originally odorless;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draelos10_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-draelos10-49">[49]</a></sup> it gains odor upon decomposition by bacteria. Because both apocrine glands and sebaceous glands open into the hair follicle, apocrine sweat is mixed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebum" title="Sebum">sebum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007175_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007175-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apocrine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="516" data-file-width="749" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Apocrine.jpg/220px-Apocrine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Apocrine.jpg/330px-Apocrine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Apocrine.jpg/440px-Apocrine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apocrine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In apocrine secretion (pictured), portions of the cell are pinched off and later disintegrate.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was originally thought that both apocrine and eccrine sweat glands used <a href="/wiki/Merocrine" title="Merocrine">merocrine secretion</a>, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesicle_(biology)" title="Vesicle (biology)">vesicles</a> in the gland released sweat via <a href="/wiki/Exocytosis" title="Exocytosis">exocytosis</a>, leaving the entire cell intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saunders03_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-saunders03-7">[7]</a></sup> More recent studies have revealed that apocrine glands release sweat in the hair follicle via apocrine secretion, where portions of the cell are actually pinched off, and disintegrate later to excrete sweat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandall2012254.E2.80.93255_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandall2012254.E2.80.93255-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saunders03_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-saunders03-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In both apocrine and eccrine sweat glands, the sweat is originally produced in the gland's coil, where it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotonicity" title="Isotonicity">isotonic</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a> there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESlegers1964265_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESlegers1964265-51">[51]</a></sup> When the rate of sweating is low, salt is conserved and reabsorbed by the gland's duct; high sweat rates, on the other hand, lead to less salt reabsorption and allow more water to evaporate on the skin (via <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>) to increase <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESlegers1964272_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESlegers1964272-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Secretion of sweat occurs when the <a href="/wiki/Myoepithelial_cell" title="Myoepithelial cell">myoepithelial cell</a> cells surrounding the secretory glands contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEroschenko2008228_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEroschenko2008228-20">[20]</a></sup> Eccrine sweat increases the rate of bacterial growth and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatilized" title="Volatilized">volatilizes</a> the odor compounds of apocrine sweat, strengthening the latter's acrid smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETsai2006497_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETsai2006497-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Normally, only a certain number of sweat glands are actively producing sweat. When stimuli call for more sweating, more sweat glands are activated, with each then producing more sweat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShibasakiWilsonCrandall20061694_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShibasakiWilsonCrandall20061694-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randall46_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-randall46-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stimuli">Stimuli</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Stimuli">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal">Thermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Thermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Both eccrine and apocrine sweat glands participate in thermal (thermoregulatory) sweating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991180_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991180-55">[55]</a></sup> which is directly controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>. Thermal sweating is stimulated by a combination of internal body temperature and mean skin temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007170_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007170-34">[34]</a></sup> In eccrine sweat glands, stimulation occurs via activation by <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a>, which binds to the gland's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscarinic_receptors" title="Muscarinic receptors">muscarinic receptors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShibasakiWilsonCrandall20061693_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShibasakiWilsonCrandall20061693-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Emotional">Emotional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Emotional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Emotional sweating is stimulated by stress, anxiety, fear, and pain; it is independent of ambient temperature. Acetylcholine acts on the eccrine glands and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrenaline" title="Adrenaline">adrenaline</a> acts on both eccrine and apocrine glands to produce sweat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171-37">[37]</a></sup> Emotional sweating can occur anywhere, though it is most evident on the palms, soles of the feet, and axillary regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007170_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007170-34">[34]</a></sup> Sweating on the palms and soles is thought to have evolved as a fleeing reaction in mammals: it increases friction and prevents slipping when running or climbing in stressful situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991180_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolk_JrSemken_Jr1991180-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gustatory">Gustatory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gustatory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Gustatory sweating refers to thermal sweating induced by the ingestion of food. The increase in metabolism caused by ingestion raises body temperature, leading to thermal sweating. Hot and spicy foods also leads to mild gustatory sweating in the face, scalp and neck: <a href="/wiki/Capsaicin" title="Capsaicin">capsaicin</a> (the compound that makes spicy food taste "hot"), binds to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_receptor" title="Sensory receptor">receptors</a> in the mouth that detect warmth. The increased stimulation of such receptors induces a thermoregulatory response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkeMartinTerstegenBiel2007171-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiperspirant">Antiperspirant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Antiperspirant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Deodorant" title="Deodorant">deodorant</a>, which simply reduces axillary odor without affecting body functions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiperspirant" title="Antiperspirant">antiperspirant</a> reduces both eccrine and apocrine sweating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.15_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.15-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draelos10_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-draelos10-49">[49]</a></sup> Antiperspirants, which are classified as drugs, cause proteins to precipitate and mechanically block eccrine (and sometimes apocrine) sweat ducts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.14_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.14-58">[58]</a></sup> The metal salts found in antiperspirants alters the <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a> <a href="/wiki/Fibril" title="Fibril">fibrils</a> in the ducts; the ducts then close and form a "horny plug". The main active ingredients in modern antiperspirants are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_chloride" title="Aluminum chloride">aluminum chloride</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_chlorohydrate" title="Aluminum chlorohydrate">aluminum chlorohydrate</a>, aluminum zirconium chlorohydrate, and buffered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_sulfate" title="Aluminum sulfate">aluminum sulfate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draelos10_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-draelos10-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On apocrine glands, antiperspirants also contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibacterial</a> agents such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trichlorocarbanilide" title="Trichlorocarbanilide">trichlorocarbanilide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexamethylene_tetramine" title="Hexamethylene tetramine">hexamethylene tetramine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_ricinoleate" title="Zinc ricinoleate">zinc ricinoleate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica-skindis_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-skindis-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.16_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.16-60">[60]</a></sup> The salts are dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and mixed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_oils" title="Essential oils">essential oils</a> high in <a href="/wiki/Eugenol" title="Eugenol">eugenol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thymol" title="Thymol">thymol</a> (such as thyme and clove oils). Antiperspirants may also contain <a href="/wiki/Levomethamphetamine" title="Levomethamphetamine">levomethamphetamine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.16_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKastureGokhalParakhParadkar200815.16-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathology">Pathology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pathology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diseases of the sweat glands include:</p>
<dl>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox-Fordyce_disease" title="Fox-Fordyce disease">Fox-Fordyce disease</a> </dt>
<dd>The apocrine sweat glands become inflamed, causing a persistent, itchy rash, usually in the axillae and pubic areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dorland-disease_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-dorland-disease-61">[61]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Frey%27s_syndrome" title="Frey's syndrome">Frey's Syndrome</a></dt>
<dd>If the auriculotemporal nerve is damaged (most often as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Parotidectomy" title="Parotidectomy">Parotidectomy</a>), excess sweat can be produced in the rear of the cheek area (just below the ear) in response to stimuli that cause salivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM-62">[62]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heatstroke" title="Heatstroke">Heatstroke</a></dt>
<dd>When the eccrine glands become exhausted and unable to secrete sweat. Heatstroke can lead to fatal <a href="/wiki/Fever#Hyperpyrexia" title="Fever">hyperpyrexia</a> (extreme rise in body temperature).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica-skindis_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-skindis-59">[59]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Hyperhidrosis" title="Hyperhidrosis">Hyperhidrosis</a></dt>
<dd>(also known as <b>hyperidrosis</b>) is a pathological, excessive sweating that can be either generalized or localized (<a href="/wiki/Focal_hyperhidrosis" title="Focal hyperhidrosis">focal hyperhidrosis</a>); focal hyperhidrosis occurs most often on the palms, soles, face, scalp and axillae. Hyperhidrosis is usually brought on by emotional or thermal stress,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETsai2006496_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETsai2006496-63">[63]</a></sup> but it can also occur or with little to no stimulus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica-skindis_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-skindis-59">[59]</a></sup> Local (or asymmetrical) hyperhidrosis is said to be caused by problems in the <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system" title="Sympathetic nervous system">sympathetic nervous system</a>: either lesions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETsai2006496_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETsai2006496-63">[63]</a></sup> or nerve inflammation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica-hyper_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-hyper-64">[64]</a></sup> Hyperhidrosis can also be caused by <a href="/wiki/Trench_foot" title="Trench foot">trench foot</a> or <a href="/wiki/Encephalitis" title="Encephalitis">encephalitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica-hyper_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-hyper-64">[64]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Milaria rubra</dt>
<dd>Also called <b>prickly heat</b>. Milaria rubra is the rupture of sweat glands and migration of sweat to other tissues. In hot environments, the skin's <a href="/wiki/Stratum_corneum" title="Stratum corneum">horny layer</a> can expand due to sweat retention, blocking the ducts of eccrine sweat glands. The glands, still stimulated by high temperatures, continues to secrete. Sweat builds up in the duct, causing enough pressure to rupture the duct where it meets the epidermis. Sweat also escapes the duct to adjacent tissues (a process called <i>milaria</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica-skindis_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-skindis-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston201119_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston201119-65">[65]</a></sup> Hypohydrosis then follows milaria (postmiliarial hypohydrosis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston201120_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston201120-66">[66]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Osmidrosis</dt>
<dd>Often called <b>bromhidrosis</b>, especially in combination with hyperhidrosis. Osmohidrosis is excessive odor from apocrine sweat glands (which are overactive in the axillae).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETsai2006496_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETsai2006496-63">[63]</a></sup> Osmidrosis is thought to be caused by changes in the apocrine gland structure rather than changes in the bacteria that acts on sweat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETsai2006497_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETsai2006497-53">[53]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_signs_in_other_illnesses">As signs in other illnesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweat_gland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: As signs in other illnesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many diseases cause sweat gland dysfunction:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acromegaly" title="Acromegaly">Acromegaly</a>, a result of excess <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone" title="Growth hormone">growth hormone</a>, causes the size of sweat glands increase, which leads to thicker skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERubinStrayer20111043_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERubinStrayer20111043-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquagenic_wrinkling_of_the_palms" title="Aquagenic wrinkling of the palms">Aquagenic wrinkling of the palms</a>, in which white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papules" title="Papules">papules</a> develop on the palms after exposure to water, can sometimes come with abnormal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquaporin_5" title="Aquaporin 5">aquaporin 5</a> in the sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011210_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011210-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">Cystic fibrosis</a> can be diagnosed by a <a href="/wiki/Sweat_test" title="Sweat test">sweat test</a>,as the disease causes the sweat glands ducts to reabsorb less chloride, leading to higher concentrations of chloride in the secreted sweat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bernstein11_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-bernstein11-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ectodermal_dysplasia" title="Ectodermal dysplasia">Ectodermal dysplasia</a> can present a lack of sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medline-ectodermal_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-medline-ectodermal-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fabry_disease" title="Fabry disease">Fabry disease</a>, characterized by excess <a href="/wiki/Globotriaosylceramide" title="Globotriaosylceramide">globotriaosylceramide</a> (GL3), causes a decrease in sweat gland function due to GL3 deposits in the eccrine glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elstein10_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-elstein10-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/GM1_gangliosidoses" title="GM1 gangliosidoses">GM<sub>1</sub> gangliosidoses</a>, characterized by abnormal lipid storage, leads to <a href="/wiki/Vacuolization" title="Vacuolization">vacuolization</a> in eccrine sweat gland cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drut78_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-drut78-74">[74]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hunter_syndrome" title="Hunter syndrome">Hunter syndrome</a> can include metachromin <a href="/wiki/Granule_(cell_biology)" title="Granule (cell biology)">granules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mucin" title="Mucin">mucin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> of the eccrine sweat gland cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011534_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011534-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">Hypothyroidism</a>'s low levels of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_hormone" title="Thyroid hormone">thyroid hormone</a> lead to decreased secretions from sweat glands; the result is dry, coarse skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERubinStrayer20111048_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERubinStrayer20111048-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kearns%E2%80%93Sayre_syndrome" title="Kearns–Sayre syndrome">Kearns–Sayre syndrome</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_myopathy" title="Mitochondrial myopathy">disease of the mitochondria</a>, involves abnormal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> in eccrine sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martin84_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-martin84-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lafora_disease" title="Lafora disease">Lafora disease</a> is a rare genetic disorder marked by the presence of abnormal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyglucosan" title="Polyglucosan">polyglucosan</a> deposits. These "Lafora bodies" appear in the ducts of sweat glands, as well as the myoepithelial cells of apocrine glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubio92_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubio92-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lichen_striatus" title="Lichen striatus">Lichen striatus</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Self-limiting_(biology)" title="Self-limiting (biology)">self-limited</a> eruption of small, slightly scaly <a href="/wiki/Papule" title="Papule">papules</a>, includes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphoid" title="Lymphoid">lymphoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(medical)" title="Infiltration (medical)">infiltrate</a> around eccrine sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011223.E2.80.93224_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011223.E2.80.93224-79">[79]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metachromatic_leukodystrophy" title="Metachromatic leukodystrophy">Metachromatic leukodystrophy</a>, a lysosomal storage disease, leads to the accumulation of lipopigments and lysosomal residual bodies in the epithelial cells of sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goebel-89_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-goebel-89-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neuronal_ceroid_lipofuscinosis" title="Neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis">Neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis</a> causes abnormal deposits of lipopigment in sweat gland epithelial cells (among other places).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carlen01_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-carlen01-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neutral_lipid_storage_disease" title="Neutral lipid storage disease">Neutral lipid storage disease</a> includes abnormal lipid deposits in cells, including those of the sweat gland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011555_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJamesBergerElston2011555-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Niemann%E2%80%93Pick_disease,_type_C" title="Niemann–Pick disease, type C">Niemann-Pick disease type C</a>, another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_storage_disease" title="Lipid storage disease">lipid storage disease</a>, includes abnormal lipid storage in sweat glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elleder75_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-elleder75-83">[83]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schindler_disease" title="Schindler disease">Schindler disease</a> causes cytoplasmic <a href="/wiki/Vacuole" title="Vacuole">vacuoles</a> that appear to be empty or contain filamentous material to manifest in eccrine sweat gland cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pavelka10_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-pavelka10-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Small_fiber_peripheral_neuropathy" title="Small fiber peripheral neuropathy">Small fiber peripheral neuropathy</a> can damage the nerves that control the sweat glands. The sweat gland nerve fiber density test can diagnose this condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
