<p><b>Tteokguk</b> (ë¡êµ­) is a traditional <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> dish eaten during the celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_New_Year" title="Korean New Year">Korean New Year</a>. The dish consists of the broth/soup (<i><a href="/wiki/Guk" title="Guk">guk</a></i>) with thinly sliced rice cakes (<i><a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">tteok</a> - it can be called a kind of rice pasta</i>). It is tradition to eat <i>tteokguk</i> on New Year's Day because it is believed to grant the consumer good luck for the year and gain a year of age. It is usually garnished with thin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julienned" title="Julienned">julienned</a> cooked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, marinated meat, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gim_(Korean_food)" title="Gim (Korean food)">gim</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tteokguk_doosan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tteokguk_doosan-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tteokguk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of eating <i>tteokguk</i> on New Year's Day is unknown. However, <i>tteokguk</i> is mentioned in the 19th century book of customs <i>Dongguksesigi</i> (ëêµ­ì¸ìê¸°, æ±åæ­²æè¨) as being made with beef or pheasant used as the main ingredient for the broth, and pepper added as seasoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tteokguk_nate_britannica_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tteokguk_nate_britannica-2">[2]</a></sup> The book also mentions the custom of having a bowl of <i>tteokguk</i> in the morning of New Year's Day to get a year older, and the custom of saying "How many bowls of <i>tteokguk</i> have you eaten?" to ask a person's age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tteokguk_nate_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tteokguk_nate-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the book <i>The Customs of Joseon</i> written in 1946 by historian <a href="/wiki/Choe_Nam-seon" title="Choe Nam-seon">Choe Nam-seon</a>, the New Year custom of eating <i>tteokguk</i> is speculated as being originated from ancient times. The white <i>tteok</i> signifying purity and cleanliness would be eaten as a ritual to start off the New Year for good fortune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tteokguk_nate_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tteokguk_nate-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Korea, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_New_Year%27s_Day" title="Lunar New Year's Day">Lunar New Year's Day</a>, a family performs ancestral rites by serving tteokguk to their ancestors during a joint meal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Although <i>tteokguk</i> is traditionally a seasonal dish, it is now eaten at all times of the year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ingredients_and_varieties">Ingredients and varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tteokguk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ingredients and varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The broth is generally made by simmering the main protein (beef, chicken, pork, pheasant, seafood) in a <i><a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">ganjang</a></i> seasoned stock. The stock is then strained to clarify the broth and long cylinder-shaped <i><a href="/wiki/Garaetteok" title="Garaetteok">garaetteok</a></i> are thin-sliced diagonally and boiled in the clear broth. Garnish is added before serving; the garnish may vary by region and personal taste, but usual staples are pan-fried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julienned" title="Julienned">julienned</a> egg yolks and whites, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gim_(Korean_food)" title="Gim (Korean food)">gim</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_onions" title="Spring onions">spring onions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tteokguk_nate_britannica_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tteokguk_nate_britannica-2">[2]</a></sup>A drizzle of sesame oil is common just prior to serving the teokguk.</p>
<p>Varieties of <i>tteokguk</i> include: <i>saeng tteokguk</i> (ìë¡êµ­) or <i>nal tteokguk</i> (ë ë¡êµ­), a specialty of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chungcheong" title="Chungcheong">Chungcheong</a> province, where a mixture of non-glutinous rice with glutinous rice is made into small balls or rolled into a <i>garaetteok</i> shape and then sliced into a boiling broth;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>joraengi tteokguk</i> (ì¡°ë­ì´ ë¡êµ­) from the <a href="/wiki/Kaesong" title="Kaesong">Kaesong</a> region with the <i>tteok</i> twisted in small cocoon shapes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <i>gon tteokguk</i> (ê³¤ë¡êµ­) from the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeju-do" title="Jeju-do">Jeju</a>, which uses sliced <i><a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">jeolpyeon tteok</a></i> rather than the usual <i><a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">garaetteok</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another variety, <i>tteokmanduguk</i> is literally <i>tteokguk</i> with additional <i><a href="/wiki/Mandu_(dumpling)" title="Mandu (dumpling)">mandu</a></i>.</p>
