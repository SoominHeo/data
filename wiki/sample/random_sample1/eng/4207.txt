<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Swarm intelligence</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Swarm intelligence</b> (<b>SI</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Collective_behavior" title="Collective behavior">collective behavior</a> of <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralized</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self-organization" title="Self-organization">self-organized</a> systems, natural or artificial. The concept is employed in work on <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>. The expression was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Gerardo_Beni" title="Gerardo Beni">Gerardo Beni</a> and Jing Wang in 1989, in the context of cellular robotic systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>SI systems consist typically of a population of simple <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">agents</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boids" title="Boids">boids</a> interacting locally with one another and with their environment. The inspiration often comes from nature, especially biological systems. The agents follow very simple rules, and although there is no centralized control structure dictating how individual agents should behave, local, and to a certain degree random, interactions between such agents lead to the <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergence</a> of "intelligent" global behavior, unknown to the individual agents. Examples in natural systems of SI include <a href="/wiki/Ant_colony" title="Ant colony">ant colonies</a>, bird <a href="/wiki/Flocking_(behavior)" title="Flocking (behavior)">flocking</a>, animal <a href="/wiki/Herding" title="Herding">herding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria#Growth_and_reproduction" title="Bacteria">bacterial growth</a>, fish <a href="/wiki/Shoaling_and_schooling" title="Shoaling and schooling">schooling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial_intelligence" title="Microbial intelligence">microbial intelligence</a>.</p>
<p>The application of swarm principles to <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Swarm_robotics" title="Swarm robotics">swarm robotics</a>, while 'swarm intelligence' refers to the more general set of algorithms. 'Swarm prediction' has been used in the context of forecasting problems.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models_of_swarm_behavior">Models of swarm behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Models of swarm behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Swarm_behaviour" title="Swarm behaviour">Swarm behaviour</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boids_.28Reynolds_1987.29">Boids (Reynolds 1987)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Boids (Reynolds 1987)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boids" title="Boids">Boids</a></div>
<p>Boids is an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life" title="Artificial life">artificial life</a> program, developed by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Reynolds_(computer_graphics)" title="Craig Reynolds (computer graphics)">Craig Reynolds</a> in 1986, which simulates the <a href="/wiki/Flocking_(behavior)" title="Flocking (behavior)">flocking</a> behaviour of birds. His paper on this topic was published in 1987 in the proceedings of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a> <a href="/wiki/SIGGRAPH" title="SIGGRAPH">SIGGRAPH</a> conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The name "boid" corresponds to a shortened version of "bird-oid object", which refers to a bird-like object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with most artificial life simulations, Boids is an example of <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergent</a> behavior; that is, the complexity of Boids arises from the interaction of individual agents (the boids, in this case) adhering to a set of simple rules. The rules applied in the simplest Boids world are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>separation</b>: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/steer#Verb" title="wikt:steer">steer</a> to avoid crowding local flockmates</li>
<li><b>alignment</b>: steer towards the average heading of local flockmates</li>
<li><b>cohesion</b>: steer to move toward the average position (center of mass) of local flockmates</li>
</ul>
<p>More complex rules can be added, such as obstacle avoidance and goal seeking.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-propelled_particles_.28Vicsek_et_al._1995.29">Self-propelled particles (Vicsek <i>et al</i>. 1995)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Self-propelled particles (Vicsek et al. 1995)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Self-propelled_particles" title="Self-propelled particles">Self-propelled particles</a></div>
<p>Self-propelled particles (SPP), also referred to as the <i>Vicsek model</i>, was introduced in 1995 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamas_Vicsek" title="Tamas Vicsek">Vicsek</a> <i>et al.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vicsek1995_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vicsek1995-4">[4]</a></sup> as a special case of the <a href="/wiki/Boids" title="Boids">boids</a> model introduced in 1986 by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Reynolds_(computer_graphics)" title="Craig Reynolds (computer graphics)">Reynolds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A swarm is modelled in SPP by a collection of particles that move with a constant speed but respond to a random perturbation by adopting at each time increment the average direction of motion of the other particles in their local neighbourhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> SPP models predict that swarming animals share certain properties at the group level, regardless of the type of animals in the swarm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buhl_et_al_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buhl_et_al-7">[7]</a></sup> Swarming systems give rise to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergent_behaviour" title="Emergent behaviour">emergent behaviours</a> which occur at many different scales, some of which are turning out to be both universal and robust. It has become a challenge in theoretical physics to find minimal statistical models that capture these behaviours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bertin_et_al_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bertin_et_al-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_et_al_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Li_et_al-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metaheuristics">Metaheuristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metaheuristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_metaphor-based_metaheuristics" title="List of metaphor-based metaheuristics">List of metaphor-based metaheuristics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_algorithm" title="Evolutionary algorithm">Evolutionary algorithms</a> (EA), <a href="/wiki/Particle_swarm_optimization" title="Particle swarm optimization">particle swarm optimization</a> (PSO), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ant_colony_optimization" title="Ant colony optimization">ant colony optimization</a> (ACO) and their variants dominate the field of nature-inspired <a href="/wiki/Metaheuristic" title="Metaheuristic">metaheuristics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This list includes algorithms published up to circa the year 2000. A large number of more recent metaphor-inspired metaheuristics have started to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_metaphor-inspired_metaheuristics#Criticism" title="List of metaphor-inspired metaheuristics">attract criticism in the research community</a> for hiding their lack of novelty behind an elaborate metaphor. For algorithms published since that time, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_metaphor-based_metaheuristics" title="List of metaphor-based metaheuristics">List of metaphor-based metaheuristics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stochastic_diffusion_search_.28Bishop_1989.29">Stochastic diffusion search (Bishop 1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stochastic diffusion search (Bishop 1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_diffusion_search" title="Stochastic diffusion search">Stochastic diffusion search</a></div>
<p>Stochastic diffusion search (SDS)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> is an agent-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probabilistic_algorithm" title="Probabilistic algorithm">probabilistic</a> global search and optimization technique best suited to problems where the objective function can be decomposed into multiple independent partial-functions. Each agent maintains a hypothesis which is iteratively tested by evaluating a randomly selected partial objective function parameterised by the agent's current hypothesis. In the standard version of SDS such partial function evaluations are binary, resulting in each agent becoming active or inactive. Information on hypotheses is diffused across the population via inter-agent communication. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Stigmergy" title="Stigmergy">stigmergic</a> communication used in ACO, in SDS agents communicate <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypotheses</a> via a one-to-one communication strategy analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Tandem_running" title="Tandem running">tandem running</a> procedure observed in <a href="/wiki/Leptothorax_acervorum" title="Leptothorax acervorum">Leptothorax acervorum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A positive feedback mechanism ensures that, over time, a population of agents stabilise around the global-best solution. SDS is both an efficient and robust global search and optimisation algorithm, which has been extensively mathematically described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Recent work has involved merging the global search properties of SDS with other swarm intelligence algorithms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ant_colony_optimization_.28Dorigo_1992.29">Ant colony optimization (Dorigo 1992)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ant colony optimization (Dorigo 1992)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ant_colony_optimization" title="Ant colony optimization">Ant colony optimization</a></div>
<p>Ant colony optimization (ACO), introduced by Dorigo in his doctoral dissertation, is a class of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimization_(mathematics)" title="Optimization (mathematics)">optimization</a> <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> modeled on the actions of an <a href="/wiki/Ant_colony" title="Ant colony">ant colony</a>. ACO is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probabilistic_algorithm" title="Probabilistic algorithm">probabilistic technique</a> useful in problems that deal with finding better paths through graphs. Artificial 'ants'—simulation agents—locate optimal solutions by moving through a <a href="/wiki/Parameter_space" title="Parameter space">parameter space</a> representing all possible solutions. Natural ants lay down <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a> directing each other to resources while exploring their environment. The simulated 'ants' similarly record their positions and the quality of their solutions, so that in later simulation iterations more ants locate better solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particle_swarm_optimization_.28Kennedy.2C_Eberhart_.26_Shi_1995.29">Particle swarm optimization (Kennedy, Eberhart &amp; Shi 1995)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Particle swarm optimization (Kennedy, Eberhart &amp; Shi 1995)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Particle_swarm_optimization" title="Particle swarm optimization">Particle swarm optimization</a></div>
<p>Particle swarm optimization (PSO) is a <a href="/wiki/Global_optimization" title="Global optimization">global optimization</a> algorithm for dealing with problems in which a best solution can be represented as a point or surface in an n-dimensional space. Hypotheses are plotted in this space and seeded with an initial <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, as well as a communication channel between the particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Particles then move through the solution space, and are evaluated according to some <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> criterion after each timestep. Over time, particles are accelerated towards those particles within their communication grouping which have better fitness values. The main advantage of such an approach over other global minimization strategies such as <a href="/wiki/Simulated_annealing" title="Simulated annealing">simulated annealing</a> is that the large number of members that make up the particle swarm make the technique impressively resilient to the problem of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_minima" title="Local minima">local minima</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Swarm Intelligence-based techniques can be used in a number of applications. The U.S. military is investigating swarm techniques for controlling unmanned vehicles. The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> is thinking about an orbital swarm for self-assembly and interferometry. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> is investigating the use of swarm technology for planetary mapping. A 1992 paper by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._Anthony_Lewis_(roboticist)" title="M. Anthony Lewis (roboticist)">M. Anthony Lewis</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_A._Bekey" title="George A. Bekey">George A. Bekey</a> discusses the possibility of using swarm intelligence to control nanobots within the body for the purpose of killing cancer tumors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Conversely al-Rifaie and Aber have used <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_diffusion_search" title="Stochastic diffusion search">stochastic diffusion search</a> to help locate tumours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Swarm intelligence has also been applied for <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">data mining</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ant-based_routing">Ant-based routing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ant-based routing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of swarm intelligence in <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_network" title="Telecommunications network">telecommunication networks</a> has also been researched, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ant_colony_optimization_algorithms" title="Ant colony optimization algorithms">ant-based routing</a>. This was pioneered separately by Dorigo et al. and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hewlett_Packard" title="Hewlett Packard">Hewlett Packard</a> in the mid-1990s, with a number of variations since. Basically, this uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probabilistic_algorithm" title="Probabilistic algorithm">probabilistic</a> routing table rewarding/reinforcing the route successfully traversed by each "ant" (a small control packet) which flood the network. Reinforcement of the route in the forwards, reverse direction and both simultaneously have been researched: backwards reinforcement requires a symmetric network and couples the two directions together; forwards reinforcement rewards a route before the outcome is known (but then one would pay for the cinema before one knows how good the film is). As the system behaves stochastically and is therefore lacking repeatability, there are large hurdles to commercial deployment. Mobile media and new technologies have the potential to change the threshold for collective action due to swarm intelligence (Rheingold: 2002, P175).</p>
<p>The location of transmission infrastructure for wireless communication networks is an important engineering problem involving competing objectives. A minimal selection of locations (or sites) are required subject to providing adequate area coverage for users. A very different-ant inspired swarm intelligence algorithm, stochastic diffusion search (SDS), has been successfully used to provide a general model for this problem, related to circle packing and set covering. It has been shown that the SDS can be applied to identify suitable solutions even for large problem instances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Airlines have also used ant-based routing in assigning aircraft arrivals to airport gates. At <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> a software program uses swarm theory, or swarm intelligence—the idea that a colony of ants works better than one alone. Each pilot acts like an ant searching for the best airport gate. "The pilot learns from his experience what's the best for him, and it turns out that that's the best solution for the airline," <a href="/wiki/Douglas_A._Lawson" title="Douglas A. Lawson">Douglas A. Lawson</a> explains. As a result, the "colony" of pilots always go to gates they can arrive at and depart from quickly. The program can even alert a pilot of plane back-ups before they happen. "We can anticipate that it's going to happen, so we'll have a gate available," Lawson says.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crowd_simulation">Crowd simulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Crowd simulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Artists are using swarm technology as a means of creating complex interactive systems or <a href="/wiki/Crowd_simulation" title="Crowd simulation">simulating crowds</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Stanley_and_Stella_in:_Breaking_the_Ice" title="Stanley and Stella in: Breaking the Ice">Stanley and Stella in: Breaking the Ice</a></i> was the first movie to make use of swarm technology for rendering, realistically depicting the movements of groups of fish and birds using the Boids system. Tim Burton's <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Returns" title="Batman Returns">Batman Returns</a></i> also made use of swarm technology for showing the movements of a group of bats. <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)"><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> film trilogy</a> made use of similar technology, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massive_(software)" title="Massive (software)">Massive</a>, during battle scenes. Swarm technology is particularly attractive because it is cheap, robust, and simple.</p>
<p>Airlines have used swarm theory to simulate passengers boarding a plane. Southwest Airlines researcher Douglas A. Lawson used an ant-based computer simulation employing only six interaction rules to evaluate boarding times using various boarding methods.(Miller, 2010, xii-xviii).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_swarming">Human swarming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Human swarming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Enabled by mediating software such as the UNU platform from <a href="/wiki/Unanimous_A.I." title="Unanimous A.I.">Unanimous A.I.</a>, networks of distributed users can be organized into "human swarms" through the implementation of real-time closed-loop control systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> As published by <a href="/wiki/Louis_B._Rosenberg" title="Louis B. Rosenberg">Rosenberg</a> (2015), such real-time systems enable groups of human participants to behave as a unified <a href="/wiki/Collective_intelligence" title="Collective intelligence">collective intelligence</a> that works as a single entity to make predictions, answer questions, and evoke opinions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Such systems have been shown to significantly amplify human intelligence, resulting in a string of high-profile predictions of extreme accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Academic testing shows that human swarms can out-predict individuals across a variety of real-world projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swarm_grammars">Swarm grammars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Swarm grammars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Swarm grammars are swarms of <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_grammar" title="Stochastic grammar">stochastic grammars</a> that can be evolved to describe complex properties such as found in art and architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> These grammars interact as agents behaving according to rules of swarm intelligence. Such behavior can also suggest <a href="/wiki/Deep_learning" title="Deep learning">deep learning</a> algorithms, in particular when mapping of such swarms to neural circuits is considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swarmic_art">Swarmic art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Swarmic art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a series of works al-Rifaie et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-40">[40]</a></sup> have successfully used two swarm intelligence algorithms—one mimicking the behaviour of one species of ants (<i>Leptothorax acervorum</i>) foraging (<a href="/wiki/Stochastic_diffusion_search" title="Stochastic diffusion search">stochastic diffusion search</a>, SDS) and the other algorithm mimicking the behaviour of birds ﬂocking (<a href="/wiki/Particle_swarm_optimization" title="Particle swarm optimization">particle swarm optimization</a>, PSO)—to describe a novel integration strategy exploiting the local search properties of the PSO with global SDS behaviour. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_algorithm" title="Hybrid algorithm">hybrid algorithm</a> is used to sketch novel drawings of an input image, exploiting an artistic tension between the local behaviour of the 'birds ﬂocking'—as they seek to follow the input sketch—and the global behaviour of the "ants foraging"—as they seek to encourage the ﬂock to explore novel regions of the canvas. The "creativity" of this hybrid swarm system has been analysed under the philosophical light of the "rhizome" in the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deleuze" title="Deleuze">Deleuze</a>'s "Orchid and Wasp" metaphor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a more recent work of al-Rifaie et al., "Swarmic Sketches and Attention Mechanism",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> introduces a novel approach deploying the mechanism of 'attention' by adapting SDS to selectively attend to detailed areas of a digital canvas. Once the attention of the swarm is drawn to a certain line within the canvas, the capability of PSO is used to produce a 'swarmic sketch' of the attended line. The swarms move throughout the digital canvas in an attempt to satisfy their dynamic roles—attention to areas with more details—associated to them via their fitness function. Having associated the rendering process with the concepts of attention, the performance of the participating swarms creates a unique, non-identical sketch each time the 'artist' swarms embark on interpreting the input line drawings. In other works while PSO is responsible for the sketching process, SDS controls the attention of the swarm.</p>
<p>In a similar work, "Swarmic Paintings and Colour Attention",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> non-photorealistic images are produced using SDS algorithm which, in the context of this work, is responsible for colour attention.</p>
<p>The "computational creativity" of the above-mentioned systems are discussed in<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> through the two prerequisites of creativity (i.e. freedom and constraints) within the swarm intelligence's two infamous phases of exploration and exploitation.</p>
<p>Michael Theodore and <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Correll" title="Nikolaus Correll">Nikolaus Correll</a> use swarm intelligent art installation to explore what it takes to have engineered systems to appear lifelike<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Notable work include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swarm_Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swarm Wall (page does not exist)">Swarm Wall</a> (2012) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Endo-exo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Endo-exo (page does not exist)">endo-exo</a> (2014).</p>
