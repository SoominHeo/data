<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lithic reduction</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Lithic reduction</b> involves the use of a hard hammer percussor, such as a <a href="/wiki/Hammerstone" title="Hammerstone">hammerstone</a>, a soft hammer fabricator (made of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Antler" title="Antler">antler</a>), or a wood or antler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punch_(engineering)" title="Punch (engineering)">punch</a> to detach <a href="/wiki/Lithic_flake" title="Lithic flake">lithic flakes</a> from a lump of <a href="/wiki/Tool_stone" title="Tool stone">tool stone</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Lithic_core" title="Lithic core">lithic core</a> (also known as the "objective piece"). As flakes are detached in sequence, the original mass of stone is reduced; hence the term for this process. Lithic reduction may be performed in order to obtain sharp flakes, of which a variety of tools can be made, or to rough out a blank for later refinement into a <a href="/wiki/Projectile_point" title="Projectile point">projectile point</a>, knife, or other object. Flakes of regular size that are at least twice as long as they are broad are called <a href="/wiki/Blade_(archaeology)" title="Blade (archaeology)">blades</a>. Lithic tools produced this way may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biface" title="Biface">bifacial</a> (exhibiting flaking on both sides) or <a href="/wiki/Uniface" title="Uniface">unifacial</a> (exhibiting flaking on one side only).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cryptocrystalline" title="Cryptocrystalline">Cryptocrystalline</a> or amorphous stone such as <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chalcedony" title="Chalcedony">chalcedony</a>, as well as other fine-grained stone material, such as <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felsite" title="Felsite">felsite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a>, were used as a source material for producing stone tools. As these materials lack natural <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(crystal)" title="Cleavage (crystal)">planes of separation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conchoidal_fracture" title="Conchoidal fracture">conchoidal fractures</a> occur when they are struck with sufficient force. The propagation of force through the material takes the form of a <a href="/wiki/Hertzian_cone" title="Hertzian cone">Hertzian cone</a> that originates from the point of impact and results in the separation of material from the objective piece, usually in the form of a partial cone, commonly known as a <a href="/wiki/Lithic_flake" title="Lithic flake">lithic flake</a>. This process is predictable, and allows the <a href="/wiki/Knapping" title="Knapping">flintknapper</a> to control and direct the application of force so as to shape the material being worked. Controlled experiments may be performed using glass cores and consistent applied force in order to determine how varying factors affect core reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>By understanding the complex processes of lithic reduction, archaeologists recognize that the pattern and amount of reduction contribute tremendous effect to lithic assemblage compositions. One of the measurements is the geometric index of reduction. There are two elements in this index: 't' and 'T'. The 'T' is the 'height' of maximum blank thickness and the 't' is the height of retouched scar from the ventral surface. The ratio between t and T is the geometric index of reduction. In theory this ratio shall range between 0 and 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The bigger the number is the larger amount of lost weight from lithic flake. By using a logarithmic scale, a linear relationship between the geometric index and the percentage of original flake weight lost through retouch is confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In choosing a reduction index, it is important to understand the strengths and weaknesses of each method, and how they fit to the intended research question, as different indices provide different levels of information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, Kuhn's geometric index of unifacial reduction (GIUR), which describes the ratio of scar height relative to the flake thickness, is highly influenced by the morphology of the flake blank which limits the applicability of this reduction index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been shown that stages in the lithic reduction sequence may be misleading and that a better way to assess the data is by looking at it as a continuum. The assumptions that archaeologists sometimes make regarding the reduction sequence based on the placement of a flake into a stage can be unfounded. For example, a significant amount of cortex can be present on a flake taken off near the very end of the reduction sequence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Removed flakes exhibit features characteristic of conchoidal fracturing, including <a href="/wiki/Striking_platform" title="Striking platform">striking platforms</a>, bulbs of force, and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Eraillure" title="Eraillure">eraillures</a> (small secondary flakes detached from the flake's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulb_of_force" title="Bulb of force">bulb of force</a>). Flakes are often quite sharp, with distal edges only a few molecules thick when they have a feather termination. These flakes can be used directly as tools or modified into other utilitarian implements, such as <a href="/wiki/Spokeshave" title="Spokeshave">spokeshaves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scraper_(archaeology)" title="Scraper (archaeology)">scrapers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Percussion_reduction">Percussion reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Percussion reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Percussion reduction, or percussion flaking, refers to removal of flakes by impact. Generally, a <a href="/wiki/Lithic_core" title="Lithic core">core</a> or other objective piece, such as a partially formed tool, is held in one hand, and struck with a hammer or percussor. Alternatively, the objective piece can also be struck between a stationary <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a>-stone, known as bipolar percussion. Percussion can also be done by throwing the objective piece at an anvil stone. This is sometimes called projectile percussion. Percussors are traditionally either a stone cobble or pebble, often referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Hammerstone" title="Hammerstone">hammerstone</a>, or a billet made of bone, antler, or wood. Often, flakes are struck from a core using a punch, in which case the percussor never actually makes contact with the objective piece. This technique is referred to as indirect percussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projectile_percussion">Projectile percussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Projectile percussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Projectile percussion is so basic as to not be considered a technique. It involves throwing the toolstone at a stationary anvil stone. This method provides virtually no control over how the toolstone will fragment, and therefore produces a great deal of shatter, and few flakes. It is difficult to be sure whether or not this method of lithic reduction was ever a commonplace practice, although noting sharp edges on a broken rock might have led early man to first recognize the value of lithic reduction.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bipolar_percussion">Bipolar percussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bipolar percussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In bipolar percussion the objective piece of toolstone is placed on an anvil stone, and then the percussion force is applied to the tool stone. Like projectile percussion, the tool stone is likely to shatter, rather than producing a single flake. Unlike projectile percussion, the technique has some degree of control to it. Bipolar percussion is not popular with hobbyists, but there is evidence that bipolar percussion was the preferred way of dealing with certain problems. Bipolar percussion has the benefit of producing many sharp flakes, and triangular pieces of stone which can be useful as drills. Bipolar percussion also does not require the manufacturer to locate a platform before setting to work, and bipolar percussion can produce sharp flakes almost the size of the original piece of tool stone. The lack of control makes bipolar percussion undesirable in many situations, but the benefits mean that it often has a use, especially if workable material is rare. Bipolar percussion is often used to break open small cobbles, or to have a second chance with spent lithic cores, broken bifaces, and tools that have been reworked so much that it is impossible to make further useful tools using traditional lithic reduction. The end result of bipolar percussion is often a big mess, with only a few pieces that can be useful as cores or flakes for further working, but if other methods would result in a total dead-end, bipolar percussion may be desirable.</p>

<p>An alternative view of the bipolar reduction technique is offered by Jan Willem Van der Drift which contradicts the suggestion that there is little control over fracturing. The characteristics of bipolar reduction are different from that occurring in conchoidal fracture and are therefore often misinterpreted by archaeologists and lithic experts.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard-hammer_percussion">Hard-hammer percussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hard-hammer percussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Hard hammer techniques are generally used to remove large flakes of stone. Early flintknappers and hobbyists replicating their methods often use cobbles of very hard stone, such as <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a>. This technique can be used by flintknappers to remove broad flakes that can be made into smaller tools. This method of manufacture is believed to have been used to make some of the earliest stone tools ever found, some of which date from over 2 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is the use of hard-hammer percussion that most often results in the formation of the typical features of <a href="/wiki/Conchoidal_fracture" title="Conchoidal fracture">conchoidal fracture</a> on the detached flake, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulb_of_percussion" title="Bulb of percussion">bulb of percussion</a> and compression rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Soft-hammer_percussion">Soft-hammer percussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Soft-hammer percussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Soft-hammer percussion involves the use of a billet, usually made of wood, bone or antler as the percussor. These softer materials are easier to shape than stone hammers, and therefore can be made into more precise tools. Soft hammers also deform around the sharp edges of worked stone, rather than shattering through them, making it desirable for working tool stone that already has been worked to some degree before. Soft hammers of course also do not have as much force behind them as hard hammers do. Flakes produced by soft hammers are generally smaller and thinner than those produced by hard-hammer flaking; thus, soft-hammer flaking is often used after hard-hammer flaking in a lithic reduction sequence to do finer work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As well as this, soft-hammers can produce longer flakes which aid in the conservation of materials because they produce a longer cutting edge per unit of mass lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In most cases, the amount of pressure applied to the objective piece in soft-hammer percussion is not enough for the formation of a typical conchoidal fracture. Rather, soft-hammer flakes are most often produced by what is referred to as a bending fracture, so-called because the flake is quite literally bent or "peeled" from the objective piece. However, it should be noted that a bending fracture can be produced with a hard hammer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Flakes removed in this manner lack a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulb_of_percussion" title="Bulb of percussion">bulb of percussion</a>, and are distinguished instead by the presence of a small lip where the flake's striking platform has separated from the objective piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_percussion">Indirect percussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Indirect percussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Indirect percussion involves the use of a punch and hammer. The punch and hammer make it possible to apply large force to very small areas of a stone tool. Indirect percussion is therefore often used to achieve detail work on smaller tools. Some modern hobbyists make use of indirect percussion almost exclusively, with little or no pressure flaking to finish their work.</p>
<p>Since indirect percussion can be so precisely placed, the platform is often much smaller on flakes produced in this way than in other methods of flake removal. Of course, indirect percussion requires two hands to hold the percussing tool set. One holds the hammer, and one holds the punch. Therefore, modern hobbyists must use a third object in order to hold the targeted piece of tool stone while they strike it. Often, some sort of clamp or vise is used. No evidence for such devices has yet been found in the archaeological record, but this is partly because they would normally be made of perishable materials, and partly because they can have great variation in design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_flaking">Pressure flaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithic_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pressure flaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pressure_Flaking.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Pressure_Flaking.jpg/220px-Pressure_Flaking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Pressure_Flaking.jpg/330px-Pressure_Flaking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Pressure_Flaking.jpg/440px-Pressure_Flaking.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pressure_Flaking.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of pressure flaking</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pressure flaking is a method of trimming the edge of a <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tool</a> by removing small <a href="/wiki/Lithic_flake" title="Lithic flake">lithic flakes</a> by pressing on the stone with a sharp instrument rather than striking it with a percussor. This method, which often uses punches made from bone or antler tines (or, among modern hobbyists, copper punches or even nails), provides a greater means of controlling the direction and quantity of the applied force than when using even the most careful percussive flaking. Copper retoucheurs to facilitate this process were widely employed in the <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Early Bronze Age</a> – and may therefore be associated with <a href="/wiki/Beaker_culture" title="Beaker culture">Beaker Culture</a> in northwestern Europe.</p>
<p>Usually, the objective piece is held clasped in the flintknapper's hand, with a durable piece of fabric or leather protecting the flintknapper's palm from the sharpness of the flakes removed. The tip of the flaking tool is placed against the edge of the stone tool and pressed hard, removing a small linear or <a href="/wiki/Lunate" title="Lunate">lunate</a> flake from the opposite side. The process also involves frequent preparation of the edge to form better platforms for pressing off flakes. This is usually accomplished with abraiders made from a coarse-grained stone such as <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a>. Great care must be taken during pressure flaking so that <a href="/wiki/Termination_type" title="Termination type">perverse fractures</a> that break the entire tool do not occur. Occasionally, <i><a href="/wiki/Termination_type" title="Termination type">outrepasse</a></i> breaks occur when the force propagates across and through the tool in such a way that the entire opposite margin is removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of pressure flaking facilitated the early production of sharper and more finely detailed tools. Pressure flaking also gave toolmakers the ability to create notches where the objective piece could be bound more securely to the shaft of the <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a> and increasing the object's utility.</p>
<p>An archaeological discovery in 2010 in <a href="/wiki/Blombos_Cave" title="Blombos Cave">Blombos Cave</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, places the use of pressure flaking by early humans to make stone tools back to 73,000 BCE, 55,000 years earlier than previously accepted. The previously accepted date, "no more than 20,000 years ago",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-20101029_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-20101029-14">[14]</a></sup> was based upon the earliest evidence previously available, which derived from findings of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Solutrean" title="Solutrean">Solutrean</a> culture in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allafrica_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-allafrica-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>A blank is a thick, shaped stone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biface" title="Biface">biface</a> of suitable size and configuration for refining into a <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tool</a>. Blanks are the beginning products of lithic reduction, and during prehistoric times were often created for trade or later refinement at another location. Blanks were often formed through the initial reduction of lumps of <a href="/wiki/Tool_stone" title="Tool stone">tool stone</a> at simple <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarries</a>, often no more than easily accessible outcroppings of the local tool stone (although this was certainly not the case at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimes_Graves" title="Grimes Graves">Grimes Graves</a> in England). Sometimes the shape of the blank hints at the shape of the final tool it will become, but this is not always the case. A blank may consist of either a large, unmodified flake or a reduced core, often with a rough subtriangular or lanceolate shape. Rough chopping tools, derived by removing a few flakes along one edge of the cobble, can also be considered to fall into this group.</p>

<p>A preform is the rough, incomplete and unused basic form of a <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tool</a>. Typically, a preform is the shaped remnant of a <a href="/wiki/Lithic_core" title="Lithic core">lithic core</a>. Larger and thicker than the intended tool, it lacks the final trimming and refinement that is present in the completed <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifact</a>. Sometimes basic features such as stems and notches have been initiated. In most cases, the term refers to an incomplete <a href="/wiki/Projectile_point" title="Projectile point">projectile point</a>.</p>
