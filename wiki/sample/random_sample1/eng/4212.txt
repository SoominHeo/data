<p><i><b>Hot Fuzz</b></i> is a 2007 British <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirical</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Wright" title="Edgar Wright">Edgar Wright</a>, written by Wright and <a href="/wiki/Simon_Pegg" title="Simon Pegg">Simon Pegg</a>, and starring Pegg and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Frost" title="Nick Frost">Nick Frost</a>. The three and the film's producer <a href="/wiki/Nira_Park" title="Nira Park">Nira Park</a> had previously worked together on the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Spaced" title="Spaced">Spaced</a></i> and the 2004 film <i><a href="/wiki/Shaun_of_the_Dead" title="Shaun of the Dead">Shaun of the Dead</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The film follows two police officers attempting to solve a series of mysterious deaths in Sandford, a fictional small <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> town.</p>
<p>Over a hundred action films were used as inspiration for developing the script. Filming took place over eleven weeks in early 2006, and featured an extensive cast along with various uncredited cameos. Visual effects were developed by ten artists to expand on or add explosions, gore, and gunfire scenes.</p>
<p>Debuting on 14 February 2007 in the United Kingdom and 20 April in the United States, <i>Hot Fuzz</i> received critical and commercial success. Shortly after the film's release, two different soundtracks were released in the UK and US.</p>
<p>The film is the second in Wright and Pegg's <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Flavours_Cornetto_trilogy" title="Three Flavours Cornetto trilogy">Three Flavours Cornetto trilogy</a></i> and was preceded by 2004's <i>Shaun of the Dead</i> and followed by 2013's <i><a href="/wiki/The_World%27s_End_(film)" title="The World's End (film)">The World's End</a></i>, each of them featuring a different flavour of <a href="/wiki/Cornetto_(ice_cream)" title="Cornetto (ice cream)">Cornetto</a> ice cream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is also the most financially successful film in the trilogy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_Constable" title="Police Constable">Police Constable</a> Nicholas Angel, a high-achieving member of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police_Service" title="Metropolitan Police Service">Metropolitan Police Service</a>, is promoted to Sergeant. He is then transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a> of Sandford, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> for making his colleagues look bad by comparison.</p>
<p>The village is generally a model of peace, a serial winner of "Village of the Year" awards and devoid of any crime, with its local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighbourhood_Watch" title="Neighbourhood Watch">Neighbourhood Watch Alliance</a> (NWA) helping to keep order. To Angel's frustration, the local police service is lazy and complacent. He meets police officer Danny Butterman, the son of town <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Inspector" title="Chief Inspector">chief Inspector</a> Frank Butterman. Danny is assigned as Angel's new partner, and constantly discusses his love for <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddy_cop_film" title="Buddy cop film">buddy cop films</a>.</p>
<p>Angel and Danny attend a rendition of <i>Romeo and Juliet</i>, with its leading roles played by Martin Blower and Eve Draper, a disliked duo in a thinly-veiled extramarital affair. Backstage after the performance, Blower and Draper are murdered by a cloaked figure. The next morning, they are found decapitated along a roadside in Blower's convertible. Angel suspects foul play, but everyone else passes it off as an accident. Angel is called to solve a neighborhood argument, in the process discovering a large stash of unlicensed firearms (as well as a <a href="/wiki/Naval_mine" title="Naval mine">sea mine</a> which nearly blows up when the man, David Webley knocks it with the end of his gun but it doesn't do anything) in an elderly man's shed. Angel confiscates the stash in the police department's evidence room.</p>
<p>Danny invites Angel to a drink at the local bar, where Angel discusses how he can't stop thinking of work. They meet Simon Skinner, the local supermarket manager, and George Merchant, a wealthy drunkard who made a fortune selling kitchen goods. Danny takes Angel home and the two binge-watch action movies. That same evening, an unseen figure causes a gas explosion that destroys Merchant's mansion, killing him. The incident is deemed an accident and Angel is ridiculed for believing otherwise.</p>
<p>At the local fair, Tim Messenger, editor and journalist at the <i>Sandford Citizen</i> newspaper, approaches Angel, claiming to have information regarding Merchant, and requests to meet him alone besides the village church. Another unseen robed person topples a severed church spire onto Messenger's head, killing him. Angel declares the incident to be a murder and Inspector Butterman believes him. Angel reads through the copies of the <i>Sandford Citizen</i> and discovers a link between the victims.</p>
<p>Angel stops at the flower shop of Leslie Tiller, and learns that she intends to sell her shop and move to the city. As he is distracted, Tiller is murdered by a cloaked figure. Angel gives chase but fails to catch the murderer. Piecing together what he knows, he accuses Skinner of the murders. However, Skinner's alibi is backed up by video footage. Angel considers the possibility of more than one killer being involved, but Inspector Butterman shoots down his idea.</p>
<p>As Angel returns home, he is attacked by Michael Armstrong, one of Skinner's supermarket employees. Angel subdues Armstrong, then follows clues that leads him to a secret NWA meeting. Angel confronts the NWA and tries to arrest them. The group confesses to the murders, and explain their motive to remove less-than-model residents threatening Sandford's chances of winning "Village of the Year" again. After Inspector Butterman reveals himself as one of them, Angel flees and discovers the bodies of many victims, some recent and young. As the NWA corners Angel, Danny appears, and fakes Angel's murder. Danny begs him to leave the village for his own safety.</p>
<p>At a petrol station, Angel is inspired by the movies he watched with Danny, and returns to Sandford. He arms himself with the confiscated guns and reunites with Danny. A series of shootouts ensue between the two officers and various NWA members. After most of the group is dispatched, the two rally their colleagues and besiege the supermarket, forcing Skinner to flee with Inspector Butterman. After a car chase that finishes in a <a href="/wiki/Model_village" title="Model village">model village</a> of Sandford itself, Skinner and the Inspector are arrested.</p>
<p>Angel's former superiors beg him to return to London, as the crime rate has risen heavily in his absence, but Angel remains in Sandford. As the police officers process the paperwork, Tom Weaver, the last NWA member, who works in the station attempts to kill Angel. Danny takes a bullet for Angel, and in the process Weaver accidentally activates the confiscated sea mine, which destroys the station but kills no one. One year later, Angel and Danny are in charge of the Sandford Police as Inspector and Sergeant, respectively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wright wanted to write and direct a cop film because "there isn't really any tradition of cop films in the UK... We felt that every other country in the world had its own tradition of great cop action films and we had none."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYP-8">[8]</a></sup> Wright and Pegg spent eighteen months writing the script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gather_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gather-9">[9]</a></sup> The first draft took eight months to develop, and after watching 138 cop-related films for dialogue and plot ideas and conducting over fifty interviews with police officers for research, the script was completed after another nine months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gather_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gather-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFB_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BFB-10">[10]</a></sup> The title was based on the various two-word titles of action films in the 1980s and 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About.movie_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-About.movie-11">[11]</a></sup> In one interview Wright declared that he "wanted to make a title that really had very little meaning...like <i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon" title="Lethal Weapon">Lethal Weapon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Point_Break" title="Point Break">Point Break</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Executive_Decision" title="Executive Decision">Executive Decision</a></i>." In the same interview, Pegg joked that many action films' titles "seem to be generated from two hats filled with adjectives and nouns and you just, 'Okay, that'll do.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About.movie_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-About.movie-11">[11]</a></sup> While writing the script, the film's director and writer, Edgar Wright, as well as Pegg, intended to include Frost as the partner for Pegg's character. Frost revealed that he would do the film only if he could name his character, and he chose "Danny Butterman".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CinemaReview2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CinemaReview2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_filming">Preparation and filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Preparation and filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hotfuzzfilming.jpg"><img alt="A man in a purple jumpsuit is at the right of the image walking down a street. On the left is a man dressed as a police officer following him. At the far right is a man seated on the back of a golf cart filming them. Storefronts can be seen in the background." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="644" data-file-width="1280" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Hotfuzzfilming.jpg/220px-Hotfuzzfilming.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Hotfuzzfilming.jpg/330px-Hotfuzzfilming.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Hotfuzzfilming.jpg/440px-Hotfuzzfilming.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hotfuzzfilming.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>During the latter half of 2005, <a href="/wiki/Working_Title_Films" title="Working Title Films">Working Title</a> approached several towns in <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a> looking for an appropriate filming location. Pegg commented, "We're both [Pegg and Wright] from the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> so it just seemed like it was the perfect and logical thing to drag those kind of ideas and those genres and those clichés back to our beginnings to where we grew up, so you could see high-octane balls-to-the-wall action in <a href="/wiki/Frome" title="Frome">Frome</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-culture_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-culture-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stow-on-the-Wold" title="Stow-on-the-Wold">Stow-on-the-Wold</a> was considered amongst others, but after being turned away, the company settled upon <a href="/wiki/Wells,_Somerset" title="Wells, Somerset">Wells</a> in <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, Wright's hometown,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CotswoldJournal_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CotswoldJournal-14">[14]</a></sup> of which he has said "I love it but I also want to trash it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comedy_map_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-comedy_map-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wells_Cathedral" title="Wells Cathedral">Wells Cathedral</a> was digitally painted out of every shot of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_city" title="Cathedral city">cathedral city</a>, as Wright wanted the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Cuthbert,_Wells" title="Church of St Cuthbert, Wells">Church of St. Cuthbert</a> to be the centre building for the fictional town of Sandford;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotFuzzDVD_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-HotFuzzDVD-16">[16]</a></sup> however, the <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Palace,_Wells" title="Bishop's Palace, Wells">Bishop's Palace</a> is identifiable in some shots (and was itself used as the setting for some scenes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HFFilmLocations_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-HFFilmLocations-17">[17]</a></sup> While shooting scenes in their uniforms, Pegg and Frost were often mistaken for genuine police officers and asked for directions by passers-by.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Filming also took place at the <a href="/wiki/Hendon_Police_College" title="Hendon Police College">Hendon Police College</a>, including the driving school skid pan and athletic track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLG_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MLG-19">[19]</a></sup> Filming commenced on 19 March 2006 and lasted for eleven weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WTFilms_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-WTFilms-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Channel_4_Film_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Channel_4_Film-21">[21]</a></sup> After editing, Wright ended up cutting half an hour of footage from the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homage">Homage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Homage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wright has said that <i>Hot Fuzz</i> takes elements from his final amateur film, <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Right_(film)" title="Dead Right (film)">Dead Right</a></i>, which he described as both "<i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon" title="Lethal Weapon">Lethal Weapon</a></i> set in Somerset" and "a <i><a href="/wiki/Dirty_Harry" title="Dirty Harry">Dirty Harry</a></i> film in Somerset".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comedy_map_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-comedy_map-15">[15]</a></sup> He uses some of the same locations in both films, including the <a href="/wiki/Somerfield" title="Somerfield">Somerfield supermarket</a>, where he used to work as a shelf-stacker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comedy_map_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-comedy_map-15">[15]</a></sup> In the scene in the Somerfield store, when Angel is confronting a <a href="/wiki/Chav" title="Chav">chav</a> for <a href="/wiki/Shoplifting" title="Shoplifting">shoplifting</a>, a DVD copy of <i>Shaun of the Dead</i> can be seen for a few frames. The title is <i>Zombies' Party</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> title for the film.</p>
<p>Further homages to <i>Shaun of the Dead</i> are also present in the film. In one scene, Nicholas wants to chase a shoplifter by jumping over garden fences; however, Danny is reluctant. Nicholas says, "What's the matter, Danny? You never taken a shortcut before?" He smiles arrogantly before jumping over three in a row (according to the DVD commentary, Pegg vaulted over three fences, and a stunt man did a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back_flip" title="Back flip">back flip</a> over the fourth). When Danny attempts it, he trips and falls through the fence. This is almost identical to a scene in <i>Shaun of the Dead</i>, including the fall-through-fence gag (in <i>Shaun of the Dead</i>, however, it happens to Pegg's character rather than Frost's, and he falls over the fence rather than through it). The DVD commentary says that Frost purposely looked back at the camera after crashing through the fence, to show that he had done the stunt rather than someone else.</p>
<p>Frost's characters (Danny in <i>Hot Fuzz</i>, Ed in <i>Shaun of the Dead</i>) have a liking for <a href="/wiki/Cornetto_(ice_cream)" title="Cornetto (ice cream)">Cornettos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZombiesParty_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZombiesParty-23">[23]</a></sup> Pegg and Wright have referred to <i>Hot Fuzz</i> as being the second film in "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Flavours_Cornetto_Trilogy" title="Three Flavours Cornetto Trilogy">Three Flavours Cornetto Trilogy</a>" with <i><a href="/wiki/Shaun_of_the_Dead" title="Shaun of the Dead">Shaun of the Dead</a></i> as the first and <i><a href="/wiki/The_World%27s_End_(film)" title="The World's End (film)">The World's End</a></i> as the third.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BloodIce_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-BloodIce-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BloodIceSavour_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BloodIceSavour-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various scenes in <i>Hot Fuzz</i> feature a variety of action film DVDs such as <i><a href="/wiki/Police_Story_3:_Super_Cop" title="Police Story 3: Super Cop">Supercop</a></i> and scenes from <i><a href="/wiki/Point_Break" title="Point Break">Point Break</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Boys_II" title="Bad Boys II">Bad Boys II</a></i>. Wright revealed that he had to get permission from every actor in each video clip, including stunt men, to use the clips and for the use of the DVD covers had to pay for the rights from the respective studios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWEb_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWEb-26">[26]</a></sup> The film parodies clichés used in other action movies. On the topic of perceived gun fetishes in these movies, Pegg has said, "Men can't do that thing, which is the greatest achievement of humankind, which is to make another human, so we make metal versions of our own penises and fire more bits of metal out of the end into people's heads... It's our turn to grab the gun by the hilt and fire it into your face."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-culture_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-culture-13">[13]</a></sup> Despite this, Pegg maintains that the film is not a spoof, in that "They lack the sneer that a lot of parodies have that look down on their source material. Because we're looking up to it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWBrit_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-EWBrit-27">[27]</a></sup> The film also includes various references to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wicker_Man_(1973_film)" title="The Wicker Man (1973 film)">The Wicker Man</a></i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Edward_Woodward" title="Edward Woodward">Edward Woodward</a> had played a policeman tough on law and order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WickerMan_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-WickerMan-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To illustrate the destruction of the mansion as a result of the gas explosion, gas mortars were placed in front of the building to create large-scale fireballs. The wave of fire engulfs the camera, and to achieve that effect, gas mortars were used again but were fired upwards into a black ceiling piece that sloped up towards the camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VFX_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-VFX-29">[29]</a></sup> When the sequence was shot at a high speed, the flames appeared to surge across the ground. For one of the final scenes of the film, the Sandford police station is destroyed by an explosion. Part of the explosion was created by using a set model that showed its windows being blown out, while the building remained intact. The actual destruction of the building was depicted by exploding a miniature model of the station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotFuzzDVD_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-HotFuzzDVD-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar to the work in <i>Shaun of the Dead</i>, blood and gore was prevalent throughout the film. <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">Visual effects</a> supervisor Richard Briscoe revealed the rationale for using the large amounts of blood: "In many ways, the more extreme you make it, the more people know it is stylised and enjoy the humour inherent in how ridiculous it is. It's rather like the (eventually) limbless <a href="/wiki/Black_Knight_(Monty_Python)" title="Black Knight (Monty Python)">Black Knight</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="Monty Python and the Holy Grail">Monty Python's Holy Grail</a></i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VFX_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-VFX-29">[29]</a></sup> The most time-consuming gore sequence involved a character's head being crushed by a section of a church. A dummy was used against a <a href="/wiki/Chroma_key" title="Chroma key">green screen</a> and the head was detonated at the point when the object was about to impact the body. Throughout the film, over seventy gunfight shots were digitally augmented; Briscoe's rationale for adding the additional effects was that "The town square shootout, for example, is full of extra little hits scattered throughout, so that it feels like our hero characters really do have it all going off, all around them. It was a great demonstration of [how] seemingly very trivial enhancements can make a difference when combined across a sequence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VFX_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-VFX-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion">Promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first two <a href="/wiki/Trailer_(promotion)" title="Trailer (promotion)">teaser trailers</a> were released on 16 October 2006. Wright, Pegg, and Frost maintained several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_blogging" title="Video blogging">video blogs</a>, which were released at various times throughout the production of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoBlo_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-JoBlo-30">[30]</a></sup> Wright and Frost held a panel at the 2006 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">Comic-Con</a> convention in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> to promote <i>Hot Fuzz</i>, which included preliminary footage and a question and answer session.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoBlo2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-JoBlo2-31">[31]</a></sup> The two returned to the convention again in 2007 to promote the US DVD release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.26C_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.26C-32">[32]</a></sup> Advance screenings of the film took place on 14 February 2007 in the UK and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_premiere" title="Film premiere">world premiere</a> was on 16 February 2007. The premiere included escorts from motorcycle police officers and the use of blue carpet instead of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Red_carpet" title="Red carpet">red carpet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WTF_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-WTF-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a> website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reported a 91% approval rating with an average rating of 7.7/10 based on 199 reviews. The website's consensus reads, "The brilliant minds behind <i>Shaun of the Dead</i> successfully take a shot at the buddy cop genre with <i>Hot Fuzz</i>. The result is a bitingly satiric and hugely entertaining parody."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It also has a <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> score of 81/100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metacritic_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-35">[35]</a></sup> Olly Richards of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> praised the chemistry between Pegg and Frost, saying: "After almost a decade together, they're clearly so comfortable in each other's presence that they feel no need to fight for the punchline, making them terrific company for two hours".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Empire_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Empire-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Vaughan" title="Johnny Vaughan">Johnny Vaughan</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i> already called it the "most arresting Brit-com of 2007".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Sun_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sun-37">[37]</a></sup> Phillip French of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i>, who did not care for <i>Shaun of the Dead</i>, warmed to the comedy team in this film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Unlimited_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Unlimited-38">[38]</a></sup> The film also received positive reviews in the United States. Derek Elley of <i>Variety</i> praised Broadbent and Dalton as "especially good as Angel's <a href="/wiki/Hail_fellow_well_met" title="Hail fellow well met">hail-fellow-well-met</a> superior and oily No. 1 suspect".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variety_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Variety-39">[39]</a></sup> As an homage to the genre, the film was well received by <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a> <a href="/wiki/Shane_Black" title="Shane Black">Shane Black</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-22">[22]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Spill.com" title="Spill.com">Spill.com</a>, it got their 2nd-highest rating of 'Full Price!!'.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">The Daily Mirror</a></i> gave <i>Hot Fuzz</i> only 2/5, stating that "many of the jokes miss their target" as the film becomes more action-based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mirror_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mirror-40">[40]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> also shared <i>The Mirror'</i>s view, saying, "It's the lack of any serious intent that means too much of it is desperately unamusing, and unamusingly desperate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daily_Mail_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_Mail-41">[41]</a></sup> Anthony Quinn of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> said, "The same impish spirit [as in <i><a href="/wiki/Spaced" title="Spaced">Spaced</a></i>] is uncorked here, but it has been fatally indulged".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Independent_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Independent-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film generated £7.1 million in its first weekend of release in the United Kingdom on 14 February 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCNews_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCNews-43">[43]</a></sup> In the 20 April US opening weekend, the film grossed $5.8 million from only 825 cinemas, making it the highest per-cinema average of any film in the top ten that week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-4">[4]</a></sup> Its opening weekend take beat the $3.3 million opening weekend gross of Pegg and Wright's previous film, <i>Shaun of the Dead</i>. In its second weekend of release, <a href="/wiki/Rogue_(company)" title="Rogue (company)">Rogue Pictures</a> expanded the film's cinema count from 825 to 1,272 and it grossed $4.9 million, representing a 17% dip in the gross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM2nd_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM2nd-44">[44]</a></sup> Altogether, <i>Hot Fuzz</i> grossed $80,573,774 worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-4">[4]</a></sup> In nine weeks, the film earned nearly twice what <i>Shaun of the Dead</i> made in the US, and more than three times its gross in other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM2-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> was released on 11 June 2007 in the UK. Over one million DVDs were sold in the UK in the first four weeks of its release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cinematical_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cinematical-46">[46]</a></sup> The two-disc set contains the feature film with <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">commentaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outtake" title="Outtake">outtakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">storyboards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">deleted scenes</a>, a making-of documentary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_blogging" title="Video blogging">video blogs</a>, featurettes, galleries, and some hidden <a href="/wiki/Easter_egg_(media)" title="Easter egg (media)">easter eggs</a>. The DVD also features Wright's last amateur film, <i>Dead Right</i>, which he described as "<i>Hot Fuzz</i> without the budget". Due to the above release date, the film arrived on region 2 DVD earlier than the theatrical release date in Germany on 14 June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kino.de_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kino.de-47">[47]</a></sup> In the commentary with director Wright and fellow filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a>, they discuss nearly 200 films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tysto_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tysto-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The US DVD and <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">HD DVD</a> release was on 31 July 2007. It opened at #2 at the American DVD sales chart, selling 853,000 units for over $14m in revenue. As per the latest figures,1,923,000 units have been sold, acquiring revenue of $33.3 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The HD DVD edition has more special features than the standard DVD release. A three-disc collector's edition was released on 27 November 2007 and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> edition on 22 September 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BluRay_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-BluRay-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">Soundtrack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_Fuzz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hot_Fuzz_(soundtrack)" title="Hot Fuzz (soundtrack)">Hot Fuzz (soundtrack)</a></div>
<p>The soundtrack album, <i>Hot Fuzz: Music from the Motion Picture</i>, was released on 19 February 2007 in the United Kingdom, and on 17 April 2007 in the United States and Canada. The UK release contains 22 tracks, and the North American release has 14. The film's score is by British composer <a href="/wiki/David_Arnold" title="David Arnold">David Arnold</a>, who scored the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Bond_(film_series)" title="James Bond (film series)">James Bond film series</a> from 1997 to 2008. The soundtrack album's "Hot Fuzz Suite" is a compilation of excerpts from Arnold's score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-51">[51]</a></sup> According to the DVD commentary, the scenes where Nicholas Angel is at a convenience store, while leaving Sandford, and his return to the police station while arming for the final shootout (found in the track "Avenging Angel"), were scored by Robert Rodríguez, who did not see the rest of the film while writing the music.</p>
<p>Other music from the film is a mix of 1960s and 1970s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_rock" title="British rock">British rock</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">The Kinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/T._Rex_(band)" title="T. Rex (band)">T.Rex</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Move" title="The Move">The Move</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Sweet" title="The Sweet">Sweet</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Troggs" title="The Troggs">The Troggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Brown_(musician)" title="Arthur Brown (musician)">Arthur Brown</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_69&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cloud 69 (page does not exist)">Cloud 69</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cozy_Powell" title="Cozy Powell">Cozy Powell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a>), <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> (<a href="/wiki/Adam_Ant" title="Adam Ant">Adam Ant</a>, <a href="/wiki/XTC" title="XTC">XTC</a>) and a Glaswegian <a href="/wiki/Independent_music" title="Independent music">indie</a> band (<a href="/wiki/The_Fratellis" title="The Fratellis">The Fratellis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soundtrack_Collector_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soundtrack_Collector-52">[52]</a></sup> The soundtrack album features dialogue extracts by Pegg, Frost, and other cast members, mostly embedded in the music tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SoundtrackNet_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-SoundtrackNet-53">[53]</a></sup> The song selection also includes some police-themed titles, including <a href="/wiki/Supergrass" title="Supergrass">Supergrass</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Caught_by_the_Fuzz" title="Caught by the Fuzz">Caught by the Fuzz</a>" as well as "Here Come the Fuzz", which was specially composed for the film by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blues_Explosion" title="Blues Explosion">Jon Spencer's Blues Explosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HotFuzzDVD_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-HotFuzzDVD-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
