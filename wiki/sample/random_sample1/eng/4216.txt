<p><b>La Masia de Can Planes</b>, usually shortened to <b>La Masia</b>, (<small>Catalan pronunciation:¬†</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Catalan" title="Help:IPA for Catalan">[…´…ô m…ôÀàzi.…ô]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">"The Farmhouse"</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholastic1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-scholastic1-1">[1]</a></sup> is often used to generically describe the Barcelona <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Youth_academy" title="Youth academy">youth academy</a>. This academy holds more than 300 young players, and has been praised since 2002 as one of the best in the world, being a significant factor in FC Barcelona's European success as well as the Spanish national team's success at the <a href="/wiki/2010_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2010 FIFA World Cup">2010 FIFA World Cup</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2008" title="UEFA Euro 2008">2008</a> and <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2012" title="UEFA Euro 2012">2012 European Championships</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2010, La Masia became the first youth academy to have trained all three finalists for the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Ballon_d%27Or" title="FIFA Ballon d'Or">Ballon d'Or</a> in a single year, with <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Iniesta" title="Andr√©s Iniesta">Andr√©s Iniesta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Messi" title="Lionel Messi">Lionel Messi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xavi" title="Xavi">Xavi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3players_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-3players-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>La Masia is also the name of <a href="/wiki/FC_Barcelona" title="FC Barcelona">FC Barcelona</a>'s football training facilities, originally located near the <a href="/wiki/Camp_Nou" title="Camp Nou">Camp Nou</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Les_Corts" title="District of Les Corts">Les Corts</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>. The original building itself was an ancient country residence (In <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>: <a href="/wiki/Masia" title="Masia">masia</a>) built in 1702, and once Camp Nou was inaugurated in 1957, the building was remodelled and extended for use as the club's social headquarters. With the gradual expansion of the club, the building became too small for headquarters, and on 20 October 1979, La Masia was converted into a dormitory for young players from outside of Barcelona. On 30 June 2011, the Masia building ceased housing the young sportsmen who are trained to become a part of the club‚Äôs professional teams. In a simple ceremony, the doors were closed and the <a href="/wiki/Ciutat_Esportiva_Joan_Gamper" title="Ciutat Esportiva Joan Gamper">Ciutat Esportiva Joan Gamper</a> took over the function of the residential center for the youngsters.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Masia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johan_Cruijff_golfer_cropped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="485" data-file-width="400" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Johan_Cruijff_golfer_cropped.jpg/200px-Johan_Cruijff_golfer_cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Johan_Cruijff_golfer_cropped.jpg/300px-Johan_Cruijff_golfer_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/32/Johan_Cruijff_golfer_cropped.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

In 1979 <a href="/wiki/Johan_Cruyff" title="Johan Cruyff">Johan Cruyff</a> wanted to establish a copy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ajax_Academy" title="Ajax Academy">Ajax Academy</a> in Barcelona. His proposal was accepted by president <a href="/wiki/Josep_Llu%C3%ADs_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez" title="Josep Llu√≠s N√∫√±ez">Josep N√∫√±ez</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>La Masia de Can Planes was an old Catalan farmhouse, built in 1702. In 1979, it was first used by the club to house its young footballers who originated from outside of Barcelona.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1-3">[3]</a></sup> The idea for the youth academy was proposed to N√∫√±ez by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Cruyff" title="Johan Cruyff">Johan Cruyff</a>, and Oriol Tort was put in charge of the facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, it was announced that Barcelona would be moving all its football training activities to La <a href="/wiki/Ciutat_Esportiva_Joan_Gamper" title="Ciutat Esportiva Joan Gamper">Ciutat Esportiva Joan Gamper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>La Masia received more publicity after Barcelona B's success with homegrown players; Rory Smith reported in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> that La Masia "has replaced the fabled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ajax_Academy" title="Ajax Academy">Ajax Academy</a> as football's foremost production line".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph1-6">[6]</a></sup> The recent fame and success of La Masia as a talent school was ascribed by Ian Hawkey of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> to the class of 1987, which featured prominent members such as <a href="/wiki/Cesc_F%C3%A0bregas" title="Cesc F√†bregas">Cesc F√†bregas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Messi" title="Lionel Messi">Lionel Messi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Piqu%C3%A9" title="Gerard Piqu√©">Gerard Piqu√©</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedro_(footballer,_born_July_1987)" title="Pedro (footballer, born July 1987)">Pedro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Louis_van_Gaal" title="Louis van Gaal">Louis van Gaal</a>, coach of Barcelona's first team, was widely ridiculed by the city sports media for his dream to win the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">Champions League</a> with 11¬†home-grown players. The first team won the trophy in 2009 with eight home-grown players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sport1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sport1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1979 to 2009, 440¬†youngsters have left their homes and families to stay at the academy. About half of them were from <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, and the rest came from other regions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Spain" title="Kingdom of Spain">Kingdom of Spain</a> and beyond, including 15 from <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, 7 from <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, 5 from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and 3 from <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. Of those 440, 40¬†made it into Barcelona's first team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Masia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>La Masia houses about 60 players: 10 in the farmhouse, and the rest in rooms of the adjacent stadium; the rest of the youth players must provide for their own accommodation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-10">[10]</a></sup> The academy is one of the most expensive in Europe, operating at a cost of ¬£5¬†million a year. The main cost is the dormitory, La Masia itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-10">[10]</a></sup> The minimum age for the youth program is six years; each year, more than 1,000¬†boys from the ages of six to eight try out for admittance. The best 200 are selected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The club also actively seeks for prospective students; it employs a system in which 15¬†scouts are deployed in Catalonia, 15 in the rest of Spain and 10 scattered throughout the world. To alleviate the expenses of this scouting, the club has an agreement with 15 local clubs for them to train players who are not ready for entry into the youth academy. In return, FC Barcelona gives money, coaching and technical advice to these clubs for their services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcshane53_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcshane53-12">[12]</a></sup> While expanding its operations abroad, the club established five schools in Mexico and one in Egypt; successful applicants to these schools become full-time students, receiving academic education and football training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>When Guardiola re-organised the reserve side, he set up a three-staged program to formalise the advancement from Juvenil to Barcelona B and finally to the first team. The first stage of a youth player's career involves a rotation scheme with Barcelona B. The second stage involves making the player aware of his importance to the team and the expectation that the player will improve cohesion and performance within the reserve side. In the last stage, he is designated a "key" player of the B team and might be called to the first team. One of the players in the third phase is made captain, regardless of the experience of older players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sport1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-sport1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The teams at Barcelona play from August to May; mild weather at La baks allows players to train outdoors throughout the year. The youth teams train after school; Barcelona B plays as a professional team, training in the morning and evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> All of the trainers at FC Barcelona are former professional footballers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/FC_Barcelona_B" title="FC Barcelona B">Barcelona B</a>, the club's main youth team, and the 12 other youth teams contained 24 coaches and more than 300 players. There are 56 other employees, including doctors, psychologists, nutritionists, cooks and physiologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-10">[10]</a></sup> In the 2009‚Äì10 season, the B team qualified for the Segunda Divisi√≥n again. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Masia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Former technical director, Pep Segura, attributes the club's success to its "philosophy of play": "It is about creating one philosophy, one mentality, from the bottom of the club to the top". The philosophy consists of the application of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_football" title="Total football">total football</a> mixed with traditional Spanish one-touch play (<i>tiqui-taka</i>). The total football approach was derived from the Netherlands football team through Cruyff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph1-6">[6]</a></sup> The total football approach requires the players to move in a fluid formation, where players can interchange positions quickly. In the youth academy, there is a large focus on technical ability, which is seen as a pre-requisite for inter-changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> An often-quoted reason for Barcelona's success is the continuity and commitment with which Barcelona follow the current philosophy of pass and move. Guardiola was the prototype of the pivotal midfielder; famous midfielders Xavi and Iniesta are its custodians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another aspect of La Masia is its marked Catalan national character‚Äîlocal talent in the service of a club with a strong, defining sense of the cultural make-up of Catalonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesonline1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-timesonline1-22">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2009, Messi became the first player from La Masia to be awarded with the <a href="/wiki/Ballon_d%27Or" title="Ballon d'Or">Ballon d'Or</a> prize for the best footballer in Europe, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Player" title="FIFA World Player">FIFA World Player</a> award, for the best footballer in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>

Including <a href="/wiki/Cesc_F%C3%A0bregas" title="Cesc F√†bregas">Cesc F√†bregas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pepe_Reina" title="Pepe Reina">Pepe Reina</a>, there were a total of nine players from La Masia in this squad.
