<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">La Brugeoise et Nivelles</h1>

<p><b>La Brugeoise et Nivelles SA</b>, later <b>BN Constructions Ferroviaires et Métalliques</b> (abbrev. <b>BN</b>), was a Belgian manufacturer of railway locomotives and other rolling stock; it was formed by a merger of two companies: <i><b>La Brugeoise et Nicaise et Delcuve</b></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Ateliers_M%C3%A9tallurgiques_de_Nivelles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Les Ateliers Métallurgiques de Nivelles (page does not exist)">Les Ateliers Métallurgiques de Nivelles</a></i>.</p>
<p>The company was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Inc." title="Bombardier Inc.">Bombardier Inc.</a> in 1988, plants in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nivelles,_Belgium" title="Nivelles, Belgium">Nivelles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manage,_Belgium" title="Manage, Belgium">Manage</a> closed in 1989 and 2000; as of 2011, the plant located in Bruges operated as <i><b>Bombardier Transportation Belgium S.A.</b></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Brugeoise_et_Nivelles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In 1851, Joseph De Jaegher founded a hardware store in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Burg_(Bruges)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Burg (Bruges) (page does not exist)">Burg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>; in 1855, this expanded with a steel workshop on the <i>Raamstraat</i>, named <i>Ateliers J. Jaegher</i>; in 1891, this merged with another steel making company in the nearby <i>Gieterijstraat</i>, the <i>Usines Ferdinand Feldhaus</i>, to form the <i>Ateliers de Construction Forges et Aceries de Bruges</i>. By 1900, the company was a major Belgian metal engineering company. In 1905, the company moved its plant and offices to a larger site with good railway connections close to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ghent_Ostend_canal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ghent Ostend canal (page does not exist)">Ghent Ostend canal</a> at <a href="/wiki/Sint-Michiels" title="Sint-Michiels">Sint-Michiels</a> in Bruges. Until 1913, the company operated as the <i>Société Anonyme La Brugeoise</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beu_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-beu-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> abbreviated as <i>"La Brugeoise"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-les_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-les-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1913, the company <i>La Brugeoise et Nicaise et Delcuve</i> was formed by the merger of the Bruges-based companies <i>La Brugeoise</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nicaise_et_Delcuve" title="Nicaise et Delcuve">Nicaise et Delcuve</a></i> during a re-organisation of the interests of the holding company <i>Trust Métallurgique Belge-Français</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-amb-4">[4]</a></sup> and capitalised at 10 million francs; the new company included a modern steel works, forge and mills at <a href="/wiki/Sint-Michiels" title="Sint-Michiels">Sint-Michiels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amb_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-amb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the facilities were occupied by German forces, and at the end of the war had been substantially damaged,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beu_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-beu-1">[1]</a></sup> however post war construction also required the organisation products. In 1919, control of the company was taken by the <i><a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_G%C3%A9n%C3%A9rale_de_Belgique" title="Société Générale de Belgique">Société Générale de Belgique</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beu_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-beu-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1956, this merged with <i>Les Ateliers Métallurgiques de Nivelles</i> to form <i>La Brugeoise et Nivelles</i>. In 1977, the company merged with <i>Constructions Ferroviaries du Centre</i> (CFC) (in Familleureux, <a href="/wiki/Hainaut_(province)" title="Hainaut (province)">Hainaut</a>, Belgium) to form <i>BN Constructions Ferroviaries et Métalliques</i> (BN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, Bombardier took at 45% share in BN, which was increased to 90.6% in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The plants at Bruges and Manage became the BN division of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Eurorail&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bombardier Eurorail (page does not exist)">Bombardier Eurorail</a> in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The factory in Nivelles closed and was demolished in 1989/90.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, Bombardier announced it was to close the subsidiary plant <i>BN Manage</i> based in <a href="/wiki/Manage,_Belgium" title="Manage, Belgium">Manage</a>, Belgium; the action attracted criticism from both <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a> and the Belgian government; perception was that Bombardier had used the 'jobs card' to win a Belgian double deck train contract worth 8.5 billion Belgian francs. The closure announcement came as a complete <i><a href="/wiki/Volte-face" title="Volte-face">volte-face</a></i> from Bombardier's previous statements which included optimistic statements about the Manage plant's future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Train-making ended at the site which was re-purposed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Duferco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Duferco (page does not exist)">Duferco</a> for steel plate processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2011, the factory in <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> was part of <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Transportation" title="Bombardier Transportation">Bombardier Transportation</a> as <i>Bombardier Transportation Belgium S.A.</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Brugeoise_et_Nivelles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1875, the Manage plant produced its first rail vehicles; in 1885, the Bruges plant produced its first tram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-les_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-les-3">[3]</a></sup> Much of the early output prior to that was freight wagons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beu_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-beu-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition to railway rolling stock, the company also manufactured bridges, locks and sluice gates, cranes, and vessels for the chemical and sugar industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beu_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-beu-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961, the company acquired a license (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_Anglo-Franco-Belge" title="Société Anglo-Franco-Belge">Anglo-Franco-Belge</a>) to manufacture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GM-EMD" title="GM-EMD">GM-EMD</a> locomotives for the European market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[note 1]</a></sup> In 1973, factories in Bruges and Familleureux produced the company's first underground trains, for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STIB" title="STIB">STIB</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-les_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-les-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>BN produced 26 <i>veículos Leves sobre trilhos (VLT)</i> <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> cars for use on <a href="/wiki/Line_2_(Rio_de_Janeiro)" title="Line 2 (Rio de Janeiro)">Line 2</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_Metro" title="Rio de Janeiro Metro">Rio de Janeiro Metro</a>, which were later converted to full metro cars. Eight cars (delivered 1979) were built in Belgium and the remaining 18 (delivered 1980 and 1982) by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cobrasma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cobrasma (page does not exist)">Cobrasma</a> SA in their plant in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> Some of the ones built by Cobrasma were briefly used by the <a href="/wiki/Campinas#Campinas_Light_Rail" title="Campinas">Campinas Light Rail</a> system.</p>
<p>The original "Type 1" cars for <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/MAX_Light_Rail" title="MAX Light Rail">MAX Light Rail</a> system, ordered in 1981, were built by BN in association with <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Transportation" title="Bombardier Transportation">Bombardier Inc.</a>. They were assembled in North America using bodyshells sourced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Pocatiere" title="La Pocatiere">La Pocatiere</a>, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, a consortium of BN, <a href="/wiki/ANF_Industrie" title="ANF Industrie">ANF Industrie</a> and Bombardier won the order for the construction of the passenger <a href="/wiki/Eurotunnel_Shuttle" title="Eurotunnel Shuttle">Eurotunnel Shuttle</a> wagons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> valued at 36 billion francs, of which BN's contribution was valued at 8 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, the company, in association with <a href="/wiki/Holec" title="Holec">Holec</a>, built commuter trains for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KRL_Jabotabek" title="KRL Jabotabek">KRL Jabotabek</a> system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1998, after takeover by Bombardier, the company obtained an order with a total value of 22.2 billion Belgian francs to produce 78 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_Voyager" title="Bombardier Voyager">Bombardier Voyager</a> class trains for the railways in the United Kingdom at the Bruges and Manage plants; BN's value share of the contract was estimated at 8 to 10 billion Belgian francs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Deliveries took place in the early 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-b1-6">[6]</a></sup> Also as part of Bombardier, the company also produced <a href="/wiki/Flexity_Outlook" title="Flexity Outlook">Flexity Outlook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flexity_Swift" title="Flexity Swift">Flexity Swift</a> vehicles for Brussels and Rotterdam respectively, the bodyshells of the <a href="/wiki/Autorail_%C3%A0_grande_capacit%C3%A9" title="Autorail à grande capacité">Autorail à grande capacité</a> for the <a href="/wiki/SNCF" title="SNCF">SNCF</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Light_Railway_rolling_stock" title="Docklands Light Railway rolling stock">B09</a> vehicles for the <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Light_Railway" title="Docklands Light Railway">Docklands Light Railway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-b1-6">[6]</a></sup> Finally, the company continues to supply the Belgian Railways (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMBS/SNCB" title="NMBS/SNCB">NMBS/SNCB</a>) with passenger rail vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-b1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
