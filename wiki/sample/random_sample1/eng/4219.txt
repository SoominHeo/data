<p><i><b>La traviata</b></i> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[la traviˈaːta; traˈvjaːta]</a></span>, <i><b>The Fallen Woman</b></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Traviata_in_dictionary_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Traviata_in_dictionary-2">[2]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> in three acts by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Giuseppe Verdi</a> set to an Italian <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> by <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Maria_Piave" title="Francesco Maria Piave">Francesco Maria Piave</a>. It is based on <i><a href="/wiki/La_Dame_aux_Cam%C3%A9lias" title="La Dame aux Camélias">La Dame aux Camélias</a></i> (1852), a play adapted from the novel by <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas,_fils" title="Alexandre Dumas, fils">Alexandre Dumas, fils</a>. The opera was originally titled <i>Violetta</i>, after the main character. It was first performed on 6 March 1853 at the <a href="/wiki/La_Fenice" title="La Fenice">La Fenice</a> opera house in Venice.</p>
<p>Piave and Verdi wanted to follow Dumas in giving the opera a contemporary setting, but the authorities at La Fenice insisted that it be set in the past, "c. 1700". It was not until the 1880s that the composer and librettist's original wishes were carried out and "realistic" productions were staged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_history">Composition history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For Verdi, the years 1851 to 1853 were filled with operatic activity. First, he had agreed with the librettist <a href="/wiki/Salvadore_Cammarano" title="Salvadore Cammarano">Salvadore Cammarano</a> on a subject for what would become <i><a href="/wiki/Il_trovatore" title="Il trovatore">Il trovatore</a></i>, but work on this opera could not proceed while the composer was writing <i><a href="/wiki/Rigoletto" title="Rigoletto">Rigoletto</a></i>, which premiered in Venice in March 1851. In addition, personal affairs in his home town limited his activities that spring, but after <i>Rigoletto</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s success in Venice, an additional commission was offered by Brenna, the secretary of La Fenice. After Verdi's return from Paris a contract was signed in May 1852, with performances scheduled for March 1853, although no subject was chosen at that time.</p>
<p><b>Verdi sees <i>The Lady of the Camellias</i> play</b></p>
<p>Verdi and <a href="/wiki/Giuseppina_Strepponi" title="Giuseppina Strepponi">Giuseppina Strepponi</a> had visited Paris from late 1851 and into March 1852. In February the couple attended a performance of <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas,_fils" title="Alexandre Dumas, fils">Alexander Dumas</a> <i>fils'</i>s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lady_of_the_Camellias" title="The Lady of the Camellias">The Lady of the Camellias</a></i>. As a result of this, Verdi biographer <a href="/wiki/Mary_Jane_Phillips-Matz" title="Mary Jane Phillips-Matz">Mary Jane Phillips-Matz</a> reports, the composer immediately began to compose music for what would later become <i>La traviata</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Julian_Budden" title="Julian Budden">Julian Budden</a> notes that Verdi had probably read the Dumas novel some time before and, after seeing the play and returning to Italy, "he was already setting up an ideal operatic cast for it in his mind,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> shown by his dealings with La Fenice. On their return to Italy, the composer had immediately set to work on <i>Trovatore</i> for the January 1853 premiere in Rome, but at the same time seemed to have ideas for the music for <i>Traviata</i> in his head.</p>
<p><b>Composing for Venice</b></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Francesco_Maria_Piave" title="Francesco Maria Piave">Francesco Maria Piave</a> was to be engaged to write the new libretto and the two men tried to come up with a suitable subject, but the composer complained that his librettist "had not yet offered him an 'original' or 'provocative' idea". Writing to Piave, he added that "I don't want any of those everyday subjects that one can find by the hundreds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> But at the same time, the composer expressed concern about censorship in Venice, something with which he was very familiar after his dealings with the censors concerning <i>Rigoletto</i>. As the months dragged on into October, it was agreed that Piave would come to Sant'Agata and work with the composer. One subject was chosen, Piave set to work, and then Verdi threw in another idea, which may have been <i>La traviata</i>. However, within a short time, a synopsis was dispatched to Venice under the title of <i>Amore e morte</i> (<i>Love and Death</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, as Budden reveals, Verdi writes to his friend De Sanctis telling him that "for Venice I'm doing <i>La Dame aux camélias</i> which will probably be called <i>La traviata</i>. A subject for our own age."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Although still bogged down at Sant'Agata, Piave was sanguine: "Everything will turn out fine, and we'll have a new masterpiece from this true wizard of modern harmonies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PM-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>When back at Sant'Agata in late January 1853 Verdi was reminded that his contract called for him to be in Venice within a week or two and for the premiere to be held on the "first Saturday in March 1853".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, it soon became clear that a modern-dress staging of the new opera was impossible—the requirement was that it should be set in the 17th century "in the era of <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu" title="Cardinal Richelieu">Richelieu</a>"—and reports from the opening of the season confirmed the limitations of the chosen soprano, the 38-year-old <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Salvini-Donatelli" title="Fanny Salvini-Donatelli">Fanny Salvini-Donatelli</a> for taking the role of Violetta. Verdi was distraught, for he held on to the notion that the opera could be staged in modern dress—as <i><a href="/wiki/Stiffelio" title="Stiffelio">Stiffelio</a></i> had been done—Piave was sent back to Sant'Agata to no avail: he could not persuade the composer to back down on his insistence that another soprano be secured, yet the 15 January deadline for securing one had come and gone. Verdi was filled with premonitions of disaster upon his arrival in Venice on 21 February for rehearsals and he made his unhappiness clear to the singers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-PM-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_history">Performance history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Performance history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>19th century</b></p>

<p>The audience jeered at times during the premiere, directing some of their scorn at the casting of soprano Fanny Salvini-Donatelli in the lead role of Violetta. Though she was an acclaimed singer, they considered her to be too old (at 38) and too overweight to credibly play a young woman dying of consumption. (Verdi had previously attempted to persuade the manager of La Fenice to re-cast the role with a younger woman, but with no success.) Nevertheless, the first act was met with applause and cheering at the end; but in the second act, the audience began to turn against the performance, especially after the singing of the <a href="/wiki/Baritone" title="Baritone">baritone</a> <a href="/wiki/Felice_Varesi" title="Felice Varesi">Felice Varesi</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tenor" title="Tenor">tenor</a> <a href="/wiki/Lodovico_Graziani" title="Lodovico Graziani">Lodovico Graziani</a>. The next day, Verdi wrote to his friend <a href="/wiki/Emanuele_Muzio" title="Emanuele Muzio">Emanuele Muzio</a> in what has now become perhaps his most famous letter: "<i>La traviata</i> last night a failure. Was the fault mine or the singers'? Time will tell."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coincidentally, as Philips-Matz points out, an Italian translation of the play <i>La Dame aux camélias</i> was being presented just a short distance from La Fenice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-PM-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>While there were demands for productions from impresarios in various Italian cities, Verdi was loath to allow them unless he could be sure of the strength of the singers, and in spite of their pleas, the composer refused. As Budden notes, it came to be Venice "that made an honest woman of Violetta" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> when Verdi allowed a performance at the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_San_Benedetto" title="Teatro San Benedetto">Teatro San Benedetto</a>. Some revisions took place between 1853 and May 1854, mostly affecting acts 2 and 3, but the opera was given again on 6 May 1854 and was a great success, largely due to <a href="/wiki/Maria_Spezia-Aldighieri" title="Maria Spezia-Aldighieri">Maria Spezia-Aldighieri</a>'s portrayal of Violetta. "Then [referring to the La Fenice performances] it was a fiasco; now it has created a furore. Draw your own conclusions!" reported Piave (who had overseen the production in Verdi's absence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The opera (in the revised version) was first performed in Madrid on 1 February 1855 with <a href="/wiki/Maria_Spezia-Aldighieri" title="Maria Spezia-Aldighieri">Maria Spezia-Aldighieri</a> at <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Real" title="Teatro Real">Teatro Real</a>, in Vienna on 4 May 1855 in Italian and in Barcelona on 25 October at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gran_Teatre_del_Liceu" title="Gran Teatre del Liceu">Gran Teatre del Liceu</a>. It was first performed in England on 24 May 1856 in Italian at <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Theatre" title="Her Majesty's Theatre">Her Majesty's Theatre</a> in London,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loewenberg_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loewenberg-14">[14]</a></sup> where it was considered morally questionable, and "the heads of the Church did their best to put an injunction upon performance; the Queen refrained from visiting the theatre during the performances, though the music, words and all, were not unheard at the palace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was first performed in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/Max_Maretzek_Italian_Opera_Company" title="Max Maretzek Italian Opera Company">Max Maretzek Italian Opera Company</a> on 3 December 1856 in Italian at the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Music_(New_York_City)" title="Academy of Music (New York City)">Academy of Music</a> in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Templeton_Strong" title="George Templeton Strong">George Templeton Strong</a> noted in his diary: "People say the plot's immoral, but I don't see that it's so much worse than many others, not to speak of <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">Don Giovanni</a></i>, which as put on the stage is little but rampant lechery",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> while the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">Evening Post</a></i> critic wrote: "Those who have quietly sat through the glaring improprieties of <i>Don Giovanni</i> will hardly blush or frown at anything in <i>La traviata</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The opera was first performed in France on 6 December 1856 in Italian by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2tre-Italien" title="Théâtre-Italien">Théâtre-Italien</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Salle_Ventadour" title="Salle Ventadour">Salle Ventadour</a> in Paris,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loewenberg_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loewenberg-14">[14]</a></sup> and on 27 October 1864 in French as <i>Violetta</i> (an adaptation by Édouard Duprez, older brother of the tenor <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Duprez" title="Gilbert Duprez">Gilbert Duprez</a>) at the <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2tre_Lyrique" title="Théâtre Lyrique">Théâtre Lyrique</a> on the Place du Châtelet with <a href="/wiki/Christina_Nilsson" title="Christina Nilsson">Christina Nilsson</a> in the title role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The French adaptation of the libretto was published in 1865.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>20th century and beyond</b></p>
<p>Today, the opera has become immensely popular and is a staple of the standard operatic repertoire. As of 2012/13 season, it was in first place on the <a href="/wiki/Operabase" title="Operabase">Operabase</a> list of the most-performed operas worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dd>Place: Paris and its vicinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Time: Beginning of the 19th century</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_1">Act 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Act 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The salon in Violetta's house</i></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carl_d%27Unker_(attr)_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3511" data-file-width="4720" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Carl_d%27Unker_%28attr%29_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg/300px-Carl_d%27Unker_%28attr%29_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Carl_d%27Unker_%28attr%29_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg/450px-Carl_d%27Unker_%28attr%29_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Carl_d%27Unker_%28attr%29_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg/600px-Carl_d%27Unker_%28attr%29_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carl_d%27Unker_(attr)_La_Traviata_Eklat_am_Spieltisch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Scene 1: Party (attrib. <a href="/wiki/Carl_d%27Unker" title="Carl d'Unker">Carl d'Unker</a>)</div>

<p>Violetta Valéry, a famed <a href="/wiki/Courtesan" title="Courtesan">courtesan</a>, throws a lavish party at her Paris <a href="/wiki/Salon_(gathering)" title="Salon (gathering)">salon</a> to celebrate her recovery from an illness. Gastone, a <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscount</a>, has brought with him a friend, Alfredo Germont, a young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourgeois" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> from a provincial family who has long adored Violetta from afar. While walking to the salon, Gastone tells Violetta that Alfredo loves her, and that while she was ill, he came to her house every day. Alfredo joins them, admitting the truth of Gastone's remarks.</p>
<p>Baron Douphol, Violetta's current lover, waits nearby to escort her to the salon; once there, the Baron is asked to give a toast, but refuses, and the crowd turns to Alfredo, who agrees to sing a <a href="/wiki/Brindisi_(music)" title="Brindisi (music)">brindisi</a> – a drinking song (Alfredo, Violetta, chorus: <i><a href="/wiki/Libiamo_ne%27_lieti_calici" title="Libiamo ne' lieti calici">Libiamo ne' lieti calici</a></i> – "Drink from the joyful cup").</p>
<p>From the next room, the sound of the orchestra is heard and the guests move there to dance. After a series of severe coughing and almost fainting, feeling dizzy, Violetta asks her guests to go ahead and to leave her to rest until she recovers. While the guests dance in the next room, Violetta looks at her pale face in her mirror. Alfredo enters and expresses his concern for her fragile health, later declaring his love for her (Alfredo, Violetta: <i><a href="/wiki/Un_d%C3%AC,_felice,_eterea" title="Un dì, felice, eterea">Un dì, felice, eterea</a></i> – "One day, happy and ethereal"). At first she rejects him because his love means nothing to her, but there is something about Alfredo that touches her heart. He is about to leave when she gives him a flower, telling him to return it when it has wilted. She promises to meet him the next day.</p>

<p>After the guests leave, Violetta wonders if Alfredo could actually be the one in her life (Violetta: <i>È strano! ... Ah, fors'è lui</i> – "Ah, perhaps he is the one"). But she concludes that she needs freedom to live her life (Violetta, Alfredo: <i>Sempre libera</i> – "Always free"). From off stage, Alfredo's voice is heard singing about love as he walks down the street.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_2">Act 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Act 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Scene 1: Violetta's country house outside Paris</i></p>
<p>Three months later, Alfredo and Violetta are living together in a peaceful country house outside Paris. Violetta has fallen in love with Alfredo and she has completely abandoned her former life. Alfredo sings of their happy life together (Alfredo: <i>De' miei bollenti spiriti / Il giovanile ardore</i> – "The youthful ardor of my ebullient spirits"). Annina, the maid, arrives from Paris, and, when questioned by Alfredo, tells him that she went there to sell the horses, carriages and everything owned by Violetta to support their country lifestyle.</p>
<p>Alfredo is shocked to learn this and leaves for Paris immediately to settle matters himself. Violetta returns home and receives an invitation from her friend, Flora, to a party in Paris that evening. Alfredo's father, Giorgio Germont, is announced and demands that she break off her relationship with his son for the sake of his family, since he reveals that Violetta's relationship with Alfredo has threatened his daughter's engagement (Giorgio: <i>Pura siccome un angelo Iddio mi diè una figlia</i> – "Pure as an angel, God gave me a daughter") because of Violetta's reputation. Meanwhile, he reluctantly becomes impressed by Violetta's nobility, something which he did not expect from a courtesan. She responds that she cannot end the relationship because she loves him so much, but Giorgio pleads with her for the sake of his family. With growing remorse, she finally agrees (Violetta, Giorgio: <i>Dite alla giovine, sì bella e pura,</i> – "Tell the young girl, so beautiful and pure,") and says goodbye to Giorgio. In a gesture of gratitude for her kindness and sacrifice, Giorgio kisses her forehead before leaving her weeping alone.</p>
<p>Violetta gives a note to Annina to send to Flora accepting the party invitation and, as she is writing a farewell letter to Alfredo, he enters. She can barely control her sadness and tears; she tells him repeatedly of her unconditional love (Violetta: <i>Amami, Alfredo, amami quant'io t'amo</i> – "Love me, Alfredo, love me as I love you"). Before rushing out and setting off for Paris, she hands the farewell letter to her servant to give to Alfredo.</p>
<p>Soon, the servant brings the letter to Alfredo and, as soon as he has read it, Giorgio returns and attempts to comfort his son, reminding him of his family in Provence (Giorgio: <i>Di Provenza il mar, il suol chi dal cor ti cancellò?</i> – "Who erased the sea, the land of Provence from your heart?"). Alfredo suspects that the Baron is behind his separation with Violetta, and the party invitation, which he finds on the desk, strengthens his suspicions. He determines to confront Violetta at the party. Giorgio tries to stop Alfredo, but he rushes out.</p>
<p><i>Scene 2: Party at Flora's house</i></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaTraviata.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="386" data-file-width="602" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/LaTraviata.jpg/300px-LaTraviata.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/LaTraviata.jpg/450px-LaTraviata.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/LaTraviata.jpg/600px-LaTraviata.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaTraviata.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Act 2, scene 2 from <a href="/wiki/Fife_Opera" title="Fife Opera">Fife Opera</a>'s 2004 production</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the party, the Marquis tells Flora that Violetta and Alfredo have separated, much to the amazement of everyone who had previously seen the happy couple. She calls for the entertainers to perform for the guests (Chorus: <i>Noi siamo zingarelle venute da lontano</i> – "We are gypsy girls who have come from afar"; <i>Di Madride noi siam mattadori</i> – "We are matadors from Madrid"). Gastone and his friends join the matadors and sing (Gastone, chorus, dancers: <i>È Piquillo un bel gagliardo Biscaglino mattador</i> – "Piquillo is a bold and handsome matador from Biscay").</p>
<p>Violetta arrives with Baron Douphol. They see Alfredo at the gambling table. When he sees them, Alfredo loudly proclaims that he will take Violetta home with him. Feeling annoyed, the Baron goes to the gambling table and joins him in a game. As they bet, Alfredo wins some large sums until Flora announces that supper is ready. Alfredo leaves with handfuls of money.</p>
<p>As everyone is leaving the room, Violetta has asked Alfredo to see her. Fearing that the Baron's anger will lead him to challenge Alfredo to a duel, she gently asks Alfredo to leave. Alfredo misunderstands her apprehension and demands that she admit that she loves the Baron. In grief, she makes that admission and, furiously, Alfredo calls the guests to witness what he has to say (<i>Questa donna conoscete?</i> – "You know this woman?"). He humiliates and denounces Violetta in front of the guests and then throws his winnings at her feet in payment for her services. She faints onto the floor. The guests reprimand Alfredo: <i>Di donne ignobile insultatore, di qua allontanati, ne desti orror!</i> ("Ignoble insulter of women, go away from here, you fill us with horror!").</p>
<p>In search of his son, Giorgio enters the hall and, knowing the real significance of the scene, denounces his son's behavior (Giorgio, Alfredo, Violetta, chorus: <i>Di sprezzo degno sè stesso rende chi pur nell'ira la donna offende.</i> – "A man, who even in anger, offends a woman renders himself deserving of contempt.").</p>
<p>Flora and the ladies attempt to persuade Violetta to leave the dining room, but Violetta turns to Alfredo: <i>Alfredo, Alfredo, di questo core non puoi comprendere tutto l'amore...</i> – "Alfredo, Alfredo, you can't understand all the love in this heart...".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_3">Act 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Act 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Violetta's bedroom</i></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giuseppe_Verdi,_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2916" data-file-width="3356" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Giuseppe_Verdi%2C_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg/300px-Giuseppe_Verdi%2C_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Giuseppe_Verdi%2C_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg/450px-Giuseppe_Verdi%2C_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Giuseppe_Verdi%2C_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg/600px-Giuseppe_Verdi%2C_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giuseppe_Verdi,_La_traviata_title_page_-_Restoration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of a circa 1855 vocal score with an engraving by Leopoldo Ratti</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dr. Grenvil tells Annina that Violetta will not live long since her tuberculosis has worsened. Alone in her room, Violetta reads a letter from Alfredo's father telling her that the Baron was only wounded in his duel with Alfredo; that he has informed Alfredo of the sacrifice she has made for him and his sister; and that he is sending his son to see her as quickly as possible to ask for her forgiveness. But Violetta senses it is too late (Violetta: <i>Addio, del passato bei sogni ridenti</i> – "Farewell, lovely, happy dreams of the past").</p>
<p>Annina rushes in the room to tell Violetta of Alfredo's arrival. The lovers are reunited and Alfredo suggests that they leave Paris (Alfredo, Violetta: <i>Parigi, o cara, noi lasceremo</i> – "We will leave Paris, O beloved").</p>
<p>But it is too late: she knows her time is up (Alfredo, Violetta: <i>Gran Dio!...morir sì giovane</i> – "Great God!...to die so young"). Alfredo's father enters with the doctor, regretting what he has done. After singing a duet with Alfredo, Violetta suddenly revives, exclaiming that the pain and discomfort have left her. A moment later, she dies in Alfredo's arms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The opera uses an <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> with the following <a href="/wiki/Instrumentation_(music)" title="Instrumentation (music)">instrumentation</a>: 2 <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flutes</a> (fl. 2 doubling <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>), 2 <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, 4 <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">horns</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>, 3 <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cimbasso" title="Cimbasso">cimbasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cymbal" title="Cymbal">cymbals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">strings</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Banda_(opera)" title="Banda (opera)">Banda</a>: 2 piccolos, A-flat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piccolo_clarinet" title="Piccolo clarinet">piccolo clarinet</a>, <a href="/wiki/E-flat_clarinet" title="E-flat clarinet">E-flat clarinet</a>, 2 B-flat clarinets, 2 horns, <a href="/wiki/Flugelhorn" title="Flugelhorn">flugelhorn</a>, 3 trumpets, 2 trombones, <a href="/wiki/Tambourine" title="Tambourine">tambourine</a>, bass drum, <a href="/wiki/Castanets" title="Castanets">castanets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_based_on_the_opera">Music based on the opera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_traviata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Music based on the opera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Donato_Lovreglio" title="Donato Lovreglio">Donato Lovreglio</a> (1841–1907), an Italian flautist and composer wrote the "Concert Fantasy on themes from Verdi's <i>La traviata</i>", Op. 45, for clarinet and orchestra; in it, Lovreglio used the overture and several arias from the opera.</li>
<li>The Spanish composer <a href="/wiki/Francisco_T%C3%A1rrega" title="Francisco Tárrega">Francisco Tárrega</a> (1852–1909) wrote a "Fantasía sobre motivos de <i>La traviata</i>." The arrangement opens with a paraphrase of the opera's prelude. The rest of the work consists of arrangements of Violetta's three arias, each featuring special techniques of the guitar. <i>Addio, del passato bei sogni ridenti</i> features a brief tremolo passage; <i>Ah fors'è lui</i> is in harmonics; and the closing arrangement of <i>Sempre libera</i> features fast scales that suggest a new genre of "coloratura guitar."</li>
</ul>
