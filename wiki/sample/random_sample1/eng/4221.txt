<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, <b>Ragnarök</b> is a series of future events, including a great battle, foretold to ultimately result in the death of a number of major figures (including the <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir" title="Æsir">gods</a> <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>, <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BDr" title="Týr">Týr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heimdallr" title="Heimdallr">Heimdallr</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">Loki</a>), the occurrence of various natural disasters, and the subsequent submersion of the world in water. Afterward, the world will resurface anew and fertile, the surviving and returning gods will meet, and the world will be repopulated by <a href="/wiki/L%C3%ADf_and_L%C3%ADf%C3%BErasir" title="Líf and Lífþrasir">two human survivors</a>. Ragnarök is an important event in the Norse mythology, and has been the subject of scholarly discourse and theory throughout the history of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_studies" title="Germanic studies">Germanic studies</a>.</p>
<p>The event is attested primarily in the <i><a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a></i>, compiled in the 13th century from earlier traditional sources, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i>, written in the 13th century by <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>. In the <i>Prose Edda</i>, and in a single poem in the <i>Poetic Edda</i>, the event is referred to as <b>Ragnarök</b> or <b>Ragnarøkkr</b> (Old Norse "Fate of the Gods" and "Twilight of the Gods" respectively), a usage popularised by 19th-century composer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> with the title of the last of his <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">Der Ring des Nibelungen</a></i> operas, <i><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6tterd%C3%A4mmerung" title="Götterdämmerung">Götterdämmerung</a></i> (1876).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> compound <i>ragnarok</i> has a long history of interpretation. Its first element, <i>ragna</i>, is unproblematic, being the genitive plural of <i>regin</i> (n. pl.) "the ruling powers, gods". The second element is more problematic, as it occurs in two variants, <i>-rök</i> and <i>-røkkr</i>. Writing in the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_philology" title="Germanic philology">philologist</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Geir_Zo%C3%ABga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Geir Zoëga (page does not exist)">Geir Zoëga</a> treats the two forms as two separate compounds, glossing <i>ragnarök</i> as "the doom or destruction of the gods" and <i>ragnarøkkr</i> as "the twilight of the gods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZo.C3.ABga2004345_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZo.C3.ABga2004345-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The plural noun <i>rök</i>, has several meanings, including "development, origin, cause, relation, fate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The word <i>ragnarök</i> as a whole is then usually interpreted as the "final destiny of the gods."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek2007259_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek2007259-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The singular form <i>ragnarøk(k)r</i> is found in a stanza of the <i>Poetic Edda</i> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Lokasenna" title="Lokasenna">Lokasenna</a></i>, and in the <i>Prose Edda</i>. The noun <i>røk(k)r</i> means "twilight" (from a verb <i>røkkva</i> "to grow dark"), suggesting a translation "twilight of the gods". This reading was widely considered a result of <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>, or a learned reinterpretation, of the original term due to the merger of /ǫ/ and /ø/ in Old Icelandic after ca. 1200<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBjordvandLindeman2007856.E2.80.93857_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBjordvandLindeman2007856.E2.80.93857-5">[5]</a></sup> (nevertheless giving rise to the <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> <i><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6tterd%C3%A4mmerung" title="Götterdämmerung">Götterdämmerung</a></i> "Twilight of the Gods" in the German reception of Norse mythology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindow2001254_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindow2001254-6">[6]</a></sup>). However, Haraldur Bernharðsson in a 2007 paper suggested that the singular form <i>-røkr</i> "twilight" (from a Proto-Germanic *<i>rekwa</i>) might have been the original reading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBernhar.C3.B0sson200730.E2.80.9332_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBernhar.C3.B0sson200730.E2.80.9332-7">[7]</a></sup> Haraldur argues that the words <i>ragnarök</i> and <i>ragnarøkkr</i> are closely related, etymologically and semantically, and suggests a meaning of "renewal of the divine powers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBernhar.C3.B0sson200735_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBernhar.C3.B0sson200735-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other terms used to refer to the events surrounding Ragnarök in the <i>Poetic Edda</i> include <i>aldar rök</i> (aldar means age, "end of an age") from a stanza of <i><a href="/wiki/Vaf%C3%BEr%C3%BA%C3%B0nism%C3%A1l" title="Vafþrúðnismál">Vafþrúðnismál</a></i>, <i>tíva rök</i> from two stanzas of <i>Vafþrúðnismál</i>, <i>þá er regin deyja</i> ("when the gods die") from <i>Vafþrúðnismál</i>, <i>unz um rjúfask regin</i> ("when the gods will be destroyed") from <i>Vafþrúðnismál</i>, <i>Lokasenna</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sigrdr%C3%ADfum%C3%A1l" title="Sigrdrífumál">Sigrdrífumál</a></i>, <i>aldar rof</i> ("destruction of the age") from <i><a href="/wiki/Helgakvi%C3%B0a_Hundingsbana_II" title="Helgakviða Hundingsbana II">Helgakviða Hundingsbana II</a></i>, <i>regin þrjóta</i> ("end of the gods") from <i><a href="/wiki/Hyndlulj%C3%B3%C3%B0" title="Hyndluljóð">Hyndluljóð</a></i>, and, in the <i>Prose Edda</i>, <i>þá er Muspellz-synir herja</i> ("when the sons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muspell" title="Muspell">Muspell</a> move into battle") can be found in chapters 18 and 36 of <i><a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek2007259_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek2007259-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the <i>Poetic Edda</i> poem <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">Völuspá</a></i>, references to Ragnarök begin from stanza 40 until 58, with the rest of the poem describing the aftermath. In the poem, a <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lva" title="Völva">völva</a></i> recites information to <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>. In stanza 41, the völva says:</p>

<dd>of fated men,</dd>

</dl>

<p>The völva then describes three <a href="/wiki/Rooster" title="Rooster">roosters</a> crowing: In stanza 42, the jötunn <a href="/wiki/Herder" title="Herder">herdsman</a> <a href="/wiki/Eggth%C3%A9r" title="Eggthér">Eggthér</a> sits on a <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">mound</a> and cheerfully plays his <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a> while the crimson rooster <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fjalar_(rooster)" title="Fjalar (rooster)">Fjalar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> "hider, deceiver"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrchard199743_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrchard199743-10">[10]</a></sup>) crows in the forest <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A1lgvi%C3%B0r" title="Gálgviðr">Gálgviðr</a>. The golden rooster <a href="/wiki/Gullinkambi" title="Gullinkambi">Gullinkambi</a> crows to the Æsir in <a href="/wiki/Valhalla" title="Valhalla">Valhalla</a>, and the third, unnamed soot-red rooster crows in the halls of the underworld location of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hel_(realm)" title="Hel (realm)">Hel</a> in stanza 43.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington19999_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington19999-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After these stanzas, the völva further relates that the hound <a href="/wiki/Garmr" title="Garmr">Garmr</a> produces deep howls in front of the cave of <a href="/wiki/Gnipahellir" title="Gnipahellir">Gnipahellir</a>. Garmr's bindings break and he runs free. The völva describes the state of humanity:</p>

<dd>sisters' children</dd>

</dl>

<p>The "sons of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADmir" title="Mímir">Mím</a>" are described as being "at play", though this reference is not further explained in surviving sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington1999265_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington1999265-13">[13]</a></sup> Heimdall raises the <a href="/wiki/Gjallarhorn" title="Gjallarhorn">Gjallarhorn</a> into the air and blows deeply into it, and Odin converses with Mím's head. The world tree <a href="/wiki/Yggdrasil" title="Yggdrasil">Yggdrasil</a> shudders and groans. The jötunn <a href="/wiki/Hrym" title="Hrym">Hrym</a> comes from the east, his shield before him. The <a href="/wiki/Midgard" title="Midgard">Midgard</a> serpent <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rmungandr" title="Jörmungandr">Jörmungandr</a> furiously writhes, causing waves to crash. "The <a href="/wiki/Hr%C3%A6svelgr" title="Hræsvelgr">eagle</a> shrieks, pale-beaked he tears the corpse," and the ship <a href="/wiki/Naglfar" title="Naglfar">Naglfar</a> breaks free thanks to the waves made by Jormungandr and sets sail from the east. The fire <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="Jötunn">jötnar</a> inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Muspelheim" title="Muspelheim">Muspelheim</a> come forth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199910_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199910-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The völva continues that <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunheimr" title="Jötunheimr">Jötunheimr</a>, the land of the jötnar, is aroar, and that the Æsir are in council. The <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(mythology)" title="Dwarf (mythology)">dwarfs</a> groan by their stone doors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDronke199719_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDronke199719-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Surtr" title="Surtr">Surtr</a> advances from the south, his sword brighter than the sun. Rocky cliffs open and the jötnar women sink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBellows200422_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBellows200422-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The gods then do battle with the invaders: <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> is swallowed whole and alive fighting the wolf <a href="/wiki/Fenrir" title="Fenrir">Fenrir</a>, causing his wife <a href="/wiki/Frigg" title="Frigg">Frigg</a> her second great sorrow (the first being the death of her son, the god <a href="/wiki/Baldr" title="Baldr">Baldr</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington1999266_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington1999266-16">[16]</a></sup> Odin's son <a href="/wiki/V%C3%AD%C3%B0arr" title="Víðarr">Víðarr</a> avenges his father by rending Fenrir's jaws apart and stabbing it in the heart with his spear, thus killing the wolf. The serpent <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rmungandr" title="Jörmungandr">Jörmungandr</a> opens its gaping maw, yawning widely in the air, and is met in combat by <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>. Thor, also a son of Odin and described here as protector of the earth, furiously fights the serpent, defeating it, but Thor is only able to take nine steps afterward before collapsing. The god <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a> fights <a href="/wiki/Surtr" title="Surtr">Surtr</a> and loses. After this, people flee their homes, and the sun becomes black while the earth sinks into the sea, the stars vanish, steam rises, and flames touch the heavens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBellows200423_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBellows200423-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The völva sees the earth reappearing from the water, and an eagle over a waterfall hunting fish on a mountain. The surviving Æsir meet together at the field of <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B0av%C3%B6llr" title="Iðavöllr">Iðavöllr</a>. They discuss Jörmungandr, great events of the past, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runic_alphabet" title="Runic alphabet">runic alphabet</a>. In stanza 61, in the grass, they find the golden game pieces that the gods are described as having once happily enjoyed playing games with long ago (attested earlier in the same poem). The reemerged fields grow without needing to be sown. The gods <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B6%C3%B0r" title="Höðr">Höðr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baldr" title="Baldr">Baldr</a> return from Hel and live happily together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199912_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199912-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The völva says that the god <a href="/wiki/H%C5%93nir" title="Hœnir">Hœnir</a> chooses wooden slips for divination, and that the sons of two brothers will widely inhabit the windy world. She sees a hall thatched with gold in <a href="/wiki/Giml%C3%A9" title="Gimlé">Gimlé</a>, where nobility will live and spend their lives pleasurably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199912_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199912-18">[18]</a></sup> Stanzas 65, found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Hauksb%C3%B3k" title="Hauksbók">Hauksbók</a></i> version of the poem, refers to a "powerful, mighty one" that "rules over everything" and who will arrive from above at the court of the gods (Old Norse <i>regindómr</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek2007262_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek2007262-19">[19]</a></sup> which has been interpreted as a <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> addition to the poem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindow2001257_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindow2001257-20">[20]</a></sup> In stanza 66, the völva ends her account with a description of the dragon <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AD%C3%B0h%C3%B6ggr" title="Níðhöggr">Níðhöggr</a>, corpses in his jaws, flying through the air. The völva then "sinks down."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199913_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199913-21">[21]</a></sup> It is unclear if stanza 66 indicates that the völva is referring to the present time or if this is an element of the post-Ragnarök world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington19993_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington19993-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vaf.C3.BEr.C3.BA.C3.B0nism.C3.A1l"><i>Vafþrúðnismál</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vafþrúðnismál">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vanir" title="Vanir">Vanir</a> god <a href="/wiki/Nj%C3%B6r%C3%B0r" title="Njörðr">Njörðr</a> is mentioned in relation to Ragnarök in stanza 39 of the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Vaf%C3%BEr%C3%BA%C3%B0nism%C3%A1l" title="Vafþrúðnismál">Vafþrúðnismál</a></i>. In the poem, Odin, disguised as <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Odin" title="List of names of Odin">Gagnráðr</a> faces off with the wise jötunn <a href="/wiki/Vaf%C3%BEr%C3%BA%C3%B0nir" title="Vafþrúðnir">Vafþrúðnir</a> in a battle of wits. Vafþrúðnismál references Njörðr's status as a hostage during the earlier <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir%E2%80%93Vanir_War" title="Æsir–Vanir War">Æsir–Vanir War</a>, and that he will "come back home among the wise Vanir" at "the doom of men."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199946_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199946-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In stanza 44, Odin poses the question to Vafþrúðnir as to who of mankind will survive the "famous" <a href="/wiki/Fimbulwinter" title="Fimbulwinter">Fimbulwinter</a> ("Mighty Winter"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindow2001115_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindow2001115-24">[24]</a></sup>). Vafþrúðnir responds in stanza 45 that those survivors will be <a href="/wiki/L%C3%ADf_and_L%C3%ADf%C3%BErasir" title="Líf and Lífþrasir">Líf and Lífþrasir</a>, and that they will hide in the forest of <a href="/wiki/Hoddm%C3%ADmis_holt" title="Hoddmímis holt">Hoddmímis holt</a>, that they will consume the morning dew, and will produce generations of offspring. In stanza 46, Odin asks what sun will come into the sky after Fenrir has consumed the sun that exists. Vafþrúðnir responds that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%B3l_(Sun)" title="Sól (Sun)">Sól</a> will bear a daughter before Fenrir assails her, and that after Ragnarök this daughter will continue her mother's path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199947_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199947-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In stanza 51, Vafþrúðnir states that, after Surtr's flames have been sated, Odin's sons Víðarr and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1li" title="Váli">Váli</a> will live in the temples of the gods, and that Thor's sons <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B3%C3%B0i_and_Magni" title="Móði and Magni">Móði and Magni</a> will possess the hammer Mjolnir. In stanza 52, the disguised Odin asks the jötunn about Odin's own fate. Vafþrúðnir responds that "the wolf" will consume Odin, and that Víðarr will avenge him by sundering its cold jaws in battle. Odin ends the duel with one final question: what did Odin say to <a href="/wiki/Baldr" title="Baldr">his son</a> before preparing his funeral pyre? With this, Vafþrúðnir realizes that he is dealing with none other than Odin, whom he refers to as "the wisest of beings," adding that Odin alone could know this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington199948.E2.80.9349_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington199948.E2.80.9349-26">[26]</a></sup> Odin's message has been interpreted as a promise of resurrection to Baldr after Ragnarök.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington1999269_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington1999269-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Helgakvi.C3.B0a_Hundingsbana_II"><i>Helgakviða Hundingsbana II</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Helgakviða Hundingsbana II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Ragnarök is briefly referenced in stanza 40 of the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Helgakvi%C3%B0a_Hundingsbana_II" title="Helgakviða Hundingsbana II">Helgakviða Hundingsbana II</a></i>. Here, the <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">valkyrie</a> <a href="/wiki/Sigr%C3%BAn" title="Sigrún">Sigrún</a>'s unnamed maid is passing the deceased hero <a href="/wiki/Helgi_Hundingsbane" title="Helgi Hundingsbane">Helgi Hundingsbane</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">burial mound</a>. Helgi is there with a retinue of men, surprising the maid. The maid asks if she is witnessing a delusion since she sees dead men riding, or if Ragnarök has occurred. In stanza 41, Helgi responds that it is neither.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarrington1999139_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarrington1999139-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prose_Edda"><i>Prose Edda</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prose Edda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Snorri Sturluson's <i>Prose Edda</i> quotes heavily from <i>Völuspá</i> and elaborates extensively in prose on the information there, though some of this information conflicts with that provided in <i>Völuspá</i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gylfaginning_chapters_26_and_34"><i>Gylfaginning</i> chapters 26 and 34</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gylfaginning chapters 26 and 34">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In the <i>Prose Edda</i> book <i><a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a></i>, various references are made to Ragnarök. Ragnarök is first mentioned in chapter 26, where the throned figure of High, king of the hall, tells <a href="/wiki/Gylfi" title="Gylfi">Gangleri</a> (King <a href="/wiki/Gylfi" title="Gylfi">Gylfi</a> in disguise) some basic information about the goddess <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B0unn" title="Iðunn">Iðunn</a>, including that her apples will keep the gods young until Ragnarök.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200536_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200536-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 34, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">High</a> describes the binding of the wolf Fenrir by the gods, causing the god <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BDr" title="Týr">Týr</a> to lose his right hand, and that Fenrir remains there until Ragnarök. Gangleri asks High why, since the gods could only expect destruction from Fenrir, they did not simply kill Fenrir once he was bound. High responds that "the gods hold their sacred places and sanctuaries in such respect that they chose not to defile them with the wolf's blood, even though the prophecies foretold that he would be the death of Odin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200542_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200542-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a consequence of his role in the death of the god Baldr, Loki (described as father of Fenrir) is bound on top of three stones with the internal organs of his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narfi_(son_of_Loki)" title="Narfi (son of Loki)">Narfi</a> (which are turned into iron) in three places. There, <a href="/wiki/Snake_venom" title="Snake venom">venom</a> drops onto his face periodically from a snake placed by the jötunn <a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skaði">Skaði</a>, and when his wife <a href="/wiki/Sigyn" title="Sigyn">Sigyn</a> empties the bucket she is using to collect the dripping venom, the pain he experiences causes convulsions, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>. Loki is further described as being bound this way until the onset of Ragnarök.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200570_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200570-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gylfaginning_chapter_51"><i>Gylfaginning</i> chapter 51</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gylfaginning chapter 51">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Chapter 51 provides a detailed account of Ragnarök interspersed with various quotes from <i>Völuspá</i>, while chapters 52 and 53 describe the aftermath of these events. In Chapter 51, High states the first sign of Ragnarök will be <a href="/wiki/Fimbulwinter" title="Fimbulwinter">Fimbulwinter</a>, during which time three winters will arrive without a summer, and the sun will be useless. High details that, prior to these winters, three earlier winters will have occurred, marked with great battles throughout the world. During this time, greed will cause brothers to kill brothers, and fathers and sons will suffer from the collapse of kinship bonds. High then quotes stanza 45 of <i>Völuspá</i>. Next, High describes that the wolf will first swallow the sun, and then his brother the moon, and mankind will consider the occurrence as a great disaster resulting in much ruin. The stars will disappear. The earth and mountains will shake so violently that the trees will come loose from the soil, the mountains will topple, and all restraints will break, causing Fenrir to break free from his bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200571.E2.80.9372_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200571.E2.80.9372-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>High relates that the great serpent <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rmungandr" title="Jörmungandr">Jörmungandr</a>, also described as a child of Loki in the same source, will breach land as the sea violently swells onto it. The ship Naglfar, described in the <i>Prose Edda</i> as being made from the <a href="/wiki/Nail_(anatomy)" title="Nail (anatomy)">human nails</a> of the dead, is released from its <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">mooring</a>, and sets sail on the surging sea, steered by a jötunn named <a href="/wiki/Hrym" title="Hrym">Hrym</a>. At the same time, Fenrir, eyes and nostrils spraying flames, charges forward with his mouth wide open, his upper jaw reaching to the heavens, his lower jaw touching the earth. At Fenrir's side, Jörmungandr sprays venom throughout the air and the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200572_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200572-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>During all of this, the sky splits into two. From the split, the "sons of <a href="/wiki/Muspelheim" title="Muspelheim">Muspell</a>" ride forth. <a href="/wiki/Surtr" title="Surtr">Surtr</a> rides first, surrounded by flames, his sword brighter than the sun. High says that "Muspell's sons" will ride across <a href="/wiki/Bifr%C3%B6st" title="Bifröst">Bifröst</a>, described in <i>Gylfaginning</i> as a rainbow bridge, and that the bridge will then break. The sons of Muspell (and their shining battle troop) advance to the field of <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADgr%C3%AD%C3%B0r" title="Vígríðr">Vígríðr</a>, described as an expanse that reaches "a hundred leagues in each direction," where Fenrir, Jörmungandr, Loki (followed by "Hel's own"), and Hrym (accompanied by all frost jötnar) join them. While this occurs, Heimdallr stands and blows the <a href="/wiki/Gjallarhorn" title="Gjallarhorn">Gjallarhorn</a> with all his might. The gods awaken at the sound, and they meet. Odin rides to <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADmisbrunnr" title="Mímisbrunnr">Mímisbrunnr</a> in search of counsel from Mímir. Yggdrasil shakes, and everything, everywhere fears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200572_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200572-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>High relates that the Æsir and the <a href="/wiki/Einherjar" title="Einherjar">Einherjar</a> dress for war and head to the field. Odin, wearing a gold helmet and an intricate <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">coat of mail</a>, carries his spear <a href="/wiki/Gungnir" title="Gungnir">Gungnir</a> and rides before them. Odin advances against Fenrir, while Thor moves at his side, though Thor is unable to assist Odin because he has engaged Jörmungandr in combat. According to High, <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a> fiercely fights with Surtr, but Freyr falls because he lacks the sword he once gave to his messenger, <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%ADrnir" title="Skírnir">Skírnir</a>. The hound <a href="/wiki/Garmr" title="Garmr">Garmr</a> (described here as the "worst of monsters") breaks free from his bonds in front of <a href="/wiki/Gnipahellir" title="Gnipahellir">Gnipahellir</a>, and fights the god Týr, resulting in both of their deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200573_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200573-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thor kills Jörmungandr, yet is poisoned by the serpent, and manages to walk nine steps before falling to the earth dead. Fenrir swallows Odin, though immediately afterward his son <a href="/wiki/V%C3%AD%C3%B0arr" title="Víðarr">Víðarr</a> kicks his foot into Fenrir's lower jaw, grips Fenrir's upper jaw, and rips apart Fenrir's mouth, killing Fenrir. Loki fights Heimdallr, and the two kill one another. Surtr covers the earth in fire, causing the entire world to burn. High quotes stanzas 46 to 47 of <i>Völuspá</i>, and additionally stanza 18 of <i><a href="/wiki/Vaf%C3%BEr%C3%BA%C3%B0nism%C3%A1l" title="Vafþrúðnismál">Vafþrúðnismál</a></i> (the latter relating information about the battlefield Vígríðr).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200573.E2.80.9375_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200573.E2.80.9375-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the beginning of chapter 52, Gangleri asks "what will be after heaven and earth and the whole world are burned? All the gods will be dead, together with the Einherjar and the whole of mankind. Didn't you say earlier that each person will live in some world throughout all ages?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200576_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200576-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The figure of Third, seated on the highest throne in the hall, responds that there will be many good places to live, but also many bad ones. Third states that the best place to be is <a href="/wiki/Giml%C3%A9" title="Gimlé">Gimlé</a> in the heavens, where a place exists called <a href="/wiki/Okolnir" title="Okolnir">Okolnir</a> that houses a hall called <a href="/wiki/Brimir" title="Brimir">Brimir</a>—where one can find plenty to drink. Third describes a hall made of <a href="/wiki/Colored_gold#Rose.2C_red.2C_and_pink_gold" title="Colored gold">red gold</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B0afj%C3%B6ll" title="Niðafjöll">Niðafjöll</a> called <a href="/wiki/Sindri_(mythology)" title="Sindri (mythology)">Sindri</a>, where "good and virtuous men will live."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200576_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200576-36">[36]</a></sup> Third further relates an unnamed hall in <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A1str%C3%B6nd" title="Náströnd">Náströnd</a>, the beaches of the dead, that he describes as a large repugnant hall facing north that is built from the spines of snakes, and resembles "a house with walls woven from branches;" the heads of the snakes face the inside of the house and spew so much venom that rivers of it flow throughout the hall, in which oath breakers and murderers must wade. Third here quotes <i>Völuspá</i> stanzas 38 to 39, with the insertion of original prose stating that the worst place of all to be is in <a href="/wiki/Hvergelmir" title="Hvergelmir">Hvergelmir</a>, followed by a quote from <i>Völuspá</i> to highlight that the dragon Níðhöggr harasses the corpses of the dead there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200576.E2.80.9377_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200576.E2.80.9377-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chapter 53 begins with Gangleri asking if any of the gods will survive, and if there will be anything left of the earth or the sky. High responds that the earth will appear once more from the sea, beautiful and green, where self-sown crops grow. The field Iðavöllr exists where Asgard once was, and, there, untouched by Surtr's flames, Víðarr and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1li" title="Váli">Váli</a> reside. Now possessing their father's hammer <a href="/wiki/Mj%C3%B6lnir" title="Mjölnir">Mjölnir</a>, Thor's sons <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B3%C3%B0i_and_Magni" title="Móði and Magni">Móði and Magni</a> will meet them there, and, coming from <a href="/wiki/Hel_(location)" title="Hel (location)">Hel</a>, Baldr and Höðr also arrive. Together, they all sit and recount memories, later finding the gold game pieces the Æsir once owned. <i>Völuspá</i> stanza 51 is then quoted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200577_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200577-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>High reveals that two humans, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%ADf_and_L%C3%ADf%C3%BErasir" title="Líf and Lífþrasir">Líf and Lífþrasir</a>, will have also survived the destruction by hiding in the wood <a href="/wiki/Hoddm%C3%ADmis_holt" title="Hoddmímis holt">Hoddmímis holt</a>. These two survivors consume the morning dew for sustenance, and from their descendants the world will be repopulated. <i><a href="/wiki/Vaf%C3%BEr%C3%BA%C3%B0nism%C3%A1l" title="Vafþrúðnismál">Vafþrúðnismál</a></i> stanza 45 is then quoted. The personified sun, Sól, will have a daughter at least as beautiful as she, and this daughter will follow the same path as her mother. <i>Vafþrúðnismál</i> stanza 47 is quoted, and so ends the foretelling of Ragnarök in <i>Gylfaginning</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByock200577.E2.80.9378_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByock200577.E2.80.9378-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>Various objects have been identified as depicting events from Ragnarök.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thorwald.27s_Cross">Thorwald's Cross</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Thorwald's Cross">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Manx_runestones#Thorwald.27s_Cross:_Br_Olsen.3B185A_.28Andreas_.28III.29.2C_MM_128.29" title="Manx runestones">Thorwald's Cross</a>, a partially surviving <a href="/wiki/Manx_runestones" title="Manx runestones">runestone</a> erected at Kirk <a href="/wiki/Andreas_(parish)" title="Andreas (parish)">Andreas</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, depicts a bearded human holding a spear downward at a wolf, his right foot in its mouth, while a large bird sits at his shoulder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rundata" title="Rundata">Rundata</a> dates it to 940,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrOlsen.3B185A_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrOlsen.3B185A-41">[41]</a></sup> while Pluskowski dates it to the 11th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158-40">[40]</a></sup> This depiction has been interpreted as Odin, with a <a href="/wiki/Huginn_and_Muninn" title="Huginn and Muninn">raven</a> or eagle at his shoulder, being consumed by Fenrir at Ragnarök.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152-42">[42]</a></sup> On the other side of the stone is a depiction of a large cross and another image parallel to the Odin figure that has been described as Christ triumphing over Satan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHunterRalston1999200_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHunterRalston1999200-43">[43]</a></sup> These combined elements have led to the cross as being described as "<a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretic art</a>"; a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Norse_religion" title="Norse religion">pagan</a> and Christian beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gosforth_Cross">Gosforth Cross</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gosforth Cross">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gosforth_Cross" title="Gosforth Cross">Gosforth Cross</a> (920–950), in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, England, is a standing cross of a typical Anglo-Saxon form, carved on all sides of the long shaft, which is nearly square in section. Apart from panels of ornament, the scenes include a Christian <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">crucifixion</a>, and possibly another scene in Hell, but the other scenes are generally interpreted as narrative incidents from the Ragnarök story,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBailey200215-23_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBailey200215-23-44">[44]</a></sup> even by a scholar as cautious of such interpretations as <a href="/wiki/David_M._Wilson" title="David M. Wilson">David M. Wilson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilson1984149.E2.80.93150_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson1984149.E2.80.93150-45">[45]</a></sup> The Ragnarök battle itself may be depicted on the north side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrchard199713_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrchard199713-46">[46]</a></sup> The cross features various figures depicted in <a href="/wiki/Viking_art#Borre_Style" title="Viking art">Borre style</a>, including a man with a spear facing a monstrous head, one of whose feet is thrust into the beast's forked tongue and on its lower jaw, while the other is placed against its upper jaw, a scene interpreted as Víðarr fighting Fenrir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158_40-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2004158-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 11th century <a href="/wiki/Ledberg_stone" title="Ledberg stone">Ledberg stone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, similarly to Thorwald's Cross, features a figure with his foot at the mouth of a four-legged beast, and this may also be a depiction of Odin being devoured by Fenrir at Ragnarök.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152-42">[42]</a></sup> Below the beast and the man is a depiction of a legless, helmeted man, with his arms in a prostrate position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152-42">[42]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Younger_Futhark" title="Younger Futhark">Younger Futhark</a> inscription on the stone bears a commonly seen memorial dedication, but is followed by an encoded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runic_alphabet" title="Runic alphabet">runic</a> sequence that has been described as "mysterious,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacLeodMees2006145_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacLeodMees2006145-47">[47]</a></sup> and "an interesting magic formula which is known from all over the ancient Norse world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJansson1987152-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skarp.C3.A5ker_stone">Skarpåker stone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Skarpåker stone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Jansson (1987) notes that at the time of the inscription, everyone who read the lines would have thought of Ragnarök and the allusion that the father found fitting as an expression of his grief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJansson1987141_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJansson1987141-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclic_time_and_Hoddm.C3.ADmis_holt">Cyclic time and <i>Hoddmímis holt</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cyclic time and Hoddmímis holt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Simek" title="Rudolf Simek">Rudolf Simek</a> theorizes that the survival of Líf and Lífþrasir at the end Ragnarök is "a case of reduplication of the <a href="/wiki/Anthropogeny" title="Anthropogeny">anthropogeny</a>, understandable from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclic_time" title="Cyclic time">cyclic nature</a> of the Eddic <a href="/wiki/Eschatology" title="Eschatology">eschatology</a>". Simek says that Hoddmímis holt "should not be understood literally as a wood or even a forest in which the two keep themselves hidden, but rather as an alternative name for the world-tree Yggdrasill. Thus, the creation of mankind from tree trunks (<a href="/wiki/Ask_and_Embla" title="Ask and Embla">Askr, Embla</a>) is repeated after the Ragnarök as well". Simek says that in <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic regions</a>, the concept of mankind originating from trees is ancient, and additionally points out legendary parallels in a <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> legend of a <a href="/wiki/Shepherd" title="Shepherd">shepherd</a> who lives inside a tree, whose descendants repopulate the land after life there has been wiped out by plague (citing a retelling by F. R. Schröder). In addition, Simek points to an Old Norse parallel in the figure of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96rvar-Oddr" title="Örvar-Oddr">Örvar-Oddr</a>, "who is rejuvenated after living as a tree-man (<i>Ǫrvar-Odds saga</i> 24–27)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek2007189_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek2007189-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muspille.2C_Heliand.2C_and_Christianity"><i>Muspille</i>, <i>Heliand</i>, and Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Muspille, Heliand, and Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Theories have been proposed about the relation between Ragnarök and the 9th century <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poem</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Muspilli" title="Muspilli">Muspilli</a></i> about the Christian Last Judgment, where the word <i>Muspille</i> appears, and the 9th century <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a> epic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Heliand" title="Heliand">Heliand</a></i> about the life of Christ, where various other forms of the word appear. In both sources, the word is used to signify the end of the world through fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek2007222.E2.80.93224_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek2007222.E2.80.93224-50">[50]</a></sup> Old Norse forms of the term also appear throughout accounts of Ragnarök, where the world is also consumed in flames, and, though various theories exist about the meaning and origins of the term, its etymology has not been solved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek2007222.E2.80.93224_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek2007222.E2.80.93224-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-Indo-European_basis">Proto-Indo-European basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Proto-Indo-European basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parallels have been pointed out between the Ragnarök of <a href="/wiki/Norse_religion" title="Norse religion">Norse religion</a> and the beliefs of other related <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European peoples</a>. Subsequently, theories have been put forth that Ragnarök represents a later evolution of a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">Proto-Indo-European</a> belief along with other cultures descending from the Proto-Indo-Europeans. These parallels include comparisons of a cosmic winter motif between the Norse <a href="/wiki/Fimbulwinter" title="Fimbulwinter">Fimbulwinter</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples#History_and_settlement" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bundahishn" title="Bundahishn">Bundahishn</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Jamshid" title="Jamshid">Yima</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997182.E2.80.93183_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997182.E2.80.93183-51">[51]</a></sup> Víðarr's stride has been compared to the <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedic</a> god <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> in that both have a "cosmic stride" with a special shoe used to tear apart a beastly wolf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997182.E2.80.93183_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997182.E2.80.93183-51">[51]</a></sup> Larger patterns have also been drawn between "final battle" events in Indo-European cultures, including the occurrence of a blind or semi-blind figure in "final battle" themes, and figures appearing suddenly with surprising skills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997182.E2.80.93183_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997182.E2.80.93183-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_eruptions">Volcanic eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Volcanic eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hilda_Ellis_Davidson" title="Hilda Ellis Davidson">Hilda Ellis Davidson</a> theorizes that the events in <i>Völuspá</i> occurring after the death of the gods (the sun turning black, steam rising, flames touching the heavens, etc.) may be inspired by the volcanic eruptions on Iceland. Records of eruptions on Iceland bear strong similarities to the sequence of events described in <i>Völuspá</i>, especially the eruption at <a href="/wiki/Laki" title="Laki">Laki</a> that occurred in 1783.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson1990208.E2.80.93209_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson1990208.E2.80.93209-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Phillpotts" title="Bertha Phillpotts">Bertha Phillpotts</a> theorizes that the figure of Surtr was inspired by Icelandic eruptions, and that he was a volcano demon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPhillpotts190514_ff._53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPhillpotts190514_ff.-53">[53]</a></sup> Surtr's name occurs in some Icelandic place names, among them the <a href="/wiki/Lava_tube" title="Lava tube">lava tube</a> caves <a href="/wiki/Surtshellir" title="Surtshellir">Surtshellir</a>, a number of dark caverns in the volcanic central region of Iceland.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bergb.C3.BAa_.C3.BE.C3.A1ttr"><i>Bergbúa þáttr</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Bergbúa þáttr">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parallels have been pointed out between a poem spoken by a <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="Jötunn">jötunn</a> found in the 13th century <a href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%A1ttr" title="Þáttr">þáttr</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bergb%C3%BAa_%C3%BE%C3%A1ttr" title="Bergbúa þáttr">Bergbúa þáttr</a></i> ("the tale of the mountain dweller"). In the tale, Thórd and his servant get lost while traveling to church in winter, and so take shelter for the night within a cave. Inside the cave they hear noises, witness a pair of immense burning eyes, and then the being with burning eyes recites a poem of 12 stanzas. The poem the being recites contains references to Norse mythology (including a mention of Thor) and also prophecies (including that "mountains will tumble, the earth will move, men will be scoured by hot water and burned by fire"). Surtr's fire receives a mention in stanza 10. <a href="/wiki/John_Lindow" title="John Lindow">John Lindow</a> says that the poem may describe "a mix of the destruction of the race of giants and of humans, as in Ragnarök" but that "many of the predictions of disruption on earth could also fit the volcanic activity that is so common in Iceland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindow200173.E2.80.9374_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindow200173.E2.80.9374-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_influence">Modern influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ragnar%C3%B6k&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Modern influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late 2013 and early 2014, English-language media outlets widely reported that Ragnarök was foretold to occur on February 22, 2014. Apparently patterned after the <a href="/wiki/2012_phenomenon" title="2012 phenomenon">2012 phenomenon</a>, the claim was at times attributed to a "Viking Calendar". No such calendar is known to have existed, and the source was a "prediction" made to media outlets by the <a href="/wiki/Jorvik_Viking_Centre" title="Jorvik Viking Centre">Jorvik Viking Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, England, intended to draw attention to an event that the institution was to hold on that date. The Jorvik Viking Centre was criticized for misleading the public to promote the event. In a 2014 article on the claims, philologist Joseph S. Hopkins perceives the media response as an example of a broad revival of interest in the Viking Age and ancient Germanic topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHopkins20147-12_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins20147-12-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
