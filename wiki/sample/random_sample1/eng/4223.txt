<p><b>Nebra</b> or <b>Raneb</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary" title="Ancient Egyptian royal titulary">Horus name</a> of the second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Dynastic_Period_of_Egypt" title="Early Dynastic Period of Egypt">early Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">king</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Second dynasty of Egypt">2nd dynasty</a>. The exact length of his reign is unknown since the <a href="/wiki/Turin_King_List" title="Turin King List">Turin canon</a> is damaged and the year accounts are lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHG_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHG-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> <a href="/wiki/Manetho" title="Manetho">Manetho</a> suggests that Nebra's reign lasted 39 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptologist" title="Egyptologist">Egyptologists</a> question Manetho's view as a <a href="/wiki/Language_interpretation" title="Language interpretation">misinterpretation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Exaggeration" title="Exaggeration">exaggeration</a> of information that was available to him. They credit Nebra with either a 10- or 14-year rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to different authors, Nebra ruled Egypt c. 2850 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> from 2820 BC to 2790 BC (<a href="/wiki/Donald_B._Redford" title="Donald B. Redford">Donald B. Redford</a>), 2800 BC to 2785 BC (<a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_von_Beckerath" title="Jürgen von Beckerath">Jürgen von Beckerath</a>) or 2765 BC to 2750 BC (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._M%C3%A1lek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. Málek (page does not exist)">J. Málek</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attestations">Attestations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebra_(Pharaoh)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Attestations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nerba's name appears on several stone vessels, mostly made of <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>. Most of the bowls were found at Abydos, <a href="/wiki/Giza" title="Giza">Giza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saqqara" title="Saqqara">Saqqara</a>. The inscriptions contain depictions of cultic buildings such as the <i>Ka</i>-house, depictions of deities such as Bastet, Neith and Seth and also the mentionings of cultic feasts. Interestingly, all found objects present Nebra's name either together with that of his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Hotepsekhemwy" title="Hotepsekhemwy">Hotepsekhemwy</a> or with his successor <a href="/wiki/Nynetjer" title="Nynetjer">Nynetjer</a>. Nebra's name never appears alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Clay seal impressions with Nebra's name were found beneath the causeway of the <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_of_Unas" title="Pyramid of Unas">Pyramid of Unas</a> at Saqqara and inside a large gallery tomb, also at Saqqara. This tomb also yielded several seal impressions with Hotepsekhemwy's name and for this reason it is debated whether the tomb belongs to Nebra or his predecessor Hotepsekhemwy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Pierre Tallet and Damien Leisnay reported three rock inscriptions with Nebra's horus name found in the south of Sinai peninsula. Each rock inscription can be found in a different wadi: Wadi Abu Madawi, Wadi Abu Koua and Wadi Ameyra. The places where Nerba's name is displayed lie along a very old route used for expeditions from the western shore of the Sinai to its inland, where copper and turquoise mines existed. Along the wadis the names of predynastic kings up to pharaohs of the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Fourth Dynasty of Egypt">4th Dynasty</a> are located at the same places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_sources">Name sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebra_(Pharaoh)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Nebra's <a href="/wiki/Serekh" title="Serekh">serekh</a> name is of great interest to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptologist" title="Egyptologist">Egyptologists</a>, since it is written with the <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyph" title="Hieroglyph">hieroglyphic</a> sign of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, which had not yet become the object of divine adoration during his lifetime. At the time of king Nebra, the most important religious cults were concentrated on the preservation of the dualistic equal status of the state patrons Horus and Seth. Nothing was more important than keeping that divine balance. The kings themselves were seen as the living representation of that godlike pair. The sun was seen as a celestial object controlled either by Horus or, as in the case of king <a href="/wiki/Seth-Peribsen" title="Seth-Peribsen">Seth-Peribsen</a>, by Seth. Therefore, the sun was no independent deity yet. The first definite proof of the existence of the sun-<a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a> occurs at the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Third dynasty of Egypt">3rd dynasty</a> during the reign of king <a href="/wiki/Djoser" title="Djoser">Djoser</a> in the names of high officials such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesyre" title="Hesyre">Hesyre</a>. And the first definitive detectable proof for a fully established royal sun cult occurs under king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radjedef" title="Radjedef">Radjedef</a>, the third ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Fourth Dynasty of Egypt">4th dynasty</a>. He was the first king who connected his birth name with the name of Ra, starting the great religious belief that Egyptian kings were the living representation of the sun alongside Horus and Seth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoKa_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-JoKa-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SteQu_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SteQu-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Therefore, Nebra's Horus name is problematic regarding its translation and meaning. The typical translation of Nebra's name as "Ra is my lord", which would be read "Raneb", is questionable, as this would assume that the Sun was already being worshiped as an independent deity. Consequently, Egyptologists have proposed the translation "Lord of the sun (of Horus)" which is read "Nebra" and implies the pharaoh's rule over the Sun (as a celestial body), which was indeed also under Horus' or Seth's control. Any solar religion or solar symbolism were not yet established in any useful form and it is now thought that king Nebra might actually have been the first king who adopted extended religious thought about the sun and the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoKa_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-JoKa-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SteQu_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-SteQu-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebra_(Pharaoh)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wife of Nebra is unknown. A “son of the king” and “priest of Sopdu” named <i><a href="/wiki/Perneb" title="Perneb">Perneb</a></i> might have been his son, but since the clay seals providing his name and titles were found in a gallery tomb which is attributed to two kings equally (Nebra and his predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Hotepsekhemwy" title="Hotepsekhemwy">Hotepsekhemwy</a>), it is unclear whose son <i>Perneb</i> really was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">Identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebra_(Pharaoh)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>King Nebra is commonly identified with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Kingdom" title="New Kingdom">Ramesside</a>-era <a href="/wiki/Cartouche" title="Cartouche">cartouche-name</a> <i>Kakau</i>, which can be translated as "The <a href="/wiki/Bull" title="Bull">bull</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apis_(god)" title="Apis (god)">Apis</a>". This links to the <a href="/wiki/Anecdote" title="Anecdote">anecdote</a> written by Manetho, who said that under king <i>Kêchoós</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> version of the name <i>Kakau</i>) the deities <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apis_(Egyptian_mythology)" title="Apis (Egyptian mythology)">Apies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banebdjedet" title="Banebdjedet">the goat of Mendes</a> and <i>Menevus</i> were "introduced and worshipped as gods". This view is questioned by modern Egyptologists, as there was already a <a href="/wiki/Cult" title="Cult">cult</a> of Apis established during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="First dynasty of Egypt">1st dynasty</a>, if not earlier. The name "Kakau" itself is problematic for this early pharaoh, as there was no name source from Nebra's time that could have been used to form the word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_name" title="Birth name">birth name</a> of Nebra also remains unclear. A <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a> by Egyptologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jochem_Kahl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jochem Kahl (page does not exist)">Jochem Kahl</a> says that Nebra was the same person as the mysterious king <a href="/wiki/Weneg_(pharaoh)" title="Weneg (pharaoh)">Weneg-Nebti</a>. He points to a vessel fragment made of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> ash, which was found in the <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tomb</a> of king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peribsen" title="Peribsen">Peribsen</a> (a later ruler during the 2nd dynasty) at <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a>. On the pot sherd he believes there are traces of the weneg-flower beneath the incised name of king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninetjer" title="Ninetjer">Ninetjer</a>. To the right of Ninetjer's name the depiction of the <i>Ka</i>-house of king Nebra is partially preserved. This arrangement led Kahl to conclude that the weneg-flower and Nebra's name were connected to each other and king Ninetjer replaced the <a href="/wiki/Epigraphy" title="Epigraphy">inscription</a>. Kahl also points out that king Ninetjer wrote his name <a href="/wiki/Mirror_writing" title="Mirror writing">mirrored</a>, so that his name deliberately runs in the opposite direction to Nebra's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Kahl's theory is the subject of continuing discussion since the vessel inscription is damaged, thus leaving plenty of room for varying <a href="/wiki/Language_interpretation" title="Language interpretation">interpretations</a>.</p>
<p>Egyptologists such as <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_von_Beckerath" title="Jürgen von Beckerath">Jürgen von Beckerath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battiscombe_Gunn" title="Battiscombe Gunn">Battiscombe Gunn</a> identify Nebra with another mysterious early pharaoh: <a href="/wiki/Nubnefer" title="Nubnefer">Nubnefer</a>. This link is questioned by other scholars, since the rulers of 2nd Dynasty often wrote their birth and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary" title="Ancient Egyptian royal titulary">Horus names</a> in same way (for example: Hor-Nebra → Nisut-Bity-Nebty-Nebra). Thus the name "Nubnefer" may be the birth name of a different king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebra_(Pharaoh)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little is known about Nebra's reign. The discovery of sealings bearing Nebra's name together with sealings of Hotepsekhemwy at Saqqara suggests that Nebra conducted the burial of <a href="/wiki/Hotepsekhemwy" title="Hotepsekhemwy">Hotepsekhemwy</a> and was his direct successor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Additional confirmation of this succession is provided by a statue <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and a stone bowl both bearing <a href="/wiki/Hotepsekhemwy" title="Hotepsekhemwy">Hotepsekhemwy</a> and Nebra's serekhs in juxtaposition. Other pot inscriptions and seal impressions surviving from his time only name <a href="/wiki/Cult" title="Cult">cult</a>-related and <a href="/wiki/Administration_(government)" title="Administration (government)">administrative</a> events, such as the "Erecting of the pillars of Horus". Under Nebra, the first depiction of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a> occurs. The exact duration of Nebra's reign is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">investigations</a>. <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction" title="wikt:Reconstruction">Reconstructions</a> of the well known <a href="/wiki/Palermo_Stone" title="Palermo Stone">Palermo Stone</a>, a black <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> table presenting the yearly events of the kings from the beginning of the 1st dynasty up to king <a href="/wiki/Neferirkare" title="Neferirkare">Neferirkare</a> in the shape of clearly divided charts, leads to the conclusion that Nebra and his predecessor, king <a href="/wiki/Hotepsekhemwy" title="Hotepsekhemwy">Hotepsekhemwy</a>, ruled altogether for 39 years. Since Nebra has fewer records of his rule than Hotepsekhemwy, Nebra is thought to have ruled for a shorter time. The calculations differ from 29 and 10 years to 25 and 14 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomb">Tomb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebra_(Pharaoh)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tomb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The location of Nebra's <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> is unknown. Egyptologists such as <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Helck" title="Wolfgang Helck">Wolfgang Helck</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Munro_(Egyptologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Munro (Egyptologist) (page does not exist)">Peter Munro</a> believe that Nebra was buried in the gallery tomb <i><b>B</b></i> beneath the causeway of the <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_of_Unas" title="Pyramid of Unas">Pyramid of Unas</a> at <a href="/wiki/Saqqara" title="Saqqara">Saqqara</a>. Indeed, most of the <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifacts</a> bearing Raneb's name were found there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
