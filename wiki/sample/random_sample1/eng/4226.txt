<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Radon measure</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> (specifically in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measure_theory" title="Measure theory">measure theory</a>), a <b>Radon measure</b>, named after <a href="/wiki/Johann_Radon" title="Johann Radon">Johann Radon</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measure</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigma_algebra" title="Sigma algebra">σ-algebra</a> of <a href="/wiki/Borel_set" title="Borel set">Borel sets</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hausdorff_topological_space" title="Hausdorff topological space">Hausdorff topological space</a> <i>X</i> that is <a href="/wiki/Locally_finite_measure" title="Locally finite measure">locally finite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inner_regular_measure" title="Inner regular measure">inner regular</a>. These conditions guarantee that the measure is "compatible" with the topology of the space, and most measures used in <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">mathematical analysis</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> are indeed Radon measures.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radon_measure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A common problem is to find a good notion of a measure on a <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a> that is compatible with the topology in some sense. One way to do this is to define a measure on the <a href="/wiki/Borel_set" title="Borel set">Borel sets</a> of the topological space. In general there are several problems with this: for example, such a measure may not have a well defined <a href="/wiki/Support_(measure_theory)" title="Support (measure theory)">support</a>. Another approach to measure theory is to restrict to <a href="/wiki/Locally_compact_space" title="Locally compact space">locally compact</a> <a href="/wiki/Hausdorff_space" title="Hausdorff space">Hausdorff spaces</a>, and only consider the measures that correspond to positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_functional" title="Linear functional">linear functionals</a> on the space of <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">continuous functions</a> with compact support (some authors use this as the definition of a Radon measure). This produces a good theory with no pathological problems, but does not apply to spaces that are not locally compact. If there is no restriction to non-negative measures and complex measures are allowed, then Radon measures can be defined as the continuous dual space on the space of <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">continuous functions</a> with compact support. If such a Radon measure is real then it can be decomposed into the difference of two positive measures. Furthermore, an arbitrary Radon measure can be decomposed into four positive Radon measures, where the real and imaginary parts of the functional are each the differences of two positive Radon measures.</p>
<p>The theory of Radon measures has most of the good properties of the usual theory for locally compact spaces, but applies to all Hausdorff topological spaces. The idea of the definition of a Radon measure is to find some properties that characterize the measures on locally compact spaces corresponding to positive functionals, and use these properties as the definition of a Radon measure on an arbitrary Hausdorff space.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radon_measure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Let <i>m</i> be a measure on the σ-algebra of Borel sets of a Hausdorff topological space <i>X</i>.</p>
<p>The measure <i>m</i> is called <b><a href="/wiki/Inner_regular_measure" title="Inner regular measure">inner regular</a></b> or <b>tight</b> if, for any Borel set <i>B</i>, <i>m</i>(<i>B</i>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supremum" title="Supremum">supremum</a> of <i>m</i>(<i>K</i>) over all compact subsets <i>K</i> of <i>B</i>.</p>
<p>The measure <i>m</i> is called <b><a href="/wiki/Regular_measure" title="Regular measure">outer regular</a></b> if, for any Borel set <i>B</i>, <i>m</i>(<i>B</i>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infimum" title="Infimum">infimum</a> of <i>m</i>(<i>U</i>) over all open sets <i>U</i> containing <i>B</i>.</p>
<p>The measure <i>m</i> is called <b><a href="/wiki/Locally_finite_measure" title="Locally finite measure">locally finite</a></b> if every point of <i>X</i> has a neighborhood <i>U</i> for which <i>m</i>(<i>U</i>) is finite. (If <i>m</i> is locally finite, then it follows that m is finite on compact sets.)</p>
<p>The measure <i>m</i> is called a <b>Radon measure</b> if it is inner regular and locally finite.</p>
<p>(It is possible to extend the theory of Radon measures to non-Hausdorff spaces, essentially by replacing the word "compact" by "closed compact" everywhere. However, there seem to be almost no applications of this extension.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radon_measures_on_locally_compact_spaces">Radon measures on locally compact spaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radon_measure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radon measures on locally compact spaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the underlying measure space is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locally_compact" title="Locally compact">locally compact</a> topological space, the definition of a Radon measure can be expressed in terms of <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">continuous</a> <a href="/wiki/Linear_map" title="Linear map">linear</a> functionals on the space of <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">continuous functions</a> with <a href="/wiki/Support_(mathematics)#Compact_support" title="Support (mathematics)">compact support</a>. This makes it possible to develop measure and integration in terms of <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>, an approach taken by <a href="#CITEREFBourbaki2004">Bourbaki (2004)</a> and a number of other authors.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integration">Integration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radon_measure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Integration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To complete the buildup of measure theory for locally compact spaces from the functional-analytic viewpoint, it is necessary to extend measure (integral) from compactly supported continuous functions. This can be done for real or complex-valued functions in several steps as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>Definition of the <b>upper integral</b> μ*(<i>g</i>) of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_semicontinuous" title="Lower semicontinuous">lower semicontinuous</a> positive (real-valued) function <i>g</i> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supremum" title="Supremum">supremum</a> (possibly infinite) of the positive numbers μ(<i>h</i>) for compactly supported continuous functions <i>h</i> ≤ <i>g</i></li>
<li>Definition of the upper integral μ*(<i>f</i>) for an arbitrary positive (real-valued) function <i>f</i> as the infimum of upper integrals μ*(<i>g</i>) for lower semi-continuous functions <i>g</i> ≥ <i>f</i></li>
<li>Definition of the vector space <i>F</i> = <i>F</i>(<i>X</i>, <i>μ</i>) as the space of all functions <i>f</i> on X for which the upper integral μ*(|<i>f</i>|) of the absolute value is finite; the upper integral of the absolute value defines a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-norm" title="Semi-norm">semi-norm</a> on <i>F</i>, and <i>F</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complete_space" title="Complete space">complete space</a> with respect to the topology defined by the semi-norm</li>
<li>Definition of the space <i>L</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>X</i>, <i>μ</i>) of <b>integrable functions</b> as the <a href="/wiki/Closure_(topology)" title="Closure (topology)">closure</a> inside <i>F</i> of the space of continuous compactly supported functions</li>
<li>Definition of the <b>integral</b> for functions in <i>L</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>X</i>, <i>μ</i>) as extension by continuity (after verifying that μ is continuous with respect to the topology of <i>L</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>X</i>, <i>μ</i>))</li>
<li>Definition of the measure of a set as the integral (when it exists) of the <a href="/wiki/Indicator_function" title="Indicator function">indicator function</a> of the set.</li>
</ol>
<p>It is possible to verify that these steps produce a theory identical with the one that starts from a Radon measure defined as a function that assigns a number to each <a href="/wiki/Borel_set" title="Borel set">Borel set</a> of <i>X</i>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">Lebesgue measure</a> on <b>R</b> can be introduced by a few ways in this functional-analytic set-up. First, it is possibly to rely on an "elementary" integral such as the <a href="/wiki/Daniell_integral" title="Daniell integral">Daniell integral</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Riemann_integral" title="Riemann integral">Riemann integral</a> for integrals of continuous functions with compact support, as these are integrable for all the elementary definitions of integrals. The measure (in the sense defined above) defined by elementary integration is precisely the Lebesgue measure. Second, if one wants to avoid reliance on Riemann or Daniell integral or other similar theories, it is possible to develop first the general theory of <a href="/wiki/Haar_measure" title="Haar measure">Haar measures</a> and define the Lebesgue measure as the Haar measure λ on <b>R</b> that satisfies the normalisation condition <i>λ</i>([0,1]) = 1.</p>

<p>The measure <i>M</i> is outer regular, and locally finite, and inner regular for open sets. It coincides with <i>m</i> on compact and open sets, and <i>m</i> can be reconstructed from <i>M</i> as the unique inner regular measure that is the same as <i>M</i> on compact sets. The measure <i>m</i> is called <b>moderated</b> if <i>M</i> is σ-finite; in this case the measures <i>m</i> and <i>M</i> are the same. (If <i>m</i> is σ-finite this does not imply that <i>M</i> is σ-finite, so being moderated is stronger than being σ-finite.)</p>
<p>On a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strongly_Lindel%C3%B6f_space" title="Strongly Lindelöf space">strongly Lindelöf space</a> every Radon measure is moderated.</p>
<p>An example of a measure <i>m</i> that is σ-finite but not moderated is given by <a href="#CITEREFBourbaki2004">Bourbaki (2004</a>, Exercise 5 of section 1) as follows. The topological space <i>X</i> has as underlying set the subset of the real plane given by the <i>y</i>-axis of points (0,<i>y</i>) together with the points (1/<i>n</i>,<i>m</i>/<i>n</i><sup>2</sup>) with <i>m</i>,<i>n</i> positive integers. The topology is given as follows. The single points (1/<i>n</i>,<i>m</i>/<i>n</i><sup>2</sup>) are all open sets. A base of neighborhoods of the point (0,<i>y</i>) is given by wedges consisting of all points in <i>X</i> of the form (<i>u</i>,<i>v</i>) with |<i>v</i>-<i>y</i>|≤|<i>u</i>|≤1/<i>n</i> for a positive integer <i>n</i>. This space <i>X</i> is locally compact. The measure <i>m</i> is given by letting the <i>y</i>-axis have measure 0 and letting the point (1/<i>n</i>,<i>m</i>/<i>n</i><sup>2</sup>) have measure 1/<i>n</i><sup>3</sup>. This measure is inner regular and locally finite, but is not outer regular as any open set containing the <i>y</i>-axis has measure infinity. In particular the <i>y</i>-axis has <i>m</i>-measure 0 but <i>M</i>-measure infinity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radon_spaces">Radon spaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radon_measure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Radon spaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radon_space" title="Radon space">Radon space</a></div>
<p>A topological space is called a <b>Radon space</b> if every finite Borel measure is a Radon measure, and <b>strongly Radon</b> if every locally finite Borel measure is a Radon measure. Any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suslin_space" title="Suslin space">Suslin space</a> is strongly Radon, and moreover every Radon measure is moderated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duality">Duality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radon_measure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Duality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On a locally compact Hausdorff space, Radon measures correspond to positive linear functionals on the space of continuous functions with compact support. This is not surprising as this property is the main motivation for the definition of Radon measure.</p>
