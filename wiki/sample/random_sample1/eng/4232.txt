<p>In <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a>, a <b>graviscalar</b> (also known as a <b>radion</b>) is a hypothetical particle that emerges as an excitation of the <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity)" title="Metric tensor (general relativity)">metric tensor</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">gravitational field</a>) but whose physical properties are virtually indistinguishable from a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a> in four dimensions, as shown in <a href="/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory" title="Kaluza–Klein theory">Kaluza–Klein theory</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar field</a> <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

<mi>ϕ<!-- ϕ --></mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \phi }</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\phi " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.396ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span> comes from a component of the metric tensor <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>

</math></span><img alt="g_{55}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9301606a8c2b3641347b4acd022ee81cdf5aab8b" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:3.01ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span> where the figure 5 labels an additional, <a href="/wiki/Five-dimensional_space" title="Five-dimensional space">fifth dimension</a>. It can be thought of as a measure of the size of the extra dimension, with variations in the scalar field representing variations in the size of the extra dimensions.</p>
<p>In models with multiple extra dimensions, there exist several such particles.</p>
<p>In theories with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extended_supersymmetry" title="Extended supersymmetry">extended supersymmetry</a>, a graviscalar is usually a <a href="/wiki/Superpartner" title="Superpartner">superpartner</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">graviton</a> that behaves as a particle with <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> 0.</p>
<p>This concept is closely related to that of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gauged_Higgs_model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gauged Higgs model (page does not exist)">gauged Higgs models</a>.</p>
