<p><b>Phra Phutthayotfa Chulalok</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">พระพุทธยอดฟ้าจุฬาโลก</span>), born <b>Thongduang</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">ทองด้วง</span>) and also known as <b>Rama I</b> (20 March 1737 – 7 September 1809), was the founder and the first <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Thailand" title="Monarchy of Thailand">monarch</a> of the reigning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Chakri" title="House of Chakri">House of Chakri</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom" title="Rattanakosin Kingdom">Siam</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>). His full title in Thai is <i>Phra Bat Somdet Phra Paramoruracha Mahachakkriborommanat Phra Phutthayotfa Chulalok</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">พระบาทสมเด็จพระปรโมรุราชามหาจักรีบรมนารถ พระพุทธยอดฟ้าจุฬาโลก</span>). He ascended the throne in 1782, after defeating a rebellion which had deposed King <a href="/wiki/Taksin" title="Taksin">Taksin</a> of Thonburi. He was also celebrated as the founder of Rattanakosin (now <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>) as the new capital of the reunited kingdom.</p>
<p>Rama I was born from a <a href="/wiki/Mon_people" title="Mon people">Mon</a> family, sharing lineage with the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Sisowath" title="House of Sisowath">House of Sisowath</a> where his father served in the royal court in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ayutthaya" title="Kingdom of Ayutthaya">Kingdom of Ayutthaya</a>, and had served King Taksin in wars against the Burmese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konbaung_dynasty" title="Konbaung dynasty">Konbaung dynasty</a> and helped him in the reunification of Siam. During this time he emerged as Siam's most powerful military leader. Thongduang was the first <i><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Somdet_Chao_Phraya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Somdet Chao Phraya</a></i>, the highest rank the nobility could attain, equaled to that of royalty. In 1782, he took control of Siam and crowned himself as the monarch. The most famous event in his reign was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burmese-Siamese_War_(1785)" title="Burmese-Siamese War (1785)">Burmese-Siamese War of 1785</a>, which was the last major Burmese assault on Siam.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other high-ranking figures of old Siam, Rama I's name changed several times during his lifetime, depending on his respective position, and even posthumously the way he was referred to change. His name at birth was Thongduang (also spelled <i>Thong Duang</i> ทองด้วง), family names had not yet been introduced in Siam at that time.</p>
<p>When Thongduang served as deputy governor of <a href="/wiki/Ratchaburi_Province" title="Ratchaburi Province">Ratchaburi Province</a> during the rule of King <a href="/wiki/Ekkathat" title="Ekkathat">Ekkathat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom" title="Ayutthaya Kingdom">Ayutthaya</a>, he bore the title of <i><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Luang" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Luang</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B8%A2%E0%B8%81%E0%B8%81%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B0%E0%B8%9A%E0%B8%B1%E0%B8%95%E0%B8%A3" title="wikt:ยกกระบัตร">Yokkrabat</a></i>. After the demise of Ayutthaya, the new king <a href="/wiki/Taksin" title="Taksin">Taksin</a> to whom he served as an important military commander, awarded him successively the titles of <i><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Phra" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Phra</a> Ratcharin Chao Krom Phra Tamruat</i> (head of the police department), <i><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Phraya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Phraya</a> Aphaironnarit</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Phraya Yommarat</i>, <i>Phraya Chakri</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Chaophraya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Chaophraya</a> Chakri</i> (minister of the northern provinces). Finally Taksin created him the title of <i><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Somdet_Chaophraya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Somdet Chaophraya</a> Maha Kasatsuek</i>, a noble title as high as no Siamese official had born before him, making him quasi-royalty.</p>
<p>When he ascended to the throne in 1782, he took the name <i>Ramathibodi</i>, just like the <a href="/wiki/Uthong" title="Uthong">founder of the Ayutthaya Kingdom</a>. His full title was much longer (<i>Phra Borommarachathirat Ramathibodi Sisin Borommaha Chakkraphat Rachathibodin</i> etc.), intended to demonstrate his universal claim to power like of earlier Siamese kings.</p>
<p>After his death, the people referred to him simply as <i>Phaendin Ton</i> ("the first reign"), to his son as <i>Phaendin Klang</i> ("the middle reign"). Continuing this system consequently, his grandson Rama III would have been "the last reign". To avoid this inauspicious title, he ended this practice by donating two Buddha statues that were placed to the sides of the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Buddha" title="Emerald Buddha">Emerald Buddha</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wat_Phra_Kaeo" title="Wat Phra Kaeo">Wat Phra Kaeo</a> and dedicated one each to his father and grandfather. He demanded to refer to his two predecessors using the names of these Buddha statues. The one dedicated to the first Chakri king was named <i>Phra Phutthayotfa Chulalok</i> ("the Buddha on top of the sky and the crown of the worlds"). This is how this king is still referred to in Thai history books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>His descendant <a href="/wiki/Vajiravudh" title="Vajiravudh">Vajiravudh</a> (Rama VI) who had studied in England, realised that most Siamese kings' names were difficult to reproduce and remember for Westerners. He therefore disposed to use for all kings of the Chakri dynasty the name <i>Rama</i> together with the respective ordinal number. So this king is <i>Rama I</i> in Western literature. In 1982, 200 years after his accession, the Thai cabinet decided to award him the epithet <i>Maharat</i> ("<a href="/wiki/List_of_people_known_as_The_Great" title="List of people known as The Great">The Great</a>").</p>

<li><b>1778–1782</b>: <i>Somdet Chao Praya</i> Maha Kasatsuek etc. (<span lang="th" xml:lang="th">สมเด็จเจ้าพระยามหากษัตริย์ศึก พิฤกมหิมา ทุกนัครระอาเดช นเรศรราชสุริยวงษ์ องค์อรรคบาทมุลิกากร บวรรัตนบรินายก</span>)</li>
<li><b>1782–1809</b>: Phra Bat Somdet Phra Borommarachathirat Ramathibodi Sisin Borommaha Chakkraphat Rachathibodin etc. (<span lang="th" xml:lang="th">พระบาทสมเด็จพระบรมราชาธิราชรามาธิบดี ศรีสินทรบรมมหาจักรพรรดิราชาธิบดินทร์ ธรณินทราธิราช รัตนากาศภาษกรวงษ์ องค์บรมาธิเบศ ตรีภูวเนตรวรนายก ดิลกรัตนราชชาติอาชาวไศรย สมุทัยดโรมนต์ สกลจักรวาฬาธิเบนทร์ สุริเยนทราธิบดินทร์ หริหรินทราธาดาธิบดี ศรีวิบูลยคุณอักนิษฐ ฤทธิราเมศวรมหันต์ บรมธรรมิกราชาธิราชเดโชไชยพรหมเทพาดิเทพนฤบดินทร ภูมินทรบรมาธิเบศโลกเชฐวิสุทธิ รัตนมกุฏประเทศคตา มหาพุทธางกูรบรมบพิตร</span>)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="An_Ayuttayan_aristocrat">An Ayuttayan aristocrat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: An Ayuttayan aristocrat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thongduang was born in 1737 in the reign of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boromakot" title="Boromakot">Boromakot</a> of Ayutthaya. His father was Thongdi, a <a href="/wiki/Mon_people" title="Mon people">Mon</a> noble serving the royal court (posthumously raised to Somdet Phra Prathom Borommahachonnok – "the grand primordial father") who was <i>Phra Akson Sunthonsat</i> (Royal Secretary of northern Siam, Keeper of the Royal Seal). Phra Akson Sunthonsat was also a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Kosa_Pan" title="Kosa Pan">Kosa Pan</a>, the leader of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Narai" title="King Narai">King Narai</a>'s embassy to the French court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His mother, Daoreung (original name Yok), was part-Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Thongduang had six other siblings.</p>
<p>Thongduang at a young age entered the Royal Palace as one of the royal pages of King <a href="/wiki/Uthumphon" title="Uthumphon">Uthumphon</a>, where he met his childhood friend Taksin. In 1757, aged 21, he became a monk temporarily, in accordance with Siamese custom. In 1760, he married <a href="/wiki/Amarindra" title="Amarindra">Nak</a>, daughter of a town patron in Samut Sakorn. He was later appointed the <i>Luang Yokkrabat</i> (deputy governor) of <a href="/wiki/Ratchaburi_Province" title="Ratchaburi Province">Ratchaburi Province</a> by King <a href="/wiki/Ekkathat" title="Ekkathat">Ekkathat</a> in 1758.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_under_Taksin">Service under Taksin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Service under Taksin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the eve of the fall of Ayutthaya, Phraya Wachiraprakan (later King <a href="/wiki/Taksin" title="Taksin">Taksin</a>) had foreseen that the fall of the city was certain. Wachiraprakan decided to break the siege of the city of Ayutthaya by the Burmese army and establish a new base outside. Phraya Ratchaburi also joined this venture. In 1767, Ayutthaya under King Ekkathat fell to Burmese invaders, the city was completely destroyed; burned and looted. Local warlords rose up to establish their supremacy in the absence of a central authority.</p>
<p>Despite the fall of Ayutthaya, Taksin and his men in the same year managed to capture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chantaburi" title="Chantaburi">Chantaburi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trat" title="Trat">Trat</a>. During this time Phraya Ratchaburi became one of Taksin's six ministers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phraya_Pichai" title="Phraya Pichai">Phraya Pichai</a> they were regarded by Taksin as his two most valuable generals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_leader">Military leader</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Military leader">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Swiftly Taksin made a strategic plan and under it recaptured Ayutthaya in one year. In 1768 Taksin crowned himself and founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Thonburi" title="Kingdom of Thonburi">Kingdom of Thonburi</a> on the west bank of the mouth of the Chao Phraya river, using <a href="/wiki/Thonburi" title="Thonburi">Thonburi</a> as a new capital. Under the new Thonburi regime, Thongduang was appointed head of the royal police department, bearing the title <i>Phra Ratcharin</i>. After subjugating the warlord of Phimai with his brother Bunma (at that time called Phra Mahamontri, the future <a href="/wiki/Maha_Sura_Singhanat" title="Maha Sura Singhanat">Maha Sura Singhanat</a>), he was raised to <i>Phraya Aphairanarit</i>.</p>
<p>After the campaign to subdue the lord of Fang in 1769, Thongduang was raised to <i>Phraya Yommarat</i> and in the next year became <i>Chao Phraya Chakri</i> – the <i>Samuhanayok</i> (chief minister of the northern provinces). Chakri commanded the Siamese troops in the wars against Burma and went on to subjugate <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. His brother Bunma (who by that time held the title of Phraya Anuchit Raja), accompanied him in various campaigns. Chakri and his brother were sent to the north to <a href="/wiki/Lan_Na" title="Lan Na">Lan Na</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burmese%E2%80%93Siamese_War_(1775%E2%80%9376)" title="Burmese–Siamese War (1775–76)">1774 to free the kingdom from Burmese rule</a> with the help of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phraya_Kawila&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phraya Kawila (page does not exist)">Phraya Kawila</a>, a prince from <a href="/wiki/Lampang" title="Lampang">Lampang</a>. In 1776, he conquered Khmer Pa Dong (around modern <a href="/wiki/Surin,_Thailand" title="Surin, Thailand">Surin</a>). He was assigned the task of conquering Lao kingdoms in 1778 and all the three kingdoms (<a href="/wiki/Vientiane" title="Vientiane">Vientiane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang" title="Luang Prabang">Luang Prabang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Champasak" title="Kingdom of Champasak">Champasak</a>) fell to the Siamese in the same year. He was eventually raised to <i>Somdet Chao Phraya Maha Kasatsuek</i>, the first official to ever hold this rank.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascension_as_King">Ascension as King</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ascension as King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1781, he went on the campaigns against Cambodia, only to return prematurely due to the instability of Thonburi. The rebellion of Phraya San had broken out and the rebels deposed King Taksin. Some sources report that Taksin was consigned to a monastery. After arriving in Thonburi in 1782, Chao Phraya defeated the Phraya San with his forces. Later sources widely reported that the general eventually executed the ousted Taksin, contradicting to some earlier sources. He then seized power and made himself King, establishing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chakri_Dynasty" title="Chakri Dynasty">Chakri Dynasty</a>, which continues to rule Thailand to this day.</p>
<p>General Maha Kasatsuek crowned himself on 6 April 1782. Soon after, he decided to move the capital of Siam to the east bank of the Chao Phraya river for several reasons, including its better strategic location and a desire to promote his legitimacy by starting from a clean slate. He decided to name his new capital "Rattanakosin" ("Keeping place of the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Buddha" title="Emerald Buddha">Emerald Buddha</a>"). Rama I also raised various members of his family to royalty. He appointed his brother Surasi (Anuchit Raja) or <a href="/wiki/Maha_Sura_Singhanat" title="Maha Sura Singhanat">Maha Sura Singhanat</a> as the "<a href="/wiki/Front_Palace" title="Front Palace">Front Palace</a>" (conventional title of the viceroy and heir apparent) and his nephew Thong-In or <a href="/wiki/Anurak_Devesh" title="Anurak Devesh">Anurak Devesh</a> as the "Rear Palace".</p>
<p>The King had 42 children. Ten of these were born to Queen Amarinda, the others by various concubines. The Queen's children included Prince Isarasundhorn, later King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha_Loetla_Nabhalai" title="Buddha Loetla Nabhalai">Buddha Loetla Nabhalai</a> (Rama II) (whom the King appointed as <a href="/wiki/Front_Palace" title="Front Palace">Front Palace</a> after the death of Maha Sura Singhanat in 1803), Prince <a href="/wiki/Maha_Senanurak" title="Maha Senanurak">Maha Senanurak</a> and Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maha_Sakdi_Polsep" title="Maha Sakdi Polsep">Maha Sakdi Polsep</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_Policy_and_War">Foreign Policy and War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreign Policy and War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam_and_Cambodia">Vietnam and Cambodia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vietnam and Cambodia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_R%E1%BA%A1ch_G%E1%BA%A7m-Xo%C3%A0i_M%C3%BAt" title="Battle of Rạch Gầm-Xoài Mút">Battle of Rạch Gầm-Xoài Mút</a></div>
<p>In 1784–1785, the last of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Lords" title="Nguyễn Lords">Nguyễn Lords</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_%C3%81nh" title="Nguyễn Ánh">Nguyễn Ánh</a>, convinced Rama I to give him forces to attack Vietnam, which was then under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%A2y_S%C6%A1n" title="Tây Sơn">Tây Sơn</a> brothers. However, the joint Nguyễn-Siam fleet was destroyed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rach_Gam%E2%80%93Xoai_Mut" title="Battle of Rach Gam–Xoai Mut">Battle of Rach Gam–Xoai Mut</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a> region. Nguyễn's appeal for Siamese assistance enabled the Siamese to exert considerable political influence over Nguyễn's court. Mac Tu Sinh, the son of Mạc Thiên Tứ and his Siamese wife, was raised among the Siamese, and held office as the governor of <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_Ti%C3%AAn" title="Hà Tiên">Hà Tiên</a> until his death in 1787. Ngo Ma, a general of Siamese descent, was appointed as its acting governor in Mac's place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nguyễn Ánh also took refuge in Siam at the King's court waiting for the opportunities to defeat Tây Sơn. These episodes demonstrated Rama I's willingness to extend Siamese power beyond his Kingdom.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, King <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reamraja_of_Cambodia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reamraja of Cambodia (page does not exist)">Reamraja of Cambodia</a> was deposed in 1779 and the throne was given to his son, the young <a href="/wiki/Ang_Eng" title="Ang Eng">Ang Eng</a>. However, the pro-Vietnamese policies of certain Cambodian aristocrats under Ang Eng alarmed Rama I. As a result, Rama I had Ang Eng captured and deported to Bangkok, where he became Rama's adopted son to implant pro-Siamese sentiments on him. Rama I also imposed Chao Phraya <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abhaya_Bhubet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abhaya Bhubet (page does not exist)">Abhaya Bhubet</a> as the Regent of Cambodia.</p>
<p>Nguyễn Ánh secretly left for Vietnam in 1787, leaving Rama I a note. Nguyen managed to recapture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> by 1788 and later ascended as Emperor Gia Long in 1802.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (Thai; Phrachao Vietnam Ya Long)</p>

<p>In 1794, upon Ang Eng's majority, Rama I reinstalled him as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Narairaja_III_of_Cambodia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Narairaja III of Cambodia (page does not exist)">Narairaja III of Cambodia</a>. The area around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siemreap" title="Siemreap">Siemreap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battambang" title="Battambang">Battambang</a> was annexed by Siam, and were governed by Abhaya Bhubet. However, Rama I allowed these territories to be ruled in accordance with Cambodian traditions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars_with_Burma">Wars with Burma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wars with Burma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine_Armies_War" title="Nine Armies War">Nine Armies War</a></div>
<p>Soon King <a href="/wiki/Bodawpaya" title="Bodawpaya">Bodawpaya</a> of Burma started to pursue his ambitious campaigns to expand his dominions over Siam. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burmese-Siamese_War_(1785%E2%80%931786)" title="Burmese-Siamese War (1785–1786)">Burmese-Siamese War (1785–1786)</a>, also known in Siam as the "Nine Armies War" because the Burmese came in nine armies, broke out. The Burmese soldiers poured into Lanna and Northern Siam. Siamese forces, commanded by Kawila, Prince of <a href="/wiki/Lampang" title="Lampang">Lampang</a>, put up a brave fight and delayed the Burmese advance, all the while waiting for reinforcements from Bangkok. When <a href="/wiki/Phitsanulok" title="Phitsanulok">Phitsanulok</a> was captured, <a href="/wiki/Anurak_Devesh" title="Anurak Devesh">Anurak Devesh</a> the <i>Rear Palace</i>, and Rama I himself led Siamese forces to the north. The Siamese relieved Lampang from the Burmese siege.</p>
<p>In the south, Bodawpaya was waiting at <a href="/wiki/Three_Pagodas_Pass" title="Three Pagodas Pass">Chedi Sam Ong</a> ready to attack. The Front Palace was ordered to lead his troops to the south and counter-attack the Burmese coming to <a href="/wiki/Ranong" title="Ranong">Ranong</a> through <a href="/wiki/Nakhon_Si_Thammarat" title="Nakhon Si Thammarat">Nakhon Si Thammarat</a>. He brought the Burmese to battle near <a href="/wiki/Kanchanaburi" title="Kanchanaburi">Kanchanaburi</a>. The Burmese also attacked Thalang (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phuket_city" title="Phuket city">Phuket</a>), where the governor had just died. Chan, his wife, and her sister Mook gathered the local people and successfully defended Thalang against the Burmese. Today, Chan and Mook are revered as heroines because of their opposition to the Burmese invasions. In their own lifetimes, Rama I bestowed on them the titles <a href="/wiki/Thao_Thep_Kasattri_and_Thao_Sri_Sunthon" title="Thao Thep Kasattri and Thao Sri Sunthon">Thao Thep Kasattri and Thao Sri Sunthon</a>.</p>
<p>The Burmese proceeded to capture <a href="/wiki/Songkhla" title="Songkhla">Songkhla</a>. Upon hearing the news, the governors of <a href="/wiki/Phatthalung" title="Phatthalung">Phatthalung</a> fled. However, a monk named Phra Maha encouraged the citizens of the area to take up arms against the Burmese, his campaign was also successful. Phra Maha was later raised to the nobility by Rama I.</p>
<p>As his armies were destroyed, Bodawpaya retreated. The next year, he attacked again, this time constituting his troops as a single army. With this force Bodawpaya passed through the Chedi Sam Ong pass and settled in Tha Din Daeng. The Front Palace marched the Siamese forces to face Bodawpaya. The fighting was very short and Bodawpaya was quickly defeated. This short war was called the <a href="/wiki/Tha_Din_Daeng_campaign" title="Tha Din Daeng campaign">Tha Din Daeng campaign</a>.</p>

<p>Chinese immigration increased during Rama I's reign, who maintained Taksin's policy of allowing Chinese immigration to sustain the country's economy. The Chinese were found mainly in the trading and mercantile sector, and by the time his son and grandson came to the throne, European explorers noted that Bangkok was filled with Chinese <a href="/wiki/Junk_(ship)" title="Junk (ship)">junks</a> of all sizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Rama I moved the capital from Thonburi, which was founded by his predecessor Taksin, and built the new capital <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>. During the first few years prior to the founding of the current capital, he saw the construction of the palaces and the Chapel Royal. The Chapel Royal or <a href="/wiki/Wat_Phra_Kaew" title="Wat Phra Kaew">Wat Phra Kaew</a> of which the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Buddha" title="Emerald Buddha">Emerald Buddha</a> is enshrined is located within his Royal Palace or the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palace" title="Grand Palace">Grand Palace</a>. With the completion of the new capital, Rama I held an official ceremony naming the new capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1804, Rama I began the compilation of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Seals_Law" title="Three Seals Law">Three Seals Law</a>, consisting of old Ayutthayan laws collected and organized. (From left to right the seals are: 1. The Royal Lion of the Minister of the Interior; 2. The Trunked Lion of the Minister of Defence; and 3. The Crystal Lotus of the Minister of the Port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSS_043_1c_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSS_043_1c-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.9/30</sup>) He also initiated a reform of government and the style of Kingship.</p>
<p>Rama I was also noted for instituting major reforms in Buddhism as well as restoring moral discipline among the monks in the country, which had gradually eroded with the fall of Ayutthaya. Monks had already dabbled in superstitions when he first came to power, and Rama I implemented a law which required a monk who wished to travel to another principality for further education to present a certificate bearing his personal particulars, which would prove a monk own's legitimacy that he had been properly ordained. The King also repeatedly emphasised in state ceremonies to place devotion to the Buddha, and not over guardian spirits and past rulers, of which vestiges of ancient <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">Animist</a> worship had a persisted among the Thais prior to his rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The King also appointed the first <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Patriarch_of_Thailand" title="Supreme Patriarch of Thailand">Supreme Patriarch</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thai_Buddhism" title="Thai Buddhism">Thai Buddhism</a>, whose responsibilities included the duty of ensuring that Rama I's laws are maintained which was to ensure law and order within the Buddhist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sangha_(Buddhism)" title="Sangha (Buddhism)">Sangha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Rama I's passion for literature, which was also connected with his concern for Buddhist order within the country. He was noted for advocating Thai translation of important <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and Buddhist texts lost in the chaos after the sacking of Ayutthaya by the Burmese in 1767, some were salvaged under the direction of Rama I. He also wrote a <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a> epos called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramakian" title="Ramakian">Ramakian</a>.</p>
<p>Rama I also, renewed the relationship with the Vatican and the Jesuits. Missionaries who were expelled during the Taksin's reign, were invited back to Siam. Catholic missionaries's activities then continued in Siam. Reportedly the numbers of local Catholics increased steadily to thousands as their churches were protected, gaining freedom to propagate their belief again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King Rama I died on 7 September 1809 after a short but acute illness,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> he was succeeded by his son Prince Isarasundhorn as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha_Loetla_Nabhalai" title="Buddha Loetla Nabhalai">Buddha Loetla Nabhalai</a> or Rama II.</p>
<p>Siam during the reign of Rama I reached a new height of power not seen since the sixteenth century. Militarily Siam was able to successfully repel Burmese invasions and exerted control over <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> and Cambodia and Vietnam. Culturally Rama I also encouraged cultural works to rehabilitate people after the successive series of wars and built many temples and monuments during his reign. His policies laid the foundation for Siam to expand within the next decades.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_memoriam">In memoriam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rama_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In memoriam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>6 April is <b>Chakri Memorial Day</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Thailand" title="Public holidays in Thailand">holiday</a> to commemorate the founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chakri_Dynasty" title="Chakri Dynasty">Chakri Dynasty</a>.</p>
