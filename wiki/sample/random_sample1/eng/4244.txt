<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Racemic mixture</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, a <b>racemic mixture</b>, or <b>racemate</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>, is one that has equal <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amounts</a> of left- and right-handed <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomers</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> molecule. The first known racemic mixture was <a href="/wiki/Racemic_acid" title="Racemic acid">racemic acid</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> found to be a mixture of the two enantiomeric <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">tartaric acid</a>. A sample with only a single enantiomer is an <i>enantiomerically pure</i>, <i>enantiopure</i> or <i>homochiral</i> compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/Racemic_acid" title="Racemic acid">racemic acid</a> found in grapes; from Latin <i>racemus</i>, meaning a bunch of grapes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A racemic mixture is denoted by the prefix <b>(±)-</b> or <b>dl-</b> (for sugars the prefix <b><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">dl</span>-</b> may be used), indicating an equal (1:1) mixture of dextro and levo isomers. Also the prefix <i><b>rac-</b></i> (or <i><b>racem-</b></i>) or the symbols <i><b>RS</b></i> and <i><b>SR</b></i> (all in <i>italic</i> letters) are used.</p>
<p>If the ratio is not 1:1 (or is not known), the prefix <b>(+)/(−)</b>, <b><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">d/l</span>-</b> or <b>d/l-</b> (with a slash) is used instead.</p>
<p>The usage of d and l is strongly discouraged by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A racemate is <a href="/wiki/Optical_rotation" title="Optical rotation">optically inactive</a>, meaning that there is no net rotation of plane-<a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarized</a> light. Although the two enantiomers rotate plane-polarized light in opposite directions, the rotations cancel because they are present in equal amounts.</p>
<p>In contrast to the two pure enantiomers, which have identical physical properties except for the direction of rotation of plane-polarized light, a racemate sometimes has different properties from either of the pure enantiomers. Different melting points are most common, but different solubilities and <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling points</a> are also possible.</p>
<p>Pharmaceuticals may be available as a racemate or as the pure enantiomer, which might have different potencies. Because biological systems have many chiral asymmetries, pure enantiomers frequently have very different biological effects; examples include <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">methamphetamine</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystallization">Crystallization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crystallization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four ways in which a racemate can be crystallized, depending on the substance; three of which <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Willem_Bakhuis_Roozeboom" title="Hendrik Willem Bakhuis Roozeboom">H. W. B. Roozeboom</a> had distinguished by the year <a href="/wiki/1899" title="1899">1899</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Conglomerate</b> (sometimes <b>racemic conglomerate</b>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>If the '<b>molecules'</b> of the substance have a greater affinity for the same enantiomer than for the opposite one, a mechanical mixture of enantiomerically pure crystals will result. The <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of the racemic conglomerate is always lower than that of the pure enantiomer. Addition of a small amount of one enantiomer to the conglomerate increases the melting point. Roughly 10% of racemic chiral compounds crystallize as conglomerates.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Racemic compound</b> (sometimes <b>true racemate</b>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>If molecules have a greater affinity for the opposite <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomer</a> than for the same enantiomer, the substance forms a single crystalline phase in which the two enantiomers are present in an ordered 1:1 ratio in the elementary cell. Adding a small amount of one enantiomer to the racemic compound decreases the melting point. But the pure enantiomer can have a higher or lower melting point than the compound. A special case of racemic compounds are <i>kryptoracemates</i>, in which the crystal itself has handedness (is enantiomorphic), despite containing both enantiomorphs in a 1:1 ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Pseudoracemate</b> (sometimes <b>racemic solid solution</b>)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>When there is no big difference in affinity between the same and opposite enantiomers, then in contrast to the racemic compound and the conglomerate, the two enantiomers will coexist in an unordered manner in the crystal lattice. Addition of a small amount of one enantiomer changes the melting point just little bit or not at all.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Quasiracemate</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>A quasiracemate is a mixture of two similar but distinct compounds, one of which is left-handed and the other right-handed. Although chemically different, they are sterically similar (isosteric) and are still able to form a racemic crystalline phase. One of the first such racemates studied, by Pasteur in 1853, forms from a 1:2 mixture of the bis <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_salt" title="Ammonium salt">ammonium salt</a> of (+)-<a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">tartaric acid</a> and the bis ammonium salt of (−)-<a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a> in water. Re-investigated in 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the crystals formed are <a href="/wiki/Dumbbell" title="Dumbbell">dumbbell</a>-shape with the central part consisting of ammonium (+)-bitartrate, whereas the outer parts are a quasiracemic mixture of ammonium (+)-bitartrate and ammonium (−)-bimalate.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resolution">Resolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Resolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The separation of a racemate into its components, the pure enantiomers, is called a <a href="/wiki/Chiral_resolution" title="Chiral resolution">chiral resolution</a>. There are various methods, including crystallization, <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a>, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>. The first successful resolution of a racemate was performed by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a>, who manually separated the crystals of a conglomerate.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Without a <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> influence (for example a chiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> or starting material), a chemical reaction that makes a chiral product will always yield a racemate. That can make the synthesis of a racemate cheaper and easier than making the pure enantiomer, because it does not require special conditions. This fact also leads to the question of how biological <a href="/wiki/Homochirality" title="Homochirality">homochirality</a> evolved on what is presumed to be a racemic primordial earth.</p>
<p>The reagents of, and the reactions that produce, racemic mixtures are said to be "not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereospecific" title="Stereospecific">stereospecific</a>" or "not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoselective" title="Stereoselective">stereoselective</a>," for their indecision in a particular <a href="/wiki/Stereoisomerism" title="Stereoisomerism">stereoisomerism</a>. A frequent scenario is that of a planar species (such as an sp<sup>2</sup> carbon atom or a <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocation</a> intermediate) acting as an electrophile. The nucleophile will have a 50% probability of 'hitting' either of the two sides of the planar grouping, thus producing a racemic mixture:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Racemic_pharmaceuticals">Racemic pharmaceuticals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Racemic pharmaceuticals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Enantiopure_drug" title="Enantiopure drug">Enantiopure drug</a></div>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">drug molecules</a> are chiral, and the enantiomers have different effects on biological entities. They can be sold as one enantiomer or as a racemic mixture. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibuprofen" title="Ibuprofen">ibuprofen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Salbutamol" title="Salbutamol">salbutamol</a>. <a href="/wiki/Adderall" title="Adderall">Adderall</a> is an unequal mixture of both <a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">amphetamine</a> enantiomers. A single amphetamine dose combines the neutral sulfate salts of <a href="/wiki/Dextroamphetamine" title="Dextroamphetamine">dextroamphetamine</a> and amphetamine, with the dextro isomer of amphetamine saccharate and D/L-amphetamine aspartate monohydrate. The prescription analgesic <a href="/wiki/Tramadol" title="Tramadol">tramadol</a> is also a racemate.</p>
<p>In some cases (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Ibuprofen" title="Ibuprofen">ibuprofen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a>), the enantiomers interconvert or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racemize" title="Racemize">racemize</a> <i><a href="/wiki/In_vivo" title="In vivo">in vivo</a></i>. This means that preparing a pure enantiomer for medication is largely pointless. However, sometimes samples containing pure enantiomers may be made and sold at a higher cost in cases where the use requires specifically one isomer (e.g., for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereospecific" title="Stereospecific">stereospecific</a> reagent); compare <a href="/wiki/Omeprazole" title="Omeprazole">omeprazole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esomeprazole" title="Esomeprazole">esomeprazole</a>.</p>
<p>While often only one enantiomer of the drug may be active, in cases like <a href="/wiki/Salbutamol" title="Salbutamol">salbutamol</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a>, the other enantiomer may be harmful. The (R) enantiomer of thalidomide is effective against morning sickness, while the (S) enantiomer is teratogenic, causing birth defects. Since the drug racemizes, the drug cannot be considered safe for use by women of child-bearing age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and its use is tightly controlled when used for treating other illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-fda_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-fda-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">Methamphetamine</a> is available by prescription under the brand name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desoxyn" title="Desoxyn">Desoxyn</a>. The active component of Desoxyn is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextromethamphetamine_(medical)" title="Dextromethamphetamine (medical)">dextromethamphetamine hydrochloride</a>. This is the right-handed isomer of methamphetamine. The left-handed isomer of methamphetamine, <a href="/wiki/Levomethamphetamine" title="Levomethamphetamine">levomethamphetamine</a>, is an <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">OTC</a> drug that is less centrally-acting and more peripherally-acting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wallach.27s_rule">Wallach's rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racemic_mixture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wallach's rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Wallach's rule</b> (first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Wallach" title="Otto Wallach">Otto Wallach</a>) states that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racemic" title="Racemic">racemic</a> crystals tend to be more dense than their <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This rule has been substantiated by crystallographic database analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
