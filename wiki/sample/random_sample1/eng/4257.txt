<p><b>Lauterbrunnen</b> is a village and a <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Switzerland" title="Municipalities of Switzerland">municipality</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Interlaken-Oberhasli_(administrative_district)" title="Interlaken-Oberhasli (administrative district)">Interlaken-Oberhasli administrative district</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">canton</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bern_(canton)" title="Bern (canton)">Bern</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Switzerland" title="Municipalities of Switzerland">municipality</a> comprises the major Lauterbrunnen Valley (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Lauterbrunnental</i></span>), the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soustal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soustal (page does not exist)">Soustal</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sefinental&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sefinental (page does not exist)">Sefinental</a>, the upper Lauterbrunnen Valley with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Untersteinberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Untersteinberg (page does not exist)">Untersteinberg</a> including several glaciers, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tschingelfirn" title="Tschingelfirn">Tschingelfirn</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rottalgletscher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rottalgletscher (page does not exist)">Rottalgletscher</a>, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_meadows" title="Alpine meadows">Alpine meadows</a> and peaks, such as <a href="/wiki/Schilthorn" title="Schilthorn">Schilthorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bietenhorn" title="Bietenhorn">Bietenhorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schwarzm%C3%B6nch" title="Schwarzmönch">Schwarzmönch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silberhorn" title="Silberhorn">Silberhorn</a>, and finally the villages Lauterbrunnen, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wengen,_Switzerland" title="Wengen, Switzerland">Wengen</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCrren" title="Mürren">Mürren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gimmelwald" title="Gimmelwald">Gimmelwald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stechelberg" title="Stechelberg">Stechelberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isenfluh" title="Isenfluh">Isenfluh</a>, and several hamlets. The population of the village Lauterbrunnen is less than that of Wengen, but larger than that of the others.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lauterbrunnen was first mentioned in 1240 as <i>"in claro fonte"</i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance language</a> place name meaning "clear spring".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By 1253 it was known to <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> speakers as <i>Liuterbrunnon</i> which by 1268 had the alternate spelling of <i>Luterbrunnen</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-3">[3]</a></sup> While the meaning of <i>brunnen</i> is undoubtedly <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrology)" title="Spring (hydrology)">spring</a> or fountain, there is some dispute about the meaning of <i>lauter</i>: Some translate it as clear, clean or bright (which compares to the earlier Romance language meaning of the place mentioned above) while others translate it as "many" or "louder". A local explanation is that the name Lauterbrunnen means "many springs" using a modern meaning of the word <i>lauter</i> in German: however this could be an example of a <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Lauterbrunnen is first mentioned in 1240 as <i>in claro fonte</i>. In 1304 it was mentioned as <i>Luterbrunnen</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oldest trace of a settlement in the area is a single <a href="/wiki/Switzerland_in_the_Roman_era" title="Switzerland in the Roman era">Roman</a> coin which was discovered in the Blumental.</p>
<p>When the Lauterbrunnen Valley first appears in the historic record, during the 13th century, it was owned by the <a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Freiherr</a> of Wädenswil. In 1240 the Freiherr of Wädenswil sold the Sefinen Valley to <a href="/wiki/Interlaken_Monastery" title="Interlaken Monastery">Interlaken Monastery</a>. Over the following century, the Monastery and other local lords began to expand their power in the Lauterbrunnen and neighboring valleys. However, around 1300, the Lord of Turn began to settle his <a href="/wiki/Walser_German" title="Walser German">Walser</a> speaking people in the nearby Lötschen Valley and into the highlands of the Lauterbrunnen Valley. By 1346, the Walser villages of Lauterbrunnen, Gimmelwald, Mürren, Sichellauenen and Trachsellauenen all had village governments and a certain amount of independence from the Monastery. Three years later, much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernese_Oberland" title="Bernese Oberland">Bernese Oberland</a> unsuccessfully rose up against Monastery. When the Monastery suppressed the rebellion, the Walser villages bore the brunt of the Monastery's wrath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 15th century, the villages of the valley were part of the large <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> of Gsteig bei Interlaken (now part of <a href="/wiki/Gsteigwiler" title="Gsteigwiler">Gsteigwiler</a>). In 1487-88 the villagers in Lauterbrunnen built a <a href="/wiki/Filial_church" title="Filial church">filial church</a> of the parish. In 1506, the parish appointed a full-time priest for Lauterbrunnen. In 1528, the city of <a href="/wiki/Bern" title="Bern">Bern</a> adopted the new faith of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> and began imposing it on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernese_Oberland" title="Bernese Oberland">Bernese Oberland</a>. Lauterbrunnen joined many other villages and the Monastery in an unsuccessful rebellion against the new faith. After Bern imposed its will on the Oberland, they secularized the Monastery and annexed all the Monastery lands. Lauterbrunnen became the center of a new Reformed parish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mines were built in the Trachsellauenen area in the upper valley beginning in the late 16th century. An iron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smelter" title="Smelter">smelter</a> was built in Zweilütschinen (now part of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCndlischwand" title="Gündlischwand">Gündlischwand</a>) in 1715 to process the iron ore from Trachsellauenen. However most of the money from the mines went to the noble landowners. The villagers remained very poor. In the 17th and 18th centuries the poverty was so widespread that many of the villagers joined mercenary regiments or emigrated. A majority of the emigrants moved to the Carolinas in the United States. Beginning in the late 18th century, foreign mountain climbers began to use Lauterbrunnen as a starting point for their expeditions into the nearby Alps. Initially the climbers stayed in the village <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectory" title="Rectory">rectory</a>. However, as Lauterbrunnen's fame grew and with the completion of a road from Interlaken in 1834 and the 1890 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernese_Oberland_Railway" title="Bernese Oberland Railway">Bernese Oberland Railway</a>, more hotels were needed for tourists. As new hotels were built, other tourist infrastructure was also built in the village. They built cable cars to Mürren in 1891 and to Wengen in 1893. But the most significant piece of infrastructure was the <a href="/wiki/Jungfrau_railway" title="Jungfrau railway">Jungfrau railway</a> which was built in 1912. The Jungfrau <a href="/wiki/Rack_railway" title="Rack railway">rack railway</a> runs 9 km (5.6 mi) from <a href="/wiki/Kleine_Scheidegg" title="Kleine Scheidegg">Kleine Scheidegg</a> to the highest railway station in Europe at <a href="/wiki/Jungfraujoch" title="Jungfraujoch">Jungfraujoch</a>. The railway runs almost entirely within a tunnel built into the <a href="/wiki/Eiger" title="Eiger">Eiger</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6nch" title="Mönch">Mönch</a> mountains and contains two stations in the middle of the tunnel, where passengers can disembark to observe the neighboring mountains through windows built into the mountainside. In 1909 the English brothers Walter and Arnold Lunn popularized skiing, curling and bobsledding at Lauterbrunnen. These winter sports provided a whole new group of winter tourists and converted the summer tourist industry into a year-round business. The tourist economy of Lauterbrunnen was devastated due to World War I and II and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>. However, following the end of World War II, tourism rebounded. Many new vacation homes and chalets were built along with ski lifts, chair lifts and a heliport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 January 1973 the former municipality of Isenfluh merged into the municipality of Lauterbrunnen. On 31 December 2009 Amtsbezirk Interlaken, the municipality's former district, was dissolved. On the following day, 1 January 2010, it joined the newly created Verwaltungskreis Interlaken-Oberhasli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mutation_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mutation-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Lauterbrunnen lies at the bottom of a U-shaped valley that extends south and then south-westwards from the village to meet the 8 kilometers (5.0 mi) <a href="/wiki/Lauterbrunnen_Wall" title="Lauterbrunnen Wall">Lauterbrunnen Wall</a>. The Lauterbrunnen Valley (<i>Lauterbrunnental</i>) is one of the deepest in the Alpine chain when compared with the height of the mountains that rise directly on either side. It is a true cleft, rarely more than one kilometre in width, between limestones precipices, sometimes quite perpendicular, everywhere of extreme steepness. It is to this form of the valley that it owes the numerous waterfalls from which it derives its name. The streams descending from the adjoining mountains, on reaching the verge of the rocky walls of the valley, form cascades so high that they are almost lost in spray before they reach the level of the valley. The most famous of these are the <a href="/wiki/Staubbach_Falls" title="Staubbach Falls">Staubbach Falls</a> within less than one kilometres of the village of Lauterbrunnen. The height of the cascade is between 800 and 900 feet (240 and 270 m), one of the highest in Europe formed of a single unbroken fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The river <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%BCtschine" title="Lütschine">Weisse Lütschine</a></i> flows through Lauterbrunnen and overflows its banks about once a year. The source of the river comes from melting snow high in the mountains, thus making it a very pure and clean source of water. It is common practice in the camp sites to chill drinks in the water. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trummelbach" title="Trummelbach">Trummelbach Falls</a> is 3 km (1.9 mi) from Lauterbrunnen, connected by bus from the station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The municipality of Lauterbrunnen extends a considerable distance beyond the village and valley, with an area of 164.56 km<sup>2</sup> (63.54 sq mi). It reaches as far as the peaks of the <a href="/wiki/Eiger" title="Eiger">Eiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6nch" title="Mönch">Mönch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jungfrau" title="Jungfrau">Jungfrau</a> to the east, the <a href="/wiki/Gletscherhorn" title="Gletscherhorn">Gletscherhorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mittaghorn" title="Mittaghorn">Mittaghorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grosshorn" title="Grosshorn">Grosshorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breithorn_(Lauterbrunnen)" title="Breithorn (Lauterbrunnen)">Breithorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tschingelhorn" title="Tschingelhorn">Tschingelhorn</a> to the south, and the <a href="/wiki/Gspaltenhorn" title="Gspaltenhorn">Gspaltenhorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schilthorn" title="Schilthorn">Schilthorn</a> to the west. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kleine_Scheidegg_Pass" title="Kleine Scheidegg Pass">Kleine Scheidegg Pass</a> crosses over to <a href="/wiki/Grindelwald" title="Grindelwald">Grindelwald</a> to the east, whilst the <a href="/wiki/Sefinenfurgge_Pass" title="Sefinenfurgge Pass">Sefinenfurgge Pass</a> crosses to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Griesalp" title="Griesalp">Griesalp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reichenbach_im_Kandertal" title="Reichenbach im Kandertal">Reichenbach im Kandertal</a> to the west; both passes carry hiking trails that form part of the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Pass_Route" title="Alpine Pass Route">Alpine Pass Route</a>, a long-distance hiking trail across Switzerland between <a href="/wiki/Sargans" title="Sargans">Sargans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montreux" title="Montreux">Montreux</a>. Besides the village of Lauterbrunnen, the municipality also includes the villages of Wengen, Mürren, Gimmelwald, Stechelberg, and Isenfluh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arealstatistik_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arealstatistik-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swtopo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-swtopo-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tita10_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tita10-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the municipal area, 36.79 km<sup>2</sup> (14.20 sq mi) or 22.4% is used for agricultural purposes, while 28.84 km<sup>2</sup> (11.14 sq mi) or 17.5% is forested. Of the rest of the land, 2.31 km<sup>2</sup> (0.89 sq mi) or 1.4% is settled (buildings or roads), 1.08 km<sup>2</sup> (0.42 sq mi) or 0.7% is either rivers or lakes and 95.39 km<sup>2</sup> (36.83 sq mi) or 58.0% is unproductive land. Of the built up area, housing and buildings made up 0.7% and transportation infrastructure made up 0.5%. Out of the forested land, 13.6% of the total land area is heavily forested and 2.0% is covered with orchards or small clusters of trees. Of the agricultural land, 3.5% is pastures and 18.9% is used for alpine pastures. All the water in the municipality is flowing water. Of the unproductive areas, 10.3% is unproductive vegetation, 31.3% is too rocky for vegetation and 16.3% of the land is covered by glaciers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFS_land_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BFS_land-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The village Lauterbrunnen has a population (as of December 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) of 2,451.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 22.6% of the population are resident foreign nationals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BE_Stat_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BE_Stat-12">[12]</a></sup> Over the last 10 years (2000-2010) the population has changed at a rate of -8.9%. Migration accounted for -6.5%, while births and deaths accounted for -3.1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the population (as of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) speaks <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> (2,483 or 85.2%) as their first language, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> is the second most common (143 or 4.9%) and <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a> is the third (59 or 2.0%). There are 42 people who speak <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, 40 people who speak <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and 5 people who speak <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the population was 49.7% male and 50.3% female. The population was made up of 970 Swiss men (37.9% of the population) and 301 (11.8%) non-Swiss men. There were 1,011 Swiss women (39.5%) and 276 (10.8%) non-Swiss women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BE_Stat_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-BE_Stat-12">[12]</a></sup> Of the population in the municipality, 1,221 or about 41.9% were born in Lauterbrunnen and lived there in 2000. There were 592 or 20.3% who were born in the same canton, while 386 or 13.2% were born somewhere else in Switzerland, and 544 or 18.7% were born outside of Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, children and teenagers (0–19 years old) make up 17.2% of the population, while adults (20–64 years old) make up 62.5% and seniors (over 64 years old) make up 20.2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 1,195 people who were single and never married in the municipality. There were 1,420 married individuals, 208 widows or widowers and 91 individuals who are divorced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 453 households that consist of only one person and 61 households with five or more people. In 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, a total of 1,090 apartments (40.4% of the total) were permanently occupied, while 1,414 apartments (52.4%) were seasonally occupied and 196 apartments (7.3%) were empty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housing_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housing-15">[15]</a></sup> As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the construction rate of new housing units was 4.3 new units per 1000 residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-13">[13]</a></sup> The vacancy rate for the municipality, in 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, was 0.66%.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_federal_election,_2011" title="Swiss federal election, 2011">federal election</a> the most popular party was the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_People%27s_Party" title="Swiss People's Party">Swiss People's Party (SVP)</a> which received 29.6% of the vote. The next three most popular parties were the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Conservative Democratic Party of Switzerland">Conservative Democratic Party (BDP)</a> (21.5%), the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">Social Democratic Party (SP)</a> (14.6%) and the <a href="/wiki/FDP.The_Liberals" title="FDP.The Liberals">FDP.The Liberals</a> (14.3%). In the federal election, a total of 733 votes were cast, and the <a href="/wiki/Voter_turnout" title="Voter turnout">voter turnout</a> was 43.0%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-election_2011_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-election_2011-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Lauterbrunnen had an unemployment rate of 1.9%. As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were a total of 1,908 people employed in the municipality. Of these, there were 147 people employed in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary economic sector</a> and about 55 businesses involved in this sector. 223 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary sector</a> and there were 35 businesses in this sector. 1,538 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary sector</a>, with 195 businesses in this sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-13">[13]</a></sup> There were 1,623 residents of the municipality who were employed in some capacity, of which females made up 43.1% of the workforce.</p>
<p>In 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> there were a total of 1,605 <a href="/wiki/Full-time_equivalent" title="Full-time equivalent">full-time equivalent</a> jobs. The number of jobs in the primary sector was 77, of which 70 were in agriculture and 7 were in forestry or lumber production. The number of jobs in the secondary sector was 207 of which 20 or (9.7%) were in manufacturing and 124 (59.9%) were in construction. The number of jobs in the tertiary sector was 1,321. In the tertiary sector; 132 or 10.0% were in wholesale or retail sales or the repair of motor vehicles, 231 or 17.5% were in the movement and storage of goods, 687 or 52.0% were in a hotel or restaurant, 75 or 5.7% were in education and 56 or 4.2% were in health care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 360 workers who commuted into the municipality and 252 workers who commuted away. The municipality is a net importer of workers, with about 1.4 workers entering the municipality for every one leaving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commuter_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-commuter-20">[20]</a></sup> Of the working population, 12.5% used public transportation to get to work, and 12.3% used a private car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>From the 2000 census<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 513 or 17.6% were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>, while 1,973 or 67.7% belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Reformed_Church" title="Swiss Reformed Church">Swiss Reformed Church</a>. Of the rest of the population, there were 34 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christianity" title="Orthodox Christianity">members of an Orthodox church</a> (or about 1.17% of the population), and there were 47 individuals (or about 1.61% of the population) who belonged to another Christian church. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1981 and 2010 Lauterbrunnen had an average of 136.5 days of rain or snow per year and on average received 1,207 mm (47.5 in) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. The wettest month was July during which time Lauterbrunnen received an average of 144 mm (5.7 in) of rain or snow. During this month there was precipitation for an average of 13.8 days. The month with the most days of precipitation was June, with an average of 14.1, but with only 133 mm (5.2 in) of rain or snow. The driest month of the year was October with an average of 78 mm (3.1 in) of precipitation over 9.5 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeteoSchweiz_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-MeteoSchweiz-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LauterbrunnenStation.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/LauterbrunnenStation.JPG/220px-LauterbrunnenStation.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/LauterbrunnenStation.JPG/330px-LauterbrunnenStation.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/LauterbrunnenStation.JPG/440px-LauterbrunnenStation.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Lauterbrunnen_railway_station" title="Lauterbrunnen railway station">Lauterbrunnen railway station</a>, in the centre of Lauterbrunnen village, is served by trains of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berner_Oberland_Bahn" title="Berner Oberland Bahn">Berner Oberland Bahn</a> (BOB), which run to <a href="/wiki/Interlaken" title="Interlaken">Interlaken</a>, and by trains of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wengernalpbahn" title="Wengernalpbahn">Wengernalpbahn</a> (WAB), which run to <a href="/wiki/Wengen" title="Wengen">Wengen</a>, Kleine Scheidegg and on to Grindelwald; at Kleine Scheidegg connection can be made with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jungfraubahn" title="Jungfraubahn">Jungfraubahn</a> (JB), which ascends inside the Eiger to the <a href="/wiki/Jungfraujoch" title="Jungfraujoch">Jungfraujoch</a>. The lower terminal of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bergbahn_Lauterbrunnen-M%C3%BCrren" title="Bergbahn Lauterbrunnen-Mürren">Bergbahn Lauterbrunnen-Mürren</a> (LSMS), a <a href="/wiki/Aerial_tramway" title="Aerial tramway">cable car</a> and connecting train, is adjacent to the main station and provides service to Mürren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swmap_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-swmap-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides Lauterbrunnen station, there are ten other railway stations within the municipality of Lauterbrunnen. These are <a href="/wiki/Sandweid_railway_station" title="Sandweid railway station">Sandweid</a> on the BOB, <a href="/wiki/Wengwald_railway_station" title="Wengwald railway station">Wengwald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wengen_railway_station" title="Wengen railway station">Wengen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allmend_railway_station" title="Allmend railway station">Allmend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wengernalp_railway_station" title="Wengernalp railway station">Wengernalp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kleine_Scheidegg_railway_station" title="Kleine Scheidegg railway station">Kleine Scheidegg</a> on the WAB, <a href="/wiki/Eigergletscher_railway_station" title="Eigergletscher railway station">Eigergletscher</a> on the JB, and <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%BCtschalp_railway_station" title="Grütschalp railway station">Grütschalp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winteregg_railway_station" title="Winteregg railway station">Winteregg</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCrren_railway_station" title="Mürren railway station">Mürren</a> on the LSMS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swmap_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-swmap-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/PostBus_Switzerland" title="PostBus Switzerland">Post bus</a> services link Lauterbrunnen village to Stechelberg and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trummelbach_Falls" title="Trummelbach Falls">Trummelbach Falls</a>, and to Isenfluh. An alternative route to Mürren is available using the bus to Stechelberg and then the Luftseilbahn Stechelberg-Mürren-Schilthorn (LSMS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Lauterbrunnen about 1,155 or (39.6%) of the population have completed non-mandatory <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Switzerland#Secondary" title="Education in Switzerland">upper secondary education</a>, and 230 or (7.9%) have completed additional higher education (either <a href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Switzerland" title="List of universities in Switzerland">university</a> or a <i><a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a></i>). Of the 230 who completed tertiary schooling, 60.0% were Swiss men, 20.9% were Swiss women, 10.0% were non-Swiss men and 9.1% were non-Swiss women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Canton of Bern school system provides one year of non-obligatory <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">Kindergarten</a>, followed by six years of Primary school. This is followed by three years of obligatory lower Secondary school where the students are separated according to ability and aptitude. Following the lower Secondary students may attend additional schooling or they may enter an <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticeship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2010-11 school year, there were a total of 247 students attending classes in Lauterbrunnen. There were 3 kindergarten classes with a total of 36 students in the municipality. Of the kindergarten students, 16.7% were permanent or temporary residents of Switzerland (not citizens) and 16.7% have a different mother language than the classroom language. The municipality had 8 primary classes and 125 students. Of the primary students, 19.2% were permanent or temporary residents of Switzerland (not citizens) and 20.8% have a different mother language than the classroom language. During the same year, there were 3 lower secondary classes with a total of 65 students. There were 10.8% who were permanent or temporary residents of Switzerland (not citizens) and 10.8% have a different mother language than the classroom language. The remainder of the students attend a private or special school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-School_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-School-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 5 students in Lauterbrunnen who came from another municipality, while 45 residents attended schools outside the municipality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commuter_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-commuter-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Crime_in_Switzerland" title="Crime in Switzerland">Crime in Switzerland</a></div>
<p>In 2014 the crime rate, of crimes listed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strafgesetzbuch_(Switzerland)" title="Strafgesetzbuch (Switzerland)">Swiss Criminal Code</a>, in Lauterbrunnen was 49.2 per thousand residents. During the same period, the rate of drug crimes was 0 per thousand residents. The rate of violations of immigration, visa and work permit laws was 6.6 per thousand residents. This rate is three times the rate in the district, as well as twice the rate in the canton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lauterbrunnen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a>'s poem <i>Gesang der Geister über den Wassern</i> (literal translation: Song of the Spirits above the Waters) was written while he stayed at the parish house near the <a href="/wiki/Staubbach_Falls" title="Staubbach Falls">Staubbach Falls</a> waterfall in Lauterbrunnen.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a> hiked from Interlaken to the Lauterbrunnen Valley while on a trip to the Continent in 1911. The landscape of the valley later provided the concept and pictorial model for his sketches and watercolours of the fictitious valley of <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>, the dwelling place of <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond Half-elven</a> and his people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lauterbrunnen featured in several scenes from the 1969 <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/On_Her_Majesty%27s_Secret_Service_(film)" title="On Her Majesty's Secret Service (film)">On Her Majesty's Secret Service</a></i>, including a car chase in which Bond (played for the only time by <a href="/wiki/George_Lazenby" title="George Lazenby">George Lazenby</a>) was driven away from henchmen of <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Stavro_Blofeld" title="Ernst Stavro Blofeld">Ernst Stavro Blofeld</a> by his girlfriend <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Bond" title="Tracy Bond">Tracy di Vicenzo</a> in a dramatic pursuit which culminated in them shaking off the pursuers in a stock car race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The 360 degree revolving restaurant <a href="/wiki/Piz_Gloria" title="Piz Gloria">Piz Gloria</a> which crowns the Schilthorn peak was used to film Blofeld's hideout. In the movie Bond escapes from it by skiing down the mountain to reach the village of Mürren at its base.</p>
