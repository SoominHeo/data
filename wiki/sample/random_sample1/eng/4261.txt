<p><b>Lounge music</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Easy_listening" title="Easy listening">easy listening</a> music popular in the 1950s and 1960s. It may be meant to evoke in the listeners the feeling of being in a place, usually with a tranquil theme, such as a jungle, an island paradise or outer space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The range of lounge music encompasses <a href="/wiki/Beautiful_music" title="Beautiful music">beautiful music</a>-influenced instrumentals, modern <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">electronica</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Chill-out_music" title="Chill-out music">chillout</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Downtempo" title="Downtempo">downtempo</a> influences), while remaining thematically focused on its retro-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_age" title="Space age">space-age</a> cultural elements. The earliest type of lounge music appeared during the 1920s and 1930s, and was known as <a href="/wiki/Light_music" title="Light music">light music</a>. These days, the term <i>lounge music</i> may also be used to describe the types of music played in <a href="/wiki/Hotel" title="Hotel">hotels</a> (the lounge, the bar), <a href="/wiki/Casino" title="Casino">casinos</a>, several <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Piano_bar" title="Piano bar">piano bars</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrospective_usage">Retrospective usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lounge_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Retrospective usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Exotica" title="Exotica">Exotica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_age_pop" title="Space age pop">space age pop</a>, and some forms of <a href="/wiki/Easy_listening" title="Easy listening">easy listening</a> music popular during the 1950s and 1960s are now <i>broadly</i> termed <i>lounge</i>. The term <i>lounge</i> does not appear in textual documentation of the period, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> magazine or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_playing" title="Long playing">long playing</a> <a href="/wiki/Album_cover" title="Album cover">album covers</a>, but has been retrospectively applied.</p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> was generally influenced by <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, lounge music was derived from <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and other musical elements borrowed from traditions around the world. <a href="/wiki/Exotica" title="Exotica">Exotica</a> from such artists as <a href="/wiki/Les_Baxter" title="Les Baxter">Les Baxter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Denny" title="Martin Denny">Martin Denny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Lyman" title="Arthur Lyman">Arthur Lyman</a> sold millions of records during its heyday. It combined music that was popular outside the USA, such as various Latin genres (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bossa_nova" title="Bossa nova">Bossa nova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cha-cha-ch%C3%A1_(music)" title="Cha-cha-chá (music)">Cha-Cha-Cha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mambo_(music)" title="Mambo (music)">Mambo</a> as in <a href="/wiki/Cal_Tjader" title="Cal Tjader">Cal Tjader</a>'s fine Latin jazz efforts), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynesian_music" title="Polynesian music">Polynesian</a>, French, etc. into a relaxed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> palatable sound. Such music could have some instruments exaggerated (e.g., a Polynesian song might have an exotic percussion arrangement using bongos, and vocalists imitating wild animals). Many of these recordings were portrayed as originating in exotic foreign lands, but in truth were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> recording studios by veteran session musicians. Another genre, space age pop, mimicked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_age" title="Space age">space age</a> sound effects of the time and reflected the public interest in <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>. With the advent of <a href="/wiki/Stereophonic_sound" title="Stereophonic sound">stereophonic</a> technology, artists such as <a href="/wiki/Juan_Garc%C3%ADa_Esquivel" title="Juan García Esquivel">Esquivel</a> used spatial audio techniques to full effect, creating whooshing sounds with his orchestra.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> describes lounge as less "adventurous" than exotica or space age pop, but not as "watered-down" as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elevator_music" title="Elevator music">Muzak</a>. "Instead, it occupied the middle ground, appealing to fans of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_pop" title="Traditional pop">traditional pop</a> as well as space age pop."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A good deal of lounge music was pure instrumental (i.e., no main vocal part, although there could be minor vocal parts). Sometimes, this music would be theme music from movies or TV shows, although such music could be produced independently from other entertainment productions. These instrumentals could be produced with an orchestral arrangement, or from an arrangement of instruments very similar to that found in jazz, or even rock and roll such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hammond_Organ" title="Hammond Organ">Hammond Organ</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_Guitar" title="Electric Guitar">electric guitar</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lounge_singers">Lounge singers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lounge_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lounge singers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Swing_music" title="Swing music">Swinging music</a> of the era is also considered lounge and consisted of a schmaltzy continuation of the swing jazz era of the 1930s and 1940s, but with more of an emphasis on the vocalist. The legendary <a href="/wiki/Rat_Pack" title="Rat Pack">Rat Pack</a> of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Davis_Jr." title="Sammy Davis Jr.">Sammy Davis Jr.</a>, along with similar artists such as <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Darin" title="Bobby Darin">Bobby Darin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Gleason" title="Jackie Gleason">Jackie Gleason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Newton" title="Wayne Newton">Wayne Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Prima" title="Louis Prima">Louis Prima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonny_King_(singer)" title="Sonny King (singer)">Sonny King</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Butera" title="Sam Butera">Sam Butera</a> are notable examples. The music of <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">Burt Bacharach</a> was soon featured as part of many lounge singers' repertoires. Such artists performed mainly at featured lounges in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas</a> casinos. Documented pioneers of the Las Vegas lounge scene, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Kaye_Trio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mary Kaye Trio (page does not exist)">Mary Kaye Trio</a> were first on the scene in the early 50's.</p>
<p>Lounge singers have a lengthy history stretching back to the decades of the early twentieth century. The somewhat derisive term <a href="/wiki/Lounge_lizard" title="Lounge lizard">lounge lizard</a> was coined then, and less well known lounge singers have often been ridiculed as dinosaurs of past eras<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and parodied for their smarmy delivery of standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In any event, these lounge singers, perhaps performing in a hotel or cocktail bar, are usually accompanied by one or two other musicians, and they favor <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover</a> songs composed by others, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_standards" title="Pop standards">pop standards</a>, many deriving from the days of <a href="/wiki/Tin_Pan_Alley" title="Tin Pan Alley">Tin Pan Alley</a>.</p>
<p>Many well-known performers got their start as lounge singers and musicians. Although he claims not to have worked for very long, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Joel" title="Billy Joel">Billy Joel</a> worked as a lounge musician and penned the song <a href="/wiki/Piano_Man_(song)" title="Piano Man (song)">"Piano Man"</a> about his experience. Not all lounge singers, however, sing lounge music.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resurgence">Resurgence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lounge_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Resurgence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lounge emerged in the late 1980s as a label of endearment by younger fans whose parents had listened to such music in the 1960s. It has enjoyed resurgences in popularity in the 1980s and 1990s, led initially by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconic" title="Iconic">iconic</a> figures such as <a href="/wiki/David_Johansen" title="David Johansen">Buster Poindexter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jaymz_Bee" title="Jaymz Bee">Jaymz Bee</a>. In Japan, producer <a href="/wiki/Yasuharu_Konishi" title="Yasuharu Konishi">Yasuharu Konishi</a> became popular for his work with <a href="/wiki/Pizzicato_Five" title="Pizzicato Five">Pizzicato Five</a>, and is often considered "the Godfather of <a href="/wiki/Shibuya-kei" title="Shibuya-kei">Shibuya-kei</a>," a genre mostly derived from 1960s lounge music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1990s the lounge revival was in full swing and included such groups as <a href="/wiki/Combustible_Edison" title="Combustible Edison">Combustible Edison</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_Jones_(music_group)" title="Love Jones (music group)">Love Jones</a>, The Cocktails, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Martini" title="Pink Martini">Pink Martini</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_High_Llamas" title="The High Llamas">the High Llamas</a>, Don Tiki, and <a href="/wiki/Nightcaps_(band)" title="Nightcaps (band)">Nightcaps</a>. Alternative band <a href="/wiki/Stereolab" title="Stereolab">Stereolab</a> demonstrated the influence of lounge with releases like <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Age_Bachelor_Pad_Music" title="Space Age Bachelor Pad Music">Space Age Bachelor Pad Music</a>,</i> and in 1996 Capitol Records began issuing the <a href="/wiki/Ultra-Lounge" title="Ultra-Lounge">Ultra-Lounge</a> series of lounge music albums. The lounge style was starkly in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> music that dominated the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These groups wore suits and played music inspired by earlier works of <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B4nio_Carlos_Jobim" title="Antônio Carlos Jobim">Antônio Carlos Jobim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Garc%C3%ADa_Esquivel" title="Juan García Esquivel">Juan García Esquivel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Prima" title="Louis Prima">Louis Prima</a> and many others.</p>
<p>In the 2000s <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cheese" title="Richard Cheese">Richard Cheese and Lounge Against the Machine</a> has added to this resurgence by covering (usually profane) hit songs of other genres (primarily <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>) in the style of a lounge singer. In 2004, the Parisian band, <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_Vague_(band)" title="Nouvelle Vague (band)">Nouvelle Vague</a>, released a <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_Vague_(album)" title="Nouvelle Vague (album)">self-titled album</a> in which they covered songs from the '80s post-punk and new wave genres in the style of Bossa Nova. Other artists have taken lounge music to new heights by recombining rock with pop, such as <a href="/wiki/Jon_Brion" title="Jon Brion">Jon Brion</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Bird_and_the_Bee" title="The Bird and the Bee">The Bird and the Bee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Sun" title="Triangle Sun">Triangle Sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Martini" title="Pink Martini">Pink Martini</a>, the <i>Buddha-Lounge</i> series, and the surrounding regulars of <a href="/wiki/Largo_(nightclub)" title="Largo (nightclub)">Café Largo</a>. The movie <i>The Rise and Fall of Black Velvet Flag</i> (2003) is a documentary about three older punk rockers who created a lounge-punk band.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultra_lounges">Ultra lounges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lounge_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ultra lounges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Ultra lounge" redirects here. For the CD compilation series, see <a href="/wiki/Ultra-Lounge" title="Ultra-Lounge">Ultra-Lounge</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>ultra lounge</b> is a style of <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclub</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">lounge</a>, that came to prominence in the late 1990s and early 2000s. Ultra lounges tend to be small to mid-sized venues, smaller than nightclubs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> featuring cocktails and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxury_good" title="Luxury good">upscale</a> atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the design details, ultra lounge-style nightclubs may have subtly changing neon and vivid-colored lights, and seating groupings for people to socialize.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ultra lounge-style clubs sometimes create privacy around the seating areas with room dividers, sheets or curtains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike most nightclubs, ultra lounges rarely have a separate dance floor. Some clubs have semi-private rooms with mattresses to lounge on and socialize.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The music is often more subdued White-Room or <a href="/wiki/Ambient_house" title="Ambient house">ambient house</a>-influenced music instead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uptempo" title="Uptempo">uptempo</a> <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a> or <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a>. These clubs offer a range of <a href="/wiki/Cocktail" title="Cocktail">cocktails</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverages" title="Alcoholic beverages">drinks</a>, and sometimes serve bar food such as <a href="/wiki/Tapas" title="Tapas">tapas</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Finger_food" title="Finger food">finger food</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_film">In film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lounge_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1980 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blues_Brothers_(film)" title="The Blues Brothers (film)">The Blues Brothers</a></i>, most of the members of the band were reduced to performing as "Murph and the Magictones" (headlining at a <a href="/wiki/Holiday_Inn" title="Holiday Inn">Holiday Inn</a>) after bandleader Jake Blues went to prison. When the band takes a break to speak with Jake and his brother Elwood, Murph switches on a Muzak version of "<a href="/wiki/Just_the_Way_You_Are_(Billy_Joel_song)" title="Just the Way You Are (Billy Joel song)">Just the Way You Are</a>," performed by <a href="/wiki/Billy_Joel" title="Billy Joel">Billy Joel</a>, a former lounge musician himself. Later, when Jake and Elwood are in an elevator, Jobim's "<a href="/wiki/The_Girl_from_Ipanema" title="The Girl from Ipanema">The Girl from Ipanema</a>" is heard on Muzak.</p>
<p>The 1989 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fabulous_Baker_Boys" title="The Fabulous Baker Boys">The Fabulous Baker Boys</a></i> starred <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges" title="Jeff Bridges">Jeff Bridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beau_Bridges" title="Beau Bridges">Beau Bridges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Pfeiffer" title="Michelle Pfeiffer">Michelle Pfeiffer</a> as a successful lounge act.</p>
<p>The film <i><a href="/wiki/Swingers_(1996_film)" title="Swingers (1996 film)">Swingers</a></i> was set during the late 1990s lounge and <a href="/wiki/Swing_revival" title="Swing revival">swing revival</a> in Los Angeles, and featured legendary performers like <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jordan" title="Louis Jordan">Louis Jordan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a>, as well as modern lounge acts like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_Jones_(music_group)" title="Love Jones (music group)">Love Jones</a>, Joey Altruda and <a href="/wiki/Big_Bad_Voodoo_Daddy" title="Big Bad Voodoo Daddy">Big Bad Voodoo Daddy</a>.</p>
<p>The soundtrack of the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Anthology_film" title="Anthology film">anthology film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Rooms" title="Four Rooms">Four Rooms</a></i> consists of lounge music.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comedy">Comedy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lounge_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comedy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andy_Kaufman" title="Andy Kaufman">Andy Kaufman</a> created a character called <a href="/wiki/Tony_Clifton" title="Tony Clifton">Tony Clifton</a>. A parody of show biz entitlement and excess, Clifton is untalented, lazy (often not bothering to remember the words to the songs), and abusive to his audiences. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">Bill Murray</a> also portrayed a particularly bad lounge singer on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nick_The_Lounge_Singer" title="Nick The Lounge Singer">Nick The Lounge Singer</a>, best known for providing his own lyrics to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Williams_(composer)" title="John Williams (composer)">John Williams</a> theme from <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> and performing an over-the-top version of the <a href="/wiki/Morris_Albert" title="Morris Albert">Morris Albert</a> hit "<a href="/wiki/Feelings_(Morris_Albert_song)" title="Feelings (Morris Albert song)">Feelings</a>". Later, <a href="/wiki/Will_Ferrell" title="Will Ferrell">Will Ferrell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ana_Gasteyer" title="Ana Gasteyer">Ana Gasteyer</a> portrayed a goofy married duo of lounge-style musicians, but in unlikely venues such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> dances. Part of the humor derived from the incongruous application of their "nerdy" and outdated style to performances of current pop-music hits. British comedians <a href="/wiki/Mel_Smith" title="Mel Smith">Mel Smith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Griff_Rhys_Jones" title="Griff Rhys Jones">Griff Rhys Jones</a> appeared as a cheesy <a href="/wiki/Electronic_keyboard" title="Electronic keyboard">keyboard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass</a> duo during the end credits of one series of their long-running sketch show. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cheese" title="Richard Cheese">Richard Cheese</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Lounge_Kittens" title="The Lounge Kittens">The Lounge Kittens</a> specialize in playing lounge-style arrangements of recent popular rock, metal and hip hop songs, sung in a croony traditional swing vocal style, contrasting an elegant jazz treatment of the music with often profane and ribald lyrics to create a humorous dissonance.</p>
