<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._E._J._Brouwer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in his career, Brouwer proved a number of theorems that were in the emerging field of topology. The main results were his fixed point theorem, the topological invariance of degree, and the <a href="/wiki/Invariance_of_domain" title="Invariance of domain">topological invariance of dimension</a>. The most popular of the three among mathematicians is the first one called the Brouwer Fixed Point Theorem. It is a simple corollary to the second, about the topological invariance of degree, and this one is the most popular among algebraic topologists. The third is perhaps the hardest.</p>
<p>Brouwer also proved the <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_approximation_theorem" title="Simplicial approximation theorem">simplicial approximation theorem</a> in the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a>, which justifies the reduction to combinatorial terms, after sufficient subdivision of <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_complex" title="Simplicial complex">simplicial complexes</a>, of the treatment of general continuous mappings. In 1912, at age 31, he was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brouwer in effect founded the mathematical philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Intuitionism" title="Intuitionism">intuitionism</a> as an opponent to the then-prevailing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formalism_(mathematics)" title="Formalism (mathematics)">formalism</a> of <a href="/wiki/David_Hilbert" title="David Hilbert">David Hilbert</a> and his collaborators <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bernays" title="Paul Bernays">Paul Bernays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ackermann" title="Wilhelm Ackermann">Wilhelm Ackermann</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a> and others (cf. Kleene (1952), p.Â 46â59). As a variety of <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(mathematics)" title="Constructivism (mathematics)">constructive mathematics</a>, intuitionism is essentially a philosophy of the <a href="/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics" title="Foundations of mathematics">foundations of mathematics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is sometimes and rather simplistically characterized by saying that its adherents refuse to use the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_excluded_middle" title="Law of excluded middle">law of excluded middle</a> in mathematical reasoning.</p>
<p>Brouwer was a member of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Significs_group" title="Significs group">Significs group</a></i>. It formed part of the early history of <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a>âthe study of symbolsâaround <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Lady_Welby" title="Victoria, Lady Welby">Victoria, Lady Welby</a> in particular. The original meaning of his intuitionism probably can not be completely disentangled from the intellectual milieu of that group.</p>
<p>In 1905, at the age of 24, Brouwer expressed his philosophy of life in a short tract <i>Life, Art and Mysticism</i> described by Davis as "drenched in romantic pessimism" (Davis (2002), p.Â 94). <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a> had a formative influence on Brouwer, not least because he insisted that all concepts be fundamentally based on sense intuitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Brouwer then "embarked on a self-righteous campaign to reconstruct mathematical practice from the ground up so as to satisfy his philosophical convictions"; indeed his thesis advisor refused to accept his Chapter II "as it stands, ... all interwoven with some kind of pessimism and mystical attitude to life which is not mathematics, nor has anything to do with the foundations of mathematics" (Davis, p.Â 94 quoting van Stigt, p.Â 41). Nevertheless, in 1908:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"... Brouwer, in a paper entitled 'The untrustworthiness of the principles of logic', challenged the belief that the rules of the classical logic, which have come down to us essentially from Aristotle (384--322 B.C.) have an absolute validity, independent of the subject matter to which they are applied" (Kleene (1952), p. 46).</dd>
</dl>
<p>"After completing his dissertation (1907 - see Van Dalen), Brouwer made a conscious decision to temporarily keep his contentious ideas under wraps and to concentrate on demonstrating his mathematical prowess" (Davis (2000), p.Â 95); by 1910 he had published a number of important papers, in particular the Fixed Point Theorem. Hilbertâthe formalist with whom the intuitionist Brouwer would ultimately spend years in conflictâadmired the young man and helped him receive a regular academic appointment (1912) at the University of Amsterdam (Davis, p.Â 96). It was then that "Brouwer felt free to return to his revolutionary project which he was now calling <i>intuitionism</i> " (ibid).</p>
<p>He was combative for a young man. He was involved in a very public and eventually demeaning controversy in the later 1920s with Hilbert over editorial policy at <i><a href="/wiki/Mathematische_Annalen" title="Mathematische Annalen">Mathematische Annalen</a></i>, at that time a leading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Learned_journal" title="Learned journal">learned journal</a>. He became relatively isolated; the development of intuitionism at its source was taken up by his student <a href="/wiki/Arend_Heyting" title="Arend Heyting">Arend Heyting</a>.</p>
<p>Dutch mathematician and historian of mathematics, <a href="/wiki/Bartel_Leendert_van_der_Waerden" title="Bartel Leendert van der Waerden">Bartel Leendert van der Waerden</a> attended lectures given by Brouwer in later years, and commented: "Even though his most important research contributions were in topology, Brouwer never gave courses in topology, but always onâand only onâthe foundations of his intuitionism. It seemed that he was no longer convinced of his results in topology because they were not correct from the point of view of intuitionism, and he judged everything he had done before, his greatest output, false according to his philosophy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>About his last years, Davis (2002) remarks:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"...he felt more and more isolated, and spent his last years under the spell of 'totally unfounded financial worries and a paranoid fear of bankruptcy, persecution and illness.' He was killed in 1966 at the age of 85, struck by a vehicle while crossing the street in front of his house." (Davis, p. 100 quoting van Stigt. p. 110.)</dd>
</dl>
