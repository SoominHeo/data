<p><b>Lima</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LY</span></span></b>-mə</i></a></span>) is a city in and the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Allen_County,_Ohio" title="Allen County, Ohio">Allen County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-5">[5]</a></sup> The municipality is located in <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Ohio" title="Northwest Ohio">northwestern</a> Ohio along <a href="/wiki/Interstate_75" title="Interstate 75">Interstate 75</a> approximately 72 miles (116 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton</a> and 78 miles (126 km) south-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a>.</p>
<p>As of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Census,_2010" title="United States Census, 2010">2010 census</a>, the city had a population of 38,771. It is the principal city of and is included in the Lima, Ohio <a href="/wiki/Lima,_Ohio_metropolitan_area" title="Lima, Ohio metropolitan area">Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>, which is included in the Lima-<a href="/wiki/Van_Wert,_Ohio" title="Van Wert, Ohio">Van Wert</a>–<a href="/wiki/Wapakoneta,_Ohio" title="Wapakoneta, Ohio">Wapakoneta, Ohio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lima-Van_Wert-Wapakoneta,_Ohio_Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Lima-Van Wert-Wapakoneta, Ohio Combined Statistical Area">Combined Statistical Area</a>. Lima was founded in 1831.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lima_Army_Tank_Plant" title="Lima Army Tank Plant">Lima Army Tank Plant</a>, built in 1941, is the sole producer of the <a href="/wiki/M1_Abrams" title="M1 Abrams">M1 Abrams</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shawnee_and_establishment">Shawnee and establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Shawnee and establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the years after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shawnee" title="Shawnee">Shawnee</a> were the most prominent residents of west central Ohio, growing in numbers and permanency after the 1794 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Greenville" title="Treaty of Greenville">Treaty of Greenville</a>. By 1817, the United States had created the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River_(Auglaize_River)" title="Ottawa River (Auglaize River)">Hog Creek</a> Reservation for the local Shawnee, covering portions of what would become <a href="/wiki/Allen_County,_Ohio" title="Allen County, Ohio">Allen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auglaize_County,_Ohio" title="Auglaize County, Ohio">Auglaize counties</a>, and including part of present-day Lima.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The creation of the Shawnee reservation freed other lands in the area for settlement, and in February 1820, the Ohio legislature formally established Allen County. In 1831 the Shawnee were forced to surrender all their land in the area to the United States and relocated to <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, opening all of Allen County to settlement. The Ohio legislature mandated that a <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> be established and "Lima" was the result.</p>
<p>The name "Lima" was reputedly chosen in a nod to the <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Peruvian capital</a>, which during the 1800s was a major source of <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a>, an anti-<a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> drug for which there had been a demand in the region, an area known as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Black_Swamp" title="Great Black Swamp">Great Black Swamp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership_and_growth">Leadership and growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leadership and growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1831, Lima has been the center of government for Allen County, the first of its three courthouses erected in the city's first year. The foundations of city life followed in quick order. The first school appeared in 1832. Lima's first surgeon, Doctor William McHenry arrived in 1834. 1836 brought the first newspaper to Lima. Lima was officially organized as a city in 1842. Henry DeVilliers Williams was its first mayor. The first public school opened in 1850. In 1854, the first train appeared in Lima, a harbinger of later economic success.</p>
<p>Also in 1854, a <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> outbreak in <a href="/wiki/Delphos,_Ohio" title="Delphos, Ohio">Delphos</a> (a town in Allen County northwest of Lima) spread throughout west central Ohio. Countywide problems caused by the contaminated water supply were not solved until 1886 when Lima started a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_water_system" title="Municipal water system">municipal water system</a>. Lima's role as a regional center for industry began early. The Lima Agricultural Works began operations in 1869. The company changed names and types of manufacturing through the years. In 1882, under the name Lima Machine Works, the industry built the first <a href="/wiki/Shay_locomotive" title="Shay locomotive">Shay-geared locomotive</a>.</p>
<p>Stimulated by the economic boom in nearby <a href="/wiki/Findlay,_Ohio" title="Findlay, Ohio">Findlay</a>, in 1885 Lima businessman Benjamin C. Faurot drilled for natural gas at his <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mill</a>. On May 19, oil was discovered instead of gas. The oil well never realized enormous profits, but it triggered Lima's oil industry, bringing <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil" title="Standard Oil">Standard Oil</a> to the city. Lima's oil field was, for about a decade, the largest in the US.</p>
<p>Economic development brought money for arts and entertainment. Benjamin Faurot's Opera House opened in 1882, a nationally renowned structure so impressive that New Yorkers used it as a model for their own theaters. In 1907, Lima built its first movie theater.</p>
<p>In the early 20th century, Benjamin A. Gramm and his close friend Max Bernstein formed the <a href="/wiki/Gramm-Bernstein_Company" title="Gramm-Bernstein Company">Gramm-Bernstein Company</a>, which became a pioneer in the motor truck industry. During World War I, Gramm created the "<a href="/wiki/Liberty_truck" title="Liberty truck">Liberty truck</a>", which was welcomed upon its arrival in Washington, D.C., by President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>. Thousands were sent to Europe to help the Allied war effort.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Roaring_20s">The Roaring 20s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Roaring 20s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After World War I, Allen County's population growth lagged the state and the nation. Galvin was an assistant superintendent at the Peru Steel Casting Co. of Peru, Ind. He then became acting manager at American Steel Foundries in Pittsburgh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1921, Lima voters approved a change in the structure of Lima city government. Voters now elected five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commissioners" title="Commissioners">commissioners</a>, with the commission chair serving as mayor. The charter sought to establish professional management, requiring the commissioners to hire a <a href="/wiki/City_manager" title="City manager">city manager</a>, who reported to the mayor. Lima proved itself to be very much in the Progressive tradition with these changes, after flirting with radicalism in 1912 when the voters elected a Socialist mayor.</p>
<p>The darker side of the progressive era revealed itself in the prominence of the <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a> in the city. It was a center for the <a href="/wiki/Black_Legion_(political_movement)" title="Black Legion (political movement)">Black Legion</a>, a notoriously violent subset of the Klan. On August 1, 1923, a KKK parade in Lima drew a crowd estimated at 100,000 people.</p>
<p>Economically, the 1920s were a time of industrial expansion in Lima. In 1925, <a href="/wiki/Lima_Locomotive_Works" title="Lima Locomotive Works">Lima Locomotive Works, Inc.</a> built the "Lima A-1", a 2-8-4 model that became the prototype for the modern steam locomotive. The Locomotive Works also created a new division, the Ohio Power Shovel Company. In 1927, local industrialist John E. Galvin helped found <a href="/wiki/Superior_Coach_Company" title="Superior Coach Company">Superior Coach Company</a>. It became the world's largest producer of school buses and funeral coaches within two decades. In 1930, eight railroad companies served Lima.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Depression">Great Depression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Great Depression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Allen County's population grew significantly faster than the state during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>. In 1933, Lima again reorganized its government. The citizens adopted a "strong mayor" model to replace the city manager of the 1920s. Despite the hardships of the decade, Lima residents supported the construction of a hospital to serve the area. Lima Memorial Hospital, named in honor of World War I veterans, opened on <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a>, 1933.</p>
<p>The Lima area was not safe from the increased crime rate of the 1930s. In 1933, gangster <a href="/wiki/John_Dillinger" title="John Dillinger">John Dillinger</a> was in the Allen County Jail, arrested for robbing the Citizens National Bank in nearby <a href="/wiki/Bluffton,_Ohio" title="Bluffton, Ohio">Bluffton</a>. Dillinger's cohorts broke him out of jail, killing Allen County Sheriff Jess Sarber in the process. The murder and jailbreak put Dillinger at the top of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI%27s_ten_most_wanted_list" title="FBI's ten most wanted list">FBI's ten most wanted list</a>. His was not the only crime outfit to plague Lima during the decade. In 1936, the notorious Brady Gang robbed a local jewelry store twice.</p>
<p>The Great Depression slowed the pace of industrial expansion. In 1930, a Lima directory listed 93 industrial employers with some 8,000 employees. By 1934, industrial employment was reduced by half. In 1935, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Corporation_(1886)" title="Westinghouse Electric Corporation (1886)">Westinghouse</a> located a Small Motor Division in Lima to build fractional horsepower electric motors. The Ohio Steel Foundry turned the corner and grew, eventually expanding its successes in its industry. The thirties were a decade for organizing labor in Lima. By 1940 there were at least fifty labor unions representing local workers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lima benefited from increased production during World War II and a growing population, but suffered a significant economic decline at the end of the decade when industry retooled for peacetime production. In May 1941, based in the steel foundry, construction began on <a href="/wiki/Lima_Army_Tank_Plant" title="Lima Army Tank Plant">Lima Army Tank Plant</a> to manufacture centrifugally cast gun tubes. In November 1942, United Motors Services took over operation of the plant to process vehicles under government contract. The plant prepared many vehicles for Europe, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M5_light_tank" title="M5 light tank">M5 light tank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/M26_Pershing" title="M26 Pershing">T-26 Pershing tank</a>. At its peak during the war, the Lima Tank Depot (now the Joint Systems Manufacturing Center, operated by <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a>), employed over 5,000 people.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_boom">Post-war boom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-war boom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The area's expanding population in the 1940s and 1950s brought hospital and school expansion. St Rita's Hospital, founded in 1918, opened a seven-story addition in 1948. With voter support, school leadership built six new elementary schools and the new centralized <a href="/wiki/Lima_Senior_High_School" title="Lima Senior High School">Lima Senior High School</a> during the 1950s. Lima's industrial production grew in the decade. During the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, the Lima Tank Depot resumed manufacturing, at a level expanded from World War II standards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_rights">Civil rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Civil rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1960s, Lima experienced both growth and community unrest. In 1962, a new Allen County Airport was built in <a href="/wiki/Perry_Township,_Allen_County,_Ohio" title="Perry Township, Allen County, Ohio">Perry Township</a>. With the passage of the city <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a> in 1966, Lima constructed a new facility for the Lima Police Department. Also during the 1960s, The <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a> established a regional campus in Lima.</p>
<p>Civil rights issues had rocked Lima in the 1950s, perhaps most prominently in the efforts to <a href="/wiki/Desegregation" title="Desegregation">desegregate</a> the city's only public swimming pool in Schoonover Park. Civil unrest continued in the 1960s and into the 1970s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rust_belt_decline">Rust belt decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rust belt decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1969, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> line in south Lima ruptured, causing 77,000 US gallons (290,000 L) of oil to escape into the city's sewer system. Explosions and fire erupted from sewers as 7,000 residents were evacuated. Governor <a href="/wiki/Jim_Rhodes" title="Jim Rhodes">Jim Rhodes</a> ordered the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_National_Guard" title="Ohio National Guard">Ohio National Guard</a> into the area to maintain order. In August 1970, further conflict erupted when a black woman was killed by police as she tried to prevent the arrest of a juvenile. Several officers were wounded in the violence that followed. Mayor Christian P. Morris declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> and the National Guard was again called in to aid local police.</p>
<p>During the 1970s and 1980s, a number of industries left Lima, part of the "<a href="/wiki/Rust_Belt" title="Rust Belt">Rust Belt</a>" decline affecting all of Ohio. In April 1971, the last "Cincinnatian," of the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_and_Ohio_Railroad" title="Baltimore and Ohio Railroad">Baltimore and Ohio Railroad</a> stopped in Lima. The Cincinnatian was an iconic lightweight <a href="/wiki/Streamliner" title="Streamliner">streamliner</a> serving the B&amp;O's Detroit line from Cincinnati. Lima had also been served by the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>'s "Broadway Limited," a high speed New York to Chicago service, the "Capital Limited" Chicago to Washington D.C. service, via Pittsburgh, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_Plate_Road" title="Nickel Plate Road">Nickel Plate Road</a>'s "Blue Arrow," and "Blue Dart," which provided high speed service to Buffalo, Cleveland and St. Louis, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erie_Lackawanna" title="Erie Lackawanna">Erie Lackawanna</a>'s "Lake Cities," which provided service to New York, Cleveland, and Chicago with direct service both ways. Many of these services were maintained by <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> until 1991, when the former Erie Lackawanna and Pennsylvania Railroad mainlines between New York and Chicago were downgraded.</p>
<p>In 1973, Lima's District <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">Tuberculosis</a> Center, which served five counties, closed its doors. <a href="/wiki/Superior_Coach_Company" title="Superior Coach Company">Superior Coach Company</a>, once the nation's largest producer of buses, closed in 1981, as did Clark Equipment. Airfoil Textron closed in 1985, and Sundstrand (formerly Westinghouse) followed ten years later. By the mid-1990s, Lima had lost more than 8,000 jobs. Lima's population dropped from 52,000 in the 1970s to 45,000 in 1999. Lima's plight and its subsequent efforts to re-define itself were captured in the <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> documentary <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lost_in_Middle_America&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lost in Middle America (page does not exist)">Lost in Middle America</a>.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lima.27s_oil_history">Lima's oil history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lima's oil history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>With the discovery of oil in Lima in 1885, Ohio began what came to be called the "Oil Boom of Northwest Ohio." Discovery actually began in <a href="/wiki/Findlay,_Ohio" title="Findlay, Ohio">Findlay, Ohio</a>, a city forty miles north of Lima. The discovery of natural gas deposits there in 1884 led to national marketing efforts advertising free gas, as Findlay's business leaders tried to "boom" the town. In 1885, Benjamin C. Faurot of Lima was one of hundreds of businessmen who visited Findlay to see the seemingly unlimited supply of natural gas burning day and night. Faurot owned the Lima Paper Mill. He spent $2,500 on energy consumption annually. Water for his operation was also a problem. So Faurot decided to drill in Lima – for gas or water. Faurot's first oil, found along the Ottawa River on May 19, 1885, was more accidental discovery than deliberate scientific experiment.</p>
<p>During the first week, the well produced more than 200 barrels (32 m<sup>3</sup>) of oil. Faurot quickly organized local businessmen into a syndicate that would purchase oil leases from farm owners. The company was called the Trenton Rock Oil Company, and by 1886, had 250 wells from Lima to <a href="/wiki/St._Marys,_Ohio" title="St. Marys, Ohio">St. Marys, Ohio</a>, and west to <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>.</p>
<p>When the news broke that northwest Ohio had oil, <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil" title="Standard Oil">Standard Oil</a> of Cleveland decided to build a <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">refinery</a> in Lima. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>'s oil, northwest Ohio's "sour crude" was high in <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> content, smelling like rotten eggs, and customers shunned it. Lima's new Solar Refinery was charged with solving the sulfur problem. Until then, Standard bought and stored as much northwest Ohio crude as was possible to maintain their monopoly. It dropped the price of crude from more than sixty cents a barrel to forty cents in an attempt to discourage further production.</p>
<p>Oil drilling fever hit northwest Ohio and "boom towns" sprang up overnight. Additional crude glutted the market, and trying to slow production, Standard Oil lowered its price to fifteen cents a barrel. This decision had little effect on the large producers elsewhere, but the smaller Lima producers, whose oil wells could not keep up, found themselves severely hampered. Fourteen independent Lima producers formed a combine – the Ohio Oil Company. Eventually, it became <a href="/wiki/Marathon_Oil" title="Marathon Oil">Marathon Oil</a>, still located in Findlay, Ohio.</p>
<p>Lima's Solar Refinery General Manager John Van Dyke and Herman Frasch, Standard's chemist, solved the distillation problem for sour crude by devising a method for removing the sulfur. The gamble that <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a> took building pipelines and storage tanks for Ohio's sour crude paid off. By 1901, the excitement about Ohio oil slowed with the news of a <a href="/wiki/Beaumont,_Texas" title="Beaumont, Texas">Beaumont, Texas</a>, gusher producing 100,000 barrels per day (16,000 m<sup>3</sup>/d).</p>
<p>In 1911, the courts declared Standard Oil Trust a monopoly and broke it into several companies. Between 1887 and 1905, the Lima Oil Field was a world-class producer, yielding 300 million barrels (48,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>). Lima was also a pipeline center. Within three years of the discovery of oil, a trunk line reached Chicago. Lima oil lit the buildings of the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">1893 World's Fair</a>. Production peaked in 1904, and then dropped off rapidly. By 1910, the field was regarded as virtually played out. Still, the Lima Refinery has survived, continuing to operate for more than 125 years under a succession of owners—Solar Refining Company (1886), a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil" title="Standard Oil">Standard Oil</a> until the breakup in 1911, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SOHIO" title="SOHIO">SOHIO</a> (1931), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Petroleum" title="British Petroleum">British Petroleum</a> (1987), Clark USA (1998), <a href="/wiki/Premcor" title="Premcor">Premcor</a> (2000), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valero_Energy_Corporation" title="Valero Energy Corporation">Valero Energy Corporation</a> (2005), and most recently <a href="/wiki/Husky_Energy" title="Husky Energy">Husky Energy</a> (2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Railroads_and_locomotives">Railroads and locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Railroads and locomotives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>For most of its history, smokestack industries and a <a href="/wiki/Blue-collar_worker" title="Blue-collar worker">blue-collar</a> <a href="/wiki/Work_ethic" title="Work ethic">work ethic</a> defined Lima. Nothing played a bigger part in shaping the city's self-image than its connection to railroads and railroading – as a Midwestern rail hub and even more as home to the <a href="/wiki/Lima_Locomotive_Works" title="Lima Locomotive Works">Lima Locomotive Works</a>, whose products for more than 70 years carried the city's name globally.</p>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotive</a> appeared in <a href="/wiki/Allen_County,_Ohio" title="Allen County, Ohio">Allen County</a> in 1854, brought in from <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a> as freight on the <a href="/wiki/Miami_and_Erie_Canal" title="Miami and Erie Canal">Miami and Erie Canal</a>. Named the Lima, the engine was used on construction of the county's first railroad, the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Fort_Wayne_and_Chicago_Railway" title="Pittsburgh, Fort Wayne and Chicago Railway">Ohio and Indiana</a>. East-west passenger service to Lima began in 1856, when the Ohio &amp; Indiana consolidated with the Pittsburgh, Fort Wayne &amp; Chicago. North-south passenger service began in 1858 on the Dayton &amp; Michigan Railroad. <a href="/wiki/Machine_shop" title="Machine shop">Machine shops</a> for the Dayton &amp; Michigan were built in Lima by 1860, and for the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie_and_Western_Railroad" title="Lake Erie and Western Railroad">Lake Erie and Western Railroad</a> by 1880. By the early years of the 20th century, the railroad shops employed 1,000 people in Lima.</p>
<p>In 1906, an average of 143 trains and 7,436 cars, carrying 223,080 tons of freight, passed through Lima every 24 hours. In addition, 49 steam and 28 electric trains landed passengers in Lima daily. Lima service on the electric interurban Ohio Western Railway began in 1902 and Lima became the hub of an interurban network that reached <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne,_Indiana" title="Fort Wayne, Indiana">Fort Wayne, Indiana</a>. In 1920, Lima was served by five steam railroads and Allen County by eight, in addition to five electric interurban lines.</p>
<p>For years, Lima was a crossroads for famous passenger trains including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_Plate_Road" title="Nickel Plate Road">Nickel Plate Road</a>'s <i>Clover Leaf</i> <i>Commercial Traveler</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Railroad" title="Erie Railroad">Erie Railroad</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Erie_Limited" title="Erie Limited">Erie Limited</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lake_Cities_(train)" title="Lake Cities (train)">Lake Cities</a>.</i> The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> train that in 1912 became known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Broadway_Limited" title="Broadway Limited">Broadway Limited</a></i> stopped in Lima from its inception in 1902 until 1990. Standard-bearer of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> in its "speed wars" with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Central_Railroad" title="New York Central Railroad">New York Central's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Limited" title="20th Century Limited">20th Century Limited</a></i>, <i>the Broadway Limited</i> catered to a rich and glamorous clientele, offering strictly first-class service between New York City and Chicago. At its peak, the <i>Broadway Limited</i> regularly averaged 60 mph (97 km/h) on its 900-mile (1,400 km) run. On a westbound run in the early morning of June 12, 1905, making up time after being held up by mechanical problems, the train was clocked just east of Lima at 127 mph (204 km/h).</p>
<p>Railroads began to cut back passenger service to Lima during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>. Electric interurban service ceased in 1937. After a brief boom for railroads during World War II, passenger service declined sharply in the 1950s. The Nickel Plate Road ended scheduled passenger service to Lima in 1959, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erie-Lackawanna" title="Erie-Lackawanna">Erie-Lackawanna</a> in 1970 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore_%26_Ohio" title="Baltimore &amp; Ohio">Baltimore &amp; Ohio</a> in 1971. Freight still moves over most of the historic rail routes in and out of the city, but the last passenger train to stop in Lima was the <i>Broadway Limited</i>, then operated by <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>, on November 11, 1990.</p>
<p>The enterprise that became the locomotive works – "the Loco," as it was commonly called in Lima – had its beginnings in 1869 when John Carnes and four partners bought a machine shop that was called the Lima Agricultural Works. The company initially manufactured and repaired agricultural equipment, then moved into the production of steam power equipment and <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmill</a> machinery. The shop designed its first narrow-gauge steam locomotive in 1878. The same year, the shop first worked on a geared locomotive designed by <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> lumberman <a href="/wiki/Ephraim_Shay" title="Ephraim Shay">Ephraim Shay</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Shay_locomotive" title="Shay locomotive">Shay locomotive</a> was built for steep grades, heavy loads and tight turns. In 1881, Shay granted the Lima works an exclusive license to manufacture his locomotives. By 1882, locomotives were the company's main product. In time, the Lima Locomotive Works – a name formally adopted in 1916 – would produce 2,761 Shay locomotives, which were sent to 48 states and 24 foreign countries. As of 2005, some were in use 100 years after they were shipped.</p>
<p>By 1910, the company was moving aggressively into direct-drive locomotives for general railroad use. A new "super power" design, introduced in 1925, enabled Lima to capture 20% of the national market for locomotives. The "super power" locomotive was created by mechanical engineer William E. Woodard. Designed to make more efficient use of <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> at high speed, it became, in the words of railroad historian Eric Hirsimaki, "one of the most influential locomotives in the history of steam power." Later years saw the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Ohio_Railway" title="Chesapeake and Ohio Railway">Chesapeake and Ohio Railway</a> 2-6-6-6, one of the largest locomotives ever built, and the glamorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Railroad" title="Southern Pacific Railroad">Southern Pacific</a> "Daylights," designed to complement the Pacific Coast scenery.</p>
<p>The locomotive works dabbled in other product lines. It produced railroad cars in the early years and acquired the Ohio Power Shovel Company in 1928. During World War II, the plant produced 1,655 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sherman_tank" title="Sherman tank">Sherman tanks</a>. Employment grew from 150 in the 1890s to 1,100 in 1912 and 2,000 in 1915, peaking at 4,300 in 1944. Over the course of its history, the Locomotive Works was a microcosm of the community, a place where each successive wave of newcomers took its place in turn. First the Germans and Italians, later African-Americans and ultimately women joining the work force during World War II. Labor organizing efforts were under way at the plant at least by the 1890s.</p>
<p>Post-war mergers attempting to keep the plant operating created the Lima-Hamilton Corporation in 1947 and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldwin-Lima-Hamilton" title="Baldwin-Lima-Hamilton">Baldwin-Lima-Hamilton</a> in 1950. The last <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> built at the plant, Nickel Plate No. 779, was delivered May 13, 1949. It is now on display in Lima's Lincoln Park. The final diesel locomotive, built by Lima-Hamilton, was delivered in 1951. After the end of locomotive production, the plant continued to produce cranes and road building equipment. The plant was sold to Clark Equipment in 1971. Clark employed 1,500 as late as 1974, but the plant closed permanently in 1981. As of 2006, the Lima Locomotive Works plant has been razed.</p>
<p>Currently, there are only a handful of railroads that serve Lima. The Chicago, Fort Wayne, and Eastern and the Indiana and Ohio railroad are owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genessee_%26_Wyoming" title="Genessee &amp; Wyoming">Genessee &amp; Wyoming</a> and are in the north and east parts of town. <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a> runs through town frequently and the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_Railway" title="Norfolk Southern Railway">Norfolk Southern Railway</a> has one train each day to Lima. The R.J. Corman Railroad/Western Ohio Line runs southwest from town.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 13.80 square miles (35.74 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 13.57 square miles (35.15 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 0.23 square miles (0.60 km<sup>2</sup>) is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_files_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer_files-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River_(Auglaize_River)" title="Ottawa River (Auglaize River)">Ottawa River</a> flows through the city. Locals sometimes refer to the river as "Hawg Creek". This resembles a traditional local name used dating back to the Hog Creek <a href="/wiki/Shawnee" title="Shawnee">Shawnee</a> community that existed between Lima and present Ada, prior to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Removal" title="Indian Removal">Shawnee removal</a> of 1831. This removal made possible the official founding of "Lima" as a formal town in that year.</p>
<p>Lima is at the intersection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Route_309_(Ohio)" title="State Route 309 (Ohio)">State Route 309</a> (the original <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Highway" title="Lincoln Highway">Lincoln Highway</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_75_(Ohio)" title="Interstate 75 (Ohio)">Interstate 75</a>, which replaced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Route_25_(Ohio)" title="U.S. Route 25 (Ohio)">U.S. Route 25</a>, one of the routes of the <a href="/wiki/Dixie_Highway" title="Dixie Highway">Dixie Highway</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lima's climate is largely reflective of the variant climate of Ohio. The region's climate can best be described as a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a>, according to the Koppen Climate Maps. The city lies roughly halfway between <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dayton" title="Dayton">Dayton</a>, Ohio and weather is at a similar medium. Summers are hot and humid and winters are generally cool to cold, with moderate precipitation year round. Due to the city's inland location the moderating effects of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a> are diminished, causing the city to experience higher averages in the summer and colder plunges in the winter than Toledo. On average the city sees around 4 more inches of snow annually than Dayton. During the summer months, Lima is prone to smog and allergens lingering when heat indexes exceed 85 °F.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_census">2010 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2010 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FactFinder_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FactFinder-16">[16]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 38,771 people, 14,221 households, and 8,319 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 2,857.1 inhabitants per square mile (1,103.1/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 16,784 housing units at an average density of 1,236.8 per square mile (477.5/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 67.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 26.4% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.3% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.5% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 1.2% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" title="Race (U.S. Census)">other races</a>, and 4.4% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 3.7% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 14,221 households of which 33.2% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 30.8% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 22.1% had a female householder with no husband present, 5.7% had a male householder with no wife present, and 41.5% were non-families. 33.5% of all households were made up of individuals and 11.3% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.42 and the average family size was 3.09.</p>
<p>The median age in the city was 32.9 years. 24.8% of residents were under the age of 18; 13.3% were between the ages of 18 and 24; 26.9% were from 25 to 44; 23.6% were from 45 to 64; and 11.4% were 65 years of age or older. The gender makeup of the city was 52.8% male and 47.2% female.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_census">2000 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2000 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the census<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-3">[3]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 40,081 people, 15,410 households, and 9,569 families residing in the city. The population density was 3,135.0 people per square mile (1,210.9/km²). There were 17,631 housing units at an average density of 1,379.0 per square mile (532.7/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 71.30% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 24.48% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.31% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.51% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.01% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.97% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 2.42% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 1.97% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 15,410 households out of which 31.9% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 37.3% were married couples living together, 19.7% had a female householder with no husband present, and 37.9% were non-families. 32.1% of all households were made up of individuals and 12.6% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.42 and the average family size was 3.06.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 27.2% under the age of 18, 11.5% from 18 to 24, 28.7% from 25 to 44, 19.4% from 45 to 64, and 13.3% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 33 years. For every 100 females there were 100.6 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 98.3 males.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Median_income" title="Median income">median income</a> for a household in the city was $27,067, and the median income for a family was $32,405. Males had a median income of $29,149 versus $22,100 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $13,882. About 19.2% of families and 22.7% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 33.3% of those under age 18 and 14.3% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Published authors from Lima have produced poetry collections, scholarly works, novels and memoirs.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Donald_Richie" title="Donald Richie">Donald Richie</a> first published "The Inland Sea" in 1970. It was later turned into a documentary on PBS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Harry Halsey Starrett was a Lima poet during the 1950s and 1960s.</li>
<li>Marilyn R. Stark has produced novels (e.g., <i>Broken Arrow, Broken Promises</i>) and historical nonfiction (e.g. 'Lima/Allen County, Ohio: a Pictorial History').</li>
<li>Lynn Lauber, a New York novelist, wrote <i>21 Sugar Street</i> and <i>White Girls</i>, both somewhat fictionalizing her Lima growing-up years during the 1960s (in "Union, Ohio").</li>
<li>Dr.Emmett Murray Jr. ("Duke"), a popular family physician, published two books of Lima-based memoirs: <i>Come Reminisce with Me</i> (2003) and <i>In the Company of Friends</i> (2008). In a tribute piece, the <a href="/wiki/Allen_County_Museum" title="Allen County Museum">Allen County Museum</a> website called him "the Mark Twain of Lima."</li>
<li>Doug Sutton-Ramspeck, writing under the name Doug Ramspeck, has published three full-length collections of poetry: <i>Black Tupelo Country</i> (BkMk Press: University of Missouri-Kansas City), <i>Mechanical Fireflies</i> (Barrow Street Press), and <i>Possum Nocturne</i> (NorthShore Press). He is also the author of a chapbook, <i>Where We Come From</i> (March Street Press), and several hundred poems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Diller" title="Phyllis Diller">Phyllis Diller</a> published an autobiography, <i>Like a Lampshade in a Whorehouse</i>, that describes her downtown Lima girlhood in detail.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lima_in_popular_culture">Lima in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Lima in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">Musical</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">comedy-drama</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/Glee_(TV_series)" title="Glee (TV series)">Glee</a></i> is set in the fictional William McKinley High School in Lima, Ohio, although the show is actually filmed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>.</p>
<p>Lima was also the focus of the 1999 TV documentary "Lost in Middle America (and What Happened Next)" directed by Scott Craig.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDB_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMDB-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Famed stand-up comic <a href="/wiki/Lenny_Bruce" title="Lenny Bruce">Lenny Bruce</a> did a comedy routine entitled "Lima, Ohio", in which he talked about the several weeks he once spent during the 1950s booked at a club in Lima. The routine appeared on his record album, "Lenny Bruce--American".</p>
<p>In the 1999 heist film, <a href="/wiki/The_Thomas_Crown_Affair_(1999_film)" title="The Thomas Crown Affair (1999 film)">The Thomas Crown Affair</a>, lead actress Rene Russo's character is an insurance adjuster from Lima, Ohio. Lead actor Piece Brosnan made a mistake during filming and pronounced Lima incorrectly.</p>
<p>The fictional killer of Buckwheat in 1983 episodes of Saturday Night Live, John David Stutts, was reported to be from Lima, Ohio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Client in the Charlie's Angels episode "Angels in Springtime" mentions that she is from Lima, Ohio.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lima_Symphony_Orchestra">Lima Symphony Orchestra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Lima Symphony Orchestra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1953, a committee composed of John LaRotonda, Ben Schultz, Dom Trovarelli and Fred Mills organized the Lima Symphony Orchestra. This committee selected Lawrence Burkhalter as the Symphony's first conductor and the LSO made its debut performance on May 23, 1954, in the Central High School auditorium.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lima is home to the Lima Express pro basketball team, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Northwestern_Ohio#Athletics" title="University of Northwestern Ohio">UNOH</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University,_Lima_Campus#Varsity_and_intramural_sports" title="Ohio State University, Lima Campus">OSU Lima</a> athletics, as well as various high school sports programs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Locally, Lima has had the same <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> mayor since the early 1980s. Federally, Lima is located in <a href="/wiki/Ohio%27s_4th_congressional_district" title="Ohio's 4th congressional district">Ohio's 4th congressional district</a>, which is represented by <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jordan_(U.S._politician)" title="Jim Jordan (U.S. politician)">Jim Jordan</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspaper">Newspaper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Newspaper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lima is served by one daily newspaper, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lima_News" title="The Lima News">The Lima News</a></i>. In addition to the immediate <a href="/wiki/Allen_County,_Ohio" title="Allen County, Ohio">Allen County</a> area, the paper serves residents in <a href="/wiki/Auglaize_County,_Ohio" title="Auglaize County, Ohio">Auglaize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hancock_County,_Ohio" title="Hancock County, Ohio">Hancock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardin_County,_Ohio" title="Hardin County, Ohio">Hardin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logan_County,_Ohio" title="Logan County, Ohio">Logan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercer_County,_Ohio" title="Mercer County, Ohio">Mercer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Putnam_County,_Ohio" title="Putnam County, Ohio">Putnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shelby_County,_Ohio" title="Shelby County, Ohio">Shelby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Van_Wert_County,_Ohio" title="Van Wert County, Ohio">Van Wert</a> counties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the 2016-2017 television season, Lima is ranked by <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Media_Research" title="Nielsen Media Research">Nielsen Media Research</a> as the second smallest television market in Ohio, ahead of only <a href="/wiki/Zanesville,_Ohio" title="Zanesville, Ohio">Zanesville</a>, and the 190th nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Lima area is served by four major broadcast stations, with three of those stations based in the city. <a href="/wiki/WLIO" title="WLIO">WLIO</a> 8 serves as its <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> affiliate (with <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a>/<a href="/wiki/MyNetworkTV" title="MyNetworkTV">MyNetworkTV</a> on <a href="/wiki/WLIO-DT2" title="WLIO-DT2">DT2</a>), while low-powered sister station <a href="/wiki/WOHL-CD" title="WOHL-CD">WOHL-CD</a> 35/<a href="/wiki/WLQP-LP" title="WLQP-LP">WLQP-LP</a> 25 operates as the market's <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> affiliate (with <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> on CD2/<a href="/wiki/WLMO-LP" title="WLMO-LP">WLMO-LP</a> 38). WLIO and WOHL-CD's chief rival is <a href="/wiki/WTLW" title="WTLW">WTLW</a> 44, a religious/family-entertainment station. The market's <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> member station, <a href="/wiki/WBGU-TV" title="WBGU-TV">WBGU-TV</a> 27, is based out of <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green_State_University" title="Bowling Green State University">Bowling Green State University</a> and also serves as a secondary <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> member station for the nearby <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a> market. <a href="/wiki/The_CW" title="The CW">CW</a> viewers are served on cable by that network's <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton</a> affiliate, <a href="/wiki/WBDT" title="WBDT">WBDT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first doctor in Allen County, Samuel Jacob Lewis, was assigned to duty at Fort Amanda in 1812.</p>
<p>Lima has been a regional medical center since its earliest days. Currently, the city's two hospitals serve a 10-county area of northwest and west central Ohio. <a href="/wiki/St._Rita%27s_Medical_Center" title="St. Rita's Medical Center">St. Rita's Medical Center</a>, a level 2 trauma center, with nearly 4,000 employees as of June 2006, is Allen County's largest employer while <a href="/wiki/Lima_Memorial_Health_System" title="Lima Memorial Health System">Lima Memorial Health System</a> ranks third. In 2005, St. Rita's embarked on a $130 million expansion expected to create up to 500 more jobs, this new addition is known as "The Medical Center of the Future".</p>
<p>The Roman Catholic Church <a href="/wiki/Sisters_of_Mercy" title="Sisters of Mercy">Sisters of Mercy</a> opened St. Rita's in December 1918, in the midst of a national (and global) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1918_influenza_epidemic" title="1918 influenza epidemic">influenza epidemic</a>. Since then, the hospital has grown dramatically, with major expansions launched in 1945 and 1967. The hospital has also created satellite facilities in the surrounding towns of <a href="/wiki/Ottawa,_Ohio" title="Ottawa, Ohio">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delphos,_Ohio" title="Delphos, Ohio">Delphos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wapakoneta,_Ohio" title="Wapakoneta, Ohio">Wapakoneta</a>. SRMC also houses a separate hospital with the walls of the main facility. This "interior" facility, "Triumph", was implemented to serve poverty-level citizens who are unable to afford continuing care otherwise. In July 2008, St. Rita's Medical Center purchased Lima Allen County Paramedics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Lima Allen County Paramedics was established in 1964 and since then has been a vital private emergency and non-emergency ambulance service in the area.</p>
<p>Lima Memorial Health System, formerly Lima Memorial Hospital, a level 2 trauma center, can trace its roots to 1899, when it began as Lima City Hospital. Formed by the Pastors Union of Lima, the 13-bed facility was the first community hospital in northwest Ohio. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the city of Lima helped to finance a larger hospital, which opened on <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> 1933 on the city's east side. The region's first open-heart surgery was performed at Lima Memorial on April 22, 1997. In 1999 LMHS entered into a Joint venture with Blanchard Valley Health Association ("BVHA") and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ProMedica_Health_System" title="ProMedica Health System">ProMedica Health System</a>. Lima Memorial Health System is currently undergoing an extensive remodel phase. The eight story patient tower is being converted to all private rooms, and all cardiac services will be combined to one area. A new surgery center is under construction at this time, and the installation a level 2 neonatal ICU is in the works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>For decades, Lima also had two other hospitals with strikingly different missions. The Ottawa Valley Hospital, which opened in 1909 as the District <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">Tuberculosis</a> Hospital, was one of the first in the state dedicated to the treatment of tubercular patients. The hospital treated patients from seven to 90 years old, at a time when tuberculosis was nearly always fatal. The average stay was three to five years. As treatment improved, the hospital closed, though the building was used until 1973.</p>
<p>A longer and stranger history is attached to the facility originally known as the Lima State Hospital for the Criminally Insane. Situated on 628 acres (2.54 km<sup>2</sup>) three miles (5 km) north of downtown Lima, the hospital was constructed between 1908 and 1915. Built at a cost of $2.1 million, it was the largest poured-concrete structure in the country until supplanted by the Pentagon.</p>
<p>For much of its history, Lima State Hospital functioned largely as a warehouse. Patients sometimes staged dramatic protests against the conditions of their confinement, and frequently escaped (more than 300 escapes by 1978). Conditions improved significantly after 1974 as a result of a class-action lawsuit filed on behalf of the patients. In a landmark ruling, US District Judge Nicholas J. Walinski spelled out detailed requirements for assuring each patient's rights to "dignity, privacy and human care." In its last years, the state hospital was used for the filming of a made-for-television movie about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attica_Prison_riots" title="Attica Prison riots">Attica Prison riots</a> in New York.</p>
<p>Starting in 1982, Lima State Hospital became a medium-security prison, the Lima Correctional Institution. The prison closed in 2004, though a smaller prison on the site, the Allen Correctional Institution, remains.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_architecture">Historic architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lima,_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Historic architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the city's most distinctive residential neighborhoods, the "Golden Block" on the west side, was almost entirely demolished in the 1960s; only the MacDonnell House, part of the <a href="/wiki/Allen_County_Museum" title="Allen County Museum">Allen County Museum</a>, and the YWCA survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Today, the city includes twenty-four buildings and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_district_(United_States)" title="Historic district (United States)">historic district</a> that are listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Allen_County_Courthouse_(Ohio)" title="Allen County Courthouse (Ohio)">Allen County Courthouse</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Post_Office_(Lima,_Ohio)" title="U.S. Post Office (Lima, Ohio)">post office</a>, the Hotel Argonne, and the <a href="/wiki/Neal_Clothing_Building" title="Neal Clothing Building">Neal Clothing Building</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
