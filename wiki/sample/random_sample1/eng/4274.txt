<p><b>Lysine</b> (abbreviated as <b>Lys</b> or <b>K</b>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> encoded by the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">codons</a> AAA and AAG, is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%91-amino_acid" title="Α-amino acid">α-amino acid</a> that is used in the biosynthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a>. It contains an α-amino group (which is in the protonated −NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> form under biological conditions), an α-carboxylic acid group (which is in the deprotonated −COO<sup>−</sup> form under biological conditions), and a side chain lysyl ((CH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>4</sub>NH<sub>2</sub>), classifying it as a charged (at physiological pH), aliphatic amino acid. It is essential in humans, meaning the body cannot synthesize it and thus it must be obtained from the diet.</p>
<p>Lysine is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_(chemistry)" title="Basic (chemistry)">base</a>, as are <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a>. The ε-amino group often participates in hydrogen bonding and as a general base in <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a>. The ε-amino group (NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup>) is attached to the fifth carbon from the α-carbon, which is attached to the carboxyl (C=OOH) group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posttranslational_modification" title="Posttranslational modification">posttranslational modifications</a> include methylation of the ε-amino group, giving <a href="/wiki/Methyllysine" title="Methyllysine">methyl-</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyllysine" title="Methyllysine">dimethyl-</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methyllysine" title="Methyllysine">trimethyllysine</a> (the latter occurring in <a href="/wiki/Calmodulin" title="Calmodulin">calmodulin</a>); also <a href="/wiki/Acetylation" title="Acetylation">acetylation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumoylation" title="Sumoylation">sumoylation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ubiquitination" title="Ubiquitination">ubiquitination</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylation" title="Hydroxylation">hydroxylation</a> - <a href="/wiki/Lysyl_hydroxylase" title="Lysyl hydroxylase">producing</a> the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylysine" title="Hydroxylysine">hydroxylysine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> and other proteins. <i>O</i>-<a href="/wiki/Glycosylation" title="Glycosylation">Glycosylation</a> of hydroxylysine residues in the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Golgi_apparatus" title="Golgi apparatus">Golgi apparatus</a> is used to mark certain proteins for <a href="/wiki/Secretion" title="Secretion">secretion</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opsins" title="Opsins">opsins</a> like <a href="/wiki/Rhodopsin" title="Rhodopsin">rhodopsin</a> and the visual opsins (encoded by the genes <a href="/wiki/OPN1SW" title="OPN1SW">OPN1SW</a>, <a href="/wiki/OPN1MW" title="OPN1MW">OPN1MW</a>, and <a href="/wiki/OPN1LW" title="OPN1LW">OPN1LW</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retinaldehyde" title="Retinaldehyde">retinaldehyde</a> forms a <a href="/wiki/Schiff_base" title="Schiff base">Schiff base</a> with a conserved lysine residue, and interaction of light with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retinylidene" title="Retinylidene">retinylidene</a> group causes signal transduction in <a href="/wiki/Color_vision" title="Color vision">color vision</a> (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_cycle" title="Visual cycle">visual cycle</a> for details). Deficiencies may cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a>, as well as many other problems due to its ubiquitous presence in proteins.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As an essential amino acid, lysine is not synthesized in animals, hence it must be ingested as lysine or lysine-containing proteins. In plants and most bacteria, it is synthesized from <a href="/wiki/Aspartic_acid" title="Aspartic acid">aspartic acid</a> (aspartate):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacyc-pw_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-metacyc-pw-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stereoisomerism#Diastereomers" title="Stereoisomerism">L</a>-aspartate is first converted to L-aspartyl-4-phosphate by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartokinase" title="Aspartokinase">aspartokinase</a> (or Aspartate kinase). <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> is needed as an energy source for this step.</li>
<li>β-Aspartate semialdehyde <a href="/wiki/Dehydrogenase" title="Dehydrogenase">dehydrogenase</a> converts this into β-aspartyl-4-semialdehyde (or β-aspartate-4-semialdehyde). Energy from <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide_phosphate" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate">NADPH</a> is used in this conversion.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate_synthase" title="4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase</a> adds a <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvate</a> group to the β-aspartyl-4-semialdehyde, and a water molecule is removed. This causes <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">cyclization</a> and gives rise to (2S,4S)-4-hydroxy-2,3,4,5-tetrahydrodipicolinate.</li>
<li>This product is reduced to 2,3,4,5-tetrahydrodipicolinate (or Δ<sup>1</sup>-piperidine-2,6-dicarboxylate, in the figure: (S)-2,3,4,5-tetrahydropyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydrodipicolinate_reductase" title="Dihydrodipicolinate reductase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate reductase</a>. This reaction consumes an NADPH molecule and releases a second water molecule.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrodipicolinate_N-acetyltransferase" title="Tetrahydrodipicolinate N-acetyltransferase">Tetrahydrodipicolinate N-acetyltransferase</a> opens this ring and gives rise to N-succinyl-L-2-amino-6-oxoheptanedionate (or N-acyl-2-amino-6-oxopimelate). Two water molecules and one <a href="/wiki/Acyl-CoA" title="Acyl-CoA">acyl-CoA</a> (succinyl-CoA) enzyme are used in this reaction.</li>
<li>N-succinyl-L-2-amino-6-oxoheptanedionate is converted into N-succinyl-LL-2,6-diaminoheptanedionate (N-acyl-2,6-diaminopimelate). This reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme succinyl diaminopimelate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aminotransferase" title="Aminotransferase">aminotransferase</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Glutamic_acid" title="Glutamic acid">glutamic acid</a> molecule is used in this reaction and an <a href="/wiki/Oxoacid" title="Oxoacid">oxoacid</a> is produced as a byproduct.</li>
<li>N-succinyl-LL-2,6-diaminoheptanedionate (N-acyl-2,6-diaminopimelate)is converted into LL-2,6-diaminoheptanedionate (L,L-2,6-diaminopimelate) by succinyl diaminopimelate desuccinylase (acyldiaminopimelate deacylase). A water molecule is consumed in this reaction and a succinate is produced a byproduct.</li>
<li>LL-2,6-diaminoheptanedionate is converted by <a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_epimerase" title="Diaminopimelate epimerase">diaminopimelate epimerase</a> into meso-2,6-diamino-heptanedionate (meso-2,6-diaminopimelate).</li>
<li>Finally, meso-2,6-diamino-heptanedionate is converted into L-lysine by <a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_decarboxylase" title="Diaminopimelate decarboxylase">diaminopimelate decarboxylase</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_Biosynthesis.png"><img alt="Lysine Biosynthesis.png" data-file-height="2130" data-file-width="2227" height="478" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/500px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/750px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/1000px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png 2x" width="500"/></a></p>
<p>Enzymes involved in this biosynthesis include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacyc-pw_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-metacyc-pw-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartokinase" title="Aspartokinase">Aspartokinase</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aspartate-semialdehyde_dehydrogenase" title="Aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase">Aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate_synthase" title="4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydrodipicolinate_reductase" title="Dihydrodipicolinate reductase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate reductase</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2,3,4,5-tetrahydropyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate_N-succinyltransferase" title="2,3,4,5-tetrahydropyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate N-succinyltransferase">2,3,4,5-tetrahydropyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate N-succinyltransferase</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succinyldiaminopimelate_transaminase" title="Succinyldiaminopimelate transaminase">Succinyldiaminopimelate transaminase</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succinyl-diaminopimelate_desuccinylase" title="Succinyl-diaminopimelate desuccinylase">Succinyl-diaminopimelate desuccinylase</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_epimerase" title="Diaminopimelate epimerase">Diaminopimelate epimerase</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_decarboxylase" title="Diaminopimelate decarboxylase">Diaminopimelate decarboxylase</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>It is worth noting, however, that in fungi, euglenoids and some prokaryotes lysine is synthesized via the <a href="/wiki/Alpha-aminoadipate_pathway" title="Alpha-aminoadipate pathway">alpha-aminoadipate pathway</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lysine is <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolised</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl-CoA</a>, via an initial <a href="/wiki/Transamination" title="Transamination">transamination</a> with <a href="/wiki/Alpha-Ketoglutaric_acid" title="Alpha-Ketoglutaric acid">α-ketoglutarate</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> degradation of lysine yields <a href="/wiki/Cadaverine" title="Cadaverine">cadaverine</a> by <a href="/wiki/Decarboxylation" title="Decarboxylation">decarboxylation</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allysine" title="Allysine">Allysine</a> is a derivative of lysine, used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Elastin" title="Elastin">elastin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a>. It is produced by the actions of the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Lysyl_oxidase" title="Lysyl oxidase">lysyl oxidase</a> on lysine in the extracellular matrix and is essential in the crosslink formation that stabilizes collagen and elastin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Synthetic, racemic lysine has long been known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A practical synthesis starts from <a href="/wiki/Caprolactam" title="Caprolactam">caprolactam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Industrially, L-lysine is usually manufactured by a fermentation process using <i>Corynebacterium glutamicum</i>; production exceeds 600,000 tons a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>L-lysine HCl is used as a dietary supplement, providing 80.03% L-lysine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DSBI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-DSBI-7">[7]</a></sup> As such, 1 g of L-lysine is contained in 1.25 g of L-lysine HCl.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_sources">Dietary sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dietary sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutritional requirement</a> per day, in milligrams of lysine per kilogram of body weight, is: infants (3–4 months) 103 mg/kg, children (2 years) 64 mg/kg, older children (10–12 years) 44 to 60 mg/kg, adults 12 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For a 70 kg adult, 12 milligrams of lysine per kilogram of body weight is 0.84 grams of lysine. Recommendations for adults have been revised upwards to 30 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Good sources of lysine are high-protein foods such as eggs, <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> (specifically red meat, lamb, pork, and poultry), <a href="/wiki/Soy_protein" title="Soy protein">soy</a>, beans and peas, cheese (particularly Parmesan), and certain fish (such as <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lysine is the <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid#Use_of_essential_amino_acids" title="Essential amino acid">limiting amino acid</a> (the essential amino acid found in the smallest quantity in the particular foodstuff) in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereal_grain" title="Cereal grain">cereal grains</a>, but is plentiful in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulse_(legume)" title="Pulse (legume)">pulses</a> (legumes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VRYoung_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-VRYoung-11">[11]</a></sup> Consequently, meals that combine cereal grains and legumes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dal" title="Dal">dal</a> with <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_cuisine" title="Middle Eastern cuisine">Middle Eastern</a> <a href="/wiki/Hummus" title="Hummus">hummus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ful_medames" title="Ful medames">ful medames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falafel" title="Falafel">falafel</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pita_bread" title="Pita bread">pita bread</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_cuisine" title="Mexican cuisine">Mexican</a> beans with rice or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tortilla" title="Tortilla">tortilla</a> have arisen to provide <a href="/wiki/Complete_protein" title="Complete protein">complete protein</a> in diets that are, by choice or by necessity, <a href="/wiki/Vegetarianism" title="Vegetarianism">vegetarian</a>. A food is considered to have sufficient lysine if it has at least 51 mg of lysine per gram of protein (so that the protein is 5.1% lysine).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>L-Lysine plays a major role in <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_(small_intestine)" title="Absorption (small intestine)">absorption</a>; building <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> protein; recovering from <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a> or sports injuries; and the body's production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormones" title="Hormones">hormones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibodies</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modifications">Modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lysine can be modified through <a href="/wiki/Acetylation" title="Acetylation">acetylation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Acetyllysine" title="Acetyllysine">acetyllysine</a>), <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">methylation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Methyllysine" title="Methyllysine">methyllysine</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ubiquitination" title="Ubiquitination">ubiquitination</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumoylation" title="Sumoylation">sumoylation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neddylation" title="Neddylation">neddylation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotinylation" title="Biotinylation">biotinylation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pupylation" title="Pupylation">pupylation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">carboxylation</a>, which tends to modify the function of the protein of which the modified lysine residue(s) are a part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17681659_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17681659-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A systematic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochrane_Review" title="Cochrane Review">Cochrane Review</a> (investigating all clinical trials, <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i> studies and <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_of_action" title="Mechanism of action">mechanism of action</a>) published in 2015 showed there is no evidence that lysine supplementation is effective against <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex_virus" title="Herpes simplex virus">herpes simplex virus</a> and it has not been approved by the FDA for herpes simplex suppression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some studies have shown that lysine supplementation can decrease cold sore outbreaks and reduce healing time. Diets high in Arginine feed the herpes simplex virus and increase replication rate, while diets high in lysine can decrease replication rate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lysine has <a href="/wiki/Anxiolytic" title="Anxiolytic">anxiolytic</a> action through its effects on <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a> receptors in the intestinal tract, and is also hypothesized to reduce anxiety through serotonin regulation in the amygdala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smriga2_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smriga2-38">[38]</a></sup> One study on rats<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smriga1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smriga1-39">[39]</a></sup> showed that overstimulation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5-HT4" title="5-HT4">5-HT4</a> receptors in the gut are associated with anxiety-induced intestinal pathology. Lysine, acting as a serotonin <a href="/wiki/Receptor_antagonist" title="Receptor antagonist">antagonist</a> and therefore reducing the overactivity of these receptors, reduced signs of anxiety and anxiety-induced diarrhea in the sample population. Another study showed that lysine deficiency leads to a pathological increase in serotonin in the <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a>, a brain structure that is involved in emotional regulation and the stress response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smriga2_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smriga2-38">[38]</a></sup> Human studies have also shown correlations between reduced lysine intake and anxiety. A population-based study in Syria included 93 families whose diet is primarily grain-based and therefore likely to be deficient in lysine. Fortification of grains with lysine was shown to reduce markers of anxiety, including cortisol levels; Smiriga and colleagues hypothesized that anxiety reduction from lysine occurs through mechanism of serotonin alterations in the central amygdala; older primary research reports hypothesized lysine to reduce anxiety through the potentiation of <a href="/wiki/Benzodiazepine" title="Benzodiazepine">benzodiazepine</a> receptors (common targets of anxiolytic drugs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xanax" title="Xanax">Xanax</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ativan" title="Ativan">Ativan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smriga3_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smriga3-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are lysine conjugates that show promise in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, by causing cancerous cells to destroy themselves when the drug is combined with the use of <a href="/wiki/Light_therapy" title="Light therapy">phototherapy</a>, while leaving non-cancerous cells unharmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lysineconjugates_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-lysineconjugates-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lysine deficiency causes <a href="/wiki/Immunodeficiency" title="Immunodeficiency">immunodeficiency</a> in chickens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> One cause of relative lysine deficiency is <a href="/wiki/Cystinuria" title="Cystinuria">cystinuria</a>, where there is impaired hepatic resorption of basic, or positively charged amino acids, including lysine. The accompanying urinary cysteine results because the same deficient amino acid transporter is normally present in the kidney as well.</p>
<p>Limited studies suggest that a high-lysine diet or <small>L</small>-lysine monochloride supplements may have a moderating effect on blood pressure and the incidence of stroke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lysine is attached to the <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-amphetamine" title="D-amphetamine">amphetamine</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Lisdexamfetamine" title="Lisdexamfetamine">lisdexamfetamine</a>, marketed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vyvanse" title="Vyvanse">Vyvanse</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_lysine_in_animal_feed">Use of lysine in animal feed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use of lysine in animal feed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lysine production for animal feed is a major global industry, reaching in 2009 almost 700,000 tonnes for a market value of over €1.22 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lysineanimalfeed1_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-lysineanimalfeed1-44">[44]</a></sup> Lysine is an important additive to animal feed because it is a limiting amino acid when optimizing the growth of certain animals such as <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pigs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a> for the production of meat. Lysine supplementation allows for the use of lower-cost plant protein (<a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, for instance, rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soy" title="Soy">soy</a>) while maintaining high growth rates, and limiting the pollution from nitrogen excretion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lysineanimalfeed_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-lysineanimalfeed-45">[45]</a></sup> In turn, however, phosphate pollution is a major environmental cost when corn is used as feed for poultry and swine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lysine is industrially produced by microbial fermentation, from a base mainly of sugar. Genetic engineering research is actively pursuing bacterial strains to improve the efficiency of production and allow lysine to be made from other substrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lysineanimalfeed1_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-lysineanimalfeed1-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lysine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1993 film <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> (based on the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> novel <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(novel)" title="Jurassic Park (novel)">of the same name</a>) features <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> that were <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically altered</a> so that they could not produce lysine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> This was known as the "lysine contingency" and was supposed to prevent the <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">cloned</a> dinosaurs from surviving outside the park, forcing them to be dependent on lysine supplements provided by the park's veterinary staff. In reality, no <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> are capable of producing lysine (it is an <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acid</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lysine is the favorite amino acid of the character Sheldon Cooper in the television show, <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Bang_Theory" title="The Big Bang Theory">The Big Bang Theory</a>. It was mentioned in season 2, episode 13, "The Friendship Algorithm". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, lysine became the focus of a <a href="/wiki/Lysine_price-fixing_conspiracy" title="Lysine price-fixing conspiracy">price-fixing case</a>, the largest in United States history. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archer_Daniels_Midland_Company" title="Archer Daniels Midland Company">Archer Daniels Midland Company</a> paid a fine of US$100 million, and three of its executives were convicted and served prison time. Also found guilty in the price-fixing case were two Japanese firms (<a href="/wiki/Ajinomoto" title="Ajinomoto">Ajinomoto</a>, Kyowa Hakko) and a South Korean firm (Sewon).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Secret video recordings of the conspirators fixing lysine's price can be found online or by requesting the video from the U.S. Department of Justice, Antitrust Division. This case served as the basis of the movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Informant!" title="The Informant!">The Informant!</a></i>, and a <a href="/wiki/The_Informant_(book)" title="The Informant (book)">book of the same title</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
