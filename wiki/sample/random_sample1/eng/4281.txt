<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Light pen</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>A <b>light pen</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> <a href="/wiki/Input_device" title="Input device">input device</a> in the form of a light-sensitive wand used in conjunction with a computer's <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">CRT display</a>.</p>
<p>It allows the user to point to displayed objects or draw on the screen in a similar way to a <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a> but with greater positional accuracy. A light pen can work with any CRT-based display and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Display_technology" title="Display technology">display technologies</a>, but its ability to be used with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCDs</a> was unclear (though Toshiba and Hitachi displayed a similar idea at the "Display 2006" show in Japan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>A light pen detects a change of brightness of nearby screen pixels when scanned by <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a> electron beam and communicates the timing of this event to the computer. Since a CRT scans the entire screen one pixel at a time, the computer can keep track of the expected time of scanning various locations on screen by the beam and infer the pen's position from the latest timestamp.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_pen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first light pen was created around 1955 as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whirlwind_(computer)" title="Whirlwind (computer)">Whirlwind</a> project at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1960s light pens were common on graphics terminals such as the <a href="/wiki/IBM_2250" title="IBM 2250">IBM 2250</a>, and were also available for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_3270" title="IBM 3270">IBM 3270</a> text-only terminal.</p>
<p>Light pen usage was expanded in the early 1980s to personal computers such as the <a href="/wiki/Fairlight_CMI" title="Fairlight CMI">Fairlight CMI</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Micro" title="BBC Micro">BBC Micro</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> compatible <a href="/wiki/Color_Graphics_Adapter" title="Color Graphics Adapter">CGA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hercules_Graphics_Card" title="Hercules Graphics Card">HGC</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Graphics_Adapter" title="Enhanced Graphics Adapter">EGA</a> graphics cards also featured a connector for a light pen, as did the <a href="/wiki/Thomson_MO5" title="Thomson MO5">Thomson MO5</a> computer family, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit" title="Atari 8-bit">Atari 8-bit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Commodore_8-bit_family" title="Category:Commodore 8-bit family">Commodore 8-bit</a> home computers.</p>
<p>Because the user was required to hold his arm in front of the screen for long periods of time (potentially causing "<a href="/wiki/Touchscreen#Gorilla_arm" title="Touchscreen">gorilla arm</a>") or to use a desk that tilts the monitor, the light pen fell out of use as a general purpose input device.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
