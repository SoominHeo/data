<p><b>Raetia</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small>Latin: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Latin" title="Help:IPA for Latin">[rajtia]</a></span>, also spelled <b>Rhaetia</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">province</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, named after the Rhaetian (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raeti" title="Raeti">Raeti</a></i> or <i>Rhaeti</i>) people. It bordered on the west with the <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Helvetii" title="Helvetii">Helvetii</a>, on the east with <a href="/wiki/Noricum" title="Noricum">Noricum</a>, on the north with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vindelicia" title="Vindelicia">Vindelicia</a>, on the west with <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Narbonensis" title="Gallia Narbonensis">Transalpine Gaul</a> and on south with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italia_(Roman_Empire)" title="Italia (Roman Empire)">Venetia et Histria</a>. It thus comprised the districts occupied in modern times by eastern and central <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (containing the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine" title="Upper Rhine">Upper Rhine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a>), southern <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Swabia" title="Upper Swabia">Upper Swabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vorarlberg" title="Vorarlberg">Vorarlberg</a>, the greater part of <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tirol</a>, and part of <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>. Later Vindelicia (today south-eastern Wuerttemberg and south-western Bavaria) formed part of Raetia. The northern border of Raetia during the times of Augustus and Tiberius was the River Danube. Later the <a href="/wiki/Limes_Germanicus" title="Limes Germanicus">Limes Germanicus</a> marked the northern boundary, stretching for 166 km north of the Danube. Raetia linked to Italy across the Alps over the <a href="/wiki/Reschen_Pass" title="Reschen Pass">Reschen Pass</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Via_Claudia_Augusta" title="Via Claudia Augusta">Via Claudia Augusta</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Romansh_people" title="Romansh people">Romansh people</a> living in Southeast Switzerland are believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be direct descendants of the Raetians. However, the exact lineage of the Romansh (or Romansch) people remains incomplete.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raetia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Switzerland_in_the_Roman_era" title="Switzerland in the Roman era">Switzerland in the Roman era</a></div>
<p>Little is known of the origin or history of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raetians" title="Raetians">Raetians</a>, who appear in the records as one of the most powerful and warlike of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpine</a> tribes. <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a> states distinctly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> that they were of <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscan</a> origin (a belief that was favored by <a href="/wiki/Barthold_Georg_Niebuhr" title="Barthold Georg Niebuhr">Niebuhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Mommsen" title="Theodor Mommsen">Mommsen</a>). A tradition reported by <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> affirmed that they were a portion of that people who had settled in the plains of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po</a> and were driven into the mountains by the invading <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>, when they assumed the name of "Raetians" from an <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponymous</a> leader <b>Raetus</b>. Even if their Etruscan origin be accepted, at the time when the land became known to the Romans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celt" title="Celt">Celtic</a> tribes were already in possession of it and had amalgamated so completely with the original inhabitants that, generally speaking, the Raetians of later times may be regarded as a Celtic people, although non-Celtic tribes (es. <a href="/wiki/Euganei" title="Euganei">Euganei</a>) were settled among them.</p>
<p>The Raetians are first mentioned (but only incidentally) by <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and little is heard of them till after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Republic</a>. There is little doubt, however, that they retained their independence until their subjugation in 15 BC by <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus" title="Nero Claudius Drusus">Drusus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At first Raetia formed a distinct province, but towards the end of the 1st century AD Vindelicia was added to it; hence <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus" title="Gaius Cornelius Tacitus">Tacitus</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Germania_(book)" title="Germania (book)">Germania</a></i>, 41) could speak of <i>Augusta Vindelicorum</i> (<a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>) as "a <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a> of the province of Raetia". The whole province (including Vindelicia) was at first under a military <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">prefect</a>, then under a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procurator_(Roman)" title="Procurator (Roman)">procurator</a>; it had no standing army quartered in it but relied on its own native troops and <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> for protection until the 2nd century AD.</p>
<p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, Raetia was governed by the commander of the <a href="/wiki/Legio_III_Italica" title="Legio III Italica">Legio III <i>Italica</i></a>, which was based in Castra Regina (<a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>) by 179 AD. Under <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>, Raetia formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> of the <i>vicarius Italiae</i>, and was subdivided into <i>Raetia prima</i>, with a <i>praeses</i> at Curia Raetorum (<a href="/wiki/Chur" title="Chur">Chur</a>) and <i>Raetia secunda</i>, with a <i>praeses</i> at Augusta Vindelicorum (Augsburg), the former corresponding to the old Raetia, the latter to Vindelicia. The boundary between them is not clearly defined, but may be stated generally as a line drawn eastwards from the <i>lacus Brigantinus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a>) to the <i>Oenus</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Inn" title="River Inn">River Inn</a>).</p>
<p>During the last years of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, the land was in a desolate condition, but its occupation by the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a> in the time of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodoric_the_Great" title="Theodoric the Great">Theodoric the Great</a>, who placed it under a <i>dux</i>, to some extent revived its prosperity. Much of <i>Raetia prima</i> remained as a separate political unit, <a href="/wiki/Raetia_Curiensis" title="Raetia Curiensis">Raetia Curiensis</a>, for several centuries, until it was attached to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Swabia" title="Duchy of Swabia">Duchy of Swabia</a> in AD 917.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raetia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The land was very mountainous, and the inhabitants, when not engaged in predatory expeditions, chiefly supported themselves by breeding cattle and cutting timber, little attention being paid to agriculture. Some of the valleys, however, were rich and fertile, and produced wine, which was considered equal to any in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italia_(Roman_province)" title="Italia (Roman province)">Italia</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" title="Augustus Caesar">Augustus Caesar</a> preferred Raetian wine to any other. Considerable trade in <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">pitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> occurred.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_geography">Human geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raetia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Human geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chief towns of Raetia (excluding Vindelicia) were <i>Tridentum</i> (<a href="/wiki/Trento" title="Trento">Trento</a>) and <i>Curia</i> (Coire or <a href="/wiki/Chur" title="Chur">Chur</a>). It was traversed by two great lines of Roman roads — the Via Claudia Augusta leading from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verona,_Italy" title="Verona, Italy">Verona</a> and Tridentum across the <a href="/wiki/Reschen_Pass" title="Reschen Pass">Reschen Pass</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Fern_Pass" title="Fern Pass">Fern Pass</a> and thence to <i>Augusta Vindelicorum</i> (<a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the other from <i>Brigantium</i> (<a href="/wiki/Bregenz" title="Bregenz">Bregenz</a>) on Lake Constance by Chur and <a href="/wiki/Chiavenna" title="Chiavenna">Chiavenna</a> to <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A4tikon" title="Rätikon">Rätikon</a> mountain range derives its name from Raetia.</p>

<div id="History" style="font-size:114%">History</div>
</th>
</tr>
<tr style="height:2px">
<td colspan="2"></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td class="navbox-list navbox-odd" colspan="2" style="width:100%;padding:0px">
<div style="padding:0em 0.25em">As found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Notitia_Dignitatum" title="Notitia Dignitatum">Notitia Dignitatum</a></i>. Provincial administration reformed and <a href="/wiki/Roman_diocese" title="Roman diocese">dioceses</a> established by <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a> 293. Permanent <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture" title="Praetorian prefecture">praetorian prefectures</a> established after the death of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a>. Empire permanently partitioned after 395. Exarchates of <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Ravenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Africa" title="Exarchate of Africa">Africa</a> established after 584. After massive territorial losses in the 7th century, the remaining provinces were superseded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_system" title="Theme system">theme system</a> in c. 640–660, although in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a> and parts of Greece they survived under the themes until the early 9th century.</div>
