<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Reichenbach Falls</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Reichenbach Falls</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reichenbachfall</i></span>) are a series of <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfalls</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reichenbach_stream" title="Reichenbach stream">Reichenbach stream</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernese_Oberland" title="Bernese Oberland">Bernese Oberland</a> region of Switzerland. They have a total drop of 250 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a> (820 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>). At 90 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a> (300 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>), the Upper Reichenbach Falls is one of the highest waterfalls in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. Today, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydro-electric" title="Hydro-electric">hydro-electric</a> power company harnesses the flow of the Reichenbach Falls during certain times of year, reducing its flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfpdf_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-rfpdf-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In popular literature, Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a> gave the falls as the location of the final physical altercation between his <a href="/wiki/Hero" title="Hero">hero</a> <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> with his greatest foe, the criminal <a href="/wiki/Professor_Moriarty" title="Professor Moriarty">Professor Moriarty</a> in "<a href="/wiki/The_Final_Problem" title="The Final Problem">The Final Problem</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reichenbach_Falls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The falls are located in the lower part of the <a href="/wiki/Reichenbachtal" title="Reichenbachtal">Reichenbachtal valley</a>, on the Reichenbach stream, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Aare" title="Aare">Aare</a>. They are some 2 km (1.2 mi) south of the town of <a href="/wiki/Meiringen" title="Meiringen">Meiringen</a>, and 25 km (16 mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Interlaken" title="Interlaken">Interlaken</a>. Politically, the falls are within the <a href="/wiki/Schattenhalb" title="Schattenhalb">municipality of Schattenhalb</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Bern" title="Canton of Bern">canton of Bern</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfpdf_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-rfpdf-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swmap_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-swmap-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The falls are made accessible by the <a href="/wiki/Reichenbachfall_funicular" title="Reichenbachfall funicular">Reichenbachfall funicular</a>. The lower station is some 20 minutes walk, or a 6-minute bus ride, from <a href="/wiki/Meiringen_railway_station" title="Meiringen railway station">Meiringen station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%BCnig_railway_line" title="Brünig railway line">Brünig railway line</a> that links Interlaken and <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfpdf_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-rfpdf-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swmap_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-swmap-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reichenbach_Falls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthur_Conan_Doyle.27s_Sherlock_Holmes_stories">Arthur Conan Doyle's <i>Sherlock Holmes</i> stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reichenbach_Falls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town and the falls are known worldwide as the setting for a fictional event: it is the location where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Sir Arthur Conan Doyle">Sir Arthur Conan Doyle</a>'s hero, <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a>, fights to the death with <a href="/wiki/Professor_Moriarty" title="Professor Moriarty">Professor Moriarty</a>, at the end of "<a href="/wiki/The_Final_Problem" title="The Final Problem">The Final Problem</a>", first published in 1893. A memorial plate at the <a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">funicular</a> station commemorates Holmes and there is also a Sherlock Holmes museum in the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Meiringen" title="Meiringen">Meiringen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plobit_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-plobit-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Out of many <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfalls</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernese_Oberland" title="Bernese Oberland">Bernese Oberland</a>, the Reichenbach Falls seems to have made the greatest impression on Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, who was shown them on a Swiss holiday by his host <a href="/wiki/Henry_Simpson_Lunn" title="Henry Simpson Lunn">Sir Henry Lunn</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Lunn_Poly" title="Lunn Poly">Lunn Poly</a>. Sir Henry's grandson, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lunn" title="Peter Lunn">Peter Lunn</a>, recalled, "My grandfather said 'Push him over the Reichenbach Falls' and Conan Doyle hadn’t heard of them, so he showed them to him." So impressed was Doyle that he decided to let his hero die there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plobit_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-plobit-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The actual ledge from which Moriarty fell is on the other side of the falls from the funicular; it is accessible by climbing the path to the top of the falls, crossing the bridge and following the trail down the hill. The ledge is marked by a plaque as illustrated here; the English inscription reads: "At this fearful place, Sherlock Holmes vanquished Professor Moriarty, on 4 May 1891." The pathway on which the duel between Sherlock Holmes and Professor Moriarty occurs ends some hundred metres away from the falls. When Doyle viewed the falls, the path ended very close to the falls, close enough to touch it, yet over the hundred years after his visit, the pathway has become unsafe and slowly eroded away, and the falls have receded farther back into the gorge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plobit_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-plobit-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_media">In other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reichenbach_Falls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Reichenbach Falls are the subject of several early 19th-century paintings by the English <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> landscape painter <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The indie band <a href="/wiki/Ravens_%26_Chimes" title="Ravens &amp; Chimes">Ravens &amp; Chimes</a> named its debut album (released in 2007) after the falls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Reichenbach_Falls_(film)" title="Reichenbach Falls (film)">Reichenbach Falls</a></i> was also the title of a 2008 <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">BBC Four</a> TV drama by James Mavor, based on an idea by <a href="/wiki/Ian_Rankin" title="Ian Rankin">Ian Rankin</a> and set in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>. Numerous historical characters associated with the city, including Conan Doyle and his mentor Dr <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bell" title="Joseph Bell">Joseph Bell</a>, are mentioned in the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes:_A_Game_of_Shadows" title="Sherlock Holmes: A Game of Shadows">Sherlock Holmes: A Game of Shadows</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/2011_in_film" title="2011 in film">2011 film</a> adaptation inspired by "The Final Problem", also hosts the falls, although in this adaptation, a large castle has been built over them, replacing the pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The third episode from the 2012 second series of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_(TV_series)" title="Sherlock (TV series)">Sherlock</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/The_Reichenbach_Fall" title="The Reichenbach Fall">The Reichenbach Fall</a>" (inspired by "The Final Problem"), is a play on the waterfall's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-woll_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-woll-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crompton_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-crompton-13">[13]</a></sup> The special episode of Sherlock, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Abominable_Bride" title="The Abominable Bride">The Abominable Bride</a></i>, which was broadcast on 1 January 2016, featured a re-creation of the showdown between Sherlock and Moriarty set in Victorian times, as depicted in the book.</p>
