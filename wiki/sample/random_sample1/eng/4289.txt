<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rāja yoga</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about a type of yoga. <p><b>Rāja yoga</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a term with a variety of meanings depending on the context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> texts Raja yoga refers to the goal of yoga (which is usually <a href="/wiki/Samadhi" title="Samadhi">samadhi</a>) and not a method of attaining it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011-2">[2]</a></sup> The term also became a modern <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a>, when in the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Swami_Vivekananda" title="Swami Vivekananda">Swami Vivekananda</a> equated raja yoga with the <a href="/wiki/Yoga_Sutras_of_Patanjali" title="Yoga Sutras of Patanjali">Yoga Sutras of Patanjali</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vivekarajayoga_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-vivekarajayoga-3">[3]</a></sup> Since then, Rāja yoga has variously referred to as "royal yoga", "royal union", "sahaj marg", "classical yoga", and "aṣṭānga yoga".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">Etymology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R%C4%81ja_yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rāja (Sanskrit: राज) means "chief, best of its kind" or "king".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Rāja yoga thus refers to "chief, best of yoga".</p>
<p>The historical use of the term <i>Rāja yoga</i> is found in other contexts, quite different than its modern usage. In ancient and medieval Sanskrit texts, it meant the highest state of yoga practice (one reaching <i>samadhi</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hatha_Yoga_Pradipika" title="Hatha Yoga Pradipika">Hatha Yoga Pradipika</a>, for example, refers to Hathayoga as one of the ways to achieve Rāja yoga.</p>
<p>The first known use of the phrase "Rāja yoga" occurs in a 16th-century commentary on a specific step in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoga_S%C5%ABtras_of_Pata%C3%B1jali" title="Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali">Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup> The Hindu scholar <a href="/wiki/Dattatreya" title="Dattatreya">Dattatreya</a>, in his medieval era Tantric work named "Yogaśāstra", explains in 334 shlokas, principles of four yoga: Mantra yoga, Hatha yoga, Laya yoga and Raja yoga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alain_Dani%C3%A9lou" title="Alain Daniélou">Alain Daniélou</a> states that <i>Rāja yoga</i> was, in the historic literature of Hinduism, one of five known methods of yoga, with the other four being <i>Hatha yoga</i>, <i>Mantra yoga</i>, <i>Laya yoga</i> and <i>Shiva yoga</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alaindanielou_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-alaindanielou-6">[6]</a></sup> Daniélou translates it as "Royal way to reintegration of Self with Universal Self (<a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a>)".</p>
<p>The term became a modern <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a> when in the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Swami_Vivekananda" title="Swami Vivekananda">Swami Vivekananda</a> equated raja yoga with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoga_S%C5%ABtras_of_Pata%C3%B1jali" title="Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali">Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vivekarajayoga_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-vivekarajayoga-3">[3]</a></sup> This sense of meaning is different from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hatha_Yoga_Prad%C4%ABpik%C4%81" title="Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā">Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā</a>, a text of the <a href="/wiki/Nath" title="Nath">Natha sampradaya</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-22011_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-22011-7">[7]</a></sup> where it is a different practice. Similarly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahma_Kumaris_World_Spiritual_University" title="Brahma Kumaris World Spiritual University">Brahma Kumaris</a>, a recent religious movement, teaches "Rāja yoga" that has very little to do with either the precepts of Hatha Yoga or Patañjali's <i>Yoga Sūtras</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup> Modern interpretations and literature that discusses <i>Raja yoga</i> often credit Patañjali's <i>Yogasūtras</i> as its textual source, but many neither adopt the teachings nor the philosophical foundations of the Yoga school of Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This mixing of concepts has led to confusion in understanding historical and modern Indian literature on Yoga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-22011_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-22011-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raja_yoga_as_samadhi">Raja yoga as samadhi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R%C4%81ja_yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Raja yoga as samadhi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Samadhi" title="Samadhi">Samadhi</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Shaivism" title="Shaivism">Shaiva</a> Yoga text, <i>Amanaska</i>, dated to be from the 12th century CE or earlier, is a dialogue between <a href="/wiki/Vamadeva" title="Vamadeva">Vamadeva</a> and deity <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>. In the second chapter, the text mentions Raja yoga, and explains why it is called so. It states that it is so named because it enables the yogin to reach the illustrious king within oneself, the supreme Self.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Raja yoga is declared as the goal and a state of <i>samadhi</i>, where one experiences nothing but the bliss of the undisturbed, the natural state of calm, serenity, peace, communion within and contentment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Raja yoga goal and state is synonymous with various terms, such as <i>Amanaska</i>, <i>Unmani</i> and <i>Sahaj</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hatha_Yoga_Pradipika" title="Hatha Yoga Pradipika">Hatha Yoga Pradipika</a> (literally, <i>Little Lamp of Hatha Yoga</i>) asserts this as follows,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hathyoga_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hathyoga-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p><b>राजयोगः समाधि</b>श्छ उन्मनी छ मनोन्मनी | अमरत्वं लयस्तत्त्वं शून्याशून्यं परं पदम || ३ ||<br/>
अमनस्कं तथाद्वैतं निरालम्बं निरञ्जनम | जीवन्मुक्तिश्छ सहजा तुर्या छेत्येक-वाछकाः || ४ ||<br/>
सलिले सैन्धवं यद्वत्साम्यं भजति योगतः | तथात्म-मनसोरैक्यं समाधिरभिधीयते || ५ ||<br/>
यदा संक्ष्हीयते पराणो मानसं छ परलीयते | तदा समरसत्वं छ समाधिरभिधीयते || ६ ||<br/>
तत-समं छ दवयोरैक्यं जीवात्म-परमात्मनोः | परनष्ह्ट-सर्व-सङ्कल्पः समाधिः सोअभिधीयते || ७ ||<br/>
<br/>
<b>Raja yoga</b>, samadhi, unmani, manonmani, amaratva, laya, tatva, <a href="/wiki/%C5%9A%C5%ABnyat%C4%81" title="Śūnyatā">sunya</a>, asunya, parama pada,<br/>
amanaska, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advaita" title="Advaita">advaita</a>, niralamba, niranjana, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jivanmukti" title="Jivanmukti">jivanmukti</a>, sahaja and turiya denote the same state of being.<br/>
Just as with salt dissolved in water becomes one with it, so the union of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atman_(Hinduism)" title="Atman (Hinduism)">Atman</a> and <i>Manas</i> (mind) is denominated samadhi,<br/>
When the breath becomes exhausted, and mind becomes <i>Praliyate</i> (still, reabsorbed), they fuse into union called samadhi.<br/>
This equality, this oneness of the two, the living self and the absolute self, when all <i>Sankalpa</i> (desire, cravings) end is called samadhi.</p>
</div>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Hath Yoga Pradipika, 4.3 - 4.7, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hathyoga_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-hathyoga-11">[11]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raja_yoga_as_a_type_of_yoga">Raja yoga as a type of yoga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R%C4%81ja_yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Raja yoga as a type of yoga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some medieval era Indian texts on Yoga list Rajayoga as one of many types of yoga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonmedievallist_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-jasonmedievallist-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, the Sarvanga yoga pradipikå, a Braj-bhashya commentary by Sundardas, from the 17th-century, teach three tetrads of Yogas. The first group is <a href="/wiki/Bhakti_yoga" title="Bhakti yoga">Bhakti yoga</a>, Mantra yoga, <a href="/wiki/Kundalini_yoga" title="Kundalini yoga">Laya yoga</a>, and Carcha yoga; the second group is <a href="/wiki/Hatha_yoga" title="Hatha yoga">Hatha yoga</a>, Raja yoga, Laksha yoga, and <a href="/wiki/Ashtanga_vinyasa_yoga" title="Ashtanga vinyasa yoga">Astanga yoga</a>; the third is <a href="/wiki/Samkhya" title="Samkhya">Samkhya</a> yoga, <a href="/wiki/Jnana_yoga" title="Jnana yoga">Jñana yoga</a>, Brahma yoga, and Advaita yoga. Of these twelve, Sundardas states that Rajayoga is the best yoga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonmedievallist_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-jasonmedievallist-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raja_yoga_as_yoga_system_of_Patanjali">Raja yoga as yoga system of Patanjali</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R%C4%81ja_yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Raja yoga as yoga system of Patanjali">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Raja yoga is a modern retronym introduced by Swami Vivekananda when he equated raja yoga with the <a href="/wiki/Yoga_Sutras_of_Patanjali" title="Yoga Sutras of Patanjali">Yoga Sutras of Patanjali</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallinson-12011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasonbirch_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-jasonbirch-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vivekarajayoga_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-vivekarajayoga-3">[3]</a></sup> After its circulation in the first half of 1st millennium CE, many Indian scholars reviewed it, then published their <i>Bhāṣya</i> (notes and commentary) on it, which together form a canon of texts called the <i>Pātañjalayogaśāstra</i> ("The Treatise on Yoga of Patañjali").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaas2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaas2006-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Axel Michaels, the <i>Yoga Sutras</i> are built upon fragments of texts and traditions from ancient India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMichaels2004267_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMichaels2004267-15">[15]</a></sup> According to Feuerstein, the <i>Yoga Sutras</i> are a condensation of two different traditions, namely "eight limb yoga" (ashtanga yoga) and action yoga (<i>kriya yoga</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFeuerstein1978108_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFeuerstein1978108-16">[16]</a></sup> The <i>kriya yoga</i> part is contained in chapter 1, chapter 2 verse 1-27, chapter 3 except verse 54, and chapter 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFeuerstein1978108_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFeuerstein1978108-16">[16]</a></sup> The "eight limb yoga" is described in chapter 2 verse 28-55, and chapter 3 verse 3 and 54.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFeuerstein1978108_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFeuerstein1978108-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are numerous parallels in the concepts in ancient <a href="/wiki/Samkhya" title="Samkhya">Samkhya</a>, Yoga and <a href="/wiki/Abhidharma" title="Abhidharma">Abhidharma</a> schools of thought, particularly from 2nd century BCE to 1st century AD, notes Larson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l4345_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-l4345-17">[17]</a></sup> Patanjali's Yoga Sutras may be a synthesis of these three traditions. From Samkhya school of Hinduism, Yoga Sutras adopt the "reflective discernment" (<i>adhyavasaya</i>) of <i>prakrti</i> and <i>purusa</i> (dualism), its metaphysical rationalism, as well its three <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemic</a> methods to gaining reliable knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l4345_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-l4345-17">[17]</a></sup> From Abhidharma Buddhism's idea of <i>nirodhasamadhi</i>, suggests Larson, Yoga Sutras adopt the pursuit of altered state of awareness, but unlike Buddhism which believes that there is neither self nor soul, Yoga is physicalist and realist like Samkhya in believing that each individual has a self and soul.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l4345_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-l4345-17">[17]</a></sup> The third concept Yoga Sutras synthesize into its philosophy is the ancient <a href="/wiki/Sannyasa" title="Sannyasa">ascetic</a> traditions of isolation, meditation and introspection, as well as the yoga ideas from the 1st millennium BCE Indian texts such as <a href="/wiki/Katha_Upanishad" title="Katha Upanishad">Katha Upanishad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shvetashvatara_Upanishad" title="Shvetashvatara Upanishad">Shvetashvatara Upanishad</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maitri_Upanishad" title="Maitri Upanishad">Maitri Upanishad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l4345_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-l4345-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_period">Islamic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R%C4%81ja_yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Islamic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 11th century, the Persian scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ab%C5%AB_Ray%E1%B8%A5%C4%81n_al-B%C4%ABr%C5%ABn%C4%AB" title="Abū Rayḥān al-Bīrūnī">Al Biruni</a> visited India, lived with Hindus for 16 years, and with their help translated several significant Sanskrit works into Arabic and Persian languages. One of these was Patanjali's Yogasutras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sptg_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-sptg-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Along with generally accurate translations, Al Biruni's text has significant differences than Yogasutra manuscripts discovered in India, during the 19th century. Al Biruni's record has helped modern scholars establish that Patanjali's <i>Yogasutras</i> manuscript existed in India in many versions, each with multiple commentaries by Hindu scholars. Some of these versions and commentaries have been lost or yet to be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sptg_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-sptg-18">[18]</a></sup> Al Biruni's translation preserved many of the core themes of Yoga philosophy of Hinduism, but certain sutras and analytical commentaries were restated making it more consistent with Islamic monotheistic theology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sptg_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-sptg-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Al Biruni's version of Yoga Sutras reached Persia and Arabian peninsula by about 1050 AD.</p>
<p>In Indian <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Indian_history#13th_century" title="Timeline of Indian history">historical timeline</a>, marking with the <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Sultanate" title="Delhi Sultanate">arrival of Islam</a> in India in twelfth century, further development and literature on Yoga philosophy of Hinduism went into decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite20146-9_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite20146-9-22">[22]</a></sup> By the sixteenth century, Patanjali's Yoga philosophy was nearly extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite20146-16_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite20146-16-23">[23]</a></sup> Yoga was preserved by <a href="/wiki/Sadhu" title="Sadhu">sadhus</a> (ascetics, <a href="/wiki/Sannyasa" title="Sannyasa">sannyasis</a>) of India. Some of the Hindu yoga elements were adopted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sufi" title="Sufi">Sufi</a> sect of Muslims in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Sufi Muslims at times adopted and protected the Yoga tradition of Hindus during the Islamic rule of India, and at other times helped the persecution and violence against those Hindus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Mughal Emperor <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a>, known for his syncretic tolerance, was attracted to and patronized Yoga philosophy of Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
