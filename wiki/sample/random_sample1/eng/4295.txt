<p><b>Latakia</b>; <b>Lattakia</b> or <b>Latakiyah</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">اللَاذِقِيَّة</span>‎‎ <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">al-Lādhiqīyah</span></i>  <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syrian_Arabic" title="Syrian Arabic">Syrian</a> pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Arabic" title="Help:IPA for Arabic">[el.laːdˈʔɪjje, -laːðˈqɪjja]</a></span>), is the principal port city of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, as well as the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Latakia_Governorate" title="Latakia Governorate">Latakia Governorate</a>. Historically, it has also been known as <b><a href="/wiki/Laodicea_in_Syria" title="Laodicea in Syria">Lāŏdĭcḗa</a></b> or <b>Lāŏdĭcḗa ad Mắre</b>. In addition to serving as a port, the city is a manufacturing center for surrounding agricultural towns and villages. According to the 2004 official census, the population of the city is 383,786.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gazetteer-4">[4]</a></sup> It is the 5th-largest city in Syria after <a href="/wiki/Aleppo" title="Aleppo">Aleppo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a>, and it borders <a href="/wiki/Tartus" title="Tartus">Tartus</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a> to the east, and <a href="/wiki/Idlib" title="Idlib">Idlib</a> to the north. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Apostolos_Andreas" title="Cape Apostolos Andreas">Cape Apostolos Andreas</a>, the north-eastern tip of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, is about 68 miles (109 km) away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the site has been inhabited since the 2nd millennium BCE, the modern-day city was first founded in the 4th century BCE under the rule of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucid_empire" title="Seleucid empire">Seleucid empire</a>. Latakia was subsequently ruled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">Romans</a>, then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ummayad" title="Ummayad">Ummayads</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">Abbasids</a> in the 8th–10th centuries CE. Under their rule, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_empire" title="Byzantine empire">Byzantines</a> frequently attacked the city, periodically recapturing it before losing it again to the Arabs, particularly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">Fatimids</a>. Afterward, Latakia was ruled successively by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk Turks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusaders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayyubid" title="Ayyubid">Ayyubids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mamluk" title="Mamluk">Mamluks</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_empire" title="Ottoman empire">Ottomans</a>. Following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Latakia was assigned to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_mandate_of_Syria" title="French mandate of Syria">French mandate of Syria</a>, in which it served as the capital of the autonomous territory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alawite" title="Alawite">Alawites</a>. This autonomous territory became the <a href="/wiki/Alawite_State" title="Alawite State">Alawite State</a> in 1922, proclaiming its independence a number of times until reintegrating into Syria in 1944.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laodicea_ad_Lycum" title="Laodicea ad Lycum">Laodicea ad Lycum</a>.</div>
<p>Like many <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid</a> cities, Latakia was named after a member of the ruling dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-le_Strange380_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-le_Strange380-6">[6]</a></sup> First named <i>Laodikeia on the Coast</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Λαοδίκεια ἡ Πάραλος</span>) by <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus I Nicator</a> in honor of his mother, <a href="/wiki/Laodice_of_Macedonia" title="Laodice of Macedonia">Laodice</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, its name became <i>Lāŏdĭcḗa ad Mắre</i>. The original name survives in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> form as <i>al-Ladhiqiyyah</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">اللاذقية</span>‎‎), from which the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>Lattaquié</i> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <i>Latakia</i> or <i>Lattakia</i> derive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-le_Strange380_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-le_Strange380-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ball_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ball-7">[7]</a></sup> To the Ottomans, it was known as <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Lazkiye</i></span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Latakia" title="Timeline of Latakia">Timeline of Latakia</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_settlement_and_founding">Ancient settlement and founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient settlement and founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The location of Latakia, the <b>Ras Ziyarah</b> <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> has a long history of occupation. The <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramitha" title="Ramitha">Ramitha</a> was located here, known to the Greeks as <i>Leukê Aktê</i> ("white coast").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The city was described in <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Geographica" title="Geographica">Geographica</a></i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"It is a city most beautifully built, has a good harbour, and has territory which, besides its other good crops, abounds in wine. Now this city furnishes the most of the wine to the Alexandreians, since the whole of the mountain that lies above the city and is possessed by it is covered with vines almost as far as the summits. And while the summits are at a considerable distance from Lāŏdĭcḗa, sloping up gently and gradually from it, they tower above Apameia, extending up to a perpendicular height."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The city was an important <a href="/wiki/Colonia_(Roman)" title="Colonia (Roman)">colonia</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">Roman empire</a> in ancient <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> for seven centuries. It was called <a href="/wiki/Laodicea_in_Syria" title="Laodicea in Syria">Laodicea in Syria</a> or "Lāŏdĭcḗa ad mắre" and was the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Theodorias_(province)" title="Theodorias (province)">Eastern Roman province of Theodorias</a> from 528 AD until 637 AD. A sizable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_people" title="Jewish people">Jewish</a> population lived in Lāŏdĭcḗa during the first century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish-11">[11]</a></sup> The heretic <a href="/wiki/Apollinaris_of_Laodicea" title="Apollinaris of Laodicea">Apollinarius</a> was bishop of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bishopric_of_Laodicea_in_Syria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bishopric of Laodicea in Syria (page does not exist)">Lāŏdĭcḗa</a> in the 4th century. The city minted coins from an early date.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crusader.2C_Ayyubid.2C_and_Mamluk_rule">Crusader, Ayyubid, and Mamluk rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crusader, Ayyubid, and Mamluk rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lattakia,CatholicChurch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2585" data-file-width="1920" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Lattakia%2CCatholicChurch.jpg/170px-Lattakia%2CCatholicChurch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Lattakia%2CCatholicChurch.jpg/255px-Lattakia%2CCatholicChurch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Lattakia%2CCatholicChurch.jpg/340px-Lattakia%2CCatholicChurch.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lattakia,CatholicChurch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Latin Church of Latakia.</div>
</div>

<p>After failed efforts by <a href="/wiki/Bohemond_I_of_Antioch" title="Bohemond I of Antioch">Bohemond I of Antioch</a> to capture Latakia from the Byzantine Empire the city was taken in 1103 by forces under the command of <a href="/wiki/Tancred,_Prince_of_Galilee" title="Tancred, Prince of Galilee">Tancred of Hauteville</a>, a veteran of the First Crusade and acting regent of the Principality of Antioch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Following the defeat of Antiochene forces at the Battle of Harran in 1104 the city was reoccupied by the Byzantines however they would again lose the city. Despite a treaty in 1108 with Bohemond promising to return Latakia to the Empire by 1110 it was firmly under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Antioch" title="Principality of Antioch">Principality of Antioch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This would remain the case with the city serving as the primary port for the Principality until after the loss of Antioch itself to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mamluks" title="Mamluks">Mamluks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In circa 1300, Arab geographer al-Dimashqi noted that Latakia had no running water and that trees were scarce, but the city's port was "a wonderful harbor... full of large ships".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1332, the Moroccan traveller <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a> visited Latakia in his journeys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_rule">Ottoman rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ottoman rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1888, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilaya" title="Wilaya">Wilayat</a> Beirut was established, Latakia became its northernmost town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Ottoman period<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, the region of Latakia became predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alawi" title="Alawi">Alawi</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Turkmen" title="Syrian Turkmen">Turkmen</a> also consisted a significant minority. The city itself, however, contained significant numbers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> and Christian inhabitants. The landlords in the countryside tended to be Sunni and Orthodox Christians, while the peasants were mostly Alawi. Like the <a href="/wiki/Druze" title="Druze">Druzes</a>, who also had a special status before the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the Alawis had a strained relationship with the Ottoman overlords. In fact, they were not even given the status of <a href="/wiki/Millet_(Ottoman_Empire)" title="Millet (Ottoman Empire)">millet</a>, although they enjoyed relative autonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rabinovich_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rabinovich-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Mandate_period">French Mandate period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: French Mandate period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alawite_State" title="Alawite State">Alawite State</a></div>
<p>In 1920, Latakia fell under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_mandate_of_Syria" title="French mandate of Syria">French mandate</a>, under which the <a href="/wiki/Alawite_State" title="Alawite State">Alawite State</a> was established. The state lasted until 1936 when it was merged with the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Republic_(1930%E2%80%9358)" title="Syrian Republic (1930–58)">Syrian Republic</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lattakia_1970.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="404" data-file-width="601" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Lattakia_1970.jpg/220px-Lattakia_1970.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Lattakia_1970.jpg/330px-Lattakia_1970.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Lattakia_1970.jpg/440px-Lattakia_1970.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lattakia_1970.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Latakia in 1970.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All but a few classical buildings have been destroyed, often by earthquakes; those remaining include a Roman <a href="/wiki/Triumphal_arch" title="Triumphal arch">triumphal arch</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corinthian_column" title="Corinthian column">Corinthian columns</a> known as the Colonnade of <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Bacchus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>An extensive port project was proposed in 1948, and construction work began on the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Latakia" title="Port of Latakia">Port of Latakia</a> in 1950, aided by a <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>6 million loan from <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>. By 1951, the first stage of the construction was completed, and the port handled an increasing amount of Syria's overseas trade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In August 1957, 4,000 Egyptian troops landed in Latakia under orders from <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Gamal Abdel Nasser</a> after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkish_army" title="Turkish army">Turkish troops</a> massed along the border with Syria, accusing it of harboring <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Turkey_(historical)" title="Communist Party of Turkey (historical)">Turkish Communists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>A major highway linked Latakia with Aleppo and the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> valley in 1968 and was supplemented by the completion of a railway line to <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a>. The port became even more important after 1975, due to the troubled situation in Lebanon and the loss of Beirut and Tripoli as ports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ring5_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ring5-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1973, during the <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur_War" title="Yom Kippur War">October War</a> (Yom Kippur War), the naval <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Latakia" title="Battle of Latakia">Battle of Latakia</a> between <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and Syria was fought just offshore from Latakia. The battle was the first to be fought using <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasures" title="Electronic countermeasures">ECM</a> (electronic countermeasures).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Syrian_Civil_War">Syrian Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Syrian Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><span id="civilwar"></span> During the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Civil_War" title="Syrian Civil War">Syrian Civil War</a>, Latakia had been a site of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demonstration_(people)" title="Demonstration (people)">protest activity</a> since March 2011. The Syrian government claimed 12 were killed there in clashes in late March,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> leading to the deployment of the military to restrict movement into and out of the city. Hundreds of Syrians were reportedly arrested, and by late July, activists in Latakia were telling foreign media they feared a more violent crackdown was coming. Protests continued despite the increased security presence and arrests. Several civilians were allegedly killed in confrontations with security officers during this early period of the siege.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On 13 August 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Army" title="Syrian Army">Syrian Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Navy" title="Syrian Navy">Syrian Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Latakia" title="Siege of Latakia">launched an operation</a> where more than 20 tanks and <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">APCs</a> rolled into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alawi" title="Alawi">Alawi</a> stronghold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enters_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-enters-25">[25]</a></sup> The city was also attacked by the Syrian army on the 14 August 2011. Activists claimed that 25 people died during the attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Latakia is the home of Russia's largest foreign electronic eavesdropping facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khmeimim_Air_Base" title="Khmeimim Air Base">Khmeimim Air Base</a> is an airbase near Latakia converted to use by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_military" title="Russian military">Russian military</a> in 2015. On 24 November 2015, Turkish forces <a href="/wiki/2015_Russian_Sukhoi_Su-24_shootdown" title="2015 Russian Sukhoi Su-24 shootdown">shot down a Russian fighter plane</a> over Latakia. One pilot was killed and the other rescued by Syrian military and brought to Khmeimim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Latakia is located 348 kilometres (216 mi) north-west of <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, 186 kilometres (116 mi) south-west from <a href="/wiki/Aleppo" title="Aleppo">Aleppo</a>, 186 kilometres (116 mi) north-west of <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a>, and 90 kilometres (56 mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Tartus" title="Tartus">Tartus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homsonline_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homsonline-30">[30]</a></sup> Nearby towns and villages include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasab,_Syria" title="Kasab, Syria">Kasab</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Al-Haffah" title="Al-Haffah">Al-Haffah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slinfah" title="Slinfah">Slinfah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qardaha" title="Qardaha">Qardaha</a> to the east in the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Coastal_Mountain_Range" title="Syrian Coastal Mountain Range">Coastal Mountain Range</a> , and <a href="/wiki/Jableh" title="Jableh">Jableh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baniyas" title="Baniyas">Baniyas</a> to the south.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Latakia is the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Latakia_Governorate" title="Latakia Governorate">Latakia Governorate</a>, in western Syria, bordering Turkey to the north. The governorate has a reported area of either 2,297 square kilometres (887 sq mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> or 2,437 square kilometres (941 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Latakia city is located in the <a href="/wiki/Latakia_District" title="Latakia District">Latakia District</a> in the northern portion of Latakia governorate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nahr_al-Kabir_al-Shamali" title="Nahr al-Kabir al-Shamali">Nahr al-Kabir al-Shamali</a> flows into the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> south of Latakia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen's climate classification</a>, Latakia has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot-summer_Mediterranean_climate" title="Hot-summer Mediterranean climate">hot-summer Mediterranean climate</a> (<i>Csa</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Latakia's wettest months are December and January, where average precipitation is around 160 mm. The city's driest month, July, only has on average about 1 millimetre (0.039 in) of rain. Average high temperatures in the city range from around 16 °C (61 °F) in January to around 30 °C (86 °F) in August. Latakia on average receives around 760 millimetres (30 in) of rainfall annually.</p>

<p>One of the first censuses was in 1825, which recorded that there were 6,000-8,000 Muslims, 1,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox" title="Greek Orthodox">Greek Orthodox</a> Christians, 30 <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Christians</a>, 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maronite_Catholic" title="Maronite Catholic">Maronite Catholics</a>, and 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> At the beginning of the 20th century, Latakia had a population of roughly 7,000 inhabitants; however, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_Society_of_Arts" title="Journal of the Society of Arts">Journal of the Society of Arts</a> recorded a population of 25,000 in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SoA_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-SoA-34">[34]</a></sup> In a 1992 estimate, Latakia had a population of 284,000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> rising to 303,000 in the 1994 census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winckler_35-6"><a href="#cite_note-Winckler-35">[35]</a></sup> The city's population continued to rise, reaching an estimated 402,000 residents in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minahan_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Minahan-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Latakia was historically a Sunni city, however the Alawatization process under Hafez al Asaad led to many Alawites moving from the rural hinterland into the city. In 2010 Latakia City was 50% Alawite, 40% Sunni and 10% Christian,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> however, the rural hinterland has an Alawite majority of roughly 70%, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_Christian" title="Arab Christian">Christians</a> making up 14%, Sunni Muslims making up 12%, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismaili" title="Ismaili">Ismailis</a> representing the remaining 2%. The city serves as the capital of the Alawite population and is a major cultural center for the religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minahan_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Minahan-38">[38]</a></sup> Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, large numbers of Alawites from the area emigrated to the country's capital <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Of the Christians, a sizable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochian_Greeks" title="Antiochian Greeks">Antiochian Greek</a> population exists in Latakia, and their <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> in the city has the largest congregation of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church_of_Antioch" title="Greek Orthodox Church of Antioch">Greek Orthodox Church of Antioch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CtS_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-CtS-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gmfa_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-gmfa-43">[43]</a></sup> There is also an <a href="/wiki/Armenians_in_Syria" title="Armenians in Syria">Armenian</a> community of 3,500 in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gmfa_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-gmfa-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The entire population speaks <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, mostly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Levantine_Arabic" title="North Levantine Arabic">North Levantine</a> dialect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the city boundaries is the "unofficial" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latakia_camp" title="Latakia camp">Latakia camp</a>, established in 1956, which has a population of 6,354 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palestinian_refugee" title="Palestinian refugee">Palestinian refugees</a>, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Jaffa" title="Jaffa">Jaffa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNRWA_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNRWA-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1339" data-file-width="2048" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg/220px-Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg/330px-Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg/440px-Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lattaquie_port_1979.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Latakia" title="Port of Latakia">Port of Latakia</a> in 1979</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Latakia" title="Port of Latakia">Port of Latakia</a> (Arabic: ميناء اللاذقية) is the main seaport in Syria.<br/>
Latakia has an extensive agricultural hinterland. Exports include bitumen (<a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>), cereals, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, fruits, eggs, vegetable oil, pottery, and <a href="/wiki/Latakia_(tobacco)" title="Latakia (tobacco)">tobacco</a>. Cotton ginning, vegetable-oil processing, tanning, and <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">sponge</a> fishing serve as local industries for the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cote_d%27Azur,_Syria" title="Cote d'Azur, Syria">Cote d'Azur</a> Beach of Latakia is Syria's premier coastal resort, and offers water skiing, jet skiing, and windsurfing. The city contains eight hotels, two of which have five-star ratings; both the Cote d'Azur de Cham Hotel and Lé Meridien Lattiquie Hotel are located 6 kilometres (3.7 mi) north of the city, at Cote d'Azur. The latter hotel has 274 rooms and is the only international hotel in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compared to other Syrian cities, window shopping and evening strolls in the markets is considered a favorite pastime in Latakia. Numerous designer-label stores line 8 Azar Street, and the heart of the city's shopping area is the series of blocks enclosed by 8 Azar Street, Yarmouk Street, and Saad Zaghloul Street in the city center. <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">Cinemas</a> in Latakia include Ugarit Cinema, al-Kindi, and a smaller theater off al-Moutanabbi Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannheim_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mannheim-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Museum of Latakia was built in 1986 near the seafront of the city. It formerly housed the residence of the Governor of the <a href="/wiki/Alawite_State" title="Alawite State">Alawite State</a> and was originally a 16th-century Ottoman <i><a href="/wiki/Caravanserai" title="Caravanserai">khan</a></i> ("caravansary") known as Khan al-Dukhan, meaning "The <i>Khan</i> of Smoke", as it served the tobacco trade. The <i>khan</i> historically served not only as an inn, but also contained private residences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CtS_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-CtS-41">[41]</a></sup> The exhibits include inscribed tablets from <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a>, ancient jewellery, coins, figurines, ceramics, pottery, and early Arab and Crusader-era <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chain-mail" title="Chain-mail">chain-mail</a> suits and swords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<p>Latakia is the home city of two football clubs: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teshrin" title="Teshrin">Teshrin Sports Club</a> was founded in 1947,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hutteen_(Latakia)" title="Hutteen (Latakia)">Hutteen Sports Club</a> was founded in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Both teams are based in the <a href="/wiki/Al-Assad_Stadium" title="Al-Assad Stadium">al-Assad Stadium</a>, which carries a capacity of 35,000 people. Just north of the city is the <a href="/wiki/Latakia_Sports_City" title="Latakia Sports City">Latakia Sports City</a> complex, which was built in 1987 to host the <a href="/wiki/1987_Mediterranean_Games" title="1987 Mediterranean Games">1987 Mediterranean Games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADL_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADL-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latakia_tobacco">Latakia tobacco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Latakia tobacco">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Latakia_(tobacco)" title="Latakia (tobacco)">Latakia (tobacco)</a></div>
<p>Latakia tobacco is a specially prepared <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> originally produced in Syria and named after the port city of Latakia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glpease_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-glpease-54">[54]</a></sup> Now the tobacco is mainly produced in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. It is cured over a <a href="/wiki/Stone_pine" title="Stone pine">stone pine</a> or oak wood fire, which gives it an intense smokey-peppery taste and smell. Rarely smoked straight, it is used as a "condiment" or "blender" (a basic tobacco mixed with other tobaccos to create a blend), especially in English, Balkan, and some American Classic blends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glpease_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-glpease-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<p>The University of Latakia was founded in 1971 and renamed <a href="/wiki/Tishreen_University" title="Tishreen University">Tishreen University</a> ("October University") in 1976 to commemorate the victory Syria claimed in the <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur_War" title="Yom Kippur War">October War of 1973</a>. The university has an enrollment of 25,660 students, 57% of which are females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lattakia_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lattakia-55">[55]</a></sup> The city houses a branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_Academy_for_Science_and_Technology_and_Maritime_Transport" title="Arab Academy for Science and Technology and Maritime Transport">Arab Academy for Science and Technology and Maritime Transport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>A school in Latakia, Syria is named after <a href="/wiki/Jules_Jammal" title="Jules Jammal">Jules Jammal</a>, an Arab Christian military officer who blew himself up in a suicide attack on a French ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia,_Syria.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia%2C_Syria.jpg/220px-Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia%2C_Syria.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia%2C_Syria.jpg/330px-Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia%2C_Syria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia%2C_Syria.jpg/440px-Modern_neighborhood_-_Latakia%2C_Syria.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The modern city still exhibits faint traces of its former importance, notwithstanding the frequent earthquakes with which it has been visited. The marina is built upon foundations of ancient columns, and there are in the town an old gateway and other antiquities, as also sarcophagi and sepulchral caves in the neighbourhood. This gateway is a remarkable triumphal arch at the southeast corner of the town, almost entire: it is built with four entrances, like the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Forum_Jani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Forum Jani (page does not exist)">Forum Jani</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. It is conjectured that this arch was built in honour of <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Verus" title="Lucius Verus">Lucius Verus</a>, or of Septimius Severus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Fragments of Greek and Latin inscriptions are dispersed all over the ruins, but entirely defaced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Notable points of interest in the nearby area include the massive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saladin%27s_Castle" title="Saladin's Castle">Saladin's Castle</a> and the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a>, where some of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabetic</a> writings have been found. There are also several popular beaches. There are numerous mosques in Latakia, including the 13th-century Great Mosque and the 18th-century Jadid Mosque constructed by Suleiman Pasha Azem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CtS_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-CtS-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Latakia has consulates general of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and honorary consulates of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roads link Latakia to Aleppo, <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tripoli,_Lebanon" title="Tripoli, Lebanon">Tripoli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-19">[19]</a></sup> The main commercial coastal road of the city is Jamal Abdel Nasser Street, named after former Egyptian president <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Gamal Abdel Nasser</a>. Lined with hotels, restaurants and the city museum, the street begins in central Latakia along the Mediterranean coast and ends at Hitteen Square. From the square, it branches southwest into al-Maghreb al-Arabi Street,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannheim2_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mannheim2-58">[58]</a></sup> south into 8 Azar Street, which continues south to form Baghdad Avenue—the main north-south road<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>—branching into Beirut Street and Nadim Hassan Street along the southern coastline. From the southern portion of Jamal Abdel Nasser Street branch off al-Yarmouk Street and al-Quds Street, the latter which ends at al-Yaman Square in western Latakia, it continues west into Abdel Qader al-Husseini Street. North from al-Yaman Square Souria Avenue and south of the square is al-Ourouba Street. Souria Avenue ends in al-Jumhouriah Square, then continues north as al-Jumhouriah Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannheim2_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mannheim2-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the city is accessible by taxi and other forms of public transportation. Buses transport people to various Syrian, Lebanese, and Turkish cities, including Aleppo, Damascus, <a href="/wiki/Deir_ez-Zor" title="Deir ez-Zor">Deir ez-Zor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a>, Tripoli, Beirut, <a href="/wiki/Safita" title="Safita">Safita</a>, Hims, <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antakya" title="Antakya">Antakya</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tartous" title="Tartous">Tartous</a>. The "luxury" Garagat Pullman Bus Station is located on Abdel Qader al-Husseini Street, and at least a dozen private companies are based at the station. On the same street is the older Hob-Hob Bus Station that operates a "depart when full" basis to Damascus and Aleppo. Local microbuses run between al-Yaman Square and the city center, as well as between the station on al-Jalaa Street and the city center. There is also a microbus station with buses departing to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qalaat_Salah_ed-Din" title="Qalaat Salah ed-Din">Qalaat Salah ed-Din</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qardaha" title="Qardaha">Qardaha</a>, Kassab, and <a href="/wiki/Jableh" title="Jableh">Jableh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannheim_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mannheim-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Latakia's railway station is located on al-Yaman Square. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemins_de_Fer_Syriens" title="Chemins de Fer Syriens">Chemins de Fer Syriens</a> operated services, including two daily runs to Aleppo and one weekly run to Damascus via Tartous. In 2005, approximately 512,167 passengers departed from Latakia's railway station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pass_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pass-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bassel_Al-Assad_International_Airport" title="Bassel Al-Assad International Airport">Bassel Al-Assad International Airport</a> is located 25 kilometers (16 mi) south of Latakia and serves as a national and regional airport with regular flights to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharjah_(city)" title="Sharjah (city)">Sharjah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Latakia" title="Port of Latakia">Port of Latakia</a> is also a link in six organized cruises between <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">İzmir</a> and Beirut. In addition, there are irregular ferry services to <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. In 2005, approximately 27,939 passengers used the port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pass_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pass-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
