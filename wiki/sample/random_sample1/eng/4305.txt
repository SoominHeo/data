<p><a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> is a member of the broad family of <a href="/wiki/Italic_languages" title="Italic languages">Italic languages</a>. Its alphabet, the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, emerged from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Italic_alphabet" title="Old Italic alphabet">Old Italic alphabets</a>, which in turn were derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">Phoenician</a> scripts. Historical Latin came from the prehistoric language of the <a href="/wiki/Latium" title="Latium">Latium</a> region, specifically around the River <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman civilization</a> first developed. How and when Latin came to be spoken by the Romans are questions that have long been debated. Various influences on Latin of <a href="/wiki/Italo-Celtic" title="Italo-Celtic">Celtic dialects</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">northern Italy</a>, the non-Indo-European <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_language" title="Etruscan language">Etruscan language</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Italy" title="Central Italy">Central Italy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">southern Italy</a> have been detected, but when these influences entered the native Latin is not known for certain.</p>
<p>Surviving <a href="/wiki/Latin_literature" title="Latin literature">Latin literature</a> consists almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a> in its broadest definition. It includes a polished and sometimes highly stylized <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a> sometimes termed Golden Latin, which spans the 1st century BC and the early years of the 1st century AD. However, throughout the history of ancient Rome the spoken language differed in both grammar and vocabulary from that of literature, and is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a>. In addition to Latin, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> was often spoken by the well-educated elite, who studied it in school and acquired Greek tutors from among the influx of enslaved educated Greek prisoners of war. In the eastern half of the Roman Empire, which became the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, the Greek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koine" title="Koine">Koine</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenism</a> remained current and was never replaced by Latin.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italic_languages" title="Italic languages">Italic languages</a></div>
<p>The name Latin derives from the Italic tribal group named <a href="/wiki/Latins_(Italic_tribe)" title="Latins (Italic tribe)">Latini</a> that settled around 10th Century BC in Latium, and the dialect spoken by these people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>Italic</b> languages form a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centum" title="Centum">centum</a></i> subfamily of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> language family. These include the <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_languages" title="Hellenic languages">Hellenic</a> languages, and a number of extinct ones.</p>
<p>Broadly speaking, in initial syllables the Indo-European simple vowels — <i>(*a), *e, *i, *o, *u</i>; short and long — are usually retained in Latin. The <a href="/wiki/Schwa#Schwa_indogermanicum" title="Schwa">schwa indogermanicum</a> (<i>*ə</i>) appears in Latin as <i>a</i> (cf. IE <i>*pəter</i> &gt; L <i>pater</i>). Diphthongs are also preserved in Old Latin, but in Classical Latin some tend to become monophthongs (for example <i>oi</i> &gt; <i>ū</i> or <i>oe</i>, and <i>ei</i> &gt; <i>ē</i> &gt; <i>ī</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In non-initial syllables, there was more vowel reduction. The most extreme case occurs with short vowels in medial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_syllable" title="Open syllable">open syllables</a> (i.e. short vowels followed by at most a single consonant, occurring neither in the first nor last syllable): All are reduced to a single vowel, which appears as <i>i</i> in most cases, but <i>e</i> (sometimes <i>o</i>) before <i>r</i>, and <i>u</i> before an <i>l</i> which is followed by <i>o</i> or <i>u</i>. In final syllables, short <i>e</i> and <i>o</i> are usually raised to <i>i</i> and <i>u</i>, respectively.</p>
<p>Consonants are generally more stable. However, the Indo-European voiced aspirates <i>bh, dh, gh, gwh</i> are not maintained, becoming <i>f, f, h, f</i> respectively at the beginning of a word, but usually <i>b, d, g, v</i> elsewhere. Note that non-initial <i>dh</i> becomes <i>b</i> next to <i>r</i> or <i>u</i>, e.g. <i>*h₁rudh-</i> "red" &gt; <i>rub-</i>, e.g. <i>rubeō</i> "to be red"; <i>*werdh-</i> "word" &gt; <i>verbum</i>. <i>s</i> between vowels famously becomes <i>r</i>, e.g. <i>flōs</i> "flower", gen. <i>flōris</i>; <i>erō</i> "I will be" vs. root <i>es-</i>; <i>aurōra</i> "dawn" &lt; *<i>ausōsā</i> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> <i>*aust-</i> &gt; English "east", <a href="/wiki/Vedic_Sanskrit" title="Vedic Sanskrit">Vedic Sanskrit</a> <i>uṣā́s</i> "dawn"); <i>soror</i> "sister" &lt; <i>*sosor</i> &lt; <i>*swesōr</i> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sweostor</i> "sister").</p>
<p>Of the original eight <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">cases</a> of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a>, Latin inherited six: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominative" title="Nominative">nominative</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocative" title="Vocative">vocative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">genitive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablative" title="Ablative">ablative</a>. The Indo-European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locative" title="Locative">locative</a> survived in the declensions of some place names and a few common nouns, such as <i>Roma</i> "Rome" (locative <i>Romae</i>) and <i>domus</i> "home" (locative <i>domī</i> "at home"). Vestiges of the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">instrumental case</a> may remain in adverbial forms ending in <i>-ē</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is believed that the earliest surviving inscription is a seventh-century B.C. pin known as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praenestine_fibula" title="Praenestine fibula">Praenestine fibula</a></i>, which reads <i>Manios med fhefhaked Numasioi</i> "Manius made me for Numerius".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Latin">Old Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Old Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duenos_inscription.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="476" data-file-width="460" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Duenos_inscription.jpg/220px-Duenos_inscription.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Duenos_inscription.jpg/330px-Duenos_inscription.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Duenos_inscription.jpg/440px-Duenos_inscription.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duenos_inscription.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duenos_inscription" title="Duenos inscription">Duenos inscription</a>, from the 6th century BC, is the second-earliest known <a href="/wiki/Old_Latin" title="Old Latin">Latin</a> text.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Old_Latin" title="Old Latin">Old Latin</a></div>
<p><b>Old Latin</b> (also called <b>Early Latin</b> or <b>Archaic Latin</b>) refers to the period of Latin texts before the age of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a>, extending from textual fragments that probably originated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_monarchy" title="Roman monarchy">Roman monarchy</a> to the written language of the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_republic" title="Roman republic">Roman republic</a> about 75 BC. Almost all the writing of its earlier phases is inscriptional.</p>
<p>Some phonological characteristics of older Latin are the case endings <i>-os</i> and <i>-om</i> (later Latin <i>-us</i> and <i>-um</i>). In many locations, classical Latin turned intervocalic /s/ into /r/. This had implications for <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declension</a>: early classical Latin, <i>honos</i>, <i>honoris</i>; Classical <i>honor</i>, <i>honoris</i> ("honor"). Some Latin texts preserve /s/ in this position, such as the <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Arvale" title="Carmen Arvale">Carmen Arvale</a>'s <i>lases</i> for <i><a href="/wiki/Lares" title="Lares">lares</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Latin">Classical Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classical Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="640" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg/300px-Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg/450px-Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg/600px-Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico" title="Commentarii de Bello Gallico">Commentarii de Bello Gallico</a> is one of the most famous classical Latin texts of the Golden Age of Latin. The unvarnished, journalistic style of this upper-class general has long been taught as a model of the urbane Latin officially spoken and written in the floruit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_republic" title="Roman republic">Roman republic</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Classical Latin</b> is the form of the Latin language used by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Romans</a> in Classical Latin literature. In the latest and narrowest <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philological model</a> its use spanned the Golden Age of Latin literature – broadly the 1st century BC and the early 1st century AD – possibly extending to the Silver Age – broadly the 1st and 2nd centuries. It was a polished <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">written</a> <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a> based on the refined spoken language of the upper classes. Classical Latin differs from Old Latin: the earliest inscriptional language and the earliest authors, such as <a href="/wiki/Ennius" title="Ennius">Ennius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plautus" title="Plautus">Plautus</a> and others, in a number of ways; for example, the early <i>-om</i> and <i>-os</i> endings shifted into <i>-um</i> and <i>-us</i> ones, and some lexical differences also developed, such as the broadening of the meaning of words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the broadest and most ancient sense, the classical period includes the authors of Early Latin, the Golden Age and the Silver Age.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golden_Age">Golden Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Golden Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Age_(metaphor)" title="Golden Age (metaphor)">golden age</a> of Latin literature</b> is a period consisting roughly of the time from 75 BC to AD 14, covering the end of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> and the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" title="Augustus Caesar">Augustus Caesar</a>. In the currently used philological model this period represents the peak of Latin literature. Since the earliest post-classical times the Latin of those authors has been an ideal norm of the best Latin, which other writers should follow.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silver_Age">Silver Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Silver Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In reference to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> literature, the <b>Silver age</b> covers the first two centuries AD directly after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_age_of_Latin_Literature" title="Golden age of Latin Literature">Golden age</a>. Literature from the Silver Age is more embellished with mannerisms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Latin">Late Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a></div>
<p><b>Late Latin</b> is the administrative and literary language of <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a> in the late Roman empire and states that succeeded the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> over the same range. By its broadest definition it is dated from about 200 AD to about 900 AD when it was replaced by written <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>. Opinion concerning whether it should be considered classical is divided. The authors of the period looked back to a classical period they believed should be imitated and yet their styles were often classical. According to the narrowest definitions, Late Latin did not exist and the authors of the times are to be considered medieval.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vulgar_Latin">Vulgar Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vulgar Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pompeii-graffiti.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="91" data-file-width="300" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3b/Pompeii-graffiti.jpg" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Vulgar Latin, as in this political <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffito</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, was the language of the ordinary people of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a> of literature.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Vulgar Latin</b> (in Latin, <i>sermo vulgaris</i>) is a blanket term covering <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> dialects of the Latin language spoken from earliest times in Italy until the latest dialects of the western empire, diverging still further, evolved into the early <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> – whose writings began to appear about the 9th century.</p>
<p>This spoken Latin differed from the <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a> of Classical Latin in its grammar and vocabulary. It is likely to have evolved over time, with some features not appearing until the late Empire. Other features are likely to have been in place much earlier. Because there are few phonetic transcriptions of the daily speech of these Latin speakers (to match, for example, the post-classical <a href="/wiki/Appendix_Probi" title="Appendix Probi">Appendix Probi</a>) Vulgar Latin must be studied mainly by indirect methods.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hocgracili.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="182" data-file-width="691" height="79" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Hocgracili.jpg/300px-Hocgracili.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Hocgracili.jpg/450px-Hocgracili.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Hocgracili.jpg/600px-Hocgracili.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hocgracili.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A replica of the Old Roman Cursive inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Vindolanda_tablets" title="Vindolanda tablets">Vindolanda tablets</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Knowledge of Vulgar Latin comes from a variety of sources. First, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparative_method" title="Comparative method">comparative method</a> reconstructs items of the mother language from the attested Romance languages. Also, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescription_and_description" title="Prescription and description">prescriptive grammar</a> texts from the Late Latin period condemn some usages as errors, providing insight into how Latin was actually spoken. The <a href="/wiki/Solecism" title="Solecism">solecisms</a> and non-Classical usages occasionally found in late Latin texts also shed light on the spoken language. A windfall source lies in the chance finds of <a href="/wiki/Wax_tablet" title="Wax tablet">wax tablets</a> such as those found at <a href="/wiki/Vindolanda" title="Vindolanda">Vindolanda</a> on <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian's Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_cursive" title="Roman cursive">Roman cursive</a> script was used on these tablets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">Romance languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Romance languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a></div>
<p>The <b>Romance languages</b>, a major branch of the Indo-European language family, comprise all languages that descended from Latin, the language of the Roman Empire. The Romance languages have more than 700 million native speakers worldwide, mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, as well as in many smaller regions scattered through the world.</p>
<p>All Romance languages descend from Vulgar Latin, the language of soldiers, settlers, and slaves of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, which was substantially different from that of the Roman <i>literati</i>. Between 200 BC and AD 100, the expansion of the Empire and the administrative and educational policies of Rome made Vulgar Latin the dominant vernacular language over a wide area which stretched from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> to the west coast of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. During the Empire's decline and after its collapse and fragmentation in the 5th century, Vulgar Latin began to evolve independently within each local area, and eventually diverged into dozens of distinct languages. The overseas empires established by <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portugal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_colonial_empires" title="French colonial empires">France</a> after the 15th century then spread these languages to other continents – about two thirds of all Romance speakers are now outside Europe.</p>
<p>In spite of the multiple influences of pre-Roman languages and later invasions, the <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> of all Romance languages are predominantly derived from Vulgar Latin. As a result, the group shares a number of linguistic features that set it apart from other Indo-European branches.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_Latin">Ecclesiastical Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Latin" title="Ecclesiastical Latin">Ecclesiastical Latin</a></div>
<p><b>Ecclesiastical Latin</b> (sometimes called <b>Church Latin</b>) is a broad and analogous term referring to the Latin language as used in documents of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgies</a> (mainly in past times) and during some periods the preaching of its ministers. Ecclesiastical Latin is not a single style: the term merely means the language promulgated at any time by the church. In terms of stylistic periods, it belongs to Late Latin in the Late Latin period, Medieval Latin in the Medieval Period, and so on through to the present. One may say that, starting from the church's decision in the early Late Latin period to use a simple and unornamented language that would be comprehensible to ordinary Latin speakers and yet still be elegant and correct, church Latin is usually a discernible substyle within the major style of the period. Its authors in the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> period are typically paradigmatic of the best Latin and that is true in contemporary times. The decline in its use within the last 100 years has been a matter of regret to some, who have formed organizations inside and outside the church to support its use and to use it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Latin">Medieval Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medieval Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="932" data-file-width="625" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg/220px-Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg/330px-Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg/440px-Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carmina_Cantabrigiensia_Manuscr-C-fol436v.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Page with medieval Latin text from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmina_Cantabrigiensia" title="Carmina Cantabrigiensia">Carmina Cantabrigiensia</a> (Cambridge University Library, Gg. 5. 35), 11. cent.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Medieval Latin</b> refers to the literary and administrative Latin used in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. It exhibits much variation between individual authors, mainly due to poor communications in those times between different regions. The individuality is characterised by a different range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solecisms" title="Solecisms">solecisms</a> and by the borrowing of different words from <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> or from the local vernacular. Some styles show features intermediate between Latin and Romance languages; others are closer to classical Latin. The stylistic variations came to an end with the rise of nations and new empires in the Renaissance period, and the authority of the first universities imposing a new style, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Latin" title="Renaissance Latin">Renaissance Latin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_Latin">Renaissance Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Renaissance Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Latin" title="Renaissance Latin">Renaissance Latin</a></div>
<p><b>Renaissance Latin</b> is a name given to the Latin written during the European <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> in the 14th-16th centuries, particularly distinguished by the distinctive Latin style developed by <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">the humanist movement</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Ad_fontes" title="Ad fontes">Ad fontes</a></i> was the general cry of the humanists, and as such their Latin style sought to purge Latin of the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">medieval Latin</a> vocabulary and stylistic accretions that it had acquired in the centuries after the fall of the Roman Empire. They looked to Golden Age Latin literature, and especially to <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">prose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a>, as the arbiters of Latin style. They abandoned the use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(poetry)" title="Sequence (poetry)">sequence</a> and other accentual forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter_in_poetry" title="Meter in poetry">meter</a>, and sought instead to revive the Greek formats that were used in <a href="/wiki/Latin_poetry" title="Latin poetry">Latin poetry</a> during the Roman period. The humanists condemned the large body of medieval Latin literature as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Medieval" title="High Medieval">gothic</a>" – for them, a term of abuse – and believed instead that only ancient Latin from the Roman period was "real Latin".</p>
<p>The humanists also sought to purge written Latin of medieval developments in its <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>. They insisted, for example, that <i>ae</i> be written out in full wherever it occurred in classical Latin; medieval scribes often wrote <i>e</i> instead of <i>ae</i>. They were much more zealous than medieval Latin writers in distinguishing <i>t</i> from <i>c</i>: because the effects of <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">palatalization</a> made them <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophones</a>, medieval scribes often wrote, for example, <i>eciam</i> for <i>etiam</i>. Their reforms even affected <a href="/wiki/Handwriting" title="Handwriting">handwriting</a>: humanists usually wrote Latin in a script derived from <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule" title="Carolingian minuscule">Carolingian minuscule</a>, the ultimate ancestor of most contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower-case" title="Lower-case">lower-case</a> <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a>, avoiding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-letter" title="Black-letter">black-letter</a> scripts used in the Middle Ages. <a href="/wiki/Erasmus" title="Erasmus">Erasmus</a> even proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Latin_regional_pronunciation" title="Latin regional pronunciation">then-traditional pronunciations</a> of Latin be abolished in favour of his <a href="/wiki/Latin_spelling_and_pronunciation" title="Latin spelling and pronunciation">reconstructed version</a> of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">classical Latin</a> pronunciation.</p>
<p>The humanist plan to remake Latin was largely successful, at least in <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>. Schools now taught the humanistic spellings, and encouraged the study of the texts selected by the humanists, largely to the exclusion of later Latin literature. On the other hand, while humanist Latin was an elegant <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a>, it became much harder to write books about <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> or contemporary <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> in Latin while observing all of the humanists' norms of vocabulary purging and classical usage. Because humanist Latin lacked precise vocabulary to deal with modern issues, their reforms accelerated the transformation of Latin from a working language to an object of antiquarian study. Their attempts at literary work, especially poetry, often have a strong element of <a href="/wiki/Pastiche" title="Pastiche">pastiche</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a></div>
<p><b>New Latin</b> (or <b>Neo-Latin</b>) is a post-medieval version of Latin, now used primarily in <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">international scientific vocabulary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Systematics" title="Systematics">systematics</a>. The term came into widespread use towards the end of the 1890s among <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientists</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">Classicists</a> use the term "Neo-Latin" to describe the use of the Latin language for any purpose, scientific or literary, after the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> (for which purpose they often use the date 1500), although, for example, the editors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Tatti_Renaissance_Library" title="I Tatti Renaissance Library">I Tatti Renaissance Library</a> call their <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> Latin language texts Neo-Latin as well. Such <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_Latin" title="Contemporary Latin">Contemporary Latin</a> includes ecclesiastical use, as well as translations from modern languages into Latin and the occasional poetry. Under the name "Living Latin", some have advocated reviving the language as a means of spoken communication.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Italic" title="Proto-Italic">Proto-Italic</a> inherited all ten of the early post-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">Proto-Indo-European</a> simple vowels (i.e. at a time when laryngeals had colored and often lengthened adjacent vowels and then disappeared in many circumstances): <i>*a, *e, *i, *o, *u, *ā, *ē, *ī, *ō, *ū</i>. It also inherited all of the post-PIE diphthongs except for <i>*eu</i>, which became <i>*ou</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Italic" title="Proto-Italic">Proto-Italic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_Latin" title="Old Latin">Old Latin</a> had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_accent" title="Stress accent">stress accent</a> on the first syllable of a word, and this caused steady reduction and eventual deletion of many short vowels in non-initial syllables while affecting initial syllables much less. Long vowels were largely unaffected in general except in final syllables, where they had a tendency to shorten.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_vowels">Pure vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pure vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_syllables">Initial syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Initial syllables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In initial syllables, Latin generally preserves all of the simple vowels of Proto-Italic (see above):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*h₂eǵros</i> "field" &gt; <i>*agro-</i> &gt; <i>ager</i>, gen. <i>agrī</i> (cf. Greek <i>agrós</i>, English <i>acre</i>, Sanskrit <i>ájra-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*kápr̥-</i> "penis" &gt; <i>*kapros</i> &gt; <i>caper</i> "he-goat", gen. <i>caprī</i> (cf. Greek <i>kápros</i> "boar", Old English <i>hæfer</i> "he-goat", Sanskrit <i>kápr̥th-</i> "penis")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*seḱs</i> "six", <i>septḿ̥</i> "seven" &gt; <i>sex, septem</i> (cf. Greek <i>heks, heptá</i>, Lithuanian <i>šešì</i>, <i>septynì</i>, Sanskrit <i>ṣáṣ, saptá</i>)</li>
<li>PIE *<i>kʷis</i> &gt; <i>quis</i> "who?" (Greek <i>tís</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Avestan <i>čiš</i>, Sanskrit <i>kíḥ</i>)</li>
<li>PIE *<i>kʷod</i> &gt; <i>quod</i> "what, that" (relative) (Old English <i>hwæt</i> "what", Sanskrit <i>kád</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*oḱtōu</i> "eight" &gt; <i>octō</i> (Greek <i>oktṓ</i>, Irish <i>ocht</i>, Sanskrit <i>aṣṭā́(u)</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*nokʷts</i> "night" &gt; <i>nox</i>, gen. <i>noctis</i> (Greek <i>nuks</i> &lt; <i>*nokʷs</i>, Sanskrit <i>nák</i> &lt; <i>*nakts</i>, Lithuanian <i>naktìs</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*(H)i̯ug-óm</i> "yoke" &gt; <i>iugum</i> (Greek <i>zugón</i>, Gothic <i>juk</i>, Sanskrit <i>yugá-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*méh₂tēr</i> "mother" &gt; <i>*mā́tēr</i> &gt; <i>māter</i> (<a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek" title="Doric Greek">Doric Greek</a> <i>mā́tēr</i>, Old Irish <i>máthir</i>, Sanskrit <i>mātár-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*sweh₂dús</i> "pleasing, tasty" &gt; <i>*swādu-</i> &gt; <i>*swādwi-</i> (remade into <i>i</i>-stem) &gt; <i>suāvis</i> (Doric Greek <i>hādús</i>, English <i>sweet</i>, Sanskrit <i>svādú-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*sēmi-</i> (or <i>*seh₁mi-</i>) "half" &gt; <i>sēmi-</i> (Greek <i>hēmi-</i>, Old English <i>sām-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*gneh₃-tó-</i> "known" &gt; <i>*gnō-tó-</i> &gt; <i>nōtus</i> (cf. <i>i-gnōtus</i> "unknown"; Welsh <i>gnawd</i> "customary", Sanskrit <i>jñātá-</i>; Greek <i>gnōtós</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*muHs</i> "mouse" &gt; <i>mūs</i> (cf. Old English <i>mūs</i>, Greek <i>mûs</i>, Sanskrit <i>mū́ṣ-</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Short vowel changes in initial syllables:</p>
<p>1. <i>*swe-</i> &gt; <i>so-</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*swesōr</i> &gt; <i>soror</i>, gen. <i>sorōris</i> "sister"</li>
<li><i>*swépnos</i> (or *<i>swópnos</i>) &gt; <i>somnus</i> "sleep"</li>
</ul>
<p>2. <i>*we-</i> &gt; <i>wo-</i> before labial consonants or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velarized_alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Velarized alveolar lateral approximant">velarized <i>l</i></a> [ɫ] (<i>l pinguis</i>; i.e. an <i>l</i> not followed by <i>i</i>, <i>ī</i> or <i>l</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*welō</i> "I want" &gt; <i>volō</i> (vs. <i>velle</i> "to want" before <i>l exīlis</i>)</li>
<li><i>*wemō</i> "I vomit" &gt; <i>vomō</i> (cf. Greek <i>eméō</i>, Sanskrit <i>vámiti</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>3. <i>e</i> &gt; <i>i</i> before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋ]</span> (spelled <i>n</i> before a velar, or <i>g</i> before <i>n</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE *<i>dn̥ǵʰwéh₂s</i> &gt; <i>*dn̥ɣwā</i> &gt; <i>*denɣʷā</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>dingua</i> &gt; <i>lingua</i> "tongue" (<i>l-</i> from <i>lingō</i> "to lick")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*pénkwe</i> "five" &gt; *<i>kʷenkʷe</i> &gt; <i>quīnque</i> (long <i>ī</i> from <i>quīntus</i> "fifth" &lt; <i>*quīnctos</i>, where lengthening is normal before <i>nct</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*deḱ-no-</i> &gt; *<i>deŋno-</i> &gt; <i>dignus</i> "worthy"</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Medial_syllables">Medial syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Medial syllables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In non-initial syllables, there was more vowel reduction of short vowels. The most extreme case occurs with short vowels in medial syllables (i.e. short vowels in a syllable that is neither the first nor the last), where all five vowels usually merge into a single vowel:</p>
<p>1. They merge into <i>e</i> before <i>r</i> (sometimes original <i>o</i> is unaffected)</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*in-armis</i> &gt; <i>inermis</i> "unarmed" (vs. <i>arma</i> "arms")</li>
<li>Latin-<a href="/wiki/Faliscan_language" title="Faliscan language">Faliscan</a> <i>*pe-par-ai</i> "I gave birth" &gt; <i>peperī</i> (vs. <i>pariō</i> "I give birth")</li>
<li><i>*kom-gesō</i> &gt; <i>congerō</i> "to collect" (vs. <i>gerō</i> "to do, carry out")</li>
<li><i>*kinis-es</i> "ash" (gen.sg.) &gt; <i>cineris</i> (vs. nom.sg. <i>cinis</i>)</li>
<li><i>*Falisioi</i> &gt; <i>Faleriī</i> "Falerii (major town of the Faliscans)" (vs. <i>Faliscus</i> "Faliscan")</li>
<li><i>*-foro-</i> "carrying" (cf. Greek <i>-phóros</i>) &gt; <i>-fero-</i>, e.g. <i>furcifer</i> "gallows bird"</li>
<li>PIE <i>*swéḱuros</i> "father-in-law" &gt; <i>*swekuros</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>*soceros</i> &gt; <i>socer</i>, gen. <i>socerī</i></li>
</ul>
<p>2. They become Old Latin <i>o</i> &gt; <i>u</i> before <i>l pinguis</i>, i.e., an <i>l</i> not followed by <i>i, ī,</i> or <i>l</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*en-saltō</i> "to leap upon" &gt; <i>īnsoltō</i> (with lengthening before <i>ns</i>) &gt; <i>īnsultō</i> (vs. <i>saltō</i> "I leap")</li>
<li><i>*ad-alēskō</i> "to grow up" &gt; <i>adolēscō</i> &gt; <i>adulēscō</i> (vs. <i>alō</i> "I nourish")</li>
<li><i>*ob-kelō</i> "to conceal" &gt; <i>occulō</i> (vs. <i>celō</i> "I hide")</li>
<li>Greek <i>Sikelós</i> "a Sicilian" &gt; <i>*Sikolos</i> &gt; <i>Siculus</i> (vs. <i>Sicilia</i> "Sicily")</li>
<li><i>te-tol-ai</i> &gt; <i>tetulī</i> "I carried" (formerly <i>l pinguis</i> here because of the original final <i>-ai</i>)</li>
<li><i>kom-solō</i> "deliberate" &gt; <i>cōnsulō</i></li>
<li>PIE <i>*-kl̥d-to-</i> "beaten" &gt; <i>*-kolsso-</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> &gt; <i>perculsus</i> "beaten down"</li>
</ul>
<p>3. But they remain <i>o</i> before <i>l pinguis</i> when immediately following a vowel:</p>
<ul>
<li>Latin-<a href="/wiki/Faliscan_language" title="Faliscan language">Faliscan</a> <i>*fili-olos</i> &gt; <i>filiolus</i> "little son"</li>
<li>Similarly, <i>alveolus</i> "trough"</li>
</ul>
<p>4. Before /w/ the result is always <i>u</i>, in which case the /w/ is not written:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*eks-lawō</i> "I wash away" &gt; <i>ēluō</i></li>
<li><i>*mon-i-wai</i> "I warned" &gt; <i>monuī</i></li>
<li><i>*tris-diw-om</i> "period of three days" &gt; <i>trīduom</i> &gt; <i>trīduum</i></li>
<li><i>*dē nowōd</i> "anew" &gt; <i>dēnuō</i></li>
</ul>
<p>5. They become <i>i</i> before one consonant other than <i>r</i> or <i>l pinguis</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*re-fakjō</i> "to remake" &gt; <i>*refakiō</i> &gt; <i>reficiō</i> (vs. <i>faciō</i> "I do, make")</li>
<li>Latin-<a href="/wiki/Faliscan_language" title="Faliscan language">Faliscan</a> <i>*ke-kad-ai</i> "I fell" &gt; <i>cecidī</i> (vs. <i>cadō</i> "I fall")</li>
<li><i>*ad-tenējō</i> &gt; <i>attineō</i> "to concern" (vs. <i>teneō</i> "I hold")</li>
<li><i>*kom-regō</i> &gt; <i>corrigō</i> "to set right, correct" (vs. <i>regō</i> "I rule; straighten")</li>
<li>Greek <i>Sikelía</i> "Sicily" &gt; <i>Sicilia</i> (vs. <i>Siculus</i> "a Sicilian")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*me-món-h₂e</i> (perfect) "thought, pondered" &gt; Latin-Faliscan <i>*me-mon-ai</i> &gt; <i>meminī</i> "I remember"</li>
<li><i>*kom-itājō</i> "accompany" &gt; <i>comitō</i></li>
<li><i>*nowotāts</i> "newness" &gt; <i>novitās</i></li>
<li><i>*kornu-kan-</i> "trumpeter" &gt; <i>cornicen</i></li>
<li><i>*kaput-es</i> "head" (gen. sg.) &gt; <i>capitis</i> (vs. nom.sg. <i>caput</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>6. But they sometimes become <i>e</i> before one consonant other than <i>r</i> or <i>l pinguis</i>, when immediately following a vowel:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*sokiotāts</i> "fellowship" &gt; <i>societās</i></li>
<li><i>*wariogājesi</i> "to make diverse" &gt; <i>variegāre</i></li>
<li>But: <i>*tībia-kan-</i> "flute-player" &gt; <i>*tībiikan-</i> &gt; <i>tībīcen</i></li>
<li>But: <i>*medio-diēs</i> "midday" &gt; <i>*meriodiēs</i> (dissimilative rhotacism) &gt; <i>*meriidiēs</i> &gt; <i>merīdiēs</i> "noon; south"</li>
</ul>
<p>7. Variation between <i>i</i> and (often earlier) <i>u</i> is common before a single labial consonant (<i>p, b, f, m</i>), underlyingly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonus_medius" title="Sonus medius">sonus medius</a> vowel:</p>
<ul>
<li>From the root <i>*-kap-</i> "grab, catch":
<ul>
<li><i>occupō</i> "seize" vs. <i>occipiō</i> "begin"</li>
<li>From the related noun <i>*-kaps</i> "catcher": <i>prīnceps</i> "chief" (lit. "seizer of the first (position)"), gen. <i>prīncipis</i>, vs. <i>auceps</i> "bird catcher", gen. <i>aucupis</i></li>
<li><i>*man-kapiom</i> &gt; <i>mancupium</i> "purchase", later <i>mancipium</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>*sub-rapuit</i> &gt; <i>surrupuit</i> "filches", later <i>surripuit</i></li>
<li><i>*mag-is-emos</i> &gt; <i>maxumus</i> "biggest", later <i>maximus</i>; similarly <i>proxumus</i> "nearest", <i>optumus</i> "best" vs. later <i>proximus</i>, <i>optimus</i></li>
<li><i>*pot-s-omos</i> &gt; <i>possumus</i> "we can"; <i>*vel-omos</i> &gt; <i>volumus</i> "we want"; but <i>*leg-omos</i> &gt; <i>legimus</i> "we gather", and all other such verbs (<i>-umus</i> is isolated in <i>sumus</i>, <i>possumus</i> and <i>volumus</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Medially before two consonants, when the first is not <i>r</i> or <i>l pinguis</i>, the vowels do not merge to the same degree:</p>
<p>1. Original <i>a</i>, <i>e</i> and <i>u</i> merge into <i>e</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Greek <i>tálanton</i> &gt; <i>*talantom</i> &gt; <i>talentum</i></li>
<li><i>*sub-raptos</i> "filched" &gt; <i>surreptus</i> (vs. <i>raptus</i> "seized")</li>
<li><i>*re-faktos</i> "remade" &gt; <i>refectus</i> (cf. <i>factus</i> "made")</li>
<li><i>*ad-tentos</i> &gt; <i>attentus</i> "concerned" (cf. <i>tentus</i> "held", <i>attineō</i> "to concern")</li>
</ul>
<p>2. But original <i>i</i> is unaffected:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*re-likʷtos</i> "left (behind)" &gt; <i>relictus</i></li>
</ul>
<p>3. And original <i>o</i> raises to <i>u</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*legontor</i> "they gather" &gt; <i>leguntur</i></li>
<li><i>*ejontes</i> "going" (gen. sg.) &gt; <i>euntis</i></li>
<li><i>rōbos-to-</i> &gt; <i>rōbustus</i> "oaken" (cf. <i>rōbur</i> "oak" &lt; <i>*rōbos</i>)</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Syncope">Syncope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Syncope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><b>Exon's Law</b> dictates that if there are two light medial syllables in a row (schematically, <i>σσ̆σ̆σ</i>, where <i>σ</i> = syllable and <i>σ̆</i> = light syllable, where "light" means a short vowel followed by only a single consonant), the first syllable syncopates (i.e. the vowel is deleted):</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*deksiteros</i> "right (hand)" &gt; <i>dexterus</i> (cf. Greek <i>deksiterós</i>)</li>
<li><i>*re-peparai</i> &gt; <i>repperī</i> "I found" (cf. <i>peperī</i> "I gave birth" &lt; <i>*peparai</i>)</li>
<li><i>*prīsmo-kapes</i> &gt; <i>prīncipis</i> "prince" gen. sg. (nom. sg. <i>prīnceps</i> &lt; <i>*prīsmo-kaps</i> by analogy)</li>
<li><i>*mag-is-emos</i> &gt; <i>maximus</i> "biggest" (cf. <i>magis</i> "more")</li>
</ul>
<p>Syncopation tends to occur after <i>r</i> and <i>l</i> in all non-initial syllables, sometimes even in initial syllables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sihler_1995_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sihler_1995-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>*feret</i> "he carries" &gt; <i>fert</i></li>
<li><i>*agros</i> "field" &gt; <i>*agr̩s</i> &gt; <i>*agers</i> &gt; <i>*agerr</i> &gt; <i>ager</i></li>
<li><i>*imbris</i> "rainstorm" &gt; <i>*imbers</i> &gt; <i>imber</i></li>
<li><i>*tris</i> "three times" &gt; <i>*tr̩s</i> &gt; <i>*ters</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>terr</i> &gt; <i>ter</i></li>
<li><i>*faklitāts</i> &gt; <i>facultās</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Sometimes early syncope causes apparent violations of Exon's Law:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>kosolinos</i> "of hazel" &gt; <i>*kozolnos</i> (not <i>**koslinos</i>) &gt; <i>*korolnos</i> &gt; <i>*korulnos</i> (<i>o &gt; u</i> before <i>l pinguis</i>, see above) &gt; <i>colurnus</i> (metathesis)</li>
</ul>
<p>Syncope of <i>-i-</i> also occurred in <i>-ndis</i>, <i>-ntis</i> and <i>-rtis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sihler_1995_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sihler_1995-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>-nts</i> then became <i>-ns</i> with lengthening of the preceding vowel, while <i>-rts</i> was simplified to <i>-rs</i> without lengthening.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*montis</i> "hill" &gt; <i>*monts</i> &gt; <i>mōns</i></li>
<li><i>*gentis</i> "tribe" &gt; <i>*gents</i> &gt; <i>gēns</i></li>
<li><i>*frondis</i> "leaf" &gt; <i>*fronts</i> &gt; <i>frōns</i></li>
<li><i>*partis</i> "part" &gt; <i>*parts</i> &gt; <i>pars</i></li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_syllables">Final syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Final syllables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In final syllables of polysyllabic words before a final consonant or cluster, short <i>a, e, i</i> merge into either <i>e</i> or <i>i</i> depending on the following consonant, and short <i>o, u</i> merge into <i>u</i>.</p>
<p>1. Short <i>a, e, i</i> merge into <i>i</i> before a single non-nasal consonant:</p>
<ul>
<li>Proto-Italic <i>*rededas, *rededat</i> &gt; <i>reddis, reddit</i> "you return, he returns"</li>
<li>PIE thematic 2nd/3rd sg. <i>*-esi, *-eti</i> &gt; <i>*-es, *-et</i> &gt; <i>-is, -it</i> (e.g. <i>legis, legit</i> "you gather, he gathers")</li>
<li>i-stem nom. sg. <i>*-is</i> &gt; <i>-is</i></li>
</ul>
<p>2. Short <i>a, e, i</i> merge into <i>e</i> before a cluster or a single nasal consonant:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*prīsmo-kaps</i> &gt; <i>prīnceps</i> "first, chief" (cf. <i>capiō</i> "to take")</li>
<li><i>*kornu-kan-(?s)</i> &gt; <i>cornicen</i> "trumpeter" (cf. <i>canō</i> "to sing")</li>
<li><i>*mīlets</i> &gt; <i>mīles</i> "soldier"</li>
<li><i>*septḿ̥</i> &gt; <i>septem</i> "seven"</li>
<li>i-stem acc. sg. <i>*-im</i> &gt; <i>em</i></li>
</ul>
<p>3. Short <i>o, u</i> merge into <i>u</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>o-stem nominative <i>*-os</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>-os</i> &gt; <i>-us</i></li>
<li>o-stem accusative <i>*-om</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>-om</i> &gt; <i>-um</i></li>
<li>PIE thematic 3rd pl. <i>*-onti</i> &gt; <i>*-ont</i> &gt; <i>-unt</i></li>
<li>PIE <i>*i̯ekʷr̥</i> &gt; <i>*jekʷor</i> &gt; <i>iecur</i> "liver"</li>
<li>PIE thematic 3rd sg. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediopassive" title="Mediopassive">mediopassive</a> <i>*-etor</i> &gt; <i>-itur</i></li>
<li><i>*kaput</i> &gt; <i>caput</i> "head"</li>
</ul>
<p>4. All short vowels apparently merge into <i>-e</i> in absolute final position.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:History_of_Latin#Dubious" title="Talk:History of Latin">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Long vowels in final syllables shorten before most consonants (but not final <i>s</i>), yielding apparent exceptions to the above rules:</p>
<ul>
<li>Proto-Italic <i>*amāt</i> &gt; <i>amat</i> "he/she loves" (cf. passive <i>amātur</i>)</li>
<li>Proto-Italic <i>*amānt</i> &gt; <i>amant</i> "they love"</li>
<li>a-stem acc. sg. <i>*-ām</i> &gt; <i>-am</i></li>
<li>PIE thematic 1st sg. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediopassive" title="Mediopassive">mediopassive</a> <i>*-ōr</i> &gt; <i>-or</i></li>
<li><i>*swesōr</i> &gt; <i>soror</i> "sister" (cf. gen. <i>sorōris</i>)</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diphthongs">Diphthongs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Diphthongs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_syllables_2">Initial syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Initial syllables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Proto-Italic maintained all PIE diphthongs except for the change <i>*eu</i> &gt; <i>*ou</i>. The Proto-Italic diphthongs tend to remain into <a href="/wiki/Old_Latin" title="Old Latin">Old Latin</a> but generally reduce to pure long vowels by Classical Latin.</p>
<p>1. PIE <i>*ei</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>ei</i> &gt; <i>ẹ̄</i>, a vowel higher than <i>ē</i> &lt; PIE <i>*ē</i>. This then developed to <i>ī</i> normally, but to <i>ē</i> before <i>v</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*deiḱ-</i> "point (out)" &gt; Old Latin <i>deicō</i> &gt; <i>dīcō</i> "to say"</li>
<li>PIE <i>*bʰeydʰ-</i> "be persuaded, be confident" &gt; *<i>feiðe-</i> &gt; <i>fīdō</i> "to trust"</li>
<li>PIE <i>*deywós</i> "god, deity" &gt; Very Old Latin <i>deiuos</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duenos_inscription" title="Duenos inscription">Duenos inscription</a>) &gt; <i>dẹ̄vos</i> &gt; <i>deus</i></li>
<li>But nominative plural <i>*deivoi</i> &gt; <i>*deivei</i> &gt; <i>*dẹ̄vẹ̄</i> &gt; <i>dīvī</i> &gt; <i>diī</i>; vocative singular <i>*deive</i> &gt; <i>*dẹ̄ve</i> &gt; <i>dīve</i></li>
</ul>
<p>2. PIE <i>*eu, *ou</i> &gt; Proto-Italic <i>*ou</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>ou</i> &gt; <i>ọ̄</i> (higher than <i>ō</i> &lt; PIE <i>*ō</i>) &gt; <i>ū</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*(H)jeug-</i> "join" &gt; *<i>joug-s-mn-to-</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>iouxmentom</i> "pack horse" &gt; <i>iūmentum</i></li>
<li>PIE <i>*louk-s-neh₂</i> &gt; <i>*louksnā</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>losna</i> (i.e. <i>lọ̄sna</i>) &gt; <i>lūna</i> "moon" (cf. Old Prussian <i>lauxnos</i> "stars", <a href="/wiki/Avestan" title="Avestan">Avestan</a> <i>raoχšnā</i> "lantern")</li>
<li>PIE *<i>deuk-</i> &gt; *<i>douk-e-</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>doucō</i> &gt; <i>dūcō</i> "lead"</li>
</ul>
<p>3. PIE <i>*ai</i>, *<i>Hei</i> &gt; <i>ae</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE *<i>kh₂ei-ko-</i> &gt; <i>*kaiko-</i> &gt; <i>caecus</i> "blind" (cf. <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> <i>cáech</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kaiχ/</span> "blind", Gothic <i>háihs</i> "one-eyed", Sanskrit <i>kekara-</i> "squinting")</li>
</ul>
<p>4. PIE <i>*au</i> &gt; <i>au</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*h₂eug-</i> &gt; *<i>augeje/o</i> &gt; <i>augeō</i> "to increase" (cf. Greek <i>aúksō</i>, Gothic <i>áukan</i>, Lithuanian <i>áugti</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>5. PIE <i>*oi</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>oi, oe</i> &gt; <i>ū</i> (occasionally preserved as <i>oe</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE *<i>h₁oi-nos</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>oinos</i> &gt; <i>oenus</i> &gt; <i>ūnus</i> "one"</li>
<li>PIE <i>*bʰoidʰ-</i> &gt; *<i>foiðo-</i> &gt; <i>foedus</i> "treaty" (cf. <i>fīdō</i> above)</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Medial_syllables_2">Medial syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Medial syllables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>All diphthongs in medial syllables become <i>ī</i> or <i>ū</i>.</p>
<p>1. (Post-)PIE <i>*ei</i> &gt; <i>ī</i>, just as in initial syllables:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*en-deik-ō</i> &gt; <i>indīcō</i> "to point out" (cf. <i>dīcō</i> "to say")</li>
</ul>
<p>2. (Post-)PIE <i>*oi</i> &gt; <i>ū</i>, just as in initial syllables:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*n̥-poini</i> "with impunity" &gt; <i>impūne</i> (cf. <i>poena</i> "punishment").</li>
</ul>
<p>3. (Post-)PIE <i>*eu, *ou</i> &gt; Proto-Italic <i>*ou</i> &gt; <i>ū</i>, just as in initial syllables:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*en-deuk-ō</i> &gt; <i>*indoucō</i> &gt; <i>indūcō</i> "to draw over, cover" (cf. <i>dūcō</i> "to lead")</li>
</ul>
<p>4. Post-PIE <i>*ai</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>ei</i> &gt; <i>ī</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*ke-kaid-ai</i> "I cut", perf. &gt; <i>cecīdī</i> (cf. <i>caedō</i> "I cut", pres.)</li>
<li><i>*en-kaid-ō</i> "cut into" &gt; <i>incīdō</i> (cf. <i>caedō</i> "cut")</li>
<li>Early Greek (or from an earlier source) <i>*elaíwā</i> "olive" &gt; <i>olīva</i></li>
</ul>
<p>5. Post-PIE <i>*au</i> &gt; <i>ū</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*en-klaud-ō</i> "enclose" &gt; <i>inclūdō</i> (cf. <i>claudō</i> "close")</li>
<li><i>*ad-kauss-ō</i> "accuse" &gt; <i>accūsō</i> (cf. <i>causa</i> "cause")</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_syllables_2">Final syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Final syllables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Mostly like medial syllables:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>*-ei</i> &gt; <i>ī</i>: PIE <i>*meh₂tr-ei</i> "to mother" &gt; <i>mātrī</i></li>
<li><i>*-ai</i> &gt; <i>ī</i> in multisyllabic words: Latin-Faliscan <i>peparai</i> "I brought forth" &gt; <i>peperī</i></li>
<li><i>*-eu/ou-</i> &gt; <i>ū</i>: post-PIE <i>manous</i> "hand", gen. sg. &gt; <i>manūs</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Different from medial syllables:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>-oi</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>-ei</i> &gt; <i>ī</i> (not <i>ū</i>): PIE o-stem plural <i>*-oi</i> &gt; <i>-ī</i> (cf. Greek <i>-oi</i>);</li>
<li><i>-oi</i> &gt; <i>ī</i> also in monosyllables: PIE <i>kʷoi</i> "who" &gt; <i>quī</i></li>
<li><i>-ai</i> &gt; <i>ae</i> in monosyllables: PIE <i>*prh₂ei</i> "before" &gt; <i>prae</i> (cf. Greek <i>paraí</i>)</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllabic_resonants_and_laryngeals">Syllabic resonants and laryngeals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Syllabic resonants and laryngeals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The PIE syllabic resonants <i>*m̥, *n̥, *r̥, *l̥</i> generally become <i>em, en, or, ol</i> (cf. Greek <i>am/a, an/a, ar/ra, al/la</i>; Germanic <i>um, un, ur, ul</i>; Sanskrit <i>am/a, an/a, r̥, r̥</i>; <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a> <i>im̃, iñ, ir̃, il̃</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*déḱm̥(t)</i> "ten" &gt; <i>decem</i> (cf. Irish <i>deich</i>, Greek <i>deka</i>, <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothic</a> <i>taíhun</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tɛhun/</span>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*(d)ḱm̥tóm</i> "hundred" &gt; <i>centum</i> (cf. Welsh <i>cant</i>, Gothic <i>hund</i>, Lithuanian <i>šim̃tas</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*n̥-</i> "not" &gt; OL <i>en-</i> &gt; <i>in-</i> (cf. Greek <i>a-/an-</i>, English <i>un-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*tn̥tós</i> "stretched" &gt; <i>tentus</i> (cf. Greek <i>tatós</i>, Sanskrit <i>tatá-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*ḱr̥d-</i> "heart" &gt; <i>*cord</i> &gt; <i>cor</i> (cf. Greek <i>kēr</i>, English <i>heart</i>, Lithuanian <i>širdìs</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*ml̥dú-</i> "soft" &gt; <i>*moldu-</i> &gt; <i>*mollwi-</i> (remade as <i>i</i>-stem) &gt; <i>*molwi-</i> &gt; <i>mollis</i> (cf. Irish <i>meldach</i> "pleasing", English <i>mild</i>, Czech <i>mladý</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The laryngeals <i>*h₁, *h₂, *h₃</i> appear in Latin as <i>a</i> when between consonants, as in most languages (but <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>e/a/o</i> respectively, Sanskrit <i>i</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*dʰh₁-tós</i> "put" &gt; L <i>factus</i>, with /k/ of disputed etymology (cf. Greek <i>thetós</i>, Sanskrit <i>hitá-</i> &lt; <i>*dhitá-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*ph₂tḗr</i> "father" &gt; L <i>pater</i> (cf. Greek <i>patḗr</i>, Sanskrit <i>pitā́</i>, English <i>father</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*dh₃-tós</i> "given" &gt; L <i>datus</i> (cf. Greek <i>dotós</i>, Sanskrit <i>ditá-</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>A sequence of syllabic resonant + laryngeal, when before a consonant, produced <i>mā, nā, rā, lā</i> (cf. Greek <i>nē/nā/nō, rē/rā/rō</i>, etc. depending on the laryngeal; Germanic <i>um, un, ur, ul</i>; Sanskrit <i>ā, ā, īr/ūr, īr/ūr</i>; Lithuanian <i>ím, ín, ír, íl</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*ǵr̥h₂-no-</span></i> "grain" &gt; <i>grānum</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>grán</i>, English <i>corn</i>, Lithuanian <i>žìrnis</i> "pea")</li>
<li>PIE <i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*h₂wl̥h₁-neh₂</span></i> "wool" &gt; <i>*wlānā</i> &gt; <i>lāna</i> (cf. Welsh <i>gwlân</i>, Greek <i>lēnos</i>, Lithuanian <i>vìlna</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*ǵn̥h₁-to-</span></i> "born" &gt; <i>gnātus</i> "son" (cf. Middle Welsh <i>gnawt</i> "relative", Greek <i>dió-gnētos</i> "Zeus' offspring", English <i>kind</i>)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">Consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Consonants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspirates">Aspirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Aspirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Indo-European voiced aspirates <i>bʰ, dʰ, gʰ, gʷʰ</i>, which were probably breathy voiced stops, first devoiced in initial position (<a href="/wiki/Fortition" title="Fortition">fortition</a>), then fricatized in all positions, producing pairs of voiceless/voiced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">fricatives</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Italic" title="Proto-Italic">Proto-Italic</a>: <i>f</i> ~ <i>β</i>, <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">θ</span></i> ~ <i>ð</i>, <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">χ</span></i> ~ <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɣ</span></i>, <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">χʷ</span></i> ~ <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɣʷ</span></i> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The fricatives were voiceless in initial position. However, between vowels and other voiced sounds, there are indications — in particular, their evolution in Latin — that the sounds were actually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" title="Voiced consonant">voiced</a>. Likewise, Proto-Italic /s/ apparently had a voiced allophone [z] in the same position.</p>
<p>In all Italic languages, the word-initial voiceless fricatives <i>f</i>, <i>θ</i>, and <i>χʷ</i> all merged to <i>f</i>; thus, in Latin, the normal outcome of initial PIE <i>bʰ, dʰ, gʰ, gʷʰ</i> is <i>f, f, h, f</i>, respectively. Examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*bʰréh₂tēr</i> "brother" &gt; <i>*bʰrā́tēr</i> &gt; <i>frāter</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>bráthair</i>, Sanskrit <i>bhrā́tar-</i>, Greek <i>phrā́tēr</i> "member of a phratry")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*bʰére</i> "carry" &gt; <i>ferō</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>beirim</i> "I bear", English <i>bear</i>, Sanskrit <i>bhárati</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*dʰwṓr</i> "door" &gt; <i>θwor-</i> &gt; *<i>forā</i> &gt; <i>forēs</i> (pl.) "door(s)" (cf. Welsh <i>dôr</i>, Greek <i>thurā</i>, Sanskrit <i>dhvā́raḥ</i> (pl.))</li>
<li>PIE <i>*dʰeh₁-</i> "put, place" &gt; *<i>dʰh₁-k-</i> &gt; *<i>θaki-</i> &gt; <i>faciō</i> "do, make" (cf. Welsh <i>dodi</i>, English <i>do</i>, Greek <i>títhēmi</i> "I put", Sanskrit <i>dádhāti</i> he puts")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*gʰabʰ-</i> "seize, take" &gt; *<i>χaβ-ē</i>- &gt; <i>habeō</i> "have" (cf. Old Irish <i>gaibid</i> "takes", Old English <i>gifan</i> "to give", Polish <i>gabać</i> "to seize")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*ǵʰh₂ens</i> "goose" &gt; *<i>χans-</i> &gt; <i>(h)ānser</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>géiss</i> "swan", German <i>Gans</i>, Greek <i>khḗn</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*ǵʰaidos</i> "goat" &gt; *<i>χaidos</i> &gt; <i>hædus</i> "kid" (cf. Old English <i>gāt</i> "goat", Polish <i>zając</i> "hare", Sanskrit <i>háyas</i> "horse")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*gʷʰerm-</i> "warm" &gt; *<i>χʷormo-</i> &gt; <i>formus</i> (cf. Old Prussian <i>gorme</i> "heat", Greek <i>thermós</i>, Sanskrit <i>gharmáḥ</i> "heat")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*gʷʰen-dʰ-</i> "to strike, kill" &gt; *<i>χʷ(e)nð-</i> &gt; <i>fendō</i> (cf. Welsh <i>gwanu</i> "to stab", Old High German <i>gundo</i> "battle", Sanskrit <i>hánti</i> "(he) strikes, kills")</li>
</ul>
<p>Word-internal <i>*-bʰ-, *-dʰ-, *-gʰ-, *-gʷʰ-</i> evolved into Proto-Italic <i>β</i>, <i>ð</i>, <i>ɣ</i>, <i>ɣʷ</i>. In Osco-Umbrian, the same type of merger occurred as that affecting voiceless fricatives, with <i>β</i>, <i>ð</i>, and <i>ɣʷ</i> merging to <i>β</i>. In Latin, this did not happen, and instead the fricatives defricatized, giving <i>b, d ~ b, g ~ h, g ~ v ~ gu</i>.</p>
<p><i>*-bʰ-</i> is the simplest case, consistently becoming <i>b</i>.</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*bʰébʰrus</i> "beaver" &gt; *<i>feβro</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>feber</i> &gt; <i>fiber</i></li>
</ul>
<p><i>*-dʰ-</i> usually becomes <i>d</i>, but becomes <i>b</i> next to <i>r</i> or <i>u</i>, or before <i>l</i>.</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*bʰeidʰ-</i> "be persuaded" &gt; *<i>feiðe</i> &gt; <i>fīdō</i> "I trust" (cf. Old English <i>bīdan</i> "to wait", Greek <i>peíthō</i> "I trust")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*medʰi-o-</i> "middle" &gt; *<i>meðio-</i> &gt; <i>medius</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>mide</i>, Gothic <i>midjis</i>, Sanskrit <i>mádhya-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE *<i>h₁rudʰ-ró-</i> "red" &gt; <i>*ruðro-</i> &gt; <i>ruber</i> (cf. Old Russian <i>rodrŭ</i>, Greek <i>eruthrós</i>, Sanskrit <i>rudhirá-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*werh₁-dʰh₁-o-</i> "word" &gt; *<i>werðo-</i> &gt; <i>verbum</i> (cf. English <i>word</i>, Lithuanian <i>var̃das</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*sth̥₂-dʰlom</i> &gt; <i>*staðlom</i> &gt; <i>stabulum</i> "abode" (cf. German <i>Stadel</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*krei(H)-dʰrom</i> "sieve, sifter" &gt; *<i>kreiðrom</i> &gt; <i>crībrum</i> "sieve" (cf. Old Irish <i>críathar</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/krīǝ̯θǝρ/</span> "sieve", Old English <i>hrīder</i> "sieve")</li>
</ul>
<p>The development of <i>*-gʰ-</i> is twofold: <i>*-gʰ-</i> becomes <i>h</i> [ɦ] between vowels but <i>g</i> elsewhere:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*weǵʰ-</i> "carry" &gt; *<i>weɣ-e/o</i> &gt; <i>vehō</i> (cf. Greek <i>okhéomai</i> "I ride", Old English <i>wegan</i> "to carry", Sanskrit <i>váhati</i> "(he) drives")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*dʰi-n-ǵʰ-</i> "shapes, forms" &gt; *<i>θinɣ-e/o</i> &gt; <i>fingō</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>-ding</i> "erects, builds", Gothic <i>digan</i> "to mold, shape")</li>
</ul>
<p><i>*-gʷʰ-</i> has three outcomes, becoming <i>gu</i> after <i>n</i>, <i>v</i> between vowels, and <i>g</i> next to other consonants. All three variants are visible in the same root <i>*snigʷʰ-</i> "snow" (cf. Irish <i>snigid</i> "snows", Greek <i>nípha</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*snigʷʰ-s</i> &gt; *<i>sniɣʷs</i> &gt; *<i>nigs</i> &gt; nom. sg. <i>nix</i> "snow"</li>
<li>PIE <i>*snigʷʰ-ós</i> &gt; *<i>sniɣʷos</i> &gt; *<i>niβis</i> &gt; gen. sg. <i>nivis</i> "of snow"</li>
<li>PIE <i>*snei-gʷʰ-e/o</i> &gt; *<i>sninɣʷ-e/o</i> (with <i>n</i>-infix) &gt; <i>ninguit</i> "it snows"</li>
</ul>
<p>Other examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*h₁le(n)gʷʰu-</i> &gt; <i>*h₁legʷʰu-</i> &gt; *<i>leɣʷus</i> &gt; *<i>leβwi-</i> (remade as <i>i</i>-stem) &gt; <i>levis</i> "lightweight" (cf. Welsh <i>llaw</i> "small, low", Greek <i>elakhús</i> "small", Sanskrit <i>laghú-</i>, <i>raghú-</i> "quick, light, small")</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Labiovelars">Labiovelars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Labiovelars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>*gʷ</i> has results much like non-initial <i>*-gʷʰ</i>, becoming <i>v</i> /w/ in most circumstances, but <i>gu</i> after a nasal and <i>g</i> next to other consonants:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE *<i>gʷih₃wos</i> &gt; <i>*ɣʷīwos</i> &gt; <i>vīvus</i> "alive" (cf. Old Irish <i>biu</i>, <i>beo</i>, Lithuanian <i>gývas</i>, Sanskrit <i>jīvá- "alive")</i></li>
<li>PIE <i>*gʷm̥i̯e/o-</i> "come" &gt; *<i>ɣʷen-je/o</i> &gt; <i>veniō</i> (cf. English <i>come</i>, Greek <i>baínō</i> "I go", Avestan <i>ǰamaiti</i> "he goes")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*gʷr̥h₂us</i> "heavy" &gt; <i>*ɣʷraus</i> &gt; <i>grāvis</i> (cf. Greek <i>barús</i>, Gothic <i>kaúrus</i>, Sanskrit <i>gurú-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*h₃engʷ-</i> &gt; <i>*onɣʷ-en</i> &gt; <i>unguen</i> "salve" (cf. Old Irish <i>imb</i> "butter", <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>ancho</i> "butter", Sanskrit <i>añjanam</i> "anointing, ointment")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*n̥gʷén-</i> "(swollen) gland" &gt; <i>*enɣʷen</i> &gt; <i>inguen</i> "bubo; groin" (cf. Greek <i>adḗn</i> gen. <i>adénos</i> "gland", Old High German <i>ankweiz</i> "pustules")</li>
</ul>
<p><i>*kʷ</i> remains as <i>qu</i> before a vowel, but reduces to <i>c</i> /k/ before a consonant or next to a <i>u</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*kʷetwóres</i>, neut. <i>*kʷetwṓr</i> "four" &gt; <i>quattuor</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>cethair</i>, Lithuanian <i>keturì</i>, Sanskrit <i>catvā́raḥ</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*sekʷ-</i> "to follow" &gt; <i>sequor</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>sechem</i>, Greek <i>hépomai</i>, Sanskrit <i>sácate</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*leikʷ-</i> (pres. *<i>li-né-kʷ-</i>) "leave behind" &gt; <i>*linkʷ-e/o-</i> : <i>*likʷ-ē-</i> &gt; <i>linquō</i> "leaves" : <i>liceō</i> "is allowed; is for sale" (cf. Greek <i>leípō, limpánō</i>, Sanskrit <i>riṇákti</i>, Gothic <i>leiƕan</i> "to lend")</li>
<li>PIE <i>*nokʷts</i> "night" &gt; <i>nox</i>, gen. sg. <i>noctis</i></li>
</ul>
<p>The sequence <i>*p ... *kʷ</i> assimilates to <i>*kʷ ... *kʷ</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*pénkʷe</i> "five" &gt; <i>quīnque</i> (cf. Old Irish <i>cóic</i>, Greek <i>pénte</i>, Sanskrit <i>páñca</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*pérkʷus</i> "oak" &gt; <i>quercus</i> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Trentino" title="Trentino">Trentino</a> <i>porca</i> "fir", Punjabi <i>pargāī</i> "holm oak", Gothic <i>faírƕus</i> "world", <i>faírgun-</i> "mountain"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*pekʷō</i> "I cook" &gt; <i>*kʷekʷō</i> &gt; <i>coquō</i> (cf. <i>coquīna, cocīnā</i> "kitchen" vs. <i>popīna</i> "tavern" &lt; Oscan, where <i>*kʷ</i> &gt; <i>p</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The sequences <i>*ḱw, *ǵw, *ǵʰw</i> develop identically to <i>*kʷ, *gʷ, *gʷʰ</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*éḱwos</i> "horse" &gt; <i>*ekʷos</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>equos</i> &gt; <i>ecus</i> &gt; <i>equus</i> (assimilated from other forms, e.g. gen. sg. <i>equī</i>; cf. Sanskrit <i>aśva-</i>, which indicates <i>-ḱw-</i> not <i>-kʷ-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*ǵʰweh₁ro-</i> "wild animal" &gt; *<i>χʷero-</i> &gt; <i>ferus</i> (cf. Greek <i>thḗr</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lesbian_Greek" title="Lesbian Greek">Lesbian</a> <i>phḗr</i>, Lithuanian <i>žvėrìs</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*mreǵʰus</i> "short" &gt; <i>*mreɣu-</i> &gt; <i>*mreɣwi-</i> (remade as <i>i</i>-stem) &gt; <i>brevis</i> (cf. Old English <i>myrge</i> "briefly", Greek <i>brakhús</i>, <a href="/wiki/Avestan" title="Avestan">Avestan</a> <i>mǝrǝzu-</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*dn̥ǵʰwéh₂</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> "tongue" &gt; <i>*dn̥ɣwā</i> &gt; <i>*denɣʷā</i> &gt; Old Latin <i>dingua</i> &gt; <i>lingua</i></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sequences">Other sequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Other sequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Initial <i>*dw-</i> (attested in Old Latin as <i>du-</i>) becomes <i>b-</i>, thus compensating for the dearth of words beginning with <i>*b</i> in PIE:</p>
<ul>
<li>PIE <i>*dwis</i> "twice" &gt; <i>duis</i> &gt; <i>bis</i> (cf. Greek <i>dís</i>, Sanskrit <i>dvis</i>)</li>
<li>PIE <i>*deu-l̥-</i> "injure" &gt; <i>duellom</i> "war" &gt; <i>bellum</i> (a variant <i>duellum</i> survived in poetry as a trisyllabic word, whence English "duel")</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="S-rhotacism">S-rhotacism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: S-rhotacism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Indo-European <i>s</i> between vowels was first voiced to [z] in late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Italic" title="Proto-Italic">Proto-Italic</a> and famously became <i>r</i> in Latin and <a href="/wiki/Umbrian_language" title="Umbrian language">Umbrian</a>, a change known as <a href="/wiki/Rhotacism_(sound_change)" title="Rhotacism (sound change)">rhotacism</a>. Early Old Latin documents still have <i>s</i> [z], and <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> once remarked that a certain Papirius Crassus officially changed his name from Papisius in 339 <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">b</span>.<span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">c</span>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> indicating the approximate time of this change. This produces many alternations in Latin declension:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>flōs</i> "flower", gen. <i>flōris</i></li>
<li><i>mūs</i> "mouse", pl. <i>mūrēs</i></li>
<li><i>est</i> "he is", fut. <i>erit</i> "he will be"</li>
</ul>
<p>Other examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>Proto-Italic *<i>ausōs</i>, <i>ausōsem</i> &gt; *<i>auzōs</i>, <i>auzōzem</i> &gt; <i>aurōra</i> "dawn" (change of suffix; cf. English <i>east</i>, <a href="/wiki/Aeolic_Greek" title="Aeolic Greek">Aeolic Greek</a> <i>aúōs</i>, Sanskrit <i>uṣā́s</i>)</li>
<li>Proto-Italic <i>*swesōr</i> &gt; <i>*swozōr</i> &gt; <i>soror</i> "sister" (cf. <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sweostor</i>, Sanskrit <i>svásar</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>However, before another <i>r</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dissimilation" title="Dissimilation">dissimilation</a> occurred with <i>sr</i> [zr] becoming <i>br</i> (likely via an intermediate <i>*ðr</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>Proto-Italic <i>*swesr-īnos</i> &gt; <i>*swezrīnos</i> ~ <i>*sweðrīnos</i> &gt; <i>sobrīnus</i> "maternal cousin" (with <i>so-</i> by analogy to <i>soror</i>)</li>
<li>Proto-Italic <i>*keras-rom</i> &gt; <i>*kerazrom</i> ~ <i>*keraðrom</i> &gt; <i>cerebrum</i> "skull, brain" (cf. Greek <i>kéras</i> "horn")</li>
</ul>
