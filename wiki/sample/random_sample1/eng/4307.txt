<p><b>Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[raffaˈɛllo ˈsantsjo da urˈbiːno]</a></span>; March 28 or April 6, 1483 – April 6, 1520),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> known as <b>Raphael</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>), was an Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painter" title="Painter">painter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> of the <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a>. His work is admired for its clarity of form, ease of composition, and visual achievement of the <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonic</a> ideal of human grandeur. Together with <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, he forms the traditional trinity of great masters of that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Raphael was enormously productive, running an unusually large workshop and, despite his death at 37, leaving a large body of work. Many of his works are found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_Palace" title="Vatican Palace">Vatican Palace</a>, where the frescoed <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Rooms" title="Raphael Rooms">Raphael Rooms</a> were the central, and the largest, work of his career. The best known work is <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">The School of Athens</a></i> in the Vatican <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanza_della_Segnatura" title="Stanza della Segnatura">Stanza della Segnatura</a>. After his early years in Rome much of his work was executed by his workshop from his drawings, with considerable loss of quality. He was extremely influential in his lifetime, though outside Rome his work was mostly known from his collaborative <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaking</a>.</p>
<p>After his death, the influence of his great rival Michelangelo was more widespread until the 18th and 19th centuries, when Raphael's more serene and harmonious qualities were again regarded as the highest models. His career falls naturally into three phases and three styles, first described by <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a>: his early years in <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a>, then a period of about four years (1504–1508) absorbing the artistic traditions of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, followed by his last hectic and triumphant twelve years in Rome, working for two Popes and their close associates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urbino">Urbino</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3576" data-file-width="2924" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg/220px-Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg/330px-Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg/440px-Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giovanni_Santi_-_Christ_supported_by_two_angels.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Santi" title="Giovanni Santi">Giovanni Santi</a>, Raphael's father; <i>Christ supported by two angels</i>, c.1490</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Raphael was born in the small but artistically significant central Italian city of <a href="/wiki/Urbino" title="Urbino">Urbino</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marche</a> region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> where his father <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Santi" title="Giovanni Santi">Giovanni Santi</a> was court painter to the Duke. The reputation of the court had been established by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federico_III_da_Montefeltro" title="Federico III da Montefeltro">Federico III da Montefeltro</a>, a highly successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condottiere" title="Condottiere">condottiere</a> who had been created <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Urbino" title="Duke of Urbino">Duke of Urbino</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_IV" title="Pope Sixtus IV">the Pope</a> - Urbino formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a> - and who died the year before Raphael was born. The emphasis of Federico's court was rather more literary than artistic, but Giovanni Santi was a poet of sorts as well as a painter, and had written a rhymed chronicle of the life of Federico, and both wrote the texts and produced the decor for <a href="/wiki/Masque" title="Masque">masque</a>-like court entertainments. His poem to Federico shows him as keen to show awareness of the most advanced North Italian painters, and <a href="/wiki/Early_Netherlandish_painting" title="Early Netherlandish painting">Early Netherlandish artists</a> as well. In the very small court of Urbino he was probably more integrated into the central circle of the ruling family than most court painters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Federico was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro" title="Guidobaldo da Montefeltro">Guidobaldo da Montefeltro</a>, who married <a href="/wiki/Elisabetta_Gonzaga" title="Elisabetta Gonzaga">Elisabetta Gonzaga</a>, daughter of the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>, the most brilliant of the smaller Italian courts for both music and the visual arts. Under them, the court continued as a centre for literary culture. Growing up in the circle of this small court gave Raphael the excellent manners and social skills stressed by <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Vasari</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Court life in Urbino at just after this period was to become set as the model of the virtues of the Italian humanist court through <a href="/wiki/Baldassare_Castiglione" title="Baldassare Castiglione">Baldassare Castiglione</a>'s depiction of it in his classic work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_the_Courtier" title="The Book of the Courtier">The Book of the Courtier</a></i>, published in 1528. Castiglione moved to Urbino in 1504, when Raphael was no longer based there but frequently visited, and they became good friends. He became close to other regular visitors to the court: <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Dovizi" title="Bernardo Dovizi">Pietro Bibbiena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bembo" title="Pietro Bembo">Pietro Bembo</a>, both later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinals</a>, were already becoming well known as writers, and would be in Rome during Raphael's period there. Raphael mixed easily in the highest circles throughout his life, one of the factors that tended to give a misleading impression of effortlessness to his career. He did not receive a full <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanistic education</a> however; it is unclear how easily he read Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_work">Early life and work</span></h2>
<p>His mother Màgia died in 1491 when Raphael was eight, followed on August 1, 1494 by his father, who had already remarried. Raphael was thus orphaned at eleven; his formal guardian became his only paternal uncle Bartolomeo, a priest, who subsequently engaged in litigation with his stepmother. He probably continued to live with his stepmother when not staying as an apprentice with a master. He had already shown talent, according to Vasari, who says that Raphael had been "a great help to his father".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Self-portrait" title="Self-portrait">self-portrait</a> drawing from his teenage years shows his precocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His father's workshop continued and, probably together with his stepmother, Raphael evidently played a part in managing it from a very early age. In Urbino, he came into contact with the works of <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Uccello" title="Paolo Uccello">Paolo Uccello</a>, previously the court painter (d. 1475), and <a href="/wiki/Luca_Signorelli" title="Luca Signorelli">Luca Signorelli</a>, who until 1498 was based in nearby <a href="/wiki/Citt%C3%A0_di_Castello" title="Città di Castello">Città di Castello</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Vasari, his father placed him in the workshop of the Umbrian master <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Perugino" title="Pietro Perugino">Pietro Perugino</a> as an apprentice "despite the tears of his mother". The evidence of an apprenticeship comes only from Vasari and another source,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and has been disputed—eight was very early for an apprenticeship to begin. An alternative theory is that he received at least some training from <a href="/wiki/Timoteo_Viti" title="Timoteo Viti">Timoteo Viti</a>, who acted as court painter in Urbino from 1495.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Most modern historians agree that Raphael at least worked as an assistant to Perugino from around 1500; the influence of Perugino on Raphael's early work is very clear: "probably no other pupil of genius has ever absorbed so much of his master's teaching as Raphael did", according to <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_W%C3%B6lfflin" title="Heinrich Wölfflin">Wölfflin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Vasari wrote that it was impossible to distinguish between their hands at this period, but many modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_historian" title="Art historian">art historians</a> claim to do better and detect his hand in specific areas of works by Perugino or his workshop. Apart from stylistic closeness, their techniques are very similar as well, for example having paint applied thickly, using an oil varnish medium, in shadows and darker garments, but very thinly on flesh areas. An excess of resin in the varnish often causes <a href="/wiki/Craquelure" title="Craquelure">cracking</a> of areas of paint in the works of both masters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Perugino workshop was active in both <a href="/wiki/Perugia" title="Perugia">Perugia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, perhaps maintaining two permanent branches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Raphael is described as a "master", that is to say fully trained, in December 1500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>His first documented work was the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baronci_altarpiece" title="Baronci altarpiece">Baronci altarpiece</a></i> for the church of Saint <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_of_Tolentino" title="Nicholas of Tolentino">Nicholas of Tolentino</a> in Città di Castello, a town halfway between Perugia and Urbino.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Evangelista da Pian di Meleto, who had worked for his father, was also named in the commission. It was commissioned in 1500 and finished in 1501; now only some cut sections and a preparatory drawing remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In the following years he painted works for other churches there, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Mond_Crucifixion" title="Mond Crucifixion">Mond Crucifixion</a></i> (about 1503) and the <a href="/wiki/Pinacoteca_di_Brera" title="Pinacoteca di Brera">Brera</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_the_Virgin_(Raphael)" title="The Marriage of the Virgin (Raphael)">Wedding of the Virgin</a></i> (1504), and for Perugia, such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oddi_altar_(Raphael)" title="Oddi altar (Raphael)">Oddi Altarpiece</a></i>. He very probably also visited Florence in this period. These are large works, some in <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a>, where Raphael confidently marshals his compositions in the somewhat static style of Perugino. He also painted many small and exquisite <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_painting" title="Cabinet painting">cabinet paintings</a> in these years, probably mostly for the connoisseurs in the Urbino court, like the <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Graces_(Raphael)" title="Three Graces (Raphael)">Three Graces</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/St._Michael_(Raphael)" title="St. Michael (Raphael)">St. Michael</a></i>, and he began to paint <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(art)" title="Madonna (art)">Madonnas</a> and portraits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1502 he went to <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> at the invitation of another pupil of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perugino" title="Perugino">Perugino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinturicchio" title="Pinturicchio">Pinturicchio</a>, "being a friend of Raphael and knowing him to be a draughtsman of the highest quality" to help with the <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a>, and very likely the designs, for a fresco series in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piccolomini_Library" title="Piccolomini Library">Piccolomini Library</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siena_Cathedral" title="Siena Cathedral">Siena Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He was evidently already much in demand even at this early stage in his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_Florence">Influence of Florence</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="870" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg/220px-Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg/330px-Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg/440px-Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raphael_Madonna_of_the_Pinks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Madonna of the Pinks</i>, c. 1506–7, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Gallery,_London" title="National Gallery, London">National Gallery, London</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Raphael led a "nomadic" life, working in various centres in Northern Italy, but spent a good deal of time in Florence, perhaps from about 1504. Although there is traditional reference to a "Florentine period" of about 1504–8, he was possibly never a continuous resident there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He may have needed to visit the city to secure materials in any case. There is a letter of recommendation of Raphael, dated October 1504, from the mother of the next Duke of Urbino to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonfaloniere_of_Florence" title="Gonfaloniere of Florence">Gonfaloniere of Florence</a>: "The bearer of this will be found to be Raphael, painter of Urbino, who, being greatly gifted in his profession has determined to spend some time in Florence to study. And because his father was most worthy and I was very attached to him, and the son is a sensible and well-mannered young man, on both accounts, I bear him great love..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>As earlier with Perugino and others, Raphael was able to assimilate the influence of Florentine art, whilst keeping his own developing style. Frescos in Perugia of about 1505 show a new monumental quality in the figures which may represent the influence of <a href="/wiki/Fra_Bartolomeo" title="Fra Bartolomeo">Fra Bartolomeo</a>, who Vasari says was a friend of Raphael. But the most striking influence in the work of these years is <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, who returned to the city from 1500 to 1506. Raphael's figures begin to take more dynamic and complex positions, and though as yet his painted subjects are still mostly tranquil, he made drawn studies of fighting nude men, one of the obsessions of the period in Florence. Another drawing is a portrait of a young woman that uses the three-quarter length pyramidal composition of the just-completed <i><a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a></i>, but still looks completely Raphaelesque. Another of Leonardo's compositional inventions, the pyramidal <i>Holy Family</i>, was repeated in a series of works that remain among his most famous easel paintings. There is a drawing by Raphael in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Collection" title="Royal Collection">Royal Collection</a> of <a href="/wiki/Leda_and_the_Swan#In_painting" title="Leda and the Swan">Leonardo's lost <i>Leda and the Swan</i></a>, from which he adapted the <a href="/wiki/Contrapposto" title="Contrapposto">contrapposto</a> pose of his own <i>Saint Catherine of Alexandria</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He also perfects his own version of Leonardo's <a href="/wiki/Sfumato" title="Sfumato">sfumato</a> modelling, to give subtlety to his painting of flesh, and develops the interplay of glances between his groups, which are much less enigmatic than those of Leonardo. But he keeps the soft clear light of Perugino in his paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leonardo was more than thirty years older than Raphael, but Michelangelo, who was in Rome for this period, was just eight years his senior. Michelangelo already disliked Leonardo, and in Rome came to dislike Raphael even more, attributing conspiracies against him to the younger man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Raphael would have been aware of his works in Florence, but in his most original work of these years, he strikes out in a different direction. His <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(Raphael)" title="Deposition (Raphael)">Deposition of Christ</a></i> draws on classical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarcophagi" title="Sarcophagi">sarcophagi</a> to spread the figures across the front of the picture space in a complex and not wholly successful arrangement. Wöllflin detects the influence of the Madonna in Michelangelo's <i><a href="/wiki/Doni_Tondo" title="Doni Tondo">Doni Tondo</a></i> in the kneeling figure on the right, but the rest of the composition is far removed from his style, or that of Leonardo. Though highly regarded at the time, and much later forcibly removed from Perugia by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borghese" title="Borghese">Borghese</a>, it stands rather alone in Raphael's work. His classicism would later take a less literal direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">Roman period</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Vatican_.22Stanze.22">The Vatican "Stanze"</span></h3>
<p>By the end of 1508, Raphael had moved to Rome, where he lived for the rest of his life. He was invited by the new <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a>, perhaps at the suggestion of his architect <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Donato Bramante</a>, then engaged on <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>, who came from just outside Urbino and was distantly related to Raphael.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Unlike Michelangelo, who had been kept lingering in Rome for several months after his first summons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Raphael was immediately commissioned by Julius to fresco what was intended to become the Pope's private library at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_Palace" title="Vatican Palace">Vatican Palace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This was a much larger and more important commission than any he had received before; he had only painted one altarpiece in Florence itself. Several other artists and their teams of assistants were already at work on different rooms, many painting over recently completed paintings commissioned by Julius's loathed predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Alexander VI</a>, whose contributions, and <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">arms</a>, Julius was determined to efface from the palace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Michelangelo, meanwhile, had been commissioned to paint the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_ceiling" title="Sistine Chapel ceiling">Sistine Chapel ceiling</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:4_Estancia_del_Sello_(El_Parnaso).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2753" data-file-width="3912" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/4_Estancia_del_Sello_%28El_Parnaso%29.jpg/220px-4_Estancia_del_Sello_%28El_Parnaso%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/4_Estancia_del_Sello_%28El_Parnaso%29.jpg/330px-4_Estancia_del_Sello_%28El_Parnaso%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/4_Estancia_del_Sello_%28El_Parnaso%29.jpg/440px-4_Estancia_del_Sello_%28El_Parnaso%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:4_Estancia_del_Sello_(El_Parnaso).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/The_Parnassus" title="The Parnassus">The Parnassus</a></i>, 1511, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanza_della_Segnatura" title="Stanza della Segnatura">Stanza della Segnatura</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This first of the famous "Stanze" or "<a href="/wiki/Raphael_Rooms" title="Raphael Rooms">Raphael Rooms</a>" to be painted, now known as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanza_della_Segnatura" title="Stanza della Segnatura">Stanza della Segnatura</a></i> after its use in Vasari's time, was to make a stunning impact on Roman art, and remains generally regarded as his greatest masterpiece, containing <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">The School of Athens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Parnassus" title="The Parnassus">The Parnassus</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Disputation_of_the_Holy_Sacrament" title="Disputation of the Holy Sacrament">Disputa</a></i>. Raphael was then given further rooms to paint, displacing other artists including Perugino and Signorelli. He completed a sequence of three rooms, each with paintings on each wall and often the ceilings too, increasingly leaving the work of painting from his detailed drawings to the large and skilled workshop team he had acquired, who added a fourth room, probably only including some elements designed by Raphael, after his early death in 1520. The death of Julius in 1513 did not interrupt the work at all, as he was succeeded by Raphael's last Pope, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>, with whom Raphael formed an even closer relationship, and who continued to commission him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Raphael's friend Cardinal Bibbiena was also one of Leo's old tutors, and a close friend and advisor.</p>
<p>Raphael was clearly influenced by Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel ceiling in the course of painting the room. Vasari said Bramante let him in secretly. The first section was completed in 1511 and the reaction of other artists to the daunting force of Michelangelo was the dominating question in Italian art for the following few decades. Raphael, who had already shown his gift for absorbing influences into his own personal style, rose to the challenge perhaps better than any other artist. One of the first and clearest instances was the portrait in <i>The School of Athens</i> of Michelangelo himself, as <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a>, which seems to draw clearly from the Sybils and <i>ignudi</i> of the Sistine ceiling. Other figures in that and later paintings in the room show the same influences, but as still cohesive with a development of Raphael's own style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Michelangelo accused Raphael of plagiarism and years after Raphael's death, complained in a letter that "everything he knew about art he got from me", although other quotations show more generous reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>These very large and complex compositions have been regarded ever since as among the supreme works of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_manner" title="Grand manner">grand manner</a> of the High <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Renaissance" title="The Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, and the "classic art" of the post-antique West. They give a highly <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealised</a> depiction of the forms represented, and the compositions, though very carefully conceived in <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawings</a>, achieve "sprezzatura", a term invented by his friend Castiglione, who defined it as "a certain nonchalance which conceals all artistry and makes whatever one says or does seem uncontrived and effortless ...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Michael_Levey" title="Michael Levey">Michael Levey</a>, "Raphael gives his [figures] a superhuman clarity and grace in a universe of Euclidian certainties".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The painting is nearly all of the highest quality in the first two rooms, but the later compositions in the Stanze, especially those involving dramatic action, are not entirely as successful either in conception or their execution by the workshop.</p>

<p>The Vatican projects took most of his time, although he painted several portraits, including those of his two main patrons, the popes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Pope_Julius_II_(Raphael)" title="Portrait of Pope Julius II (Raphael)">Julius II</a> and his successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Pope_Leo_X_(Raphael)" title="Portrait of Pope Leo X (Raphael)">Leo X</a>, the former considered one of his finest. Other portraits were of his own friends, like Castiglione, or the immediate Papal circle. Other rulers pressed for work, and King <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a> was sent two paintings as <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_gift" title="Diplomatic gift">diplomatic gifts</a> from the Pope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Agostino_Chigi" title="Agostino Chigi">Agostino Chigi</a>, the hugely rich banker and Papal Treasurer, he painted the <i><a href="/wiki/Galatea_(Raphael)" title="Galatea (Raphael)">Triumph of Galatea</a></i> and designed further decorative frescoes for his <a href="/wiki/Villa_Farnesina" title="Villa Farnesina">Villa Farnesina</a>, and painted two chapels in the churches of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_della_Pace" title="Santa Maria della Pace">Santa Maria della Pace</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_del_Popolo" title="Santa Maria del Popolo">Santa Maria del Popolo</a>. He also designed some of the decoration for the Villa Madama, the work in both villas being executed by his workshop.</p>
<p>One of his most important papal commissions was the <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Cartoons" title="Raphael Cartoons">Raphael Cartoons</a> (now in the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>), a series of 10 <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a>, of which seven survive, for tapestries with scenes of the lives of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" title="Paul of Tarsus">Saint Paul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a>. The cartoons were sent to <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> to be woven in the workshop of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pier_van_Aelst" title="Pier van Aelst">Pier van Aelst</a>. It is possible that Raphael saw the finished series before his death—they were probably completed in 1520.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> He also designed and painted the <i><a href="/wiki/Vatican_loggias" title="Vatican loggias">Loggie</a></i> at the Vatican, a long thin gallery then open to a courtyard on one side, decorated with Roman-style <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grottesche" title="Grottesche">grottesche</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> He produced a number of significant altarpieces, including <a href="/wiki/The_Ecstasy_of_St._Cecilia_(Raphael)" title="The Ecstasy of St. Cecilia (Raphael)"><i>The Ecstasy of St. Cecilia</i></a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Sistine_Madonna" title="Sistine Madonna">Sistine Madonna</a></i>. His last work, on which he was working up to his death, was a large <i><a href="/wiki/Transfiguration_(Raphael)" title="Transfiguration (Raphael)">Transfiguration</a></i>, which together with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ_Falling_on_the_Way_to_Calvary_(Raphael)" title="Christ Falling on the Way to Calvary (Raphael)">Il Spasimo</a></i> shows the direction his art was taking in his final years—more proto-<a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mannerist" title="Mannerist">Mannerist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting_materials">Painting materials</span></h2>
<p>Raphael painted several of his works on wood support (<a href="/wiki/Madonna_of_the_Pinks" title="Madonna of the Pinks">Madonna of the Pinks</a>) but he also used canvas (<a href="/wiki/Sistine_Madonna" title="Sistine Madonna">Sistine Madonna</a>) and he was known to employ drying oils such as <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed</a> or <a href="/wiki/Walnut_oil" title="Walnut oil">walnut oils</a>. His palette was rich and he used almost all of the then available pigments such as <a href="/wiki/Ultramarine" title="Ultramarine">ultramarine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead-tin-yellow" title="Lead-tin-yellow">lead-tin-yellow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">carmine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madder_lake" title="Madder lake">madder lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verdigris" title="Verdigris">verdigris</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ochres" title="Ochres">ochres</a>. In several of his paintings (<a href="/wiki/Ansidei_Madonna" title="Ansidei Madonna">Ansidei Madonna</a>) he even employed the rare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilwood" title="Brazilwood">brazilwood</a> lake, metallic powdered <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and even less known metallic powdered <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Workshop">Workshop</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6939" data-file-width="4921" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg/200px-Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg/300px-Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg/400px-Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro_-_WGA18653.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro" title="Guidobaldo da Montefeltro">Guidobaldo da Montefeltro</a>, Duke of Urbino from 1482-1508, c.1507</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Vasari says that Raphael eventually had a workshop of fifty pupils and assistants, many of whom later became significant artists in their own right. This was arguably the largest workshop team assembled under any single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_master" title="Old master">old master</a> painter, and much higher than the norm. They included established masters from other parts of Italy, probably working with their own teams as sub-contractors, as well as pupils and journeymen. We have very little evidence of the internal working arrangements of the workshop, apart from the works of art themselves, which are often very difficult to assign to a particular hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most important figures were <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Romano" title="Giulio Romano">Giulio Romano</a>, a young pupil from Rome (only about twenty-one at Raphael's death), and <a href="/wiki/Gianfrancesco_Penni" title="Gianfrancesco Penni">Gianfrancesco Penni</a>, already a Florentine master. They were left many of Raphael's drawings and other possessions, and to some extent continued the workshop after Raphael's death. Penni did not achieve a personal reputation equal to Giulio's, as after Raphael's death he became Giulio's less-than-equal collaborator in turn for much of his subsequent career. <a href="/wiki/Perino_del_Vaga" title="Perino del Vaga">Perino del Vaga</a>, already a master, and <a href="/wiki/Polidoro_da_Caravaggio" title="Polidoro da Caravaggio">Polidoro da Caravaggio</a>, who was supposedly promoted from a labourer carrying building materials on the site, also became notable painters in their own right. Polidoro's partner, <a href="/wiki/Maturino_da_Firenze" title="Maturino da Firenze">Maturino da Firenze</a>, has, like Penni, been overshadowed in subsequent reputation by his partner. <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Udine" title="Giovanni da Udine">Giovanni da Udine</a> had a more independent status, and was responsible for the decorative <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> work and grotesques surrounding the main frescoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Most of the artists were later scattered, and some killed, by the violent <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">Sack of Rome in 1527</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> This did however contribute to the diffusion of versions of Raphael's style around Italy and beyond.</p>
<p>Vasari emphasises that Raphael ran a very harmonious and efficient workshop, and had extraordinary skill in smoothing over troubles and arguments with both patrons and his assistants—a contrast with the stormy pattern of Michelangelo's relationships with both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> However though both Penni and Giulio were sufficiently skilled that distinguishing between their hands and that of Raphael himself is still sometimes difficult,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> there is no doubt that many of Raphael's later wall-paintings, and probably some of his easel paintings, are more notable for their design than their execution. Many of his portraits, if in good condition, show his brilliance in the detailed handling of paint right up to the end of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other pupils or assistants include <a href="/wiki/Raffaellino_del_Colle" title="Raffaellino del Colle">Raffaellino del Colle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Sabbatini" title="Andrea Sabbatini">Andrea Sabbatini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bartolommeo_Ramenghi" title="Bartolommeo Ramenghi">Bartolommeo Ramenghi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pellegrino_Aretusi" title="Pellegrino Aretusi">Pellegrino Aretusi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Tamagni" title="Vincenzo Tamagni">Vincenzo Tamagni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battista_Dossi" title="Battista Dossi">Battista Dossi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_Vincidor" title="Tommaso Vincidor">Tommaso Vincidor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timoteo_Viti" title="Timoteo Viti">Timoteo Viti</a> (the Urbino painter), and the sculptor and architect <a href="/wiki/Lorenzetto" title="Lorenzetto">Lorenzetto</a> (Giulio's brother-in-law).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The printmakers and architects in Raphael's circle are discussed below. It has been claimed the Flemish <a href="/wiki/Bernard_van_Orley" title="Bernard van Orley">Bernard van Orley</a> worked for Raphael for a time, and <a href="/wiki/Luca_Penni" title="Luca Penni">Luca Penni</a>, brother of Gianfrancesco and later a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_School_of_Fontainebleau" title="First School of Fontainebleau">First School of Fontainebleau</a>, may have been a member of the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portraits">Portraits</span></h3>
<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional" style="max-width: 972px;_width: 972px;">
<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 235px">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="232" data-file-width="350" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg/220px-PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg/330px-PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PalazzoBranconioDellAquila.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Branconio_dell%27Aquila" title="Palazzo Branconio dell'Aquila">Palazzo Branconio dell'Aquila</a>, now destroyed</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After Bramante's death in 1514, Raphael was named architect of the new <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St Peter's</a>. Most of his work there was altered or demolished after his death and the acceptance of Michelangelo's design, but a few drawings have survived. It appears his designs would have made the church a good deal gloomier than the final design, with massive piers all the way down the nave, "like an alley" according to a critical posthumous analysis by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_da_Sangallo_the_Younger" title="Antonio da Sangallo the Younger">Antonio da Sangallo the Younger</a>. It would perhaps have resembled the temple in the background of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Expulsion_of_Heliodorus_from_the_Temple" title="The Expulsion of Heliodorus from the Temple">The Expulsion of Heliodorus from the Temple</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>He designed several other buildings, and for a short time was the most important architect in Rome, working for a small circle around the Papacy. Julius had made changes to the street plan of Rome, creating several new thoroughfares, and he wanted them filled with splendid palaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>An important building, the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Branconio_dell%27Aquila" title="Palazzo Branconio dell'Aquila">Palazzo Branconio dell'Aquila</a> for Leo's Papal <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_(office)" title="Chamberlain (office)">Chamberlain</a> <a href="/wiki/Giovanbattista_Branconio_dell%27Aquila" title="Giovanbattista Branconio dell'Aquila">Giovanni Battista Branconio</a>, was completely destroyed to make way for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernini" title="Bernini">Bernini</a>'s <i>piazza</i> for St. Peter's, but drawings of the façade and courtyard remain. The façade was an unusually richly decorated one for the period, including both painted panels on the top story (of three), and much sculpture on the middle one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main designs for the Villa Farnesina were not by Raphael, but he did design, and paint, the <a href="/wiki/Chigi_Chapel" title="Chigi Chapel">Chigi Chapel</a> for the same patron, <a href="/wiki/Agostino_Chigi" title="Agostino Chigi">Agostino Chigi</a>, the Papal Treasurer. Another building, for Pope Leo's doctor, the <i>Palazzo di Jacobo da Brescia</i>, was moved in the 1930s but survives; this was designed to complement a palace on the same street by Bramante, where Raphael himself lived for a time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ChigiLorenzetto.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1361" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/ChigiLorenzetto.jpg/220px-ChigiLorenzetto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/ChigiLorenzetto.jpg/330px-ChigiLorenzetto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/ChigiLorenzetto.jpg/440px-ChigiLorenzetto.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ChigiLorenzetto.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of the <a href="/wiki/Chigi_Chapel" title="Chigi Chapel">Chigi Chapel</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Villa_Madama" title="Villa Madama">Villa Madama</a>, a lavish hillside retreat for Cardinal Giulio de' Medici, later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Pope Clement VII</a>, was never finished, and his full plans have to be reconstructed speculatively. He produced a design from which the final construction plans were completed by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_da_Sangallo_the_Younger" title="Antonio da Sangallo the Younger">Antonio da Sangallo the Younger</a>. Even incomplete, it was the most sophisticated villa design yet seen in Italy, and greatly influenced the later development of the genre; it appears to be the only modern building in Rome of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladio" title="Palladio">Palladio</a> made a measured drawing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only some floor-plans remain for a large palace planned for himself on the new <a href="/wiki/Via_Giulia" title="Via Giulia">via Giulia</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regola_(rione_of_Rome)" title="Regola (rione of Rome)">rione of Regola</a>, for which he was accumulating the land in his last years. It was on an irregular island block near the river Tiber. It seems all façades were to have a <a href="/wiki/Giant_order" title="Giant order">giant order</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pilaster" title="Pilaster">pilasters</a> rising at least two storeys to the full height of the <a href="/wiki/Piano_nobile" title="Piano nobile">piano nobile</a>, "a gandiloquent feature unprecedented in private palace design".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1515 he was given powers as "Prefect" over all antiquities unearthed entrusted within the city, or a mile outside. Raphael wrote a letter to Pope Leo suggesting ways of halting the destruction of ancient monuments, and proposed a visual survey of the city to record all antiquities in an organised fashion. The Pope's concerns were not exactly the same; he intended to continue to re-use ancient masonry in the building of St Peter's, but wanted to ensure that all ancient inscriptions were recorded, and sculpture preserved, before allowing the stones to be reused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawings">Drawings</span></h2>
<p>Raphael was one of the finest draftsmen in the history of Western art, and used drawings extensively to plan his compositions. According to a near-contemporary, when beginning to plan a composition, he would lay out a large number of stock drawings of his on the floor, and begin to draw "rapidly", borrowing figures from here and there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Over forty sketches survive for the <i>Disputa</i> in the Stanze, and there may well have been many more originally; over four hundred sheets survive altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> He used different drawings to refine his poses and compositions, apparently to a greater extent than most other painters, to judge by the number of variants that survive: "... This is how Raphael himself, who was so rich in inventiveness, used to work, always coming up with four or six ways to show a narrative, each one different from the rest, and all of them full of grace and well done." wrote another writer after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/John_Shearman" title="John Shearman">John Shearman</a>, Raphael's art marks "a shift of resources away from production to research and development".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>When a final composition was achieved, scaled-up full-size cartoons were often made, which were then pricked with a pin and "pounced" with a bag of soot to leave dotted lines on the surface as a guide. He also made unusually extensive use, on both paper and plaster, of a "blind stylus", scratching lines which leave only an indentation, but no mark. These can be seen on the wall in <i>The School of Athens</i>, and in the originals of many drawings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The "Raphael Cartoons", as tapestry designs, were fully coloured in a glue <a href="/wiki/Distemper_(paint)" title="Distemper (paint)">distemper</a> medium, as they were sent to Brussels to be followed by the weavers.</p>
<p>In later works painted by the workshop, the drawings are often painfully more attractive than the paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Most Raphael drawings are rather precise—even initial sketches with naked outline figures are carefully drawn, and later working drawings often have a high degree of finish, with shading and sometimes highlights in white. They lack the freedom and energy of some of Leonardo's and Michelangelo's sketches, but are nearly always aesthetically very satisfying. He was one of the last artists to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalpoint" title="Metalpoint">metalpoint</a> (literally a sharp pointed piece of silver or another metal) extensively, although he also made superb use of the freer medium of red or black chalk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> In his final years he was one of the first artists to use female models for preparatory drawings—male pupils ("garzoni") were normally used for studies of both sexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printmaking">Printmaking</span></h2>
<p>Raphael made no <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">prints</a> himself, but entered into a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Marcantonio_Raimondi" title="Marcantonio Raimondi">Marcantonio Raimondi</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engravings</a> to Raphael's designs, which created many of the most famous Italian prints of the century, and was important in the <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print#The_rise_of_the_reproductive_print" title="Old master print">rise of the reproductive print</a>. His interest was unusual in such a major artist; from his contemporaries it was only shared by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, who had worked much less successfully with Raimondi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> A total of about fifty prints were made; some were copies of Raphael's paintings, but other designs were apparently created by Raphael purely to be turned into prints. Raphael made preparatory drawings, many of which survive, for Raimondi to translate into engraving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most famous original prints to result from the collaboration were <i>Lucretia</i>, the <i>Judgement of Paris</i> and <i>The Massacre of the Innocents</i> (of which two virtually identical versions were engraved). Among prints of the paintings <i><a href="/wiki/The_Parnassus" title="The Parnassus">The Parnassus</a></i> (with considerable differences)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and <i>Galatea</i> were also especially well-known. Outside Italy, reproductive prints by Raimondi and others were the main way that Raphael's art was experienced until the twentieth century. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baviero_Carocci&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baviero Carocci (page does not exist)">Baviero Carocci</a>, called "Il Baviera" by Vasari, an assistant who Raphael evidently trusted with his money,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> ended up in control of most of the copper plates after Raphael's death, and had a successful career in the new occupation of a publisher of prints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_life_and_death">Private life and death</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fornarina.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1424" height="309" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Fornarina.jpg/220px-Fornarina.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Fornarina.jpg/330px-Fornarina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Fornarina.jpg/440px-Fornarina.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fornarina.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/La_Fornarina" title="La Fornarina">La Fornarina</a></i>, Raphael's mistress</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From 1517 until his death, Raphael lived in the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Caprini" title="Palazzo Caprini">Palazzo Caprini</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Borgo_(rione_of_Rome)" title="Borgo (rione of Rome)">Borgo</a>, in rather grand style in a palace designed by Bramante. He never married, but in 1514 became engaged to Maria Bibbiena, Cardinal Medici Bibbiena's niece; he seems to have been talked into this by his friend the Cardinal, and his lack of enthusiasm seems to be shown by the marriage not having taken place before she died in 1520.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-75">[75]</a></sup> He is said to have had many affairs, but a permanent fixture in his life in Rome was "La Fornarina", <a href="/wiki/Margarita_Luti" title="Margarita Luti">Margherita Luti</a>, the daughter of a baker (<i>fornaro</i>) named Francesco Luti from Siena who lived at Via del Governo Vecchio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> He was made a "<a href="/wiki/Valet_de_chambre" title="Valet de chambre">Groom of the Chamber</a>" of the Pope, which gave him status at court and an additional income, and also a knight of the Papal <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Golden_Spur" title="Order of the Golden Spur">Order of the Golden Spur</a>. Vasari claims he had toyed with the ambition of becoming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinal</a>, perhaps after some encouragement from Leo, which also may account for his delaying his marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Vasari, Raphael's premature death on <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a> (April 6, 1520), which was possibly his 37th birthday, was caused by a night of excessive sex with Luti, after which he fell into a fever and, not telling his doctors that this was its cause, was given the wrong cure, which killed him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Vasari also says that Raphael had also been born on a Good Friday, which in 1483 fell on March 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whatever the cause, in his acute illness, which lasted fifteen days, Raphael was composed enough to confess his sins, receive the <a href="/wiki/Last_rites" title="Last rites">last rites</a>, and to put his affairs in order. He dictated his will, in which he left sufficient funds for his mistress's care, entrusted to his loyal servant Baviera, and left most of his studio contents to Giulio Romano and Penni. At his request, Raphael was buried in the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>His funeral was extremely grand, attended by large crowds. The inscription in his marble sarcophagus, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elegiac_poetry" title="Elegiac poetry">elegiac</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distich" title="Distich">distich</a> written by <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bembo" title="Pietro Bembo">Pietro Bembo</a>, reads: "Ille hic est Raffael, timuit quo sospite vinci, rerum magna parens et moriente mori", meaning: "Here lies that famous Raphael by whom Nature feared to be conquered while he lived, and when he was dying, feared herself to die."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_(Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister,_Dresden,_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo,_265_x_196_cm).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4151" data-file-width="3028" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_%28Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister%2C_Dresden%2C_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_265_x_196_cm%29.jpg/220px-RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_%28Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister%2C_Dresden%2C_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_265_x_196_cm%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_%28Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister%2C_Dresden%2C_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_265_x_196_cm%29.jpg/330px-RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_%28Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister%2C_Dresden%2C_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_265_x_196_cm%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_%28Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister%2C_Dresden%2C_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_265_x_196_cm%29.jpg/440px-RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_%28Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister%2C_Dresden%2C_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo%2C_265_x_196_cm%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RAFAEL_-_Madonna_Sixtina_(Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alter_Meister,_Dresden,_1513-14._%C3%93leo_sobre_lienzo,_265_x_196_cm).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Sistine_Madonna" title="Sistine Madonna">Sistine Madonna</a></i> (1512)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Raphael was highly admired by his contemporaries, although his influence on artistic style in his own century was less than that of Michelangelo. <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a>, beginning at the time of his death, and later the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a>, took art "in a direction totally opposed" to Raphael's qualities;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> "with Raphael's death, classic art – the High Renaissance – subsided", as <a href="/wiki/Walter_Friedl%C3%A4nder" title="Walter Friedländer">Walter Friedländer</a> put it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> He was soon seen as the ideal model by those disliking the excesses of Mannerism:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>the opinion ...was generally held in the middle of the sixteenth century that Raphael was the ideal balanced painter, universal in his talent, satisfying all the absolute standards, and obeying all the rules which were supposed to govern the arts, whereas Michelangelo was the eccentric genius, more brilliant than any other artists in his particular field, the drawing of the male nude, but unbalanced and lacking in certain qualities, such as grace and restraint, essential to the great artist. Those, like <a href="/wiki/Lodovico_Dolce" title="Lodovico Dolce">Dolce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Aretino" title="Pietro Aretino">Aretino</a>, who held this view were usually the survivors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance_Humanism" title="Renaissance Humanism">Renaissance Humanism</a>, unable to follow Michelangelo as he moved on into Mannerism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Vasari himself, despite his hero remaining Michelangelo, came to see his influence as harmful in some ways, and added passages to the second edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Most_Excellent_Painters,_Sculptors,_and_Architects" title="Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects">Lives</a></i> expressing similar views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="443" height="376" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg/260px-Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg/390px-Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ef/Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pantheon-raphaels-tomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raphael%27s_grave,_Pantheon_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Raphael's sarcophagus</div>

<p>Raphael's compositions were always admired and studied, and became the cornerstone of the <a href="/wiki/Academic_art" title="Academic art">training of the Academies of art</a>. His period of greatest influence was from the late 17th to late 19th centuries, when his perfect decorum and balance were greatly admired. He was seen as the best model for the <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history painting</a>, regarded as the highest in the <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_of_genres" title="Hierarchy of genres">hierarchy of genres</a>. Sir <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a> in his <i>Discourses</i> praised his "simple, grave, and majestic dignity" and said he "stands in general foremost of the first [i.e., best] painters", especially for his frescoes (in which he included the "Raphael Cartoons"), whereas "Michael Angelo claims the next attention. He did not possess so many excellences as Raffaelle, but those he had were of the highest kind..." Echoing the sixteenth-century views above, Reynolds goes on to say of Raphael:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The excellency of this extraordinary man lay in the propriety, beauty, and majesty of his characters, his judicious contrivance of his composition, correctness of drawing, purity of taste, and the skilful accommodation of other men’s conceptions to his own purpose. Nobody excelled him in that judgment, with which he united to his own observations on nature the energy of Michael Angelo, and the beauty and simplicity of the antique. To the question, therefore, which ought to hold the first rank, Raffaelle or Michael Angelo, it must be answered, that if it is to be given to him who possessed a greater combination of the higher qualities of the art than any other man, there is no doubt but Raffaelle is the first. But if, according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longinus_(literature)" title="Longinus (literature)">Longinus</a>, the sublime, being the highest excellence that human composition can attain to, abundantly compensates the absence of every other beauty, and atones for all other deficiencies, then Michael Angelo demands the preference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Reynolds was less enthusiastic about Raphael's panel paintings, but the slight sentimentality of these made them enormously popular in the 19th century: "We have been familiar with them from childhood onwards, through a far greater mass of reproductions than any other artist in the world has ever had..." wrote <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_W%C3%B6lfflin" title="Heinrich Wölfflin">Wölfflin</a>, who was born in 1862, of Raphael's Madonnas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Germany, Raphael had an immense influence on religious art of the <a href="/wiki/Nazarene_movement" title="Nazarene movement">Nazarene movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf_school_of_painting" title="Düsseldorf school of painting">Düsseldorf school of painting</a> in the 19th century. In contrast, in England the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood" title="Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood">Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood</a> explicitly reacted against his influence (and that of his admirers such as <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a>), seeking to return to styles that pre-dated what they saw as his baneful influence. According to a critic whose ideas greatly influenced them, <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The doom of the arts of Europe went forth from that chamber [the Stanza della Segnatura], and it was brought about in great part by the very excellencies of the man who had thus marked the commencement of decline. The perfection of execution and the beauty of feature which were attained in his works, and in those of his great contemporaries, rendered finish of execution and beauty of form the chief objects of all artists; and thenceforward execution was looked for rather than thought, and beauty rather than veracity.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>And as I told you, these are the two secondary causes of the decline of art; the first being the loss of moral purpose. Pray note them clearly. In mediæval art, thought is the first thing, execution the second; in modern art execution is the first thing, and thought the second. And again, in mediæval art, truth is first, beauty second; in modern art, beauty is first, truth second. The mediæval principles led up to Raphael, and the modern principles lead down from him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He was still seen by 20th-century critics like <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Berenson" title="Bernard Berenson">Bernard Berenson</a> as the "most famous and most loved" master of the High Renaissance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> but it would seem he has since been overtaken by Michelangelo and Leonardo in this respect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
