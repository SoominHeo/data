<p><b>Rafic Baha El Deen Al Hariri</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">رفيق بهاء الدين الحريري</span>‎‎; <small>Arabic pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Arabic" title="Help:IPA for Arabic">[rafiːq ħariːriː]</a></span> 1 November 1944 – 14 February 2005) was a Lebanese business tycoon and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Lebanon" title="Prime Minister of Lebanon">Prime Minister of Lebanon</a> from 1992 to 1998 and again from 2000 until his resignation on 20 October 2004<span style="display:none"> (<span class="dtend">2004-10-20</span>)</span>. He headed five cabinets during his tenure. Hariri dominated the country's post-war political and business life and is widely credited with reconstructing the capital <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_Civil_War" title="Lebanese Civil War">15-year civil war</a>.</p>
<p>Hariri was assassinated on 14 February 2005 when explosives equivalent to around 1800 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a> were detonated as his <a href="/wiki/Motorcade" title="Motorcade">motorcade</a> drove past the St. George Hotel in Beirut. The investigation of his assassination by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Tribunal_for_Lebanon" title="Special Tribunal for Lebanon">Special Tribunal for Lebanon</a> is still ongoing and currently led by the independent investigator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bellemare" title="Daniel Bellemare">Daniel Bellemare</a>. In its first two reports in 2014, UNIIIC indicated that the <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrian</a> government may be linked to the assassination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebanon_on_a_tinderbox_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lebanon_on_a_tinderbox-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Lawyers tasked with prosecuting those responsible for the 2005 bombing said they had received evidence linking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bashar_Assad" title="Bashar Assad">Bashar Assad</a>'s phone to the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Hariri's killing was a catalyst for dramatic political change in Lebanon. The massive protests of the <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Revolution" title="Cedar Revolution">Cedar Revolution</a> helped achieve the withdrawal of Syrian troops and security forces from Lebanon, and a change in governments.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hariri was born on 1 November 1944 to a modest <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam_in_Lebanon" title="Sunni Islam in Lebanon">Sunni Muslim</a> family in the Lebanese port city of <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a>. He had two siblings (brother Shafic and sister <a href="/wiki/Bahia_Hariri" title="Bahia Hariri">Bahia</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foundation-4">[4]</a></sup> He attended elementary and secondary school in Sidon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foundation-4">[4]</a></sup> and graduated in business administration from <a href="/wiki/Beirut_Arab_University" title="Beirut Arab University">Beirut Arab University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafik_Hariri_NYT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rafik_Hariri_NYT-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965, Hariri went to <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> to work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafik_Hariri_NYT_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rafik_Hariri_NYT-5">[5]</a></sup> There, he taught for a short period of time before shifting to the construction industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafik_Hariri.2C_Ex-Premier_of_Lebanon.2C_Dies_at_60_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rafik_Hariri.2C_Ex-Premier_of_Lebanon.2C_Dies_at_60-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1978, he gained <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian_nationality_law" title="Saudi Arabian nationality law">Saudi Arabian citizenship</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafik_Hariri_NYT_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rafik_Hariri_NYT-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gary2001july_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gary2001july-7">[7]</a></sup> in addition to his <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_nationality_law" title="Lebanese nationality law">Lebanese citizenship</a>.</p>
<p>In 1969, Hariri established Ciconest, a small subcontracting firm, which soon went out of business. He then went in business with the French construction firm Oger for the construction of a hotel in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%E2%80%99if" title="Ta’if">Ta’if</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, the timely construction of which earned praise from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Khaled" title="King Khaled">King Khaled</a>. Hariri took over Oger, forming <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Oger" title="Saudi Oger">Saudi Oger</a>, which became the main construction firm used by the Saudi Royal family for all their important developments. As a result, a few years after his first contract with King Khaled, Hariri had become a multi-billionaire.</p>

<p>Having accumulated his wealth, Hariri started a number of philanthropic projects, including the building of educational facilities in Lebanon. His first initiative in Lebanon was the Islamic Association for Culture and Education, which he founded in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mermay_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mermay-8">[8]</a></sup> The association was later renamed the Hariri Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mermay_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mermay-8">[8]</a></sup> Hariri became progressively more embroiled in politics. His appeals to the United Nations and services as an emissary to the Saudi Royal family won him international recognition on the political stage for his humanitarian efforts.</p>
<p>In 1982, Hariri donated $12 million to Lebanese victims of the <a href="/wiki/1978_South_Lebanon_conflict" title="1978 South Lebanon conflict">1978 South Lebanon conflict</a> and helped clean up Beirut's streets with his company's money. After the conflict, he acted as an envoy of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">Saudi royal family</a> to Lebanon. He laid the groundwork that led to the 1989 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taif_Accord" title="Taif Accord">Taif Accord</a>, which Saudi Arabia organised to bring the warring factions together. Taif put an end to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebanese_civil_war" title="Lebanese civil war">civil war</a>, building goodwill for Hariri politically.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hariri returned to Lebanon in the early 1980s as a wealthy man and began to build a name for himself by making large donations and contributions to various groups in Lebanon. However, he continued to serve as a political advisor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Bandar_bin_Sultan" title="Prince Bandar bin Sultan">Prince Bandar bin Sultan</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saad9july_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-saad9july-9">[9]</a></sup> He was implanted as the Saudis' strong man following the collapse of the PLO and the paucity of any viable Sunni leadership in the country as well as a response to the rising power of the Shiite militia Amal. As a former Saudi diplomatic representative, he played a significant role in constructing <a href="/wiki/Taif_Agreement" title="Taif Agreement">the 1990 Taif Agreement</a> that ended Lebanon's sixteen-year civil war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neal_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-neal-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1992, Hariri became the first post-civil war prime minister of Lebanon under president <a href="/wiki/Elias_Hrawi" title="Elias Hrawi">Elias Hrawi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mer_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-mer-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-are2007_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-are2007-12">[12]</a></sup> Hariri put the country back on the financial map through the issuing of <a href="/wiki/Eurobond" title="Eurobond">Eurobonds</a> and won plaudits from the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> for his plan to borrow reconstruction money as the country's debt grew to become the largest per capita in the world. His first premiership lasted until 1998, and Hariri was replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salim_Hoss" title="Salim Hoss">Salim Hoss</a> as prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gary2001july_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gary2001july-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mer_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-mer-11">[11]</a></sup> In fact, as a result of the power struggle between Hariri and newly elected president <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Lahoud" title="Émile Lahoud">Émile Lahoud</a>, he left office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fakih23feb_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-fakih23feb-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2000, Hariri was again appointed prime minister, replacing Salim Hoss, and formed the cabinet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gary2001july_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-gary2001july-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aps28oct_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-aps28oct-14">[14]</a></sup> In September 2004, Hariri defended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Security_Council_Resolution_1559" title="UN Security Council Resolution 1559">UN Security Council Resolution 1559</a>, which called for "all remaining foreign forces to withdraw from Lebanon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mak2010_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-mak2010-15">[15]</a></sup> On 20 October 2004, his second term ended when he resigned from office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-har05sum_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-har05sum-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Omar_Karami" title="Omar Karami">Omar Karami</a> succeeded him as prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-safa06jan_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-safa06jan-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lwire27oct_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-lwire27oct-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1992-1998_economic_political_policies">1992-1998 economic political policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1992-1998 economic political policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris,_2011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2912" data-file-width="3868" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris%2C_2011.jpg/220px-Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris%2C_2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris%2C_2011.jpg/330px-Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris%2C_2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris%2C_2011.jpg/440px-Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris%2C_2011.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rafiq_Hariri%27s_former_residence_in_Paris,_2011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rafic Hariri's former residence in Paris</div>

<p>Hariri implemented an aggressive new economic policy. Perhaps Hariri's most important creation in the beginning of his career was "Horizon 2000" the government's name for its new rejuvenation plan. A large component of "Horizon 2000" was Solidere, the privately owned<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> construction company that was established to reconstruct post-war Lebanon. Solidere was owned by the government and private investors. <a href="/wiki/Solidere" title="Solidere">Solidere</a> was largely focused on redeveloping Beirut's downtown and turning it into a new urban center as quickly as possible as one aspect of the various infrastructure redevelopment plans that would be implemented by "Horizon 2000". Another aspect of the decade-long plan was the privatization of major industries. Numerous contracts were awarded in important industries such as energy, telecommunications, electricity, airports and roads.</p>
<p>The last and perhaps most significant aspect of "Horizon 2000" was economic stimulus via foreign direct investment. Specifically, Hariri supported foreign firms and individuals taking an interest in Lebanon's developmental potential. Hariri simplified tax codes and provided tax breaks to foreign investors. Due to his previous successes in the private sector and the numerous resulting international connections, Hariri was able to garner a significant amount of low-interest loans from foreign investors. Hariri also pursued aggressive macroeconomic policy such as maintaining strict regulations on bank reserves and inter-bank interest rates to curb inflation and raise the value of the Lebanese pound relative to the dollar.</p>
<p>Hariri's economic policies were a remarkable success during his first year in office. From 1992 to 1993 there was a 6 percent increase in real national income, the capital base of commercial banks effectively doubled, the budgetary earnings hovered at around a billion dollars, and commercial banks’ consolidated balance sheets increased about 25%. By 1998, however, real GDP growth was around 1%, a year later it would be -1%, national debt had skyrocketed 540% from two to eighteen billion dollars, Lebanon's economy was in a miserable state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hariri_and_Lebanon.27s_political_environment">Hariri and Lebanon's political environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hariri and Lebanon's political environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="400" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg/220px-Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg/330px-Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rafik_Hariri_George_W._Bush.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and Hariri meeting in the White House</div>

<p>Amid the political crisis brought on by the extension of President <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Lahoud" title="Émile Lahoud">Émile Lahoud</a>'s term, Hariri resigned as Prime Minister, saying: "I have... submitted the resignation of the government, and I have declared that I will not be a candidate to head the (next) government."</p>
<p>During a BBC interview in 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Harīrī was asked by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Sebastian" title="Tim Sebastian">Tim Sebastian</a> why he refused to hand over members of <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a> that were accused by America of being terrorists. He responded that Hezbollah were the ones protecting Lebanon against the Israeli occupation and called for implementation of passed United Nations resolutions against Israel. He was further accused of making the American coalition in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_on_Terrorism" title="War on Terrorism">war on terrorism</a> worthless and asked if he was ready for the consequences of his refusal, reminding him that <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> had said: "Either you are with us, or you are with the terrorists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He replied that he had hoped there would be no consequences, but would deal with them if they arrive. Hariri further said that he opposed the killing of all humans – Israeli, Palestinian, Syrian or Lebanese – and believed in dialogue as a solution. He further went on to say that Syria would have to stay in Lebanon for protection of Lebanon until they are no longer needed and Lebanon asks them to leave.</p>
<p>Lebanese <a href="/wiki/Druze" title="Druze">Druze</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Walid_Jumblatt" title="Walid Jumblatt">Walid Jumblatt</a>, a recent recruit of the anti-Syrian opposition, emboldened by popular anger and civic action now being called Lebanon's <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Revolution" title="Cedar Revolution">Cedar Revolution</a>, alleged in the wake of the assassination that on 26 August 2004 Syrian President <a href="/wiki/Bashar_al-Assad" title="Bashar al-Assad">Bashar al-Assad</a> threatened Hariri, saying "[President of Lebanon] Lahoud is me. ... If you and Chirac want me out of Lebanon, I will break Lebanon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He was quoted as saying "When I heard him telling us those words, I knew that it was his condemnation of death." This meeting between Hariri and Assad, which had been on 26 August 2004, lasted for just fifteen minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-safa06jan_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-safa06jan-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raad27aug_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-raad27aug-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seeberg07_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-seeberg07-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 June 2005, Beirut International Airport was renamed <a href="/wiki/Beirut%E2%80%93Rafic_Hariri_International_Airport" title="Beirut–Rafic Hariri International Airport">Rafic Hariri International Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Foundation-4">[4]</a></sup> Additionally, Beirut General University hospital was renamed Rafiq Hariri Hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Foundation-4">[4]</a></sup> Rafic Hariri was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Saad_Hariri" title="Saad Hariri">Saad Hariri</a> as leader of the Future Party.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hariri married twice. He had seven children. In 1965, he married an Iraqi woman, Nida Bustani, who is the mother of his three sons; <a href="/wiki/Bahaa_Hariri" title="Bahaa Hariri">Bahaa</a> (born 1967), who is a businessman, <a href="/wiki/Saad_Hariri" title="Saad Hariri">Saad</a>, who succeeded his father as leader of the future movement, and Houssam—who died in a traffic accident in the US in the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medpol_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-medpol-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> They divorced. He married his second spouse, <a href="/wiki/Nazik_Hariri" title="Nazik Hariri">Nazik Audi</a>, in 1976 and she is the mother of Hariri's four children, including <a href="/wiki/Ayman_Hariri" title="Ayman Hariri">Ayman Hariri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fahd_Hariri" title="Fahd Hariri">Fahd Hariri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hind_Hariri" title="Hind Hariri">Hind Hariri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medpol_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-medpol-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1982 until his death Hariri owned <a href="/wiki/2%E2%80%938a_Rutland_Gate" title="2–8a Rutland Gate">2–8a Rutland Gate</a> a large house in London's Knightsbridge district. The house was gifted to the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prince_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Crown Prince of Saudi Arabia">Crown Prince of Saudi Arabia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultan_bin_Abdulaziz" title="Sultan bin Abdulaziz">Sultan bin Abdulaziz</a>, after Hariri's assassination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTSep12_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTSep12-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Rafic_Hariri" title="Assassination of Rafic Hariri">Assassination of Rafic Hariri</a></div>
<p>On 14 February 2005, Hariri was killed when explosives equivalent to around 1,800 kilograms (4,000 lb) of <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a> concealed inside a parked <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi" title="Mitsubishi">Mitsubishi</a> van were detonated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erikmitzi_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-erikmitzi-29">[29]</a></sup> as his <a href="/wiki/Motorcade" title="Motorcade">motorcade</a> drove near the St. George Hotel in Beirut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-edge-30">[30]</a></sup> Another 22 people lost their lives in the explosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anniversary_of_Lebanese_Prime_Minister_Rafik_Hariri.27s_Assassination_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anniversary_of_Lebanese_Prime_Minister_Rafik_Hariri.27s_Assassination-31">[31]</a></sup> Among the dead were several of Hariri's bodyguards and his friend and former Minister of the Economy <a href="/wiki/Bassel_Fleihan" title="Bassel Fleihan">Bassel Fleihan</a>. Hariri was buried along with his bodyguards, who died in the bombing, in a location near <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Al-Amin_Mosque" title="Mohammad Al-Amin Mosque">Mohammad Al-Amin Mosque</a>.</p>
<p>A 2006 report by <a href="/wiki/Serge_Brammertz" title="Serge Brammertz">Serge Brammertz</a> indicated that <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> evidence collected from the crime scene suggests that the assassination might be the act of a young male <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicide_bomber" title="Suicide bomber">suicide bomber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">Canadian Broadcasting Corporation</a> news investigation claimed that the special UN investigation team had found evidence for the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a> in the assassination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_Investigation:_Who_killed_Lebanon.27s_Rafik_Hariri.3F_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_Investigation:_Who_killed_Lebanon.27s_Rafik_Hariri.3F-33">[33]</a></sup> A UN-backed tribunal issued four arrest warrants to members of <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hariri_murder:_UN_tribunal_issues_arrest_warrants_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hariri_murder:_UN_tribunal_issues_arrest_warrants-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hezbollah blamed the assassination on Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hezbollah_leader_says_Israel_was_behind_Hariri_killing_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hezbollah_leader_says_Israel_was_behind_Hariri_killing-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Revolution" title="Cedar Revolution">Cedar Revolution</a></div>
<p>Hariri was well regarded among international leaders, for example, he was a close friend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_President" title="French President">French President</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">Jacques Chirac</a>. Chirac was one of the first foreign dignitaries to offer condolences to Hariri's widow in person at her home in Beirut. <a href="/wiki/Special_Tribunal_for_Lebanon" title="Special Tribunal for Lebanon">The Special Tribunal for Lebanon</a> was also created at his instigation. Syria was initially accused of the assassination, which led to the withdrawal of Syrian troops from Lebanon following widespread protests.</p>

<p>Major General <a href="/wiki/Jamil_Al_Sayyed" title="Jamil Al Sayyed">Jamil Al Sayyed</a>, then head of Lebanese General Security, Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Hamdan" title="Mustafa Hamdan">Mustafa Hamdan</a>, Major General <a href="/wiki/Ali_Al_Hajj" title="Ali Al Hajj">Ali Hajj</a> and Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Azar_(security_chief)" title="Raymond Azar (security chief)">Raymond Azar</a> were all arrested in August 2005 at the request of German prosecutor <a href="/wiki/Detlev_Mehlis" title="Detlev Mehlis">Detlev Mehlis</a>, who was carrying out the UN investigation about the assassination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu09_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-reu09-36">[36]</a></sup> Sayyed was one of the persons who decided to assassinate Rafik Hariri according to a leaked draft version of the <a href="/wiki/Mehlis_report" title="Mehlis report">Mehlis report</a> along with other Syrian high-rank intelligence and security officers and officials, namely <a href="/wiki/Assef_Shawkat" title="Assef Shawkat">Assef Shawkat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maher_al-Assad" title="Maher al-Assad">Maher Assad</a>, Hassan Khalil and Bahjat Suleyman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, later reports about the assassination did not repeat the allegations against Jamil Al Sayyed and other three Lebanese generals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu09_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-reu09-36">[36]</a></sup> Four Lebanese generals were held in Roumieh prison, northeast of Beirut from 2005 to 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu09_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-reu09-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowl2009_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-nowl2009-38">[38]</a></sup> They were released from the prison due to lack of evidence in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowl2009_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-nowl2009-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following Hariri's death, there were several other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_Lebanon_bombings" title="2005 Lebanon bombings">bombings and assassinations</a> against minor anti-Syrian figures. These included <a href="/wiki/Samir_Kassir" title="Samir Kassir">Samir Kassir</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Hawi" title="George Hawi">George Hawi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gebran_Tueni" title="Gebran Tueni">Gebran Tueni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Amine_Gemayel" title="Pierre Amine Gemayel">Pierre Amine Gemayel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Ghanem" title="Antoine Ghanem">Antoine Ghanem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walid_Eido" title="Walid Eido">Walid Eido</a>. Assassination attempts were made on <a href="/wiki/Elias_Murr" title="Elias Murr">Elias Murr</a>, <a href="/wiki/May_Chidiac" title="May Chidiac">May Chidiac</a>, and Samir Shehade (who was investigating Hariri's death).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The United Nations special tribunal (see <a href="/wiki/Special_Tribunal_for_Lebanon" title="Special Tribunal for Lebanon">Special Tribunal for Lebanon</a>) investigating the murder of Hariri is expected to issue draft indictments accusing <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a> of murdering Hariri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Evidence_Points_to_Hezbollah_in_Hariri_Murder_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Evidence_Points_to_Hezbollah_in_Hariri_Murder-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hezbollah accused Israel of the assassination of Hariri. According to Hezbollah officials, the assassination of Hariri was planned by the Mossad as a means of expelling the Syrian army from Lebanon. In August 2010, Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah presented evidence, consisting of intercepted Israeli spy-drone video footage, which he said implicated Israel in the assassination of Hariri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> After an altercation between male Tribunal staff and women at a gynecology clinic in October 2010, Hezbollah demanded that the Lebanese government stop all cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/Special_Tribunal_for_Lebanon" title="Special Tribunal for Lebanon">Special Tribunal</a>, claiming the tribunal to be an infringement on Lebanese sovereignty by western governments. On 1 November 2010, a report was leaked by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Akhbar_(Lebanon)" title="Al-Akhbar (Lebanon)">Al Akhbar</a></i>, a local secular, leftist newspaper, stating that Hezbollah drafted plans for a quick takeover of the country in the case an indictment against its members is issued by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Tribunal_for_Lebanon" title="Special Tribunal for Lebanon">UN Special Tribunal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The report states that Hezbollah conducted a simulation of the plan on 28 October, immediately following a speech by its secretary general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other side, it was revealed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikileaks" title="Wikileaks">leaked</a> US embassy cables that then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_General_Intelligence_Directorate" title="Egyptian General Intelligence Directorate">Egyptian General Intelligence Directorate</a> director <a href="/wiki/Omar_Suleiman" title="Omar Suleiman">Omar Suleiman</a> reported that Syria "desperately" wanted to stop the investigation of the Tribunal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corruption">Corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafic_Hariri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hariri was considered as the principal actor in the widespread corruption that plagued Lebanon during the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_occupation_of_Lebanon" title="Syrian occupation of Lebanon">Syrian occupation</a>. His wealth grew from less than $1 billion when he was appointed prime minister in 1992, to over $16 billion when he died. The Company for the Development and Reconstruction of Beirut's Central District, known as <a href="/wiki/Solidere" title="Solidere">Solidere</a>, in which Hariri is the primary shareholder, expropriated most property in the <a href="/wiki/Beirut_Central_District" title="Beirut Central District">central business district</a> of Beirut, compensating each owner with shares in the company, were worth as little as 15% of the property's value. That Hariri and his business associates profited immensely from this project was an open secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corruption-nation_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-corruption-nation-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hariri and his protégés were not the only beneficiaries of this spending spree. In order to secure support from militia chieftains, and pro-Syrian ideologues that Damascus had installed in the government, Hariri allowed kickbacks from public spending to enrich all major government figures. Contracts for the import of petroleum were awarded to the two sons of President <a href="/wiki/Elias_Hrawi" title="Elias Hrawi">Elias Hrawi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corruption-nation_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-corruption-nation-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>As result of the growing criticism and popular discontent with Hariri's policies, the government banned public demonstrations in 1994 and relied upon the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebanese_Army" title="Lebanese Army">Army</a> to enforce the decree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corruption-nation_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-corruption-nation-45">[45]</a></sup> In return for a relatively free hand in economic matters, Hariri cooperated with Syria's drive to consolidate its control over Lebanon. Under the guise of "regulating" the audiovisual media, the government placed control of all major television and radio stations in the hands of pro-Syrian elites. Supporters of <a href="/wiki/Michel_Aoun" title="Michel Aoun">Michel Aoun</a> were also perpetually harassed and detained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lebanon.27s_vast_web_of_corruption_unravels-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corruption-nation_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-corruption-nation-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
