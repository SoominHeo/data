<p><b>Lactose</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> that is found in <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>. Lactose makes up around 2–8% of milk (by weight),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carper_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carper-4">[4]</a></sup> although the amount varies among species and individuals, and milk with <a href="/wiki/Milk#Reduction_or_elimination_of_lactose" title="Milk">a reduced amount of lactose</a> also exists. It is extracted from sweet or sour <a href="/wiki/Whey" title="Whey">whey</a>. The name comes from <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">lac</span></i> (gen. <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">lactis</span></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for milk, plus the <i>-ose</i> ending used to name sugars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It has a formula of C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>22</sub>O<sub>11</sub> and the hydrate formula C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>22</sub>O<sub>11</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O, making it an <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lactose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first crude isolation of lactose, by Italian physician Fabrizio Bartoletti (1576–1630), was published in 1633.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1700, the Venetian pharmacist Lodovico Testi (1640–1707) published a booklet of testimonials to the power of milk sugar (<i>saccharum lactis</i>) to relieve, among other ailments, the symptoms of arthritis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1715, Testi's procedure for making milk sugar was published by Antonio Vallisneri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lactose was identified as a <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> in 1780 by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linko1981_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Linko1981-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1812, Heinrich Vogel (1778-1867) recognized that glucose was a product of hydrolyzing lactose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1856, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> crystallized the other component of lactose, galactose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By 1894, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Emil_Fischer" title="Hermann Emil Fischer">Emil Fischer</a> had established the configurations of the component sugars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lactose was named by the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas" title="Jean-Baptiste Dumas">Jean Baptiste André Dumas</a> (1800-1884) in 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_reactions">Structure and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lactose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="967" data-file-width="1100" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/220px-Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/330px-Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/440px-Alpha-lactose-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

The molecular structure of α-lactose, as determined by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Lactose is a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> derived from the <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, which form a β-1→4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycosidic" title="Glycosidic">glycosidic</a> linkage. Its systematic name is β-<small>D</small>-galactopyranosyl-(1→4)-<small>D</small>-glucose. The glucose can be in either the α-<a href="/wiki/Pyranose" title="Pyranose">pyranose</a> form or the β-pyranose form, whereas the galactose can only have the β-pyranose form: hence α-lactose and β-lactose refer to the <a href="/wiki/Anomer" title="Anomer">anomeric</a> form of the glucopyranose ring alone.</p>
<p>Lactose is hydrolysed to glucose and galactose, isomerised in alkaline solution to <a href="/wiki/Lactulose" title="Lactulose">lactulose</a>, and catalytically hydrogenated to the corresponding polyhydric alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Lactitol" title="Lactitol">lactitol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linko1981_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Linko1981-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lactose monohydrate crystals have a characteristic tomahawk shape that can be observed with a light microscope.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isolation">Isolation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lactose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isolation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several million tons are produced annually as a by-product of the dairy industry. Whey is made of up 6.5% solids of which 4.8% is lactose that may be purified by crystallisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Whey or milk plasma is the liquid remaining after milk is curdled and strained, for example in the production of cheese. Lactose makes up about 2–8% of milk by weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carper_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carper-4">[4]</a></sup> Industrially, lactose is produced from whey permeate – that is whey filtrated for all major proteins. The protein fraction is used in infant nutrition and sport nutrition while the permeate can be evaporated to 60–65% solids and crystallized while cooling .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Lactose can also be precipitated from whey using ethanol. Since it is insoluble in ethanol, lactose precipitates, in about 65% yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lactose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lactose_intolerance" title="Lactose intolerance">Lactose intolerance</a></div>
<p>Infant <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> nurse on their mothers to drink milk, which is rich in lactose. The intestinal <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_villus" title="Intestinal villus">villi</a> secrete the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> called <a href="/wiki/Lactase" title="Lactase">lactase</a> (β-D-galactosidase) to digest it. This enzyme cleaves the lactose molecule into its two subunits, the simple <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a>, which can be absorbed. Since lactose occurs mostly in milk, in most mammals, the production of lactase gradually decreases with maturity due to a lack of continuing consumption.</p>
<p>Many people with ancestry in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Asia" title="West Asia">West Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> belt in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> and a few other parts of <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a> maintain lactase production into adulthood. In many of these areas, milk from mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goats" title="Goats">goats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a> is used as a large source of food. Hence, it was in these regions that genes for lifelong lactase production first <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a>. The genes of adult lactose tolerance have evolved independently in various ethnic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> By descent, more than 70% of western Europeans can drink milk as adults, compared with less than 30% of people from areas of Africa, eastern and south-eastern Asia and Oceania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In people who are lactose intolerant, lactose is not broken down and provides food for gas-producing <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a>, which can lead to diarrhea, bloating, flatulence, and other gastrointestinal symptoms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lactose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Food industry applications have markedly increased since the 1960s. For example, its bland flavor has lent to its use as a carrier and stabiliser of aromas and pharmaceutical products. Lactose is not added directly to many foods, because its solubility is less than that of other sugars commonly used in food. Infant formula is a notable exception, where the addition of lactose is necessary to match the composition of human milk.</p>
<p>Lactose is not fermented by most <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> during brewing, which may be used to advantage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linko1981_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Linko1981-10">[10]</a></sup> For example, lactose may be used to sweeten stout beer; the resulting beer is usually called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milk_stout" title="Milk stout">milk stout</a> or a cream stout.</p>
<p>Yeast belonging to the genus Kluyveromyces have a unique industrial application as they are capable of fermenting lactose for ethanol production. Surplus lactose from the whey by-product of dairy operations is a potential source of alternative energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another major use of lactose is in the pharmaceutical industry. Lactose is added to pills as a filler because of its physical properties, <i>i.e.</i>, compressibility, and low price. For similar reasons it can be used to <a href="/wiki/Lacing_(drugs)#Heroin" title="Lacing (drugs)">dilute heroin</a>.</p>

[Note: "Manna" was the dried, sweet sap of the tree <i><a href="/wiki/Fraxinus_ornus" title="Fraxinus ornus">Fraxinus ornus</a></i>.] Gently distill whey via a heat bath until the buttery scum settles to the bottom of the vessel, to which substance some whitish salt [i.e., precipitate] attaches. This curious [substance once] separated, is truly the essential salt of whey; or, on account of which nitre, is called "nitre of whey", and all [life] force is in this that will be expelled. [Note: "Nitre" was an alchemical concept. It was the power of life, which gave life to otherwise inanimate matter. See the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Sendivogius" title="Sendivogius">Sendivogius</a>.] Dissolve it in [its] own water and coagulate. Repeat the operation until you have cream of whey, recalling, by [its] taste, only manna.)<br/>
In 1688, the German physician Michael Ettmüller (1644–1683) reprinted Bartoletti's preparation. See: Ettmüller, Michael, <i>Opera Omnia</i> … (Frankfurt am Main ("Francofurtum ad Moenum"), [Germany]: Johann David Zunner, 1688), book 2, <a class="external text" href="http://digital.slub-dresden.de/werkansicht/dlf/79899/913/1/cache.off" rel="nofollow">page 163.</a> From page 163: <i>"Undd</i> Bertholetus <i>praeparat ex sero lactis remedium, quod vocat</i> mannam <i>S. [alchemical symbol for salt, salem]</i> seri lactis <i>vid. in</i> Encyclopaed<i>. p. 400. Praeparatio est haec: … "</i> (Whence Bartoletti prepared a medicine from milk whey, which he called <i>manna</i> or <i>salt of milk whey</i>, see in [his] <i>Encyclopedia</i> [note: this is a mistake; the preparation appeared in Bartoletti's <i>Methodus in dyspnoeam</i> … ], p. 400. This is the preparation: … )</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Lodovico Testi, <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=hwM9AAAAcAAJ&amp;pg=PP9#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow"><i>De novo Saccharo Lactis</i></a> [On the new milk sugar] (Venice, (Italy): Hertz, 1700).</span></li>
