<p>The <b>2011–12 Australian region cyclone season</b> was a below average tropical cyclone season, with 7 cyclones forming rather than the usuall 11. It began on 1 November 2011, and ended on 14 May 2012. The regional tropical cyclone operational plan defines a "tropical cyclone year" separately from a "tropical cyclone season"; the "tropical cyclone year" began on 1 July 2011 and ended on 30 June 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scope of the Australian region is limited to all areas south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, east of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">90°E</a> and west of 160°E. This area includes <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, western parts of the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a> and southern parts of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tropical cyclones in this area are monitored by five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone_Warning_Centre" title="Tropical Cyclone Warning Centre">Tropical Cyclone Warning Centres</a> (TCWCs): the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology_(Australia)" title="Bureau of Meteorology (Australia)">Australian Bureau of Meteorology</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brisbane,_Queensland" title="Brisbane, Queensland">Brisbane</a>; TCWC <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>; and TCWC <a href="/wiki/Port_Moresby" title="Port Moresby">Port Moresby</a> in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> issues unofficial warnings for the region, designating tropical depressions with the "S" suffix when they form west of 135°E, and the "P" suffix when they form east of 135°E.</p>

<p>During each tropical cyclone year, Australia's Bureau of Meteorology National Climate Centre (BoM), the Guy Carpenter Asia-Pacific Climate Impact Centre, New Zealand's National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research (NIWA) and partners issue seasonal forecasts for the Australian region and its various subregions. Since a tropical cyclone can move through a region, the actual number of tropical cyclones in a region include any that form in or move into a region from another.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bureau_of_Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2011%E2%80%9312_Australian_region_cyclone_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bureau of Meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2011, ahead of the tropical cyclone season starting on November 1, the BoM issued seasonal forecasts for the whole Australian region, one for each of the subregions Western, North-Western, Northern and Eastern Australia with each one covering the whole <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Times" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlook_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Outlook-7">[7]</a></sup> For each forecast they took into account, the current neutral ENSO conditions to weak La Niña conditions that had been observed during that summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlook_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Outlook-7">[7]</a></sup> For the whole region they predicted that there was an 80% chance that the season would be above average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlook_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Outlook-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the Western region between 90°E and 125°E, the BoM forecast that the area would see activity near to or slightly above the average, with a 35% chance of a below average cyclone activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlook_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Outlook-7">[7]</a></sup> For the North-Western subregion between 105°E and 130°E, it was predicted that there was a 60% chance of above average tropical cyclone activity, while TCWC Perth noted that there was a likelihood of two tropical cyclones and one severe tropical cyclone impacting Western Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTWC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PTWC-8">[8]</a></sup> The Northern Territory which was defined as being between as being 125°E and 142.5°E had a 60% chance of being above average, while the Eastern region had a 65% chance of having an above average tropical cyclone season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlook_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Outlook-7">[7]</a></sup> During February after Cyclone Heidi and Iggy had threatened Western Australia, it was reported that there was a likelihood of at least one more coastal impact and a significant risk that it could be a severe tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTWC2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PTWC2-9">[9]</a></sup> The BoM also issued a seasonal forecasts for the South Pacific region between 142.5°E and 165°E and one for the Eastern Southern Pacific region between 165°E and 120°W.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPOutlook_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPOutlook-10">[10]</a></sup> The outlook called for a westward shift in tropical cyclone activity with a 65% chance that the Western Pacific Region will see more than the average number of tropical cyclones while there was a 40% chance that the Eastern Pacific Region will see above average number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPOutlook_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-SPOutlook-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 3 December, TCWC Perth and TCWC Jakarta started monitoring a tropical low that had developed about 1220 km (760 mi) to the southwest of Jakarta, Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alenga_PTCR_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alenga_PTCR-11">[11]</a></sup> Over the next few days, the low moved towards the southeast, while the system's low-level circulation centre gradually developed further, in an area of moderate vertical wind shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alenga_PTCR_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alenga_PTCR-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During the next day, the low moved out of the Australian region and moved into the South-West Indian Ocean, where it was named Alenga by the Mauritius Meteorological Service on 5 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alenga_PTCR_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alenga_PTCR-11">[11]</a></sup> During that day, RSMC La Reunion reported that Alenga reached an initial 10-minute peak windspeed of 100 km/h (65 mph), as it started to recurve and move towards the Australian region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alenga_PTCR_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Alenga_PTCR-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The system then weakened slightly, before re-intensifying and surpassing its initial peak intensity, as it moved back into the Australian region during 7 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBT_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-RBT-14">[14]</a></sup> Later that day, the JTWC reported that Alenga had become equivalent to a category one hurricane on the Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale (SSHS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBT_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-RBT-14">[14]</a></sup> During the next day, TCWC Perth reported that Alenga had become a Category 3 Severe Tropical Cyclone, and reached its 10-minute peak windspeeds of 150 km/h (90 mph) before the JTWC also reported that Alenga had intensified further and peaked with 1-minute windspeeds of 175 km/h (110 mph), and had become equivalent to a category 3 severe tropical cyclone, on the SSHWS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alenga_PTCR_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Alenga_PTCR-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBT_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-RBT-14">[14]</a></sup> Alenga then started to rapidly weaken as it moved into an area of higher vertical wind shear, with convection diminishing and the low-level circulation centre becoming elongated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tech1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tech1-15">[15]</a></sup> On 9 December, TCWC Perth reported that Alenga had degenerated into a tropical low, while the JTWC followed suit and issued their final advisory on Alenga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tech1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tech1-15">[15]</a></sup> TCWC Perth then tracked the remnants for another three days, in their tropical weather outlooks, before dropping it on 12 December. Alenga brought severe storms to Perth on 12 and 13 Dec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 18 December, TCWC Brisbane reported that a tropical low had developed within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Convergence_Zone" title="South Pacific Convergence Zone">South Pacific Convergence Zone</a>, to the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Port_Moresby" title="Port Moresby">Port Moresby</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fina_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fina-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STWA_12-19_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-STWA_12-19-18">[18]</a></sup> Over the next few days, the low moved towards the south-southwest under the influence of a weak upper ridge of high pressure in an area of weak vertical wind shear. On 20 December, as convection had consolidated over the systems low level circulation centre, the JTWC issued a tropical cyclone formation alert on the system. During the next day TCWC Brisbane reported that the low had become a category one tropical cyclone and named it Fina during the next day as it had 10-minute windspeeds of 65 km/h (40 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, as soon as Fina was named, it started weakening as an upper level trough of low pressure moving through eastern Australia, increased windshear over the system, which meant that Fina's low level circulation centre became exposed, with convection displaced over 135 kilometres (85 mi) from the centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fina_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fina-17">[17]</a></sup> As a result of this, the JTWC decided to cancel their tropical cyclone formation alert late on 22 December, while TCWC Brisbane reported that Fina had degenerated into an extratropical cyclone. Over the next few days Cyclone Fina developed storm force winds and produced dangerous surfing conditions in southeast Queensland over the Christmas holiday. In post-analysis on early-2012, the BoM downgraded Fina to a tropical low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fina_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fina-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 21 December, TCWC Perth reported within their tropical weather outlooks that a tropical low was developing about 770 km (410 mi) to the northeast of the <a href="/wiki/West_Island,_Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="West Island, Cocos (Keeling) Islands">West Island</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="Cocos (Keeling) Islands">Cocos (Keeling) Islands</a>. Over the next few days they continued to monitor it, as the system slowly moved west-southwestward. On 28 December it moved into RSMC La Réunion's area of responsibility, where it commenced a strengthening trend.</p>

<p>An area of thunderstorms north-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a> was designated Tropical Low 04U by TCWC Darwin on 21 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Tropical Low 04U developed into a Category 1 tropical cyclone on the morning of 25 December,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and reached Category 2 strength in the afternoon of 25 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A cyclone watch was declared between Dundee Beach and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nhulunbuy,_Northern_Territory" title="Nhulunbuy, Northern Territory">Nhulunbuy</a>, whilst a cyclone warning was issued for areas between <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fourcroy" title="Cape Fourcroy">Cape Fourcroy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milingimbi" title="Milingimbi">Milingimbi</a> including the Tiwi Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A gale warning had been issued for Darwin and the <a href="/wiki/Tiwi_Islands" title="Tiwi Islands">Tiwi Islands</a> on 24 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon intensification to Category 1 on 25 December the cyclone was moving slowly towards the <a href="/wiki/Cobourg_Peninsula" title="Cobourg Peninsula">Cobourg Peninsula</a>. The system intensified further on 25 December, reaching Category 2 status in the afternoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The system crossed <a href="/wiki/Van_Diemen_Gulf" title="Van Diemen Gulf">Van Diemen Gulf</a> and made a second landfall east of Point Stuart in the early morning of 26 December. Shortly thereafter the cyclone was downgraded to Category 1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and by the afternoon of that day it was downgraded to an ex-tropical low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 December in the evening, the system moved back onto the waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and was downgraded to a tropical low. The system continued to move eastward across the <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Sea" title="Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a>, even as the storm weakened, until Grant underwent an extratropical transition on 2 January 2012. Later on the same day, the extratropical remnant of Grant dissipated.</p>

<p>Early on 9 January, both the JTWC and TCWC Perth started to monitor a tropical low that had developed within a monsoon trough, about 950 km (590 mi) to the north of Port Hedland in Western Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hedi_impact_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hedi_impact-30">[30]</a></sup> Over the next two days, the low gradually moved southwards and developed in favourable conditions into a tropical cyclone, before late on 10 January, it was assigned the designation 06S by the JTWC and named Heidi by TCWC Perth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hedi_impact_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hedi_impact-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In preparation for the cyclone Port Hedland Port Authority closed the port, oilfields and gas production off the North-West coast were halted, and flights from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> to Port Hedland were cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early morning of 12 January the system made landfall just east of Port Hedland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and after a few hours the system was downgraded to Category 1 cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Port Hedland received 128.0 mm (5.04 in) of rain by 9:00am with gusts of 131 km/h (81 mph) and <a href="/wiki/Wallal" title="Wallal">Wallal Downs</a> recording 166.0 mm (6.54 in) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> More than 3,500 homes and businesses in Port Hedland were left without power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Late on 12 January the system was downgraded to a tropical low about 45 kilometres (30 mi) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Price,_Western_Australia" title="Tom Price, Western Australia">Tom Price</a>. Flood Warnings continued for the <a href="/wiki/Ashburton_River_(Western_Australia)" title="Ashburton River (Western Australia)">Ashburton</a> and <a href="/wiki/De_Grey_River" title="De Grey River">De Grey River</a> areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On 13 January, the remnants of Heidi continued to move south through the <a href="/wiki/Gascoyne" title="Gascoyne">Gascoyne</a> region with widespread rainfall totals of up to 30 mm (1.2 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Significant rainfall was recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Gascoyne" title="Gascoyne">Gascoyne</a> region through early morning 14 January at the <a href="/wiki/List_of_homesteads_in_Western_Australia:_Mi-My" title="List of homesteads in Western Australia: Mi-My">homestead</a> of Mount Vernon with 117.0 mm (4.61 in) of rain and <a href="/wiki/List_of_homesteads_in_Western_Australia:_T-V" title="List of homesteads in Western Australia: T-V">Tangadee</a> with 74.0 mm (2.91 in) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gascoy_1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-gascoy_1-39">[39]</a></sup> In the early morning of 15 January a severe weather warning was cancelled for northeast Gascoyne. However showers from the remants of the tropical low still persisted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gascoy_2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-gascoy_2-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ahead of Cyclone Heidi making landfall during 12 January, various tropical cyclone alerts and warnings were issued for parts of the <a href="/wiki/Pilbara" title="Pilbara">Pilbara Coast</a> between Wallal and Dampier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Track data subsequently released by the BoM indicated that Heidi's winds were equivalent to a Category 3 system.</p>

<p>On 21 January, JTWC reported that an area of convection with a weak low level circulation had developed about 480 kilometres (300 mi) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Hedland" title="Port Hedland">Port Hedland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> By 24 January, TCWC Perth reported that a tropical low had formed, and that it was 720 kilometres (445 mi) north northwest of <a href="/wiki/Exmouth,_Western_Australia" title="Exmouth, Western Australia">Exmouth</a> moving west at 23 km/h (14 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The tropical low was then given the identification <i>11U</i>. Late on 25 January, the JTWC upgraded it into a tropical depression and designated as <i>09S</i>. Soon after the system strengthened and the TCWC Perth had named the system Tropical Cyclone <i>Iggy</i>. The system was located 960 kilometres (595 mi) northwest of Exmouth, moving west at 5 km/h (3.1 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>During its formative stages, Iggy was responsible for triggering severe weather, including tornadoes and flooding in <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Lombok" title="Lombok">Lombok, Indonesia</a>, causing the deaths of at least 16 people, and the destruction of thousands of houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early on 26 January, the system began to recurve and headed slowly towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilbara_coast" title="Pilbara coast">Pilbara coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Early on 27 January, the system was upgraded to a Category 2 system, while located 610 kilometres (380 mi) northwest of Exmouth moving east southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> By late 27 January a cyclone warning had been issued from <a href="/wiki/Mardie_Station" title="Mardie Station">Mardie</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ningaloo_Station" title="Ningaloo Station">Ningaloo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> On 28 January, Iggy reached its peak intensity, with a minimum central pressure of 970 mbars. By 29 January, the system became stationary. Wind shear and lower sea surface temperatures, which the cyclone remained stationary over inhibited intensification. Later in the day, the cyclone weakened into a Category 1 cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> On 30 January, after regaining forward momentum, the system began moving southwest at 6 km/h (3.7 mph), and restrengthened into a Category 2 system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Early on 31 January, the system had once again weakened into a Category 1 cyclone, only to reintensify into a Category 2 cyclone on 1 February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iggy eventually lost cyclone strength on 2 February, as it made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Jurien_Bay,_Western_Australia" title="Jurien Bay, Western Australia">Jurien Bay</a>. A Severe Weather Warning for damaging winds and heavy rain was issued for the area bound by <a href="/wiki/Kalbarri,_Western_Australia" title="Kalbarri, Western Australia">Kalbarri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morawa,_Western_Australia" title="Morawa, Western Australia">Morawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wongan_Hills" title="Wongan Hills">Wongan Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narrogin,_Western_Australia" title="Narrogin, Western Australia">Narrogin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvey,_Western_Australia" title="Harvey, Western Australia">Harvey</a> including <a href="/wiki/Geraldton" title="Geraldton">Geraldton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> (the first ex-tropical cyclone to affect the region since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone_Bianca_(2011)" title="Tropical Cyclone Bianca (2011)">Bianca</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Mandurah" title="Mandurah">Mandurah</a>. The town of <a href="/wiki/Lancelin,_Western_Australia" title="Lancelin, Western Australia">Lancelin</a> received 98.0 mm (3.86 in) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> On 3 February, Iggy dissipated inland.</p>

<p>Severe Tropical Cyclone Jasmine was a long–lived <a href="/wiki/Annular_tropical_cyclone" title="Annular tropical cyclone">annular tropical cyclone</a> that affected several countries, particularly <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, over a 19-day span. The sixth cyclone of the season, Cyclone Jasmine developed from an area of disturbed weather on 1 February in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a>. Initially, the storm moved towards the east and across the <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jasmine_TCR_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jasmine_TCR-53">[53]</a></sup> Dry air and wind shear prevented the system from strengthening significantly, but a moisture field over the Australian continent helped the storm maintain its intensity. Wind shear would later be subdued, allowing Jasmine to strengthen quicker. On 5 February, Jasmine reached its peak intensity in the Australian basin as a Category 2 on the Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale, with 10-minute sustained winds of 95 m/h (60 mph). After moving to the southeast in response to a nearby subtropical ridge, Jasmine exited the BOM's area of responsibility on 6 February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasmineadv_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-jasmineadv-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasminebom_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-jasminebom-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Queensland, Jasmine produced heavy rains and gusty winds. Heavy rainfall from Jasmine's outer <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> produced large precipitation totals across southern areas of Queensland. After moving into the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Sea" title="Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">surface trough</a> attached to the system produced more rain showers over Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QLD_Feb_12_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-QLD_Feb_12-56">[56]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cairns,_Queensland" title="Cairns, Queensland">Cairns, Queensland</a>, several roads were closed and trees were uprooted by strong winds associated with Jasmine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severeweathercairns_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-severeweathercairns-57">[57]</a></sup> In addition, power lines that were knocked down in the city's northern suburbs caused <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">power outages</a> for affected areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jasminemovesaway_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-jasminemovesaway-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 5 February, TCWC Perth reported within their tropical weather outlooks that a tropical low was developing near 12°S 95°E, 200 km (120 mi) to the west of the <a href="/wiki/West_Island,_Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="West Island, Cocos (Keeling) Islands">West Island</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="Cocos (Keeling) Islands">Cocos (Keeling) Islands</a>. Over the next few days, TCWC Perth continued to monitor the storm, as it slowly moved westward. TCWC Perth predicted it would move west of 90°E without developing into a Tropical Cyclone. On 14 February, JTWC classified the storm as <i>Tropical Cyclone 13S</i>, as the storm moved out of the BoM's area of responsibility, and into the South-West Indian Ocean basin.</p>

<p>On 8 March, a low-pressure area developed well off the coast of Pilbara. On 9 March, TCWC Perth noted the low had become a tropical low, and then assigned the identification <i>16U</i>. On 13 March, both the BoM and the JTWC upgraded the system to a category 1 tropical cyclone and named it <i>Lua</i>. On 14 March, Lua intensified into a category 2 tropical cyclone as it began moving westwards away from <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. Lua became a category 3 severe tropical cyclone on 16 March. Lua intensified into a category 4 severe tropical cyclone early on 17 March, and it made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Pardoo_Station" title="Pardoo Station">Pardoo</a> later. Early on 18 March, Lua weakened below tropical cyclone intensity. The renmants of the storm dissipated later on that day, after moving deeper into Western Australia.</p>
<p>Lua is so far the strongest cyclone to impact the Australian coast, since <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Yasi" title="Cyclone Yasi">Yasi</a> in 2011, and the <a href="/wiki/Pilbara" title="Pilbara">Pilbara</a> coast, since <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Laurence" title="Cyclone Laurence">Laurence</a> in 2009. Lua is also notable for maintaining cyclone strength as far south as <a href="/wiki/Wiluna,_Western_Australia" title="Wiluna, Western Australia">Wiluna</a>, making it the closest a cyclone has approached the <a href="/wiki/Goldfields-Esperance" title="Goldfields-Esperance">Goldfields-Esperance</a> region, since <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Steve" title="Cyclone Steve">Steve</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The city of <a href="/wiki/Kalgoorlie" title="Kalgoorlie">Kalgoorlie</a> received 59.4 mm of rainfall from the system, its heaviest rainfall in 12 years, while <a href="/wiki/Norseman,_Western_Australia" title="Norseman, Western Australia">Norseman</a> received 60 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 9 March, an area of low pressure developed to the north of <a href="/wiki/Kimberley_(Western_Australia)" title="Kimberley (Western Australia)">Kimberley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Over the next 3 days, the low moved into the Timor Sea, as the storm slowly intensified. On 12 March, TCWC Darwin reported that the storm had intensified into a tropical low and gave it the identification <i>17U</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> As the storm approached the northern coast of Western Australia, it continued to intensify slowly. On 14 March, Tropical Low 17U slowly began to weaken as the storm made landfall on the northeastern border of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. Several hours later, 17U made a second landfall over the northwestern border of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>. Afterwards, TCWC Darwin issued their last bulletin on the storm as it degenerated into a weak low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> During the next several hours, 17U continued moving eastwards deep into the Northern Territory while maintaining its intensity. However, TCWC Darwin continued to track 17U on their 3-Day Outlook, until the storm entered TCWC Brisbane's area of responsibility, on 16 March. Late on 15 March, 17U crossed over into Northern <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> with the northern part of the storm emerging into the southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a>. Soon afterwards, 17U began to reintensify. Late on 17 March, 17U began to stall in the southeastern Gulf of Carpentaria. Late on 18 March, TWCW Brisbane issued their last asvisory on the storm, as it was not expected to become a tropical cyclone, at this point. However, the agency continued to track the storm on their outlook, until the storm's dissipation. Late on 19 March, Tropical Low 17U began moving southward, deeper into Queensland. Early on 20 March, wind shear began weakening the storm, as it turned eastward. Late on 21 March, 17U began emerging off the eastern coast of Queensland, as it was drawn towards a powerful extratropical cyclone, to the east. By 22 March, the extratropical storm had absorbed Tropical Low 17U, and thus the storm was dropped from TCWC Brisbane's outlook.</p>

<p>On 5 May, the JTWC reported that a tropical disturbance had developed within the <a href="/wiki/Banda_Sea" title="Banda Sea">Banda Sea</a>, about 425 km (265 mi) to the northeast of <a href="/wiki/Dili" title="Dili">Dili</a>, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timor_Island" title="Timor Island">Timor Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STWA_05-05_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-STWA_05-05-67">[67]</a></sup> On 7 May, TCWC Jakarta and the JTWC reported that the disturbance strengthened into a tropical depression, off the Indonesian east coast, and gave the storm the identifier <i>19S</i>. Soon afterwards, the storm reached its peak intensity, with a central low pressure of 996 mbars. Late on 7 May, vertical wind shear from the north caused the system's northern convection to dissipate, as the storm turned to the southeast, prompting the JTWC to issue their last advisory on the system. However, on 8 May, the wind shear weakened, and the system regained its convection, as it began to reorganize. Later on the same day, 19S turned to the south-southwest and narrowly passed in between a few Indonesia islands. On 10 May, 19S turned to the southwest, as it approached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timor_Island" title="Timor Island">Timor Island</a>. On 11 May, Tropical Depression 19S turned westward, as it slightly organized. Early on 11 May, Tropical Depression 19S impacted southwestern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timor_Island" title="Timor Island">Timor Island</a> and stalled there for 2 days, while beginning to weaken. On 12 May, Tropical Depression 19S began to weaken more rapidly, as it turned to the northwest. Early on 14 May, Tropical Depression 19S passed to the east of <a href="/wiki/Sumba" title="Sumba">Sumba</a> and impacted <a href="/wiki/Flores" title="Flores">Flores</a>, as the storm continued to move northwestwards. Later on the same day, TCWC Jakarta and the BoM both issued their last advisories on Tropical Depression 19S, as the storm dissipated just north of Flores.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2011%E2%80%9312_Australian_region_cyclone_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Other systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 26 December, TCWC Perth reported that a tropical low had developed near the Western boundary of the region, located over in moderate vertical wind shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The tropical low slowly moved in a westward direction and the JTWC issued a TCFA;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> although TCWC Perth issued its final advisory on the tropical low on 28 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> On 5 January, TCWC Darwin had spotted a weak tropical low, formed from the monsoon trough over to the north of <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a>, however this was the only known advisory for that system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Two tropical lows also developed during January, but those storms remained extremely weak. On 3 March, TCWC Brisbane had reported a slow-moving low over in the central Coral Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The low lingered for a few days and on 6 March, the JTWC upgraded it to a medium chance of becoming a tropical cyclone, and was reported that the low was having some subtropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> TCWC Brisbane made its final advisory on the low on 7 March, as it became a remnant low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
