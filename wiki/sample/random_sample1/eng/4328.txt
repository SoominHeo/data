<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Marterie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marterie first played professionally at age 14 in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MM-1">[1]</a></sup> In the 1940s, he played <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a> for various bands. His first job as a bandleader was courtesy of the US Navy during World War 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MM-1">[1]</a></sup> He was then hired by the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC Radio</a> network,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-MM-1">[1]</a></sup> and the reputation built from these broadcasts led to a recording contract with <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Records" title="Mercury Records">Mercury Records</a>. His highest success in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">charts</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Skokiaan" title="Skokiaan">Skokiaan</a>" in 1954. In 1953 he recorded a version of Bill Haley's "Crazy, Man, Crazy", which is generally regarded as the first rock and roll song. His version of "Crazy, Man, Crazy" reached #13 on the Billboard jockey chart and #11 on Cashbox in June, 1953. His <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">recordings</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Pretend_(song)" title="Pretend (song)">Pretend</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caravan_(1937_song)" title="Caravan (1937 song)">Caravan</a>" also made the <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 10</a>. "Caravan" sold over one million copies, and was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">gold disc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1957, he hit #25 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> with "Tricky", and in 1957 he hit #10 with "Shish-Kebab".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His compositions included "Dancing Trumpet", "Dry Marterie", and "Carla".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joel_Whitburn" title="Joel Whitburn">Joel Whitburn</a>'s pop chart research books say that Marterie's version of "The Song Of Love" peaked at #84 for the week ending December 26, 1955. However, <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> did not put out an issue that week and Marterie never recorded this tune; the listing is in fact a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copyright_trap" title="Copyright trap">copyright trap</a>, to prevent others from stealing Whitburn's work.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>He died on October 10, 1978, in <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
