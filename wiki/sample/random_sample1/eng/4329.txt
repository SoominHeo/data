<p><b>Ralf Brown's Interrupt List</b> (<b>RBIL</b>) is a comprehensive list of <a href="/wiki/Interrupt" title="Interrupt">interrupts</a>, calls, <a href="/wiki/Hooking" title="Hooking">hooks</a>, interfaces, <a href="/wiki/Data_structure" title="Data structure">data structures</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAM" title="RAM">memory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_port_(hardware)" title="Computer port (hardware)">port</a> addresses, and processor <a href="/wiki/Opcode" title="Opcode">opcodes</a> for <a href="/wiki/X86" title="X86">x86</a> machines from the very start of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">PC</a> era in 1981 up into the year 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBIL_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RBIL-1">[1]</a></sup> most of it still applying to PCs.</p>
<p>It covers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operating_systems" title="Operating systems">operating systems</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Device_drivers" title="Device drivers">device drivers</a>, and application <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>; both documented and <a href="/wiki/Undocumented_feature" title="Undocumented feature">undocumented</a> information including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bug_(computing)" title="Bug (computing)">bugs</a>, shortcomings, and workarounds, with version and date information, often at a detail level not found in the contemporary literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS-2">[2]</a></sup> A large part of it covers system <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOSes</a> and internals of operating systems such as <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/OS/2" title="OS/2">OS/2</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows" title="Windows">Windows</a>, as well as their interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The project is the result of the research and collaborative effort of hundreds of contributors worldwide over many years, and is maintained by Ralf Brown, a researcher at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Language_Technologies_Institute" title="Language Technologies Institute">Language Technologies Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_Homepage_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_Homepage-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been a widely used resource by <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">IBM PC</a> system developers as well as application programmers in the pre-<a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS-3">[3]</a></sup> Parts of the compiled information have been used for and in the creation of various books on systems programming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1994_Undocumented-DOS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman_1990_Undocumented-DOS-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_PC-Interrupts_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_PC-Interrupts-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_Network-Interrupts_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_Network-Interrupts-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_1994_Uninterrupted-Interrupts_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_1994_Uninterrupted-Interrupts-7">[7]</a></sup> As such it has proven to be an important resource in developing various open source operating systems, including <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and <a href="/wiki/FreeDOS" title="FreeDOS">FreeDOS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_2002_FreeDOS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_2002_FreeDOS-8">[8]</a></sup> Today it is still used as a reference to BIOS calls and to develop programs for DOS as well as other system-level software.</p>
<p>The list is currently at Revision 61 as of 17 July 2000, and is almost 8 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> in <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> text,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBIL_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-RBIL-1">[1]</a></sup> which would result in more than 2500 pages of condensed information when printed.</p>
