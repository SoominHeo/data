<p><b>Lambayeque</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Peru" title="Administrative divisions of Peru">region</a> in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> known for its rich <a href="/wiki/Moche_culture" title="Moche culture">Moche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chim%C3%BA_culture" title="Chimú culture">Chimú</a> historical past. The region's name originates from the ancient pre-<a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca</a> civilization of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sican_culture" title="Sican culture">Lambayeque</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambayeque_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Lambayeque</i> is a Spanish derivation of the god <i>Yampellec</i>, said to have been worshipped by the first Lambayeque king, <i>Naymlap</i>. The Spanish gave the name to the early people.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambayeque_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The territory of the Lambayeque Region is made up of wide plains irrigated by rivers from the Andes; in most of the arid area, irrigation is needed to support any farming. The fertile river valleys produce half of the sugar cane crop of Peru. In addition, Lambayeque and the <a href="/wiki/Piura_Region" title="Piura Region">Piura Region</a> provide most of the rice crops consumed in Peru.</p>
<p>Increased agricultural harvest is expected with completion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olmos_Transandino_Project" title="Olmos Transandino Project">Olmos Transandino Project</a>. The water supply project will transfer up to 2 billion m<sup>3</sup> annually of water from the <a href="/wiki/Huancabamba_River" title="Huancabamba River">Huancabamba River</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cajamarca_Region" title="Cajamarca Region">Cajamarca Region</a> east of Lambayeque.</p>
<p>In the smaller scale farming of earlier centuries, the Olmos <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carob" title="Carob">Carob</a> Tree Forest supported goat herds that fed on carobs. The fine goatskins were tanned to create the fine, pale, leather known as "cordoban" or "cordovan", from the Spanish town of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Andalusia" title="Córdoba, Andalusia">Córdoba</a>, where the process was developed. Goat fat was used to make soap.</p>
<p>There are two small islands off the Pacific coast of the Lambayeque Region: Lobos de Afuera, and Lobos de Tierra; there was a dispute with the Piura Region over ownership of the latter island.</p>
<p>The region is bordered by the Piura Region on the north, the Cajamarca Region on the southeast, the <a href="/wiki/La_Libertad_Region" title="La Libertad Region">La Libertad Region</a> on the south and the Pacific Ocean on the west.</p>

<p>The region is divided into 3 <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Peru" title="Provinces of Peru">provinces</a> (<i>provincias</i>, singular: <i>provincia</i>), which are composed of 38 <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Peru" title="Districts of Peru">districts</a> (<i>distritos</i>, singular: <i>distrito</i>). The provinces, with their capitals in parentheses, are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chiclayo_Province" title="Chiclayo Province">Chiclayo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chiclayo" title="Chiclayo">Chiclayo</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ferre%C3%B1afe_Province" title="Ferreñafe Province">Ferreñafe</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ferre%C3%B1afe" title="Ferreñafe">Ferreñafe</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lambayeque_Province" title="Lambayeque Province">Lambayeque</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lambayeque,_Peru" title="Lambayeque, Peru">Lambayeque</a>)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambayeque_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Legend tells that in ancient times, a great float of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balsa" title="Balsa">balsa</a> rafts arrived at the beaches of the existing <a href="/wiki/San_Jos%C3%A9_District,_Lambayeque" title="San José District, Lambayeque">San José cove</a>. Formed by a brilliant cortege of nine foreign warriors, this float was led by a man of great talent and courage, named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naymlap" title="Naymlap">Naymlap</a>, the mythical founder of the first northwest civilization.</p>
<p>Among the descendants of Naylmlap were the <a href="/wiki/Moche_culture" title="Moche culture">Moche</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chim%C3%BA_culture" title="Chimú culture">Chimú</a>, the latter builders of a great civilization forged in Lambayeque before being conquered by the later <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a>. The Chimú grew to acquire a notable state parallel to the Inca. The Chimú moved their capital to the northern area, establishing great urban centers there. They were great farmers, textile experts and, wonderful goldsmiths, with extraordinary works in gold.</p>
<p>The Inca conquest of what today is Lambayeque, lasted almost four decades. <a href="/wiki/Pachacuti" title="Pachacuti">Pachacuti</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tupac_Inca_Yupanqui" title="Tupac Inca Yupanqui">Tupac Inca Yupanqui</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huayna_Capac" title="Huayna Capac">Huayna Cápac</a>, successively, ruled during the process.</p>
<p>In the 16th century, the Spaniard leader <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a> took his conquistadors across the region on the way to <a href="/wiki/Cajamarca" title="Cajamarca">Cajamarca</a> to conclude the defeat of the Inca empire. He was amazed by the gold exposed in vases and utensils.</p>
<p>During Colonial times, a rivalry started between the people of the towns of Lambayeque and Santiago de Miraflores de Saña. The reason of the conflict was the opulence in which the latter lived, even provoking the greed of pirates. A flood in 1720, however, destroyed Saña and marked the end of a flourishing city.</p>
<p>The people of Lambayeque followed Juan Manuel Iturregui as their leader in the struggles for emancipation and independence from Spain. He spread the libertarian ideas and helped get arms for the cause.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_from_Lambayeque">Music from Lambayeque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambayeque_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Music from Lambayeque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most famous composer from Lambayeque was Luis Abelardo Nuñez, born in <a href="/wiki/Ferre%C3%B1afe" title="Ferreñafe">Ferreñafe</a> on 22 November 1926. His songs are among the most popular ones in Peruvian music. These included the following:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.lostroveroscriollos.com/video.php?contentID=238" rel="nofollow">"Marinera norteña"</a>, Los Troveros Criollos</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.lostroveroscriollos.com/video.php?contentID=96" rel="nofollow">Waltz: "Porqué no volverás?"</a></li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.lostroveroscriollos.com/video.php?contentID=228" rel="nofollow">Waltz: "Embrujo"</a></li>
</ul>
